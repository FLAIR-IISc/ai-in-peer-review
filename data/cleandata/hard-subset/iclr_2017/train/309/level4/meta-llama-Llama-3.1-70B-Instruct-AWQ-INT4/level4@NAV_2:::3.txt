This paper proposes a novel approach to reinforcement learning by introducing auxiliary control and reward prediction tasks to improve data efficiency and robustness. The authors argue that traditional unsupervised learning methods may not be effective in learning useful representations, and instead, propose learning to control local and internal features of the environment. The proposed tasks are intuitive and well-explained, and the methodology is sound.

The paper claims to contribute to the field of reinforcement learning by introducing a new approach that combines the benefits of two state-of-the-art methods. The authors demonstrate significant improvements in performance on discrete-action-continuous-space RL tasks, achieving baseline performance in 10x less iterations.

I accept this paper, with two key reasons for this choice. Firstly, the approach is novel and proposes a very interesting alternative to unsupervised learning that takes advantage of the "possibility" of control that an agent has over the environment. Secondly, the methodology is sound, and the authors demonstrate significant improvements in performance on challenging tasks.

The paper is well-written and clear, making it easily understandable for readers familiar with deep reinforcement learning. The authors provide a good balance of high-level intuition and low-level details, making it easy to follow their arguments.

One area for improvement is the lack of experimental analysis of the effect of the auxiliary tasks apart from their impact on performance. It would be interesting to see more in-depth analysis of the effects of pixel and feature control on the learned representations and the agent's behavior. Additionally, the authors could provide more insights into why the proposed tasks are effective and how they relate to the underlying structure of the environment.

Some specific questions I would like the authors to address are: (1) How do the auxiliary tasks affect the learned representations, and are there any notable differences between the representations learned by the UNREAL agent and the A3C agent? (2) Can the authors provide more insights into why pixel and feature control are effective, and are there any other auxiliary tasks that could be used to improve performance? (3) How do the authors plan to extend this work to more complex environments and tasks, and are there any potential limitations or challenges that they foresee?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. With some additional analysis and insights, it has the potential to be even more impactful.
