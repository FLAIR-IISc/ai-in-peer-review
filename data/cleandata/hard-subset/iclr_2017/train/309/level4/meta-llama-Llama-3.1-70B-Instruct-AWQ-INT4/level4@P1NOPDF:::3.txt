This work proposes training reinforcement learning (RL) agents to perform auxiliary tasks, hypothesizing that this approach will help models learn stronger features. The authors introduce two pseudo-control tasks: controlling changes in pixel intensity and controlling the activation of latent features. Additionally, they propose a supervised regression task that predicts immediate reward following a sequence of events. The latter is learned offline through a skewed sampling of an experience replay buffer, which balances the chance of seeing a reward or not to 1/2.

Agents trained with these auxiliary tasks achieve significant performance gains on discrete-action-continuous-space RL tasks, reaching baseline performance in approximately 10 times fewer iterations. This work contrasts with traditional "passive" unsupervised or model-based learning approaches. Instead of forcing the model to learn a potentially useless representation of the input or an impossible task-modelling objective due to partial observability, learning to control local and internal features of the environment complements learning the optimal control policy.

In my opinion, the approach is novel and presents an interesting alternative to unsupervised learning that leverages the "possibility" of control an agent has over the environment. The proposed tasks are explained at a high level, making it convenient to understand the intuition behind them. However, I believe that providing some lower-level details would be beneficial. For instance, L_PC should be explicitly mentioned before the appendix. Otherwise, the work is clear and easily understandable for readers familiar with Deep RL.

The methodology is sound. Although the distribution of best hyperparameters might differ between A3C and UNREAL, measuring the top-3 ensures that the per-method best hyperparameters are found, assuming that the best hyperparameters for both A3C and UNREAL are within the explored intervals. I think one weakness of the paper is the limited experimental analysis of the effect of the auxiliary tasks, apart from their strong impact on performance. Furthermore, pixel/feature control seems to have the most significant impact, as evidenced by A3C+PC outperforming other methods in Labyrinth (except for UNREAL). I believe it would be worth investigating this aspect in more depth, measuring more than just performance on RL tasks.
