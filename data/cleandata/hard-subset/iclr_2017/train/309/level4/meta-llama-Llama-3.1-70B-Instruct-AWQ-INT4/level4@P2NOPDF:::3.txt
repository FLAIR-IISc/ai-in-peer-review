This work proposes a novel approach to training reinforcement learning (RL) agents by incorporating auxiliary tasks that complement the primary control objective. The authors posit that this multi-task learning paradigm enables models to learn stronger features, leading to improved performance in RL tasks. 

The proposed method introduces two pseudo-control tasks: controlling changes in pixel intensity and controlling the activation of latent features. Additionally, a supervised regression task is proposed to predict immediate rewards following a sequence of events. The latter is learned offline using a skewed sampling of an experience replay buffer to balance the probability of seeing a reward or not. 

The experimental results demonstrate that agents trained with these auxiliary tasks achieve significant performance gains on discrete-action-continuous-space RL tasks, reaching baseline performance in substantially fewer iterations (approximately 10 times less). 

This approach contrasts with traditional unsupervised or model-based learning methods, which often require the model to learn a potentially useless representation of the input or an infeasible task-modelling objective due to partial observability. In contrast, learning to control local and internal features of the environment complements the learning of optimal control policies.

The proposed approach is novel and presents an interesting alternative to unsupervised learning, leveraging the agent's control over the environment. The explanation of the proposed tasks is provided at a high level, making it easy to understand the intuition behind the method. However, additional low-level details, such as explicitly mentioning L_PC before the appendix, would be beneficial for clarity.

The methodology is sound, and the authors take a reasonable approach to hyperparameter tuning by considering the top-3 performing configurations. This ensures that the best hyperparameters for both A3C and UNREAL are likely to be found, assuming they lie within the explored intervals.

One potential weakness of the paper is the limited experimental analysis of the effect of the auxiliary tasks beyond their impact on performance. A more in-depth examination of the individual contributions of each task, particularly pixel/feature control, would be valuable. For instance, the results in Labyrinth suggest that A3C+PC outperforms other methods (except UNREAL), warranting further investigation into the isolated effects of this task or a more detailed analysis of its impact on RL performance.
