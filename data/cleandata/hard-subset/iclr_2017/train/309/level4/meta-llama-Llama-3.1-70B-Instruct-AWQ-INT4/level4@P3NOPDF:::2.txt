This paper proposes an innovative approach to improve feature learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the main policy's optimization problem with terms corresponding to domain-independent auxiliary tasks. These tasks focus on control, immediate reward prediction, and value function replay, with the latter being the only task not used for shaping features through a shared CNN+LSTM feature extraction network. The experimental results demonstrate the benefits of this approach on Atari and Labyrinth problems, showcasing improved data efficiency compared to A3C.

The paper is well-written, and the ideas presented are sound. The results are convincing, making this a strong candidate for acceptance. However, I have a few high-level comments to provide further clarity and improvement:

1. The authors should discuss the extra computational cost associated with optimizing these auxiliary tasks. Specifically, they should quantify the loss in training speed and identify the most computationally expensive components.

2. To avoid confusion, the abstract and introduction should clearly state that the agent learns different policies for each task. The current phrasing in the abstract, "the agent also maximises many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously by reinforcement learning," may be misinterpreted as the agent learning a single policy to optimize all rewards together.

3. The "feature control" idea, although intriguing, lacks empirical validation. The preliminary experiment in Figure 5 is not convincing, as it only shows a slight improvement initially. I am concerned that the changing task during learning, due to the modification of extracted features, may lead to stability and convergence issues.

4. Given that the performance of the agents is still improving, it would be beneficial to continue training them to observe their full potential, at least for the top-performing agents.

5. The authors should consider making the auxiliary task weight parameters (λ*) hyperparameters to optimize. It is unclear whether using λ* = 1 is the optimal choice, and experiments validating this decision would strengthen the paper.

6. The authors should acknowledge that the auxiliary tasks are not trained with "true" Q-Learning, as they are trained off-policy with more than one step of empirical rewards, as discussed in the OpenReview comments.

Minor comments:

* In the sentence "Policy gradient algorithms adjust the policy to maximise the expected reward, L_pi = -...", L_pi is actually a loss to be minimized.
* In equation 1, λ_c should be within the sum.
* Just below equation 1, rt^(c) should be rt+k^(c).
* Figure 2 is not referenced in the text, and Figure 1(d) should be referenced in section 3.3.
* The sentence "the features discovered in this manner is shared" should be "the features discovered in this manner are shared."
* The text around equation 2 refers to the loss L_PC, but this term is not defined and is not explicitly included in equation 2.
* The legend of Figure 3 should explain what "Clip" means for dueling networks.
* Additional ablated versions on Atari would be beneficial to observe if the same patterns of individual contribution as on Labyrinth are present.
* The legend of Figure 3 mentions percentages, but it is unclear that these refer to Labyrinth.
* In section 4.1.2, "Figure 3 (right) shows..." should be "Figure 3 (top left) shows...". Additionally, "This is shown in Figure 3 Top" should be "Figure 3 Top Right."
* The sentence "Figure 5 shows the learning curves for the top 5 hyperparameter settings on three Labyrinth navigation levels" likely refers to the left and middle plots of the figure, showing results for only two levels.
* In section 4.2, a comma is missing after "methods" in the sentence "The left side shows the average performance curves of the top 5 agents for all three methods the right half shows..."
* The appendix mentions "Further details are included in the supplementary materials," but it is unclear where these materials are located.
* The value of λ_PC is not specified, but it is likely equal to 1.

Overall, this paper presents a compelling approach to improving feature learning in deep reinforcement learning. Addressing the above comments will further strengthen the paper and provide clarity for the readers.
