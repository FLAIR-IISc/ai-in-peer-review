This paper proposes to improve feature learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the main policy optimization with (domain-independent) auxiliary tasks. These tasks include control (learning separate policies that attempt to maximally alter the state space, here the pixels), immediate reward prediction, and value-function replay. Except for the latter, the auxiliary tasks are used solely to shape the shared representation by training a common CNN+LSTM feature extractor. Experiments on Atari and Labyrinth demonstrate clear benefits, in particular substantially better data efficiency than A3C.

The paper is well written, the ideas are sound, and the results are quite convincing; I recommend acceptance. At a high level I have only a few comments, none of which is a major concern:
- Please discuss the additional computational cost of optimizing the auxiliary tasks. How much training speed is lost, and which components dominate the overhead?
- In the abstract/introduction, clarify that the agent learns distinct policies for each auxiliary control task. The sentence “also maximises many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously by reinforcement learning” led me to initially (and incorrectly) believe a single policy was trained to jointly optimize all rewards; I only realized the intended setup upon reaching Eq. 1.
- The “feature control” idea lacks strong empirical validation. The preliminary result in Fig. 5 is not very compelling, as it seems to help only slightly and only early in training. I like the direction, but I am concerned that the task itself changes during learning because the features evolve, which could introduce stability or convergence issues.
- Since you note that “the performance of our agents is still steadily improving,” why not continue training (at least for the best agents) to see their eventual performance?
- Why are the auxiliary-task weights (the λ_*) not treated as tunable hyperparameters? Were experiments performed to justify setting them to 1?
- Please note that the auxiliary tasks are not trained with “true” Q-learning, as they use off-policy data with targets spanning more than a single step of empirical rewards (as discussed in the OpenReview comments).
