The paper proposes UNREAL: an A3C-based agent augmented with domain-agnostic auxiliary tasks to learn stronger representations under sparse extrinsic rewards.  
UNREAL jointly optimizes (i) off-policy auxiliary control tasks via n-step Q-learning (pixel/feature control), (ii) immediate reward prediction with rebalanced replay, and (iii) value-function replay, all sharing a CNN+LSTM encoder.  
Most auxiliary heads shape features only and do not act.  
UNREAL yields large empirical gains: ~10× speedup on Labyrinth (mean ~87% human-normalized) and ~880% human performance on Atari, with improved data efficiency and robustness.  
Decision: Accept.  
Main reason: well-motivated, clearly positioned contribution using auxiliary control and reward prediction for representation learning within an on-/off-policy framework.  
Main reason: large, consistent empirical gains and careful evaluation showing robustness over A3C.  
Targets the core challenge of learning useful features in sparse-reward, high-dimensional environments; pseudo-rewards as control tasks align representations with downstream control better than reconstruction.  
Method is clearly contrasted with options/Horde/UVFA/successor representations and model-based auxiliary predictions; novelty is multiple off-policy control heads used purely for representation shaping.  
Ablations show control-based auxiliaries outperform pixel reconstruction and immediate-change prediction in Labyrinth; robustness analyses over hyperparameters and seeds are a strength.  
Feature-control idea is interesting but only preliminarily evaluated; core claims remain well supported.  
Request to report wall-clock training time vs A3C, break down cost per auxiliary component, and provide hardware and throughput (frames/sec).  
Ask to clarify in abstract/intro that separate policies/heads are learned for each auxiliary control task (to avoid misinterpretation).  
Feature-control stability: discuss non-stationarity and stabilization strategies (target networks, stop-gradient, delayed updates); add empirical evidence or move to appendix.  
Extend training for best agents as some learning curves were still improving to show asymptotic performance.  
Justify fixing loss weights λ (λVR, λPC, λRP), provide sensitivity analysis, and specify actual λPC used (which may differ by domain).  
Clarify that auxiliary Q-learning uses off-policy n-step returns (not one-step Q-learning), discuss potential bias and why it worked in practice.  
Include per-component ablations on Atari to verify whether Labyrinth patterns generalize.  
List of editorial/minor corrections: clarify Lπ wording; fix Eq. (1) notation and place λC correctly; define LPC and align Eq. (2); grammar fixes; reference/fix figures and legends (Figure 2, 1(d), Fig. 3 “Clip”/percentages/labels, Fig. 5 level count); punctuation in Section 4.2; specify appendix access and λPC value.  
Questions for authors — sensitivity of performance to pixel-control grid resolution and 80×80 crop; whether pixel-change rewards were normalized across games with different intensity dynamics.  
Questions — for reward prediction, does balancing zero/non-zero classes suffice when rewards vary in magnitude or sign; any representation bias or negative transfer from rebalanced replay?  
Questions — were there cases where auxiliary tasks slowed or distracted learning; any safeguards like annealing λPC/λRP?  
Questions — report exact environment-frame counts corresponding to reported steps (with action repeat of 4) for comparability.  
Questions — please share wall-clock training times per domain and relative cost per auxiliary task.
