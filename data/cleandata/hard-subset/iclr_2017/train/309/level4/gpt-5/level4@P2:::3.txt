Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, a deep RL agent that augments standard policy/value learning with auxiliary tasks to accelerate representation learning and improve data efficiency. The key auxiliary control tasks are: (1) pixel control—learning policies that maximize changes in local pixel intensities over a spatial grid; and (2) feature control—learning policies that maximize activations of internal network units. In addition, the agent learns reward-focused auxiliary tasks: immediate reward prediction (via skewed replay to oversample rare rewards) and value-function replay. All tasks share a common representation, and the auxiliary tasks are trained off-policy with experience replay while the main policy/value are learned on-policy (A3C).

Empirically, UNREAL delivers large gains on both 3D Labyrinth and Atari: substantially faster learning (around 10× fewer steps to match A3C’s best scores on Labyrinth), higher final performance (e.g., averaging 87% of expert human on Labyrinth and 880% expert human on Atari), and improved robustness to hyperparameters. Ablations show that auxiliary tasks—especially pixel/feature control—drive much of the improvement over A3C.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons: (1) The approach is novel and well motivated, offering a compelling alternative to traditional unsupervised/model-based auxiliary objectives by exploiting control as the learning signal. (2) The empirical results are strong, broad, and carefully evaluated, showing significant and robust improvements in both performance and data efficiency.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses learning useful representations when extrinsic rewards are sparse. Instead of reconstructing inputs or learning full models (which may be unnecessary or intractable under partial observability), the agent learns to predict and control salient aspects of its sensorimotor stream. This is well positioned relative to options, Horde/UVFA, and successor representations, with the key distinction that auxiliary policies are used to shape representations rather than as temporally extended actions.
- Methodological soundness: The combined on-policy/off-policy training is coherent: A3C trains the policy/value on-policy with entropy regularization; auxiliary control tasks use n-step Q-learning from replay; reward prediction uses skewed replay to counter sparsity without directly biasing the policy/value targets. The unified loss is clearly specified, and implementation details are provided.
- Empirical support: Results span 13 Labyrinth levels and 57 Atari games, include ablations, and evaluate robustness via broad hyperparameter sweeps and top-k reporting. The fairness of comparing top-3 per method is reasonable given that hyperparameter ranges are searched for each method.
- Clarity: The high-level intuition is clear, and the overall architecture is easy to follow. The appendices supply the missing implementation specifics.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Move key definitions from the appendix into the main text, e.g., the explicit form of the auxiliary control loss (L_PC), the exact pixel-control reward, and the deconvolutional head. This will aid reproducibility without requiring readers to consult the appendix.
- Provide deeper analysis of why and how auxiliary tasks help, beyond final scores. Suggestions:
  - Quantify contributions of each auxiliary task across both Labyrinth and Atari (e.g., pixel control vs. feature control vs. reward prediction vs. value replay), and report their interactions.
  - Probe representation quality (e.g., linear probes for reward-related features, generalization to unseen goals, or transfer across tasks).
  - Report sensitivity analyses: grid resolution for pixel control, replay buffer size, skew ratio for reward prediction, λ weights for losses.
  - Discuss computational overhead (training time, memory, wall-clock vs. sample efficiency).
- Clarify the potential for bias leakage: although reward prediction does not directly affect policy/value targets, its shared CNN could indirectly bias features. A brief discussion or control experiment (e.g., a separate encoder) would strengthen the claim that policy learning remains unbiased.
- Consider reporting whether auxiliary policies themselves improve exploration (e.g., occasional rollouts under pixel-control policies) or can be composed into options—a natural next step.

Questions for the authors
- Of the auxiliary tasks, which consistently contributes most to the gains on Atari and Labyrinth? Can you provide a per-domain/per-game breakdown, especially comparing pixel control to feature control?
- How sensitive is performance to the reward-prediction replay skew (e.g., the 50/50 zero vs. non-zero split)? Does the benefit persist with different skews or under highly imbalanced reward distributions?
- Does sharing the convolutional encoder between reward prediction and policy/value introduce any measurable bias or instability? Have you tried a separate encoder for the reward predictor?
- How large is the computational overhead (training time and memory) from auxiliary heads and replay relative to A3C? Are gains primarily sample-efficiency or also wall-clock?
- For pixel control, how do grid size and the definition of “pixel change” affect results? Are improvements robust across these design choices?
- Feature control is promising in Labyrinth. How does it perform on Atari, and which layer’s activations are most effective as pseudo-rewards?
- Could the learned auxiliary policies be leveraged for exploration or as options during planning? If attempted, what were the outcomes?
- Do the benefits hold in continuous-action domains, or does the method require adaptation for such settings?
