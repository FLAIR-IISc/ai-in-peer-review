Summary:
This paper proposes improving representation learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the main policy’s optimization objective with domain-independent auxiliary tasks. These include auxiliary control tasks (learning additional policies that attempt to maximally modify the state, here the pixels), immediate reward prediction, and value-function replay. All but the last are used solely to shape the shared features via a shared CNN+LSTM encoder. Experiments on Atari and Labyrinth show clear benefits, especially markedly improved data efficiency over A3C.

Overall assessment:
The paper is well written, the ideas are sound, and the empirical results are convincing. I recommend acceptance.

Major comments and questions:
- Computational overhead: Please quantify the additional computational cost of optimizing the auxiliary tasks. How much wall-clock training speed is lost compared to the base agent? Which components (e.g., auxiliary control, reward prediction, replayed updates) dominate the cost?
- Clarify policy multiplicity in the abstract/intro: The abstract states that the agent “also maximises many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously by reinforcement learning.” This can be misread as a single policy optimizing all rewards jointly. Please make it clear earlier (not only at Eq. 1) that the agent learns separate policies for the auxiliary control tasks.
- Feature-control validation and stability: The “feature control” idea is not convincingly validated empirically; the preliminary result in Fig. 5 suggests only a slight initial benefit. I like the idea, but I am concerned that the target of the task changes during learning as the features evolve, which could introduce stability/convergence issues. Some analysis or additional experiments addressing this would strengthen the paper.
- Training to convergence: You note that “the performance of our agents is still steadily improving.” Why not train the best agents longer to see how far they ultimately go?
- Auxiliary-task weights: Why aren’t the auxiliary loss weights (the λ_*) treated as hyperparameters? Were experiments run to validate that setting them to 1 is a good choice?
- Q-learning detail: Please explicitly mention that the auxiliary tasks are not trained with “true” Q-learning, because they use off-policy updates with more than one step of empirical rewards (as discussed in the OpenReview comments).
