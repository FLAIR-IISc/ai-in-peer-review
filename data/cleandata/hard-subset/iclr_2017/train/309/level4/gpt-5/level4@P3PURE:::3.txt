This paper proposes training reinforcement learning agents on auxiliary tasks to encourage the learning of stronger features. Specifically, it introduces two pseudo-control tasks—controlling changes in pixel intensity and controlling the activation of latent features—and a supervised regression task that predicts immediate reward following a sequence of events. The latter is trained offline using skewed sampling from an experience replay buffer to balance the probability of seeing reward versus no reward at 1/2.

Empirically, the agents perform strongly on discrete-action, continuous-space RL tasks and reach baseline performance with roughly 10× fewer iterations.

Conceptually, the work contrasts with traditional “passive” unsupervised or model-based learning. Rather than forcing the agent to learn a potentially useless representation of the input or to solve a possibly impossible task-modeling objective (e.g., due to partial observability), learning to control local and internal features of the environment complements learning the optimal control policy. To me, the approach is novel and offers a very interesting alternative to unsupervised learning that leverages the agent’s “possibility” of control over the environment.

Clarity is generally good for readers familiar with Deep RL. The auxiliary tasks are explained at a high level, which is helpful for conveying intuition, but some lower-level detail would be useful. For example, L_PC should be explicitly defined in the main text rather than deferred to the appendix.

The methodology appears sound. On the one hand, the distribution of best hyperparameters may differ for A3C and UNREAL; on the other hand, reporting top-3 results helps ensure that—presuming the best hyperparameters for both A3C and UNREAL lie within the explored intervals—the best per-method settings are identified.

A key weakness (or, given space constraints, a clear direction for essential future work) is the limited experimental analysis of the auxiliary tasks beyond their strong impact on aggregate performance. In particular, pixel/feature control seems to have the most influence: in Labyrinth, A3C+PC alone outperforms all other variants except UNREAL. It would be valuable to study this either in isolation or in greater depth, and to measure more than just downstream RL task performance.
