This paper trains RL agents to perform auxiliary tasks alongside the main objective, with the hypothesis that doing so encourages the learning of stronger representations. The authors introduce two auxiliary pseudo-control tasks—controlling changes in pixel intensity and controlling the activation of latent features—and a supervised regression task that predicts the immediate reward following a sequence of events. The reward-prediction head is trained offline using skewed sampling from an experience replay buffer to balance reward vs. no-reward cases at a 1/2 probability.

The resulting agents perform strongly on discrete-action, continuous-state RL tasks and achieve baseline performance in roughly ten times fewer training iterations. The work stands in contrast to traditional “passive” unsupervised or model-based approaches: rather than forcing the model to learn a potentially unhelpful representation of the input or to solve an often impossible task-modeling objective under partial observability, learning to control local and internal features of the environment complements the learning of the optimal control policy.

I find the approach novel and a compelling alternative to unsupervised learning that leverages the agent’s capacity to exert control over the environment. The auxiliary tasks are described at a high level, which conveys the intuition well, but some additional technical detail would help. For example, L_PC should be defined explicitly in the main text rather than only in the appendix. Aside from this, the paper is clear and readily understandable to readers familiar with deep RL.

The methodology appears sound. While the distribution of optimal hyperparameters may differ between A3C and UNREAL, reporting top-3 performance mitigates this: assuming that each method’s optimal settings lie within the explored ranges, the per-method best hyperparameters are likely to be identified.

A key weakness (or, given space constraints, an important direction for future work) is the limited experimental analysis of the auxiliary tasks’ effects beyond their strong impact on aggregate performance. Relatedly, pixel/feature control appears to drive much of the improvement: in Labyrinth, A3C+PC alone outperforms all other variants (except UNREAL). It would be valuable to analyze this component either in isolation or in greater depth, with metrics beyond task performance.
