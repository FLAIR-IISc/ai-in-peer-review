This paper proposes training RL agents on auxiliary tasks as well, arguing that this leads to stronger learned features.
They introduce two pseudo-control tasks—controlling changes in pixel intensity and controlling the activation of latent features—and a supervised regression task that predicts the immediate reward after a sequence of events. The latter is trained offline using biased sampling from an experience replay buffer to make the chance of observing a reward versus no reward roughly 1/2.
These agents perform markedly well on RL problems with discrete actions in continuous spaces, achieving baseline performance in 10× fewer iterations.
This work contrasts with traditional “passive” unsupervised or model-based approaches. Rather than pushing the model to learn potentially unhelpful input representations or to solve possibly infeasible (due to partial observability) modeling objectives, learning to control local and internal environmental features complements learning the optimal control policy.
In my view, the approach is novel and presents a compelling alternative to unsupervised learning that leverages the agent’s capacity to exert control over the environment.
The auxiliary tasks are described at a fairly high level, which aids intuition, but some additional low-level detail would help. For instance, L_PC should be introduced explicitly before the appendix. Aside from that, the paper is clear and accessible to readers familiar with Deep RL.
The methodology appears sound. While the distributions of best hyperparameters may differ for A3C and UNREAL, reporting top-3 results helps ensure that—assuming the optimal settings for both A3C and UNREAL lie within the explored ranges—the best per-method hyperparameters are identified.
A key weakness (or, given space limits, an important direction for future work) is the limited experimental analysis of how the auxiliary tasks influence learning beyond their strong impact on performance. Similarly, pixel/feature control seems most influential; in Labyrinth, A3C+PC alone outperforms everything else (except UNREAL). It would have been valuable to examine this either in isolation or in greater depth, with metrics beyond standard RL task performance.
