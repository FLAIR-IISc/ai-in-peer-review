This paper augments RL agents with auxiliary tasks to encourage the learning of more robust representations. It introduces two pseudo-control objectives—controlling changes in pixel intensity and controlling activations of latent features—and a supervised regression task that predicts immediate reward following a sequence of observations. The reward prediction head is trained offline from a replay buffer with skewed sampling that balances reward and no-reward cases at roughly 50/50. On discrete-action, continuous-state tasks, the proposed agents achieve strong results and match baseline performance in roughly one-tenth the number of iterations.

The approach contrasts with more “passive” unsupervised or model-based objectives. Rather than learning potentially irrelevant input representations or attempting full environment modeling—which may be infeasible under partial observability—the method focuses on controllability of local and internal features, thereby complementing the primary control objective. I find this idea novel and a compelling alternative to conventional unsupervised learning, leveraging the agent’s capacity to influence the environment.

The presentation is generally clear and accessible to readers familiar with deep RL. However, some components are described at a high level. More concrete details would improve reproducibility and comprehension—for instance, explicitly defining the pixel-control loss (L_PC) in the main text rather than relegating it to the appendix.

The experimental methodology is sound. While the optimal hyperparameter distributions may differ between A3C and UNREAL, the use of top-3 performance across hyperparameter settings helps mitigate selection bias. Assuming the explored ranges contain near-optimal settings for each method, this procedure should surface strong per-method configurations.

A key limitation, and a promising direction for future work, is the limited analysis of how each auxiliary task contributes beyond headline performance. The pixel/feature control objectives appear to drive most of the gains; for example, in Labyrinth, A3C with pixel control alone outperforms all alternatives except UNREAL. A deeper ablation—isolating each auxiliary task, examining their interactions, and measuring effects on learned representations (not just downstream RL returns)—would strengthen the paper. Additional diagnostics (e.g., feature diversity, representation stability across policies, data efficiency per task) could clarify why and when each auxiliary objective helps.

Overall, this is a well-executed and innovative contribution that demonstrates substantial practical benefits. With more detailed task definitions and a richer analysis of auxiliary-task impacts, the work would be even stronger.
