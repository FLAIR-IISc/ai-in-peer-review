1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper augments a deep RL agent (A3C) with unsupervised auxiliary tasks to shape its representation and accelerate learning. The auxiliary control tasks optimize policies for pseudo-rewards (e.g., pixel- and feature-control), and auxiliary reward tasks focus representation on extrinsic rewards (e.g., immediate reward prediction). These tasks are trained off-policy via replay alongside on-policy A3C updates, and share a common representation with the main policy. The resulting UNREAL agent is evaluated on Labyrinth and Atari, where it substantially outperforms a strong A3C baseline and learns much faster. Overall, the paper is clearly written and should be accessible to readers familiar with deep RL.

2. Decision
Accept. Primary reasons:
- Strong empirical gains over A3C, including faster learning, across both Labyrinth and Atari.
- A compelling and well-executed proof of concept demonstrating that appropriately chosen auxiliary tasks can serve as effective representation shaping for RL.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: How to learn robust, useful representations in environments with sparse or delayed extrinsic rewards by leveraging additional training signals available in the sensorimotor stream. The paper proposes to use multiple pseudo-reward maximization tasks and immediate reward prediction as auxiliary objectives while training a single shared representation.
- Motivation and relation to prior work: The idea that auxiliary tasks can aid representation learning is well motivated and grounded in literature on options/pseudo-rewards (e.g., options, Horde, UVFAs, successor representations) and auxiliary prediction. The paper positions its contribution as using these tasks not for their policies per se, but to shape the shared features that the main policy uses. While the approach is conceptually intuitive rather than surprising, the instantiation is thoughtful: off-policy Q-learning for control tasks, replay and skewed sampling for reward prediction, and shared CNN/LSTM features.
- Empirical support and rigor: The experiments compare to A3C and ablations in Labyrinth and Atari. Results show substantial improvements in final performance and data efficiency, as well as increased robustness to hyperparameters. The ablation results (e.g., pixel control vs. reconstruction vs. input-change prediction) support the claim that learning to control is more beneficial than merely predicting or reconstructing inputs. The evaluation covers many levels/games with multiple seeds/hyperparameters, which strengthens the empirical case. As stated in my summary, this is a solid demonstration, even if the main idea is not surprising: relevant auxiliary tasks should help representation learning.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (not central to the accept decision)
- Reproducibility and resources: The overall architecture combines several components (A3C with LSTM, control heads for multiple tasks, experience replay with skewed sampling, value replay, reward prediction). To aid reproducibility, please detail computational resources (e.g., number/type of GPUs/CPUs, wall-clock training time for Labyrinth and Atari) and any engineering choices necessary to reach the reported results.
- Open-sourcing: Given the complexity of the full system, releasing code, or at least pseudo-code and reference implementations of the auxiliary heads and replay pipelines, would significantly benefit the community.
- Clarity: A concise training loop diagram (what is updated on-policy vs. off-policy, cadence for auxiliary updates, how the replay buffers are partitioned and sampled) would further improve readability for practitioners.

Questions for the authors
- What computational resources (hardware, number of actors/threads, training time per environment) are required to train the full UNREAL agent on Labyrinth and Atari to the reported performance?
- Given the overall complexity of the system, are you willing to release source code or a reference implementation?
