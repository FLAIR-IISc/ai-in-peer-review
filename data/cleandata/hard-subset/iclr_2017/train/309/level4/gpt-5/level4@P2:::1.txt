1. Summary of claimed contributions
The paper augments an A3C-style deep RL agent with unsupervised auxiliary tasks to accelerate representation learning and improve performance. Two families of auxiliary objectives are introduced: (a) auxiliary control tasks that learn policies to maximise pseudo-rewards (e.g., pixel- or feature-control via off-policy n-step Q-learning using replay), and (b) reward-focused tasks that predict immediate rewards and perform value-function replay to combat reward sparsity and stabilise learning. The combined UNREAL agent jointly optimises the main A3C loss with these auxiliary losses. Experiments on 13 3D Labyrinth levels and 57 Atari games show substantial gains in both final performance and data efficiency, along with improved robustness to hyperparameters. The paper is clearly written and situates the approach within related work on options, UVFAs, successor representations, and auxiliary predictions.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong and consistent empirical improvements over strong baselines across diverse domains, with clear gains in data efficiency and robustness.
- A practical, well-motivated integration of auxiliary control and reward-prediction tasks with A3C, demonstrating a scalable recipe applicable to visually rich environments.

3. Supporting arguments
Strengths:
- Empirical rigor: Results cover both 3D Labyrinth and Atari-57, with ablations (e.g., removing pixel control), comparisons to reconstruction and immediate-change prediction, and robustness analyses across many hyperparameters.
- Data efficiency: Large speedups in Labyrinth (mean ~10× to reach A3C’s best) are compelling for real-world training regimes.
- Conceptual coherence: Treating controllable features of the sensorimotor stream as pseudo-rewards provides an intuitive bridge between unsupervised representation learning and control. Reward prediction and value replay directly address reward sparsity without biasing the policy gradient updates.
- Engineering clarity: The paper specifies architectural choices (CNN-LSTM body, deconvolutional heads for spatial control, replay buffer partitioning) and optimization details, which aids reproducibility.

Weaknesses / limitations:
- Novelty is incremental: The core idea—that relevant auxiliary tasks can shape better features and speed learning—is expected. The main contribution is the concrete instantiation and thorough empirical validation.
- Architectural complexity: The full stack (A3C + multiple off-policy control heads + reward prediction + value replay + prioritised sampling) is intricate; understanding the minimal effective subset could help practitioners.
- Compute and scalability details are under-specified: Training cost, wall-clock time, hardware (CPU/GPU count), and frame throughput are not clearly reported, making it difficult to assess practicality for typical research labs.
- Sensitivity to auxiliary-task weighting: While robustness to some hyperparameters is shown, the effect of the auxiliary loss weights (especially λPC) on training stability and final performance could be clearer.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not central to the decision)
- Clarify the interplay between on-policy A3C updates and off-policy Q-learning heads. A brief discussion of stability considerations and why this combination works well in practice would be valuable.
- Provide a more systematic ablation isolating each component (pixel control, feature control, reward prediction, value replay) across multiple levels to quantify marginal contributions.
- Report compute: GPUs/CPUs used, training wall-clock to reach specified scores, frames per second processed, and memory footprints for replay and models.
- Discuss generality: Comment on applicability beyond pixel-based tasks (e.g., proprioceptive inputs, language-conditioned goals) and whether feature-control substitutes for pixel-control in non-visual domains.
- Hyperparameter fairness: Ensure comparable hyperparameter search budgets for baselines and UNREAL; briefly summarise the search spaces and selection criteria in the main text.
- Release code and trained models if possible; given architectural complexity, this would greatly aid adoption and reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- Compute and efficiency: What hardware (number/type of GPUs/CPUs) and wall-clock time were required to train UNREAL on Labyrinth and Atari to the reported performance? What was the frame throughput and typical training duration per game/level?
- Sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the auxiliary loss weights (λPC, λRP, λVR)? Do you observe instability or performance degradation outside the reported ranges?
- Minimal subset: Which subset of auxiliary tasks yields most of the gains across domains? For practitioners with limited compute, which components would you recommend prioritising?
- Off-policy interactions: Did you observe any adverse interference between off-policy Q-learning updates and on-policy A3C learning? Were target networks or additional stabilisation tricks needed?
- Feature vs pixel control: On which tasks does feature control match or exceed pixel control? Can feature control replace pixel control to reduce dependence on domain-specific pixel-change heuristics?
- Generalisation and transfer: Does the shared representation learned via auxiliary control transfer to new tasks or reward functions in the same environment?
- Code release: Are you planning to release the implementation and training scripts to facilitate replication?

Overall, this paper presents a compelling and practical recipe for leveraging auxiliary tasks in deep RL, with strong empirical evidence across challenging benchmarks.
