1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper introduces an RL agent that jointly learns the main task while optimizing multiple auxiliary objectives designed as pseudo-reward control tasks and reward-focused prediction. Concretely, it proposes (i) auxiliary control over pixel-intensity changes and over the activations of latent features, trained via off-policy n-step Q-learning from a shared representation; and (ii) a reward-prediction task trained on sequences with replay that is intentionally skewed to balance rewarding vs. non-rewarding events, shaping features without biasing the policy/value learning. The approach leverages experience replay to make auxiliary updates efficient and robust, and contrasts with “passive” unsupervised or model-based objectives by directly learning to control aspects of the sensorimotor stream that are more aligned with downstream goals. Empirically, the agent achieves strong gains on discrete-action, continuous-state benchmarks (e.g., 3D Labyrinth and Atari) and reaches baseline performance in roughly 10× fewer iterations, indicating large improvements in data efficiency.

2. Decision
Accept. The method offers a compelling, well-motivated alternative to conventional unsupervised/model-based auxiliary learning, and the empirical results show large, consistent improvements in performance and data efficiency.

3. Supporting arguments
- Novelty and motivation: Framing auxiliary tasks around controllable pseudo-rewards (pixel/feature control) is a clear conceptual step beyond reconstruction or short-horizon prediction. It aligns representation learning with what the agent can influence, which is particularly appealing under partial observability.
- Methodological soundness: Using off-policy updates from a single stream of experience for many auxiliary tasks is technically sensible, and the replay-based rebalancing for reward prediction is carefully designed not to bias the policy/value updates. The fairness of the evaluation is helped by comparing top-performing hyperparameter settings per method, which mitigates confounds due to hyperparameter differences (assuming both methods’ best settings lie in the explored ranges).
- Empirical strength: The approach consistently improves speed and final performance on challenging 3D tasks and Atari, with a roughly 10× reduction in steps to reach the baseline’s best score. Pixel/feature control appears to be especially impactful; in Labyrinth, A3C+pixel-control already outperforms most variants short of the full agent, underscoring the value of the control-based auxiliaries.
- Clarity: The high-level exposition is clear and intuitive for readers versed in deep RL.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not necessarily part of the decision)
- Provide more low-level detail in the main text for reproducibility and clarity (e.g., define the auxiliary control loss L_PC and targets explicitly before the appendix, with enough description to reimplement without cross-referencing).
- Deepen the analysis of auxiliary-task effects beyond final performance. For instance:
  - Isolate and probe pixel vs. feature control in more settings; report their individual and joint contributions.
  - Include diagnostics that illuminate mechanisms (e.g., how auxiliary losses evolve relative to returns; representation similarity analyses; saliency/attention on reward-relevant cues; success/failure modes).
  - Explore sensitivity to key hyperparameters (λ_PC, λ_RP, replay buffer size, skewing ratio) and report how robust the gains are across ranges.
- Given that pixel/feature control seems to drive much of the benefit in Labyrinth (A3C+PC is very strong), consider a more detailed discussion of why this particular auxiliary is so effective (e.g., does it systematically induce behaviors that expose reward, improve exploration, or stabilize credit assignment?).
- Clarify the definition of “iterations/steps” used for reporting data efficiency (environment frames vs. learner updates across asynchronous actors), so comparisons are unambiguous.

Questions for the authors
- Auxiliary control objective: Could you define L_PC precisely in the main text (target values, bootstrapping, dueling head details, and how the per-cell pixel-change rewards are normalized)? How were grid size and cropping chosen, and how sensitive are results to these choices?
- Reward prediction skewing: Can you provide empirical evidence that rebalancing replay for reward prediction does not bias the learned policy/value (e.g., an ablation with/without rebalancing showing similar returns but different predictor accuracy)?
- Sensitivity and robustness: How sensitive are results to λ_PC and λ_RP, and to replay buffer size? Does value-function replay alone (without pixel/feature control) account for a meaningful portion of the gains?
- Use of auxiliary policies: Are the learned pseudo-control policies ever executed for exploration, or are they exclusively training signals? If only auxiliary, have you tested whether occasionally following them improves exploration or sample efficiency further?
- Attribution of gains: In Labyrinth, A3C+pixel-control nearly matches or surpasses many ablations. Can you share qualitative behavioral differences (e.g., path coverage, object interaction) that might explain its outsized impact?
- Data efficiency metric: The paper notes a ~10× reduction in iterations to reach baseline performance. Does this refer to environment interaction steps (aggregated across all asynchronous workers) or to learner updates, and how are these normalized across methods?
