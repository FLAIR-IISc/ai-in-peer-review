This work proposes enhancing feature learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the main policy optimization with domain-agnostic auxiliary objectives. The auxiliary tasks include control (policies that aim to maximally change aspects of the state, instantiated here as pixel-control and feature-control), immediate reward prediction, and value-function replay. Except for the latter, these objectives are used solely to shape the shared CNN+LSTM representation. Empirical results on Atari and DeepMind Lab show strong gains, especially in data efficiency relative to A3C.

The paper is clearly written, the ideas are well grounded, and the evidence is compelling; I recommend acceptance. My main comments are minor and do not affect the overall assessment:
- Please quantify the additional computational overhead from training auxiliary tasks. How much wall-clock slowdown is incurred, and which components dominate the cost?
- Consider clarifying in the abstract/introduction that the agent learns distinct policies for each auxiliary control task. The phrase “maximises many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously” initially suggested a single policy jointly optimizing multiple rewards until Eq. 1 made the intent clear.
- The “feature control” concept is intriguing but under-validated. The preliminary result in Fig. 5 shows only a slight early boost. Also, because the controlled features evolve during training, the target itself is nonstationary; this may raise stability/convergence concerns.
- Since you note that “the performance of our agents is still steadily improving,” why not run the best agents longer to characterize asymptotic performance?
- Why aren’t the auxiliary loss weights (the λ_*) treated as tunable hyperparameters? Were experiments run to justify fixing them to 1?
- Please note that the auxiliary control updates are not “true” Q-learning: they are off-policy with multi-step targets, which matters for technical accuracy (as discussed in the OpenReview comments).
