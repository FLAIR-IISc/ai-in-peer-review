This paper proposes improving representation learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the main policy optimization with domain-agnostic auxiliary tasks. These include control tasks (learning additional policies that attempt to maximally change the input pixels), immediate reward prediction, and value function replay. Except for value replay, the auxiliary tasks are used primarily to shape the shared CNN+LSTM features. Experiments on Atari and Labyrinth show clear gains in data efficiency over A3C. The paper is well written, the ideas are sound, and the results are convincing; I recommend acceptance.

High-level comments:
- Computational overhead: Please quantify the extra training cost introduced by the auxiliary tasks. How much wall-clock speed do you lose relative to the base agent? Which components dominate the cost (e.g., control heads, replayed updates, reward prediction)?
- Clarify in the abstract/introduction that the agent learns separate policies for the auxiliary control tasks. The phrase “maximises many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously” can be read as a single policy jointly optimizing all rewards; this only became clear to me at Eq. 1.
- The “feature control” idea is intriguing but not convincingly validated. Figure 5 suggests only a small initial benefit. Moreover, because the features evolve during training, the auxiliary task’s target itself is non-stationary; please discuss potential stability/convergence implications and, if possible, include stronger evidence.
- Since some agents were still improving, why not continue training the best ones longer to show asymptotic performance?
- Loss-weighting: Why fix all auxiliary-task weights λ_* to 1? Were sensitivity studies or simple hyperparameter sweeps performed to justify this choice? If not, could you report preliminary results or rationale?
- Please explicitly note that the auxiliary control tasks are trained off-policy with multi-step returns and therefore do not correspond to “true” Q-learning in the standard sense (as also discussed in the comments).
