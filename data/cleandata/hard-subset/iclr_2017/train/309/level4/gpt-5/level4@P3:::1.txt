Summary and contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an augmented deep RL agent that combines standard A3C with auxiliary tasks to shape shared representations. Two classes of auxiliary objectives are introduced: (i) auxiliary control tasks that learn policies to maximize pseudo-rewards (notably “pixel control” over a spatial grid and “feature control” over internal activations), trained off-policy via n-step Q-learning from replay; and (ii) auxiliary reward-focused tasks, primarily immediate reward prediction trained on a skewed replay distribution to de-sparsify reward signals, plus value-function replay to accelerate value learning. All tasks share the same convolutional and recurrent representation, allowing learning to continue in the absence of extrinsic reward and to focus features on reward-relevant aspects when rewards occur. Experiments on 13 Labyrinth levels and 57 Atari games show substantial gains in final performance, learning speed, and robustness to hyperparameters. Ablations indicate that learning to control aspects of the sensorimotor stream outperforms simple prediction or reconstruction.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong and consistent empirical improvements in performance and data efficiency across diverse domains, backed by ablations and robustness analyses.
- Well-motivated and carefully integrated auxiliary objectives that advance representation learning for RL, addressing sparse rewards without biasing the policy/value updates.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles the question of what to learn when extrinsic rewards are sparse and how to extract richer training signals from the sensorimotor stream. The approach is well positioned relative to Horde, UVFAs, successor representations, and model-based auxiliary prediction: rather than using pseudo-reward policies directly for planning/control, they are used to shape a shared representation that benefits the main task. This is a clear and meaningful distinction.
- Methodology: The combination of on-policy A3C for the base task with off-policy Q-learning for auxiliary control, together with replay (including rewarded/non-rewarded stratification for reward prediction), is technically sound and practically sensible. The architectural choices (shared CNN/LSTM, deconvolutional heads for spatial control, feedforward head for immediate reward prediction) are clearly described.
- Empirical support: Results on Labyrinth show an average 10× speedup to match A3C’s best performance and a large gain in human-normalized score (to ~87%). Atari results report substantial improvements over A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN, including increased robustness to hyperparameters. Ablation studies suggest a hierarchy of usefulness: control > prediction > reconstruction, which directly supports the central claim that control-oriented auxiliary tasks produce more useful features than purely predictive ones.
- Rigor and clarity: The paper includes multiple baselines, ablations, and hyperparameter sweeps. The replay and training schedules, as well as auxiliary loss weights, are documented. The narrative is clear and accessible to readers familiar with deep RL.

Caveats
- Novelty is incremental conceptually: using auxiliary tasks to shape representations is not surprising, and the method integrates known components (A3C, Q-learning, replay). The contribution lies in a well-engineered, empirically validated instantiation that scales to challenging 3D and Atari domains.
- Complexity: The full system has many moving parts (multiple auxiliary heads, replay buffers, loss weights), which may hinder reproducibility and make it hard to attribute gains to specific choices beyond the provided ablations.
- Metrics: Human-normalized means can be skewed by outliers; median and per-game breakdowns mitigate this but clearer reporting of wall-clock time and compute would strengthen the claims of data efficiency.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not central to the accept decision)
- Report compute budgets and efficiency: number/type of CPUs/GPUs, total training frames per domain, wall-clock time, and the overhead of auxiliary heads. Plot time-to-threshold in wall-clock, not just environment steps.
- Reproducibility: Consider releasing code and training scripts; provide a minimal reference implementation and detailed hyperparameter settings (including λ weights and auxiliary architectures) per domain.
- Sensitivity analyses: Include small sweeps for the pixel-control grid size, replay buffer size, and λPC/λRP/λVR to guide practitioners. Report how much each auxiliary task increases training time.
- Clarify evaluation protocols on Atari (e.g., number of evaluation episodes, life-loss handling, no-op starts) and Labyrinth (episode lengths, termination conditions).
- Transfer and generalization: Add a transfer experiment to a new reward function or unseen Labyrinth maps to test whether the learned representation accelerates learning beyond the training distribution.
- Stability: Discuss any observed instability from mixing on-policy (A3C) and off-policy (Q-learning) updates and how you mitigated it (e.g., target networks, replay freshness).
- Analysis: Expand qualitative/quantitative probes of the learned representation (e.g., saliency maps, feature control visualizations) to connect auxiliary tasks to improved downstream value/policy learning.
- Editing: Clean up reference formatting and minor typos.

Questions for the authors
- Compute and runtime: What hardware (CPUs/GPUs) and wall-clock time were required per domain? What is the training-time overhead of auxiliary tasks relative to the A3C baseline?
- Sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the pixel-control grid size and to λPC/λRP/λVR? Can you provide a brief sweep or guidance for setting these?
- Off-policy effects: Did you observe any instability from off-policy Q-learning with replay alongside on-policy A3C? How fresh is replayed data, and do target networks θ− materially affect stability?
- Reward prediction bias: Given the rebalanced replay for reward prediction, did you verify that this did not bias the policy/value learning (e.g., via changes in the state visitation distribution)? Any ablation where the reward-prediction CNN is not shared?
- Feature control: How do feature-control tasks perform on Atari when combined with pixel control? Could feature control replace pixel control to reduce reliance on low-level signals?
- Exploration: Are auxiliary control policies ever used to influence behavior (e.g., as exploratory options), or are they strictly used for representation learning? If not used for exploration, have you tried it?
- Memory footprint: What is the memory cost of maintaining replay buffers and auxiliary heads? Does this limit scalability to higher-resolution inputs?

Post-rebuttal: No change in the review.
