Summary of contributions
The paper proposes augmenting reinforcement learning agents with auxiliary tasks to learn stronger, more useful representations. In addition to the main policy/value learning, the agent learns:
- Auxiliary control tasks: (i) control of pixel-intensity changes over an n×n grid (pixel control), and (ii) control of internal network activations (feature control). These are optimized off-policy with n-step Q-learning from a shared representation.
- An auxiliary supervised task: prediction of immediate reward given a short sequence of observations. This predictor is trained from an experience replay buffer with a skewed sampling distribution to balance positive/negative/zero reward events, improving feature learning without biasing the main policy/value updates.
Empirically, the approach yields substantial gains in visually rich, discrete-action environments (Labyrinth and Atari), achieving comparable or better performance with roughly 10× fewer iterations. Conceptually, this contrasts with “passive” unsupervised or model-based objectives: instead of reconstructing inputs or learning possibly intractable models under partial observability, the agent learns to control local and internal features, which appears to complement learning the optimal control policy.

Decision: Accept
Main reasons: (1) Strong empirical results with large gains in both data efficiency and robustness over strong baselines; (2) A well-motivated, novel use of control-based auxiliary objectives that are better aligned with downstream RL goals than standard reconstruction/modeling tasks.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and motivation: The idea of using many pseudo-reward control problems to shape shared representations is compelling and addresses a key limitation of standard unsupervised learning in RL: reconstruction can emphasize irrelevant visual details, whereas learning to predict and control aspects of the sensorimotor stream is closer to the agent’s goals. The reward-prediction task is also carefully designed to avoid biasing the policy/value (trained only to shape features with skewed sampling).
- Methodological soundness: Although the optimal hyperparameters for A3C and UNREAL may differ, evaluating the top-k (e.g., top-3) configurations per method helps ensure each method’s best settings are represented, assuming the explored ranges include good configurations. Off-policy n-step Q-learning for auxiliary control tasks, combined with shared CNN/LSTM features and on-policy A3C for the main task, is technically coherent and leverages replay to improve sample efficiency.
- Empirical strength: The reported 10× speedup to baseline performance and strong final performance across Labyrinth and Atari are persuasive. The ablations suggest auxiliary control, especially pixel/feature control, provides much of the benefit; UNREAL’s combination delivers the best overall results.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not essential to my decision)
- Provide more lower-level details in the main text. In particular, define the auxiliary control loss (e.g., LPC), including the precise pseudo-reward definitions, n-step targets, and any target-network details, without requiring readers to consult the appendix.
- Deepen the experimental analysis of the auxiliary tasks beyond end-task performance. For example:
  - Analyze representational changes: do auxiliary tasks improve linear separability of reward-relevant states or value prediction accuracy?
  - Quantify per-task contributions with more granular ablations across levels (e.g., pixel control alone vs feature control alone vs both), especially given that A3C+pixel control nearly matches UNREAL on Labyrinth.
  - Compare control-vs-prediction objectives directly: the Labyrinth baselines suggest controlling pixel changes outperforms predicting them, and both outperform reconstruction. A broader analysis would strengthen the claim that control objectives are better aligned with RL.
- Clarify how the skewed sampling for reward prediction interacts with learning stability and whether similar skewing of other tasks was explored.
- Consider moving key implementation choices (e.g., replay buffer size, sampling strategy, update frequencies) to the main text since they are central to the agent’s performance.

Questions for the authors
- Loss definitions and targets: Could you state the full form of the auxiliary control loss LPC in the main text, including the pseudo-reward for pixel control and feature control, the n-step target used, and any target-network update scheme?
- Sensitivity to reward prediction skew: How sensitive are results to the 50/50 reward/non-reward sampling ratio? Does performance degrade in dense-reward settings, and what happens if the skew is reduced or increased?
- Pixel vs feature control: On Labyrinth, A3C+pixel control is very strong. Do you have results for feature control alone and for the combination on more tasks? Any insight into when feature control helps most?
- Beyond performance: Did you evaluate intermediate metrics (e.g., value loss, reward-prediction accuracy, exploration behavior) to shed light on why pixel/feature control yields such gains?
- Generality: How do these auxiliary tasks perform in domains with different visual statistics or action spaces? For example, are there cases where pixel control is less effective and feature control is preferable?

Overall, the paper presents a well-motivated and practically impactful approach with strong empirical evidence. The work would be even stronger with more detail on the auxiliary losses and a deeper analysis of why control-based auxiliary tasks help, beyond their effect on final performance.
