This paper proposes training RL agents on auxiliary tasks to encourage learning stronger features. The authors introduce two pseudo-control objectives: controlling changes in pixel intensities and controlling activations of latent features. They also add a supervised regression head that predicts immediate reward following a sequence of events. This reward-prediction head is trained offline using a skewed sampling strategy from the experience replay buffer to balance rewarding and non-rewarding samples at roughly 50/50.

Empirically, the resulting agents perform very well on discrete-action, continuous-state RL tasks, achieving baseline performance in roughly ten times fewer iterations. The approach contrasts with traditional “passive” unsupervised or model-based learning: rather than forcing the agent to learn potentially unhelpful input representations or to model dynamics that may be impossible to infer under partial observability, the agent learns to control local and internal features of the environment in a way that complements learning the optimal control policy.

I find the approach novel and a compelling alternative to unsupervised representation learning that explicitly exploits the agent’s ability to control aspects of its environment.

The exposition of the auxiliary tasks is mostly at a high level, which makes the intuition clear, but some lower-level details would improve clarity. For example, the pixel-control loss L_PC should be defined explicitly in the main text, rather than leaving this to the appendix. Otherwise, the paper is clearly written and should be readily understandable to readers familiar with deep RL.

The methodology appears sound. While the distributions of optimal hyperparameters may differ between A3C and UNREAL, reporting top-3 performance helps ensure that, assuming the best configurations for each method are within the explored ranges, the per-method optima are identified.

A notable weakness (or, given space constraints, an important direction for future work) is the limited experimental analysis of how the auxiliary tasks affect learning beyond their clear impact on overall performance. In particular, pixel/feature control seems to drive most of the gains; on Labyrinth, A3C with pixel control alone outperforms everything except UNREAL. It would be valuable to analyze this more deeply—either in isolation or with more ablations—and to measure effects beyond final RL performance (e.g., learning speed, representation quality, stability), to better understand why and when each auxiliary task helps.
