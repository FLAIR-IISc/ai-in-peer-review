Summary:
The paper proposes augmenting reinforcement learning (RL) with auxiliary tasks to help agents learn stronger, more useful representations. In addition to the main control objective, the agent learns two pseudo-control tasks—modulating pixel-intensity changes and modulating activations of latent features—and a reward-prediction task trained from replay with a sampling scheme that balances reward and non-reward events. Empirically, this approach significantly accelerates learning on discrete-action, continuous-state environments, achieving baseline performance in roughly an order of magnitude fewer iterations. Conceptually, the work contrasts with “passive” unsupervised or model-based objectives by leveraging the agent’s ability to act: learning to control local and internal features complements learning the optimal policy.

Strengths:
- Clear and compelling idea: use auxiliary control and reward-prediction tasks to shape representation learning for RL, exploiting the agent’s control rather than unsupervised reconstruction or potentially brittle model learning under partial observability.
- Strong empirical gains: the method greatly improves sample efficiency and final performance, particularly in challenging environments such as Labyrinth.
- Sensible engineering: the replay-balancing strategy for reward prediction addresses class imbalance and appears to stabilize learning.
- Methodology is generally sound: hyperparameter selection is handled fairly via top-k evaluation across reasonable search ranges, mitigating the risk that improvements stem from cherry-picked settings.
- The paper is generally well written and accessible to readers familiar with deep RL; the high-level motivation is well articulated.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Insufficient objective-level detail: Key components, such as the exact loss for pixel control (e.g., L_PC), are deferred to the appendix. Bringing explicit loss definitions, normalizations, discounting choices, and weighting coefficients into the main text would improve reproducibility and enable more precise comparison to baselines.
- Limited analysis of auxiliary-task contributions: While performance gains are clear, the paper offers little insight into why particular auxiliary tasks help. The results suggest pixel/feature control contributes substantially (e.g., A3C+PC performing strongly, in some Labyrinth cases approaching the full method), but a more systematic ablation would strengthen the claims. Suggestions:
  - Isolate each auxiliary task (pixel control, feature control, reward prediction) and report effects on learning curves, final returns, stability, and variance.
  - Vary the weights of auxiliary losses and the proportion of updates spent on each task to assess sensitivity.
  - Evaluate representational benefits beyond control performance (e.g., linear probes on features, transfer to related tasks, sample efficiency on downstream tasks).
- Replay balancing details and impact: The reward-prediction task uses skewed sampling to achieve ~50% reward occurrence. Please clarify:
  - The exact sampling mechanism, buffer segmentation, and any bias correction.
  - Whether the prediction target is binary/ternary or real-valued, and the effect of each choice.
  - Ablations without balancing, or with different reward-to-non-reward ratios, to understand robustness and the role of this design choice.
- Scope and generality: The paper focuses on discrete-action, continuous-state domains. Discussion or preliminary evidence for applicability to continuous-action control (e.g., MuJoCo) would broaden impact. Similarly, given the motivation around partial observability, an explicit POMDP benchmark (requiring memory) would be informative.
- Computational overhead: Pixel/feature control updates and replay-based training incur additional cost. Reporting wall-clock speedups/slowdowns and total environment frames to a fixed threshold would contextualize the “10x fewer iterations” claim in practical terms.

Clarity and presentation:
- The high-level exposition is strong and intuitive. Elevating crucial definitions (e.g., pixel-control objective, architecture choices for feature control, and reward-prediction target) from the appendix to the main body would markedly improve clarity.
- A brief schematic showing how auxiliary losses integrate with the policy/value network, and how update schedules interleave (on-policy vs. off-policy), would help readers implement the method correctly.

Methodological soundness:
- The empirical methodology is largely sound. Using a top-k evaluation across a pre-defined hyperparameter grid is a reasonable way to compare A3C and the proposed method, provided both search spaces are similar in breadth and anchored around community norms. Please confirm that the explored ranges cover typical settings for each algorithm.
- The contrast to passive unsupervised or model-based learning is persuasive, but it would be helpful to include at least one competitive unsupervised baseline (e.g., reconstruction-based pretraining) to disentangle the benefits of “control-based” auxiliary tasks from generic representation learning.

Questions for the authors:
- How sensitive are the results to the spatial/temporal resolution of pixel control (e.g., grid size, frame differencing window) and to the choice of latent features for feature control?
- Do auxiliary tasks ever interfere with the main objective (e.g., by incentivizing local changes that are myopic relative to long-horizon goals)? If so, how is this mitigated?
- Could intrinsic rewards derived from control success replace or complement the auxiliary losses, and with what trade-offs?

Overall assessment:
This is a strong, well-motivated paper with clear empirical benefits and a compelling perspective on leveraging controllability for representation learning. The idea is simple, practical, and appears robust, with impressive gains in sample efficiency. The main shortcoming is the limited ablation and mechanistic analysis of the auxiliary tasks themselves; adding objective-level details and deeper ablations would substantially strengthen the paper. Despite these caveats, the work represents a valuable contribution likely to influence how auxiliary objectives are used in deep RL. I recommend acceptance, with the above clarifications and analyses considered for the final version.
