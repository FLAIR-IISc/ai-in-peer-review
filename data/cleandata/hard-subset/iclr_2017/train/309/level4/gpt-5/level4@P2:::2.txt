1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, a deep RL agent that augments A3C with domain-agnostic auxiliary tasks to learn stronger representations, especially when extrinsic rewards are sparse. The agent jointly optimizes: (i) off-policy auxiliary control tasks via n-step Q-learning (e.g., pixel control, and a proposed feature control), (ii) immediate reward prediction with replay rebalancing to reduce perceptual sparsity of reward, and (iii) value-function replay to improve stability and data efficiency. All tasks share a CNN+LSTM encoder with the main policy; most auxiliary heads are used solely to shape features, not to act. UNREAL substantially improves data efficiency, robustness to hyperparameters, and final performance on 13 Labyrinth levels (mean ~10× speedup; ~87% human-normalized) and across 57 Atari games (mean ~880% human performance), surpassing strong baselines.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) The approach is well-motivated and well-situated in the literature, with a clear contribution: using auxiliary control and reward-focused prediction strictly for representation learning within a unified off-policy/on-policy framework. (2) The empirical gains are large, consistent, and carefully evaluated, demonstrating strong data efficiency and robustness improvements over A3C.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The work targets the central challenge of learning useful features in sparse-reward, high-dimensional settings. Framing diverse pseudo-rewards as auxiliary control tasks more closely aligns representation learning with downstream control than standard reconstruction objectives.
- Positioning: The method is clearly contrasted with options, Horde, UVFA, successor representations, and model-based auxiliary predictions. The novelty lies in training many off-policy control heads purely as auxiliary objectives that shape a shared representation.
- Claims and evidence: Results in both 3D Labyrinth and Atari are compelling, with ablations in Labyrinth showing that control-based auxiliaries outperform pixel reconstruction and immediate-change prediction. Robustness analyses over hyperparameters and seeds are a strength. While feature control is only preliminarily evaluated, the core claims (UNREAL > A3C; data efficiency; robustness) are well supported.

4. Feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all affect my accept decision)
- Computational overhead
  • Please report wall-clock training time relative to A3C and break down the cost of each auxiliary component (pixel/feature control, reward prediction, value replay). Include hardware details and throughput (frames/sec).

- Clarify that multiple policies are learned
  • In the abstract and introduction, explicitly state that the agent learns separate policies/heads for each auxiliary control task (not a single policy optimizing all pseudo-rewards jointly). Eq. (1) makes this clear but the abstract could prevent misinterpretation.

- Feature control stability and evidence
  • The idea is interesting but currently weakly supported. Because the controlled features change during learning, please discuss potential non-stationarity and any stabilization strategies (e.g., target networks, stop-gradient, delayed updates). Consider adding more empirical results or moving it to an appendix as exploratory.

- Training horizon
  • Since several learning curves were still improving, consider extending training for the best agents to show asymptotic performance.

- Loss weights as hyperparameters
  • Please justify fixing λ terms (λVR, λPC, λRP) rather than tuning them. Provide sensitivity analysis or a rationale for using 1 (and specify the actual λPC used, which appears to differ between domains). Summarize the effect of these weights on stability and performance.

- Off-policy n-step Q-learning caveat
  • Clarify that auxiliary Q-learning uses off-policy n-step returns and is not “true” one-step Q-learning; briefly discuss potential bias and why it worked well in practice.

- Ablations on Atari
  • Include per-component ablations (e.g., remove pixel control, remove reward prediction) to test whether the same patterns seen in Labyrinth hold on Atari.

- Editorial and minor corrections
  • Policy gradient section: Lπ is a loss to be minimized.
  • Eq. (1): λC should be inside the sum; use r(c)t+k in R(c)t:t+n for consistency.
  • Define LPC in the text and include it explicitly in Eq. (2) (or align notation).
  • Fix subject–verb agreement: “the features … are shared.”
  • Reference Figure 2 in the main text; also reference Figure 1(d) in Section 3.3.
  • Figure 3: explain “Clip” in the legend; clarify that the listed percentages are for Labyrinth; fix references (“Figure 3 (top left)” rather than “right”; “Figure 3 top right” where applicable).
  • Figure 5 appears to show two levels on the left/middle; please reconcile with the text claiming three levels.
  • Section 4.2: add punctuation after “methods”.
  • Appendix: specify where supplementary materials can be accessed.
  • State the value/range used for λPC.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the pixel-control grid resolution and the 80×80 crop? Did you normalize pixel-change rewards across games with different intensity dynamics?
- For reward prediction, does balancing zero/non-zero classes suffice when rewards vary in magnitude or sign? Any observed representation bias or negative transfer due to rebalanced replay?
- Did you observe cases where auxiliary tasks slowed learning or distracted from extrinsic rewards? Any safeguards (e.g., annealing λPC/λRP)?
- What are the exact environment-frame counts corresponding to reported steps (given action repeat of 4)? Please report frames and steps for comparability.
- Can you share wall-clock training times per domain and the relative cost per auxiliary task?
