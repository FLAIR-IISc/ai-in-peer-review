Summary and contributions
The paper introduces UNREAL, a deep RL architecture that augments a standard A3C agent with auxiliary tasks designed to shape the learned representation and accelerate learning. The key ideas are:
- Auxiliary control tasks: learn value functions and policies for pseudo-reward signals computed from the agent’s own sensorimotor stream. Two instantiations are studied: (i) pixel control (maximizing local pixel-intensity changes in an n×n grid) and (ii) feature control (maximizing the activation of internal network features).
- Reward-focused auxiliary tasks: immediate reward prediction trained with skewed replay that balances reward vs. no-reward cases, plus value replay updates, to reduce perceptual sparsity of reward and improve value estimation.
- Joint training: on-policy A3C for the base task combined with off-policy n-step Q-learning for auxiliary control tasks via a shared CNN-LSTM representation.

Empirically, UNREAL achieves substantial gains: on Labyrinth, a mean 10× speedup to reach A3C’s best performance and 87% human-normalized performance; on Atari-57, 880% mean and 250% median human-normalized performance, with improved robustness to hyperparameters. Ablations indicate that each auxiliary component helps, with pixel/feature control particularly impactful.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Strong, well-motivated idea that reframes “unsupervised RL” as learning to control aspects of the sensorimotor stream, yielding better representations than passive prediction/reconstruction.
- Compelling, broad empirical gains across two challenging domains with careful ablations and robustness analyses.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets the scarcity of extrinsic rewards and the need for representation learning that aligns with control. Rather than reconstructing inputs or learning full environment models, the agent learns to predict and control perceptually and internally meaningful signals. This is well positioned relative to Horde/UVFA/successor representations (learning many value functions) and model-based or predictive auxiliary tasks; here, auxiliary policies are used for representation shaping rather than direct behavior, which is a clear and novel design choice.
- Methodology: The combined loss and training pipeline are sensible: on-policy A3C for the base task; off-policy n-step Q-learning for auxiliary control from replay; replay-skewed immediate reward prediction; and additional value replay. Sharing the CNN/LSTM across all heads is a natural way to ensure auxiliary learning benefits the base policy. The off-policy nature of auxiliary tasks is appropriate for data efficiency and decouples control learning from behavior.
- Empirical support: Results are strong and consistent. The Labyrinth suite tests navigation, exploration, and first-person control; Atari adds breadth and comparability. The authors evaluate over many hyperparameter settings and report top-k and aggregate robustness, reducing concerns about cherry-picked tuning. Ablations show that each auxiliary component contributes; notably, pixel/feature control outperforms pure prediction/reconstruction baselines, supporting the central hypothesis that learning to control yields more useful features than passive prediction.
- Scientific rigor: The claims are supported by extensive experiments, reasonable baselines (A3C, Prioritized Dueling DQN), and ablations, and the paper reports human-normalized scores, learning speed, and robustness. While some implementation specifics appear in the appendix, the core training objective is clearly stated.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all decision-critical)
- Earlier exposition of auxiliary tasks: Define the pixel/feature control reward functions and the precise auxiliary control loss (LPC as a sum of n-step Q-learning losses) in the main text before the appendix. A concise mathematical definition of pixel-control rewards and the deconvolutional head would aid reproducibility.
- Off-policy considerations: Clarify stability mechanisms (e.g., target networks, update schedules) for the off-policy Q-learning heads and any use of double Q or other bias-reduction techniques. Discuss why importance sampling corrections are not required here and any observed interference between off-policy and on-policy updates given shared parameters.
- Replay details: Specify replay buffer size rationale (2k steps), sampling ratios for value replay vs. control vs. reward prediction, and sensitivity to replay size and skew. A brief sensitivity analysis would strengthen the robustness claim.
- Analysis beyond performance: Provide more insights into what the representation learns. For instance, visualize feature control targets, show that pixel/feature control improves saliency on reward-relevant cues, or probe unit selectivity and temporal credit assignment.
- Component contributions: Pixel control seems especially effective in Labyrinth; feature control is promising but less explored. Include more systematic comparisons of pixel vs. feature control across tasks (including Atari), and report interactions (e.g., are gains additive or redundant?).
- Generality: Comment on applicability to continuous control (actions and states) and non-visual input modalities. A small continuous-control experiment or discussion would broaden impact.
- Evaluation clarity: Report exact training frames, wall-clock compute, number of seeds per setting, and variance bars for key curves. These details help interpret the 10× speedup and 880% mean performance claims.

Questions for the authors
1) Auxiliary control rewards: What normalization is applied to pixel-change rewards across games with different intensity dynamics? How sensitive is performance to λPC and reward scaling?
2) Feature control: Which layers/features work best as targets? Did you try controlling multiple layers simultaneously, and how did that interact with pixel control?
3) Off-policy stability: Do you use target networks (θ−) for the auxiliary Q-heads, and are they synchronized with A3C updates on a fixed schedule? Any issues with divergence or interference?
4) Importance sampling: Since auxiliary tasks learn off-policy from a replay buffer skewed for reward prediction, did you explore IS corrections or other methods to mitigate distribution shift? Why are they unnecessary here?
5) Replay buffer design: Why 2k recent steps? How do results change with larger buffers or prioritized replay for control/value heads?
6) Ablations: Pixel control alone often yields most of the gains in Labyrinth. Can you provide per-task ablations on Atari to confirm this pattern and discuss when feature control adds unique benefits?
7) Reward prediction skew: Does balancing positive/negative/zero rewards bias the representation in sparse or highly asymmetric reward settings? Any degradation when extrinsic reward is dense?
8) Transfer and generalization: Do features learned via auxiliary control transfer across levels or games (e.g., pretrain on one set, fine-tune on another)? Any evidence for faster adaptation?
9) Continuous control: Have you tested UNREAL on MuJoCo or similar domains? If not, what adaptations would be needed for continuous actions?
10) Compute and reproducibility: Please provide training time, hardware, and the exact hyperparameter ranges used per domain, and consider releasing code or pseudocode for the auxiliary heads.

Overall, this is a well-motivated and carefully executed paper that advances representation learning for deep RL via control-oriented auxiliary tasks. The empirical gains and robustness are substantial, and the idea is broadly applicable.
