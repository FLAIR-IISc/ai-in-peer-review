Summary
This paper proposes UNREAL, a deep RL architecture that augments a base A3C agent with auxiliary tasks to improve representation learning and data efficiency. The auxiliary tasks are domain-independent and include: (1) control tasks that learn separate policies to maximise pseudo-reward signals (notably pixel-control and feature-control), (2) immediate reward prediction trained on replay with a skewed sampling toward rewarding events, and (3) value function replay. Importantly, except for value replay, these auxiliary tasks are used solely to shape the shared CNN+LSTM representation and are not executed as options. Experiments on Labyrinth and 57 Atari games show substantial gains in performance, robustness to hyperparameters, and striking improvements in data efficiency over A3C.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: The approach is well-motivated and carefully placed in the literature, and the empirical results are strong and convincing, especially the large gains in data efficiency and robustness.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses how to learn useful representations when extrinsic reward is sparse, by leveraging auxiliary RL objectives that are better aligned with long-term control than unsupervised reconstruction alone. This is clearly motivated and connected to prior work (Horde, UVFAs, successor representations).
- Approach: The method integrates on-policy A3C with off-policy auxiliary control via n-step Q-learning on replay, reward prediction with rebalanced sampling, and value replay. The design choices (e.g., deconvolutional heads exploiting spatial structure for pixel control) are thoughtful and well justified.
- Empirical support: On Labyrinth, UNREAL more than doubles human-normalised performance relative to A3C (mean 87% vs. 54%) and achieves up to 18× speedups, requiring under 10% of the data to match A3C’s final performance. On Atari, UNREAL achieves 880% mean and 250% median human-normalised performance and improved robustness to hyperparameters compared to A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN. The ablations on Labyrinth help isolate the contribution of each component.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not central to my accept decision)
- Computational cost: Please quantify the additional training cost from auxiliary tasks. How much wall-clock slowdown occurs relative to A3C, and which components dominate (pixel-control head, reward prediction, replayed updates)?
- Clarify multiple policies: In the abstract/intro, make it explicit that the agent learns separate policies for each auxiliary control task. The phrasing “maximises many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously” can be read as a single policy optimising a combined reward.
- Feature control: The empirical validation of feature-control is limited (Fig. 5 suggests only slight initial gains). Since the target features change as the network learns, please discuss potential instability/convergence issues and any mitigations (e.g., slower updates, target networks, layer choice).
- Training horizon: You note that “performance is still steadily improving.” Why not run the best agents longer to show asymptotic performance and whether UNREAL’s gap widens or narrows?
- Weighting of auxiliary losses: Why weren’t λ-weights (λVR, λPC, λRP) treated as hyperparameters to tune per domain? Were experiments done to confirm “1” (or the reported ranges) is a good choice?
- Off-policy updates: Please explicitly note that auxiliary control uses off-policy n-step Q-learning rather than “true” 1-step off-policy Q-learning, as discussed in the OpenReview comments.
- Minor corrections and clarity:
  - “Policy gradient algorithms adjust the policy to maximise the expected reward, Lπ = − …” → this is a loss to be minimised.
  - In Eq. 1, consider λc inside the sum to allow per-task weights; also r(c) t should be r(c) t+k just below Eq. 1.
  - Figure references: Figure 2 is not referenced; Figure 1(d) should be referenced in Section 3.3.
  - Subject-verb agreement: “the features discovered in this manner is shared” → are shared.
  - Around Eq. 2, LPC is referenced in text; ensure it is defined precisely and matches the notation in Eq. 2.
  - Figure 3: explain what “Clip” means for dueling networks in the legend; clarify that the percentages in the legend refer to Labyrinth; fix “Figure 3 (right)” (should be top left) and “Figure 3 Top” → “Figure 3 Top Right.”
  - Figure 5: text says “three Labyrinth navigation levels,” but the plots referenced appear to be two levels (left and middle).
  - Section 4.2: add a comma in “for all three methods, the right half shows …”
  - Appendix: “Further details are included in the supplementary materials.” Please provide a pointer or remove.
  - More ablations on Atari would be valuable to see if the Labyrinth component-wise contributions replicate.
- Hyperparameter reporting: The value (or distribution) of λPC should be clearly stated where Eq. 2 is introduced, and consistently reported across main text and appendix.

Questions for the authors
- What is the wall-clock training overhead of UNREAL versus baseline A3C on Labyrinth and Atari? Which auxiliary component contributes most to the cost?
- How sensitive is performance to λVR, λPC, and λRP? Did you explore per-task λc (inside the sum in Eq. 1) or adaptive weighting?
- For feature-control, which layer(s) worked best and did you try stabilisation mechanisms (e.g., target features, slower updates)?
- Could you provide Atari ablations isolating pixel control, reward prediction, and value replay contributions?
- What does “Clip” denote in the dueling architecture in Figure 3?
- Please confirm the intended value/distribution of λPC in the main text where Eq. 2 appears, and reconcile with the appendix description.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear idea, careful integration of methods, and compelling empirical results. I recommend acceptance.
