- Augments an A3C-style agent with unsupervised auxiliary tasks (auxiliary control + reward-focused tasks) to speed representation learning and improve performance (UNREAL).  
- Auxiliary control: learn policies to maximise pseudo-rewards (pixel/feature control) via off-policy n-step Q-learning with replay.  
- Reward-focused tasks: predict immediate rewards and perform value-function replay to mitigate sparsity and stabilise learning.  
- Jointly optimises A3C loss with auxiliary losses; evaluated on 13 Labyrinth levels and 57 Atari games with substantial gains in final performance and data efficiency.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Main reasons: strong, consistent empirical improvements across diverse domains; practical, scalable integration of auxiliary tasks.  
- Empirical rigor: broad benchmarks, ablations (e.g., removing pixel control), comparisons to alternatives, and robustness analyses.  
- Data efficiency: large speedups in Labyrinth (≈10× faster to reach A3C’s best), beneficial for real-world training.  
- Conceptual coherence: treating controllable sensorimotor features as pseudo-rewards links unsupervised representation learning and control; reward tasks address sparsity without biasing policy gradients.  
- Engineering clarity: architecture (CNN-LSTM, deconv heads), replay partitioning, and optimization details support reproducibility.  
- Novelty is incremental: core idea (auxiliary tasks shape features) is expected; main contribution is concrete instantiation and strong empirical validation.  
- Architectural complexity: full system (A3C + multiple off-policy heads + replay + prioritisation) is intricate; minimal effective subset is unclear.  
- Compute/scalability under-specified: missing hardware, wall-clock, and throughput details hinder practicality assessment.  
- Sensitivity to auxiliary-task weighting: effects of loss weights (e.g., λPC) on stability and performance need clearer analysis.  
- Suggest clarifying interaction and stability between on-policy A3C updates and off-policy Q-learning heads.  
- Suggest systematic ablation isolating pixel control, feature control, reward prediction, and value replay across multiple levels.  
- Suggest reporting compute details: GPUs/CPUs, wall-clock to reach scores, frames/sec, and memory for replay/models.  
- Suggest discussing generality beyond pixels (proprioception, language-conditioned goals) and whether feature control can substitute pixel control.  
- Suggest ensuring hyperparameter fairness (search budgets) between baselines and UNREAL and summarising choices.  
- Suggest releasing code and trained models to aid adoption and reproducibility.  
- Questions: what hardware and wall-clock time were needed for Labyrinth and Atari, and what was frame throughput/training duration per task?  
- Questions: how sensitive are results to auxiliary loss weights (λPC, λRP, λVR) and are there instabilities outside reported ranges?  
- Questions: which minimal subset of auxiliary tasks yields most gains and what to prioritise with limited compute?  
- Questions: any adverse interference between off-policy Q-learning and on-policy A3C; were target networks or stabilisation tricks required?  
- Questions: when does feature control match/exceed pixel control, and can it replace pixel-control heuristics?  
- Questions: does the learned shared representation transfer to new tasks or reward functions in the same environment?  
- Questions: do the authors plan to release code and training scripts?
