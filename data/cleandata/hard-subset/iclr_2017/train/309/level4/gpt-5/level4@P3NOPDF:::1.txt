This paper introduces UNREAL, an approach for augmenting deep RL agents such as A3C with unsupervised auxiliary tasks. The authors propose a set of auxiliary control and reward-related tasks designed to improve representation learning and sample efficiency, and evaluate the method on DeepMind Lab (Labyrinth) and Atari. Empirically, UNREAL significantly outperforms A3C in both final performance and learning speed. The paper is clearly written and accessible to readers with a deep RL background.

Strengths:
- Clear and compelling empirical gains over a strong baseline (A3C) across two challenging domains.
- Demonstrates faster learning as well as better asymptotic performance, suggesting that the auxiliary tasks improve representation quality and exploration efficiency.
- The paper reads well and is straightforward to follow, with an intuitively appealing motivation for using auxiliary objectives.

Concerns and limitations:
- The main conceptual idea—leveraging auxiliary tasks that are aligned with the primary objective to shape features—is sensible and, to some extent, expected to help. As such, the contribution feels more like a strong empirical validation and systemization than a surprising theoretical or algorithmic breakthrough.
- The architecture and training setup are relatively complex, with several components and losses. This raises questions about implementation burden, sensitivity to hyperparameters, and reproducibility.
- The paper would benefit from more detailed analysis isolating the impact of each auxiliary task and their relative weighting. While the results are convincing, it is hard to attribute the gains to specific design choices without thorough ablations.

Questions for the authors:
- Computational resources: Please provide concrete details on the compute budget required to train UNREAL (e.g., number of CPU cores/GPUs, wall-clock time per environment, total environment frames, and any parallelization specifics). How does this compare to vanilla A3C under similar settings?
- Sensitivity and ablations: How sensitive is performance to the choice and weighting of auxiliary losses? If ablations exist, please quantify the contribution of each auxiliary task and report variance across runs.
- Generality: To what extent do the proposed auxiliary tasks transfer across domains? For example, how domain-specific are the control/reward tasks for Labyrinth versus Atari?
- Reproducibility and code: Given the architectural complexity, are the authors willing to release code and detailed training scripts/configurations to facilitate replication?

Overall assessment:
This is a solid and timely contribution that demonstrates the practical value of unsupervised auxiliary tasks in deep RL. The empirical improvements are substantial and likely to influence follow-up work on representation learning in RL. While the core idea is not conceptually surprising, the thorough validation on challenging benchmarks and the demonstrated gains in both speed and performance make this paper a worthwhile addition to the conference. Clarifications on compute requirements, ablation studies, and code availability would further strengthen the contribution.
