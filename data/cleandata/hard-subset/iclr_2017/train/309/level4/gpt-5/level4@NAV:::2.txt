This paper proposes improving representation learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the main policy objective with terms for (domain-agnostic) auxiliary tasks. These include control (learning separate policies that try to maximally alter the state, i.e., pixels), immediate reward prediction, and value-function replay. Except for the last, these auxiliary tasks serve only to shape the features by sharing the CNN+LSTM feature extractor. Experiments demonstrate benefits on Atari and Labyrinth, notably much better sample efficiency than A3C.

The paper is clearly written, the ideas are solid, and the results are persuasive; I view this as a clear accept. At a high level I have only a few comments, none critical:
- Please discuss the additional computational overhead of optimizing the auxiliary tasks. How much training speed do you give up? Which components are most expensive?
- If possible, clarify in the abstract/intro that the agent learns distinct policies for each task. When I read in the abstract that the agent “also maximises many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously by reinforcement learning”, I initially thought a single policy was trained on all rewards jointly, and only realized otherwise upon reaching Eq. 1.
- The “feature control” idea lacks strong empirical support (the preliminary result in Fig. 5 is not very convincing, as it only seems to help slightly at the start). I like the concept, but since the features evolve during learning, the task itself changes, which may raise stability/convergence concerns.
- Since you note that “the performance of our agents is still steadily improving,” why not run them longer to see how far they can go (at least the best-performing ones)?
- Why aren’t the auxiliary-task weights (the lambda_*) treated as hyperparameters to tune? Did you test whether setting them to 1 is adequate?
- Please mention that the auxiliary tasks are not trained with “true” Q-learning, as they are optimized off-policy with multi-step empirical returns (as discussed in the OpenReview comments).
