This paper proposes a method to improve representation learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the primary A3C agent's objective function with several auxiliary tasks. These domain-independent tasks are designed to provide richer training signals and include: 1) auxiliary control tasks, where separate policies learn to maximize change in the sensory input (e.g., pixel values); 2) an auxiliary reward prediction task; and 3) value function replay. With the exception of value function replay, these tasks contribute to the loss solely to shape the features of a shared representation network (a CNN-LSTM). The empirical results on the Atari suite and the visually complex 3D Labyrinth environment are impressive, showing that the proposed UNREAL agent significantly outperforms A3C in both final score and data efficiency.

Decision: Accept

This is a strong paper with a clear and well-motivated contribution, supported by convincing experimental results. The main reasons for my recommendation are:
1.  The proposed method of combining on-policy learning for the main task with several off-policy auxiliary control and prediction tasks is an elegant and effective way to improve representation learning and data efficiency in deep RL.
2.  The empirical evaluation is thorough and demonstrates substantial performance gains over state-of-the-art baselines across two challenging domains, Atari and the new 3D Labyrinth environment. The reported 10x mean speedup in learning is particularly noteworthy.

### Supporting Arguments

The core idea of using auxiliary tasks to shape a shared representation is not entirely new, but the specific formulation in this paper is novel and compelling. By defining auxiliary tasks as separate control problems (e.g., pixel control) and learning their associated policies off-policy, the agent is encouraged to learn features that relate to the controllability of its environment. This is a more active and potentially more useful signal than passive prediction or reconstruction tasks.

The experimental results strongly support the authors' claims. The UNREAL agent achieves state-of-the-art performance on both Atari and the visually rich Labyrinth environment. The improvement is not just in final performance but, critically, in learning speed and robustness to hyperparameters. This increased data efficiency is a significant contribution, as it is one of the primary hurdles for deep RL. The ablation studies on Labyrinth effectively demonstrate the contribution of each auxiliary component, justifying the overall architecture.

### Additional Feedback

The following points are suggestions intended to further strengthen the paper. They did not factor negatively into my decision.

*   **Computational Cost:** The paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks. How much does training time increase in terms of wall-clock time compared to the A3C baseline? Which components are the most computationally expensive?
*   **Clarity on Multiple Policies:** In the abstract and introduction, it could be made clearer that the agent learns distinct policies for each auxiliary control task. My initial reading of the phrase "learns separate policies for maximising many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously" was that a single policy was trained on a composite reward. A minor rewording could prevent this ambiguity.
*   **Feature Control Task:** The idea of "feature control" is interesting, but the empirical validation is weak. The preliminary experiment in Figure 5 shows only a marginal initial benefit. I am also concerned about the stability of this task, as the policy is trying to control features that are themselves changing during learning. This could lead to a non-stationary target and potential training instability. It would be valuable if the authors could comment on this.
*   **Hyperparameter λ:** The paper does not specify how the weights for the auxiliary losses (λ_VR, λ_PC, λ_RP) were chosen. Were they tuned as hyperparameters? The appendix mentions λ_PC is sampled from a distribution, but λ_VR and λ_RP are set to 1. Some justification for these choices would be welcome.
*   **Training Duration:** Since the learning curves for the UNREAL agent appear to be still trending upward at the end of training, it might be worth running the best-performing agents longer to see the full extent of their final performance.
*   **Atari Ablation Study:** It would be insightful to see a more detailed ablation study on Atari, similar to the one performed for Labyrinth. This would help to verify if the individual contributions of each auxiliary task are consistent across different domains.
*   **Off-Policy Q-Learning:** For technical precision, it's worth clarifying that the auxiliary tasks are not trained with standard Q-learning, but rather with an off-policy variant that uses n-step returns.
