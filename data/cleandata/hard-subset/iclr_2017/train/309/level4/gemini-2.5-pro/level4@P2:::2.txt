### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), a novel deep reinforcement learning agent designed to improve learning speed and final performance by enriching the learning signal. The core idea is to augment a standard A3C agent with a set of auxiliary tasks that are trained concurrently with the main policy. These tasks include auxiliary control objectives, where the agent learns separate policies to maximize local changes in its perception (pixel control), and an auxiliary reward prediction task, where the agent predicts the immediate future reward from a short sequence of past observations. These tasks, trained using experience replay, share a common representation network with the main agent, compelling it to learn more general and useful features. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach with extensive experiments on the Atari 2600 suite and a challenging 3D first-person environment called Labyrinth, achieving state-of-the-art results in both domains.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The paper presents a compelling and well-motivated method for integrating auxiliary self-supervised objectives into a state-of-the-art deep RL agent. This approach directly addresses the critical challenge of representation learning in RL, especially in reward-sparse environments.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The claims are substantiated by extensive and convincing empirical evidence. The UNREAL agent not only achieves state-of-the-art performance on Atari but also demonstrates massive improvements in data efficiency (a 10x mean speedup) and final performance on the visually complex 3D Labyrinth environment.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's primary strength lies in its strong and well-executed empirical evaluation, which provides clear support for its claims. The UNREAL agent significantly outperforms the A3C baseline, more than doubling the mean human-normalized score on Labyrinth (from 54% to 87%) and achieving an impressive 880% average expert human performance on Atari.

Beyond final performance, the reported 10x speedup in learning on Labyrinth is a crucial result, highlighting a dramatic improvement in data efficiency. This is a significant contribution, as sample efficiency remains a major bottleneck for deep RL algorithms. Furthermore, the ablation studies systematically demonstrate that each auxiliary component contributes positively to the overall performance, validating the architectural design choices. The authors also show that their method increases robustness to hyperparameter settings, which is a valuable practical benefit.

The proposed auxiliary tasks are domain-agnostic and are shown to be effective across both 2D (Atari) and complex 3D (Labyrinth) environments. This generality suggests that the principles behind UNREAL could be a valuable addition to a wide range of future RL agents. The paper is well-written, the ideas are clearly explained, and the work is well-positioned within the existing literature on representation learning and auxiliary tasks in RL.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to further strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

**Major Suggestions:**
*   **Computational Overhead:** The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of the computational cost of the auxiliary tasks. How much do they increase training time (wall-clock time) and model parameter count compared to the A3C baseline? Identifying the most computationally expensive components would be useful for future work.
*   **Clarity on Multiple Policies:** In the abstract, the phrase "learns separate policies for maximising many other pseudo-reward functions" could be made even clearer. My initial interpretation was that a single policy was trained on a mixture of rewards. Explicitly stating that the agent learns a collection of distinct auxiliary policies that share a common representation would prevent any ambiguity.
*   **"Feature Control" Validation:** The idea of feature control is intriguing, but the empirical validation is noted as preliminary. A potential concern with this task is non-stationarity, as the agent is learning to control features that are themselves changing during training. It would be valuable to acknowledge this potential challenge and perhaps frame the results as a promising direction for future investigation.
*   **Ablation Study on Atari:** While the Labyrinth results include a thorough ablation, a similar breakdown for Atari would be welcome. It would be interesting to see if the relative contributions of each auxiliary task are consistent across both domains.
*   **Off-Policy Learning with n-step Returns:** For technical precision, it may be worth clarifying that the auxiliary control tasks are trained with n-step Q-learning updates from replayed data, which is a common but important detail differentiating it from standard single-step off-policy Q-learning.
