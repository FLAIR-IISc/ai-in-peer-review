This paper presents a method for enhancing representation learning in deep reinforcement learning agents by incorporating several auxiliary tasks into the training process. Alongside the primary goal of maximizing extrinsic reward, the agent learns to solve auxiliary control tasks (e.g., maximizing pixel changes in the input), predict immediate rewards, and replay value function updates from experience. These tasks are used to train a shared feature extractor (a CNN-LSTM network), leading to more robust and general-purpose representations. The authors provide strong empirical evidence on the Atari and Labyrinth environments, demonstrating significant improvements in both final performance and data efficiency over a strong A3C baseline.

My recommendation is to **Accept** this paper. The proposed ideas are well-motivated and technically sound, the paper is clearly written, and the experimental results are highly convincing.

The primary strengths of this work are its novel combination of auxiliary tasks and the impressive empirical gains. The core idea of using control as an unsupervised objective for representation learning is compelling and the results strongly support its utility. The reported improvements, particularly the 10x speedup and the leap to 87% human performance on the challenging Labyrinth suite, are substantial. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the contribution of each component to the final performance.

For the final version of the paper, I offer the following suggestions and questions for improvement. These points are intended to be constructive and did not negatively impact my decision.

**Major Feedback & Questions:**
*   **Computational Cost:** The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks. What is the impact on wall-clock training time compared to the A3C baseline? Which of the auxiliary components are the most computationally intensive?
*   **Clarity in Abstract/Introduction:** The abstract could be more precise in stating that the agent learns *separate* policies for each auxiliary task. The current phrasing ("maximises many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously") could initially be misread as learning a single policy for a multi-objective reward, a point that is only clarified by Equation 1.
*   **Feature Control Task:** The empirical support for the "feature control" task seems preliminary. The results in Figure 5 suggest only a minor initial benefit. While the idea is interesting, it introduces a non-stationary learning problem where the control target (the network's features) is constantly changing. It would be valuable to hear the authors' thoughts on the potential stability challenges this might pose.
*   **Training Duration:** The authors note that "the performance of our agents is still steadily improving." It would be informative to see how far the top-performing agents could go with longer training runs to better establish their asymptotic performance.
*   **Auxiliary Loss Weights:** The weighting parameters for the auxiliary losses (λ_*) were not presented as hyperparameters to be tuned. Was there a principled reason for setting them to 1, or was this choice validated empirically?
*   **Off-Policy Method:** For technical precision, it may be worth clarifying that the auxiliary tasks are trained off-policy using n-step returns, which is a variation on standard single-step Q-Learning.

**Minor Feedback:**
*   In the Background section, the description of the policy gradient update seems to be formulated as a reward to be maximized rather than a loss to be minimized.
*   In Equation 1, the hyperparameter λ_C should likely be placed inside the summation. Just below it, the return should probably use r_{t+k}^{(c)}.
*   The term L_PC is mentioned in the text around Equation 2 but does not appear explicitly in the combined loss function.
*   Figure 2 and Figure 1(d) do not appear to be explicitly referenced in the main text.
*   There is a grammatical error in Section 3.2: "the features discovered... is shared" should be "are shared."
*   In the legend for Figure 3, it would be helpful to explain what "Clip" refers to in the context of dueling networks. Also, the percentages mentioned seem to apply specifically to Labyrinth, which could be made clearer.
*   There appear to be incorrect plot references in Section 4.1.1. For instance, "Figure 3 (right)" seems to refer to the top-left plot, and "Figure 3 Top" seems to refer to the top-right plot.
*   The description for Figure 5 mentions experiments on "three Labyrinth navigation levels," but the figure seems to show learning curves for only two levels.
*   The Atari experiments would benefit from a more detailed ablation study, similar to that provided for Labyrinth, to see if the contributions of each auxiliary task are consistent across both domains.
