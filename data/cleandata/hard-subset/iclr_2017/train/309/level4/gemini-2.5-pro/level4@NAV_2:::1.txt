**Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces UNREAL, a deep reinforcement learning agent that augments the Asynchronous Advantage Actor-Critic (A3C) framework with a variety of auxiliary tasks. The central idea is to improve representation learning and data efficiency by training the agent not only to maximize the extrinsic reward but also to solve auxiliary problems in parallel. The authors propose two main classes of auxiliary tasks: auxiliary control tasks, where the agent learns policies to control features of its sensory input (pixel changes) or its own internal state (network activations), and auxiliary reward tasks, where the agent predicts the onset of immediate rewards from recent experience. These tasks are integrated into the main A3C agent using an experience replay buffer, allowing for efficient, off-policy training of the auxiliary objectives. The authors demonstrate that the resulting UNREAL agent achieves state-of-the-art performance, significantly outperforming previous methods on 57 Atari games (averaging 880% expert human performance) and a challenging suite of 3D Labyrinth tasks, where it not only reaches 87% expert human performance but also learns on average 10 times faster than the A3C baseline.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper with compelling empirical results. My decision is based on two primary reasons:
1.  The proposed UNREAL agent achieves substantial and convincing performance improvements over a state-of-the-art baseline (A3C) on two complex and distinct domains (Atari and Labyrinth). The gains are not only in final performance but also, critically, in data efficiency and robustness to hyperparameters.
2.  The paper provides a thorough empirical investigation into the utility of different auxiliary tasks, successfully integrating multiple complementary objectives into a single cohesive architecture. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the value of each proposed component.

**Supporting Arguments**

The claims made in the paper are well-supported by extensive experiments. The authors compare their full UNREAL agent against the A3C baseline and ablated versions of their own agent across a wide range of tasks. The results presented in Figure 3 are particularly persuasive, showing that each auxiliary task contributes to improved performance and that their combination yields the best results. The 10x mean speedup in learning on the Labyrinth environment is a significant practical contribution, addressing one of the key challenges in deep RL: sample efficiency.

While the high-level concept of using auxiliary tasks for representation learning is not new, this paper serves as an excellent proof of concept for how to effectively implement and combine these ideas in a modern, large-scale deep RL agent. The specific formulation of auxiliary tasks as *control* problems (e.g., maximizing pixel changes) rather than just passive prediction or reconstruction tasks is a key insight. The experiments in section 4.1.1 (Figure 5) provide direct evidence for this, showing that pixel control outperforms both pixel prediction and reconstruction. This validates the authors' hypothesis that learning to control features of the sensorimotor stream leads to more useful representations for goal-directed behavior. The overall architecture, though complex, is a well-motivated synthesis of on-policy learning for the main task and off-policy learning from a replay buffer for the auxiliary tasks.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The paper is very well-written and easy to follow for readers with a background in deep reinforcement learning. The following points are intended as suggestions for further strengthening the work and are not critical for my decision:

*   The discussion could benefit from a clearer positioning of the conceptual contribution. The paper's strength is overwhelmingly empirical. While the results are impressive, the underlying idea that auxiliary tasks help learning is somewhat intuitive. A more focused discussion on *why* this particular combination of control, reward prediction, and value replay is so effective would elevate the paper's contribution beyond a powerful demonstration of a known principle.
*   The connection between the auxiliary tasks and the specific challenges of the environments could be explored more deeply. For example, does pixel control help more in visually dynamic games, while reward prediction is more critical in sparse-reward settings? A brief analysis of this could provide valuable insights for future work.

**Questions for Authors**

To help clarify my understanding and strengthen my assessment, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  The UNREAL architecture integrates several additional loss components and network heads compared to the A3C baseline. Could you comment on the additional computational overhead (in terms of both memory and wall-clock time) required to train the UNREAL agent versus the A3C baseline?
2.  Given the complexity of the combined architecture, reproducing these results could be challenging. Are there plans to release the source code for the agent and the Labyrinth environment to facilitate future research in this area?
