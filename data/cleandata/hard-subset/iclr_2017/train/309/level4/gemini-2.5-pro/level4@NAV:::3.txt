This paper introduces a method for training reinforcement learning agents by augmenting the primary objective with auxiliary tasks, based on the hypothesis that this encourages the development of more robust feature representations. The authors propose two pseudo-control tasks—one focused on maximizing changes in pixel intensity and another on controlling activations within the network's hidden layers—alongside a supervised regression task for predicting the immediate upcoming reward. This reward prediction task is trained offline using a biased sampling strategy from an experience replay buffer, which balances the occurrence of positive and zero-reward samples to a 1:1 ratio.

Agents trained with this approach demonstrate a substantial performance improvement on RL tasks characterized by discrete actions and continuous state spaces, achieving the performance of baseline methods with a tenfold reduction in training iterations.

The proposed method stands in contrast to conventional "passive" approaches like unsupervised pre-training or model-based learning. Rather than compelling the agent to learn a potentially irrelevant representation of its input or to master a world model which may be intractable due to partial observability, this work demonstrates that learning to control local and internal environmental features serves as a valuable complement to learning the main control policy.

I find the approach to be novel, and it presents a compelling alternative to standard unsupervised learning techniques, specifically because it capitalizes on the agent's inherent capacity for control within its environment.

The auxiliary tasks are described conceptually, which is effective for conveying the core intuition. However, the inclusion of more specific, low-level details in the main body would be beneficial. For instance, the formulation for the pixel control loss (L_PC) should be explicitly defined before the appendix. Despite this, the paper is well-written and should be readily accessible to readers with a background in Deep Reinforcement Learning.

The experimental methodology is robust. While the optimal hyperparameter distributions may indeed differ between the A3C baseline and the proposed UNREAL agent, the authors' approach of measuring the top-3 performing runs helps ensure that the best hyperparameters for each method are identified, provided they lie within the searched range.

A notable weakness, or perhaps more accurately, a critical area for future work, is the limited experimental analysis into *why* these auxiliary tasks are so effective, beyond their clear and substantial impact on performance. Following this point, the pixel and feature control tasks appear to be the primary drivers of the performance gains. For example, in the Labyrinth environment, the A3C agent augmented solely with pixel control (A3C+PC) outperforms all other methods except the full UNREAL agent. This particular component warrants a more focused investigation, either in isolation or through a deeper analysis utilizing metrics that go beyond final task performance.
