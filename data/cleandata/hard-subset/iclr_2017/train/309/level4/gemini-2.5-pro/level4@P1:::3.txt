### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel method for accelerating and improving deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the standard reward-maximization objective with several auxiliary tasks. The central hypothesis is that training an agent to control and predict aspects of its sensory input stream will lead to the development of richer, more useful representations. The authors introduce two primary types of auxiliary tasks:
1.  **Auxiliary Control Tasks:** The agent learns off-policy value functions for "pseudo-rewards" corresponding to maximizing local changes in pixel intensity (Pixel Control) and maximizing the activations of its own hidden units (Feature Control). This encourages the agent to learn how its actions affect its environment and internal state.
2.  **Auxiliary Prediction Task:** The agent learns to predict the immediate extrinsic reward from a short sequence of recent observations. This task is trained using an experience replay buffer with a skewed sampling strategy that over-represents rewarding events, helping the model learn reward-relevant features even when rewards are sparse.

These tasks are integrated into a state-of-the-art A3C agent, resulting in the proposed UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent. The authors demonstrate that this approach significantly outperforms strong baselines on 57 Atari games and a visually complex, first-person 3D environment called Labyrinth. Notably, the UNREAL agent learns substantially faster, achieving the baseline's peak performance with up to 10x fewer environment steps. The core contribution is a conceptual shift away from passive representation learning (e.g., auto-encoding) towards active, control-oriented objectives that are more aligned with the agent's purpose.

### **Decision and Justification**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper introduces a novel and compelling approach to representation learning in RL. By framing auxiliary objectives as control problems, the agent is encouraged to learn about cause and effect in its environment, which is a powerful and more active alternative to traditional unsupervised methods like reconstruction.
2.  The empirical results are exceptionally strong and well-supported. The UNREAL agent demonstrates dramatic improvements in both final performance and data efficiency over a state-of-the-art A3C baseline across two diverse and challenging domains. The reported 10x speedup in learning on Labyrinth is a particularly significant result.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The core novelty of this work lies in its formulation of auxiliary tasks. The idea of learning policies to control features of the agent's own sensory stream or internal representations is a clever way to generate a rich training signal. This contrasts with more "passive" unsupervised objectives, such as input reconstruction, which can force the model to expend capacity on irrelevant details. By learning to control its environment, the agent inherently learns a representation that is grounded in the environment's dynamics, which proves to be highly beneficial for the primary control task.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare their UNREAL agent against a strong A3C baseline on the standard Atari benchmark and the visually rich 3D Labyrinth environment. The performance gains are substantial across the board, not only in terms of final score but also in learning speed and robustness to hyperparameter settings. The ablation studies in Figure 3 clearly demonstrate that each auxiliary component contributes positively to performance, with the combination yielding the best results. The methodology, which involves a broad search over hyperparameters for all compared methods, provides strong evidence that the reported gains are due to the architectural innovations rather than simply better tuning.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The paper is well-written and the core ideas are communicated effectively. The following points are intended as constructive feedback to further strengthen the work.

*   **Analysis of Auxiliary Task Effects:** While the paper convincingly demonstrates *that* the auxiliary tasks improve performance, it offers limited insight into *why* they are so effective. A deeper analysis of the learned representations or behaviors would elevate the paper's scientific contribution. For instance, the pixel control task appears to have the largest impact, especially in Labyrinth (Figure 3 shows A3C+PC is a very strong agent on its own). It would be fascinating to investigate this component in more detail. Visualizing what the agent learns to do to maximize pixel change in a specific region, or probing the resulting features for specific invariances, could provide valuable insights.
*   **Clarity on Technical Details:** Some key implementation details are deferred to the appendix, which slightly hinders the readability and self-containment of the main paper. For example, the precise mathematical definition of the pixel control pseudo-reward (average absolute pixel difference) is a crucial detail that would be beneficial to include in Section 3.1 or 4.

### **Questions for the Authors**

*   The pixel control (PC) task provides a very significant performance boost on its own, particularly in the Labyrinth environment. Do you have an intuition as to why this specific objective is so much more effective than reward prediction or value replay in isolation? Could it be that learning to actively influence the visual environment is a more fundamental and generalizable skill for an agent to acquire?
*   In your comparison with unsupervised learning (Figure 5), you show that pixel control is superior to pixel reconstruction. This supports your hypothesis about active control vs. passive prediction. Have you considered comparing against more modern "passive" representation learning techniques, such as contrastive methods, which are also designed to avoid focusing on pixel-level details?
*   The "feature control" task is intriguing as it encourages the agent to learn to control its own internal representations. Could you elaborate on what kind of behaviors this task encourages? Does the agent learn specific policies to activate certain groups of neurons that might correspond to high-level concepts (e.g., "seeing an enemy")?
