This paper presents a method for enhancing feature learning in deep reinforcement learning by supplementing the primary policy's optimization objective with several domain-agnostic auxiliary tasks. Specifically, these auxiliary tasks include pixel control (learning policies to maximize state-space changes), immediate reward prediction, and value function replay. With the exception of value function replay, these tasks contribute exclusively to shaping the feature representation by sharing a common CNN-LSTM feature extraction network. The empirical evaluation, conducted on Atari and Labyrinth environments, demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach, highlighting significant improvements in data efficiency compared to the A3C baseline.

The manuscript is well-articulated, the proposed ideas are conceptually sound, and the experimental results are compelling. I recommend acceptance. I have a few high-level comments, none of which are major concerns:

-   The authors should discuss the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks. An analysis of the impact on training speed and an identification of the most computationally expensive components would strengthen the paper.
-   The abstract and introduction could be clarified to state explicitly that the agent learns distinct policies for each auxiliary task. The current phrasing, "maximises many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously by reinforcement learning," could be misinterpreted as learning a single, multi-objective policy, a misconception I initially had until reaching Equation 1.
-   The empirical validation for the "feature control" concept appears limited. The preliminary results in Figure 5 show only a marginal initial benefit. Furthermore, there is a potential concern regarding stability, as the features being controlled are themselves changing during the learning process.
-   Given the acknowledgment that agent performance was "still steadily improving" at the end of training, it would be valuable to see results from longer training runs, at least for the top-performing agents, to better understand their asymptotic performance.
-   The paper should address why the auxiliary task weights (λ_*) were not treated as hyperparameters for optimization. Were experiments conducted to justify the choice of unity for these weights?
-   It would be beneficial to clarify that the auxiliary tasks are trained off-policy using multi-step returns and therefore do not strictly adhere to the standard Q-Learning algorithm, as noted in the public comments.

Minor Points:

-   In Equation 1, the λ_c term should be positioned inside the summation.
-   In the text immediately following Equation 1, r_t^(c) should be r_t+k^(c).
-   Figure 2 appears to be unreferenced in the main text. Additionally, Section 3.3 should reference Figure 1(d).
-   A minor grammatical correction: "the features discovered in this manner is shared" should be "...are shared."
-   The term L_PC is used in the text surrounding Equation 2 but is not explicitly defined in the equation itself.
-   The legend for Figure 3 should explain what "Clip" signifies in the context of dueling networks.
-   To better understand the individual contributions of the auxiliary tasks, it would have been insightful to include more comprehensive ablation studies on the Atari benchmark, similar to those performed for Labyrinth.
-   The legend of Figure 3 mentions percentages that correspond to the Labyrinth experiments; this should be made explicit.
-   In Section 4.1.2, the reference to "Figure 3 (right)" points to the top-left plot. Similarly, "Figure 3 Top" should be "Figure 3 Top Right."
-   The caption for Figure 5 states it shows results on "three Labyrinth navigation levels," but it appears to depict results from only two levels (left and middle plots).
-   A comma is needed in Section 4.2: "...for all three methods, the right half shows..."
-   The appendix mentions "supplementary materials," but their location is not specified.
-   The value for λ_PC should be stated (presumably 1).
