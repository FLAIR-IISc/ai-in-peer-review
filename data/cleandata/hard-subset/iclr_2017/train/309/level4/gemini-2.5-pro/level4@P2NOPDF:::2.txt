### **Summary**

This paper proposes a method for improving feature learning and data efficiency in deep reinforcement learning. The core idea is to augment the standard A3C agent's objective function with several domain-independent auxiliary tasks. These tasks, which include learning control policies to maximize pixel changes (Pixel Control), predicting immediate rewards (Reward Prediction), and replaying value function estimates (Value Replay), contribute to the loss function to shape the representations learned by a shared feature extraction network (a CNN+LSTM). The proposed method, UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), is evaluated on the Atari 2600 suite and 3D Labyrinth environments, demonstrating significant improvements in data efficiency and overall performance compared to the A3C baseline.

### **Assessment**

Overall, this is an excellent paper and a clear acceptance. The paper is well-written, and the core idea is both intuitive and well-motivated. The use of unsupervised auxiliary tasks to accelerate and improve feature learning is a valuable contribution to the field. The empirical results are compelling, showcasing substantial performance gains, particularly in terms of data efficiency, which is a critical challenge in deep RL.

The following points are suggestions for strengthening the paper, none of which are major concerns.

1.  **Computational Overhead:** The paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks. How does the training speed (e.g., in wall-clock time or samples/sec) compare to the A3C baseline? An analysis of which auxiliary components are the most computationally expensive would also be insightful for future work in this direction.

2.  **Clarity on Multiple Policies:** The abstract and introduction could be clarified regarding the policy learning structure. The phrase "also maximises many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously by reinforcement learning" might be misinterpreted as learning a single policy for a composite reward. I initially had this interpretation before reading the detailed formulation in Equation 1. Explicitly stating that separate policies are learned for the auxiliary control tasks earlier in the paper would prevent this potential confusion.

3.  **Validation and Stability of Feature Control:** The empirical validation for the "feature control" task feels preliminary. The results in Figure 5 show only a marginal initial benefit, and a more thorough ablation study, particularly on the Atari suite, would be valuable to demonstrate its contribution more convincingly. Additionally, could the authors comment on the stability of this task? Since the features being controlled are themselves changing during training, this creates a non-stationary learning problem that might have interesting convergence properties or potential instabilities.

4.  **Training Duration:** The paper notes that "the performance of our agents is still steadily improving" at the end of the training period. While understanding computational constraints, it would be interesting to see how far the performance of the best agents could be pushed with longer training runs, at least for the top-performing model. This could help establish a new state-of-the-art benchmark.

5.  **Auxiliary Task Weighting:** The weighting parameters for the auxiliary losses (the λ coefficients) are all set to 1. It would be useful to know if these values were chosen based on preliminary experiments or if they are considered hyperparameters that could be tuned. A brief discussion on the model's sensitivity to these weights would be a valuable addition.

6.  **Off-Policy Auxiliary Updates:** It is worth clarifying that the auxiliary control and reward prediction tasks are trained using an off-policy, n-step Q-learning-style update. A brief mention of this distinction from standard one-step Q-learning would add technical precision, especially since the experience is generated by the main policy.

### **Minor Comments and Typos**

*   In Section 3.1: "Policy gradient algorithms adjust the policy to maximise the expected reward, L_pi = -..." — L_pi here is a loss to be minimized, not a reward to be maximized.
*   In Equation 1, the weight λ_c should be inside the summation over the auxiliary control tasks.
*   Just below Equation 1, the reward r_t^(c) should likely be r_{t+k}^(c) to align with the n-step formulation.
*   Figure 2 does not appear to be referenced in the main text.
*   Figure 1(d) should be referenced in Section 3.3 when discussing reward prediction.
*   In Section 3.3: "...the features discovered in this manner is shared" should be "...are shared."
*   The text around Equation 2 refers to a loss L_PC, but this term is not explicitly defined in the equation. Please clarify its relation to the presented formula.
*   In the legend of Figure 3, please explain what "Clip" means in the context of dueling networks.
*   More extensive ablation studies on Atari would be welcome to see if the individual contributions of the auxiliary tasks follow the same patterns observed on Labyrinth.
*   In the legend of Figure 3, the percentages mentioned correspond to Labyrinth performance. This should be made clear, as the figure also shows Atari results.
*   In Section 4.1.2: "Figure 3 (right) shows..." refers to the top-left plot. Later, "This is shown in Figure 3 Top" should probably be "Figure 3 Top Right."
*   In Section 4.1.2: "Figure 5 shows the learning curves for the top 5 hyperparameter settings on three Labyrinth navigation levels" appears to refer to the left and middle plots, which show results on only two levels.
*   In Section 4.2: "The left side shows the average performance curves of the top 5 agents for all three methods the right half shows..." — this sentence is missing punctuation after "methods."
*   In the Appendix: "Further details are included in the supplementary materials." — It is not clear where to find these materials.
*   The value for λ_PC is not stated. Is it also set to 1?
