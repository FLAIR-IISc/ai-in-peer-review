- Proposes training RL agents with auxiliary tasks to learn more effective feature representations.  
- Introduces two pseudo-control auxiliary tasks: maximizing pixel-intensity changes and controlling latent feature activations.  
- Adds a supervised reward-prediction task trained offline with skewed sampling to balance rewarding vs non-rewarding events (~1:1).  
- Demonstrates large performance gains on discrete-action, continuous-state tasks, achieving baseline performance with one-tenth the training iterations (improved data efficiency).  
- Frames the approach as an active alternative to passive unsupervised pre-training or model-based learning by leveraging the agent’s control to shape representations.  
- Presents a novel and compelling idea that learning to control local and internal features complements policy learning.  
- Paper is generally clear for DRL readers but lacks some low-level implementation details in the main text (e.g., explicit pixel-control loss L_PC).  
- Experimental methodology is reasonable and fair, using top-3 hyperparameter settings for comparisons to account for differing optimal ranges.  
- Ablation results suggest pixel and feature control are most impactful (e.g., A3C+PC in Labyrinth outperforms most baselines), but analysis is limited.  
- Primary weakness/future direction: insufficient in-depth analysis of auxiliary tasks’ individual effects beyond final performance; more isolated studies and diagnostics are needed.
