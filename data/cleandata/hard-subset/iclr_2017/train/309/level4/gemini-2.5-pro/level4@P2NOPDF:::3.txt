This paper proposes a method for improving the data efficiency and performance of deep reinforcement learning agents by training them on auxiliary tasks alongside the main policy. The central hypothesis is that these auxiliary tasks encourage the agent to learn more robust and generalizable features from high-dimensional observations.

The authors introduce two novel self-supervised, pseudo-control tasks: Pixel Control, which trains the agent to control the change in pixel intensities in different regions of the screen, and Feature Control, which trains a policy to control the activations of hidden units in a specific network layer. In addition, they propose a supervised auxiliary task, Reward Prediction, where the agent learns to predict the immediate future reward from a short sequence of past observations. This reward prediction task is learned offline from an experience replay buffer, using a skewed sampling strategy to balance the prevalence of positive and negative reward examples.

When integrated into an A3C framework to create the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, this approach yields significant performance gains on a suite of discrete-action, continuous-state tasks (DeepMind Lab). The UNREAL agent demonstrates substantially improved data efficiency, achieving baseline performance levels using 10 times fewer training iterations.

A key conceptual contribution of this work is its contrast with traditional "passive" unsupervised or model-based learning methods. Instead of tasking the model with learning a potentially overly complex or intractable representation of the environment (e.g., full state reconstruction or dynamics modeling, which is difficult under partial observability), this approach leverages the agent's ability to exert control. By learning to actively control local and internal features, the agent develops a representation that is inherently grounded in its interaction with the environment, complementing the primary goal of learning an optimal control policy.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach is novel and presents a compelling alternative to traditional unsupervised pre-training or multi-task learning for RL. The idea of using "pseudo-control" tasks as the auxiliary objective is particularly interesting, as it directly utilizes the agent's capacity for action to shape its representation learning.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow for readers familiar with deep reinforcement learning. The high-level intuition behind the auxiliary tasks is explained effectively.
*   **Methodology:** The experimental methodology is sound. For instance, the hyperparameter search accounts for the potential differences between the A3C baseline and the proposed UNREAL agent by reporting the top-3 performing runs for each. This provides a fair basis for comparison, assuming the explored hyperparameter ranges were sufficiently broad for both methods.
*   **Results:** The empirical results are very strong, demonstrating a clear and significant improvement in both final performance and, crucially, data efficiency across a range of challenging tasks.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Lack of Technical Detail in the Main Text:** While the high-level explanations are effective for building intuition, the paper would benefit from additional low-level detail in the main body. For example, the specific loss function for Pixel Control (L_PC) is a key component of the method but is only defined in the appendix. Including such details in the main text would make the work more self-contained and easier to reproduce.
*   **Limited Analysis of Auxiliary Task Effects:** A significant weakness of the paper is the limited experimental analysis of *how* the auxiliary tasks contribute to the performance gains, beyond showing the final score improvements. The paper convincingly demonstrates that they work, but less so *why* they work.
    *   For instance, the Pixel Control and Feature Control tasks appear to have the most substantial impact. In the Labyrinth environment, the A3C+PC agent alone outperforms all other baselines except for the full UNREAL agent. It would have been valuable to investigate this component in more depth.
    *   Future work should include a more thorough ablation study or qualitative analysis. For example, analyzing the learned representations (e.g., via t-SNE) or examining the policies learned for the auxiliary control tasks themselves could provide valuable insights into the mechanisms behind the performance improvement. This would strengthen the claim that the agent is learning a more robust representation of its environment.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel idea, clear motivation, and impressive results. The proposed method of using auxiliary control tasks is an important contribution to the field of deep reinforcement learning. The main area for improvement lies in providing a deeper analysis of the effects of these tasks to better understand the underlying reasons for their success.
