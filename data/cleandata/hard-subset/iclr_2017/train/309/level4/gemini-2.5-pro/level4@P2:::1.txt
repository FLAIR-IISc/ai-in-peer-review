### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, a novel architecture that significantly enhances deep reinforcement learning by incorporating auxiliary tasks to accelerate and stabilize learning. The core contribution is to augment a strong asynchronous actor-critic agent (A3C) with a set of self-supervised objectives that are learned in parallel from a single stream of experience. These auxiliary tasks are intelligently designed to serve two purposes: 1) developing a rich and general representation of the environment through auxiliary control tasks (e.g., maximizing pixel changes or internal feature activations), and 2) focusing this representation on reward-relevant features through an auxiliary reward prediction task.

A key architectural insight is the combination of on-policy learning for the main policy with efficient off-policy learning (using Q-learning from an experience replay buffer) for the auxiliary tasks. This hybrid approach allows the agent to learn a robust representation from dense, self-generated signals, even when the primary reward is sparse. The authors validate their approach with extensive experiments on the 57-game Atari suite and a new, visually complex 3D first-person environment called Labyrinth. The results are impressive, demonstrating that the UNREAL agent achieves state-of-the-art performance, substantially outperforming A3C in final score, data efficiency (up to a 10x speedup in learning), and robustness to hyperparameter settings.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Significant Performance and Efficiency Gains:** The paper presents a novel and well-motivated architecture that achieves state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks (Atari) and a challenging new 3D environment (Labyrinth). The improvements in learning speed and data efficiency are particularly compelling and represent a significant advance for deep RL.

2.  **Novel Combination of Auxiliary Tasks and Learning Methods:** The paper's primary contribution lies in its specific formulation and successful integration of auxiliary tasks. The idea of using RL itself to solve auxiliary *control* tasks is a powerful concept. Furthermore, the hybrid architecture that combines on-policy A3C for the main task with off-policy Q-learning and experience replay for the auxiliary tasks is an elegant and effective solution for maximizing data utilization.

### Supporting Arguments

The claims made in the paper are strongly supported by a thorough and rigorous empirical evaluation. The authors not only demonstrate superior performance over a strong A3C baseline but also conduct valuable ablation studies (Figure 3) that isolate the contribution of each auxiliary component (pixel control, reward prediction, value replay). This analysis clearly shows that each element contributes positively, and their combination yields the best results.

Furthermore, the paper provides a crucial comparison against more traditional unsupervised learning methods. In Section 4.1.1, the authors show that their auxiliary *control* task (pixel control) is more effective than auxiliary *prediction* (predicting pixel changes) or *reconstruction* (auto-encoding the input). This result validates their core hypothesis that learning to control features of the sensorimotor stream is a more powerful representation learning objective for an RL agent than simply reconstructing them.

While the general idea of using auxiliary tasks to aid representation learning is not new, this paper provides a concrete, well-engineered, and highly effective instantiation of this principle for deep RL agents. The introduction and evaluation on the Labyrinth environment, which features richer visuals and more complex physics than many existing RL platforms, is also a valuable contribution to the community. The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow for readers familiar with deep reinforcement learning.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The following points are intended to provide constructive feedback for improving the paper and are not central to my decision to accept.

*   **Computational Cost:** The proposed UNREAL architecture adds several components (deconvolutional heads, a reward prediction network, and an experience replay buffer) to the A3C baseline. Could you comment on the increase in computational cost, both in terms of memory footprint and training time per step, compared to the A3C agent? This information would be valuable for researchers and practitioners looking to apply this method.

*   **Generality of Auxiliary Tasks:** The paper focuses on pixel and feature control as the primary auxiliary control tasks. Have you explored other forms of control, such as controlling game-specific features (e.g., game score, ammo count if available) or other statistical properties of the observation? It would be interesting to understand the generality of this control-based approach beyond low-level visual features.

*   **Code and Environment Release:** The UNREAL agent involves several interacting components and hyperparameters (e.g., the loss weights λ). To facilitate reproducibility and encourage further research building on this excellent work, are there plans to release the source code for the agent and the Labyrinth environment?
