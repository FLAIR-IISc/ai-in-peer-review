This paper proposes training reinforcement learning (RL) agents to simultaneously perform several auxiliary tasks, positing that this multi-task approach helps the agent learn more robust and useful features.

The authors introduce two novel pseudo-control tasks: maximizing changes in pixel intensity (pixel control) and maximizing the activation of specific units in a hidden layer (feature control). Additionally, they propose a supervised regression task: predicting the immediate reward from a recent sequence of observations (reward prediction). To train the reward prediction task effectively, the authors employ a skewed sampling strategy from an experience replay buffer, balancing the training data to ensure a 50% chance of sampling sequences that lead to a reward.

The resulting agent, UNREAL, demonstrates significant performance improvements on challenging discrete-action, continuous-state RL tasks, achieving baseline performance levels with as little as one-tenth of the training experience.

This work presents a compelling alternative to traditional "passive" unsupervised or model-based learning approaches for feature learning. Instead of requiring the agent to learn a potentially irrelevant representation of the entire input (as in some unsupervised methods) or to model complex, possibly intractable dynamics under partial observability (as in model-based methods), this approach focuses the agent on learning to control specific, local features of its environment and internal state. I find the approach to be novel and insightful. It cleverly leverages the agent's inherent ability to exert control over its environment as a powerful signal for representation learning, complementing the primary goal of learning an optimal policy.

The paper is generally clear and easily understandable for readers familiar with Deep RL. However, the proposed auxiliary tasks are explained at a high level. While this is effective for conveying the core intuition, the paper would benefit from including more low-level details in the main text. For instance, the specific loss function for pixel control (L_PC) should be explicitly defined and discussed before the appendix.

The experimental methodology is sound. One might be concerned that the optimal hyperparameter distributions could differ significantly between the A3C baseline and the proposed UNREAL agent. However, the authors' approach of reporting the performance of the top-3 hyperparameter settings helps to mitigate this concern. Assuming that the optimal parameters for both A3C and UNREAL lie within the searched intervals, this evaluation method provides confidence that the results represent the best achievable performance for each respective method.

A minor weakness of the paper—or perhaps more accurately, a direction for crucial future work—is the limited experimental analysis of *how* the auxiliary tasks contribute to the agent's success, beyond their clear and significant impact on final performance. For example, the ablation studies suggest that pixel and feature control have the most substantial impact; in the Labyrinth environment, A3C augmented with just pixel control (A3C+PC) outperforms all other baselines. It would be highly valuable to investigate this component more deeply. This could involve studying the control tasks in isolation, analyzing the learned representations more directly, or evaluating their effects through metrics other than just the final RL task score.
