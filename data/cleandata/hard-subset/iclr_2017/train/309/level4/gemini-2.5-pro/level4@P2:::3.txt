### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel deep reinforcement learning agent, termed UNREAL, which significantly enhances learning speed and final performance by incorporating auxiliary tasks alongside the main goal of maximizing extrinsic reward. The core contribution is to move beyond traditional passive unsupervised learning (e.g., input reconstruction) and instead use the agent's control capabilities to actively learn a rich representation of the environment. The authors propose two main classes of auxiliary tasks: 1) auxiliary control tasks, where the agent learns policies to maximize local changes in pixel intensity (pixel control) or activations of its own hidden units (feature control), and 2) an auxiliary reward prediction task, which predicts the immediate future reward from a short sequence of observations. These tasks are trained efficiently in parallel with the primary policy using an off-policy experience replay mechanism, which allows for biased sampling to better handle sparse rewards. The resulting UNREAL agent, built upon an A3C foundation, achieves state-of-the-art results on the 57-game Atari suite and demonstrates a remarkable 10x improvement in learning speed on a challenging, visually rich 3D Labyrinth environment.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper with a novel and well-motivated approach, supported by compelling empirical results. The key reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Novel Conceptual Contribution:** The paper presents an elegant and intuitive idea: framing representation learning as a set of active *control* problems rather than passive prediction or reconstruction tasks. This is a powerful alternative to traditional unsupervised auxiliary objectives that is fundamentally well-suited for reinforcement learning agents.
2.  **Significant Empirical Performance:** The proposed method yields substantial improvements over a very strong baseline (A3C) across two complex and distinct domains (Atari and 3D Labyrinth). The reported 10x speedup in learning and dramatic increase in final performance and hyperparameter robustness provide convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's main strength lies in its conceptual shift. Instead of forcing an agent to learn a potentially complex and partially irrelevant world model or to reconstruct high-dimensional observations, the auxiliary control tasks encourage the agent to learn about cause-and-effect in its environment. Learning policies to directly influence pixel values or internal feature activations intrinsically pushes the model to discover controllable aspects of its surroundings, which is a far more relevant and targeted objective for a control agent than simple reconstruction. This approach cleverly sidesteps issues with partial observability that can make traditional model-learning difficult, while still promoting the development of robust, general-purpose features.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and persuasive. The Labyrinth environment serves as an excellent testbed, highlighting the method's effectiveness in visually complex, 3D settings. The results are not marginal; UNREAL more than doubles the performance of A3C in this domain. The 10x speedup to reach the A3C baseline's peak performance is a particularly important result, demonstrating a significant improvement in data efficiency. The ablation studies in Figure 3 clearly demonstrate that each auxiliary component contributes positively to the final performance, validating the overall architecture design. Furthermore, the experiments on Atari show that the method's benefits generalize beyond 3D navigation, establishing a new state-of-the-art on this widely-used benchmark.

The experimental methodology is sound. The authors perform a thorough hyperparameter search for both the baseline and the proposed model. By reporting the performance of the top-performing runs (e.g., top 3 in Figure 3), they provide a fair comparison that mitigates the risk of having simply found a better hyperparameter region for their own method. The analysis of robustness to hyperparameter choice is also a welcome and important contribution, showing that the auxiliary tasks not only improve peak performance but also make the learning process more stable.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical factors in my decision.

*   **Deeper Analysis of Learned Representations:** While the performance improvements are compelling, the paper would be further strengthened by a deeper analysis of *why* these auxiliary tasks are so effective. What has the agent learned by trying to control pixels or internal features? For instance, visualizations of the learned pixel control policies for different grid cells could provide insight into how the agent learns to interact with its environment. Does it learn to focus on controllable objects, its own hands/gadgets, or specific parts of the scene? This would add a valuable qualitative dimension to the strong quantitative results.
*   **The Power of Pixel Control:** The ablation study in Figure 3 suggests that pixel control provides a very significant performance boost on its own, outperforming other individual components on Labyrinth. This component seems particularly powerful and might warrant a more focused investigation to better understand its properties. Have the authors considered any failure modes? For example, in an environment with many irrelevant but easily controllable visual elements (like flickering lights), could the pixel control task distract the agent from the main objective?
*   **Clarity on Loss Formulation:** For improved readability, it would be helpful to briefly define the auxiliary loss components, such as the n-step Q-learning loss for pixel control ($L_Q^{(c)}$), in the main body of the paper before they are combined in the final loss function in Equation 2. While these details are available in the appendix and Section 3.1, a more explicit formulation in Section 3.4 would make the paper more self-contained.

**Question for the authors:**
Could you comment on the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks? While the UNREAL agent is clearly more data-efficient (in terms of environment steps), how does it compare to the A3C baseline in terms of wall-clock time or computational resources (FLOPs) required per training step?
