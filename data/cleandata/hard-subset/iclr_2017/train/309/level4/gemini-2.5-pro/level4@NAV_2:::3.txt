This paper introduces a novel architecture for deep reinforcement learning agents that significantly enhances representation learning by incorporating auxiliary tasks alongside the main goal of maximizing extrinsic reward. The authors propose learning off-policy value functions for a set of self-supervised, auxiliary control tasks, such as maximizing pixel changes (pixel control) or activating specific neural network features (feature control). Additionally, an auxiliary reward prediction task is introduced, which is trained on a replay buffer with a skewed sampling strategy to better handle sparse rewards. The resulting agent, named UNREAL, is shown to achieve state-of-the-art performance on the 57-game Atari suite and a challenging 3D Labyrinth environment, demonstrating substantial improvements in final performance, learning speed, and hyperparameter robustness over a strong A3C baseline.

I recommend **Accepting** this paper. The primary reasons are:
1.  The work presents a novel and well-motivated approach for representation learning in RL that shifts from passive observation (like reconstruction) to active control, which is highly relevant for an agent-based framework.
2.  The claims are substantiated by extensive and compelling empirical results across multiple complex domains, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance and a dramatic increase in data efficiency.

**Supporting Arguments:**
The core idea of using auxiliary tasks to shape an agent's representation is powerful and well-motivated. The paper makes a compelling case against traditional unsupervised learning objectives (e.g., auto-encoding), which may force the agent to model irrelevant aspects of the environment. In contrast, the proposed auxiliary control tasks encourage the agent to learn about the controllable aspects of its environment—an understanding that is intrinsically useful for any goal-directed task. This "active" approach to self-supervision is a significant conceptual contribution.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and provides strong support for the paper's claims. The UNREAL agent not only achieves a new state-of-the-art on the challenging Atari benchmark (averaging 880% human performance) but also shows remarkable results in the visually rich 3D Labyrinth environment. The reported 10x mean speedup in learning on Labyrinth is a testament to the data efficiency of the proposed method. Furthermore, the authors' methodology is sound; they perform a robust hyperparameter search and demonstrate that the UNREAL agent is less sensitive to hyperparameter settings than the A3C baseline, which is a valuable practical contribution. The ablation studies clearly show the positive contribution of each auxiliary component, reinforcing the overall design of the agent.

**Additional Feedback:**
The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical for my decision.
*   The paper does an excellent job of conveying the high-level intuition behind the auxiliary tasks. However, the manuscript could be made more self-contained by moving some key implementation details from the appendix to the main text. For instance, a more explicit definition of the auxiliary control loss (`L_PC`) in Section 3.4 would be beneficial for the reader.
*   The performance improvements are very impressive, particularly from the pixel and feature control tasks. The paper would be even stronger with more analysis into *why* these tasks are so effective. While the main results focus on extrinsic reward, some qualitative analysis—for example, visualizing the behaviors learned by the pixel control policies—could provide fascinating insights into the representations being learned.
*   The results in Figure 4 suggest that pixel control alone accounts for a very large portion of the performance gain in Labyrinth. This is a very interesting finding and perhaps warrants a bit more discussion in the main text. It highlights the power of this specific auxiliary task and could inspire a great deal of future work.

**Questions for the Authors:**
To help me better understand the work, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:
*   The ablation study on Labyrinth (Figure 3) shows that the reward prediction (RP) and value replay (VR) tasks provide a smaller, though still positive, contribution compared to pixel control (PC). In your view, is this because control is fundamentally a better learning signal in these environments, or is it a matter of tuning the relative importance (the `λ` weights) of these tasks?
*   Have you considered the potential failure modes of pixel control? For instance, in an environment with significant stochasticity or irrelevant dynamic textures (like flickering lights), could the agent be distracted from the main task by trying to control these sources of pixel change?
