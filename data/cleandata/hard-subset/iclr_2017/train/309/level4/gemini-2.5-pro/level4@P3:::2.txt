### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces UNREAL, a deep reinforcement learning agent designed to improve data efficiency and overall performance by learning richer representations. The core idea is to augment the standard reinforcement learning objective (maximizing extrinsic reward) with a suite of auxiliary tasks that provide additional learning signals. These auxiliary tasks are cleverly designed to be domain-independent and are learned simultaneously from a single stream of experience. They include: 1) auxiliary control tasks, where the agent learns separate off-policy value functions to control features of its sensory input (e.g., maximizing pixel changes) or its own internal representations; 2) an auxiliary reward prediction task, which helps focus the representation on reward-relevant events, especially in sparse-reward settings; and 3) value function replay, which adds extra value-learning updates from replayed experience. These tasks are integrated into a state-of-the-art asynchronous actor-critic (A3C) agent, where they share a common visual and recurrent network, thereby shaping the learned features. The authors provide extensive empirical validation, showing that the UNREAL agent dramatically outperforms A3C on 57 Atari games and achieves a new state-of-the-art. Furthermore, on a new, visually complex 3D environment called Labyrinth, the agent demonstrates a remarkable 10x mean speedup in learning time and more than doubles the final performance of the baseline.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a clear accept. The paper is well-written, the proposed method is well-motivated and technically sound, and the results are highly significant. The key reasons for this recommendation are:
1.  **Novel and Effective Framework:** The paper presents a novel and compelling framework for accelerating representation learning in RL. The idea of using auxiliary *control* tasks, rather than just passive prediction or reconstruction, is a powerful concept that encourages the agent to learn about the causal structure of its environment.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The claims are supported by extensive and convincing experiments on two challenging domains. The reported improvements in both data efficiency (10x speedup) and final performance over a very strong baseline (A3C) are substantial and demonstrate the practical value of the approach.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this work lies in its thoughtful combination of several complementary learning objectives. While unsupervised learning has been proposed as a way to pre-train or assist RL agents, the UNREAL agent goes a step further by framing representation learning as a set of active control problems. Forcing the agent to learn policies that maximize change in its perceptual stream (pixel control) encourages the discovery of features related to object interaction and environmental dynamics, which are broadly useful for any subsequent goal.

This general-purpose learning is balanced by the inclusion of reward-focused auxiliary tasks. The reward prediction and value replay components help to mitigate the problem of sparse rewards by ensuring the agent's representation remains sensitive to the primary objective. The use of experience replay with skewed sampling for these tasks is a practical and effective mechanism for increasing the learning signal from rare but important rewarding events.

The empirical results are outstanding. The introduction of the new Labyrinth environment, with its rich visuals and complex navigation and interaction tasks, is a valuable contribution in itself. The fact that the UNREAL agent not only learns much faster but also achieves superhuman performance (averaging 87% expert human performance vs. A3C's 54%) on this difficult domain is a strong testament to the method's effectiveness. The consistent and significant gains across the 57-game Atari suite further prove that this is not a domain-specific trick but a general enhancement to deep RL agents. The ablation studies convincingly demonstrate that each auxiliary component contributes positively to the final performance.

One minor point of critique is that the "feature control" concept, while intriguing, is not as thoroughly validated as the other components. The single experiment shown is described as preliminary and shows only a slight initial benefit. Given that the target features are non-stationary during training, this task could introduce stability challenges, a point that warrants further investigation. However, this is a direction for future work and does not detract from the impressive results achieved with the other auxiliary tasks.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Clarity on Multiple Policies:** In the abstract and introduction, it could be made clearer that the agent learns *separate* policies for each auxiliary control task. The phrase "learns separate policies for maximising many other pseudo-reward functions" could be misinterpreted as learning a single policy for a combined pseudo-reward. Clarifying this upfront would prevent potential confusion.
*   **Computational Cost:** A brief discussion of the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks would be valuable. How much does training time increase in terms of wall-clock time per environment step? Which components are the most computationally intensive?
*   **Technical Precision on Q-Learning:** It is worth clarifying that the off-policy auxiliary tasks are trained with n-step returns, which is a departure from the standard 1-step updates in Q-learning. While this is a known and effective technique, explicitly stating it would improve technical precision.
*   **Minor Corrections:**
    *   In Section 2, the policy gradient loss is presented as a quantity to be maximized, but is assigned to `L_pi`, suggesting minimization. Please clarify the sign convention.
    *   In Equation 1, λ_C should be inside the summation.
    *   Just below Equation 1, the return for auxiliary reward should likely be `r(c)_(t+k)`.
    *   The loss `L_PC` is referenced in the text around Equation 2 but is not explicitly defined in the equation itself.
    *   Please add references in the text to Figure 1(d) (in Section 3.3) and Figure 2.
    *   The legend of Figure 3 (bottom row) contains percentages specific to the Labyrinth results, which may be confusing in the context of the Atari plot.
    *   There are a few minor typos and incorrect figure references noted in the draft review that should be corrected (e.g., "Figure 3 (right) shows..." should be "top left"; "is shared" should be "are shared").

### **Questions for the Authors**

*   The weights for the auxiliary loss components (`λ_VR`, `λ_PC`, `λ_RP`) appear to have been chosen based on prior experiments or heuristics. Were these treated as hyperparameters? Could you comment on the sensitivity of the agent's performance to these weights?
*   The learning curves in many experiments are still trending upward at the end of training. Did you consider running the best-performing agents for longer to see if the performance gap between UNREAL and the baseline continues to widen?
*   The ablation study on Labyrinth was very insightful. Have you performed similar ablations on the Atari suite to see if the relative contributions of the auxiliary tasks are consistent across domains?
