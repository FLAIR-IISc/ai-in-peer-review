This paper proposes training reinforcement learning (RL) agents with auxiliary tasks, based on the hypothesis that this will lead to the development of more effective feature representations. Specifically, the authors introduce two auxiliary pseudo-control tasks: maximizing changes in pixel intensity and controlling the activations of latent features. Additionally, they propose a supervised regression task: predicting immediate future rewards. This reward prediction task is trained offline using a skewed sampling strategy from an experience replay buffer to balance the ratio of rewarding to non-rewarding events to approximately 1:1.

The resulting agents demonstrate significantly improved performance on discrete-action, continuous-state RL tasks. Notably, they achieve baseline performance levels using only one-tenth of the training iterations, indicating a substantial improvement in data efficiency.

The approach offers a compelling contrast to traditional "passive" methods like unsupervised pre-training or model-based learning. Rather than forcing the agent to learn a potentially irrelevant representation of the input, or to model environment dynamics that may be intractable due to partial observability, this work leverages the agent's ability to exert control. By learning to control local and internal features, the agent develops representations that are inherently complementary to the primary goal of learning an optimal control policy. Overall, the approach is novel and presents an interesting alternative to standard unsupervised learning by actively leveraging an agent's capacity for control over its environment.

While the proposed tasks are explained at a high level that effectively conveys the intuition, the paper would benefit from including more low-level implementation details in the main text. For instance, the specific loss function for pixel control (L_PC) should be explicitly defined before the appendix to provide a more complete picture of the method. Despite this, the paper is generally clear and should be easily understood by readers familiar with Deep Reinforcement Learning.

The experimental methodology is sound. While the optimal hyperparameter distributions may differ between the A3C baseline and the proposed UNREAL agent, the authors' approach of measuring the performance of the top-3 hyperparameter settings for each method is a fair way to conduct the comparison. This ensures that, assuming the optimal settings for both methods lie within the searched intervals, the results reflect the best achievable performance for each.

A primary weakness of the paper—or perhaps more accurately, a crucial direction for future work—is the limited experimental analysis of the auxiliary tasks beyond their very strong effect on final performance. For example, the pixel and feature control tasks appear to have the most significant impact; in the Labyrinth environment, A3C with pixel control alone (A3C+PC) outperforms all other ablations and baselines except the full UNREAL agent. It would have been valuable to investigate these components in greater isolation or depth, perhaps through analyses that measure more than just final performance on the primary RL task.
