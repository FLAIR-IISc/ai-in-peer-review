### **Summary**

This paper proposes a method for accelerating and improving the performance of deep reinforcement learning agents by training them on auxiliary tasks alongside the main control policy. The central hypothesis is that these auxiliary tasks encourage the learning of richer, more robust features, which in turn benefit the primary RL objective.

Specifically, the authors introduce two novel self-supervised pseudo-control tasks: maximizing change in pixel intensity (Pixel Control) and controlling the activations of specific latent features (Feature Control). Additionally, they incorporate a supervised regression task, Reward Prediction, where the agent predicts the immediate future reward. This third task is learned offline from a replay buffer, using a skewed sampling strategy to balance the prevalence of rewarding and non-rewarding experiences.

When combined with an A3C agent (termed UNREAL), this approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on a suite of discrete-action, continuous-space RL tasks (DeepMind Lab). Notably, the agent reaches baseline performance levels up to 10 times faster than the vanilla A3C agent.

### **Strengths**

The core contribution of this work is novel and insightful. It presents a compelling alternative to more traditional "passive" unsupervised learning methods for representation learning in RL, such as autoencoders or world models. Instead of forcing the model to learn a potentially useless representation of the input or to solve the possibly intractable task of modeling world dynamics (especially under partial observability), this approach leverages the agent's inherent ability to exert control over its environment. This concept of using control itself as a supervisory signal for learning features is a powerful and elegant idea that complements the main goal of learning an optimal control policy.

The experimental results are very strong, demonstrating a significant improvement in both sample efficiency and final performance. The fact that the UNREAL agent learns an order of magnitude faster is a substantial practical contribution.

The experimental methodology is sound. The authors fairly compare their UNREAL agent against a strong A3C baseline by conducting a hyperparameter search for both. While the optimal hyperparameter distributions might differ between A3C and UNREAL, the practice of measuring the top-3 performing runs helps ensure that the best configuration for each method is likely found within the explored range, making for a robust comparison.

### **Weaknesses**

While the paper is generally clear and accessible to readers familiar with Deep RL, the explanation of the auxiliary tasks is somewhat high-level in the main text. Providing more concrete details, for instance, by explicitly defining the Pixel Control loss function (L_PC) before the appendix, would enhance the self-containedness and clarity of the paper.

A primary weakness of the paper is the limited experimental analysis into the *effects* of the auxiliary tasks, beyond their significant impact on final performance. While the performance gains are impressive, the paper would be substantially strengthened by a more in-depth investigation into *why* and *how* these tasks help. For example, it is difficult to disentangle the individual contributions of each auxiliary task from the ablation studies provided. The results suggest that Pixel/Feature control has the most significant impact—in the Labyrinth environment, A3C with only Pixel Control (A3C+PC) outperforms nearly all other baselines except the full UNREAL agent. This finding is intriguing and warrants a more focused analysis, perhaps by studying the learned representations directly or evaluating the auxiliary policies in isolation. A deeper dive into what these auxiliary tasks are learning, separate from their utility to the final RL score, would be a valuable addition.

### **Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and interesting contribution to the field of deep reinforcement learning. The idea of using active control-based objectives as auxiliary tasks for representation learning is a significant one, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate its effectiveness. The primary area for improvement lies in providing a deeper analysis of the mechanisms behind the performance gains. Despite this, the paper is a clear accept.
