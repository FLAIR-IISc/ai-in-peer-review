### **Summary**

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), an agent that significantly advances the state-of-the-art in deep reinforcement learning. The core contribution is to augment a standard RL agent (A3C) with a set of auxiliary tasks designed to accelerate and stabilize the learning of a powerful shared representation. The authors propose two main classes of auxiliary objectives:

1.  **Auxiliary Control Tasks:** Instead of passively learning from the environment, the agent actively learns policies to control features of its sensory input and its own internal state. Specifically, it learns to maximize changes in pixel intensity in different regions of the input (pixel control) and to maximize the activations of its own hidden units (feature control). These tasks are learned off-policy using Q-learning on replayed experiences.

2.  **Auxiliary Reward and Value Tasks:** To combat reward sparsity, the agent is trained on two additional tasks. First, it learns to predict the immediate upcoming reward from a short sequence of observations. This supervised task is trained on a replay buffer where rewarding events are oversampled to be as frequent as non-rewarding ones. Second, additional value function regression is performed on replayed data to better exploit recent experience.

The UNREAL agent, which combines the on-policy A3C algorithm for the main task with these off-policy auxiliary tasks, achieves outstanding empirical results. On a challenging suite of 3D Labyrinth tasks, it achieves 87% of expert human performance, more than doubling the baseline A3C score, and learns up to 10 times faster. On the 57-game Atari benchmark, it achieves a mean score of 880% of human performance, substantially outperforming previous state-of-the-art methods.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Significant Conceptual Contribution:** The paper introduces a compelling and novel alternative to traditional unsupervised learning for representation learning in RL. The shift from passive objectives (e.g., reconstruction) to active control-based objectives is a powerful idea that naturally leverages an agent's ability to interact with its environment. This provides a new and effective methodology for learning useful features, especially when extrinsic rewards are sparse.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The claims are supported by an extensive and rigorous set of experiments on two very different and challenging domains. The performance improvements are not incremental but represent a substantial leap forward in both final performance and data efficiency. The thorough ablation studies and hyperparameter robustness analysis provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed components.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The primary strength of this work lies in its reframing of auxiliary task learning for RL. While prior work has explored unsupervised learning (e.g., input reconstruction) or model-based learning to improve representations, this paper convincingly argues that these "passive" objectives can be suboptimal. An agent does not merely observe its environment; it acts within it. The introduction of auxiliary *control* tasks aligns the representation learning objective with the agent's fundamental nature. By learning policies to control its perceptual stream (pixel control), the agent is forced to discover the causal structure of the environment and what aspects of it are under its influence. The empirical comparison in Figure 5, which shows that pixel control significantly outperforms both pixel prediction and reconstruction, provides strong support for this core hypothesis.

The empirical evaluation is exemplary. The introduction of the Labyrinth environment, with its rich visuals and complex 3D navigation and combat tasks, provides a challenging new benchmark that tests aspects of RL beyond what is captured by Atari. The results on both domains are remarkable. Achieving a 10x mean speedup to reach the baseline's peak performance on Labyrinth highlights a dramatic improvement in data efficiency, which is a critical problem in deep RL. Furthermore, the analysis of robustness to hyperparameters (Figure 3, right column) demonstrates that the proposed auxiliary tasks not only improve peak performance but also make the learning process more stable and less sensitive to tuning, which is of great practical importance. The ablation studies clearly disentangle the contributions of the different auxiliary tasks, showing that each provides a benefit and that they are complementary.

### **Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper**

The following points are suggestions for improvement and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Clarity on Loss Functions:** While the components of the UNREAL loss are described, the exact formulation of the auxiliary control loss (referred to as `L_PC`) is not explicitly defined in the main text until Equation 2, where it is defined as a sum of Q-learning losses. It would improve clarity to present the specific n-step Q-learning loss for a single control task `c` in Section 3.1 before generalizing to the sum over all tasks.

*   **Deeper Analysis of Learned Behaviors:** The paper provides a very strong quantitative analysis of performance improvements. However, a key weakness is the lack of qualitative analysis into *what* the auxiliary tasks cause the agent to learn. The pixel control task, in particular, is a major driver of the performance gains. It would be fascinating to see an analysis of the policies learned for these auxiliary tasks. For example, does the agent learn to shoot walls to cause pixel changes, or move towards dynamic objects? Such an analysis would provide deeper insight into why this approach is so effective and could inspire the design of even better auxiliary objectives.

*   **Feature Control as a Promising Direction:** The results for feature control are presented as preliminary but are very encouraging. This idea seems powerful as it is more general than pixel control and not tied to a specific input modality. Expanding on the motivation and potential of this approach, perhaps with more extensive experiments, could further strengthen the paper.

### **Questions for the Authors**

*   Could you provide more intuition or qualitative analysis on the behaviors learned by the pixel control policies? For instance, what kinds of actions does the agent learn to take to maximize pixel changes in different parts of the screen, and how might these micro-behaviors contribute to success in the main task (e.g., navigation or combat)?

*   The paper contrasts control-based objectives with predictive ones, showing control to be superior (Figure 5). Have you considered other control objectives beyond pixel changes and feature activations? What general principles might guide the design of effective auxiliary control tasks?

*   The preliminary results for feature control are very promising. Could you elaborate on why this was only tested on one level? Do you see this as a potentially more general and powerful alternative to pixel control in the long run, as it is less tied to the specific visual modality of the input?
