This paper proposes training reinforcement learning agents with auxiliary tasks to learn more effective feature representations. The authors introduce two novel pseudo-control tasks—controlling the change in pixel intensity and controlling the activation of latent features—as well as a supervised reward prediction task. The reward prediction task is trained offline using a skewed sampling of an experience replay buffer to ensure a 1:1 balance between rewarding and non-rewarding events.

The resulting agent, UNREAL, achieves significantly improved performance on RL tasks with discrete actions and continuous state spaces, reaching baseline performance levels with a 10-fold improvement in data efficiency.

A key insight of this work is its departure from traditional "passive" auxiliary objectives, such as unsupervised learning or model-based prediction. Instead of forcing the agent to learn a potentially useless representation or an impossibly complex world model (especially under partial observability), the proposed approach has the agent learn to control local and internal features. This active control objective proves to be a powerful complement to learning the main control policy. I find the approach novel and compelling, as it presents an interesting alternative to standard unsupervised learning by leveraging the agent's inherent ability to exert control over its environment.

The paper is generally clear and accessible to readers familiar with deep reinforcement learning. However, while the high-level descriptions of the auxiliary tasks are effective for building intuition, the paper would benefit from more low-level detail in the main text. For instance, the specific loss function for pixel control (L_PC) should be defined before the appendix.

The experimental methodology is sound. While the optimal hyperparameter distributions may differ between the A3C baseline and the UNREAL agent, the practice of reporting the average of the top-3 performing runs helps ensure a fair comparison. This approach makes it likely that the best-performing hyperparameters for each method were identified, assuming they fell within the searched ranges.

A primary weakness—or perhaps more accurately, a crucial direction for future work—is the limited analysis of *why* the auxiliary tasks are so effective beyond their impact on final performance. For example, the results on the Labyrinth environment show that A3C augmented with just pixel control (A3C+PC) outperforms all other baselines except for the full UNREAL agent. This suggests that the control-based tasks have the most impact, and it would have been valuable to see these components analyzed in greater depth, perhaps by measuring their effects on the learned representations or other metrics beyond task performance.
