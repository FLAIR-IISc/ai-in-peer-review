### Summary

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), an agent that enhances a standard deep reinforcement learning framework (A3C) with auxiliary tasks to accelerate and improve learning. The core idea is to leverage the rich data stream available to the agent for more than just maximizing the main extrinsic reward. The authors propose two main classes of auxiliary tasks: 1) "control tasks," where the agent learns off-policy value functions to control features of the environment (e.g., maximizing pixel changes in different regions of the screen or maximizing activations in its own network), and 2) "reward prediction tasks," where the agent predicts the immediate next reward from a short sequence of past observations, using a rebalanced replay buffer to focus on rare reward events. These tasks are trained in parallel with the main A3C policy, sharing a common representation but using different learning mechanisms (e.g., off-policy Q-learning from a replay buffer). The paper provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation on 57 Atari games and a new, visually rich 3D environment called Labyrinth. The results demonstrate that the UNREAL agent significantly outperforms the A3C baseline and prior state-of-the-art methods in terms of final performance, data efficiency (achieving a 10x speedup on Labyrinth), and robustness to hyperparameters.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Significant Empirical Contribution:** The paper presents a novel and well-engineered agent that achieves state-of-the-art results on the extensive Atari benchmark and demonstrates remarkable performance and data efficiency on the challenging new 3D Labyrinth environment. The scale of the improvement is substantial.
2.  **Effective Integration of Auxiliary Tasks:** The method provides a compelling framework for combining on-policy policy-gradient learning for the main task with off-policy value-based learning for auxiliary tasks. The specific tasks proposed—pixel/feature control and reward prediction—are well-motivated and shown to be highly effective at shaping a useful representation, especially in sparse reward settings.

### Supporting Arguments

The key strength of this work lies in its thorough and convincing demonstration of how carefully chosen auxiliary tasks can dramatically improve deep RL agents. While the general idea that auxiliary tasks can aid representation learning is not new, this paper provides a powerful and well-executed instantiation of this concept in a modern deep RL context.

The architectural design is clever. It combines the stability and effectiveness of on-policy A3C for the main policy with the data efficiency of off-policy Q-learning from a replay buffer for the auxiliary tasks. This allows the shared network layers to benefit from intensive training on recent experiences without destabilizing the main policy. The ablation studies in Figure 3 clearly show that each component (Pixel Control, Reward Prediction, Value Replay) contributes positively to the final performance, with their combination yielding the best results.

The choice of auxiliary tasks is a key contribution. Moving beyond simple reconstruction (which the authors show can even hurt final performance), the proposed "pixel control" tasks encourage the agent to learn a representation of what is controllable in its environment. This is a more active form of unsupervised learning that aligns well with the goals of a reinforcement learning agent. Furthermore, the reward prediction task, coupled with skewed replay for non-zero rewards, is an elegant solution to the temporal credit assignment problem in sparse reward environments, as it forces the network to become sensitive to reward-predicting states.

The empirical results are outstanding. On Atari, UNREAL achieves an average of 880% human performance, a significant leap over previous methods. More impressively, on the visually complex Labyrinth environment, the agent not only achieves super-human performance on some levels but does so with an average 10x speedup in learning time compared to the A3C baseline. This dramatic improvement in data efficiency is a crucial step for making deep RL practical for more complex problems. The paper also validates that these gains are not the result of fine-tuning, but are robust across a wide range of hyperparameters (Figure 3, right column).

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The paper is well-written and the experiments are comprehensive. The following points are intended to help improve the final version and clarify some practical aspects of the work.

*   The UNREAL architecture integrates several components and loss functions. Could you comment on the computational overhead (in terms of wall-clock time or FLOPs) introduced by these auxiliary tasks compared to the A3C baseline? A brief discussion of the trade-off between computational cost and performance gain would be very valuable for practitioners.
*   The overall architecture is quite complex. To facilitate reproducibility and encourage further research building on your excellent work, are you planning to release the source code for the UNREAL agent and the Labyrinth environment?
*   While the paper demonstrates robustness to learning rate and entropy cost, the weights for the auxiliary loss components (λVR, λPC, λRP) also seem to be important hyperparameters. Could you provide some intuition or discussion on how these were set or how sensitive the agent is to their values?
