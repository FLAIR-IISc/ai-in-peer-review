This paper investigates training reinforcement learning (RL) agents with auxiliary tasks to encourage the development of more robust feature representations. The authors introduce two novel pseudo-control tasks – controlling pixel intensity changes and controlling the activation of latent features – alongside a supervised regression task predicting immediate reward based on action sequences. This reward prediction task is trained offline using a biased sampling strategy from the experience replay buffer to achieve a balanced presentation of reward and non-reward events (approximately 50/50).

The resulting agents demonstrate substantial performance gains on discrete-action, continuous-space RL environments, achieving baseline performance levels in significantly fewer training iterations (10x reduction). This work offers a compelling alternative to traditional passive unsupervised or model-based learning approaches. Rather than learning potentially irrelevant input representations or tackling the challenging task-modelling problem inherent in partially observable environments, the proposed method leverages the agent’s ability to actively control local and internal environmental features to enhance policy learning. 

The approach is both novel and presents a promising direction for research, offering a distinct advantage over standard unsupervised learning techniques by capitalizing on the agent’s control capabilities. While the tasks are initially presented at a high level for intuitive understanding, greater detail regarding the loss function for pixel control (L_PC) earlier in the paper – rather than solely in the appendix – would be beneficial. Overall, the paper is well-written and accessible to readers with a background in Deep RL.

The methodology appears robust. The approach of evaluating top-3 hyperparameter configurations mitigates potential distribution shifts between algorithms (A3C and UNREAL) and ensures identification of near-optimal parameters within the explored search space.

A potential limitation, or an area for future work given space constraints, is the limited analysis of the auxiliary tasks’ individual effects beyond their observed performance improvements. Specifically, the disproportionate impact of pixel and feature control – exemplified by A3C+PC being a leading performer in the Labyrinth environment – warrants further investigation. Exploring these tasks in isolation or with more detailed analysis, and measuring impacts beyond standard RL performance metrics, would be a valuable extension of this work.
