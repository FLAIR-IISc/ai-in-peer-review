This paper presents a compelling approach to improving feature learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the primary policy optimization with auxiliary tasks. These tasks – control (state space modification), immediate reward prediction, and value function replay – are designed to shape the learned features through a shared CNN+LSTM network. The experimental results on Atari and Labyrinth demonstrate significant improvements in data efficiency compared to A3C, indicating a successful contribution. Overall, the paper is well-written, the ideas are sound, and the results are convincing, leading me to recommend acceptance. 

I have a few suggestions for improvement, none of which are critical:

*   It would be beneficial to discuss the computational overhead introduced by optimizing the auxiliary tasks. Quantifying the impact on training speed and identifying the most computationally expensive components would strengthen the analysis.
*   The abstract and introduction could be clarified to explicitly state that the agent learns distinct policies for each task. The current phrasing ("maximises many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously") initially suggests a single, unified policy, a misconception I resolved only upon reaching equation 1.
*   The empirical validation of the “feature control” idea (Fig. 5) is currently weak. The observed initial improvement is not particularly convincing, and I am concerned about potential stability or convergence issues arising from the dynamic nature of the task during learning as the extracted features evolve.
*   Given the authors’ observation that agent performance continues to improve, extending the training duration for the best-performing agents could reveal further gains.
*   The weighting of the auxiliary tasks (lambda\*) was fixed at 1. Exploring these values as hyperparameters and presenting the results of such an optimization would be valuable.
*   Please acknowledge that the auxiliary tasks are not trained using traditional Q-Learning due to their off-policy training with multi-step empirical rewards, as discussed in the OpenReview comments.
