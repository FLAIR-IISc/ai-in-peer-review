This paper discusses improving feature learning in deep reinforcement learning, specifically focusing on the optimization problem of the main policy (domain-independent) with the help of auxiliary tasks. These tasks are related to control (learning other policies that try to maximize the state space, i.e., the pixels here), immediate reward prediction, and value function replay. Except for the latter, these auxiliary tasks are only used to help shape the feature (by sharing a CNN+LSTM feature extraction network). Experiments show the benefits of this approach on Atari and labyrinth problems, especially the much better data efficiency compared to A3C.

The paper is well-written, the ideas are solid, and the results are quite convincing, so it's a clear acceptance for me. At a high level, I only have a few things to say, none of which are major concerns:

- I believe you should say something about the additional computational cost of optimizing these auxiliary tasks. How much do you lose in terms of training speed? What are the most expensive components?
- If possible, please try to clarify in the abstract/introduction that the agent is learning different policies for each task. When I read in the abstract that the agent "also maximizes several other pseudo-reward tasks simultaneously by reinforcement learning," my first understanding was that it learned a single policy to optimize all the rewards simultaneously, and I only realized my mistake when reaching equation 1.
- The idea of "feature control" is not empirically valid (the initial experiment in Figure 5 is far from convincing because it initially only seems to help a little). I like that idea, but I am concerned about the fact that the task is changing during learning, as the extracted features are being modified. There may be issues of stability/convergence here.
- Since, as you mentioned, "the performance of our agents is still continuously improving," why not continue to see how far they go? (At least the best ones)
- Why aren't the auxiliary tasks weighted parameters (lambda_*) for optimization? Were there any experiments to validate that using 1 was a good choice?
- Please mention the fact that the auxiliary tasks are not trained with "true" Q-learning because they are trained off-policy with more than one step of empirical rewards (as discussed in the OpenReview comments).
