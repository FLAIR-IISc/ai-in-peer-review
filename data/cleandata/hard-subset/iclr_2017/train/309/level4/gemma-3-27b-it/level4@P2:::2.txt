This paper introduces a method for improving feature learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the primary policy optimization with auxiliary tasks focused on control (maximizing state modification, e.g., pixel changes), immediate reward prediction, and value function replay. These auxiliary tasks are implemented through separate policies sharing the core CNN+LSTM feature extraction network, rather than a single policy optimizing all rewards simultaneously. The experiments demonstrate performance gains in terms of data efficiency and final performance on both Atari and Labyrinth environments, surpassing the A3C baseline.

**Decision:** Accept. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and supported by compelling empirical results demonstrating significant improvements in both sample efficiency and overall performance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper addresses a crucial challenge in deep reinforcement learning – effective feature learning, particularly in environments with sparse rewards. The core idea of leveraging auxiliary tasks to shape the representation learned by the agent is well-grounded in the literature on unsupervised learning and intrinsic motivation. The implementation, using separate policies for each auxiliary task while sharing the representation, is a sensible and effective design choice. The results on both Atari and the more challenging Labyrinth environments are convincing, with substantial improvements in data efficiency and final performance compared to the strong A3C baseline. The ablation studies further support the contribution of each auxiliary task. The paper is also well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and experimental setup.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

While the results are strong, addressing the following points would further strengthen the paper:

*   **Computational Cost:** Quantify the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks. A discussion of the trade-off between improved sample efficiency and increased computational cost would be valuable. Which auxiliary tasks contribute most to this cost?
*   **Clarify Abstract/Introduction:** Rephrase the abstract and introduction to more explicitly state that separate policies are learned for each task. The current wording could lead readers to initially assume a single, multi-objective policy.
*   **Feature Control Stability:** The "feature control" task's stability during learning is a concern. While preliminary results are shown, more thorough empirical validation and a discussion of potential convergence issues are warranted. Exploring techniques to stabilize learning for this task (e.g., regularization, curriculum learning) would be beneficial.
*   **Extended Training:** Given that agent performance is still improving, consider extending the training runs for the best-performing models to better understand their ultimate potential.
*   **Hyperparameter Optimization:** Explain the rationale for the fixed weighting (λ\*) assigned to the auxiliary tasks. Were any experiments performed to optimize these weights, or were they determined through intuition or preliminary trials?
*   **Clarify Q-Learning Implementation:** Explicitly state that the auxiliary tasks are not trained with “true” Q-Learning, and are instead trained off-policy using n-step empirical rewards. (This point has been raised in OpenReview comments).
*   **Minor Edits:** Address the minor typographical errors and inconsistencies (detailed in the original review, e.g., equation formatting, figure references, language clarity). Specifically, clarify the legend of Figure 3 regarding the percentage values.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost associated with each auxiliary task (e.g., training time, memory usage)?
*   What strategies, if any, did you explore to stabilize the learning process for the "feature control" task?
*   Were any experiments conducted to optimize the weighting parameters (λ\*) for the auxiliary tasks? If so, what were the results?
*   Could you elaborate on the specifics of how the auxiliary tasks are trained off-policy with n-step empirical rewards, and how this differs from standard Q-Learning?
*   Regarding the Labyrinth experiments, what metrics beyond the final score were used to evaluate the robustness and generalization ability of the agents?
