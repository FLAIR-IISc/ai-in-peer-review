This work introduces a novel approach to reinforcement learning by augmenting standard RL training with auxiliary tasks designed to encourage the learning of robust and useful feature representations. The core idea is that, rather than passively learning representations or attempting full environment modeling, agents can benefit from actively learning to control aspects of their own internal states and the environment itself. 

The authors propose two “pseudo-control” tasks: controlling changes in pixel intensity (L<sub>PC</sub>) and controlling the activation of latent features. In addition, they introduce a supervised regression task – predicting immediate reward following a sequence of events – trained offline using a skewed sampling strategy from an experience replay buffer to balance positive and negative reward examples. This reward prediction task aims to provide a dense signal where the environment’s native reward is sparse.

The experimental results demonstrate that agents trained with these auxiliary tasks achieve significantly improved performance on discrete-action, continuous-space RL problems, reaching baseline performance in approximately ten times fewer iterations than standard RL algorithms. This improvement is particularly pronounced on challenging environments.

This work presents a compelling alternative to traditional unsupervised or model-based learning techniques. Existing approaches often involve learning representations that may be irrelevant to the ultimate control task or attempting to model the environment’s dynamics, which can be intractable due to partial observability. In contrast, learning to control local environment features and internal model states directly complements the learning of an optimal control policy, leveraging the agent’s inherent capacity for action. The proposed method is novel and offers a promising direction for research in representation learning for RL.

The paper is generally well-written and accessible to readers familiar with deep reinforcement learning. The high-level explanation of the proposed tasks effectively conveys the intuition behind the approach. However, the introduction of L<sub>PC</sub>, a key component, feels somewhat delayed; explicitly mentioning the loss function earlier in the paper (rather than relegating it to the appendix) would improve clarity. 

The methodology employed is sound. While acknowledging that optimal hyperparameters may differ between A3C and UNREAL, the authors mitigate this concern by reporting the top-3 performing hyperparameter configurations, ensuring the best-performing parameters for each algorithm are identified within the explored search space. This careful consideration of hyperparameter optimization strengthens the validity of the results.

A potential weakness of the paper, and perhaps more accurately, an area for future work, is the limited analysis of the individual contributions of the auxiliary tasks beyond their overall impact on RL performance. While the results clearly demonstrate the benefit of combining these tasks, further investigation into their specific effects would be valuable. For instance, the results from the Labyrinth environment suggest that pixel control (L<sub>PC</sub>) is particularly effective, with A3C+PC outperforming most other configurations (excluding UNREAL). A more focused analysis of L<sub>PC</sub> – perhaps in isolation, or with more detailed metrics beyond RL task performance – could offer further insights into its mechanisms and potential applications. Exploring metrics that directly assess the quality of the learned representations, or the degree of control achieved in the auxiliary tasks themselves, would enrich the understanding of how these tasks contribute to improved RL performance.
