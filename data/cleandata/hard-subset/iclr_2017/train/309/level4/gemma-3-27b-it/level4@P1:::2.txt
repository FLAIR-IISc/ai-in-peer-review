This paper introduces a method for improving feature learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the main policy's optimization problem with auxiliary tasks. These tasks involve learning separate policies for controlling different aspects of the environment (pixel changes), predicting immediate reward, and utilizing value function replay. Importantly, these auxiliary tasks primarily serve to shape the learned features, sharing a common CNN+LSTM feature extraction network with the main policy. The paper demonstrates the benefits of this approach on Atari and Labyrinth environments, showcasing improved data efficiency compared to A3C.

**Decision: Accept**

The approach is well-motivated and addresses a significant challenge in deep reinforcement learning – effective feature learning. The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating substantial improvements in both data efficiency and final performance across diverse environments.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles the crucial problem of learning robust and generalizable representations in reinforcement learning. While maximizing cumulative reward is the standard approach, the authors convincingly argue that leveraging the wealth of information present in the sensorimotor stream through auxiliary tasks can lead to superior learning. The proposed method, UNREAL, effectively integrates these auxiliary objectives into the learning process, resulting in significant performance gains. 

The experimental evaluation is thorough. The results on both Atari and the more complex Labyrinth environment demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Notably, the 10x speedup in learning observed in Labyrinth is particularly impressive, highlighting the practical benefits of the method. The ablation studies effectively isolate the contributions of different auxiliary tasks.  The robustness to hyperparameters is also a significant positive finding.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, here are some suggestions to further improve clarity and impact:

*   **Computational Cost:** Briefly discuss the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks. Quantifying the impact on training time would be valuable. Which auxiliary tasks are the most computationally expensive?
*   **Abstract/Introduction Clarity:**  The description of learning multiple policies could be clarified in the abstract and introduction. Currently, the phrasing suggests a single policy optimizing all rewards, which is a potential misinterpretation. Explicitly stating that separate policies are learned for each auxiliary task early on would improve understanding.
*   **Feature Control Validation:** The empirical validation of "feature control" appears limited. While the idea is intriguing, the preliminary results in Figure 5 are not strong enough to establish its effectiveness. A more comprehensive evaluation, potentially with a dedicated set of experiments, would strengthen the paper.  Consider discussing potential stability issues associated with modifying the feature extractor during learning.
*   **Continued Training:** Given the statement that "the performance of our agents is still steadily improving," consider extending the training runs, especially for the best-performing configurations, to explore the potential for further gains.
*   **Hyperparameter Optimization:**  Explain the rationale for using fixed weights (lambda values) for the auxiliary tasks. Were experiments conducted to optimize these weights? 
*   **Off-Policy Clarification:** Acknowledge and clarify in the text that the auxiliary tasks are not trained with "true" Q-Learning, as they utilize off-policy updates with multi-step empirical rewards (as discussed in OpenReview comments).

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost associated with each auxiliary task?
2.  Were any experiments conducted to optimize the weights (lambda values) assigned to the different auxiliary tasks? If so, what were the results?
3.  Regarding the feature control task, what strategies could be employed to mitigate potential stability or convergence issues arising from modifying the feature extractor during learning?
4.  Can you elaborate on how the auxiliary tasks are trained off-policy with multi-step empirical rewards, and why this doesn't constitute "true" Q-Learning?
