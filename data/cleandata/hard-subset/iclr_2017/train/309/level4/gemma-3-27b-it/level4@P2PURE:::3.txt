This work introduces a method for training reinforcement learning (RL) agents to simultaneously perform auxiliary tasks, with the hypothesis that this will encourage the learning of more robust features.
The authors propose two pseudo-control tasks: controlling changes in pixel intensity and controlling the activation of latent features. They also introduce a supervised regression task focused on predicting immediate reward following a sequence of actions, trained offline using a biased sampling strategy from the experience replay buffer to achieve a balanced reward/no-reward observation rate of approximately 50%.
Agents trained with this approach demonstrate significantly improved performance on discrete-action, continuous-space RL tasks, achieving baseline performance in ten times fewer iterations.
This work offers a distinct approach compared to traditional “passive” unsupervised or model-based learning techniques. Rather than forcing the model to learn a potentially irrelevant representation of the input or attempting the potentially intractable task of environment modeling (due to partial observability), learning to control local and internal environmental features complements the learning of an optimal control policy.
I find this approach novel and it presents a compelling alternative to unsupervised learning that leverages the agent’s capacity for control within the environment.
While the proposed tasks are explained at a high level, which aids in intuitive understanding, greater detail—such as an earlier introduction of the L_PC loss—might be beneficial, rather than solely relying on the appendix. Otherwise, the work is clearly written and accessible to readers familiar with Deep RL.
The methodology appears sound; while the optimal hyperparameters may differ between A3C and UNREAL, reporting the top-3 results ensures that the best hyperparameters for each method were identified within the explored parameter space.
A potential limitation of this work—or, more accurately, a direction for crucial future research—is the limited experimental analysis of the auxiliary tasks beyond their strong impact on overall performance. In particular, pixel/feature control appears to be the most impactful, as in the Labyrinth environment, A3C+PC outperforms all other combinations except UNREAL. Further investigation into this phenomenon, either in isolation or with more extensive analysis beyond RL task performance, would be valuable.
