This paper introduces a novel approach to enhance deep reinforcement learning agents by incorporating auxiliary tasks alongside standard reward maximization. The authors propose an agent that simultaneously learns policies for maximizing multiple pseudo-reward functions, leveraging a shared representation that develops even in the absence of extrinsic rewards. A key innovation is a mechanism for focusing this representation on extrinsic rewards, enabling rapid adaptation to the primary task. The proposed UNREAL agent demonstrates significant performance improvements on both Atari games, exceeding state-of-the-art results by a substantial margin (averaging 880% expert human performance), and the challenging Labyrinth environment, achieving a 10x speedup in learning and 87% expert human performance. This work represents a valuable contribution to the field.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The significant performance gains observed on challenging benchmarks, coupled with the clear articulation of the approach and its theoretical underpinnings, strongly support publication. The combination of auxiliary control and reward prediction tasks demonstrably improves both sample efficiency and robustness, addressing crucial challenges in deep reinforcement learning.

The paper tackles the important problem of improving sample efficiency and generalization in deep reinforcement learning. The proposed approach—learning to control various aspects of the sensorimotor stream via auxiliary tasks—is well-motivated, building upon prior work in representation learning, temporal abstraction, and auxiliary prediction. The authors appropriately position their work within the existing literature, citing relevant publications and clearly differentiating their contributions from those of others (e.g., Horde, UVFA, successor representations). 

The paper provides strong empirical support for its claims. The results on both Atari and Labyrinth are compelling, showing significant and consistent improvements over existing methods. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the contribution of each component of the UNREAL architecture. The reported speedups in learning are particularly noteworthy, highlighting the practical benefits of the proposed approach. The experimental methodology appears sound, and the results are presented clearly and comprehensively.

To further strengthen the paper, I would appreciate clarifications on the following points:

*   Could the authors provide more detail on the computational resources required to train the UNREAL agent, including the number of CPU/GPU hours and memory usage? This information is crucial for reproducibility and for assessing the scalability of the approach.
*   Given the architectural complexity of the UNREAL agent, would the authors be willing to release the source code to facilitate further research and enable the community to build upon their work?
*   The paper mentions that the weighting terms (λVR, λPC, λRP) are sampled from log-uniform distributions. What motivated this particular choice of sampling strategy, and how sensitive are the results to different sampling distributions or fixed values for these hyperparameters?
*   In the Labyrinth experiments, the authors mention that auxiliary tasks improve robustness to hyperparameters. Could they provide a more quantitative analysis of this robustness, perhaps by showing the variance in performance across different hyperparameter settings?
