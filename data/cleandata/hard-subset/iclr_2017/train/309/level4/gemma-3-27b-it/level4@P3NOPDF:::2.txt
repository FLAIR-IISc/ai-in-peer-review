This paper presents an intriguing approach to improving feature learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the primary policy optimization with auxiliary tasks. These tasks—control (modifying the state space), immediate reward prediction, and value function replay—are designed to shape the learned features, sharing a common CNN+LSTM network with the main policy. The results demonstrate significant improvements in data efficiency, particularly when compared to A3C, on both Atari and Labyrinth environments. Overall, the paper is well-written, the ideas are sound, and the results are compelling, leading me to recommend acceptance.

I have a few suggestions that could further strengthen the work. First, a discussion of the computational cost introduced by the auxiliary tasks would be valuable. Quantifying the impact on training speed, and identifying the most computationally expensive components, would provide a more complete picture of the method’s practicality. 

Second, the abstract and introduction could be clarified to explicitly state that the agent learns *different* policies for each task. My initial interpretation of the abstract – that a single policy was being optimized for all rewards concurrently – was incorrect and only resolved upon reaching equation (1). Emphasizing the multi-policy aspect upfront would improve clarity.

The “feature control” task, while conceptually interesting, lacks strong empirical validation. The preliminary experiment in Figure 5 offers only a slight initial benefit and is not particularly convincing. Given that the task changes during learning as the features are modified, I am concerned about potential stability or convergence issues. Further investigation into this aspect would be beneficial.

Since the authors note that their agents continue to improve with further training, extending the training runs for the best-performing agents to assess their ultimate potential would be worthwhile. 

I also question the fixed weighting of the auxiliary tasks (λ<sub>*</sub> = 1). While the authors likely have a reason for this choice, exploring these weights as hyperparameters through optimization could lead to further performance gains. Any experiments conducted to justify the selection of 1 should be mentioned. 

Finally, it's important to clarify that the auxiliary tasks are not trained using traditional Q-Learning, as they are trained off-policy with multi-step empirical rewards, a point discussed in the OpenReview comments.
