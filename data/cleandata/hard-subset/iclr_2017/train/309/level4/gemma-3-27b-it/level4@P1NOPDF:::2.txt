This paper presents a method for improving feature learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the primary policy’s optimization problem with terms corresponding to domain-independent auxiliary tasks. These tasks involve control (learning policies to maximally modify the state space, specifically the pixels), immediate reward prediction, and value function replay. With the exception of value function replay, these auxiliary tasks are used to shape the features by sharing a CNN+LSTM feature extraction network. Experiments demonstrate the benefits of this approach on Atari and Labyrinth environments, achieving significantly better data efficiency than A3C.

The paper is well-written, the ideas are sound, and the results are quite convincing, leading me to recommend clear acceptance. I have only a few relatively minor comments:

*   Could you please discuss the additional computational cost associated with optimizing the auxiliary tasks? What is the impact on training speed, and which components are the most computationally expensive?
*   The abstract and introduction could be clarified to explicitly state that the agent learns *different* policies for each task. Initially, the phrasing “also maximises many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously by reinforcement learning” led me to believe a single policy was being optimized for all rewards, a misunderstanding that wasn’t resolved until reaching equation (1).
*   The “feature control” idea lacks strong empirical validation. The preliminary experiment in Figure 5 is not particularly convincing, showing only a slight initial improvement. While I find the idea appealing, I’m concerned that the task changes during learning, potentially causing stability or convergence issues as the extracted features are modified.
*   Given that, as you mention, the performance of your agents is still improving, would it be beneficial to continue training the best-performing agents to see how far they can progress?
*   Why weren’t the weights of the auxiliary tasks (the λ<sub>*</sub>) treated as hyperparameters to be optimized? Were any experiments conducted to justify using a value of 1?
*   Please acknowledge that the auxiliary tasks are not trained using “true” Q-Learning, as they are trained off-policy with multi-step empirical rewards (as discussed in the OpenReview comments).
