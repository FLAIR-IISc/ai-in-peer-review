This paper presents an approach to improve feature learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the policy optimization objective with auxiliary tasks. These tasks – control (learning policies to modify the state space), immediate reward prediction, and value function replay – are designed to encourage the learning of robust and generalizable features, all sharing a common CNN+LSTM feature extraction network. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on Atari and Labyrinth environments, achieving notably improved data efficiency compared to A3C.

Overall, this is a strong paper with sound ideas and compelling results, warranting acceptance. I have a few suggestions for improvement, none of which are critical.

First, the paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational overhead introduced by optimizing the auxiliary tasks. Quantifying the impact on training speed, and identifying the most computationally expensive components, would be valuable. 

Second, the abstract and introduction could be clarified to explicitly state that the agent learns *different* policies for each task. The phrasing "maximises many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously by reinforcement learning" initially led me to believe a single policy was being optimized for all rewards, a misunderstanding that was only resolved upon reaching equation 1.

The “feature control” idea, while conceptually interesting, lacks strong empirical validation. The preliminary results in Figure 5 are not particularly convincing, showing only a marginal initial benefit. I am concerned that the dynamic nature of the control task during learning – as the extracted features are themselves changing – may introduce stability or convergence issues.

Given the authors' observation that performance is still improving, it would be interesting to see the best-performing agents trained for longer periods to explore their full potential.

I question the choice of fixed weights (λ*) for the auxiliary tasks. Were these weights considered hyperparameters and subject to optimization? If so, could the authors describe those experiments and justify the selection of a value of 1? 

The paper should also clarify that the auxiliary tasks are not trained using traditional Q-Learning. They are trained off-policy, utilizing multiple steps of empirical rewards, as discussed in the OpenReview comments.

*   In equation 1, λc should be included within the summation.
*   Just below equation 1, rt^(c) should be rt+k^(c).
*   Figure 2 is not referenced in the text. Additionally, Figure 1(d) should be referenced in section 3.3.

*   Please clarify what “Clip” refers to in the context of dueling networks within the legend of Figure 3.
*   I would appreciate seeing more ablation studies on the Atari games to determine if the individual task contributions observed in the Labyrinth environment are consistent.
*   The percentages in the legend of Figure 3 refer to Labyrinth, but this is not explicitly stated in the accompanying text.
*   In section 4.1.2, “Figure 3 (right) shows…” actually refers to the top left plot of the figure. Later, “This is shown in Figure 3 Top” should be “Figure 3 Top Right.”
*   “Figure 5 shows the learning curves for the top 5 hyperparameter settings on three Labyrinth navigation levels” appears to refer to the left and middle plots of the figure, representing only two levels. The text above may also require adjustment.
*   In section 4.2, “The left side shows the average performance curves of the top 5 agents for all three methods the right half shows…” is grammatically awkward and requires a comma or similar correction after “methods”.
*   The Appendix states “Further details are included in the supplementary materials.” However, these materials are not provided.
*   What is the value of λPC? (It appears to be 1, but explicit confirmation would be helpful).
