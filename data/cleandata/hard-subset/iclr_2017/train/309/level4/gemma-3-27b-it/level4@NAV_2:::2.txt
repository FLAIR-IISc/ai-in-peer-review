This paper introduces a method for improving feature learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the primary policy optimization with auxiliary tasks focused on control, immediate reward prediction, and value function replay. These tasks are designed to shape the learned features, with the agent learning separate policies for each. The approach is evaluated on Atari and Labyrinth environments, demonstrating improved data efficiency compared to A3C.

**Accept** – This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning, demonstrating a compelling approach to improve learning efficiency and robustness through the incorporation of auxiliary tasks. The results on both Atari and Labyrinth are strong and the ideas are clearly presented.

The paper is well-written and the proposed ideas are sound, supported by convincing results. The core concept of leveraging auxiliary tasks to improve feature representation learning is well-motivated and effectively implemented. The experimental validation on diverse environments (Atari, Labyrinth) strengthens the claims made in the paper. The observed improvements in data efficiency and robustness are particularly noteworthy.

While the paper is strong, several points could further enhance its clarity and impact:

*   It would be valuable to quantify the computational cost associated with optimizing the auxiliary tasks. A discussion of the trade-off between improved performance and increased training time would be insightful. Identifying the most computationally expensive components would be helpful.
*   Clarifying in the abstract and introduction that the agent learns separate policies for each task would improve understanding. Currently, the phrasing suggests a single policy optimized for all rewards.
*   The “feature control” idea, while promising, requires further empirical validation. The preliminary experiment in Figure 5 is not conclusive. Addressing potential stability or convergence issues arising from the dynamic nature of feature extraction during learning would strengthen this aspect of the work.
*   Given the ongoing performance improvements reported, exploring the potential for further training of the best-performing agents could reveal additional gains.
*   Investigating the sensitivity of performance to the weighting parameters (λ\*) of the auxiliary tasks through hyperparameter optimization would be beneficial. Justifying the choice of a fixed value (e.g., 1) would add rigor.
*   Acknowledging that the auxiliary tasks are trained with off-policy Q-learning using multiple steps of empirical rewards, as discussed in OpenReview comments, would provide further context.
