- Proposes augmenting deep RL agents with auxiliary tasks (pixel changes and internal activation control) plus reward prediction to improve representations and learning.
- Uses a skewed sampling strategy for the reward prediction task to address reward sparsity.
- Introduces the UNREAL architecture that jointly trains policy and auxiliary tasks.
- Empirically outperforms prior methods on 3D Labyrinth and Atari benchmarks.
- Reports UNREAL achieves >87% human-level on Labyrinth and a 10x learning speedup.
- Reports UNREAL averages 880% expert human performance on Atari.
- Decision: Accept — motivated by strong empirical gains and novelty of auxiliary control tasks.
- Paper is well-structured, clearly motivates tackling sparse rewards via control-based auxiliary tasks.
- Experimental evaluation is rigorous, with baseline comparisons, ablation studies, and hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
- Requests clearer technical exposition of auxiliary-task losses (e.g., L_PC) rather than deferring to appendix.
- Recommends deeper analysis of individual auxiliary tasks’ contributions, noting pixel control seems most impactful in Labyrinth.
- Suggests evaluating learned representations directly (e.g., activations) beyond RL performance metrics.
- Asks authors to justify the skewed sampling design and sensitivity to the 0.5 threshold for reward prediction.
- Asks for hypotheses on why pixel control is especially effective in Labyrinth and whether its effect varies across Atari games.
- Asks for explanation why pixel reconstruction loss can hurt performance and whether that holds across environments.
- Requests example visualizations of learned features or activations to demonstrate auxiliary-task effects.
