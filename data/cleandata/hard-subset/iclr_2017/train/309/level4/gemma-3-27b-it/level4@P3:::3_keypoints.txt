- Proposes augmenting deep RL agents with auxiliary tasks to improve representation learning.  
- Hypothesis: learning to control environment aspects (not just reward) yields more robust, efficient learning.  
- Introduces two pseudo-control tasks: maximize pixel changes and maximize hidden feature activations, plus a reward-prediction task.  
- Implements reward prediction via skewed sampling in experience replay.  
- UNREAL agent achieves strong empirical gains: state-of-the-art on Atari (880% human) and Labyrinth (87% human) with ~10x learning speedup.  
- Decision: Accept — approach is compelling and empirically well-supported.  
- Well-motivated argument that control-based auxiliary tasks are more aligned with RL objectives than input reconstruction.  
- Ablation studies show each auxiliary component contributes, with pixel control particularly impactful.  
- Robustness/hyperparameter analysis strengthens conclusions; implementation details appear sufficient for reproducibility.  
- Related work is appropriately discussed and positioned.  
- Suggest explicitly stating the auxiliary control loss (L_PC) earlier for readability.  
- Recommend deeper analysis/visualization to explain why pixel control is especially effective.  
- Request more specifics on the reward-prediction task implementation (e.g., network architecture).  
- Suggest discussing potential transferability of learned representations to other environments or tasks.  
- Ask for an estimate/discussion of the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks versus standard A3C.  
- Questions for authors: why pixel control is so effective; how the reward-skew ratio was chosen/tuned and its sensitivity; potential for transferring representations/policies; estimate of added computational cost.
