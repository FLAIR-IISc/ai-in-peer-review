This work proposes training reinforcement learning (RL) agents to simultaneously perform auxiliary tasks, with the hypothesis that this will encourage the development of more robust feature representations. 

The authors propose two pseudo-control tasks: controlling changes in pixel intensity, and controlling the activation of latent features. They also introduce a supervised regression task – predicting immediate reward following a sequence of events – which is learned offline using a skewed sampling strategy from an experience replay buffer to balance the representation of reward and non-reward events at a 1:1 ratio.

Agents trained with these auxiliary tasks demonstrate significantly improved performance on discrete-action, continuous-space RL problems, achieving baseline performance in ten times fewer iterations. 

This work offers a compelling contrast to traditional “passive” unsupervised or model-based learning approaches. Instead of forcing the model to learn a potentially unhelpful representation of the input, or to tackle the potentially intractable task-modeling objective arising from partial observability, learning to control local and internal features of the environment complements the learning of an optimal control policy.

I find the approach novel and it presents a very interesting alternative to unsupervised learning, capitalizing on the agent’s inherent ability to control its environment.

The proposed tasks are explained at a relatively high level, which aids in understanding the underlying intuition. However, I believe some additional detail would be beneficial; for example, the loss function for pixel control (L_PC) should be explicitly mentioned before being relegated to the appendix. Otherwise, the work is clear and readily understandable for readers familiar with Deep RL.

The methodology appears sound. While the optimal hyperparameter distributions may differ between A3C and UNREAL, measuring the top-3 results ensures that, assuming the best hyperparameters for each method fall within the explored ranges, the per-method optimum is identified.

I believe a key weakness of the paper – or, more accurately, a crucial area for future work given the constraints of paper length – is the limited experimental analysis of the auxiliary tasks’ individual effects beyond their strong impact on overall performance. In a similar vein, pixel/feature control appears to have the greatest impact; in the Labyrinth environment, only A3C+PC outperforms anything else (excluding UNREAL). It would have been valuable to investigate this further, either in isolation or with more depth, and to measure effects beyond performance on the primary RL tasks.
