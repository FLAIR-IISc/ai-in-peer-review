This work proposes training reinforcement learning (RL) agents to perform auxiliary tasks, with the hypothesis that this will encourage the learning of stronger features. 
The authors propose two pseudo-control tasks: controlling changes in pixel intensity and controlling the activation of latent features. They also introduce a supervised regression task – predicting immediate reward following a sequence of events – which is learned offline via skewed sampling of an experience replay buffer to balance the frequency of observing reward and non-reward events to a 50/50 chance.
Agents trained with these auxiliary tasks demonstrate significantly improved performance on discrete-action, continuous-space RL tasks, achieving baseline performance in ten times fewer iterations. 
This approach contrasts with traditional “passive” unsupervised or model-based learning methods. Rather than forcing the model to learn a potentially unhelpful representation of the input, or tackle the potentially intractable task-modeling objective arising from partial observability, learning to control local and internal features of the environment complements the learning of the optimal control policy.
I find this approach novel and a very interesting alternative to unsupervised learning, leveraging the agent’s inherent capacity for control over the environment.
The proposed tasks are initially explained at a high level, which aids in understanding the underlying intuition. However, I believe including a more detailed explanation of, for example, the L<sub>PC</sub> loss function before the appendix would be beneficial. Otherwise, the paper is clear and readily understandable for readers familiar with Deep RL.
The methodology appears sound. While the optimal hyperparameter distributions may differ between A3C and UNREAL, the use of top-3 performance measurements ensures, assuming the best hyperparameters for each method fall within the explored range, that per-method optimal configurations are identified.
A potential weakness of the paper—or, more accurately, an area for crucial future work given the constraints of paper length—is the limited experimental analysis of the effects of the auxiliary tasks beyond their strong impact on RL performance. In a similar vein, pixel/feature control appears to be the most impactful component, as in the Labyrinth environment, only A3C+PC consistently outperforms other configurations (except UNREAL). It would have been worthwhile to investigate this observation further, either in isolation or with more in-depth analysis, measuring aspects beyond just performance on the primary RL tasks.
