This paper presents a compelling approach to improving feature learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the primary policy optimization with auxiliary tasks. These tasks – control (state space modification), immediate reward prediction, and value function replay – are designed to enhance feature representation (through a shared CNN+LSTM network) without directly contributing to the main policy’s reward. The experimental results on Atari and Labyrinth environments demonstrate significant gains in data efficiency compared to A3C, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed method.

Overall, the paper is well-written, the ideas are sound, and the results are convincing, leading me to recommend acceptance. I have a few suggestions for improvement, though none are critical:

*   Could the authors discuss the computational overhead introduced by optimizing the auxiliary tasks? Specifically, quantifying the impact on training speed and identifying the most computationally expensive components would be valuable.
*   The description in the abstract and introduction could be clarified to explicitly state that the agent learns separate policies for each task. My initial interpretation of the abstract suggested a single policy optimizing all rewards, which was only clarified upon reaching equation (1).
*   The empirical validation of the "feature control" idea appears weak (Figure 5). While the concept is intriguing, the observed initial benefit is subtle, and the dynamic nature of the task during learning raises concerns about potential stability or convergence issues as the feature extractor evolves.
*   Given the authors’ observation that agent performance continues to improve, exploring longer training runs for the best-performing agents might reveal further performance gains.
*   Considering the auxiliary task weights (λ\*) as hyperparameters subject to optimization seems worthwhile. Were any experiments conducted to justify the choice of using a value of 1 for these weights?
*   It would be helpful to acknowledge that the auxiliary tasks are not trained using standard Q-Learning, as they are trained off-policy with multi-step empirical rewards (as discussed in the OpenReview forum).
