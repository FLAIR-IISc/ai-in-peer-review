This paper introduces a novel approach to enhancing deep reinforcement learning agents by incorporating auxiliary tasks – both control-based and reward-focused – alongside the primary reinforcement learning objective. The authors propose the UNREAL agent, which learns to maximize multiple pseudo-reward functions concurrently, fostering a richer and more adaptable representation of the environment. The agent is evaluated on challenging 3D Labyrinth environments and a suite of Atari games, demonstrating significant performance improvements over the A3C baseline, along with faster learning speeds and increased robustness to hyperparameter settings. This work represents a valuable contribution to the field, effectively demonstrating the benefits of leveraging a broader range of learning signals within a reinforcement learning framework.

**Decision: Accept**

The primary reason for acceptance is the substantial and consistent performance gains achieved by the UNREAL agent across diverse and challenging environments. The results on both Labyrinth and Atari convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Furthermore, the improved data efficiency and hyperparameter robustness are significant practical advantages.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon existing work in representation learning, auxiliary tasks, and multi-objective reinforcement learning. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of traditional RL approaches that rely solely on extrinsic rewards and present a compelling argument for learning to control various aspects of the sensorimotor stream. The architecture is logically presented and the experimental results are thoroughly analyzed, providing strong support for the authors' claims. The ablation studies are particularly insightful, demonstrating the contribution of each component of the UNREAL agent. The reported speedups in learning are also highly encouraging.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the results are compelling, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational costs associated with training the UNREAL agent. A comparison of training time and resource requirements (e.g., GPU hours) with the A3C baseline would be valuable for assessing the practical feasibility of this approach. 

The complexity of the UNREAL architecture could be a barrier to adoption by other researchers. Providing publicly available source code would greatly facilitate reproducibility and further exploration of this promising approach. 

It would also be helpful to explore the transferability of the learned representations. Could the representations learned through these auxiliary tasks be effectively utilized in other, related tasks without further fine-tuning?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could the authors provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources required to train the UNREAL agent, compared to the A3C baseline? Specifically, what is the difference in training time and GPU hours needed to achieve comparable performance?
2.  Do the authors plan to release the source code for their implementation? If so, when will it be available?
3.  Have the authors investigated the transferability of the learned representations to other, related tasks? If so, what were the results? If not, are they planning to explore this in future work?
4.  The paper mentions skewing the replay buffer for reward prediction. Could the authors elaborate on the specific sampling strategy and the rationale behind choosing equal representation of zero and non-zero rewards?
