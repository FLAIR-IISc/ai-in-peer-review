This paper presents a method for improving feature learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the policy optimization objective with terms derived from auxiliary tasks. These tasks include learning control policies to modify the state space (pixels in this case), predicting immediate rewards, and replaying value functions. With the exception of value function replay, these auxiliary tasks are used solely to shape the learned features, sharing a common CNN+LSTM feature extraction network. Experimental results on Atari and Labyrinth environments demonstrate the benefits of this approach, achieving notably improved data efficiency compared to A3C.

The paper is well-written, the ideas are sound, and the results are convincing, leading me to recommend acceptance. I have a few relatively minor comments:

- It would be helpful to discuss the computational overhead associated with optimizing the auxiliary tasks. Specifically, how much does training time increase, and which components are the most computationally expensive?
- The abstract and introduction could be clarified to explicitly state that the agent learns separate policies for each task. Currently, the phrasing “maximises many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously by reinforcement learning” initially suggests a single policy optimizing all rewards, a misunderstanding I resolved only upon reaching equation 1.
- The empirical validation of the “feature control” idea is weak (the preliminary experiment in Figure 5 provides only limited support, showing only a slight initial benefit). While I find the concept appealing, I am concerned that the changing nature of the task during learning may introduce stability or convergence issues, as the extracted features are concurrently being modified.
- Given that the agents’ performance is still improving, I suggest continuing training the best-performing agents to explore their ultimate potential.
- Why were the weights for the auxiliary tasks (lambda_*) fixed at 1, rather than being treated as hyperparameters to be optimized? Were any experiments conducted to assess the suitability of this choice?
- Please acknowledge that the auxiliary tasks are not trained using standard Q-Learning due to their off-policy training with multi-step empirical rewards, as discussed in the OpenReview comments.
