This paper proposes a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning by augmenting agents with auxiliary tasks designed to improve representation learning. The authors hypothesize that learning to control various aspects of the environment, beyond maximizing cumulative reward, will lead to more robust and efficient learning. They introduce two pseudo-control tasks – maximizing pixel changes and maximizing activations of hidden network features – alongside a reward prediction task, implemented through a skewed sampling strategy in experience replay. The resulting agent, UNREAL, demonstrates significant performance improvements on both Atari games and a challenging 3D Labyrinth environment, achieving state-of-the-art results and substantial speedups in learning.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a compelling approach to improving reinforcement learning agents through auxiliary tasks, supported by strong empirical results. The improvements on both Atari and Labyrinth are substantial and demonstrate the potential of this method.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of leveraging auxiliary tasks to shape the agent’s representation is well-motivated and addresses a key challenge in reinforcement learning: efficient exploration and learning in complex environments. The paper convincingly argues that simply reconstructing the input (as in traditional unsupervised learning) is not necessarily the most effective approach and that learning to *control* features of the environment is more directly aligned with achieving goals. The experimental results strongly support this claim. The performance gains on both Atari (880% human performance) and Labyrinth (87% human performance, 10x speedup) are substantial and demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach. The ablation studies further highlight the contribution of each component, particularly the pixel control task. The robustness analysis regarding hyperparameters further strengthens the conclusions. The authors demonstrate awareness of related work and clearly position their contributions within the existing literature. The implementation details provided seem sufficient for reproducibility.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is well-written and the core ideas are clear, a few minor improvements could further enhance its impact:

*   **Mathematical Notation:** While not critical, explicitly stating the auxiliary control loss term (L_PC) earlier in the text, before referencing the appendix, would improve readability.
*   **Task Analysis:** The paper notes that pixel control appears particularly impactful. A more detailed analysis of *why* this task is so effective would be valuable. Investigating the types of features learned when focusing solely on pixel control, or visualizing the learned pixel control policies, could provide further insights.
*   **Reward Prediction Details:** More specifics on the implementation of the reward prediction task (e.g., network architecture details) would be useful.
* **Generalization Discussion**: While results are shown on two distinct environments, discussing the potential transferability of the learned representations to other domains or tasks would be beneficial. Could the auxiliary tasks learned in Labyrinth be transferred to other 3D environments, for example?
* **Computational Cost:** A brief discussion on the increased computational overhead associated with the auxiliary tasks compared to a standard A3C agent would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The results indicate pixel control is particularly effective. Can you provide further insights into *why* you believe this is the case? Are there specific types of visual features that the agent learns to control that are particularly beneficial for the primary task?
2.  The paper mentions skewed sampling for reward prediction. What criteria were used to determine the skewing ratio (balancing zero and non-zero rewards)? Was this ratio tuned, and how sensitive are the results to this parameter?
3.  Could you elaborate on the potential for transferring the learned representations (or the auxiliary task policies) to new, unseen environments or tasks? Are there any challenges you anticipate in doing so?
4. Could you provide an estimate of the computational overhead introduced by adding the auxiliary tasks, compared to a standard A3C agent?
