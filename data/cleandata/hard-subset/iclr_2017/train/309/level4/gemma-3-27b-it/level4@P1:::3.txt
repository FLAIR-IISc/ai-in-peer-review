This work proposes to train reinforcement learning agents to perform auxiliary tasks alongside their primary objective, hypothesizing that this will lead to the development of more robust and effective feature representations.

The authors propose two pseudo-control tasks: maximizing changes in pixel intensity and maximizing the activation of latent features within the network. They also introduce a supervised regression task – predicting immediate reward given a sequence of observations – trained offline using a skewed sampling strategy from an experience replay buffer to ensure a balanced representation of rewarding and non-rewarding events (a 50/50 split). 

These agents demonstrate significantly improved performance on challenging discrete-action, continuous-space reinforcement learning tasks, achieving baseline performance in approximately 10 times fewer iterations. 

This approach distinguishes itself from traditional “passive” unsupervised or model-based learning methods. Instead of forcing the model to learn a potentially unhelpful representation of the input or tackle the potentially intractable task of environment modeling (due to partial observability), learning to control local and internal features of the environment complements learning the optimal control policy.

I find the approach novel and compelling, offering an interesting alternative to unsupervised learning that leverages an agent’s inherent ability to influence its environment.

The proposed tasks are explained at a high level, which aids intuitive understanding. However, I believe some additional detail might be beneficial. For example, the auxiliary control loss, *L<sub>PC</sub>*, could be explicitly mentioned before the appendix. Otherwise, the work is clear and readily understandable for readers familiar with deep reinforcement learning.

The methodology appears sound. While the distribution of optimal hyperparameters might differ between A3C and UNREAL, evaluating the top 3 performing hyperparameter sets helps to mitigate this concern by ensuring that, assuming the best hyperparameters for both methods fall within the explored range, the per-method best configurations are identified.

One potential weakness of the paper – or, more accurately, a crucial avenue for future work, given the scope of a single paper – is the limited experimental analysis of the effects of the auxiliary tasks beyond their strong impact on overall performance. Similarly, pixel and feature control appear to have the most substantial influence; in the Labyrinth experiments, A3C augmented with pixel control outperforms all other configurations (except UNREAL). It would have been valuable to investigate these tasks further, either in isolation or in greater depth, by measuring metrics beyond performance on the primary reinforcement learning tasks.

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Novelty and Potential Impact:** The proposed approach of using auxiliary control tasks to improve representation learning in reinforcement learning is novel and promising.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The agent consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods on challenging benchmarks (Labyrinth and Atari), demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Ablation Study Detail:** Expand the discussion of ablation study results. Specifically, provide a more detailed analysis of why pixel control appears particularly effective.
*   **Auxiliary Task Analysis:** Investigate and report metrics related to the auxiliary tasks themselves (e.g., how well are pixels changed, how well are features activated?). This could provide deeper insights into what the agent is learning.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the top-3 hyperparameter approach is sensible, discuss the sensitivity of the results to the hyperparameter ranges explored.
*   **Clarify L_PC:** Explicitly mention and briefly define the auxiliary control loss *L<sub>PC</sub>* in the main body of the text before referencing the appendix.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   The paper mentions that learning to reconstruct the input (in the unsupervised baseline) can hurt performance. Could you elaborate on *why* you believe this is the case? Is it simply that the agent focuses on irrelevant pixel changes?
*   Could you provide more intuition for *why* pixel control seems to be the most impactful auxiliary task, particularly in the Labyrinth environment?
*   What is the computational overhead introduced by adding the auxiliary tasks? How does it affect training time?
*   You mention that the reward prediction task uses a simpler feedforward network than the main A3C agent. Have you explored using a more complex network (e.g., an LSTM) for the reward prediction task, and if so, what were the results?
