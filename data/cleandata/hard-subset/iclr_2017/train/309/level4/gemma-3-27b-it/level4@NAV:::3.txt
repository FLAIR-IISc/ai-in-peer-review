This paper investigates training reinforcement learning (RL) agents with auxiliary tasks to encourage the development of more robust feature representations. 

The authors introduce two novel pseudo-control tasks – controlling pixel intensity changes and controlling the activation of latent features – alongside a supervised regression task focused on predicting immediate reward from event sequences. This reward prediction task is trained offline using a biased sampling strategy from the experience replay buffer to achieve a balanced presentation of rewarded and non-rewarded experiences (approximately 50/50).

Experimental results demonstrate substantial performance gains on discrete-action, continuous-space RL benchmarks, achieving baseline levels of performance in ten times fewer iterations. 

This work presents a compelling departure from traditional passive, unsupervised, or model-based learning approaches. Rather than learning potentially irrelevant input representations or tackling the intractable problem of complete environment modeling caused by partial observability, the proposed approach leverages the agent’s ability to actively control local and internal environmental features to facilitate optimal policy learning. 

The proposed methodology is novel and offers a promising alternative to standard unsupervised learning techniques by capitalizing on the agent's inherent control capabilities. 

While the tasks are initially presented at a high level, aiding intuitive understanding, a more detailed explanation – specifically mentioning the loss function L_PC – earlier in the paper (rather than solely in the appendix) would be beneficial. Overall, the paper is clearly written and accessible to readers with a background in Deep RL.

The experimental design is rigorous; acknowledging potential hyperparameter distribution differences between A3C and UNREAL while employing a top-3 selection strategy effectively ensures the identification of optimal per-method configurations, assuming these optima lie within the explored ranges.

However, a key area for future work is a more detailed analysis of the auxiliary tasks themselves, beyond their demonstrated impact on RL performance. The observation that pixel/feature control appears particularly impactful – as evidenced in the Labyrinth environment where A3C+PC outperforms most other configurations (excluding UNREAL) – suggests that further investigation, either in isolation or with more comprehensive metrics, would be valuable.
