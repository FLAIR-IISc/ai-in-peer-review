This work introduces a novel approach to reinforcement learning by proposing agents that simultaneously learn to maximize multiple pseudo-reward functions alongside the primary task reward. The core idea is that learning to control various aspects of the environment, even seemingly unrelated ones, fosters the development of more robust and useful representations, ultimately improving learning efficiency and performance.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is both innovative and demonstrably effective, achieving state-of-the-art results on challenging benchmarks (Atari and Labyrinth) and exhibiting significantly improved sample efficiency. The work addresses a key limitation of traditional reinforcement learning – the reliance on sparse extrinsic rewards – by leveraging a richer set of learning signals.

The paper convincingly argues for the benefits of auxiliary task learning. The authors introduce auxiliary control tasks (pixel change and network feature control) alongside an auxiliary reward prediction task. These tasks are integrated into a unified learning framework, building upon the A3C algorithm. Extensive experiments demonstrate that the proposed UNREAL agent consistently outperforms A3C and other baselines, exhibiting both higher final performance and faster learning speeds. The results on the Labyrinth environment are particularly impressive, showcasing significant improvements in both average and median performance, along with a substantial speedup in learning. The robustness analysis regarding hyperparameter sensitivity further strengthens the contribution. 

To further improve the paper, I suggest the following: 
*   While the high-level explanation of the proposed tasks is clear, providing more detail regarding the specific loss functions (e.g., explicitly mentioning L_PC before the appendix) could enhance the reader’s understanding.
*   A more in-depth analysis of the individual auxiliary tasks would be valuable. Investigating the impact of each task in isolation, or analyzing their interplay in greater detail, could provide deeper insights into their respective contributions. The observation that pixel control appears particularly impactful in the Labyrinth environment warrants further exploration.
*   Exploring metrics beyond final RL task performance to assess the quality of the learned representations (e.g., transferability to new tasks, visualization of learned features) would further strengthen the empirical evaluation.

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors: 
*   The reward prediction task employs a skewed sampling strategy to balance rewarding and non-rewarding events. What criteria were used to determine the optimal skewing ratio (0.5 in the experiments)? Was this ratio sensitive to the specific environment?
*   Regarding the feature control task, how was the specific hidden layer selected for auxiliary control? Was any experimentation done to identify the most informative layer?
*   Could the authors elaborate on the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks? Specifically, how does the training time per environment step compare between A3C and UNREAL?
*   In the Labyrinth experiments, the results show a substantial performance gap between A3C and UNREAL. Is this gap consistent across different random seeds and hyperparameter settings, or are there specific scenarios where A3C approaches UNREAL’s performance?
