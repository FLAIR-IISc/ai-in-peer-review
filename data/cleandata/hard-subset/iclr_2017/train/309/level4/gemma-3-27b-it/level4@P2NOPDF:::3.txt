This paper introduces a novel approach to reinforcement learning (RL) by incorporating auxiliary tasks designed to encourage the development of robust feature representations. The core idea is that learning to control both external aspects of the environment (pixel intensity) and internal representations (latent feature activations) alongside the primary RL objective can significantly improve learning efficiency and performance. Additionally, the authors propose a supervised regression task: predicting immediate reward based on sequences of past events, implemented using a biased sampling strategy from the experience replay buffer to balance positive and negative reward examples.

The proposed method demonstrates substantial gains on discrete-action, continuous-state RL environments, achieving baseline performance in approximately ten times fewer iterations than standard approaches. This work distinguishes itself from traditional unsupervised or model-based learning paradigms. Unlike those methods, which may force the model to learn irrelevant representations or grapple with intractable environment modeling due to partial observability, the proposed auxiliary tasks leverage the agent’s capacity for control to promote learning of useful, locally relevant features that directly support optimal policy learning. 

I find the approach to be both novel and promising, offering a compelling alternative to purely unsupervised representation learning that intelligently utilizes the agent’s active role in the environment. The high-level explanations of the proposed tasks are effective for conveying the underlying intuition. However, greater detail regarding specific loss functions, such as L_PC, would be beneficial and ideally presented earlier in the paper, rather than exclusively within the appendix. Overall, the paper is clearly written and readily accessible to readers familiar with deep RL.

The methodology appears sound. The authors acknowledge potential distributional differences in optimal hyperparameters between A3C and UNREAL and mitigate this concern through a robust hyperparameter search and selection strategy that identifies the best performing configuration *within* the explored ranges for each algorithm, using top-3 performance as a metric.

A key area for future work—and a potential weakness of the current paper, given inherent space constraints—is the limited analysis of the individual contributions of each auxiliary task beyond their collective impact on RL performance. While the results demonstrably show a positive effect, further investigation into *how* each task contributes to learning would be valuable. Notably, pixel control (PC) appears to be particularly impactful; in the Labyrinth environment, A3C+PC consistently outperforms all other configurations except UNREAL. A more detailed exploration of PC, either in isolation or through more extensive analysis, and utilizing metrics beyond just RL task performance, would strengthen the paper. This could include analyzing the learned feature representations or evaluating the effectiveness of pixel control as a standalone learning objective.
