This paper introduces a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning by augmenting agents with auxiliary tasks designed to foster the development of more robust and efficient representations. The authors propose that learning to control various aspects of the environment – specifically, pixel changes and internal network activations – alongside maximizing cumulative reward can significantly improve learning speed and performance. They further incorporate a reward prediction task, trained with a skewed sampling strategy to address reward sparsity.

The proposed UNREAL architecture demonstrates substantial improvements over state-of-the-art methods on both challenging 3D Labyrinth environments and a suite of Atari games. Notably, UNREAL achieves over 87% human-level performance on Labyrinth, with a 10x speedup in learning, and surpasses existing results on Atari, averaging 880% expert human performance. This work presents a compelling alternative to traditional unsupervised or model-based learning approaches by leveraging the agent’s ability to actively influence its sensory experience.

**Decision: Accept**

The primary reasons for acceptance are the strong empirical results and the novelty of the proposed approach. The UNREAL agent consistently outperforms existing methods across diverse and challenging environments, demonstrating a significant advancement in reinforcement learning. The core idea of utilizing auxiliary control tasks to shape the learning process is well-motivated and effectively implemented.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper is well-structured and clearly articulates the motivation behind the proposed method. The problem of learning effective representations in environments with sparse rewards is clearly defined, and the authors convincingly argue that learning to control various features of the environment can address this challenge. The experimental setup is rigorous, with thorough comparisons to baseline methods and ablation studies demonstrating the contribution of each component of the UNREAL architecture. The results presented in both the Labyrinth and Atari environments are compelling and statistically significant. The careful consideration of hyperparameter sensitivity and the use of a large sample of random configurations further strengthen the validity of the findings.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is generally well-written, expanding on the technical details of the auxiliary tasks could be beneficial. For example, providing a more detailed explanation of the loss function L_PC before referencing the appendix would improve readability. A deeper analysis of the individual auxiliary tasks, beyond their overall impact on performance, could also be valuable. Specifically, investigating the relative contributions of pixel control and feature control – particularly the observation that pixel control appears most impactful in the Labyrinth experiments – could offer further insights. Exploring metrics beyond reinforcement learning performance, such as the learned representations themselves, might provide a more nuanced understanding of the benefits of this approach.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind using a skewed sampling strategy for the reward prediction task? What is the impact of the 0.5 probability threshold on performance, and how sensitive is the approach to this parameter?
2.  The results suggest that pixel control is particularly effective in the Labyrinth environment. Do you have any hypotheses as to why this might be the case? Does the effectiveness of pixel control vary significantly across different Atari games, and if so, what factors might contribute to these differences?
3.  You mention that learning to reconstruct the input (with the baseline pixel reconstruction loss) can actually *hurt* performance. Could you discuss this in more detail? Is this observation consistent across all environments, and what are the potential reasons for this phenomenon?
4.  Could you provide some examples of the learned features (e.g., visualizations of activations) that demonstrate the impact of the auxiliary tasks on the agent's internal representations?
