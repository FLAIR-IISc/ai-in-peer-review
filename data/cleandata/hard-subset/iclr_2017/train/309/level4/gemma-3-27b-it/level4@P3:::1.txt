## Review of "Deep Reinforcement Learning with Auxiliary Control Tasks"

This paper proposes a novel approach to enhance deep reinforcement learning agents by incorporating auxiliary control and reward prediction tasks alongside the primary reinforcement learning objective. The authors introduce the UNREAL agent, which leverages auxiliary tasks to learn a more robust and efficient representation, leading to significant performance improvements on both the challenging 3D Labyrinth environment and a suite of Atari games. The results demonstrate that UNREAL substantially outperforms the A3C baseline, achieving both higher final performance and faster learning speeds – averaging 880% expert human performance on Atari and a 10x speedup in learning on Labyrinth, while also reaching 87% expert human performance on Labyrinth. This is a valuable contribution to the field, offering a promising avenue for improving the sample efficiency and robustness of deep RL algorithms.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a compelling approach with strong empirical results and clear potential impact. The improvements over the strong A3C baseline, demonstrated on diverse environments, justify acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper successfully tackles the problem of improving sample efficiency and representation learning in deep reinforcement learning. The core idea – to augment RL with a diverse set of auxiliary tasks focused on predicting and controlling aspects of the sensorimotor stream – is well-motivated. The authors effectively draw connections to existing work in unsupervised learning, options frameworks, and related architectures (UVFA, Horde, successor representations) while clearly differentiating their approach. The key distinction lies in the utilization of these auxiliary tasks *solely* for representation learning, rather than direct policy execution, and the focus on simultaneously learning to control numerous features. 

The empirical results are particularly strong. The substantial performance gains on both Labyrinth and Atari, along with the observed speedups in learning, provide strong support for the claims. The ablation studies in Labyrinth, comparing the performance of UNREAL with and without various components, further strengthen the evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The robustness analysis of hyperparameter settings is also a valuable addition, demonstrating the practical benefits of the learned representations. The inclusion of a video demonstrating agent performance is also helpful.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

While the paper is generally well-written, some sections could benefit from further clarification. For example, the discussion of how the auxiliary tasks are weighted (λVR, λPC, λRP) could be expanded. Providing more details on the hyperparameter search process would also be beneficial. 

The paper presents a complex architecture. While Figure 1 provides a good overview, a more detailed breakdown of the network structure, particularly the deconvolutional network used for pixel control, could be helpful for readers aiming to implement the approach.

The comparison to pixel reconstruction baselines in Labyrinth is interesting. It might be beneficial to briefly discuss *why* the reconstruction baseline performs worse than the control-based approach, given that both aim to leverage the visual stream.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. **Computational Resources:** Could the authors provide more details on the computational resources (e.g., number of CPUs/GPUs, training time) required to train the UNREAL agent, especially for the Labyrinth environment? This information is crucial for assessing the practical feasibility of applying this approach to other complex environments.
2. **Code Release:** Given the complexity of the architecture and the potential interest from the community, are the authors planning to release the source code for the UNREAL agent?
3. **Task Selection:** What guidance can you provide for selecting appropriate auxiliary tasks for a given environment? Is there a principled way to determine which features of the sensorimotor stream are most likely to yield beneficial representations?
4. **Scaling to More Complex Environments:** How well do you anticipate this approach scaling to even more complex and high-dimensional environments beyond Labyrinth and Atari, such as real-world robotic manipulation tasks? What modifications might be necessary?
