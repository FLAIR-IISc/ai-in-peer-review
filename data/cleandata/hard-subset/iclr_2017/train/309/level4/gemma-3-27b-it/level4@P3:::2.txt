This paper introduces a novel approach to improving feature learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the primary policy optimization with auxiliary tasks focused on control, immediate reward prediction, and value function replay. These tasks are designed to shape the learned representations by sharing a common CNN+LSTM feature extraction network, rather than being directly utilized for action selection. The results on Atari and Labyrinth environments demonstrate significant improvements in data efficiency compared to A3C, alongside strong overall performance.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound approach that demonstrably improves performance and sample efficiency in challenging reinforcement learning environments. The results are compelling and suggest a significant contribution to the field.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of learning useful representations by simultaneously optimizing for multiple, related objectives is well-grounded in both reinforcement learning and unsupervised learning literature. The authors effectively combine elements of auxiliary task learning with a robust reinforcement learning algorithm (A3C). The experimental validation is strong; the UNREAL agent consistently outperforms A3C on both Atari and Labyrinth, and the reported speedup in learning is particularly impressive. The ablation studies provide some insight into the contribution of each auxiliary task, although further investigation in this area (see feedback below) would be beneficial. The detailed description of the experimental setup and the inclusion of multiple hyperparameter settings increases confidence in the robustness of the findings.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

This feedback is intended to help strengthen the paper and is not essential for acceptance.

*   **Computational Cost:** It would be valuable to discuss the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks. Quantifying the increase in training time or computational resources required to optimize these tasks would provide a more complete picture of the method’s practicality. Identifying the most computationally expensive components would also be useful.
*   **Clarify Simultaneous Optimization:** The abstract and introduction could be slightly reworded to more clearly emphasize that the agent learns *separate* policies for each auxiliary task, while sharing the underlying feature extractor. Currently, the phrasing suggests a single policy optimizing all rewards, which is a potential source of confusion.
*   **Feature Control Validation:** The “feature control” idea is intriguing but lacks strong empirical support. The preliminary results in Figure 5 are inconclusive. I encourage the authors to invest more effort in validating this component, potentially through more extensive experiments or a more targeted analysis. The concern about stability during learning is valid and should be addressed.
*   **Extended Training:** Given the reported continued improvement in performance ("performance is still steadily improving"), it would be worthwhile to explore the long-term performance of the best-performing agents.
*   **Hyperparameter Optimization:** The choice of fixed weights (λ\*) for the auxiliary tasks is somewhat arbitrary. Investigating whether optimizing these weights via hyperparameter search would yield further improvements could strengthen the results.
*   **Clarify Off-Policy Training:** Explicitly acknowledge that the auxiliary tasks are trained off-policy, utilizing more than one step of empirical rewards, as discussed in OpenReview comments.
*   **Minor Edits:**
    *   In the section on policy gradient algorithms, correct "L_pi = -" to "L_pi =". This is a loss function to be minimized.
    *   Ensure consistency in equation notation (e.g., lambda_c within the summation in eq. 1).
    *   Correct minor typographical errors and inconsistencies in references to figures and equations as noted in the draft review.
    *   Clarify the meaning of "Clip" in the legend of Figure 3.
    *   Expand the ablation studies on Atari to mirror those performed on Labyrinth.
    *   Clearly state the percentage values in Figure 3 refer to Labyrinth.
    *   Address the potential ambiguity in Figure 5 regarding the number of levels being considered.
    *   Confirm the value of lambda_PC.
*   **Supplementary Materials:** Please include the promised supplementary materials.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the computational overhead (e.g., training time increase) associated with adding the auxiliary tasks?
2.  Have you explored methods for adaptively weighting the auxiliary tasks (i.e., optimizing the lambda parameters) during training? If so, what were the results?
3.  Regarding the feature control task, have you considered any techniques to address potential instability during learning, such as regularization or gradual introduction of the task?
4. Could you elaborate on the choice of 1 for λPC and λVR? Was this chosen based on initial experiments or intuition?
