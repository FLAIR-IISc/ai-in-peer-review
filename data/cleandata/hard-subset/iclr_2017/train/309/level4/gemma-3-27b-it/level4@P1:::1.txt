This paper proposes a method for enhancing deep reinforcement learning agents, such as A3C, by incorporating unsupervised auxiliary tasks. The authors introduce a set of auxiliary control tasks and auxiliary reward tasks, and evaluate the resulting agent, termed UNREAL, on the Labyrinth and Atari environments. The UNREAL agent demonstrates significantly improved performance and faster learning compared to A3C. This represents a valuable contribution to the conference. While the result isn't entirely unexpected – adding relevant auxiliary tasks should generally aid feature learning and accelerate training – the paper provides a strong proof of concept for this idea.

The paper is well-written and readily accessible to readers with a background in deep reinforcement learning. 

**Decision:** Accept. The strong empirical results, particularly the significant improvements on both Labyrinth and Atari, justify acceptance despite the relatively intuitive nature of the core idea. The authors demonstrate a thorough exploration of different auxiliary tasks and convincingly show their benefit.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The results presented are compelling. The UNREAL agent consistently outperforms A3C across a range of challenging environments. The reported speedup in learning (10x on Labyrinth) is substantial and suggests a significant improvement in data efficiency. Achieving 880% expert human performance on Atari and 87% on Labyrinth demonstrates a high degree of success. The robustness analysis (Figure 8 in the supplementary material) further strengthens the claim that the method is reliable and not overly sensitive to hyperparameter settings. The ablation studies presented in the Labyrinth experiments effectively demonstrate the contribution of each auxiliary task.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, the following points could further improve it:

*   **Computational Cost:** Providing more detail regarding the computational resources required to train the UNREAL agent (e.g., number of CPUs, GPUs, training time) would be beneficial for reproducibility and allow readers to assess the practicality of the approach.
*   **Code Release:** Releasing the source code would greatly facilitate further research and allow the community to build upon this work.
*   **Discussion of Failure Cases:** While the results are largely positive, discussing any environments or scenarios where UNREAL *doesn’t* perform well, or situations where the gains are minimal, would add nuance and provide a more complete picture of the method’s limitations.
*   **Clarify Connection to Related Work:** While the Related Work section is comprehensive, a more direct comparison of UNREAL’s approach to UVFA and successor representations (specifically highlighting the differences in how pseudo-rewards are used) could be useful.
* **Ablation Study Justification:** A slightly stronger justification for the specific auxiliary tasks chosen (pixel changes, network features, reward prediction) would strengthen the paper. Why these particular tasks, and not others? 

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources (CPU/GPU count, training time) required to train the UNREAL agent on both the Labyrinth and Atari environments?
2.  Do you have any plans to release the source code for your implementation? If so, what is the anticipated timeline?
3.  Were there any environments or specific settings within Atari or Labyrinth where UNREAL did *not* demonstrate a significant improvement over A3C? If so, could you describe those scenarios?
4.  Could you elaborate on how UNREAL differs from related approaches like UVFA and successor representations in terms of how pseudo-rewards are integrated into the learning process?
