**Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions**

The paper introduces a novel deep reinforcement learning (RL) agent, called UNREAL, that improves feature learning by incorporating domain-independent auxiliary tasks. These tasks, including control, reward prediction, and value replay, share a common representation and are trained using off-policy reinforcement learning. The authors demonstrate that UNREAL significantly outperforms the state-of-the-art on Atari and Labyrinth environments, achieving a mean speedup of 10x in learning and averaging 87% expert human performance on Labyrinth.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

I decide to accept this paper with minor revisions. The key reasons for this decision are:

1. The paper presents a well-motivated approach to improving feature learning in deep RL by incorporating auxiliary tasks, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, showing significant improvements over the state-of-the-art on challenging environments.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the related work, clearly explaining how the proposed approach differs from existing methods. The authors also provide a detailed description of the UNREAL agent architecture and the auxiliary tasks used to improve feature learning.

The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach on both Atari and Labyrinth environments. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the results, including ablation studies and comparisons to baseline methods.

**Additional Feedback**

To further improve the paper, I suggest the following:

1. Clarify the abstract and introduction to explicitly state that separate policies are learned for each auxiliary task, rather than a single policy optimizing all rewards.
2. Provide more details on the computational cost of the auxiliary tasks, including training speed loss and which components are most expensive.
3. Address the stability and convergence concerns raised by Figure 5, which shows the performance of the feature-control auxiliary task.
4. Consider training the agents for longer to see if the performance continues to improve.
5. Justify the choice of fixed weights (λ_*) for the auxiliary tasks, rather than treating them as hyperparameters or validating them experimentally.
6. Mention explicitly that the auxiliary tasks are trained off-policy and not with "true" Q-learning due to multi-step empirical rewards.
7. Correct minor errors, such as the formatting of Equation 1 and the legend of Figure 3.
8. Provide more ablation studies on Atari to see if the per-component contributions match the Labyrinth patterns.
9. Clarify that the percentages in Figure 3's legend refer to Labyrinth (not clear in the text).
10. Correct incorrect figure references in the text.
11. Fix the caption and text mismatch in Figure 5.
12. Specify where the supplementary materials are located.
