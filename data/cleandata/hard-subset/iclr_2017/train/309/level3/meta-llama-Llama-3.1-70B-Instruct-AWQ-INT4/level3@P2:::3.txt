**Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions**

The paper proposes a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning by training agents with auxiliary tasks to learn stronger feature representations. The authors introduce two pseudo-control tasks: control change in pixel intensity and control activation of latent features. Additionally, they add a supervised regression task to predict immediate reward after a sequence, learned offline via skewed replay sampling to balance reward/no-reward. The proposed UNREAL agent combines these auxiliary tasks with the primary policy learning task, resulting in improved performance and data efficiency on challenging tasks such as Labyrinth and Atari games.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

I recommend accepting this paper. The key reasons for this decision are:

1. The paper proposes a novel and well-motivated approach to deep reinforcement learning, leveraging the agent's ability to influence the environment as a learning signal.
2. The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, with the UNREAL agent achieving state-of-the-art performance on Labyrinth and Atari games, while requiring significantly fewer iterations to reach baseline performance.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper provides a clear and well-motivated introduction to the problem of learning effective feature representations in deep reinforcement learning. The authors argue that traditional approaches to unsupervised learning, such as reconstruction or model-based learning, may not be sufficient to learn useful representations in partially observed environments. The proposed auxiliary tasks are designed to address this limitation by providing a controllable learning signal that complements policy learning.

The empirical results are impressive, with the UNREAL agent achieving significant improvements in performance and data efficiency on both Labyrinth and Atari games. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the results, including ablation studies and comparisons to baseline methods.

**Additional Feedback and Suggestions**

While the paper is well-written and clear, there are a few areas that could be improved:

1. The authors could provide more low-level detail on the implementation of the auxiliary tasks, such as the specific architectures used for the pixel control and feature control tasks.
2. The paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the auxiliary tasks themselves, including metrics beyond RL performance. For example, the authors could provide visualizations of the learned feature representations or analyze the impact of the auxiliary tasks on the agent's exploration behavior.
3. The authors acknowledge the differences in hyperparameters between the A3C and UNREAL agents, but it would be helpful to provide more information on how these hyperparameters were tuned and how sensitive the results are to these choices.

**Questions for the Authors**

1. Can you provide more information on how the auxiliary tasks are weighted and combined with the primary policy learning task? How sensitive are the results to these weights and combinations?
2. How do the auxiliary tasks affect the agent's exploration behavior, particularly in the early stages of learning?
3. Can you provide more visualizations or analysis of the learned feature representations, particularly for the pixel control and feature control tasks?
4. How do the results generalize to other environments and tasks, particularly those with different types of observations or actions?
