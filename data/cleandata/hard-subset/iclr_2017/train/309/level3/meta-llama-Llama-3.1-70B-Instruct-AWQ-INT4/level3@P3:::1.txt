This paper proposes an innovative approach to deep reinforcement learning (RL) by incorporating unsupervised auxiliary tasks into the Asynchronous Advantage Actor-Critic (A3C) framework. The authors introduce two types of auxiliary tasks: control tasks, which aim to maximize pseudo-reward functions, and reward tasks, which focus on predicting the onset of immediate rewards. The UNREAL agent, which combines these auxiliary tasks, demonstrates significant improvements in performance and learning speed compared to the baseline A3C agent.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow for readers with deep RL expertise. The authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, including the architecture and training procedures. The experimental results are thorough and convincing, with evaluations on both the Labyrinth and Atari environments.

I accept this paper based on its solid contribution to the field of deep RL and its well-motivated approach. The results are impressive, with the UNREAL agent outperforming the A3C agent by a significant margin. The paper also provides a thorough analysis of the benefits of auxiliary tasks and their impact on the learning process.

One potential criticism of the paper is that the results are not entirely surprising. The use of relevant auxiliary tasks can aid in feature shaping, and it is reasonable to expect that this would lead to improved performance. However, the paper serves as a proof-of-concept, demonstrating the effectiveness of this approach in a deep RL setting.

I would like to request additional information on the computational resources required to train the UNREAL agent. The authors mention that the agent is trained on 32 asynchronous threads with shared RMSprop, but it would be helpful to know more about the specific hardware and software configurations used.

Furthermore, I would like to ask if the authors plan to release the source code for the UNREAL agent. The architecture is quite complicated, and having access to the code would be beneficial for reproducing the results and exploring further modifications.

In terms of additional feedback, I suggest that the authors consider exploring other types of auxiliary tasks and their impact on the learning process. The paper focuses primarily on control and reward tasks, but there may be other tasks that could be used to improve performance.

Overall, this paper makes a solid contribution to the field of deep RL, and I believe it will be of interest to researchers in the area. With some additional information and clarification, I am confident that the paper will be even more effective in conveying its message.
