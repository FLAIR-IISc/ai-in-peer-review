**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes an agent that learns to maximize multiple pseudo-reward functions simultaneously using reinforcement learning. The agent shares a common representation that continues to develop in the absence of extrinsic rewards. The authors introduce a novel mechanism for focusing this representation on extrinsic rewards, allowing for rapid adaptation to the most relevant aspects of the actual task. The agent is evaluated on Atari and Labyrinth environments, achieving state-of-the-art results and demonstrating improved data efficiency and robustness to hyperparameter settings.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

I decide to **accept** this paper. The key reasons for this choice are:

1. The paper proposes a novel and well-motivated approach to improving feature learning in deep reinforcement learning agents.
2. The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements in data efficiency and robustness to hyperparameter settings over the state-of-the-art A3C algorithm.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper is well-written, and the results are convincing. The authors provide a clear and thorough explanation of their approach, including the introduction of auxiliary control tasks, reward prediction, and value replay. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both Atari and Labyrinth environments. The results demonstrate significant improvements in data efficiency and robustness to hyperparameter settings, making a strong case for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Additional Feedback**

To further improve the paper, I suggest the following:

* Quantify the extra computational cost of the auxiliary tasks, including training speed loss and the most expensive components.
* Clarify in the abstract and introduction that separate policies are learned for each auxiliary task, rather than a single policy optimizing all rewards.
* Provide stronger empirical validation for the feature-control auxiliary task, addressing stability and convergence concerns.
* Consider training the agents for longer periods to report how far the best agents go.
* Justify the choice of fixed auxiliary task weights (lambda_*) or treat them as hyperparameters to be validated experimentally.
* Explicitly mention that auxiliary tasks are trained off-policy and not with "true" Q-learning due to multi-step empirical rewards.
* Address minor corrections, such as formatting and grammar issues.
* Provide more ablation studies on Atari to see if per-component contributions match Labyrinth patterns.
* Clarify the percentages in Figure 3's legend, which refer to Labyrinth.
* Correct figure references in the text and provide explicit annotations for Figure 3.
* Fix the caption and text mismatch in Figure 5.
* Report the value of lambda_PC (assumed 1) explicitly.
* Specify where the supplementary materials are located in the appendix.

**Questions for the Authors**

To clarify my understanding of the paper and provide additional evidence, I would like the authors to answer the following questions:

* Can you provide more details on the computational cost of the auxiliary tasks and how they affect training speed?
* How do you plan to address the stability and convergence concerns raised by the feature-control auxiliary task?
* Can you provide more information on the choice of fixed auxiliary task weights (lambda_*) and whether they were validated experimentally?
* Are there any plans to extend the approach to other environments or domains?
* Can you provide more details on the implementation of the reward prediction task and how it affects the agent's performance?
