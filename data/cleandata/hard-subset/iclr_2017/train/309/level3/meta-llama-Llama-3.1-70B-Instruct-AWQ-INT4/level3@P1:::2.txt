**Summary of the Paper's Contributions**

This paper presents a novel approach to improving feature learning in deep reinforcement learning (RL) by introducing domain-independent auxiliary tasks that share CNN+LSTM features. The proposed UNREAL agent combines three auxiliary tasks: control, reward prediction, and value replay. The authors demonstrate improved data efficiency over the state-of-the-art A3C algorithm on both Atari and Labyrinth environments. The paper is well-written, and the results are convincing, making a strong case for acceptance.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

Based on the paper's contributions and the quality of the results, I recommend accepting this paper. The two key reasons for this decision are:

1. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to improving feature learning in deep RL, which is a critical aspect of achieving state-of-the-art performance in complex environments.
2. The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements in data efficiency over the state-of-the-art A3C algorithm on both Atari and Labyrinth environments, making a strong case for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's contributions are well-motivated by the need to improve feature learning in deep RL. The authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of existing approaches and how their proposed auxiliary tasks address these limitations. The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating significant improvements in data efficiency over the state-of-the-art A3C algorithm.

The paper is also well-written, with clear explanations of the proposed approach, experimental setup, and results. The authors provide sufficient details about the implementation, including the architecture of the UNREAL agent and the hyperparameters used in the experiments.

**Additional Feedback**

To further improve the paper, I suggest the following:

* Quantify the extra computational cost of training the UNREAL agent, including training speed loss and which auxiliary components are most expensive.
* Clarify in the abstract and introduction that separate policies are learned for each auxiliary task, rather than a single policy optimizing all rewards.
* Provide stronger empirical validation for the feature-control auxiliary task, as the current results in Figure 5 are unconvincing and raise stability/convergence concerns.
* Train the agents for longer periods to report how far the best agents go and to ensure that the performance is not still improving.
* Consider treating the auxiliary-task weights (lambda_*) as hyperparameters or validating them experimentally, rather than fixing them at 1.
* Mention explicitly that the auxiliary tasks are trained off-policy and not with "true" Q-learning due to multi-step empirical rewards.
