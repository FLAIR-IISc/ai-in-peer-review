This paper proposes a novel approach to training reinforcement learning (RL) agents by incorporating auxiliary tasks to learn stronger feature representations. The authors introduce two types of auxiliary control tasks: pixel control and feature control, which are trained using off-policy reinforcement learning. Additionally, they propose an auxiliary reward prediction task to focus the agent's representation on extrinsic rewards. The UNREAL algorithm combines these auxiliary tasks with the primary policy learning task, resulting in improved performance and data efficiency.

I decide to accept this paper, primarily due to its novel approach and impressive empirical results. The authors demonstrate that their UNREAL agent outperforms the state-of-the-art on Atari games and achieves significant improvements on the Labyrinth environment. The paper is well-motivated, and the approach is well-placed in the literature, building upon existing work on auxiliary tasks and representation learning.

The paper supports its claims through extensive experiments on both Atari and Labyrinth environments. The results show that the UNREAL agent achieves higher performance and faster learning speeds compared to the baseline A3C agent. The authors also provide ablation studies to demonstrate the effectiveness of each auxiliary task and their combination.

One of the strengths of the paper is its clear presentation of the UNREAL algorithm and its components. The authors provide a detailed description of the auxiliary tasks, the experience replay mechanism, and the optimization process. However, I would have liked to see more low-level details on the auxiliary tasks, such as the introduction of L_PC before the appendix.

The methodology is generally sound, and the authors acknowledge the differences in hyperparameters between the A3C and UNREAL agents. The use of top-3 reporting helps mitigate this issue, but I would have liked to see more analysis on the robustness of the results to hyperparameter choices.

One weakness of the paper is the lack of experimental analysis on the auxiliary tasks themselves. The authors demonstrate that the pixel control and feature control tasks are effective, but it would be interesting to see a more in-depth study on these tasks, including metrics beyond RL performance.

To improve the paper, I suggest the authors provide more details on the auxiliary tasks, such as the architecture and optimization process. Additionally, a more thorough analysis of the robustness of the results to hyperparameter choices would strengthen the paper. Finally, I would like to see more discussion on the potential applications of the UNREAL algorithm beyond the Atari and Labyrinth environments.

Questions for the authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the architecture and optimization process of the auxiliary tasks, particularly the pixel control and feature control tasks?
2. How do you plan to extend the UNREAL algorithm to more complex environments, such as those with continuous action spaces or high-dimensional state spaces?
3. Can you provide more analysis on the robustness of the results to hyperparameter choices, particularly the learning rate and entropy cost?
4. How do you think the UNREAL algorithm could be applied to real-world problems, such as robotics or autonomous driving?
