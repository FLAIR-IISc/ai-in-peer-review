This paper proposes a novel approach to improve feature learning in deep reinforcement learning (RL) by introducing domain-independent auxiliary tasks that share CNN+LSTM features. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach, called UNREAL, on both Atari and Labyrinth environments, achieving state-of-the-art results.

The paper tackles the specific question of how to improve feature learning in deep RL, which is a crucial aspect of achieving good performance in complex environments. The authors' approach is well-motivated, drawing on insights from both RL and unsupervised learning. By introducing auxiliary tasks that share features with the main policy, the authors aim to improve the representation learning of the agent.

The paper provides convincing empirical results, demonstrating that the UNREAL agent outperforms the state-of-the-art A3C algorithm on both Atari and Labyrinth. The authors also provide ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of each auxiliary task to the overall performance.

I accept this paper, with the main reason being the convincing empirical results and the well-motivated approach. The authors have demonstrated a clear improvement over the state-of-the-art, and their approach has the potential to be widely applicable to other deep RL domains.

However, there are some aspects of the paper that could be improved. Firstly, the authors do not provide a clear quantification of the extra computational cost incurred by the auxiliary tasks. It would be useful to know the training speed loss and which auxiliary components are most expensive. Secondly, the feature-control auxiliary task lacks strong empirical validation, and the results in Figure 5 are not entirely convincing. The authors should consider providing more evidence to support the effectiveness of this task.

Additionally, the authors could consider training the agent for longer periods to see if the performance continues to improve. It would also be useful to report how far the best agents go in terms of achieving the optimal performance. Furthermore, the authors should clarify why the auxiliary-task weights (λ*) were fixed at 1 rather than being treated as hyperparameters or validated experimentally.

The paper could also benefit from more ablation studies on Atari to see if the per-component contributions match the Labyrinth patterns. Additionally, the authors should clarify that the percentages in Figure 3's legend refer to Labyrinth (not clear in the text).

Some minor corrections are also needed, such as correcting the formatting of Equation 1, where λc should be inside the summation. Additionally, the legend of Figure 3 should explain what "Clip" means for dueling networks.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of deep RL. With some minor revisions to address the above points, it has the potential to be even more impactful.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide a clear quantification of the extra computational cost incurred by the auxiliary tasks?
* Why did you fix the auxiliary-task weights (λ*) at 1 rather than treating them as hyperparameters or validating them experimentally?
* Can you provide more evidence to support the effectiveness of the feature-control auxiliary task?
* How far do the best agents go in terms of achieving the optimal performance?
* Can you provide more ablation studies on Atari to see if the per-component contributions match the Labyrinth patterns?

Additional feedback:

* The paper could benefit from more discussion on the relationship between the auxiliary tasks and the main policy. How do the auxiliary tasks influence the representation learning of the agent?
* The authors could consider exploring other types of auxiliary tasks that could be used to improve feature learning in deep RL.
* The paper could benefit from more analysis on the robustness of the UNREAL agent to hyperparameter settings. How sensitive is the performance of the agent to changes in the hyperparameters?
