This paper proposes a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning by training agents with auxiliary tasks to learn stronger feature representations. The authors introduce two pseudo-control tasks: control change in pixel intensity and control activation of latent features. Additionally, they add a supervised regression task to predict immediate reward after a sequence, learned offline via skewed replay sampling to balance reward/no-reward. The empirical results show that agents perform strongly on discrete-action-continuous-space tasks and reach baseline performance in approximately 10 times fewer iterations.

I recommend accepting this paper. The approach is novel and leverages the agent's ability to influence the environment as a learning signal. The presentation is clear, and the methodology is generally sound. The authors acknowledge the hyperparameter differences between A3C and UNREAL and use top-3 reporting to mitigate this.

However, there are a few areas that could be improved. The auxiliary tasks are described at a high level, and more low-level detail is needed. For example, introducing L_PC before the appendix would be helpful. Furthermore, the authors could provide more experimental analysis of the auxiliary tasks themselves. Pixel/feature control appears to be the most impactful and warrants deeper, isolated study and metrics beyond RL performance.

Some specific questions I would like the authors to address:

1. Can you provide more details on how the auxiliary tasks are implemented, such as the architecture and hyperparameters used?
2. How do the auxiliary tasks affect the learning process, and what are the key factors that contribute to their success?
3. Can you provide more analysis on the performance of the individual auxiliary tasks, such as pixel control and feature control, and how they contribute to the overall performance of the agent?
4. How do the authors plan to extend this approach to more complex tasks and environments?

Additional feedback:

* The paper could benefit from more visualizations and illustrations to help explain the concepts and results.
* The authors could provide more discussion on the implications of their approach and how it relates to other work in the field.
* The paper could be improved by providing more details on the experimental setup and hyperparameters used.
* The authors could consider providing more analysis on the robustness of their approach to different hyperparameters and environments.

Overall, this paper presents a novel and promising approach to deep reinforcement learning, and with some revisions to address the above points, it has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
