This paper introduces a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning by augmenting standard RL with auxiliary tasks designed to learn stronger feature representations. The core idea is to train agents not only to maximize extrinsic reward but also to control various aspects of the sensory stream – changes in pixel intensity and activations of latent features – and to predict immediate rewards. Empirical results demonstrate significant performance gains on both Atari and a challenging 3D Labyrinth environment, achieving state-of-the-art results and substantial speedups in learning. The approach is well-motivated, leveraging the agent's ability to actively influence its environment as a learning signal, and contrasts favourably with passive unsupervised or model-based learning methods.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper presents a compelling and novel contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. The approach is technically sound, the experimental results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method, and the potential impact is significant. The significant speedup in learning, particularly on the more complex Labyrinth environment, is a major strength.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper clearly articulates the problem of sparse rewards and the limitations of relying solely on extrinsic feedback. The introduction of auxiliary control tasks is a clever way to provide denser, more frequent learning signals, guiding the agent toward developing useful representations. The combination of control tasks (pixel and feature control) with reward prediction appears particularly effective. The experimental setup is robust, with thorough comparison to baseline A3C agents and ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component. The reporting of top-3 results across multiple hyperparameters helps mitigate the sensitivity to hyperparameter tuning, increasing confidence in the observed performance improvements. The acknowledgement of differences in hyperparameters between A3C and UNREAL further supports the rigor of the evaluation. The inclusion of both Atari and Labyrinth benchmarks strengthens the generalizability of the findings. The results on Labyrinth are particularly impressive, showcasing the method's ability to tackle complex, partially observable environments. The authors' point about contrasting their work with passive unsupervised methods is well-taken, as their approach leverages the agent's agency in a more direct manner.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong overall, a few minor improvements could further enhance its clarity and impact. The description of the auxiliary tasks, particularly the feature control task, feels somewhat high-level. Providing more low-level detail about the implementation – for example, introducing the L_PC loss function (mentioned in the text) before the appendix – would improve understanding. The intuition behind selecting pixel changes and feature activations as control targets is reasonable, but a more detailed explanation of *why* these particular choices are effective would be valuable. 

The analysis of the auxiliary tasks themselves is somewhat limited. While the paper demonstrates that adding these tasks improves RL performance, it doesn’t provide much insight into *which* tasks are most impactful or *why*. A more isolated study of the pixel and feature control tasks, with metrics beyond just RL performance (e.g., the variance of pixel changes achieved, the entropy of feature activations), could reveal important insights and guide future research. Furthermore, a visualization of what the agent is actually *doing* when performing these auxiliary tasks (e.g., showing the areas of the image where pixel changes are being maximized) would make the concepts more concrete.

The discussion of the reward prediction task would benefit from clarifying why a multiclass classification loss was chosen over a mean-squared error loss. It might also be helpful to analyze the learned reward predictor itself – for example, by visualizing the features it uses to predict reward.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing pixel changes and feature activations as the specific control targets? Were other potential control targets considered, and if so, why were these ultimately selected?
2.  The paper mentions that input reconstruction (as a baseline) hurt final performance. Do you have a hypothesis as to *why* this occurred, beyond the idea of focusing on irrelevant parts of the image? Was the reconstruction loss applied to the raw pixels, or to some feature representation?
3.  Regarding the reward prediction task, why was a multiclass cross-entropy loss chosen instead of a mean-squared error loss? What impact did this choice have on the learned features and overall performance?
4.  Can you provide some qualitative examples of what the agent *does* when actively maximizing the pixel control or feature control auxiliary rewards? (e.g., screenshots or visualizations illustrating the agent’s behavior).
5.  The appendix includes detailed implementation details. Could you expand on the computational cost associated with the auxiliary tasks, particularly in terms of memory usage and training time?
