## Review of “UNREAL: Reinforcement Learning with Auxiliary Control Tasks”

This paper presents UNREAL, a novel deep reinforcement learning agent that leverages unsupervised auxiliary tasks—both control and reward-focused—to improve learning speed and performance. The core idea of learning to control various features of the sensory stream alongside maximizing extrinsic reward is well-motivated and intuitively appealing. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over A3C on both the challenging Labyrinth environment and a suite of Atari games, achieving impressive results, notably averaging 880% human performance on Atari and a 10x speedup in learning on Labyrinth. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the architecture and experimental setup, making it accessible to readers familiar with deep RL.

I recommend **Acceptance** of this paper. The substantial performance gains and demonstrated speedup represent a valuable contribution to the field. While the underlying principle – that well-chosen auxiliary tasks can aid feature learning – isn’t entirely surprising, this work provides a compelling proof-of-concept and a robust implementation that significantly advances the state-of-the-art.

The strength of this paper lies in its empirical results. The improvements on both Atari and Labyrinth are substantial and consistently observed across multiple hyperparameters. The ablation studies further support the effectiveness of the proposed auxiliary tasks. The analysis demonstrating increased robustness to hyperparameter settings is also a positive indicator of the agent’s generalizability. However, the architecture of UNREAL appears quite complex, with numerous components and weighting parameters. While clearly explained, understanding the interplay between these elements requires careful consideration. 

To further strengthen this work, I would appreciate more insight into the computational resources required to train the UNREAL agent. Specifically, what hardware (number/type of GPUs, CPU) and training time were needed for each environment? Additionally, given the complexity of the architecture, releasing the source code would be immensely beneficial to the research community, facilitating reproducibility and further exploration.

Finally, while the ablation studies are helpful, it would be interesting to see a more detailed analysis of the contribution of each auxiliary task individually. For example, how does the performance change when only the pixel control task is used, versus only the reward prediction task, across a wider range of environments?
