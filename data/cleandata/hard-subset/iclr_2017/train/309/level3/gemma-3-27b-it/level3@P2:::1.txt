This paper introduces UNREAL, a deep reinforcement learning agent that augments standard A3C with auxiliary control and reward prediction tasks. The core idea is to learn a richer representation by simultaneously maximizing multiple pseudo-reward functions, enabling faster learning and improved performance. The authors demonstrate significant gains on both the challenging 3D Labyrinth environment and a suite of Atari games, achieving state-of-the-art results and increased robustness to hyperparameter settings.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a strong contribution to the field of deep reinforcement learning, demonstrating a clear improvement over existing methods with a well-motivated and thoroughly evaluated approach.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles a relevant and important problem: improving the sample efficiency and robustness of deep reinforcement learning. The motivation for exploring auxiliary tasks is well-grounded in the idea that richer representations, learned through diverse objectives, can accelerate learning and enhance generalization. The authors effectively position their work within the existing literature on related approaches like options, Horde, UVFA, and successor representations, clearly highlighting the novelty of their contribution – specifically, the simultaneous learning of diverse auxiliary tasks without direct use of their policies, and the combination of control *and* reward prediction tasks.

The empirical results are compelling. The 87% human-normalised performance on Labyrinth, alongside a 10x speedup in learning, is a substantial achievement. The impressive results on Atari (averaging 880% expert human performance) further solidify the effectiveness of the UNREAL architecture. The ablation studies performed in Labyrinth convincingly demonstrate the benefit of each auxiliary task and their combined effect. The analysis of robustness to hyperparameter settings is a valuable addition, showing that UNREAL is less sensitive to tuning than the A3C baseline. The included figures and videos provide excellent visualization of the results and agent behaviour.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the complex architecture and methodology. Readers familiar with deep reinforcement learning will find it easy to follow the technical details and understand the contributions of the work. The inclusion of implementation details in the appendix is appreciated, enabling reproducibility.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the results are impressive, the complexity of the UNREAL architecture is considerable. The combination of A3C, auxiliary control tasks, reward prediction, and experience replay with skewed sampling creates a system with many hyperparameters and moving parts. While the ablation studies help to understand the individual contributions, understanding the optimal interaction between these components remains somewhat challenging.

It would be helpful to have more information about the computational resources required to train the UNREAL agent, especially for the Labyrinth environment. Specifying the number of CPUs, GPUs, and training time would provide a better understanding of the practical feasibility of applying this approach to other domains.

Given the complexity of the implementation, releasing the source code would be a significant contribution to the community and would facilitate further research and development in this area.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. Could you provide details on the computational resources (e.g., number of GPUs, CPU cores, training time) required to train the UNREAL agent on both the Labyrinth and Atari environments?
2. Do you plan to release the source code for the UNREAL agent to the research community?
3. The paper mentions that pixel control helps but that input reconstruction can hurt performance. Can you elaborate on why reconstructing the input might be detrimental to learning a good policy? Is it due to the agent focusing on irrelevant features, or are there other factors at play?
4. The reward prediction task uses a simpler feedforward network than the main policy network. Have you explored using a similar LSTM-based architecture for the reward prediction task, and if so, did it impact performance?
