## Review of UNREAL: Reinforcement Learning with Auxiliary Tasks

This paper proposes a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning (DRL) by augmenting the standard A3C algorithm with unsupervised auxiliary tasks – both control-based and reward-prediction-based. The authors hypothesize, and demonstrate empirically, that learning to control various features of the environment’s sensorimotor stream facilitates faster learning and improved performance. The UNREAL agent achieves significantly better results than A3C on both the challenging Labyrinth 3D environment and a suite of Atari games, exhibiting a substantial speedup in learning. Overall, this is a solid conference contribution presenting a clear and well-motivated idea with strong empirical validation.

**Accept** – The paper tackles an important problem in DRL – sample efficiency and robustness – and provides a compelling solution with substantial empirical support. The performance gains observed on both Labyrinth and Atari are noteworthy.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of leveraging auxiliary tasks to shape the agent’s representation is well-motivated. The authors clearly articulate how focusing on controlling different aspects of the environment (pixel changes, network features, reward prediction) can lead to a more robust and efficient learning process. This builds on existing work in representation learning and temporal abstraction, and provides a practical implementation within the widely-used A3C framework. The experimental results strongly support the claims. The performance improvements on Labyrinth are particularly impressive, with a 10x speedup in learning and 87% human-level performance. Similarly, the 880% expert human performance on Atari demonstrates the broad applicability of the UNREAL approach. The ablation studies effectively highlight the contribution of each auxiliary task, and the comparison to related approaches (pixel reconstruction, input change prediction) provides valuable insights. The inclusion of robustness analysis with respect to hyperparameters further strengthens the paper.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is generally very strong, several points could be addressed to further improve it:

*   **Computational Resources:** The paper lacks details regarding the computational resources required to train the UNREAL agent. Specifically, information about the number of CPUs/GPUs used, training time, and memory requirements would be valuable for reproducibility and understanding the practical feasibility of the approach.
*   **Code Release:** The authors acknowledge the complexity of the architecture and the reviewer’s request for source code release.  Releasing the code would significantly increase the impact of the work and facilitate further research in this area.
*   **Analysis of Learned Features:** While the paper demonstrates *that* auxiliary tasks improve performance, it doesn’t provide much insight into *why*. Visualizing or analyzing the learned features – perhaps showing which features are most activated during specific tasks or behaviors – could provide a deeper understanding of the mechanism at play.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions robustness to hyperparameters, but a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the auxiliary task weights (λVR, λPC, λRP) would be beneficial. How do performance and learning speed change as these weights are varied?
*   **Reward Prediction Details:** The reward prediction task is framed as a three-class classification problem. The rationale for this choice (vs. regression) could be elaborated on. Also, the method used to balance positive and negative rewards during training could be described more precisely.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a detailed breakdown of the computational resources (number of GPUs/CPUs, training time, memory usage) required to train the UNREAL agent on both the Labyrinth and Atari environments?
2.  Do you have plans to release the source code for this project? If so, when can we expect it to be available?
3.  Can you provide any examples or visualizations of the features learned by the agent, particularly those related to the auxiliary tasks? Do these features seem to correspond to meaningful aspects of the environment?
4.  How sensitive are the results to the weighting of the different loss components in the overall loss function (λVR, λPC, λRP)? Did you perform a systematic exploration of these hyperparameters?
5.  Regarding the reward prediction task, why was a three-class classification approach chosen over a regression approach? What specific method was used to achieve the 50/50 balance of zero and non-zero rewards in the training data?
