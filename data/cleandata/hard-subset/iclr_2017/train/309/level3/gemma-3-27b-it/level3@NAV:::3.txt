## Review of "Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning"

This paper presents a compelling approach to improving reinforcement learning agents by augmenting their training with auxiliary tasks designed to learn stronger feature representations. The core idea – training agents to control aspects of the sensory stream and predict immediate rewards – is novel and intuitively appealing, leveraging the agent’s active interaction with the environment as a learning signal, contrasting it with passive approaches. The empirical results are strong, demonstrating significant performance improvements on both Atari and a challenging 3D Labyrinth environment, with a reported 10x speedup in learning. The authors convincingly argue that this approach addresses limitations of partial observability by promoting controllable, internal feature learning.

I recommend **Accept** this paper. The approach is both novel and demonstrably effective, representing a valuable contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. The results provide strong support for the claim that auxiliary tasks can significantly improve data efficiency and robustness.

The strength of this paper lies in its solid methodology and impressive empirical validation. The authors acknowledge and address potential confounding factors, such as hyperparameter differences between A3C and UNREAL, and utilize top-3 reporting to mitigate these concerns. The experimental setup is well-defined, and the comparisons to baseline methods are thorough. The analysis of the auxiliary tasks, particularly the pixel control task, is promising and lays the groundwork for future investigation. The presented speedup in learning is a significant practical advantage.

However, the paper could be strengthened by providing more detailed insight into the auxiliary tasks themselves. While the high-level concept is clear, a more granular explanation of the implementation (e.g., introducing L_PC earlier in the text, rather than only in the appendix) would be beneficial. Furthermore, the experimental analysis of the auxiliary tasks feels somewhat incomplete. While the paper demonstrates that adding more tasks improves performance, a dedicated, isolated study of each task – examining its impact on learned features, rather than solely relying on RL performance as a proxy – would be highly valuable. What specific features are being learned via pixel control versus feature control? Providing metrics beyond overall RL performance (e.g., feature visualization, transfer learning experiments) would bolster the claims about the quality of the learned representations.

Finally, I have a few clarifying questions for the authors:

*   Regarding the skewed replay sampling for reward prediction, how sensitive is the performance to the 0.5 weighting used for zero/non-zero rewards?
*   The paper mentions that the feature control task produced slightly worse results than pixel control in initial experiments. Can you elaborate on potential reasons for this difference, and what modifications might improve the performance of feature control?
*   Could you provide more details on the computational cost associated with adding the auxiliary tasks, beyond the reported speedup in reaching baseline performance? Is there a significant increase in training time per iteration?
