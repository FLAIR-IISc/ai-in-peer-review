## Review of UNREAL: Auxiliary Tasks for Deep Reinforcement Learning

This paper presents UNREAL, a deep reinforcement learning agent that learns to maximize cumulative reward alongside a multitude of auxiliary tasks defined as pseudo-rewards. The core idea is to develop a more robust and efficient representation by simultaneously training policies to control various features of the sensorimotor stream. The authors demonstrate significant performance improvements on both Atari games and the challenging Labyrinth environment, achieving state-of-the-art results and notably faster learning speeds. The paper is well-written and the empirical results are compelling.

**Decision:** **Accept** with high confidence. The paper addresses an important problem – improving sample efficiency and robustness in deep reinforcement learning – and proposes a novel and effective solution. The thorough experimental evaluation demonstrates a clear advantage over existing methods.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper convincingly argues that enriching the learning signal beyond simple reward maximization can lead to superior representations and more efficient learning. The approach of treating different aspects of the environment as separate reinforcement learning problems is well-motivated, building on existing work in auxiliary tasks and representation learning. The experimental results on both Atari and Labyrinth strongly support the claims. Specifically, the significant speedup in learning (10x on Labyrinth) and the substantial performance gains (880% expert human performance on Atari, 87% on Labyrinth) are particularly impressive. The ablation studies presented effectively demonstrate the contribution of each auxiliary task, further strengthening the validation of the proposed approach. The comparison to existing methods (A3C and prioritized dueling DQN) demonstrates the clear advantages of UNREAL.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

While the paper is strong, addressing the following points could further enhance its impact:

*   **Computational Cost:** While the performance gains are significant, it would be valuable to quantify the additional computational cost introduced by the auxiliary tasks. Specifically, how much does training time increase, and which auxiliary components are the most computationally expensive?
*   **Clarification of Auxiliary Policies:** The abstract and introduction could be clarified to explicitly state that separate policies are learned for *each* auxiliary task, rather than a single policy optimized for all rewards simultaneously.
*   **Feature Control Task Validation:** The empirical support for the feature control auxiliary task appears weaker than for the other tasks. The results presented in Figure 5 are not entirely convincing and raise concerns regarding stability and convergence. A more detailed analysis of the performance of this task would be beneficial.
*   **Training Duration:** The authors mention that agent performance was still improving at the end of training. Reporting the final performance achieved with extended training, and indicating how far the best agents progressed, would provide a more complete picture of the potential of the approach.
*   **Auxiliary Task Weights:** The auxiliary task weights (λ\*) are fixed at 1 throughout the experiments. It would be interesting to explore treating these weights as hyperparameters and investigating their optimal values through experimentation.
*   **Off-Policy Clarification:** The paper utilizes off-policy learning for the auxiliary tasks. It's crucial to explicitly state that the Q-learning updates aren't “true” Q-learning because they are based on empirical n-step returns, not perfect oracle returns.
*   **Minor Corrections:** A few minor formatting and grammatical corrections are suggested:
    *   The equation for the policy gradient loss (L\_pi) is presented as a maximization but is written as a minimization problem.
    *   In Eq. 1, lambda\_c should be inside the summation.
    *   Below Eq. 1, r\_t^(c) should be r\_{t+k}^{(c)}.
    *   Figure 2 is not referenced in the text.
    *   Figure 1(d) should be referenced in Section 3.3.
    *   Grammar: “the features discovered in this manner is shared” → “are shared”.
    *   Eq. 2 references L\_PC, which is not explicitly defined in the equation.
    *   Legend of Figure 3: explain what “Clip” means for dueling networks.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a breakdown of the computational overhead associated with each auxiliary task?
*   What is the rationale behind fixing the auxiliary task weights (λ\*) at 1? Were any attempts made to optimize these weights experimentally?
*   Could you elaborate on the implementation details of the off-policy learning for the auxiliary tasks and address that these updates are not true Q-learning as they are based on empirical returns?
*   In the experiments, what was the actual value of lambda\_PC, and how might it change with different environments?
*   What is the furthest progress that the agent would eventually reach when the experiment was continued beyond the reported runs?
