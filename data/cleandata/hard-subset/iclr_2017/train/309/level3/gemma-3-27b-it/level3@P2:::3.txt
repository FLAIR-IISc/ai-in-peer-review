This paper proposes a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning by augmenting a standard A3C agent with auxiliary tasks designed to learn more robust and useful feature representations. The core idea is to train the agent to not only maximize extrinsic reward but also to control various aspects of the sensory input stream – specifically, changes in pixel intensity and the activation of internal network features – as well as to predict immediate reward. The authors demonstrate significant performance improvements on both the Labyrinth 3D environment and a suite of Atari games, achieving state-of-the-art results and substantial speedups in learning.

**Decision:** Accept

**Reasons:** The paper presents a compelling and well-motivated approach to improving reinforcement learning agents. The experimental results are strong, demonstrating a clear benefit from incorporating the proposed auxiliary tasks. The approach addresses a relevant and important problem – improving sample efficiency and robustness in deep RL – and offers a novel solution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The motivation for the work is clear: environments often provide sparse extrinsic rewards, and learning useful representations is crucial for efficient learning. The authors convincingly argue that treating features of the sensory stream as pseudo-rewards for reinforcement learning can lead to more effective representations than traditional unsupervised approaches like reconstruction. The architecture, UNREAL, is well-described, and the integration of auxiliary tasks with the A3C framework appears seamless. 

The empirical results are particularly impressive. The 87% human-normalised performance on the Labyrinth tasks and 880% on Atari represent significant improvements over existing methods. The reported 10x speedup in learning is also a substantial benefit, highlighting the practical impact of the approach. The thorough experimentation, including ablation studies and comparisons to alternative approaches like pixel reconstruction, strengthens the conclusions. The robustness analysis across hyperparameters further validates the effectiveness of the proposed method. The authors also acknowledge the potential downsides and limitations, fostering trust in the work.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, several minor points could be addressed to further improve its clarity and impact. 

*   **Auxiliary Task Detail:** The descriptions of the pixel control and feature control tasks could be expanded. While the appendix provides more detail, introducing key equations or parameters (e.g., L_PC) earlier in the main text would improve readability.
*   **Ablation Study Analysis:** The ablation study shows the individual contributions of each auxiliary task. Further analysis of *why* certain tasks are more effective than others would be valuable. For instance, what properties of the pixel control task make it so impactful?
*   **Reward Prediction:** The intuition behind skewed sampling for reward prediction is sound, but a brief discussion of the potential biases introduced by this sampling strategy would be beneficial.
*  **Impact of Hyperparameter Tuning:** A more detailed explanation of how hyperparameters were tuned for the different methods (A3C, UNREAL, and ablations) is necessary. The authors acknowledge differences, but a thorough comparison of tuning strategies would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Visualizations:** While a video link is provided, including a figure illustrating the pixel control grid overlaid on an image from Labyrinth or Atari would help readers visualize the task more concretely.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of adding the auxiliary tasks? Does it significantly increase training time per step, and if so, how is this balanced by the speedup in learning?
2.  You mention that feature control outperformed reward prediction in your preliminary results. Can you share more details on these experiments and discuss potential reasons for this difference?
3.  The skewed sampling for reward prediction introduces a bias towards rewarding events. How sensitive are the results to the specific sampling ratio (e.g., 50/50)? Have you explored different ratios?
4.  The paper focuses on value-based RL methods (A3C). Do you anticipate similar benefits from applying this approach to policy gradient methods directly?
5.  The performance improvement on Atari is substantial. Could you provide more granular results, such as the improvement on individual Atari games or categories of games (e.g., break-out style vs. action games)?
