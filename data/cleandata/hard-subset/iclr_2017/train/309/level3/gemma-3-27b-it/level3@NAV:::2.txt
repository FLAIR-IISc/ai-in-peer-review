## Review of "Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning"

This paper presents a compelling approach to improving data efficiency in deep reinforcement learning by incorporating domain-independent auxiliary tasks – control, reward prediction, and value replay – alongside the primary reinforcement learning objective. The core idea of sharing CNN+LSTM features across these tasks to develop a more robust and informative representation is well-motivated and aligns with recent trends in unsupervised and self-supervised learning. The results are particularly impressive, demonstrating significant performance gains over A3C on both Atari and the challenging Labyrinth environments. The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the motivations, methodology, and experimental setup, making the core contributions readily accessible.

**Accept** – The clear performance improvements and strong experimental validation justify acceptance. The proposed UNREAL agent represents a significant step forward in improving the efficiency of deep RL, particularly in complex environments.

The empirical results are convincing, consistently showing improvements over the baseline A3C. The 10x speedup in Labyrinth learning is especially noteworthy. The ablation studies, while not exhaustive, suggest that the auxiliary tasks are indeed contributing to the overall performance gain. The human-normalized scores are a useful metric for evaluating performance. 

However, several points could further strengthen the paper:

*   **Computational Cost:** The paper should quantify the additional computational cost incurred by incorporating the auxiliary tasks. Specifically, report the training speed loss and identify which auxiliary components are the most computationally expensive.
*   **Policy Clarification:** Clarify in the abstract and introduction that separate policies are learned for each auxiliary task, rather than a single policy optimizing all rewards simultaneously.
*   **Feature-Control Validation:** The empirical validation for the feature-control auxiliary task (Section 3.1) appears weak. Figure 5 is unconvincing, and the changing tasks during training raise concerns about stability and convergence. More robust evaluation is needed.
*   **Training Duration:** Consider training the agents for longer, as performance was still improving in some cases. Reporting the maximum achieved performance for the best agents would be valuable.
*   **Auxiliary Task Weights:** Investigate the sensitivity of performance to the auxiliary task weights (λ*). Treating these as hyperparameters and performing experimental validation could yield further improvements. Also, explicitly report the value of λPC, which is currently assumed to be 1.
*   **Off-Policy Training:** Explicitly mention that the auxiliary tasks are trained off-policy and not with “true” Q-learning due to the multi-step empirical rewards.
*   **Minor Corrections:**
    *   Equation 1: lambda_c should be inside the summation.
    *   Below Eq.1: r_t^(c) should be r_{t+k}^{(c)}.
    *   Correct the minor typographical errors and figure references mentioned in the supervisor’s notes (e.g., Figure 2 and Figure 1(d) references, Figure 3 annotations, Figure 5 caption).
    *   Fix: “the features discovered in this manner is shared” → “are shared”.
    *   Eq.2: Define or explicitly include L_PC in the equation.
    *   Figure 3: Explain “Clip” in the legend.
*   **Ablation Studies (Atari):** Conduct more ablation studies on Atari to determine if the per-component contributions observed in Labyrinth generalize.
*    **Percentages in Figure 3:** Clarify that the percentages in Figure 3’s legend refer to Labyrinth.
*   **Appendix:** Specify the location of the supplementary materials.



These suggestions are aimed at further strengthening an already strong paper and are not preconditions for acceptance. I encourage the authors to address these points to maximize the impact of their work.
