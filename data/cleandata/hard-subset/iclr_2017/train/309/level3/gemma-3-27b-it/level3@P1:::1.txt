## Review of UNREAL: Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning

This paper proposes a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning by augmenting a standard A3C agent with a suite of auxiliary tasks designed to improve representation learning. These tasks include auxiliary control tasks (maximizing changes in pixels and network activations) and auxiliary reward tasks (predicting immediate rewards). The resulting UNREAL agent demonstrates significantly improved performance on both the challenging 3D Labyrinth environment and a suite of Atari games, exhibiting both faster learning and increased robustness to hyperparameter settings.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a compelling and well-supported approach to improving deep reinforcement learning through the strategic incorporation of auxiliary tasks. The substantial performance gains on both Labyrinth and Atari, coupled with the demonstrated improvements in learning speed and robustness, strongly suggest a significant contribution to the field.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of leveraging auxiliary tasks to enhance representation learning is well-motivated. The authors correctly identify the limitations of solely relying on extrinsic rewards, particularly in environments with sparse reward signals. The proposed tasks—controlling pixels, network activations, and predicting rewards—are intuitively aligned with the goal of developing a more informative and robust state representation. The architecture, while complex, appears thoughtfully designed to balance the benefits of parallel learning with the efficient use of experience replay.

The empirical results are particularly convincing. The substantial performance gains on Labyrinth (averaging 87% of human performance, a 10x speedup) and Atari (880% expert human performance) are impressive and clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the UNREAL agent. The ablation studies, showcasing the contribution of each auxiliary task, further strengthen the validity of the findings.  The results also point to increased robustness to hyperparameter settings – a crucial attribute for practical application of RL algorithms. The detailed comparison to alternative approaches like pixel reconstruction underscores the advantage of the proposed control-based auxiliary tasks.

The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation, methodology, and results. The related work section provides a comprehensive overview of existing approaches to representation learning and auxiliary task learning in RL, appropriately positioning the UNREAL agent within the broader research landscape. The inclusion of extensive experimental details, including hyperparameter settings and implementation notes, enhances the reproducibility of the research.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the performance gains are significant, the complexity of the UNREAL architecture is notable. The interactions between the A3C agent and the various auxiliary tasks, along with the different optimization schemes, could be challenging for other researchers to implement and extend. Therefore, making the source code publicly available would be invaluable to the research community.  

It would be helpful to understand the computational resources required to train the UNREAL agent, particularly on the more demanding Labyrinth environment. Providing details on the hardware used (e.g., number and type of GPUs) and training time would allow for a more accurate assessment of the practical feasibility of the approach.

It would be interesting to see further exploration of the interplay between different auxiliary tasks. The current ablation studies provide valuable insights, but a more systematic investigation of task combinations and weighting strategies could potentially reveal even more optimal configurations. Further analysis of *what* features are being learned as a result of these auxiliary tasks would be valuable. Visualization of learned representations or a detailed analysis of network activations could provide a deeper understanding of the underlying mechanisms driving the performance gains.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What were the computational resources (e.g., number and type of GPUs, CPU) and approximate training time required to achieve the reported results on Labyrinth and Atari?
2.  Will the authors be releasing the source code for the UNREAL agent to facilitate reproducibility and further research?
3.  Have the authors explored different weighting strategies for the various loss components in Equation 2? If so, could they share any insights into the sensitivity of performance to these weights?
4. Can the authors elaborate on any insights into the type of features being learned by the agent through the auxiliary tasks? Are there specific network activations that are strongly correlated with successful performance?
