## Review of "Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning"

This paper proposes a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning by augmenting standard RL agents with a suite of auxiliary tasks. The core idea is that learning to control various aspects of the sensory stream – pixel changes, latent feature activations, and immediate reward prediction – fosters the development of richer, more robust representations, ultimately leading to improved performance and learning speed. The authors demonstrate significant improvements on both the Labyrinth 3D environment and a suite of Atari games, achieving state-of-the-art results and substantially faster learning compared to existing methods.

**Decision: Accept**

This paper presents a compelling and well-supported contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. The proposed method demonstrates clear and significant performance gains across diverse environments, and the justification for the approach—building better representations through multi-faceted control—is intuitive and well-motivated. The experimental results are convincing and the writing is generally clear.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary strength of this work lies in its innovative approach to representation learning within the RL framework. The authors move beyond traditional unsupervised learning methods like autoencoding or environment modeling and instead propose actively *controlling* different aspects of the sensory input. This is a crucial distinction; by actively learning to influence the environment and internal representations, the agent develops a more grounded and potentially more transferable understanding of its surroundings. The introduction of both pixel control and feature control tasks offers a clever way to encourage the agent to explore and understand different levels of abstraction in the environment. The reward prediction task, coupled with the skewed replay buffer, is a particularly insightful addition, addressing the common issue of sparse rewards and accelerating learning.

The experimental results provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed UNREAL agent. The substantial improvements on Labyrinth, achieving human-level performance and a 10x speedup in learning, are particularly impressive. The strong performance on Atari, surpassing previous state-of-the-art results, further demonstrates the generalizability of the approach. The authors’ acknowledgement and attempted mitigation of hyperparameter sensitivity through reporting results from multiple settings adds credibility to their findings. 

The related work section is comprehensive and accurately positions the paper within the existing literature on reinforcement learning, representation learning, and auxiliary tasks.  The authors clearly articulate how their work builds upon and distinguishes itself from prior research, such as UVFAs, successor representations, and Horde architectures.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong overall, a few minor points could be addressed to further improve clarity and impact.  The descriptions of the auxiliary tasks, particularly pixel and feature control, could benefit from more detailed elaboration *within* the main body of the paper, rather than relying heavily on the appendix. For example, introducing the notation *L<sub>PC</sub>* before relegating the details to the appendix would improve readability. 

Furthermore, while the ablation studies demonstrate the benefit of combining the different auxiliary tasks, a more in-depth analysis of the individual tasks themselves would be valuable.  Specifically, it would be insightful to understand *why* pixel control and feature control are effective. What types of features are being learned? How do they contribute to improved performance? Examining the learned features and activations associated with each auxiliary task would provide a deeper understanding of the underlying mechanisms.  Metrics beyond just final RL performance, such as the diversity or interpretability of the learned features, could be included.

Finally, exploring the transferability of the learned representations to different, unseen tasks would strengthen the claims about the quality of the learned representations. Could the features learned on Labyrinth be effectively utilized in a different 3D environment?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide examples of the types of features that are learned through the feature control auxiliary task? Visualizing the activations of the hidden units being controlled would be insightful.
2.  The reward prediction task leverages skewed sampling from the replay buffer. What specific criteria are used to determine the skewing factor, and how sensitive is the performance to this parameter?
3.  You mention that pixel control outperforms reward prediction. Do you have any hypotheses about why controlling pixel changes is more effective than simply predicting reward?
4.  Have you investigated the computational cost associated with the added auxiliary tasks? Does the increased complexity significantly impact training time or memory requirements?
5.  Could you elaborate on the potential challenges of applying this approach to environments with extremely high-dimensional or complex state spaces?  What modifications might be necessary?
