## Review for ICLR 2017

This paper introduces a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning that leverages auxiliary tasks – control tasks and reward prediction – to improve data efficiency and robustness. The core idea is to learn a shared representation that benefits from simultaneously optimizing multiple reinforcement learning problems, focusing the representation on relevant features of the environment. The results presented demonstrate significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods on both Atari and the challenging Labyrinth environment, with a substantial speedup in learning and impressive final performance.

**Decision:** Accept with minor revisions. The paper tackles an important problem in reinforcement learning – sample efficiency – and proposes a compelling solution with strong empirical validation. The improvements over existing methods are substantial, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper effectively motivates its approach by highlighting the limitations of relying solely on extrinsic rewards, especially in environments where these rewards are sparse. The concept of learning to control different features of the sensorimotor stream, akin to unsupervised learning but framed as reinforcement learning problems, is insightful. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of this approach. The 880% expert human performance on Atari and the 10x speedup in learning on Labyrinth are impressive achievements. The ablation studies provide valuable insight into the contribution of each auxiliary task. The clear comparison to existing methods like A3C and Prioritized DQN strengthens the validity of the findings. The inclusion of both Atari and Labyrinth demonstrates the generalizability of the approach.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong overall, several minor points could be addressed to further enhance its clarity and impact. 

1. **Computational Cost:** The paper would benefit from a quantification of the additional computational cost introduced by the auxiliary tasks. Specifically, what is the impact on training speed, and which auxiliary components are the most computationally expensive?

2. **Clarification of Auxiliary Policies:** The abstract and introduction could be clarified to explicitly state that *separate* policies are learned for each auxiliary task, rather than a single policy attempting to optimize all rewards simultaneously.

3. **Feature Control Validation:** The empirical validation of the feature control auxiliary task appears weaker than that of other components. Figure 5 is not particularly convincing and raises concerns about stability and convergence. A more robust evaluation or further analysis of this component would be beneficial.

4. **Training Length & Convergence:** The experiments suggest that agent performance was still improving at the end of the reported training runs. It would be helpful to report how far the best agents eventually progress with longer training and to discuss convergence characteristics.

5. **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The choice to fix the weights of the auxiliary tasks (λ\*) at 1 is somewhat surprising. A more systematic exploration of these weights as hyperparameters, potentially through validation experiments, could yield further improvements.

6. **Off-Policy Clarification:**  The paper should explicitly acknowledge that the auxiliary tasks are trained off-policy, and that this deviates from “true” Q-learning due to the use of multi-step empirical rewards.

7. **Minor Corrections:**
    *   The presentation of L\_pi = -… appears as a maximization but is written as a loss to be minimized.
    *   In Eq. 1, lambda\_c should be inside the summation.
    *   Below Eq. 1, r\_t^(c) should be r\_{t+k}^(c).
    *   Figure 2 is not referenced in the text.
    *   Figure 1(d) should be referenced in Section 3.3.
    *   “the features discovered in this manner is shared” should be “are shared”.
    *   Eq. 2 references L\_PC which is not defined or explicitly included in the equation.

8. **Figure 3 Legend:** Clarify what “Clip” means in the legend of Figure 3.

9. **Ablation Studies (Atari):** More ablation studies on Atari would be helpful to determine if the per-component contributions observed in Labyrinth generalize to this different domain.

10. **Figure 3 Percentages:** Clarify that the percentages in the legend of Figure 3 refer to Labyrinth.

11. **Figure References:** Correct the incorrect figure references in the text (e.g., 4.1.2 “Figure 3 (right)” actually refers to the top-left plot).

12. **Figure 5 Caption:** The caption of Figure 5 and the accompanying text are inconsistent; the text claims the figure shows results on three Labyrinth levels, while the figure itself seems to depict only two.

13. **Appendix Details:** Expand on the statement “Further details are included in the supplementary materials” by specifying where those materials are located.

14. **Report lambda_PC:** Report the value of lambda_PC (assumed to be 1) explicitly.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational overhead associated with each of the auxiliary tasks, and how this scales with the complexity of the environment?
2.  The feature control task shows less compelling results. What potential factors might be limiting its performance, and what modifications could be explored to improve its effectiveness?
3.  You mention that the reward prediction task samples rewarding events with a skewed distribution. How sensitive are the results to the specific skewing factor (e.g., the 0.5 probability of a non-zero reward)?
4.  You fixed the lambda values. What experiments did you conduct to estimate this parameter? Would a schedule, adapting this value throughout training, change results?
