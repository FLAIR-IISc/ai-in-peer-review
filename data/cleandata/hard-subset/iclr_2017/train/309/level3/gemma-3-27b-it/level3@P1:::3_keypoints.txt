- Decision: Accept.

- Contribution: Proposes augmenting RL agents with auxiliary tasks to learn richer, more robust representations.

- Core idea: Actively controlling pixel changes, latent feature activations, and predicting immediate rewards fosters better representations and faster learning.

- Results: Significant improvements on Labyrinth and Atari, achieving state-of-the-art performance and substantially faster learning (e.g., ~10x speedup on Labyrinth).

- Motivation: Active control of sensory and internal signals is more grounded and transferable than passive unsupervised objectives.

- Auxiliary tasks: Pixel control and feature control encourage exploration at different abstraction levels; reward prediction with skewed replay addresses sparse rewards.

- Experimental rigor: Convincing results, hyperparameter sensitivity partially mitigated by reporting multiple settings.

- Related work: Comprehensive positioning relative to UVFAs, successor representations, Horde, and other prior work.

- Clarity suggestion: Move more detailed descriptions of auxiliary tasks (e.g., notation like L_PC) from appendix into the main text for readability.

- Analysis suggestion: Provide deeper analysis of individual auxiliary tasks—why they work, what features are learned, and how they contribute to performance.

- Evaluation suggestion: Include metrics beyond final RL performance (e.g., feature diversity/interpretability) and test transferability of learned representations to unseen tasks.

- Questions for authors: Request visualization/examples of learned features; details and sensitivity of skewed replay sampling; why pixel control outperforms reward prediction; computational cost impact; and applicability to extremely high-dimensional or complex state spaces.
