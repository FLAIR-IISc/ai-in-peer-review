

This paper proposes a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning by training agents with auxiliary tasks to learn stronger feature representations. The authors introduce two pseudo-control tasks—controlling pixel intensity changes and controlling activation of latent features—and add a supervised regression task to predict immediate reward after a sequence, learned offline via skewed replay sampling. The resulting UNREAL agent significantly outperforms baselines, achieving 880% expert human performance on Atari and 87% on Labyrinth, while reaching baseline performance in ~10x fewer iterations.

I recommend acceptance. The paper tackles an important problem in RL—improving representation learning for better data efficiency and performance—and makes a compelling case for why controllable internal feature learning complements traditional reward maximization. The approach is well-motivated within the literature, contrasting with passive unsupervised learning by leveraging the agent's ability to influence the environment as a learning signal. The empirical results are robust, with thorough ablation studies demonstrating the contribution of each auxiliary component, and the authors acknowledge hyperparameter differences between A3C and UNREAL while using top-3 reporting to mitigate this.

The main weakness is insufficient experimental analysis of the auxiliary tasks themselves. While pixel/feature control appears most impactful, the paper lacks deeper, isolated study of these components with metrics beyond RL performance. The presentation could also be strengthened by providing more low-level detail on the auxiliary tasks earlier in the paper (e.g., introducing L_PC before the appendix).

To further improve the paper, I recommend: 1) Providing more detailed analysis of how each auxiliary task contributes to representation learning, including visualization of learned features; 2) Conducting ablation studies that isolate the impact of each auxiliary task on representation quality; 3) Clarifying the relationship between the auxiliary tasks and the agent's ability to handle partial observability. 

Questions for authors: How do the representations learned through these auxiliary tasks specifically differ from those learned through standard unsupervised methods? Could you provide evidence that the feature control task is learning meaningful representations rather than just memorizing pixel patterns?
