- Introduces UNREAL: agent that learns multiple pseudo-rewards jointly to produce evolving common representations even without extrinsic rewards.  
- Proposes two novel auxiliary control tasks: pixel control (maximize changes in image regions) and feature control (maximize activations of hidden units).  
- Introduces a reward prediction task with skewed replay sampling to balance rare reward events without biasing the main policy.  
- Integrates all components with A3C into a unified learning framework.  
- Demonstrates large empirical gains: 880% human-normalized on Atari and 87% on Labyrinth, with 10× faster learning on Labyrinth versus prior SOTA.  
- Recommends acceptance, citing a compelling contribution that active control tasks yield better representations than passive reconstruction.  
- Empirical results are robust and well-supported, with clear ablation studies showing each component’s contribution and full UNREAL outperforming variants.  
- Well-motivated and well-positioned within literature, contrasting effectively with Horde, UVFA, and successor representations.  
- Theoretical foundation and formalization of auxiliary tasks and their integration are sound.  
- Evaluation is thorough: multiple environments, hyperparameter robustness checks, and meaningful human-normalized metrics.  
- Could strengthen the paper with deeper analysis of which auxiliary task contributes most and why pixel control is particularly impactful.  
- Lacks some implementation details (e.g., L_PC) in the main text; these should be moved from the appendix for clarity.  
- Methodology is sound and hyperparameter differences with A3C are reasonably mitigated by reporting top-3 results.  
- Comparison with unsupervised baselines supports the core claim that active control is superior to passive learning.  
- Suggested improvements: more ablations/visualizations of pixel vs. feature control, earlier implementation details, and discussion of limitations/failure cases.  
- Questions for authors: why does pixel control contribute more than feature control; how does reward prediction shape representations and can it be visualized; what are the computational costs and scaling behavior of the auxiliary tasks?
