

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper introduces the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, which significantly improves deep reinforcement learning by incorporating auxiliary tasks that learn to control features of the sensorimotor stream. The authors propose two main auxiliary mechanisms: (1) auxiliary control tasks that learn policies for maximizing pseudo-rewards (pixel changes and network feature activations), and (2) reward prediction tasks that learn to anticipate immediate rewards. By combining these with the A3C framework, the UNREAL agent achieves state-of-the-art performance on both Atari games (880% expert human performance) and a challenging 3D Labyrinth environment (87% expert human performance), with a 10× speedup in learning. The paper is well-motivated, with a clear hypothesis that learning to control multiple features of the sensorimotor stream will develop more useful representations than traditional approaches, and provides thorough empirical validation across multiple environments.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating that auxiliary tasks designed to shape the agent's representation through control of meaningful features substantially improves performance and data efficiency in deep reinforcement learning. The results are scientifically rigorous, with comprehensive ablation studies and robustness analysis across hyperparameters.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses a meaningful problem in reinforcement learning: how to develop more useful representations when extrinsic rewards are sparse. The approach is well-placed in the literature, building on prior work with Horde, UVFA, and successor representations while making a novel contribution by using these auxiliary tasks for representation learning rather than just temporal abstraction. The results strongly support the authors' claims: UNREAL outperforms A3C by 45% on average across Labyrinth levels (87% vs 54% human-normalized performance) and achieves 880% mean human performance on Atari, a significant improvement over previous state-of-the-art. The ablation studies demonstrate that both auxiliary control and reward prediction tasks contribute to the improvement, with the full UNREAL agent providing the best results. The paper's empirical evidence is thorough, with detailed analysis of learning curves, robustness across hyperparameters, and comparisons to relevant baselines.

The paper is well-written and accessible to researchers with deep RL expertise, with clear explanations of the methodology and results. While the core idea that relevant auxiliary tasks should improve representation learning is not entirely surprising, the paper provides a compelling proof-of-concept with strong empirical evidence. The authors have carefully addressed the concerns raised in the literature about unsupervised learning in RL by designing auxiliary tasks that are directly tied to the agent's control capabilities rather than just reconstruction.

## 4. Additional Feedback
The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the UNREAL agent compared to A3C, particularly regarding the additional memory requirements for the replay buffer and the complexity of the auxiliary networks. While the paper mentions the replay buffer size (2k observations), it would be helpful to know the absolute memory requirements for training and inference.

Additionally, while the architecture is complex, the authors should consider releasing the source code to facilitate reproducibility and further research. The paper mentions the code will be made available, but a specific timeline would be helpful for the community.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that the UNREAL agent requires "less than 10% of the data to reach the final performance of A3C" on Labyrinth. Could the authors provide more specific computational resource requirements (e.g., GPU hours, memory usage) for training UNREAL compared to A3C?

2. The auxiliary control tasks use a deconvolutional network to produce Qaux values. How sensitive is the performance to the architecture of this auxiliary network, and would simpler architectures work equally well?

3. The paper mentions that the UNREAL agent is "more robust to hyperparameter settings" than A3C. Could the authors provide a more quantitative analysis of this robustness (e.g., standard deviation across hyperparameter settings) to better understand the extent of this improvement?
