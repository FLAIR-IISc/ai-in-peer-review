

# Review of "UNREAL: Training a Single Agent to Play Multiple Atari Games with Unsupervised Auxiliary Tasks"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces the UNREAL agent, which significantly improves deep reinforcement learning by incorporating unsupervised auxiliary tasks into the A3C framework. The authors propose two main types of auxiliary tasks: auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) that train the agent to maximize changes in specific visual features, and auxiliary reward tasks that predict immediate rewards. By sharing a common representation across all tasks, the agent develops a more effective feature space that accelerates learning and improves final performance. The authors demonstrate that UNREAL substantially outperforms A3C on both the challenging 3D Labyrinth environment (achieving 87% human performance, a 61% relative improvement) and 57 Atari games (averaging 880% expert human performance), while also learning 10× faster and being more robust to hyperparameter choices. The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation and thorough empirical validation of their approach.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a solid contribution to deep reinforcement learning with well-motivated auxiliary tasks that demonstrably improve performance and efficiency. The results are scientifically rigorous and the approach is well-placed in the literature.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper effectively addresses a meaningful problem in deep RL: how to better utilize the rich structure of the sensorimotor stream to develop more effective representations. The authors correctly identify that while extrinsic rewards are the primary learning signal, the environment contains abundant additional information that could shape better representations. Their hypothesis that "an agent that can flexibly control its future experiences will also be able to achieve any goal" is well-motivated and supported by their results.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, with thorough discussion of related work on auxiliary tasks (Horde, UVFA, successor representations), unsupervised learning in RL, and environment models. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from previous work, particularly noting that their auxiliary tasks are used solely for representation learning rather than as separate policies.

The empirical results are compelling and scientifically rigorous. The authors provide extensive ablation studies showing the contribution of each auxiliary task component. Their evaluation on both Labyrinth (a challenging 3D environment with diverse tasks) and Atari (a standard benchmark) demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple metrics. The paper includes proper statistical analysis (averaging over multiple hyperparameter settings and random seeds), and the results are clearly presented with learning curves, human-normalized scores, and speedup metrics. The 10× speedup in learning on Labyrinth is particularly impressive.

## 4. Additional Feedback

The paper is well-written and accessible to readers with deep RL expertise, which is a significant strength. To further improve the paper, the authors might consider:

1. Adding a brief discussion of the computational overhead of the auxiliary tasks (beyond the requested information about resources), particularly regarding inference time and memory requirements.

2. Including a more detailed comparison of the auxiliary tasks' relative contributions across different environments (e.g., why pixel control works better on Atari than feature control).

3. Providing more intuition about why the reward prediction task helps, beyond stating it "removes perceptual sparsity."

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. Could you provide more detail on the computational resources required to train UNREAL (e.g., number of GPUs, total training time)? The paper mentions "32 asynchronous threads" but doesn't specify hardware.

2. The architecture is indeed quite complex with multiple auxiliary tasks and specialized networks. Will the authors release the source code to facilitate reproducibility and further research?

3. The paper notes that "learning to reconstruct only led to faster initial learning and actually made the final scores worse when compared to vanilla A3C." Could you elaborate on why this happens, particularly given that prior work (e.g., Kulkarni et al., 2016) showed benefits from similar reconstruction tasks?

4. In the Labyrinth experiments, the UNREAL agent achieves 116% human-normalized score on lt hallway slope. How does this compare to the performance of the human experts who provided the benchmark scores?
