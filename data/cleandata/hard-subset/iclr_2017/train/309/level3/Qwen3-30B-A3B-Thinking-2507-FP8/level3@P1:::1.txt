

This paper introduces UNREAL, an agent that augments deep reinforcement learning with auxiliary control and reward prediction tasks to improve representation learning. The authors propose learning separate policies for maximizing multiple pseudo-reward functions (pixel changes and network features) simultaneously, while also introducing a novel mechanism for focusing the representation on extrinsic rewards. The UNREAL architecture combines these auxiliary tasks with A3C's parallel learning framework to significantly improve data efficiency and performance.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The key reasons for this decision are: 1) The paper makes a clear, well-motivated contribution to deep reinforcement learning by demonstrating that auxiliary control tasks (learning to control features rather than just reconstruct them) substantially improve representation learning; 2) The empirical results are strong, demonstrating significant performance improvements over A3C on both Atari and Labyrinth environments, with a 10× speedup in learning on Labyrinth and 880% human-normalized performance on Atari.

The paper's contribution is well-placed in the literature, building on and distinguishing from related work like Horde, UVFA, and successor representations. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from previous methods by using auxiliary tasks solely for representation learning rather than as direct solutions to the main task. The motivation for learning to control features (e.g., the baby example) is compelling and well-explained.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and scientifically rigorous. The authors conduct comprehensive ablation studies showing that each auxiliary component contributes to performance, with the full UNREAL agent outperforming all baselines. The results on Labyrinth are particularly impressive, with the agent reaching 87% human-normalized performance and demonstrating a 10× speedup in learning compared to A3C. The Atari results are equally strong, with UNREAL surpassing the state-of-the-art across all 57 games. The authors also demonstrate improved robustness to hyperparameters, a critical aspect for practical reinforcement learning systems.

The paper is well-written and accessible to readers with deep RL expertise, with clear explanations of the architecture and experimental setup. The visualizations of the auxiliary task outputs (in the supplementary video) provide valuable intuition about how the representation develops.

For additional feedback to improve the paper: The authors should clarify the computational requirements for training UNREAL, including the number of GPUs, total training time, and memory usage. This information would be valuable for practitioners considering implementing the method. Additionally, the architecture is quite complex with multiple auxiliary tasks and replay mechanisms; the authors should confirm whether they plan to release the source code, as this would greatly facilitate reproducibility and adoption by the community.

I have two specific questions for the authors: First, what is the exact computational overhead of adding the auxiliary tasks (e.g., how much slower is each training iteration compared to A3C)? Second, could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of how the auxiliary control tasks differ from previous work on "auxiliary predictions" in 3D environments (e.g., Lample & Chaplot, 2016; Mirowski et al., 2016), particularly in terms of their theoretical foundation and practical implementation?

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field by demonstrating that carefully chosen auxiliary tasks can dramatically improve deep reinforcement learning performance and data efficiency. The results are convincing, the methodology is sound, and the paper is well-executed. While the core idea that relevant auxiliary tasks can aid learning may not be entirely surprising, the paper provides a comprehensive and rigorous demonstration of this principle in both 2D and 3D environments, making it a valuable contribution to the deep RL literature.
