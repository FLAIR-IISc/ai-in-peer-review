

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces UNREAL, an agent that learns to maximize multiple pseudo-reward functions simultaneously through reinforcement learning, while sharing a common representation. The authors propose three key auxiliary tasks: (1) auxiliary control tasks that learn to maximize pixel changes or network feature activations, (2) reward prediction tasks that learn to anticipate immediate rewards, and (3) value function replay to improve representation learning. The approach significantly improves data efficiency and performance on both Atari and Labyrinth environments, achieving 880% human-normalized performance on Atari (a 2.5x improvement over prior work) and 87% on Labyrinth (a 61% improvement over A3C). The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and provides convincing empirical evidence supporting the approach.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to deep reinforcement learning by demonstrating that auxiliary control tasks substantially improve feature learning and data efficiency. The results on both Atari and Labyrinth are compelling and significantly outperform previous state-of-the-art.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully addresses a meaningful problem in reinforcement learning: how to learn effective representations in environments with sparse rewards. The key insight—that learning to control various features of the sensorimotor stream leads to better representations than simply reconstructing them—is well-motivated and supported by experiments. The ablation studies (Figures 3-5) clearly demonstrate that each component contributes to the overall performance improvement, with the combination of all three auxiliary tasks providing the largest gains. The results on Labyrinth are particularly impressive, showing a 10x speedup in learning and 87% human performance across 13 challenging 3D navigation tasks. The paper also provides a thoughtful analysis of the relationship between the auxiliary tasks and the main task, which is an important contribution to the field.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these suggestions would further improve it:

1. The paper should clarify in the abstract and introduction that separate policies are learned for each auxiliary task (not a single policy optimizing all rewards), as this is a critical aspect of the approach.

2. The feature-control auxiliary task (Section 3.1) lacks strong empirical validation. The results in Figure 5 are unconvincing and raise questions about stability during training. More thorough analysis of this component would strengthen the paper.

3. Please report the value of λ_PC (assumed to be 1) explicitly and discuss whether this hyperparameter should be treated as a tunable parameter rather than fixed.

4. The paper claims to use "true" Q-learning for the auxiliary tasks, but the rewards are empirical (from the environment) rather than true rewards. Please clarify this distinction.

5. The grammar needs correction: "the features discovered in this manner is shared" should be "are shared."

6. The equation formatting needs attention: Eq. 1 should have λ_C inside the summation, and Eq. 2 references L_PC which isn't defined.

7. The supplementary materials should be explicitly referenced (where they are located).

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper mentions "the features discovered in this manner are shared with the primary LSTM" (Section 3.2). Could you clarify how the features are shared (e.g., weight sharing, feature concatenation) and provide more details on the implementation?

2. The auxiliary tasks are trained off-policy using experience replay. Could you provide more details on how the off-policy training affects the stability and convergence of the auxiliary tasks compared to on-policy training?

3. The paper states that the feature-control auxiliary task was "only slightly worse than for pixel control" (Section 4.1.1). Could you provide more details on why this is the case and whether the performance difference is statistically significant?

4. In Figure 3, the legend refers to "human-normalized scores" but doesn't specify whether these are for Labyrinth or Atari. Please clarify this in the text.

5. The paper reports 880% human-normalized performance on Atari. Could you clarify whether this refers to average performance across all 57 games or a subset, and how this compares to the previous state-of-the-art (which was previously reported as 250% for Prioritized Dueling DQN)?

These questions would help clarify the paper's contributions and ensure the results are properly understood and reproducible.
