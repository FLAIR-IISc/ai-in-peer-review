

# Review for "UNREAL: Training a Single Agent to Play Multiple Atari Games" (ICLR 2017)

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning), a novel approach for training deep reinforcement learning agents by incorporating auxiliary tasks that learn stronger feature representations through reinforcement learning. The authors propose two key auxiliary control tasks: (1) "pixel control" where agents learn to maximize changes in specific regions of the input image, and (2) "feature control" where agents learn to maximize activation of specific hidden units in the neural network. Additionally, they introduce a supervised reward prediction task that predicts immediate reward after a sequence, learned offline via skewed replay sampling to balance reward/no-reward examples. The UNREAL agent significantly outperforms baseline methods, achieving 880% human performance on Atari (averaging across 57 games) and 87% human performance on Labyrinth tasks, while reaching baseline performance in approximately 10x fewer iterations. The core insight is that learning to *control* features through reinforcement learning—rather than passively reconstructing them—creates more useful representations that complement policy learning, particularly under partial observability.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to reinforcement learning by demonstrating that auxiliary tasks learned via reinforcement learning (not passive unsupervised learning) substantially improve data efficiency and performance. The empirical results are compelling, with strong evidence across multiple domains (Atari and Labyrinth), and the approach is well-positioned in the literature.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully addresses a meaningful problem: RL agents typically only learn from extrinsic rewards, while environments contain abundant other learning signals. The authors persuasively argue that their approach—using RL to control features rather than simply reconstructing them—creates more useful representations than previous unsupervised methods. Their ablation studies (Figure 5) clearly show that learning to control pixel changes is superior to simply predicting or reconstructing pixels, and the feature control task provides additional benefits. The empirical results are robust across multiple domains (Atari and Labyrinth), with UNREAL achieving 10x faster learning on Labyrinth and substantially outperforming prior work on Atari. The methodology is sound, with appropriate baselines, careful attention to hyperparameter sensitivity (Figure 3 top right), and acknowledgment of hyperparameter differences between A3C and UNREAL. The top-3 reporting of hyperparameters helps mitigate concerns about methodological differences.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by demonstrating that the agent's ability to influence the environment can serve as a powerful learning signal. The insight that "an agent that can flexibly control its future experiences will also be able to achieve any goal" is compelling and well-supported by the results. The UNREAL architecture is a natural extension of A3C that incorporates these auxiliary tasks in a principled way.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, the following points would improve it:

1. The auxiliary tasks are described at a high level, with key concepts like L_PC (the pixel control loss) introduced only in the appendix. The authors should define these earlier (e.g., in Section 3.1) to improve clarity and accessibility.

2. The paper lacks sufficient experimental analysis of the auxiliary tasks themselves. While Figure 5 shows pixel control outperforms other baselines, the authors should provide more isolated study of each auxiliary task's contribution. Why is pixel control more impactful than feature control? What specific features does the agent learn to control? Additional metrics beyond RL performance (e.g., feature representation quality, correlation with reward) would strengthen the claims.

3. The paper states "pixel/feature control appears most impactful" but doesn't provide detailed analysis of how these controls relate to the actual task. A deeper investigation into the learned representations would help validate the core hypothesis.

4. The authors could better clarify how the reward prediction task differs from the auxiliary control tasks and how they complement each other.

## Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide more detail on how the pixel control task differs from simply predicting pixel changes (as in the unsupervised baseline), and why the control aspect is more beneficial for representation learning?

2. The paper mentions feature control was only slightly worse than pixel control—can you share more details about the specific hidden layer used for feature control and why it might be less effective than pixel control?

3. Could you provide additional metrics on the quality of the learned representations (e.g., how well the representations correlate with task-relevant information) beyond RL performance?

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of reinforcement learning with a novel, well-motivated approach that significantly improves learning efficiency and performance. With the additional clarifications suggested, it would be an even more impactful contribution to the literature.
