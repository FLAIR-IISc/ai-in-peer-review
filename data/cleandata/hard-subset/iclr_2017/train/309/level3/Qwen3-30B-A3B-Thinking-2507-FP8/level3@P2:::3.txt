

**Review for ICLR 2017**

1. **Summary of Contributions**
This paper introduces UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that simultaneously learns to maximize multiple pseudo-reward functions alongside the primary extrinsic reward. The key innovation is using reinforcement learning to learn policies for controlling specific features of the sensorimotor stream (pixel changes and latent feature activations), rather than simply reconstructing them as in unsupervised learning. The authors also introduce a reward prediction auxiliary task that uses skewed replay sampling to focus on rewarding states. UNREAL significantly outperforms A3C on Atari (880% expert human performance vs. 250% median) and Labyrinth (87% human performance with 10× faster learning), demonstrating that learning to control environmental features creates more useful representations than passive unsupervised learning.

2. **Decision**
I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a strong contribution to reinforcement learning by demonstrating that auxiliary tasks learned through reinforcement learning (rather than passive reconstruction) significantly improve data efficiency and performance, with robust empirical results across two challenging domains.

3. **Supporting Arguments**
The paper is well-motivated by clearly distinguishing its approach from related work (Horde, UVFA, successor representation) and providing a compelling theoretical argument that learning to control features is necessary for flexible behavior. The baby analogy effectively illustrates why controlling features is more useful than reconstructing them.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous and well-supported. The ablation studies (Figure 4) clearly demonstrate that all components of UNREAL contribute to the performance gains. The authors address potential concerns about hyperparameter differences between A3C and UNREAL by reporting top-3 results across hyperparameter settings, and the robustness analysis (Figure 3 top right) shows UNREAL is significantly less sensitive to hyperparameter choices than A3C.

The learning speedup (10×) is particularly impressive and practical, as it translates to requiring less than 10% of the data to reach A3C's final performance. The results on Labyrinth are especially compelling given the environment's complexity (dynamic textures, 3D navigation, and laser-tag gameplay).

4. **Additional Feedback**
While the paper makes a strong case for the approach, it could benefit from more detailed exposition of the auxiliary tasks in the main text. The description of L_PC (the auxiliary control loss) appears only in the appendix, and readers would better understand the contribution with a clearer explanation earlier in the paper. A brief description of how pixel control and feature control differ in implementation would also help.

The paper could provide deeper analysis of the auxiliary tasks themselves. While it states that "pixel control appears most impactful," it doesn't provide metrics to quantify this beyond RL performance. A more detailed analysis of what specific features the agent learns to control, how these features relate to the task, and why pixel control outperforms feature control would strengthen the contribution.

5. **Questions for Authors**
1. The paper states "learning to control pixel changes is indeed better than simply predicting immediate pixel changes." Could you provide more specific metrics to quantify this improvement (e.g., accuracy of the control task itself, correlation between auxiliary task performance and primary task performance)?

2. In Figure 5, the authors compare their approach to pixel reconstruction and input change prediction. Could you clarify how the "input change prediction" baseline relates to the reward prediction task? Is it predicting the same immediate reward as the reward prediction task?

3. The paper mentions that "the optimal policy would begin by exploring the maze to rapidly learn its layout" in Labyrinth. Could you elaborate on how the auxiliary tasks specifically contribute to this exploration behavior, rather than just improving the representation?

4. The authors note that "learning to reconstruct only led to faster initial learning and actually made the final scores worse." Could you provide more insight into why this occurs, particularly given that previous work (e.g., Kulkarni et al., 2016) has shown reconstruction can help in 3D environments?
