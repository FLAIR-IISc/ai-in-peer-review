

This paper introduces UNREAL, a novel reinforcement learning agent that significantly improves data efficiency and robustness by learning multiple auxiliary tasks alongside the main reward maximization objective. The key contribution is a unified framework that simultaneously learns separate policies for maximizing various pseudo-rewards (pixel changes, network features) while also predicting immediate rewards and replaying value functions. By sharing a common CNN-LSTM representation across all tasks, the agent develops a more robust and useful feature space that generalizes better to the primary task.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of deep reinforcement learning, with convincing empirical results showing substantial improvements over A3C on both Atari and Labyrinth environments. The method is well-motivated, clearly explained, and supported by rigorous experiments.

The main reasons for acceptance are: 1) the paper demonstrates a significant performance improvement (880% human-normalized performance on Atari and 87% on Labyrinth) with a clear explanation of why the auxiliary tasks improve learning; and 2) the proposed approach is both conceptually novel and practically effective, with the auxiliary tasks being well-integrated into the standard RL framework.

The supporting arguments for these reasons are compelling. The experiments show that UNREAL achieves a mean speedup of 10× in learning compared to A3C on Labyrinth, requiring less than 10% of the data to reach A3C's final performance. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate that each auxiliary task contributes to the final performance, with the combination of all tasks yielding the best results. The paper's comparison to unsupervised learning baselines (pixel reconstruction and input change prediction) is particularly convincing, showing that learning to control features (rather than just predicting them) leads to significantly better performance. The results are robust across hyperparameter settings, with UNREAL consistently outperforming A3C even when hyperparameters are varied.

For improvements, I suggest the authors provide more detailed analysis of computational costs. It would be valuable to quantify the additional training time or memory requirements introduced by the auxiliary tasks, particularly which components are most expensive. This would help readers understand the practical trade-offs of implementing UNREAL.

Regarding the feature control auxiliary task, the current evidence in Figure 5 is somewhat weak. The paper claims this task is promising but the results shown are not as compelling as those for pixel control. The authors should either strengthen this evidence or clarify the limitations of feature control in their discussion. Additionally, the paper should explicitly state that separate policies are learned for each auxiliary task (not a single policy optimizing all rewards), as this is a key aspect of their approach that isn't sufficiently emphasized in the abstract and introduction.

The authors should also clarify why the auxiliary task weights (λ) were fixed at 1 rather than treated as hyperparameters or validated experimentally. This choice significantly impacts the relative importance of each auxiliary task, and the paper should justify this decision or conduct additional experiments to validate the chosen weights.

I note that the paper mentions using off-policy reinforcement learning for auxiliary tasks but doesn't explicitly address how the multi-step empirical rewards affect the true Q-learning process. This should be clarified in the method section.

The paper contains several minor formatting issues that should be corrected: Eq.1 has λ_C outside the summation where it should be inside; the text has a grammar error ("the features... is shared" should be "are shared"); and there are some figure reference inconsistencies (e.g., "Figure 3 (right)" actually refers to the top-left plot).

Finally, I would appreciate clarification on the value of λ_PC (assumed to be 1) and whether it was explicitly reported in the experiments. The legend of Figure 3 should also explain what "Clip" means for dueling networks.

These suggestions are intended to strengthen an already strong paper, not to undermine its contributions. The work presents a valuable advancement in reinforcement learning that could significantly impact future research in the field.
