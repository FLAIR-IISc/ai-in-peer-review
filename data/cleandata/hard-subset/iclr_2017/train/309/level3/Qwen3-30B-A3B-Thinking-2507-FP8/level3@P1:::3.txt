

The paper introduces UNREAL, an agent that simultaneously learns to maximize multiple pseudo-reward functions through reinforcement learning, developing a common representation that continues to evolve even in the absence of extrinsic rewards. The approach incorporates two novel auxiliary tasks: (1) pixel control, where the agent learns to maximize changes in specific image regions, and (2) feature control, where the agent learns to maximize activations of specific hidden units. Additionally, the paper introduces a reward prediction task that uses skewed replay sampling to balance rare reward events, and combines all components with A3C in a unified framework. The authors demonstrate significant performance improvements on Atari (880% human-normalized performance) and Labyrinth (87% human-normalized performance with 10× faster learning) compared to previous state-of-the-art methods.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a compelling contribution by demonstrating that auxiliary control tasks—where the agent actively influences features in the environment rather than passively reconstructing them—lead to substantially better representations than previous unsupervised approaches. The empirical results are robust and well-supported, with clear ablation studies showing the contribution of each component. The 10× speedup in learning on Labyrinth and the consistent performance gains across 57 Atari games represent significant improvements over previous work.

The approach is well-motivated and properly positioned within the literature. The authors effectively contrast their work with related approaches like Horde, UVFA, and successor representations, highlighting how their method differs by using controllable features as learning signals rather than passive reconstruction. The introduction of reward prediction with skewed replay sampling is particularly clever, addressing the sparsity of rewards while avoiding bias to the primary policy. The paper's theoretical foundation in reinforcement learning is sound, with clear formalization of the auxiliary tasks and their integration into the learning framework.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors provide detailed results across multiple environments (Labyrinth levels and Atari games), with careful attention to hyperparameter robustness (showing UNREAL is significantly more robust than A3C). The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the value of each component, with the full UNREAL agent outperforming all variants. The human-normalized scores provide a meaningful metric for comparison with previous work.

The paper could be strengthened by providing more detailed analysis of the auxiliary tasks themselves. While the paper shows that combining all components works best, it would be valuable to understand which auxiliary task contributes most to the performance gains. The pixel control task appears particularly impactful, but the paper lacks detailed analysis of why this is the case (e.g., through visualization of learned policies or feature maps). More low-level details about the implementation of the auxiliary tasks (e.g., introducing L_PC earlier in the paper rather than in the appendix) would also improve clarity.

The methodology is generally sound, with appropriate attention to experimental design. The authors acknowledge hyperparameter differences between A3C and UNREAL and mitigate this by reporting top-3 results across multiple hyperparameter settings, which is a strong practice. The comparison with unsupervised baselines (pixel reconstruction and input change prediction) effectively supports the paper's core thesis that active control of features is superior to passive learning.

To improve the paper, I recommend:
1. Adding more detailed analysis of the relative contribution of pixel control vs. feature control tasks, perhaps through additional ablation studies or visualization of learned policies.
2. Providing more implementation details about the auxiliary tasks earlier in the paper (before the appendix) to help readers understand the technical approach.
3. Including a brief discussion of potential limitations or failure cases of the approach.

Questions for authors:
1. Can you provide more insight into why pixel control appears to contribute more significantly to performance than feature control, and how these two tasks might complement each other in the learning process?
2. How does the reward prediction task specifically shape the representation, and could this be analyzed through feature visualization or other interpretability methods?
3. What are the computational costs of the auxiliary tasks compared to the base A3C agent, and how do they scale to larger environments?
