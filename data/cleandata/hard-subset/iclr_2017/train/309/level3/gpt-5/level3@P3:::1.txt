Summary of contributions
- The paper introduces UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with unsupervised auxiliary tasks designed to shape representation learning. Two families of auxiliary objectives are proposed: (i) auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) optimised via off-policy n-step Q-learning on replayed experience, and (ii) auxiliary reward-focused tasks (reward prediction and value-function replay) trained from a reward-skewed replay buffer.
- The approach yields large empirical gains: on 13 Labyrinth tasks, UNREAL more than doubles A3C’s final human-normalized performance (mean 87% vs 54%) and achieves substantial data-efficiency improvements (≈10× speedup to reach A3C’s best performance, median 11×). On 57 Atari games, UNREAL surpasses A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN, reporting 880% mean and 250% median human-normalized performance, along with improved robustness to hyperparameters.
- The paper is clearly written, well organized, and easy to follow for readers with deep RL expertise. It provides ablations showing the benefits of pixel control vs input reconstruction/change prediction, and demonstrates that combining auxiliary tasks is beneficial.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical performance and data-efficiency improvements across both 3D Labyrinth and Atari, with ablations and hyperparameter robustness analyses that support the claims.
- The idea that relevant auxiliary tasks can shape features is well motivated and this paper offers a convincing proof-of-concept at scale, making it a solid conference contribution.

Supporting arguments
1) What problem is tackled?
- How to accelerate and stabilize deep RL by exploiting abundant unsupervised signals in the sensorimotor stream beyond sparse extrinsic reward. The paper asks whether auxiliary control and reward-focused tasks can improve representation learning to yield faster and better policy learning.

2) Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature?
- Yes. The paper connects to options/pseudo-rewards (Sutton et al., Konidaris & Barreto), Horde, UVFAs, successor representations, and recent auxiliary prediction work in 3D environments. It motivates control-type auxiliary objectives (not merely reconstruction) as more aligned with long-horizon goals, and cleanly separates reward prediction from policy/value learning to avoid bias. Using replay and off-policy Q-learning for auxiliary tasks is also well grounded in prior work.

3) Do the results support the claims, and are they rigorous?
- The empirical support is strong: extensive evaluation on 13 diverse Labyrinth levels and 57 Atari games, clear performance and data-efficiency gains, and hyperparameter robustness sweeps. Ablations (pixel control vs reconstruction/change prediction, with some feature control results) strengthen the argument that control-focused auxiliary tasks are particularly helpful. While the overall result aligns with intuition (auxiliary tasks improve representation), the breadth of evidence and consistency across domains substantiate the contribution. Some aspects (e.g., compute costs, sensitivity to design choices) could be documented more fully.

Additional feedback and questions (to improve the paper; not critical to my decision)
- Compute/resources and wall-clock time:
  • Please report computational resources required to reproduce the main results: number and type of CPUs/GPUs, number of actor/learner threads, memory footprint, cluster/machine count, total environment frames, and wall-clock training time per domain (best-performing settings). 
  • Provide training throughput (frames/sec) and wall-clock to reach key milestones (e.g., A3C’s best score) for UNREAL vs A3C.

- Code release:
  • The overall architecture has many moving parts (A3C + LSTM, replay buffer with reward balancing, deconvolutional heads for pixel/feature control, off-policy n-step Q-learning for multiple heads, value replay, and reward prediction). Given this complexity and the number of design and implementation details, releasing code and training scripts would greatly aid reproducibility and adoption. Will you release the source code?

- Clarity on auxiliary task design and sensitivity:
  • Pixel control: How sensitive are gains to the grid resolution, crop size, and reward definition (absolute difference vs other metrics)? Does using color vs grayscale change outcomes on Atari where pixel intensity dynamics vary?
  • Feature control: Results are promising but limited—could you expand ablations that combine feature control with reward prediction/value replay, and report systematically on Atari as well?
  • Loss weights: Provide a sensitivity analysis for λPC, λRP, and λVR beyond the sampled ranges, including interactions between them and the learning rate/entropy cost.

- Replay details and off-policy stability:
  • Please elaborate on how often each auxiliary head is updated relative to on-policy A3C updates, target network usage (if any) for Q-learning heads, and the impact of the 2k replay buffer size. Did you try prioritized replay for pixel/feature control, and if so, what were the effects?
  • Any observed instabilities from combining on-policy A3C with off-policy Q-learning? Diagnostics such as value/policy loss scales and gradient norms over training would help.

- Robustness and statistical reporting:
  • The hyperparameter sweep is commendable. In addition, please report per-game means and confidence intervals across multiple random seeds for a fixed hyperparameter setting to separate hyperparameter sensitivity from run-to-run variability.
  • For Labyrinth, can you quantify exploration improvements attributable to pixel/feature control (e.g., time-to-first-reward, coverage metrics)?

- Scope and generality:
  • The result is compelling but not entirely surprising: auxiliary tasks that correlate with control and reward should improve representation learning. It would help to discuss when such tasks might fail or conflict (e.g., pixel control in visually noisy domains, or distractor-heavy environments), and how to detect/mitigate negative transfer.
  • Please comment on transfer: do representations learned with certain auxiliary tasks help when the extrinsic task changes (e.g., goal location changes, new reward functions)?

- Minor clarifications:
  • Report pre-processing (frame skip, observation stacking policy vs LSTM memory), action repeat specifics for Atari, and whether the reward predictor uses shared or separate normalization per domain.
  • Clarify whether Atari results include sticky actions and the exact evaluation protocol.

Post-rebuttal note
- I appreciate the authors’ responses. My overall assessment and decision remain unchanged. The paper is a strong, well-executed contribution demonstrating that carefully chosen auxiliary tasks can significantly improve both learning speed and robustness in deep RL.
