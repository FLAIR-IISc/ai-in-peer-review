Summary of contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with unsupervised auxiliary tasks that shape the representation learned by deep RL. Two families of auxiliary tasks are introduced: (1) auxiliary control via off-policy n-step Q-learning on pseudo-rewards (pixel control and feature control), and (2) reward-focused auxiliary tasks (immediate reward prediction with skewed replay toward rare rewards, plus value-function replay). These are trained jointly with the base on-policy A3C using a combined loss and a small replay buffer. Empirically, UNREAL yields substantial improvements in both data efficiency and final performance: on Labyrinth it more than doubles mean human-normalized performance (to 87%) and speeds learning by about 10× on average; on 57 Atari games it achieves new state-of-the-art results (mean 880% and median 250% human), with markedly improved robustness to hyperparameters. Ablations show that control-based auxiliary tasks outperform prediction-only or reconstruction-based auxiliaries.

Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) strong empirical gains with careful ablations and robustness analyses; (2) a well-motivated and well-placed approach that convincingly demonstrates that control-oriented auxiliary tasks can shape representations to benefit RL on challenging visual domains.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? The paper addresses how to learn useful representations in deep RL when extrinsic rewards are sparse and environments are rich. It proposes learning to predict and control multiple aspects of the sensorimotor stream as auxiliary objectives that continue to improve even in the absence of extrinsic reward.
- Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The paper clearly situates the work relative to options, Horde, UVFAs, successor representations, and model learning. The key methodological distinction is using many off-policy control tasks purely as auxiliary objectives for shared representation learning, rather than for hierarchical execution or transfer. The argument that control-oriented targets are better aligned with long-term goals than reconstruction is compelling and supported experimentally.
- Do the results support the claims? Largely yes. Results span 13 diverse Labyrinth tasks and 57 Atari games, with hyperparameter sweeps, multiple seeds, and detailed ablations: (i) pixel control vs. prediction-only vs. reconstruction; (ii) contribution of reward prediction and value replay; (iii) robustness analyses. The improvements in both speed and final score are substantial and consistent. While some gains may be anticipated (auxiliary tasks often help), this paper provides a thorough, convincing proof-of-concept at scale on challenging domains.

Strengths
- Significant empirical improvements over strong baselines (A3C, Prioritized Dueling DQN).
- Careful ablations that isolate the contribution of control-based auxiliaries and reward-focused shaping.
- Improved data efficiency and robustness to hyperparameters—important practical properties.
- Clear writing and comprehensive implementation details.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Engineering complexity: the architecture combines on-policy A3C, off-policy Q-learning heads, replay with class-imbalance sampling, and deconvolutional modules. Reproducibility and implementation accessibility may be challenging without code.
- Novelty is partly incremental: the constituent ideas (auxiliary tasks, replay, off-policy learning) are known; the contribution is a well-executed integration and strong empirical validation. The main conceptual claim—that control-oriented auxiliaries are more beneficial than reconstruction—is supported but not theoretically analyzed.
- Computational cost and resource requirements are not reported; wall-clock efficiency, hardware load, and training budget relative to baselines are unclear.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Report computational resources: number/type of CPUs/GPUs, total environment frames, wall-clock training times for A3C vs. UNREAL on representative tasks, and throughput (frames/sec). This will help assess the practical trade-off between data efficiency and compute.
- Clarify evaluation protocol details to ensure fairness, especially on Atari (training frames, action repeat, evaluation with/without life-loss, sticky actions if applicable, and definition of “expert human”).
- Provide more systematic analysis of sensitivity to λVR, λPC, λRP and to replay buffer size and replay ratio; include a brief guideline for setting these in new domains.
- Expand on feature control results (currently preliminary). A direct comparison of pixel vs. feature control across several games, and their combination, would strengthen the generality claim.
- Discuss the domain dependence of pixel-control rewards (e.g., sensitivity to textures and illumination changes). Consider normalizations or invariances to reduce task-specific tuning.
- If possible, release code and training scripts to improve reproducibility and facilitate adoption.

Questions for the authors
- What hardware and wall-clock time were required to train UNREAL to the reported results on Labyrinth and Atari (per run and per hyperparameter setting)? What is the replay/update ratio and average frames per second achieved?
- How were λPC, λRP, and λVR chosen across domains? How sensitive are results to these weights, and is there a heuristic for tuning them?
- Does the reward prediction oversampling introduce any measurable bias into the learned representation that could harm tasks with dense rewards? Have you evaluated UNREAL on dense-reward Atari games specifically?
- For Atari comparisons, is the training budget (frames) matched to A3C and Dueling DQN? Are evaluation settings identical (frame skip, preprocessing, no-ops, life termination)?
- Can you comment on stability when mixing on-policy A3C with off-policy Q-learning updates on shared parameters? Did you observe interference between heads, and how did you mitigate it?
- How critical is the particular pixel-control design (grid size, cell reward as abs diff, deconv architecture)? Have you tried multi-scale grids or feature-space change as the control target?
- Will you release code and pre-trained models?

Post-rebuttal
I did not change my assessment after the rebuttal. The paper remains a solid contribution with strong empirical evidence; addressing the compute/resource reporting and releasing code would further strengthen impact.
