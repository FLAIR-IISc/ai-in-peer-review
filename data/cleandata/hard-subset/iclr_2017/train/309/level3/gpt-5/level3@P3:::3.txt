Summary and contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an RL agent that augments on-policy policy/value learning (A3C with an LSTM) with off-policy auxiliary tasks designed to learn stronger, task-relevant representations. The key idea is to exploit the agent’s ability to control aspects of its sensorimotor stream as a learning signal, rather than passively reconstructing inputs or predicting pixels. Concretely:
- Auxiliary control tasks: learn policies to maximise pseudo-rewards for (i) pixel control—change in pixel intensity per cell in an image grid—and (ii) feature control—activation of units in an internal hidden layer. These are trained off-policy via n-step Q-learning from replay and share the representation with the main policy.
- Reward-focused auxiliary tasks: a supervised predictor of immediate reward from a short history (γ=0 analogue), trained from replay with skewed sampling to balance reward/no-reward, thereby densifying reward-related signals without biasing the base policy/value learning.
- Value-function replay: additional value regression updates from replay to exploit newly shaped features.
These components are combined in a single loss L_UNREAL = L_A3C + λ_VR L_VR + λ_PC Σ_c L_Q^(c) + λ_RP L_RP. Empirically, UNREAL substantially improves data efficiency and robustness: on 13 3D Labyrinth tasks (discrete actions in a rich, continuous spatial world) it achieves ~10× speedup to reach A3C’s best performance and higher final returns (e.g., 87% mean human-normalized vs 54% for A3C), and on 57 Atari games it surpasses prior state of the art with strong robustness across hyperparameters. The paper positions this approach as complementary to model-based or purely unsupervised auxiliary objectives, emphasizing controllable local/internal feature learning under partial observability.

Decision: Accept
Reasons: (1) Strong, well-substantiated empirical gains in both sample efficiency and final performance across two challenging domains; (2) A clear and novel framing of auxiliary control as pseudo-reward RL tasks that leverage controllability to shape shared representations, with a simple, practical instantiation.

Supporting arguments
1. Specific question/problem: How can we accelerate and stabilize deep RL in visually rich, partially observed environments with sparse rewards by using additional training signals beyond extrinsic reward? The paper tackles representation learning for RL by introducing controllable auxiliary tasks that align with the agent’s long-term goals.
2. Motivation and placement in the literature: The work is well motivated and clearly contrasted with (i) passive unsupervised learning (reconstruction/prediction) and (ii) model-based approaches. It connects to Horde/UVFAs/successor features but differs by using pseudo-control strictly as auxiliary objectives to sculpt shared features for the main task, rather than for transfer or options execution. The partial observability angle via LSTM and the control-based learning signal are compelling.
3. Do the results support the claims? Yes. The Labyrinth suite demonstrates large gains in both data efficiency (~10× fewer iterations to reach A3C’s best) and final scores, with ablations indicating that pixel control and reward prediction/value replay each contribute meaningfully; preliminary feature-control results suggest further promise. Atari results show higher mean/median human-normalized performance and improved robustness across 50 hyperparameter settings. The practice of reporting the top-3 runs and also providing across-all-hyperparameters plots mitigates fairness concerns. Methodological details (replay sizes, off-policy updates, deconvolutional heads, skewed sampling) are adequate to assess correctness, and training appears stable despite mixing on- and off-policy updates.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Clarity/presentation:
  - Define and explain L_PC, the pseudo-control loss, earlier in the main text (before Section B). A more concrete description of the pixel-control head (grid size, reward scaling, deconv architecture) and feature-control setup in the main body would aid reproducibility.
  - Provide a compact diagram summarizing which components are trained on-policy vs. off-policy and which share which weights.
- Ablations/analysis:
  - Deeper analysis of auxiliary tasks themselves would strengthen the paper. Pixel control appears particularly impactful; a more isolated study varying grid resolution, reward scaling, and deconv capacity, and comparing to immediate-change prediction and reconstruction under matched capacity, would clarify causal contributions.
  - For feature control, extend beyond preliminary results: Which layers/units are most beneficial? How does the choice of controlled layer affect performance? Are learned features more reward-selective or predictive?
  - Include metrics beyond RL returns to quantify representation quality (e.g., linear probes for reward/onset detection, controllability scores, mutual information with future rewards, value prediction error, or sample complexity of downstream probes).
- Fairness and robustness:
  - While top-3 reporting is common, also include mean±SEM over all seeds/hyperparameters in the main figures/tables for key benchmarks. Consider wall-clock comparisons to account for extra computation from replay and auxiliary heads.
  - Expand sensitivity analysis for λ_PC, λ_RP, and λ_VR; report failure modes (e.g., when off-policy Q-learning destabilizes the shared representation).
- Scope and generality:
  - Discuss extension to continuous action spaces and whether auxiliary Q-heads scale or if actor-critic-style auxiliary objectives would be preferable.
  - Comment on generalization: do representations transfer across related tasks (e.g., train auxiliaries on one Labyrinth level and evaluate on another)?

Questions for the authors
- Pixel control details: What is the final setup used by default (crop size, grid resolution, reward normalization across cells/games)? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- Feature control: Which hidden layer(s) were controlled, and how were units selected? Is any normalization applied to feature activations to keep pseudo-reward scales comparable?
- Replay skew for reward prediction: How sensitive is performance to the 50/50 reward/no-reward sampling ratio? Did you try prioritization by TD-error for L_VR or L_PC?
- Stability: Did you observe interference between off-policy Q-learning updates and on-policy actor-critic, especially through the shared LSTM? Any tricks (target networks, update schedules) critical for stability?
- Compute budget: What is the wall-clock overhead of UNREAL vs A3C for the same number of environment frames? Does the ~10× sample-efficiency gain translate into similar or better wall-clock gains?
- Auxiliary utility at test time: Are the auxiliary policies/heads ever executed or regularized at test time, or strictly used for training the shared representation? If disabled, do any performance regressions occur?

Overall, this paper makes a clear, novel, and practical contribution to representation learning for RL by leveraging controllable auxiliary tasks, and backs it with strong empirical evidence. I recommend acceptance.
