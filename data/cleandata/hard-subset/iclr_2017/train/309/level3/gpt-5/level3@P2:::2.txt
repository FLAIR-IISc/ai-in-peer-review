Summary and contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with domain-independent auxiliary tasks that shape the shared CNN+LSTM representation to improve data efficiency and performance. The auxiliary tasks comprise (i) control tasks that learn separate off-policy Q-functions for pseudo-rewards (notably pixel-control and feature-control), (ii) reward-prediction from short histories to densify learning about reward-relevant features, and (iii) value-function replay. The combined loss is optimized jointly, with on-policy A3C updates and off-policy replay for auxiliary heads. On 13 3D Labyrinth levels, UNREAL achieves large gains in final performance and a mean 10× speedup in learning over A3C; on 57 Atari games it substantially improves mean/median human-normalized scores and hyperparameter robustness over A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN. The motivation is crisp, the method is simple to apply, and the empirical gains are strong and broad.

Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) Clear, general, and well-motivated idea—using auxiliary control and reward-focused predictions to learn better representations for RL—placed well in the literature; (2) Strong and convincing empirical evidence of large, robust gains across diverse benchmarks, with ablations on Labyrinth and hyperparameter sweeps.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? How to learn better, reward-relevant representations in deep RL, especially under sparse rewards, by leveraging additional training signals beyond extrinsic reward—without requiring task-specific shaping or environment models.
- Is the approach well motivated and placed? Yes. The paper situates UNREAL relative to options, Horde, UVFAs, successor representations, and model-based auxiliary tasks. The key distinction is using many off-policy auxiliary control tasks purely for representation learning (not execution), plus reward prediction to reduce perceptual sparsity, and value replay for stability/efficiency.
- Do results support claims? Largely yes. The Labyrinth suite shows consistent improvements in both final performance and data efficiency, with ablations demonstrating the contribution of individual components. Atari results are strong and suggest improved robustness. The methodology (parallel actors, fixed replay window, n-step targets) is standard and appropriate. Two weaknesses: (i) limited empirical validation of feature-control compared to pixel-control; (ii) lack of computational cost breakdown and wall-clock overhead analysis. These do not undermine the main empirical conclusions.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (non-blocking)
- Clarity and correctness
  - Make explicit early that auxiliary control learns separate policies/Q-heads per pseudo-reward; these are not executed at test time but exist solely to shape the shared features.
  - State clearly that auxiliary control uses off-policy n-step Q-learning from replay; while common, it is not “true” one-step Q-learning. Briefly discuss stability considerations.
  - Provide a brief discussion of non-stationarity for feature-control (targets are changing activations) and any stabilizers used (e.g., target networks, slower updates).
  - Quantify computational overhead: (i) memory/compute cost of auxiliary heads and replay; (ii) wall-clock slowdown vs A3C; (iii) which components dominate cost.
  - Report wall-clock speedups (or slowdowns) in addition to sample-efficiency gains.
  - Consider training curves longer on a subset of tasks to show asymptotic behavior; several curves appear to still improve.
  - Treat λRP, λVR, λPC (and potentially per-task λc) as explicit hyperparameters; report their selected values (e.g., λPC is sometimes described as sampled; elsewhere it seems 1). Include brief sensitivity analysis.
  - Reward prediction: since CNN is shared, discuss whether the reward-skewed replay could bias the representation in ways that affect the policy/value and how you mitigated this.
- Ablations and analysis
  - Provide Atari ablations analogous to Labyrinth (UNREAL minus pixel-control; minus reward prediction; minus value replay) to see if the contribution patterns generalize.
  - For feature-control, include stronger evidence (e.g., more tasks, stability metrics, or visualization), given the promising but limited results in Fig. 5.
- Notational/typo fixes
  - Eq. (1): consider λc inside the summation or clearly justify a single λC; also, use r(t+k)^(c) rather than r(t)^(c) below Eq. (1).
  - A3C loss: ensure consistent sign conventions. If LA3C is minimized, Lπ should be the negative policy gradient term (e.g., −E[log π(a|s) A]) and entropy added with a negative sign in the loss.
  - Eq. (2): ensure notation consistency—define LPC explicitly and use either λPC Σc L(c)Q or λPC·LPC, not both variants across text/equation.
  - Figures: reference Figure 2 in the main text; reference Figure 1(d) in Section 3.3 (experience replay); correct “Figure 3 (right)” vs “top-left” mentions; clarify “Top Right/Left” consistently.
  - Figure 3: explain “Clip” in the dueling networks context; clarify that reported percentages in the legend refer to Labyrinth.
  - Figure 5: caption/text mismatch (claims top-5 on three levels but appears to show fewer); fix to match content.
  - Grammar: “the features discovered in this manner is shared” → “are shared”; Section 4.2: add comma after “methods”.
  - Appendix/supplement: specify where the supplementary materials can be accessed.
- Presentation
  - Briefly justify the chosen replay buffer size (2k steps) and frequency of auxiliary updates; include a short sensitivity comment.

Questions for the authors
- Computational cost: What is the wall-clock overhead of UNREAL vs A3C on Labyrinth and Atari (per training step and to reach A3C’s final score)? Which auxiliary components dominate compute/memory?
- Hyperparameters: What exact λRP, λVR, and λPC were used per domain? Did you try per-task λc in Eq. (1)? How sensitive are results to these weights?
- Off-policy n-step Q-learning: Any observed stability issues? Did you try different n, target networks, or retrace/importance sampling corrections?
- Feature-control: How stable are the control heads when targets are moving (hidden activations)? Any tricks (e.g., lagged targets) used? Can you provide extended results beyond Fig. 5?
- Atari ablations: Can you report per-component ablations analogous to Labyrinth to confirm whether pixel-control, reward prediction, and value replay contribute similarly?
- Reward prediction: Does skewed sampling toward reward events measurably bias the shared representation in ways that harm exploration or generalization? Any empirical checks?
- Longer training: Do UNREAL and A3C converge to the same asymptote if trained longer, or does the gap persist/widen? Please include extended curves for representative tasks.
- Replay design: Why 2k buffer and 20-step cadence? Did larger buffers or prioritized replay for control/value help?
- Pixel-control: For Atari, where intensity change is uneven across games, how did you normalize the pixel-change reward, and how sensitive are results to this normalization?
