1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with auxiliary tasks that shape the representation to enable faster and more robust reinforcement learning under sparse rewards and partial observability. The auxiliary tasks include: (i) off-policy control over pseudo-rewards, notably pixel-control (maximize local pixel-intensity change in a grid) and feature-control (maximize activation of internal units), and (ii) reward-focused prediction (supervised prediction of immediate reward) trained from replay with skewed sampling to overrepresent rewarding events. The unified architecture shares the convolutional and recurrent state representation across the policy and all auxiliary heads, and combines on-policy A3C with off-policy Q-learning for auxiliary control and replayed value regression. Empirically, UNREAL substantially accelerates learning and improves final performance on challenging 3D Labyrinth tasks (≈10× fewer steps to reach A3C’s best and 87% human-normalized performance) and sets a new state of the art on 57 Atari games (mean 880% expert human performance, median 250%), while also increasing robustness to hyperparameters. Ablations indicate that each auxiliary component contributes, with pixel/feature control particularly impactful. The paper is well positioned in the literature relative to Horde, UVFA, successor representations, intrinsic motivation, and reconstruction/model-based approaches, and makes a clear conceptual shift: using many control tasks solely to train a shared representation for the extrinsic task.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, broad empirical gains with careful ablations and robustness analyses across two challenging domains, including state-of-the-art Atari results and large data-efficiency improvements on 3D navigation.
- A well-motivated and novel approach that leverages controllable pseudo-rewards and reward-focused prediction to continuously shape representations even when extrinsic rewards are sparse.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and framing: The paper tackles representation learning for RL in sparse-reward, partially observed settings. Unlike passive unsupervised objectives (e.g., reconstruction), the proposed auxiliary tasks are aligned with control, better matching downstream RL goals.
- Methodological soundness: The combination of on-policy A3C for the main task and off-policy Q-learning on shared features for many auxiliary control tasks is coherent and efficient. Reward prediction with skewed replay is a sensible way to densify reward-related supervision without biasing the policy/value estimators. Value-function replay increases sample efficiency and stabilizes training.
- Positioning in prior work: Clear distinctions from Horde (separate value functions without shared representation), UVFA/successor features (factored value functions used for transfer rather than shaping), and model-based and reconstruction objectives (passive prediction). The focus on control-based auxiliary learning purely as a representation-learning signal is a useful and original perspective.
- Empirical support and rigor: Results cover 13 diverse Labyrinth levels and 57 Atari games, with hyperparameter sweeps and reporting of top agents to mitigate cherry-picking. Ablations show additive benefits of pixel control, reward prediction, and value replay; comparisons to reconstruction and immediate-change prediction support the claim that controllable auxiliary tasks are more helpful than passive prediction. Reported robustness to hyperparameters and consistent speedups strengthen the case.
- Limitations are acknowledged: λPC tuning differs between domains; the main text defers some low-level details to the appendix; deeper analyses of auxiliary-task contributions could be expanded. These do not materially undermine the main claims.

4. Suggestions for improvement (helpful, not central to the accept decision)
- Clarify and front-load technical details: Define the auxiliary control loss (e.g., LPC and its weighting) and deconvolutional heads earlier in the main text. Provide a concise schematic of the complete loss with brief per-term explanations before the appendix.
- Deeper analysis of auxiliary tasks: Beyond RL performance, quantify the quality/impact of pixel and feature control (e.g., returns on the pseudo-tasks, coverage/novelty of induced behaviors, correlation between auxiliary performance and extrinsic returns across seeds). Provide per-component sensitivity to λPC, λRP, λVR and number/placement of controlled features.
- Off-policy considerations: Discuss why Q-learning on shared features from on-policy trajectories does not destabilize A3C, and whether any off-policy corrections or target networks were critical. Ablate replay buffer size, recency bias, and the skewing strategy for reward prediction.
- Computational overhead: Report wall-clock speed vs A3C (training time per environment step, memory footprint), and whether the 10× data-efficiency translates to proportional wall-clock gains.
- Generality: While the discrete-action focus is appropriate here, a short discussion of potential extensions to continuous control and to tasks with dense rewards would help scope the method’s applicability.
- Representation transfer: Since the auxiliary tasks are designed to shape general features, consider a transfer experiment (e.g., new reward function or goal variants) to test whether the learned representation accelerates adaptation.

Questions for the authors
- Pixel control:
  - How sensitive are results to the grid resolution, crop choice, reward normalization, and ReLU on Qaux? Did you explore scale-invariant or contrast-normalized pixel-change rewards?
  - Does pixel control ever encourage undesirable “thrashing” behaviors in visually dynamic environments, and how is this mitigated?
- Feature control:
  - Which layers/units were selected for control, how many auxiliary heads were used, and how were units normalized to avoid trivial saturation? How does feature control interact with pixel control when both are present?
- Loss balancing and optimization:
  - How were λPC, λRP, and λVR chosen per domain? Provide sensitivity plots and any adaptive weighting tried. Did gradient normalization or clipping across heads matter?
  - What is the effect of removing the value-replay term while keeping reward prediction, and vice versa?
- Replay and off-policy:
  - What is the replay buffer size sensitivity (2k frames vs larger)? Did you test prioritized replay for control tasks beyond the reward/non-reward split?
  - Are target networks used for the auxiliary Q-learners; if so, what update period? Any stability issues observed?
- Reward prediction:
  - How does the skewed sampling ratio (0.5 non-zero vs zero) affect performance across tasks? Did you try multi-step reward prediction or reward shaping via auxiliary predictions?
- Robustness and compute:
  - What are the wall-clock training time comparisons vs A3C for Atari and Labyrinth (including auxiliary computations)? Does the method remain advantageous under equal compute budgets?

Overall, the paper makes a clear, well-motivated contribution with strong empirical support, and the suggested clarifications would further strengthen its impact.
