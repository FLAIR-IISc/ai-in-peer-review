- Proposes UNREAL: an A3C agent augmented with unsupervised auxiliary tasks to shape deep RL representations.
- Introduces two auxiliary task families: (1) auxiliary control via off-policy n-step Q-learning on pseudo-rewards (pixel control and feature control).
- Introduces reward-focused auxiliaries: immediate reward prediction with skewed replay toward rare rewards and value-function replay.
- Trains auxiliaries jointly with on-policy A3C using a combined loss and a small replay buffer.
- Empirically yields large gains: on Labyrinth doubles mean human-normalized performance (to 87%) and ~10× faster learning on average.
- Achieves state-of-the-art on 57 Atari games (mean 880% and median 250% human) with improved hyperparameter robustness.
- Ablations show control-based auxiliary tasks outperform prediction-only or reconstruction-based auxiliaries.
- Decision: Accept — due to strong empirical gains, careful ablations, and convincing demonstration that control-oriented auxiliaries shape useful representations.
- Paper addresses learning useful representations in sparse-reward, rich visual RL by predicting/controlling sensorimotor aspects as auxiliaries.
- Well-situated in literature (options, Horde, UVFAs, successor reps, model learning); key distinction is many off-policy control tasks used purely for shared representation learning.
- Experimental evidence spans 13 Labyrinth tasks and 57 Atari games with hyperparameter sweeps, multiple seeds, and detailed ablations.
- Strength: Significant empirical improvements over strong baselines (A3C, Prioritized Dueling DQN).
- Strength: Careful ablations isolating contributions of control-based and reward-focused auxiliaries.
- Strength: Improved data efficiency and robustness to hyperparameters; clear writing and implementation details.
- Weakness: Engineering complexity (mixing on-policy A3C, off-policy Q-heads, replay with class-imbalance sampling, deconv modules) may hinder reproducibility.
- Weakness: Novelty partly incremental—combines known components; lacks theoretical analysis of why control-oriented auxiliaries help.
- Weakness: Computational cost and resource usage (wall-clock time, hardware) are not reported.
- Suggestion: Report computational resources, frames, wall-clock times, and throughput to assess compute vs. data-efficiency tradeoffs.
- Suggestion: Clarify evaluation protocol details for Atari to ensure fair comparisons (frames, action repeat, life-loss handling, sticky actions, human baseline).
- Suggestion: Provide systematic sensitivity analysis for λVR, λPC, λRP, replay buffer size and replay ratio; give tuning guidelines.
- Suggestion: Expand feature control experiments and directly compare pixel vs. feature control across games.
- Suggestion: Discuss domain dependence of pixel-control rewards (textures, illumination) and possible normalizations for invariance.
- Suggestion: Release code and training scripts to improve reproducibility and adoption.
- Questions: What hardware and wall-clock time per run; replay/update ratio and FPS achieved?
- Questions: How were λPC, λRP, λVR chosen; sensitivity and heuristics for tuning them?
- Questions: Does reward-prediction oversampling bias representations and harm dense-reward tasks; evaluated on dense-reward Atari?
- Questions: Are training budgets and evaluation settings matched to A3C and Dueling DQN on Atari?
- Questions: Stability of mixing on-policy A3C with off-policy Q-learning on shared parameters; observed interference and mitigations?
- Questions: How critical are pixel-control design choices (grid size, abs-diff cell reward, deconv architecture); tried multi-scale or feature-space change targets?
- Question: Will you release code and pre-trained models?
- Post-rebuttal: Reviewer did not change assessment; paper remains solid and would benefit from compute reporting and code release.
