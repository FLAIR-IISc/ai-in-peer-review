1. Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with unsupervised auxiliary tasks that shape the shared representation. The auxiliary control tasks are pseudo-reward maximization problems (pixel control and feature control) trained off-policy via n-step Q-learning using a replay buffer; the auxiliary reward task predicts immediate reward from short histories with rebalanced sampling to oversample rare rewards; and value-function replay performs extra regression updates from the buffer. A single combined loss balances on-policy A3C with these off-policy auxiliary objectives.
- Conceptually, the work reframes unsupervised learning in RL as learning to predict and control features of the sensorimotor stream, contrasting with reconstruction-based objectives. The architecture leverages shared CNN/LSTM state, deconvolutional Q-heads for spatial control, and prioritized-like replay for reward prediction without biasing the policy/value learning.
- Empirically, UNREAL significantly outperforms strong baselines: on 13 3D Labyrinth tasks, it achieves 87% human-normalized performance with ~10× data-efficiency vs A3C; on 57 Atari games, it reports 880% mean and 250% median human-normalized performance, surpassing A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN, and shows improved robustness to hyperparameters. Ablations demonstrate that each auxiliary task contributes and that control-based auxiliaries outperform reconstruction or immediate-pixel-change prediction.
- The method is well positioned in the literature relative to options, Horde/UVFA/successor representations, and model learning; its novelty lies in using many auxiliary control tasks solely for representation shaping and integrating them with an on-policy recurrent agent and replayed off-policy updates.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (i) strong and broad empirical gains in both efficiency and final performance across two challenging domains with careful ablations; (ii) a clear, well-motivated integration of auxiliary control/prediction tasks into a standard deep RL stack, likely to influence practice.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Sparse extrinsic rewards impede representation learning. Using pseudo-rewards that capture controllable structure in the sensorimotor stream is well motivated and clearly situated relative to unsupervised reconstruction and prior RL formulations (options, Horde, UVFA, successor features).
- Methodological soundness: The combined loss and training procedure are reasonable—on-policy A3C for the main objective, off-policy Q-learning for auxiliary control, rebalanced replay for reward prediction. The off-policy nature is appropriate for learning many auxiliary tasks from a single stream. Using deconvolutional heads to exploit spatial structure is sensible.
- Evidence: The experimental scope is large (57 Atari, 13 Labyrinth), with multiple seeds/hyperparameter sweeps and ablations isolating contributions (pixel control vs feature control vs reconstruction vs input-change prediction; reward prediction; value replay). Improvements in both speed and robustness bolster the claims. While the headline result (auxiliary tasks help) is not surprising, the extent and consistency of gains make the contribution significant.
- Clarity: The paper is well written and easy to follow for readers familiar with deep RL. Implementation details are provided (architectures, losses, replay buffer, task weights).
- Limitations: Novelty is incremental conceptually; the architecture is complex with many components and hyperparameters, which may challenge reproducibility. Fairness of comparisons and compute budgets could be documented more completely.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are part of the accept decision)
- Reproducibility: Given architectural complexity, releasing code and default hyperparameters (including λPC/λVR/λRP schedules, reward normalization for pixel control, buffer sizes) would meaningfully lower the barrier to adoption. A minimal reference implementation would be valuable.
- Compute and protocol transparency: Report wall-clock time, number/type of CPUs/GPUs, number of parallel actors, total frames per game, and energy usage where feasible. Clarify how hyperparameters were selected and ensure baselines receive comparable sweeps and budgets.
- Evaluation details: Specify for Atari the use of no-op or human starts, life-loss termination, action repeat/sticky actions, and episode termination criteria. Provide per-game mean±std over seeds and confidence intervals for aggregate metrics.
- Off-policy considerations: Discuss absence of importance sampling for auxiliary Q-updates, any instabilities observed, and how losses are balanced to prevent domination by auxiliary gradients. Report sensitivity to replay buffer size and rebalancing strategy.
- Negative transfer and analysis: Examine when auxiliary control might hurt (e.g., visually noisy games), sensitivity to λPC and reward scaling across games, and feature visualizations linking auxiliaries to improved policy/value features. Quantify overhead vs performance gains (FPS and wall-clock).
- Broader comparisons: Include stronger self-supervised baselines (e.g., predictive coding/forward models) and recent intrinsic control objectives, to contextualize the advantage of control-based auxiliaries beyond reconstruction.

5. Questions for the authors
- Computational resources: How many CPUs/GPUs, threads, and hours were required per Atari game and per Labyrinth level to reach reported performance? What was the average frames-per-second and total environment frames per run?
- Code release: Do you plan to release code, trained models, and configuration files? If so, what is the expected timeline?
- Hyperparameter fairness: For Atari and Labyrinth, did A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN baselines receive identical hyperparameter sweep sizes and training-frame budgets? Were the top-3 curves selected post hoc per game, and was selection based on validation or training performance?
- Replay and rebalancing: How sensitive is performance to the replay buffer size (2k) and the 50/50 reward rebalancing for reward prediction? What happens if rebalancing is removed or varied?
- Loss balancing and stability: How are auxiliary losses normalized across games with different pixel-intensity dynamics? Did you observe divergence or strong interference without careful λ tuning? Any automatic weighting strategies tried?
- Evaluation protocol: For Atari, which starting-state regime (no-op/human starts), life loss handling, and sticky actions were used? Please detail episode termination and score normalization procedures to facilitate exact reproduction.
- Feature control: Which layer did you control, how was it chosen, and how sensitive are results to this choice? Do feature-control policies ever get executed, or are they purely representational?
- Generalization/transfer: Does pretraining auxiliaries on one set of tasks and fine-tuning on others help? Any cross-game transfer experiments?
- Failure cases: Are there games or levels where auxiliaries hurt final performance or robustness? If so, can you characterize them and suggest mitigations?

After reading the rebuttal, I did not change my assessment. The core strengths—broad, consistent empirical gains and a practical, well-motivated design—remain compelling, and the clarifications requested above would further strengthen the contribution.
