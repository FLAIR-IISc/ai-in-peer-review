Summary and contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an RL agent that augments on-policy learning (A3C) with a suite of auxiliary tasks designed to shape a shared representation and accelerate learning, especially under sparse rewards and partial observability. The auxiliary tasks include:
- Auxiliary control: off-policy Q-learning for (i) pixel-control (maximizing local pixel change in an n×n grid) and (ii) feature-control (maximizing activations of selected hidden units).
- Reward-focused auxiliary tasks: immediate reward prediction trained on skewed replay that balances positive/negative vs zero-reward transitions.
- Value function replay: additional value regression updates from recent experience.

All tasks share the visual encoder (and LSTM for control heads), and losses are combined into a single objective. Empirically, UNREAL significantly improves data efficiency, robustness to hyperparameters, and final performance on both 3D Labyrinth and Atari: on Labyrinth, mean human-normalized performance increases from 54% to 87% and requires <10% of the data to match A3C’s final scores (∼10× speedup); on Atari, UNREAL surpasses A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN with 880% mean and 250% median human-normalized performance. Ablations indicate that auxiliary control, reward prediction, and value replay each help, and that control (pixel/feature) outperforms purely predictive unsupervised baselines (reconstruction, next-change prediction).

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, consistent empirical gains in both data efficiency and robustness across two challenging domains, with careful ablations.
- A well-motivated and novel use of multiple auxiliary control and reward-focused tasks that leverage the agent’s ability to influence the environment to learn more task-relevant representations.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is addressed? How to learn useful representations and learn efficiently when extrinsic rewards are sparse and observation streams are rich and partially observed.
- Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The paper clearly positions itself relative to options/pseudo-reward methods (which typically use learned policies directly), Horde/UVFAs/successor representations (multi-task value learning and generalization), and unsupervised/model-based prediction. The key novelty is to use many control tasks purely as auxiliary objectives for representation learning, trained off-policy with replay, and to explicitly focus features on reward through biased reward prediction.
- Do results support claims? Largely yes. The experiments cover 13 diverse 3D Labyrinth levels and 57 Atari games; they include ablations (no pixel control, no auxiliary tasks), comparisons to predictive baselines (reconstruction, input-change prediction), hyperparameter robustness analyses, and clear speedup metrics (steps to reach A3C best). The improvement trends are consistent: auxiliary control > prediction-only > reconstruction, which supports the hypothesis that control-oriented auxiliary signals produce more task-relevant features. Reporting top-k hyperparameters and robustness plots helps mitigate tuning confounds.

Caveats:
- Fairness of comparison: UNREAL performs additional replay-based updates not available in baseline A3C; while this is integral to the approach, it muddies the attribution of gains between better training signal vs more updates per environment step. The authors do provide ablations and robustness sweeps, which partially address this.
- Analysis depth: While pixel/feature control appears most impactful, a deeper, isolated study of these tasks (e.g., metrics of controllability, sensitivity to grid size/normalization, when they help/hurt) would strengthen the causal story.
- Presentation: Some core components (e.g., the auxiliary control loss and architectural details) are deferred to the appendix; moving essentials (definitions of L_PC, reward normalization, grid specs) into the main text would improve clarity.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Clarify training budget: Report both environment steps and total gradient updates for each method to better contextualize data efficiency vs compute efficiency. A matched-update A3C baseline (if feasible) would be informative.
- Expand analysis of auxiliary task design:
  - Pixel control: study grid resolution, cropping, and reward normalization across games; analyze sensitivity and failure cases (e.g., visually noisy environments where pixel-change is uninformative).
  - Feature control: include more results; compare controlling earlier vs later layers; study potential redundancy/conflicts with pixel control.
- Reward prediction skew: quantify how the balanced sampling (non-zero vs zero) affects feature learning and whether it introduces covariate shift; show performance when removing the skew or varying the skew ratio.
- Off-policy stability: provide diagnostics on Q-learning targets (e.g., TD error distributions) and the impact of replay buffer size and recency window.
- Generalization: consider preliminary tests on continuous-control domains or other 3D platforms to assess how pixel/feature control transfers.
- Visualization and interpretability: add qualitative analyses linking auxiliary Q-maps/feature activations to downstream policy behavior, and measure whether auxiliary-head policies are themselves competent.

Questions for the authors
- Compute budget and fairness:
  - How many auxiliary/off-policy updates are performed per environment step? Can you report wall-clock and update-normalized comparisons to A3C?
  - Did you try a variant of A3C with value replay only (no control/reward prediction) to isolate the effect of replay itself?
- Auxiliary control details:
  - How sensitive is performance to the pixel-control grid size, cropping strategy, and reward normalization (especially on Atari with heterogeneous pixel dynamics)?
  - For feature control, which layer/units were selected and why? Did you observe conflicts between pixel and feature control gradients?
- Reward prediction:
  - How does the 50/50 skew between non-zero and zero reward affect convergence? What happens with different skew ratios or without skew?
  - Given shared convolutional features, can the skew bias the policy indirectly? Any observed negative transfer?
- Replay and off-policy learning:
  - What is the effect of replay buffer size and recency (2k frames) on stability and performance? Did you try prioritized replay for control/value updates?
  - Any indications of divergence or instability from off-policy Q-learning with recurrent networks? How are target networks/lag managed?
- Robustness and generality:
  - Are there domains where pixel control hurts (e.g., highly dynamic textures with irrelevant motion)? Any mitigation strategies?
  - How would you adapt UNREAL to continuous action spaces or tasks with vector observations rather than images?
- Evaluation protocol:
  - For the top-3 reporting, how were hyperparameters selected (validation vs test)? How many seeds per setting, and do results hold when aggregating all seeds/settings?

Overall, this is a strong, well-motivated paper with compelling empirical results and a clear contribution to representation learning for RL via auxiliary control and reward-focusing tasks. The suggested clarifications and extended analyses would further solidify the claims and broaden the impact.
