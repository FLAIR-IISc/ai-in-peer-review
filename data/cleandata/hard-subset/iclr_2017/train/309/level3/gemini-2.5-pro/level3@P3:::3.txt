### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel and effective method for improving the sample efficiency and final performance of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core contribution is the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, which augments a standard A3C agent with a set of auxiliary tasks designed to promote the learning of richer and more robust feature representations. The authors introduce two innovative self-supervised auxiliary control tasks where the agent learns policies to maximize local changes: one for controlling pixel intensity in different regions of the input image, and another for controlling the activations of its own hidden units. These are complemented by a supervised reward prediction task, which is trained offline on rebalanced experience to better handle sparse rewards, and a value function replay task. The empirical evaluation is extensive, demonstrating that the UNREAL agent achieves state-of-the-art results on 57 Atari games and a new, challenging 3D first-person environment called Labyrinth. Notably, the agent learns approximately 10 times faster than the A3C baseline and shows significantly greater robustness to hyperparameter settings.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Strong Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper introduces a compelling and well-motivated approach for representation learning in RL. The idea of using auxiliary *control* tasks, where the agent learns to actively influence its sensory input and internal state, is a significant departure from more passive unsupervised or model-based methods. This leverages the agent's interaction with the environment as a rich, intrinsic learning signal.
2.  **Strong Empirical Support:** The claims are backed by extensive and convincing empirical results across two complex domains. The reported 10x speedup in learning and the substantial performance gains over a state-of-the-art baseline (A3C) provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed auxiliary tasks.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The primary strength of this work lies in its conceptual shift from passive observation to active control for representation learning. While prior work has used auxiliary prediction tasks (e.g., reconstructing inputs), this paper posits that learning to *control* features of the sensorimotor stream is a more powerful objective. By training policies to maximize pixel changes or feature activations, the agent is forced to learn representations that capture the causal structure of the environment—what aspects it can influence and how. This active learning paradigm is a natural fit for RL and provides a dense training signal that is present even when extrinsic rewards are sparse, helping to mitigate challenges in partially observable environments.

The empirical validation is thorough and methodologically sound. The authors evaluate their UNREAL agent on the standard Atari benchmark as well as the visually rich and complex Labyrinth environment, demonstrating the generality of their approach. The performance gains are not marginal; achieving an average of 880% human performance on Atari and reaching the baseline's peak performance in an order of magnitude fewer steps are highly significant results. The authors are transparent about methodological details, such as differences in hyperparameter tuning between their agent and the A3C baseline, and mitigate potential bias by reporting performance over the top-3 hyperparameter settings, which adds confidence in the robustness of their findings.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to further strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision.

1.  **Clarity on Auxiliary Task Implementation:** The description of the auxiliary tasks, particularly the control tasks, is quite high-level in the main body of the paper. For instance, the specific loss formulation for pixel control (L_PC) and the details of its deconvolutional architecture are deferred to the appendix. Including a more formal definition and a concise architectural diagram for the auxiliary heads within the main text would improve the paper's self-containment and make the method easier for readers to understand and reimplement.

2.  **Deeper Analysis of Auxiliary Task Contributions:** The ablation studies convincingly show that the combination of auxiliary tasks is beneficial. However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of the individual components. The pixel and feature control tasks appear particularly impactful and warrant a deeper investigation beyond final RL performance. Future work could explore questions such as: What specific features do these control tasks encourage the network to learn? How does the representation learned by UNREAL differ qualitatively from that of A3C (e.g., via t-SNE visualizations of the LSTM state)? Providing such insights would elevate the paper from demonstrating a method that *works* to explaining *why* it works.

### **Questions for the Authors**

To help clarify my understanding and strengthen my assessment, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  The ablation study in Figure 3 shows the cumulative benefit of adding tasks. Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the individual contribution of each auxiliary task (e.g., A3C + Pixel Control alone, A3C + Reward Prediction alone) on a representative subset of environments? This would help clarify the relative importance of each component.
2.  The pixel control task partitions the input into a 20x20 grid. How sensitive is the agent's performance to the granularity of this grid? For example, would a coarser or finer grid yield similar improvements?
3.  The UNREAL agent introduces several additional network heads and loss computations. Could you comment on the computational overhead (e.g., wall-clock training time per million frames) compared to the A3C baseline? This would be useful for understanding the practical trade-offs of the approach.
