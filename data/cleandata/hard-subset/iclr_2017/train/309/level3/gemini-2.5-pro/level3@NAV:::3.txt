This paper proposes a novel and effective method for accelerating reinforcement learning by training agents on a set of auxiliary tasks alongside the main reward-maximization objective. The core idea is that by learning to control and predict aspects of its sensory input, the agent develops a richer and more robust feature representation, which significantly benefits the primary policy. The authors introduce two innovative pseudo-control tasks—controlling pixel changes and latent feature activations—and a supervised reward prediction task. The empirical results are outstanding, showing that their UNREAL agent achieves state-of-the-art performance on the Atari benchmark and demonstrates a remarkable 10x speedup in learning on a challenging 3D Labyrinth environment.

**Decision: Accept**
The primary reasons for acceptance are (1) the novelty and strong motivation behind using active, self-supervised control tasks for representation learning in RL, and (2) the extensive and compelling empirical evidence that demonstrates a significant leap in sample efficiency and final performance.

This work’s main strength lies in reframing auxiliary task learning from passive prediction (e.g., reconstruction) to active control. By training policies to intentionally influence pixel intensities or internal feature activations, the agent is forced to learn a causal understanding of its own impact on the environment. This is a powerful, intrinsic learning signal that is more aligned with an agent's ultimate goal of goal-directed behavior than simply modeling the environment's dynamics. The empirical validation is thorough and convincing. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the positive contribution of each auxiliary task, and the performance gains are not just marginal but substantial, particularly the 10x learning speedup. The robustness of the method to hyperparameter settings is another strong point that highlights its practical value. The methodology is sound, and the authors are transparent about architectural differences from the baseline.

**Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper**
While the results are excellent, the paper could be improved with more detailed explanations and analysis.
-   The descriptions of the auxiliary tasks in the main text are quite high-level. For instance, the pixel control task and its associated loss L_PC are central contributions but are not formally introduced until the appendix. Moving some of these architectural and implementation details into the main body would greatly improve clarity and reproducibility for the reader.
-   The paper convincingly shows that the auxiliary tasks work, but a deeper analysis of *how* they work would be beneficial. The pixel and feature control tasks appear to be the most impactful; future work should include a more isolated study of these components. It would be valuable to see metrics beyond overall RL score that evaluate the performance of the control policies themselves.

**Questions for Authors**
1.  The ablation study suggests pixel control is a major contributor to the performance gains. Have you visualized or analyzed the pixel control policies themselves? Do they learn locally meaningful behaviors (e.g., moving towards high-frequency textures), and does the quality of control correlate with downstream task performance?
2.  For the reward prediction task, you use a skewed replay sampling of 50/50 zero/non-zero rewards. How sensitive is performance to this ratio, and did you consider other forms of prioritization?
