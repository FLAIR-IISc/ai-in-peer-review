### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), an agent that enhances the Asynchronous Advantage Actor-Critic (A3C) framework with a suite of unsupervised auxiliary tasks. The central hypothesis is that by learning to predict and control features of its sensorimotor stream, an agent can develop a richer and more effective representation, leading to faster and more robust learning of the main reinforcement learning objective. The authors propose two main classes of auxiliary tasks: 1) Auxiliary control tasks, where the agent learns off-policy Q-functions to maximize changes in pixel inputs (pixel control) or in its own network activations (feature control). 2) Auxiliary reward tasks, where the agent predicts immediate future rewards from a sequence of observations, with training data rebalanced to oversample rewarding events. These auxiliary objectives are trained off-policy using an experience replay buffer alongside the main on-policy A3C updates. The effectiveness of the UNREAL agent is demonstrated through extensive experiments on a new, visually complex 3D environment called Labyrinth, and on the standard Atari 2600 benchmark. The results are impressive, showing that UNREAL significantly outperforms the A3C baseline in both final performance and data efficiency.

### **Decision and Justification**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that makes a solid and valuable contribution to the field of deep reinforcement learning. The key reasons for this recommendation are:

1.  **Significant Empirical Improvement:** The paper presents compelling and substantial performance gains over a state-of-the-art baseline (A3C). The UNREAL agent achieves an average 10x learning speedup on the Labyrinth environment and more than doubles the final human-normalized score. On Atari, it sets a new state-of-the-art, achieving an average of 880% human performance. These are not incremental improvements but a significant leap forward.
2.  **Effective Integration of Auxiliary Tasks:** The paper provides an excellent proof-of-concept for how unsupervised and self-supervised objectives can be integrated into a deep RL agent to accelerate representation learning. The specific choice of tasks—pixel control and reward prediction—are well-motivated and the ablation studies clearly demonstrate their individual and combined benefits.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this work lies in its comprehensive empirical validation. The authors introduce and evaluate their method on two challenging and diverse domains. The new Labyrinth environment, with its rich visuals and complex 3D navigation and combat tasks, is a valuable contribution in its own right and serves as a challenging testbed for modern RL agents. The results on this domain are particularly convincing.

The paper’s methodology is sound. It builds upon the strong A3C baseline and systematically adds auxiliary components. The architectural design, which combines on-policy A3C for the main task with off-policy Q-learning from a replay buffer for the auxiliary tasks, is a clever way to maximize data efficiency without destabilizing the main policy learning. The ablation studies clearly isolate the impact of each component (pixel control, reward prediction, value replay), lending strong support to the authors' claims about their utility.

While the core idea that relevant auxiliary tasks can improve representation learning is not surprising, the paper's value is in its concrete implementation and rigorous demonstration. It moves beyond a general concept to show *how* a specific combination of unsupervised control and prediction tasks can be successfully engineered to achieve state-of-the-art performance in complex RL environments. The work is also well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow for readers with expertise in deep reinforcement learning.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to the decision to accept.

The overall architecture of the UNREAL agent is quite complex, combining an on-policy actor-critic algorithm with several off-policy auxiliary losses trained from a replay buffer, each with its own network head. This complexity could present a barrier to reproducibility and wider adoption. In light of this, I have a few questions for the authors:

1.  **Computational Resources:** The proposed method adds significant computational machinery on top of the A3C baseline. Could you provide some information on the computational overhead of the UNREAL agent? For example, what is the increase in wall-clock training time or required CPU/GPU resources to achieve the reported results compared to the A3C baseline for a fixed number of environment steps? This information would be very helpful for researchers looking to build upon this work.
2.  **Code Release:** Given the complexity of the proposed agent and the introduction of the new Labyrinth environment, do you plan to release the source code? A public implementation would be an immense contribution to the community, facilitating the verification of these impressive results and enabling future research in this direction.
