### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel deep reinforcement learning agent, named UNREAL, that significantly advances the state-of-the-art by incorporating auxiliary tasks to accelerate and improve representation learning. The core idea is to enrich the learning process beyond just maximizing the main extrinsic reward. The agent simultaneously learns policies for several auxiliary "pseudo-reward" tasks in an off-policy manner, forcing its shared network representation to capture a much richer understanding of the environment's dynamics.

The key contributions are threefold:
1.  **Auxiliary Control Tasks:** The paper proposes a novel form of unsupervised learning where the agent learns to *control* aspects of its sensory input (pixel changes in different regions of the screen) or its own internal state (activations of hidden layer features). This is framed as a set of parallel reinforcement learning problems, which encourages the agent to learn about cause and effect within the environment.
2.  **Auxiliary Reward Prediction:** The agent is trained on an auxiliary supervised task to predict the immediate reward from a recent sequence of observations. Critically, this is trained using experience replay with a skewed sampling strategy that over-represents rare but important rewarding events, helping the agent learn value-relevant features even when rewards are sparse.
3.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The combined UNREAL agent demonstrates remarkable empirical success. It achieves an average of 880% expert human performance on the Atari 2600 benchmark, a substantial improvement over previous methods. Furthermore, on a new and challenging suite of first-person 3D Labyrinth tasks, it shows a 10x mean speedup in learning time compared to the A3C baseline, showcasing its data efficiency and general applicability.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper with a clear and significant contribution. The key reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Novelty and Significance of Auxiliary Control:** The concept of training auxiliary policies to control features of the sensory stream or the agent's internal representation is a powerful and novel idea. It shifts the paradigm of auxiliary tasks from passive prediction/reconstruction to active, goal-directed interaction, providing a much richer training signal that is highly relevant for an embodied agent.
2.  **Outstanding Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling evidence that the proposed methods are highly effective. The UNREAL agent not only achieves state-of-the-art performance on the standard Atari benchmark but also demonstrates massive gains in data efficiency (a 10x speedup) on a complex, visually rich 3D environment. This combination of top-tier performance and improved learning speed is a major practical achievement.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper is well-motivated by the fundamental challenge of sparse rewards in reinforcement learning. Instead of waiting for extrinsic reward signals, the UNREAL agent generates its own dense training signals by trying to control its environment. This approach is well-placed in the literature, correctly identifying connections to Horde, UVFAs, and successor representations, while clearly distinguishing its unique contribution of using auxiliary policies purely for representation learning.

The claims are strongly supported by rigorous experiments. The use of A3C as a powerful baseline is appropriate. The ablation studies in Figure 3 are crucial, as they systematically demonstrate that each of the proposed components (pixel control, reward prediction, value replay) contributes positively to the final performance. This validates the overall design of the UNREAL architecture. The inclusion of the new Labyrinth environment is also a strength, as it pushes the boundaries beyond Atari and shows the agent's effectiveness in domains requiring 3D navigation, exploration, and fine-grained motor control.

The comparison with more traditional unsupervised tasks like pixel reconstruction is particularly insightful. The results in Figure 5 suggest that active control is a more effective auxiliary objective for RL than passive reconstruction, which can even hurt final performance by focusing representation capacity on irrelevant details. This finding is an important contribution in itself. Finally, the analysis showing increased robustness to hyperparameter settings is a significant practical benefit that further strengthens the paper's claims.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors

The work is excellent, and the following points are intended to help further improve its clarity and impact. They are not critical for the acceptance decision.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Clarity on Architecture:** Some key architectural details for the auxiliary tasks are described in the appendix (Section B) but are central to the method. For instance, the use of a deconvolutional network to produce the Q-values for pixel control is a clever way to exploit spatial structure. A brief description or diagram of this in the main paper would improve readability and make the work more self-contained. Similarly, explicitly defining `L_PC` as the sum of Q-learning losses in the main text before its use in Equation 2 would be helpful.
*   **Deeper Analysis of Auxiliary Tasks:** The paper convincingly shows *that* the auxiliary tasks improve performance, but provides limited insight into *what* the auxiliary control policies actually learn. The work could be made even more impactful with a qualitative analysis of these learned behaviors. For example, visualizing a policy that maximizes change in a corner of the screen might reveal interesting and understandable behaviors. This would provide a deeper understanding of why this mechanism works so well.
*   **Feature Control:** The feature control experiments are mentioned as preliminary but show very promising results (Figure 5). This seems like a powerful idea that is perhaps even more general than pixel control. Expanding on this, for instance by analyzing which features the agent learns to control, could be a very fruitful direction for future work and might merit more discussion.

**Questions:**
1.  Could you provide any qualitative examples of the learned pixel control policies? For instance, what actions does an agent take when its sole objective is to maximize pixel change in a specific 4x4 grid cell? Does this lead to interpretable sub-policies like "find and shoot an enemy" or "move to a shimmering wall"?
2.  The reward prediction task is trained on rebalanced data to ensure a 50/50 split between zero and non-zero rewards. How sensitive is the agent's performance to this ratio? Is there a risk of the representation becoming overly biased towards reward-predictive features at the expense of general environment dynamics?
3.  The paper compares auxiliary control to pixel *prediction* and *reconstruction*. The reconstruction baseline appears to learn faster initially but then achieves a worse final score than vanilla A3C. Could you elaborate on your hypothesis for this performance degradation? Does the reconstruction objective effectively "distract" the convolutional encoder from learning features that are critical for long-term value estimation?
