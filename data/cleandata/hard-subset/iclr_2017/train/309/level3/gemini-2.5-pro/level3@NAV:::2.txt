**Summary of Claims and Contributions**

This paper introduces the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, a novel architecture that significantly enhances feature learning and data efficiency in deep reinforcement learning. The core contribution is the augmentation of a base A3C agent with a set of domain-independent auxiliary tasks: pixel control, feature control, reward prediction, and value function replay. These tasks share a common CNN-LSTM representation with the main policy, enabling the agent to learn a richer set of features even with sparse extrinsic rewards. The authors provide strong empirical evidence on 57 Atari games and a challenging 3D Labyrinth environment, demonstrating substantial improvements in learning speed and final performance over state-of-the-art baselines.

**Recommendation and Justification**

Clear Accept. This is a well-written and impactful paper that presents a compelling and well-motivated method. The two key reasons for this recommendation are: 1) The proposed auxiliary tasks lead to substantial and convincingly demonstrated performance gains over a very strong baseline (A3C) in complex, high-dimensional environments. 2) The experimental methodology is rigorous, including ablation studies that effectively validate the contribution of individual components.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's claims are strongly supported by its empirical results. Achieving an average of 880% expert human performance on Atari and a mean learning speedup of 10x on the visually rich Labyrinth environment are significant achievements. The concept of using self-supervised auxiliary objectives to shape a shared representation is an elegant solution to the challenge of representation learning in RL, particularly in reward-sparse settings. The ablation studies in the Labyrinth environment clearly show that each auxiliary component contributes positively to the final performance, reinforcing the design of the overall UNREAL agent.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to further improve the manuscript:
*   **Clarity:** Please clarify in the abstract/introduction that separate policies are learned for each auxiliary task. It would also be helpful to explicitly mention that the auxiliary tasks are trained off-policy using multi-step empirical returns and not strictly with Q-learning.
*   **Feature Control Validation:** The empirical support for the feature-control task is less compelling than for other components. Figure 5 is unconvincing and raises questions about stability, as the target network features are non-stationary during training.
*   **Minor Corrections:** Several minor fixes are needed: Eq.1 should have λ_c inside the summation; Figure 2 is not referenced in the text; Eq.2 references an undefined L_PC; there are multiple incorrect figure references (e.g., in section 4.1.2); a grammar fix is needed (“features… are shared”).

**Questions for Authors**
*   Could you quantify the additional computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks in terms of wall-clock training time? Which components are most expensive?
*   The auxiliary task weights (λ_VR, λ_RP) were set to 1. Could you comment on this choice and whether they were validated as hyperparameters?
*   Since agent performance was still improving at the end of training in some figures, have you considered running experiments longer? It would be interesting to see the peak performance.
*   Could you provide more ablation studies on the Atari suite to confirm if the per-component benefits are consistent with those observed in Labyrinth?
