**Summary**

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), a novel deep reinforcement learning agent that significantly enhances a strong baseline agent (A3C) by incorporating auxiliary tasks. The central idea is to leverage the rich sensorimotor data stream for representation learning, especially when extrinsic rewards are sparse. The authors propose two main classes of auxiliary tasks: 1) auxiliary control tasks, where the agent learns separate off-policy value functions to control features of the environment (e.g., maximizing pixel changes or hidden unit activations); and 2) auxiliary reward tasks, which involve predicting the onset of immediate rewards from recent experience, using a skewed sampling strategy to focus on rare but important reward events. The UNREAL agent cleverly combines the on-policy A3C algorithm for the main task with off-policy Q-learning for the auxiliary control tasks and supervised learning for reward prediction, all trained from a shared replay buffer. The empirical results are impressive, demonstrating substantial improvements in both learning speed and final performance over A3C on the standard 57 Atari games and a new, visually complex 3D first-person environment called Labyrinth. The agent achieves state-of-the-art results, averaging 880% expert human performance on Atari and showing a 10x mean speedup in learning on Labyrinth.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that I recommend for acceptance. My decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  The paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound method for integrating unsupervised and self-supervised objectives into a state-of-the-art deep RL agent. The proposed auxiliary tasks, particularly the control-oriented ones, are a novel and effective way to drive representation learning.
2.  The claims are supported by extensive and compelling empirical evidence across two challenging domains. The significant performance gains, accelerated learning, and increased hyperparameter robustness are clearly demonstrated through thorough experiments and ablation studies.

**Supporting Arguments**

The core idea of using auxiliary tasks to aid representation learning is not entirely new, but this paper provides one of the most convincing and comprehensive demonstrations of its effectiveness in the context of deep RL. The formulation of "pixel control" is particularly insightful. Rather than simply reconstructing or predicting future frames—a passive objective that might waste capacity on irrelevant details—the agent is tasked with actively learning to cause change. This objective encourages the agent to discover the controllable aspects of its environment, which should intuitively lead to more causal and useful features for the main control task.

The empirical evaluation is a major strength of this work. The authors test their agent on both the widely-used Atari benchmark and a new, challenging 3D Labyrinth environment, which features richer visuals and more complex physics. The results are not marginal improvements; they represent a significant leap over a very strong A3C baseline. The ablation study presented in Figure 3 clearly decomposes the contribution of each auxiliary component, justifying the complexity of the final UNREAL architecture. Furthermore, the direct comparison in Figure 5 between pixel control, pixel change prediction, and pixel reconstruction provides strong evidence for the authors' hypothesis that control-based objectives are more beneficial for learning than predictive/reconstructive ones. The demonstrated improvement in robustness to hyperparameters is also a significant practical contribution, as it suggests the method is more reliable and easier to deploy.

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow for a reader familiar with deep reinforcement learning. It does an excellent job of placing the work in the context of prior literature and motivating its architectural choices.

**Additional Feedback**

While the overall contribution is strong, the following points are intended to help improve the paper further. These are suggestions and do not detract from my decision to accept.

*   The UNREAL agent is an elegant synthesis of several ideas (A3C, experience replay, Q-learning, dueling networks, auxiliary losses), but this also makes it a rather complex system. The ablation study is excellent at showing that all parts are necessary for the best performance, but it would be interesting to see some discussion on the relative importance of each component. For instance, is pixel control generally more impactful than reward prediction, or does their importance vary depending on the environment's reward sparsity?
*   The comparison against pixel reconstruction and prediction in Section 4.1.1 is very valuable. This experiment is key to supporting the central claim about the superiority of control tasks. While I understand the computational constraints, extending this specific comparison to a few representative Atari games would have further strengthened this claim.

**Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points to improve my understanding of the practical implications of their work:

1.  **Computational Cost:** The UNREAL agent adds several network heads and performs additional training steps on data from a replay buffer. Could you quantify the computational overhead of this approach? For instance, what is the approximate increase in wall-clock training time per million environment steps compared to the A3C baseline?
2.  **Reproducibility:** Given the complexity of the architecture, which combines on-policy and off-policy updates from multiple loss functions, reproducing the results could be challenging. Do you plan to release the source code for the UNREAL agent and the Labyrinth environment to facilitate further research in this promising direction?
