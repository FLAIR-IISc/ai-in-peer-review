### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel deep reinforcement learning agent, UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), that significantly improves upon the state-of-the-art A3C agent. The core contribution is the augmentation of the standard RL objective with a set of domain-independent auxiliary tasks designed to accelerate and enrich representation learning. These tasks include: 1) auxiliary control tasks, where the agent learns off-policy value functions for maximizing pseudo-rewards like pixel changes ("pixel control") or internal network activations ("feature control"); 2) an auxiliary reward prediction task, which uses a skewed replay buffer to predict immediate future rewards; and 3) value function replay, which performs additional value iteration steps on recent experience. By sharing the convolutional and recurrent network layers between the main policy and these auxiliary tasks, the agent develops a more robust and general-purpose representation. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach with impressive empirical results, showing substantial improvements in data efficiency, final performance, and hyperparameter robustness on both the 57-game Atari suite and a new, visually rich 3D environment called Labyrinth.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a clear accept. The paper is well-written, the proposed method is well-motivated, and the empirical results are convincing and substantial. The key reasons for my decision are:

1.  **Significant Performance Improvement:** The UNREAL agent achieves state-of-the-art results, averaging 880% of expert human performance on Atari and 87% on the challenging Labyrinth domain. The 10x mean speedup in learning on Labyrinth is particularly impressive and directly addresses the critical issue of data efficiency in deep RL.
2.  **Novel and Generalizable Method:** The introduction of auxiliary control and prediction tasks as a general mechanism for representation learning is a strong contribution. These tasks are unsupervised in the sense that they do not require extrinsic reward signals, making the approach widely applicable and a valuable addition to the deep RL toolbox.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main claim is that augmenting an A3C agent with auxiliary tasks improves learning speed, final performance, and robustness. This claim is well-supported by extensive experiments. The ablation studies on the Labyrinth environment clearly demonstrate that each auxiliary component (pixel control, reward prediction, value replay) contributes positively to the overall performance, justifying their inclusion. The comparison against alternative unsupervised methods, such as pixel reconstruction, further strengthens the case for the proposed auxiliary control tasks.

The introduction of the Labyrinth environment is itself a valuable contribution, providing a new, challenging benchmark with richer visuals and more complex physics than many existing domains. The agent's success in this environment highlights the method's ability to handle complex perceptual and control challenges. The paper is also well-situated in the literature, clearly distinguishing its approach from related work like Horde, UVFAs, and successor representations by focusing on using auxiliary policies purely for representation learning rather than for temporal abstraction or planning.

### 4. Additional Feedback and Questions

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

**Feedback for Improvement:**
*   **Clarity on Auxiliary Policies:** In the abstract and introduction, it could be made clearer that the agent learns *separate* policies for each auxiliary task, rather than a single policy that tries to maximize a weighted sum of all rewards. The current phrasing, "learns separate policies for maximising many other pseudo-reward functions," is good but could be emphasized earlier.
*   **Feature Control Validation:** The empirical validation for the feature control task feels preliminary. The results in Figure 5 show it performs slightly worse than pixel control and seems to exhibit some instability. Given that the features being controlled are themselves changing during training, a brief discussion on the stability and convergence properties of this objective would be welcome.
*   **Off-Policy Learning Details:** The paper states that auxiliary control tasks are trained with Q-learning. However, since n-step returns are used, this is technically not standard Q-learning. Explicitly mentioning this nuance (e.g., as n-step off-policy value learning) would add precision.
*   **Training Duration:** In many of the learning curves (e.g., Figure 3), the UNREAL agent's performance is still trending upward at the end of training. It would be interesting to see how much further the performance improves with longer training runs.
*   **Atari Ablation Study:** The ablation studies are excellent for Labyrinth. A similar, perhaps smaller-scale, ablation on a subset of Atari games would strengthen the paper by showing whether the relative contributions of each auxiliary task generalize across domains.
*   **Minor Corrections:**
    *   Please check all figure references. For example, in Section 4.1.1, "Figure 3 (top left)" appears to be correct, but "Figure 3 Top Right" is ambiguous and seems to refer to the same plot. Please ensure all `left/right/top/bottom` annotations are precise.
    *   In Section 3.2, "the features discovered in this manner is shared" should be "are shared".
    *   Equation 2 references $L_{PC}$, which is defined just before the equation but not explicitly included in the summation itself. For clarity, consider writing out the full loss.
    *   In Figure 5's caption and text, there is a mismatch. The text mentions three Labyrinth levels, but the figure appears to show results for only two.
    *   In Appendix B, the sentence "Further details are included in the supplementary materials" is vague. Please specify where these materials can be found.

**Questions for the Authors:**
1.  **Computational Cost:** The auxiliary tasks add computational overhead. Could you quantify this cost? For instance, what is the wall-clock time slowdown per training step compared to the A3C baseline, and which auxiliary components are the most computationally expensive?
2.  **Hyperparameters for Auxiliary Losses:** The weights for the value replay ($\lambda_{VR}$) and reward prediction ($\lambda_{RP}$) losses were fixed at 1. Was this choice validated experimentally? Could these weights be treated as tunable hyperparameters, similar to $\lambda_{PC}$?
3.  **"Clip" in Dueling DQN:** The legend in Figure 3 (bottom) mentions "Dueling DQN (Clip)". Could you clarify what "Clip" refers to in this context?
