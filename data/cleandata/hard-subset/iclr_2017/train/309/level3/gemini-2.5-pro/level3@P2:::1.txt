### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), an agent that augments a deep reinforcement learning agent (A3C) with a set of auxiliary tasks to accelerate learning and improve final performance. The core idea is that by learning to predict and control various aspects of its sensorimotor stream, the agent can develop a richer and more useful representation of the environment, even in the absence of extrinsic rewards. The authors propose three main auxiliary tasks: 1) auxiliary control, where the agent learns off-policy value functions for maximizing pseudo-rewards such as changes in pixel intensity (pixel control) or hidden unit activations (feature control); 2) reward prediction, where the agent predicts the immediate next reward from a short sequence of observations, trained on a balanced set of rewarding and non-rewarding experiences; and 3) value function replay, where additional value function updates are performed on replayed data. The paper presents a novel architecture that effectively combines the on-policy A3C algorithm for the main task with off-policy learning for the auxiliary tasks using an experience replay buffer. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach with extensive experiments on a new, visually rich 3D environment called Labyrinth and on the 57-game Atari benchmark, showing significant improvements over the state-of-the-art in both final performance and data efficiency.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound method for improving deep reinforcement learning agents by incorporating a suite of unsupervised auxiliary tasks. The proposed UNREAL architecture is a novel and principled combination of on-policy and off-policy learning that effectively addresses the challenge of representation learning in RL, especially in sparse reward settings.
2.  The claims are supported by very strong and comprehensive empirical evidence. The UNREAL agent achieves state-of-the-art results on the Atari benchmark and drastically outperforms the A3C baseline on the challenging new 3D Labyrinth domain, demonstrating substantial gains in final performance, learning speed, and robustness to hyperparameters.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper is a solid conference contribution that provides a convincing proof-of-concept for the benefits of auxiliary tasks in deep RL.

The approach is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors correctly identify that the rich sensorimotor stream available to an agent contains much more information than the often-sparse extrinsic reward signal. Leveraging this information through auxiliary tasks to shape the agent's representation is a powerful idea. The paper does an excellent job of connecting its contributions to related work such as Horde, successor representations, and other uses of auxiliary prediction, while clearly distinguishing its "control" objective from prior "reconstruction" objectives.

The proposed method is technically sound. The combination of an on-policy actor-critic (A3C) for the main policy with off-policy Q-learning and experience replay for the auxiliary tasks is an elegant solution that allows for stable and data-efficient learning. The specific auxiliary tasks—pixel control, reward prediction with skewed replay, and value function replay—are intuitive and cleverly designed to improve the agent's representation in complementary ways.

The experimental validation is thorough and compelling. The introduction of the new Labyrinth environment, which features rich visuals and complex navigation/control challenges, is a valuable contribution in itself. The results on both Labyrinth and Atari are impressive, with the UNREAL agent achieving an average of 880% human performance on Atari and showing a 10x mean speedup on Labyrinth. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the positive contribution of each auxiliary component, and the comparison against pixel reconstruction further strengthens the case for the proposed auxiliary control tasks. The analysis of robustness to hyperparameters is also a welcome and important result. While the idea that relevant auxiliary tasks should help is not entirely surprising, the magnitude of the improvement shown here is significant and demonstrates the efficacy of the specific architecture and tasks proposed.

### Additional Feedback

The following points are meant to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   The UNREAL architecture is quite complex, integrating A3C with a replay buffer, several auxiliary heads (pixel control, reward prediction), and multiple loss terms with their own weighting hyperparameters (λVR, λPC, λRP). This complexity could be a barrier to adoption. It would be helpful to include a brief discussion on the sensitivity to these λ weights and any guidance on how to set them.
*   The paper clearly demonstrates a massive improvement in data efficiency (number of environment steps). It would also be valuable to understand the trade-offs in terms of computational cost. A short discussion of the wall-clock time and memory overhead of the UNREAL agent compared to the A3C baseline would be useful for researchers looking to implement or build upon this work.

### Questions for the Authors

*   Could you comment on the computational resources required to train the UNREAL agent compared to the A3C baseline? For instance, what is the approximate increase in wall-clock training time per million environment steps?
*   Given the complexity of the full agent, do you plan to release the source code to facilitate reproducibility and allow the community to build on this excellent work?
