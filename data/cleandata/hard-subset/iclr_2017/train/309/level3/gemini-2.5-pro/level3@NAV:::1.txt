This paper introduces the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, which augments the state-of-the-art A3C architecture with several unsupervised auxiliary tasks. The central hypothesis is that training an agent to simultaneously predict and control features of its sensory stream—in the absence of extrinsic rewards—will help it learn a richer and more versatile representation. The authors propose two main classes of auxiliary tasks: auxiliary control (learning policies to maximize pixel changes or hidden unit activations) and auxiliary reward prediction (predicting the onset of immediate reward). By combining these off-policy auxiliary tasks with experience replay and the on-policy A3C algorithm, the UNREAL agent achieves substantial performance gains on the challenging 3D Labyrinth environment and 57 Atari games.

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are its strong empirical results and its value as a comprehensive proof-of-concept. The paper provides a clear and powerful demonstration that thoughtfully chosen auxiliary tasks can dramatically accelerate learning and improve the final performance of deep reinforcement learning agents in complex domains.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly written, making it easy to follow for readers familiar with deep RL. The core strength lies in its extensive and convincing experimental validation. The UNREAL agent not only surpasses the A3C baseline but does so by a significant margin, achieving an average 10x learning speedup in Labyrinth and 880% of expert human performance on Atari. While the underlying idea—that relevant auxiliary tasks aid feature shaping—is not entirely surprising, this work serves as a crucial and well-executed validation of this principle at scale. The contribution is more empirical and architectural than theoretical, but it is a solid and valuable one for the field.

To further improve the paper, I would offer the following feedback and questions:

The overall UNREAL architecture is quite complex, combining an on-policy A3C agent with multiple off-policy auxiliary heads and an experience replay buffer. This complexity raises practical concerns for reproducibility and adoption.
1.  Could the authors provide information on the computational resources (e.g., CPU/GPU hours) required to train the UNREAL agent compared to the A3C baseline? Understanding the trade-off between performance gain and computational cost would be valuable.
2.  Given the implementation complexity, do the authors intend to release the source code for the agent and environments to facilitate further research?
