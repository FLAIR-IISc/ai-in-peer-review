### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), a novel agent architecture designed to improve the data efficiency and final performance of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core contribution is the augmentation of a standard A3C agent with a set of auxiliary tasks that are trained simultaneously with the main policy. These tasks are designed to facilitate the learning of a richer and more robust state representation, especially in environments with sparse rewards.

The authors propose two main classes of auxiliary tasks:
1.  **Auxiliary Control Tasks:** The agent learns off-policy value functions for maximizing pseudo-rewards. Two specific tasks are introduced: "pixel control," where the agent learns to maximize pixel changes in different regions of the input image, and "feature control," where it learns to maximize the activation of units in its own hidden layers. This compellingly reframes unsupervised learning from a passive prediction problem to an active control problem.
2.  **Auxiliary Reward Tasks:** To better handle sparse extrinsic rewards, the agent is trained on two additional prediction tasks using experience replay. First, a reward prediction task learns to predict the immediate next reward from a short sequence of observations, using a skewed sampling strategy that over-represents rewarding events. Second, a value function replay task provides additional updates for the main value function from replayed data.

The combined UNREAL agent is evaluated on the 57-game Atari suite and a challenging, visually rich 3D first-person environment called Labyrinth. The results are state-of-the-art, showing that UNREAL achieves an average of 880% expert human performance on Atari and 87% on Labyrinth. Critically, the paper also demonstrates a massive improvement in data efficiency, with the agent reaching the baseline's peak performance up to 10 times faster.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper with a novel and impactful contribution, supported by excellent empirical results. The key reasons for my decision are:

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Method:** The central idea of using auxiliary *control* tasks for representation learning is a significant conceptual advance over more common passive unsupervised objectives (like auto-encoding or frame prediction). The agent learns about the affordances of the environment by actively trying to control its sensory input and internal state, which is a powerful and intuitive learning signal.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance and Data Efficiency:** The UNREAL agent demonstrates substantial and convincing performance gains on two challenging and diverse benchmarks. The reported 10x speedup in learning is a major practical achievement that directly addresses the critical issue of sample inefficiency in deep RL. The ablation studies clearly show that each component of the proposed architecture contributes to the final performance.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper successfully answers the key questions outlined in the reviewing guidelines.

**1. What is the specific question/problem tackled by the paper?**
The paper tackles the problem of representation learning and data efficiency in deep RL. In many complex environments, particularly those with sparse rewards, agents struggle to learn meaningful features from the high-dimensional sensory stream. The paper proposes a method to provide dense, self-generated training signals to accelerate and enrich this representation learning process.

**2. Is the approach well motivated, including being well-placed in the literature?**
Yes, the approach is very well-motivated. The authors correctly identify the limitations of relying solely on sparse extrinsic rewards. They situate their work effectively with respect to related ideas like options, Horde, successor representations, and model-based learning. They clearly distinguish their contribution by highlighting that the auxiliary policies are used exclusively to shape the shared representation, not for hierarchical control or planning. The contrast drawn between passive prediction (e.g., reconstructing pixels) and active control is a key part of this motivation and is empirically supported by experiments showing that learning to control pixel changes is more effective than just predicting or reconstructing them.

**3. Does the paper support the claims?**
Yes, the claims are thoroughly supported by rigorous empirical evidence.
*   **Performance:** The agent achieves state-of-the-art results on both Atari and Labyrinth, significantly outperforming the strong A3C baseline.
*   **Data Efficiency:** The claim of a ~10x speedup is clearly demonstrated in learning curves, a crucial result for the field.
*   **Component Contributions:** An extensive ablation study (Figure 3) methodically demonstrates the positive impact of each auxiliary task (pixel control, reward prediction, value replay), justifying the complexity of the final UNREAL agent.
*   **Robustness:** The authors show that UNREAL is more robust to hyperparameter choices than the A3C baseline, which is an important practical advantage. The methodology of reporting results over multiple hyperparameter settings is sound and lends confidence to the conclusions.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Clarity on Auxiliary Task Implementation:** While the concepts are well-explained, key implementation details of the auxiliary tasks (e.g., the deconvolutional architecture for pixel control, the exact formulation of the `L_PC` loss) are in the appendix. The paper would be more self-contained and easier to understand if some of these core architectural details were summarized in the main text.
*   **Analysis of Learned Representations:** The evaluation focuses on extrinsic task performance, which is the ultimate goal. However, the paper's central claim is about learning better representations. This claim could be strengthened with more direct analysis. For instance, visualizing the learned pixel control policies or using dimensionality reduction techniques (like t-SNE) to compare the LSTM state representations of UNREAL and A3C agents could provide valuable insights into *why* the method works so well.

### 5. Questions for Authors

*   The pixel control task divides the screen into a 20x20 grid. Have you investigated the sensitivity of the agent's performance to this grid resolution? Is there a risk that the agent learns trivial policies to maximize change (e.g., rapidly shaking the camera), and if so, does the architecture have an implicit or explicit mechanism to prevent this?
*   The results for the feature control auxiliary task seem very promising ("only slightly worse than for pixel control" in a preliminary experiment). Since this approach is more general and avoids hand-crafting a perceptual objective, it seems highly valuable. Could you elaborate on why this was not more broadly tested or included in the final UNREAL agent? Were there challenges with stability or computational cost?
*   The UNREAL agent combines on-policy updates from A3C with off-policy updates from the replay buffer for the auxiliary tasks. Could you comment on the dynamics and stability of this hybrid training scheme? Did you observe any challenges from the potential mismatch between the current on-policy data distribution and the older, off-policy data in the replay buffer?
