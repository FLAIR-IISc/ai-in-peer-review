## Review of "Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning"

This paper presents a compelling approach to improving reinforcement learning by augmenting the standard reward maximization objective with a suite of auxiliary tasks designed to encourage the agent to learn richer, more controllable feature representations. The core idea – leveraging the agent’s ability to influence its environment to generate learning signals beyond the primary reward – is novel and intuitively appealing. The introduction of pixel and feature control tasks, alongside a supervised regression task for immediate reward prediction, demonstrates a thoughtful exploration of potential auxiliary objectives. The empirical results are strong, showcasing significant performance gains on both Labyrinth and Atari environments, including a substantial reduction in training iterations. The contrast drawn with passive unsupervised or model-based learning effectively positions this work as a unique contribution, focusing on active, controllable feature learning.

I recommend **Accept** this paper. The approach is both novel and demonstrably effective, addressing a key challenge in reinforcement learning – sample efficiency and robustness. The significant speedup in learning, particularly in complex environments like Labyrinth, is a particularly strong result.

My decision is primarily based on the clear positive results and the novelty of the approach. The authors successfully demonstrate that learning to control aspects of the environment, beyond simply maximizing reward, leads to more efficient and robust learning. The performance improvements on both Atari and Labyrinth are substantial and convincingly presented. Furthermore, the acknowledgement and handling of hyperparameter differences between A3C and UNREAL, along with the reporting of top-3 results, strengthens the validity of the findings.

However, while the results are impressive, the paper would benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the individual auxiliary tasks. While the authors acknowledge pixel control appears most impactful, a dedicated ablation study focusing *solely* on pixel control, with detailed metrics beyond overall RL performance (e.g., the variance of pixel changes achieved, the learned control policies themselves), would be valuable. It would also be helpful to understand *why* feature control is less impactful – is it the choice of features, the layer from which they are extracted, or something else? 

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   Could you elaborate on the choice of the 80x80 crop for pixel control? Was this determined empirically, and how sensitive are the results to the crop size?
*   Regarding the reward prediction task, what specific architectural choices were made for the feedforward network, and how were these choices justified?
*   You mention that the auxiliary tasks are performed every 20 environment steps. Was this frequency optimized, or is it a fixed parameter?
*   Could you provide more insight into the learned policies for the pixel control task? Are they interpretable, and do they align with intuitive strategies for changing pixel intensities?
