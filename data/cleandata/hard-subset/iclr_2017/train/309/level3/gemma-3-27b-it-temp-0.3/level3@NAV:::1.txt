## Review of "UNREAL: Reinforcement Learning with Auxiliary Control Tasks"

This paper presents UNREAL, an agent that augments the A3C deep reinforcement learning algorithm with unsupervised auxiliary tasks – both control and reward prediction – to improve learning speed and performance. The core idea of leveraging a wider range of training signals beyond the primary reward is well-motivated and intuitively appealing. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over A3C on both the challenging 3D Labyrinth environment and a suite of Atari games, achieving impressive results, including averaging 880% expert human performance on Atari. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the architecture and experimental setup, making it accessible to readers familiar with deep reinforcement learning.

I recommend **accepting** this paper for presentation at the conference. The results demonstrate a clear and substantial improvement over existing methods, and the approach offers a valuable contribution to the field of deep reinforcement learning. While the concept of auxiliary tasks isn’t entirely novel, the specific implementation and the scale of the performance gains are noteworthy. 

The strength of this work lies in its empirical validation. The authors provide compelling evidence that the proposed auxiliary tasks lead to faster learning and higher final performance across diverse environments. The ablation studies help to isolate the contributions of different components of the UNREAL agent. The robustness analysis further strengthens the claim that UNREAL is a more reliable approach. The inclusion of a video demonstrating the agent’s performance is also a valuable addition.

However, some details would benefit from further clarification. Specifically, I would like to know the **computational resources required to train UNREAL**. What hardware (number of GPUs, CPU, memory) and training time were needed for the Labyrinth and Atari experiments? This information is crucial for assessing the practicality of the approach. Furthermore, the overall architecture appears quite complex. I strongly encourage the authors to **release the source code** to facilitate reproducibility and further research. 

Finally, while the results are impressive, it would be helpful to understand *why* these auxiliary tasks are so effective. A more detailed analysis of the learned representations, perhaps through visualization or probing, could provide valuable insights.
