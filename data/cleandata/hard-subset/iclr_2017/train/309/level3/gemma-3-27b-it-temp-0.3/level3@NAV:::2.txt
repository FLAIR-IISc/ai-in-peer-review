## Review of "Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning"

This paper presents a compelling approach to improving data efficiency in deep reinforcement learning by incorporating domain-independent auxiliary tasks – control, reward prediction, and value replay – alongside the primary reinforcement learning objective. The core idea of sharing CNN+LSTM features across these tasks to develop a more robust and informative representation is well-motivated and aligns with recent trends in representation learning. The experimental results, demonstrating significant performance gains on both Atari and Labyrinth, are convincing and clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed UNREAL agent. The writing is generally excellent, making the paper easy to follow.

**Accept** – The paper presents a significant contribution to the field, with strong empirical validation and a clear, well-written presentation. The improvements in data efficiency and robustness are particularly noteworthy.

The strength of this work lies in its empirical success. The substantial performance improvements on both Atari and Labyrinth, particularly the 10x speedup in learning on Labyrinth, are impressive. The ablation studies provide some insight into the contributions of each auxiliary task, and the robustness analysis further strengthens the claims. However, several points require clarification and further investigation.

Firstly, the authors should quantify the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks. Specifically, reporting the training speed loss and identifying which auxiliary components are the most computationally expensive would be valuable. Secondly, the abstract and introduction should explicitly state that separate policies are learned for each auxiliary task, rather than a single policy optimizing all rewards. 

The feature-control auxiliary task lacks strong empirical validation. Figure 5 is unconvincing and raises concerns about stability and convergence, especially given the changing task during training. I recommend training the agents for a longer duration, as performance was still improving, and reporting the maximum performance achieved. The fixed weighting of the auxiliary tasks (lambda_* = 1) is also questionable; exploring these as hyperparameters would be beneficial. Furthermore, the authors should explicitly mention that the auxiliary tasks are trained off-policy and not with “true” Q-learning due to the use of multi-step empirical rewards. 

Minor issues include a correction needed in the presentation of L_pi (should be a minimization, not maximization), formatting errors in Eq. 1 and below it, unreferenced figures (2 and 1(d)), a grammatical error, an undefined variable in Eq. 2, clarification needed for the “Clip” term in the legend of Figure 3, and inconsistencies in figure references throughout the text. Finally, the caption of Figure 5 appears to be inaccurate regarding the number of Labyrinth levels shown. The location of supplementary materials should also be specified.
