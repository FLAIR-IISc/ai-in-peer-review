- Introduces UNREAL: an RL framework augmenting agents with auxiliary tasks to improve data efficiency, robustness, and performance.
- Proposes three auxiliary tasks: pixel intensity control, latent feature activation control (pseudo-control), and supervised reward prediction.
- Uses skewed sampling for reward prediction and trains auxiliary tasks off-policy with experience replay.
- Claims significant empirical gains: up to 10× fewer iterations to reach baseline and improved performance on 3D Labyrinth and Atari.
- Contrasts with unsupervised learning by leveraging agent control to focus on controllable, task-relevant features.
- Decision: Accept — novel contributions, strong empirical results, and well-motivated methodology.
- Novelty and impact: auxiliary control tasks present a new, compelling alternative to unsupervised feature learning.
- Empirical strength: extensive results showing substantial improvements in learning speed and performance across diverse environments.
- Methodology: sound integration of auxiliary tasks, experience replay, and skewed sampling addressing sparse rewards.
- Evaluation: robust experiments including ablations, hyperparameter analyses, and baseline comparisons.
- Literature placement: well-situated with contrasts to unsupervised learning, model-based RL, and related auxiliary-task work.
- Suggestion: provide deeper analysis of why pixel/feature control tasks are effective and their generalizability.
- Suggestion: clarify the auxiliary control loss term (LPC) earlier in the methodology for readability.
- Suggestion: expand experimental scope with additional environments to validate generality.
- Questions: how do auxiliary tasks interact, can they extend to continuous-action settings, and what is the computational overhead vs. observed speedup?
- Conclusion: novel, effective approach with substantial empirical gains and a solid foundation, warranting acceptance.
