### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), a novel deep reinforcement learning agent designed to improve data efficiency, final performance, and robustness. The core idea is to enrich the learning process of a primary agent by jointly training it on a set of auxiliary tasks. The authors build upon the powerful A3C agent and augment its on-policy training with three off-policy auxiliary tasks that share the agent's sensory-processing network layers.

The key contributions are:
1.  **Auxiliary Control Tasks:** The agent learns off-policy value functions to control sensory inputs, specifically maximizing pixel changes in different regions of the input image ("pixel control") or maximizing the activations of its own hidden units ("feature control"). This encourages the agent to learn about cause and effect in the environment.
2.  **Auxiliary Reward Prediction:** The agent is trained to predict the immediate upcoming reward from a short sequence of past observations. This task is trained using a rebalanced replay buffer to over-sample rare but important rewarding events, helping the representation focus on reward-relevant features.
3.  **Value Function Replay:** In addition to the on-policy value updates in A3C, the agent replays recent experiences to perform additional value function regression, accelerating the propagation of value information.

The authors provide an extensive empirical evaluation on two challenging domains: 57 Atari games and a new, visually rich 3D first-person environment called Labyrinth. The results are impressive, showing that UNREAL significantly outperforms the A3C baseline and achieves state-of-the-art performance on both benchmarks, reaching a mean human-normalized score of 880% on Atari and 87% on Labyrinth, with a remarkable 10x learning speedup on the latter.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper with a clear and significant contribution. The proposed UNREAL agent elegantly combines several well-motivated auxiliary tasks to address the critical challenges of data efficiency and representation learning in deep RL. The two key reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Novel and Effective Method:** The paper presents a compelling synthesis of on-policy learning for the main task and off-policy learning for a diverse set of auxiliary tasks. The idea of using reinforcement learning itself for the auxiliary objective (i.e., learning to *control* the environment's features rather than just passively predicting them) is a powerful concept that is well-supported by the results.
2.  **Extensive and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and the results are state-of-the-art. The authors demonstrate the agent's effectiveness not just on the standard Atari benchmark but also on the challenging and visually complex Labyrinth environment. The inclusion of detailed ablation studies, comparisons to alternative unsupervised methods (like reconstruction), and analysis of hyperparameter robustness provides strong evidence for the efficacy and practicality of the proposed components.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The UNREAL architecture is a thoughtful and technically sound extension of the A3C framework. By leveraging an experience replay buffer for the auxiliary tasks, the agent can learn efficiently from recent experience in an off-policy manner, without interfering with the stability of the on-policy A3C updates for the main task. This hybrid on-policy/off-policy approach is one of the paper's key strengths.

The choice of auxiliary tasks is well-motivated. Pixel control encourages the agent to learn about interactive elements in the environment, while reward prediction directly forces the network to learn features that are predictive of the extrinsic goal. The ablation study in Figure 3 clearly shows that each component (pixel control, reward prediction, and value replay) provides a significant and cumulative benefit to performance, confirming that they capture complementary aspects of the learning problem.

The empirical results are outstanding. On Labyrinth, UNREAL not only achieves much higher final scores than A3C (87% vs 54% mean human-normalized score) but also learns dramatically faster, reaching A3C's best performance with 10x fewer environment steps. This massive improvement in data efficiency is a crucial result. On Atari, the agent sets a new state-of-the-art, surpassing strong contemporary methods. The paper's claim that auxiliary tasks lead to more robust learning is also well-supported by the hyperparameter sensitivity analysis (Figure 3, right column), which is a valuable contribution for the community.

Finally, the comparison against more traditional unsupervised objectives like pixel reconstruction (Section 4.1.1) is particularly insightful. The finding that actively learning to control pixel changes is more beneficial than passively predicting them or reconstructing the input image provides strong evidence for the paper's core hypothesis and suggests a promising direction for future research in representation learning for RL.

### 4. Additional Feedback

The paper is well-written and the work is of high quality. The following suggestions are aimed at further strengthening the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Computational Overhead:** The UNREAL agent involves several additional network heads and loss computations. A brief discussion of the computational cost (e.g., wall-clock time per training step) compared to the A3C baseline would be helpful for practitioners to understand the trade-offs between sample efficiency and computational efficiency.
*   **Clarification on Feature Control:** The feature control task is presented as a compelling idea, but the results are described as "preliminary" and only shown for one environment. It would be beneficial to either integrate this task into the main UNREAL agent results or provide more context on why it was evaluated separately. For example, were there challenges in combining it with pixel control?
*   **Weighting of Auxiliary Losses:** The paper notes that the weight for the pixel control loss (λPC) was tuned separately for Atari and Labyrinth. It would be useful to elaborate on the sensitivity to this hyperparameter and provide more intuition on why different domains might require different weightings. This would help others apply the method to new environments.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points during the discussion period:

1.  The policies learned for the auxiliary control tasks are not used for action selection. This seems like a missed opportunity. Have you experimented with or do you have any thoughts on how these control policies could be leveraged more directly, for instance, as part of an intrinsic motivation scheme for exploration or as options in a hierarchical framework?
2.  The reward prediction task relies on a 50/50 rebalancing of rewarding and non-rewarding events. How sensitive is the performance of this component to this specific ratio? In environments with extremely sparse rewards, would a more aggressive over-sampling of rewarding events be even more beneficial?
3.  The paper compellingly shows that control is superior to prediction for auxiliary tasks. Could you expand on your intuition for why this is the case? Does learning to control necessitate a deeper understanding of the agent's influence on the environment, leading to a more causal representation compared to a purely correlational one learned via prediction?
