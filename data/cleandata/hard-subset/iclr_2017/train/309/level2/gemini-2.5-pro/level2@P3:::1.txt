**Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel and powerful deep reinforcement learning agent that significantly improves upon the state-of-the-art in terms of both final performance and data efficiency. The core contribution is the augmentation of a strong on-policy agent (A3C) with a set of auxiliary tasks that are learned off-policy from a replay buffer. These tasks are designed to build a richer and more useful state representation by leveraging the dense information stream available to the agent, beyond just the extrinsic reward signal.

The proposed auxiliary tasks fall into two main categories. First, auxiliary *control* tasks frame representation learning as a set of reinforcement learning problems where the agent learns policies to maximize pseudo-rewards, such as causing maximal change in pixel values (pixel control) or maximally activating its own internal feature units (feature control). This is contrasted with more common auxiliary prediction tasks. Second, auxiliary *reward* tasks are introduced to focus the representation on the extrinsic goal. These include predicting the immediate reward from recent history and replaying value function updates, with the reward prediction component using a skewed sampling strategy to focus on sparse rewarding events.

The complete agent, termed UNREAL, combines these components in a unified framework. The work provides extensive empirical validation on the 57-game Atari benchmark and a new, visually rich 3D first-person environment called Labyrinth. The results are impressive, showing that the UNREAL agent achieves an average of 880% expert human performance on Atari and 87% on Labyrinth, representing a major advance over previous methods. The paper also demonstrates a mean learning speedup of 10x and improved robustness to hyperparameters.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

Decision: **Accept**

1.  The paper presents a compelling and well-motivated framework for improving representation learning in deep RL. The introduction of auxiliary *control* tasks is a conceptually significant idea that is shown to be more effective than simple auxiliary prediction or reconstruction. This provides a new and effective way to leverage unsupervised signals in the environment.
2.  The claims are supported by exceptionally strong and thorough empirical results. The agent achieves state-of-the-art performance on two challenging and diverse domains. The inclusion of detailed ablation studies successfully demonstrates the contribution of each component, and the analysis of robustness to hyperparameters strengthens the practical value of the proposed method.

**Supporting Arguments**

The core strength of this work lies in its principled approach to data efficiency and representation learning. By combining an on-policy policy-gradient method for the main task with off-policy value-based methods for a suite of auxiliary tasks, the agent architecture makes effective use of each stream of experience. This hybrid on-policy/off-policy design is a valuable contribution in itself.

The formulation of unsupervised learning as a set of control problems is a key insight. The comparison in Section 4.1.1, showing that pixel control outperforms both pixel prediction and reconstruction, provides strong evidence for this hypothesis. This suggests that learning to influence the environment is a more powerful driver for representation learning than passively modeling it.

The experimental evaluation is exemplary. The introduction of the Labyrinth environment provides a new, challenging benchmark for the community, with more complex visuals and physics than many existing domains. The performance gains are not marginal; achieving more than double the performance of a strong A3C baseline on Labyrinth and setting a new state-of-the-art on Atari with an 880% mean score are significant accomplishments. The ablation studies in Figure 3 clearly justify the inclusion of each auxiliary component, showing that they are complementary and all contribute to the final performance.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The work is very comprehensive, but the following points could help further strengthen the paper and provide avenues for future work. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from the paper's overall contribution.

*   The interplay between the different auxiliary loss weights (λVR, λPC, λRP) is a crucial part of the algorithm. While the paper describes sampling them from a distribution for hyperparameter search, a brief sensitivity analysis or discussion on the relative importance and typical ranges of these weights would be insightful for practitioners seeking to apply this method.
*   The feature control task is introduced as a promising idea but is evaluated less thoroughly than pixel control. Expanding on the choice of layer for feature control and including it in the main ablation study would provide a more complete picture of its potential.
*   A brief qualitative analysis of the learned auxiliary policies could provide valuable intuition. For example, for pixel control, does the agent learn to move its viewpoint, or does it learn to interact with specific objects to cause visual change? Including a few illustrative examples in the appendix could complement the linked video.
*   The acronym "UNREAL" is used to describe the agent, but its full name (presumably Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) is not stated. Adding this would improve clarity.

**Clarifying Questions**

A few points could be clarified to aid understanding:

*   For the feature control task, the second hidden layer of the CNN was chosen. What was the rationale for this specific choice? It would be interesting to know if other layers were considered and how this architectural decision might affect the learned representation.
*   The reward prediction task uses a skewed replay distribution to emphasize rewarding events, while the value function replay does not. Could the paper elaborate on the reasoning behind this design choice? One might expect that prioritized sampling for value replay, as mentioned in the context of Schaul et al. (2015b), would also be beneficial.
*   The pixel control Q-values are generated by a deconvolutional network head. Was this architecture found to be critical for performance compared to a simpler, non-spatial MLP head? Understanding the importance of this specific architectural component would be useful.
