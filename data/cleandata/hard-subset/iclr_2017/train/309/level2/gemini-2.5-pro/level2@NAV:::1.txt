This paper introduces UNREAL, a deep reinforcement learning agent designed to improve data efficiency and overall performance by learning from a richer set of training signals than extrinsic reward alone. The core contribution is the augmentation of a strong on-policy agent (A3C) with a set of off-policy auxiliary tasks. These tasks include auxiliary control, where the agent learns to maximize pseudo-rewards like pixel changes or hidden unit activations, and auxiliary prediction, where it predicts the onset of immediate reward from a replay buffer. The authors demonstrate that this combination allows the agent's internal representation to develop continuously, leading to significant performance gains, faster learning, and improved hyperparameter robustness.

I strongly recommend to **Accept** this paper. The primary reasons are (1) the method addresses the critical and well-known challenge of data efficiency in deep RL, particularly in sparse reward settings, and (2) the claims are substantiated by an extensive and rigorous set of experiments across two distinct and challenging domains, Atari and the visually complex 3D Labyrinth environment.

The empirical evaluation is a key strength of this work. The authors not only show that the full UNREAL agent drastically outperforms the A3C baseline (achieving a 10x mean speedup) but also provide comprehensive ablation studies that effectively isolate the contribution of each auxiliary component. This analysis validates the design choices and provides valuable insight into why the agent works. Furthermore, demonstrating the method's superiority over alternative unsupervised approaches, such as pixel reconstruction, strengthens the claim that learning to *control* environmental features is a more effective strategy for representation learning than simply predicting them. The introduction and evaluation on the Labyrinth environment is also a welcome contribution, pushing the boundaries of DRL evaluation.

For potential improvement, a more detailed discussion on the interplay between the on-policy A3C updates and the off-policy auxiliary updates would be beneficial. While the results are compelling, the theoretical justification for why this specific hybrid approach is so effective could be further elaborated. Additionally, the selection of auxiliary tasks (pixel changes, feature activations) feels somewhat heuristic; an analysis of how different types of auxiliary tasks affect learning in different environments could add significant value.

I have a few questions for the authors:
1. What is the computational overhead (in terms of wall-clock time or FLOPs) of the auxiliary tasks relative to the A3C baseline?
2. How was the 20x20 grid resolution for pixel control chosen? Have you explored its sensitivity, and is there a trade-off between finer control and computational cost?
