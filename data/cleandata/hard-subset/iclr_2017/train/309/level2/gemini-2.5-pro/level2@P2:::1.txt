**Summary**

This paper introduces the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, a novel architecture designed to improve the data efficiency, performance, and robustness of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core idea is to supplement the standard extrinsic reward maximization objective of an A3C agent with a set of auxiliary tasks that encourage the development of a richer and more versatile representation of the environment. The authors propose three main auxiliary components: (1) Auxiliary control tasks, where the agent learns off-policy value functions for maximizing pseudo-rewards, such as changes in pixel intensity (pixel control) or activations of hidden units (feature control). (2) An auxiliary reward prediction task, where a feedforward network predicts the next immediate reward from a short sequence of observations, trained on a reward-balanced replay buffer to combat reward sparsity. (3) Value function replay, which uses the replay buffer to perform additional value function updates. By training these tasks jointly with the main A3C policy-gradient objective, the UNREAL agent achieves state-of-the-art results on the 57-game Atari suite, averaging 880% mean human performance. Furthermore, on a new, visually rich, 3D first-person environment called Labyrinth, the agent demonstrates a mean 10x speedup in learning compared to a strong A3C baseline and achieves 87% mean human performance.

**Decision: Accept**

This is an excellent paper that I strongly recommend for acceptance. My decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  The proposed UNREAL agent represents a significant and novel contribution to deep reinforcement learning. It intelligently combines on-policy learning for the main task with off-policy learning for a diverse set of auxiliary tasks, leading to a substantial advance in the state-of-the-art.
2.  The empirical evaluation is exceptionally thorough and convincing. The authors demonstrate the agent's effectiveness across two challenging and distinct domains (Atari and the new 3D Labyrinth environment), provide comprehensive ablation studies that validate the contribution of each component, and directly compare their control-based auxiliary objective against more traditional unsupervised prediction tasks.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strengths are numerous. The central hypothesis—that an agent can learn a more useful representation by learning to control its sensorimotor stream, rather than just passively predicting it—is compelling and well-motivated. The implementation of this idea through auxiliary control tasks is novel and contrasts clearly with prior work on options (which are used for temporal abstraction) and Horde (which learned separate value functions without a shared representation).

The experimental methodology is a model of rigor.
*   **Strong Baselines:** The use of A3C as the baseline is appropriate, as it was the state-of-the-art at the time of submission.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** Testing on both the standard Atari benchmark and the new, challenging Labyrinth environment demonstrates the generality of the approach. The Labyrinth environment itself is a valuable contribution, presenting perceptual and physical complexities beyond those in Atari.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The ablation results presented in Figure 3 are crucial. They clearly show that each of the proposed auxiliary tasks (pixel control, reward prediction, value replay) contributes positively to performance and that their combination yields the best results. This provides strong evidence that the overall performance gain is not due to a single trick but rather the synergistic effect of the entire architecture.
*   **Supporting the Core Hypothesis:** The experiment in Figure 5, which compares pixel control against pixel prediction and reconstruction, is a key result. It provides direct evidence for the paper's claim that learning to *control* features is a more effective auxiliary objective for RL than simply learning to *predict* or *reconstruct* them. This is a significant finding for the broader field of representation learning for control.

The reported results are a substantial leap forward. An average score of 880% on Atari is a remarkable improvement over previous methods. The 10x learning speedup on Labyrinth is perhaps even more important, as data efficiency is one of the most significant hurdles for the practical application of deep RL. The demonstrated improvement in hyperparameter robustness is also a highly valuable practical contribution. The paper is well-written, the architecture is clearly explained, and the claims are strongly supported by the evidence provided.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These are suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not critical for my decision.

*   **Computational Overhead:** The paper focuses on improvements in data efficiency (environment steps). A brief discussion of the computational cost (e.g., wall-clock time, GPU/CPU usage) compared to the A3C baseline would be helpful for readers to understand the trade-offs involved.
*   **Analysis of Feature Control:** The results for "feature control" are presented as preliminary. While promising, a more detailed analysis would be beneficial. For instance, which layer's features are most effective to control? How does performance vary with the choice of layer? How does feature control compare when combined with the other auxiliary tasks?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity (λPC):** The authors note that the weight for the pixel control loss (λPC) was set differently for Atari and Labyrinth. It would be valuable to include a short discussion on the sensitivity to this hyperparameter and any intuitions about how to set it for new environments. This would improve the reproducibility and applicability of the method.

**Questions for Authors**

I have a few questions that would help clarify my understanding:
1.  For the pixel control task, a 20x20 grid of 4x4 cells was used. What was the rationale behind this specific granularity? Have you experimented with coarser or finer grids, and how does performance change?
2.  The reward prediction task was formulated as a 3-class classification problem (positive, negative, zero). Was a regression-based objective also considered? If so, why was classification chosen? My intuition is that balancing classification is easier than handling the magnitude of rewards in regression, but I would appreciate your insight.
3.  The paper compellingly argues that control is a better auxiliary objective than prediction. Do you have a deeper intuition as to why this is the case? Could it be that the control objective forces the agent to learn longer-horizon dependencies and causal relationships in the environment, whereas immediate prediction does not?
