## Review of "Deep Reinforcement Learning with Auxiliary Control Tasks"

This paper introduces a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning by augmenting standard RL agents with auxiliary tasks designed to learn useful representations of the environment. The core idea – simultaneously learning policies for maximizing multiple pseudo-reward functions – is well-motivated, drawing parallels to how agents learn in the real world and addressing the challenge of sparse rewards. The authors convincingly argue that learning to control various features of the sensorimotor stream can lead to more robust and efficient learning, and demonstrate this with impressive results on both Atari and a challenging 3D Labyrinth environment. The reported 880% expert human performance on Atari and 10x speedup in Labyrinth are significant achievements.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is innovative and the experimental results are compelling, demonstrating a substantial improvement over existing state-of-the-art methods. The paper clearly tackles an important problem in RL – sample efficiency and robustness – and offers a promising solution.

The strength of this work lies in the thorough experimental validation. The authors not only demonstrate superior performance but also provide ablation studies to show the contribution of each auxiliary task. The comparison to unsupervised learning baselines (pixel reconstruction) further strengthens the argument for using reinforcement learning to drive representation learning. The detailed description of the Labyrinth environment and the extensive results across multiple levels are particularly valuable.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification in a few areas. While the intuition behind the auxiliary tasks is clear, a more detailed discussion of *why* specific tasks (e.g., pixel changes, network features) are effective would be helpful. Furthermore, the choice of weighting parameters (λVR, λPC, λRP) seems somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic exploration of these parameters, or a method for automatically tuning them, would be beneficial. Finally, the computational cost of training with multiple auxiliary tasks is not explicitly addressed.

I would be interested to know: 1) How sensitive are the results to the specific grid size used for pixel control? 2) Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the learned representations – for example, by visualizing the features activated by different auxiliary tasks? 3) What is the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks compared to a standard A3C agent?
