- Introduces UNREAL: augmenting deep RL with auxiliary control tasks and reward prediction to improve representations.  
- Core idea: train agents to maximize multiple pseudo-reward functions to learn more robust, generalizable sensorimotor representations.  
- Hypothesis and experiments: controlling aspects of the sensorimotor stream improves learning speed and final performance.  
- Empirical results: outperforms prior methods—880% expert human on Atari, ~10x speedup, and 87% human on Labyrinth.  
- Recommendation: reviewer recommends accepting the paper.  
- Significance: addresses important problems in RL—sample efficiency and generalization—with a compelling solution.  
- Positioning: well-motivated, builds on auxiliary tasks and representation learning, cites relevant prior work.  
- Experimental rigor: thorough setup with strong baselines and ablation studies to isolate component contributions.  
- Evidence strength: performance gains are strong, statistically significant, and consistent across environments and hyperparameters.  
- Practical impact: high practical usefulness due to improved sample efficiency and robustness for real-world deployment.  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of how and why different auxiliary tasks interact and which combinations are most effective.  
- Weakness: needs more granular ablation separating pixel-control versus feature-control contributions, and lacks analysis of computational overhead.  
- Questions/clarifications requested: criteria for selecting pseudo-reward functions and whether any were harmful.  
- Questions/clarifications requested: sensitivity of performance to loss-component weighting and any correlation between hyperparameter robustness and specific auxiliary tasks.
