This paper introduces a novel approach to reinforcement learning by simultaneously learning policies for maximizing multiple pseudo-reward functions, alongside the primary extrinsic reward. The core idea is to develop a richer agent representation by encouraging exploration and learning of various aspects of the environment, ultimately leading to faster learning and improved performance. The authors demonstrate strong results on both Atari games and a challenging 3D Labyrinth environment, surpassing state-of-the-art performance in both domains.

**Reject, with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit.** While the results are compelling, the paper lacks sufficient detail regarding the experimental setup and hyperparameter tuning to fully assess the reproducibility and generalizability of the findings. Furthermore, the novelty of the approach relative to existing work, particularly UVFA and successor representations, is not sufficiently emphasized.

The paper’s primary strength lies in the impressive empirical results. Achieving 880% expert human performance on Atari and substantial speedups in the Labyrinth environment clearly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The ablation studies in the Labyrinth setting provide some insight into the contribution of each auxiliary task, suggesting that combining them yields the best performance. The claim of improved robustness to hyperparameters is also significant and potentially valuable. 

However, several concerns prevent a positive assessment. First, the related work section, while covering relevant areas, doesn’t thoroughly differentiate the proposed approach from existing methods like UVFA and successor representations. The paper states that the auxiliary policies aren't directly used for planning, but this distinction feels superficial. UVFA, for example, also learns a factored representation of value functions for different pseudo-rewards, and the main difference appears to be in the implementation details rather than a fundamental conceptual shift. A more detailed comparison addressing this would strengthen the paper.

Second, the experimental details, particularly for the Atari results, are sparse. The paper mentions evaluating 50 random hyperparameter settings, but provides limited information on the search space, the computational resources used, and the criteria for selecting the final results. This lack of transparency makes it difficult to assess the robustness and reproducibility of the reported performance. A more thorough description of the hyperparameter tuning process is crucial.

Third, the ablation studies, while helpful, could be more comprehensive. For example, it would be beneficial to investigate the impact of different combinations of auxiliary tasks and their weighting factors. The authors mention the weighting factor λPC differed during experimentation – further explanation of *why* this was and its effect would be helpful.

Finally, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach. Are there specific types of environments where this method might not be effective? What are the potential challenges associated with scaling this approach to even more complex tasks?



**Additional Feedback & Questions:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Could the authors provide more detail on the hyperparameter search space used for both Labyrinth and Atari? Were any techniques like Bayesian optimization employed? A table summarizing the best hyperparameter settings for each environment would be highly valuable.
* **Comparison to Baselines:** While the results are compared to A3C, a more direct comparison to state-of-the-art algorithms like Prioritized DQN (on Atari) and other relevant methods would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** What is the computational overhead associated with training the additional auxiliary tasks? Does this significantly increase the training time or require more resources?
* **Generalization:** Do the learned representations transfer well to different variations of the same environment (e.g., different Labyrinth levels)?
* **Qualitative Analysis:** Providing some qualitative examples of the learned features or policies for the auxiliary tasks could offer further insight into how they contribute to overall performance. Are there any interesting emergent behaviors observed?
* **UVFA/Successor Representations:** How does the proposed approach differ *functionally* from UVFA and successor representations beyond the point that the learned policies are not used directly? Is there a key architectural or algorithmic difference that provides a significant advantage? Could a UVFA architecture be adapted to achieve similar results?
* **Weighting of Auxiliary Tasks:** How sensitive is the performance to the weighting of the auxiliary tasks (λVR, λPC, λRP)? A sensitivity analysis would provide valuable insights.
* **Choice of Auxiliary Tasks:** How were the specific auxiliary tasks (pixel changes, network features, reward prediction) chosen? Was there a systematic exploration of other potential auxiliary tasks?



In summary, this paper presents a promising approach to reinforcement learning with strong empirical results. However, addressing the concerns regarding reproducibility, novelty, and experimental detail is essential for a successful publication. I encourage the authors to revise and resubmit their work after carefully considering these points.
