This paper introduces a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the standard reward maximization objective with a suite of auxiliary tasks focused on predicting and controlling various features of the sensorimotor stream. The core idea – learning useful representations by simultaneously optimizing for multiple pseudo-rewards – is well-motivated and intuitively appealing, especially in environments with sparse extrinsic rewards. The authors demonstrate significant performance improvements on both Atari and a challenging 3D Labyrinth environment, achieving state-of-the-art results and substantial gains in learning speed.

**Reject.** While the results are impressive, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity in the experimental details and a somewhat superficial ablation study. The improvements demonstrated are significant, but the reasons *why* UNREAL performs so well, beyond a general claim of improved representation learning, remain somewhat opaque. This makes it difficult to assess the true contribution and potential impact of the work.

The primary concern lies in the presentation of the experimental setup. The description of the auxiliary tasks, particularly the pixel and feature control, is somewhat vague. For example, the deconvolutional network used to produce auxiliary values isn’t fully justified; it’s simply stated as exploiting the spatial nature of the task. A more detailed explanation of the architecture choices and their rationale is needed. Similarly, the weighting of different loss components (λVR, λPC, λRP) is described as being sampled from log-uniform distributions, but the specific ranges and motivations behind these choices aren't fully explained. Providing a more thorough analysis of how these hyperparameters impact performance would strengthen the paper.

The ablation study, while present, lacks depth. Simply demonstrating that adding each auxiliary task improves performance doesn't illuminate *which* tasks are most important, or how they interact. A more systematic ablation, perhaps involving combinations of tasks and varying weighting schemes, would provide a clearer understanding of the contribution of each component. The comparison with pixel reconstruction and input change prediction is a good start, but further investigation into why pixel control specifically outperforms these alternatives would be valuable. The claim that pixel reconstruction hurts final performance is interesting but requires more robust justification.

Furthermore, the robustness analysis, while presented in Figures 7 and 8, feels somewhat disconnected from the main body of the paper. It would be more impactful to integrate this discussion into the main results section, highlighting how the auxiliary tasks contribute to increased robustness. The notation used in the equations is also inconsistent (e.g., using both θ and θ-).

Despite these concerns, the core idea is strong and the results are promising. With further clarification and a more in-depth analysis, this work could be a significant contribution to the field.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Clarify the motivation for the LSTM.** The paper states that UNREAL builds on A3C, which uses an LSTM, but doesn't explicitly explain *why* an LSTM is necessary or advantageous in this context.
*   **Provide more details on the Labyrinth environment.** While the description is adequate, a more detailed explanation of the levels and the challenges they present would be helpful.
*   **Address the computational cost.** The addition of auxiliary tasks increases the computational burden. Discuss the practical implications of this increased cost.
*   **Consider alternative loss functions.** Exploring different loss functions for the reward prediction task (beyond the multiclass cross-entropy) could potentially further improve performance.
*   **Visualize the learned representations.** Providing visualizations of the learned representations (e.g., the activations of hidden units) would help to understand how the auxiliary tasks are shaping the agent's understanding of the environment.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What is the intuition behind using a deconvolutional network for producing auxiliary values in the pixel control task? Are there alternative architectures that were considered?
*   Can you provide a more detailed justification for the ranges chosen for the hyperparameters (learning rate, entropy cost, and the λ values)?
*   How do the different auxiliary tasks interact with each other? Is there any evidence of negative transfer between them?
*   What is the computational overhead of adding the auxiliary tasks, and how does it scale with the complexity of the environment?
*   Could you comment on the generalizability of this approach to other reinforcement learning domains beyond Atari and Labyrinth?
