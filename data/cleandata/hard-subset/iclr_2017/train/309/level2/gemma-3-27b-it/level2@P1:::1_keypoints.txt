- Introduces RL approach that learns policies for multiple pseudo-rewards alongside the extrinsic reward to enrich agent representations and boost exploration.
- Strong empirical results: 880% human expert on Atari and substantial speedups in 3D Labyrinth; ablations suggest combined auxiliaries perform best and claim hyperparameter robustness.
- Recommendation: Reject with encouragement to revise and resubmit due to gaps in presentation and analysis.
- Main critique: Novelty relative to prior work (UVFA, successor representations) is not convincingly differentiated; differences may be implementation-level rather than conceptual.
- Experimental details are sparse—Atari setup, hyperparameter search space, compute used, and selection criteria for reported results are under-specified.
- Reproducibility concern: claiming evaluation over 50 random hyperparameter settings without full transparency undermines robustness claims.
- Ablation analyses are insufficient—need more combinations of auxiliary tasks and clearer reporting on the role and effect of each.
- Weighting of auxiliaries (e.g., λPC) changed during experiments without explanation; impact of these choices is unclear.
- Paper lacks a clear discussion of limitations and environments where the method may not work or scalability challenges.
- Request for hyperparameter sensitivity: provide search ranges, tuning method (e.g., Bayesian optimization), and a table of best settings per environment.
- Need comparisons to stronger baselines (e.g., Prioritized DQN on Atari) beyond A3C to contextualize gains.
- Computational cost is unclear—report training overhead, time, and resource requirements from adding auxiliary tasks.
- Generalization: unclear whether learned representations transfer across environment variations (e.g., different Labyrinth levels).
- Qualitative analysis missing—show learned features, auxiliary policies, or emergent behaviors to illustrate how auxiliaries help.
- Clarify functional/architectural differences from UVFA and successor representations and whether those alternatives could achieve similar results.
- Explain choice of auxiliary tasks and whether other auxiliary objectives were systematically evaluated.
