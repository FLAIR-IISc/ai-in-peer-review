This paper introduces a novel approach to reinforcement learning by augmenting standard RL with auxiliary tasks designed to learn a more robust and generalizable representation of the environment. The core idea is to simultaneously learn policies for maximizing multiple pseudo-reward functions, alongside the primary task reward, fostering a richer understanding of the sensorimotor stream. The work demonstrates significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods on both Atari games and a challenging 3D Labyrinth environment, achieving impressive results in terms of performance and learning speed.

The decision is to **accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling approach, well-supported by empirical results, and addresses a relevant problem in reinforcement learning – improving sample efficiency and generalization. The performance gains on both Atari and Labyrinth are substantial and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

The strength of this work lies in its thorough exploration of auxiliary tasks and the clear demonstration of their benefits. The combination of control tasks (pixel and feature control) with reward prediction seems particularly effective. The experimental setup is comprehensive, including ablation studies that clearly illustrate the contribution of each component. The achieved performance improvements, both in absolute terms and in terms of learning speed, are significant and convincing. The analysis of robustness to hyperparameters is also a valuable contribution.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification in a few areas. First, the precise mechanisms by which the different auxiliary tasks interact and contribute to improved performance are not fully elucidated. While the results demonstrate a positive correlation, a more detailed analysis of the learned representations and their transferability would be beneficial. For example, visualizing the learned feature maps for different auxiliary tasks could provide insights into their individual roles. Second, the choice of specific auxiliary tasks (pixel changes, network features, reward prediction) appears somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic exploration of potential auxiliary tasks and a justification for the selected ones would strengthen the work. Third, the impact of the weighting factor λ on performance is not thoroughly investigated. A more detailed sensitivity analysis of this parameter would be valuable. Finally, while the paper mentions that the UNREAL agent outperforms previous methods, it would be helpful to provide a more direct comparison with recent state-of-the-art algorithms beyond A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN. For instance, comparing against distributional RL or other sample-efficient methods would strengthen the claims.

Specific questions for the authors:

1.  Could the authors provide examples of learned feature maps for different auxiliary tasks to illustrate how they capture different aspects of the environment?
2.  What criteria were used to select the specific auxiliary tasks explored in this paper (pixel changes, network features, reward prediction)? Were other potential auxiliary tasks considered and, if so, why were they not pursued?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of the UNREAL agent to the weighting factor λ? Could the authors provide a sensitivity analysis showing the impact of different values of λ on the results?
4.  How does the proposed method compare to more recent state-of-the-art RL algorithms beyond A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN, such as those based on distributional RL or other sample-efficient techniques?
5.  The paper describes the Labyrinth environment as comparable to VizDoom and Minecraft, but with richer visuals and more realistic physics. Could the authors clarify the specific differences and challenges presented by Labyrinth that make it a more demanding benchmark?
