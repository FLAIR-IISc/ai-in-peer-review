## Review of "Deep Reinforcement Learning with Auxiliary Control Tasks"

This paper introduces a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning (DRL) by augmenting standard RL with auxiliary control tasks and reward prediction. The core idea is to train agents to simultaneously maximize multiple pseudo-reward functions, fostering a more robust and generalizable representation of the environment. The authors hypothesize, and demonstrate through experiments, that learning to control various aspects of the sensorimotor stream improves learning speed and performance on both Atari games and a challenging 3D Labyrinth environment. The approach, termed UNREAL, significantly outperforms state-of-the-art methods, achieving impressive results – 880% expert human performance on Atari and an average 10x speedup in learning and 87% human performance on Labyrinth.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The research tackles a significant problem in RL - sample efficiency and generalization - and presents a compelling solution. The results are particularly impressive, demonstrating substantial improvements over existing baselines on challenging benchmarks.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon prior work in auxiliary tasks and representation learning. The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature, citing relevant publications. The experimental setup is thorough, with comparisons to strong baselines and ablations to understand the contribution of each component of the UNREAL architecture. The support for claims is strong; the reported performance gains are statistically significant and consistently observed across different environments and hyperparameter settings. The practical usefulness of this work is high, as improved sample efficiency and robustness are crucial for deploying DRL in real-world applications.

While the paper is strong overall, some aspects could be improved. The discussion of the interplay between the auxiliary tasks could be more detailed. Specifically, it would be helpful to understand *why* certain combinations of auxiliary tasks are more effective than others. Also, while the ablation studies are useful, a more granular analysis of the contributions of pixel control versus feature control would be beneficial. Finally, the computational cost of introducing these auxiliary tasks isn't explicitly addressed; a discussion of the overhead would be helpful.

I would appreciate clarification on the following: What criteria were used to select the specific pseudo-reward functions for the auxiliary control tasks? Were there any that proved ineffective or detrimental to performance? How sensitive is the performance to the weighting of the different loss components in the combined loss function? And, is there any observed correlation between the robustness to hyperparameters and the specific auxiliary tasks employed?
