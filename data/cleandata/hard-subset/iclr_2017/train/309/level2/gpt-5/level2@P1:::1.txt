Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with auxiliary reinforcement learning objectives that operate off-policy from recent experience. The two main families of auxiliary tasks are: (i) control tasks defined by pseudo-rewards (pixel-control over a spatial grid; feature-control via maximizing hidden-unit activations) optimized with n-step Q-learning from replay; and (ii) reward-focused auxiliary tasks (immediate reward prediction from short histories, trained on a skewed replay buffer) plus value function replay. All tasks share a common representation, with the aim of shaping features toward controllable and reward-relevant aspects of the environment.
- A combined loss integrates on-policy A3C with off-policy auxiliary objectives, enabling continued representation learning even when extrinsic rewards are sparse.
- Empirically, the agent substantially outperforms strong baselines: on 13 Labyrinth tasks it achieves large gains in both final performance and data efficiency (mean 10× fewer frames to A3C’s best), and on 57 Atari games it improves mean/median human-normalized scores over A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN, while also showing improved robustness to hyperparameters.
- Ablations in Labyrinth suggest auxiliary control is more beneficial than pixel reconstruction or immediate pixel-change prediction, supporting the premise that learning to control the perceptual stream yields more useful features than reconstruction.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons: (1) Strong, consistent empirical improvements in two challenging domains, with careful ablations and robustness analyses; (2) A clear, well-motivated integration of auxiliary control and reward-focused prediction within a unified on/off-policy framework that addresses sparse rewards and representation learning.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets the challenge of learning useful representations in environments with sparse or delayed extrinsic rewards. It argues that learning to predict and control aspects of the sensorimotor stream can shape representations better than unsupervised reconstruction, and shows how to focus these representations toward reward-relevant features. This is well placed with respect to Horde, UVFAs, successor representations, model-based prediction tasks, and prior A3C/DQN work.
- Methodological soundness: The use of off-policy Q-learning heads for many pseudo-rewards is appropriate and computationally efficient via replay. The design of pixel-control (spatial deconv head) leverages structure in visual inputs. Reward prediction trained on balanced reward/no-reward samples is thoughtfully decoupled from policy/value updates to avoid biasing the policy while still shaping shared features. Value function replay complements on-policy A3C updates.
- Empirical support: Results are strong on Labyrinth (final performance and dramatic data-efficiency gains) and competitive on a broad Atari suite. The paper evaluates robustness by sweeping learning rate and entropy cost and presents ablations showing contributions from each component (in Labyrinth) and an additional “UNREAL without pixel control” setting on Atari.
- Rigor and clarity: The implementation details (architectures, replay, losses, hyperparameter ranges) are described in sufficient detail to enable reproduction. The paper explicitly compares to relevant baselines and provides targeted ablations that test the central hypothesis (control vs prediction vs reconstruction).

Points tempering enthusiasm (do not undermine acceptance)
- Compute vs data efficiency: UNREAL performs extra gradient updates per environment step via replayed auxiliary losses. While the paper reports large gains in sample efficiency, it would be helpful to quantify compute cost (e.g., optimizer steps per frame, wall-clock) and show that gains persist when controlling for total updates.
- Ablations on Atari: The full component-wise ablation set (e.g., reward prediction only; value replay only; pixel control only; feature control only) is presented primarily for Labyrinth. An analogous breakdown on a subset of Atari games would strengthen the claim of generality.
- Evaluation protocol details on Atari: The paper should clearly specify training budget (frames), evaluation method (no-op or human starts; life-loss termination), and whether scores are averaged across seeds per hyperparameter, to facilitate comparison with prior work.

Additional feedback and questions for the authors (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Please report compute cost: wall-clock training time and number of parameter updates per frame for A3C vs UNREAL in both domains. If possible, include a control that gives A3C additional replay-based value regression (without auxiliary control) to isolate the effect of pixel/feature control from simply “more learning.”
- Atari evaluation protocol: Clarify the number of training frames per game, evaluation procedure (no-op starts vs fixed starts; life-loss as terminal), and whether human-normalized scores use the same protocol as the baselines you compare against. If the “top-3 hyperparameters” curves are shown, also provide the average over all 50 settings and median across seeds to avoid selection bias (you already provide some robustness figures; consolidating them would help).
- Stability of mixed on/off-policy training: Elaborate on stabilizing choices (e.g., target networks for auxiliary Q-heads, frequency of target updates, use of importance sampling or not) and whether sharing the LSTM between on- and off-policy losses caused instability. Any observed failure modes?
- Pixel-control head: You apply a ReLU to Qaux; since pixel-change rewards are non-negative, this seems reasonable, but did you observe value overestimation or learning slowdowns due to clipping? Any sensitivity to the grid size or crop region?
- Feature control: Results are preliminary on Labyrinth; could you expand with more tasks or provide intuition when feature control helps relative to pixel control? Did you try multi-layer feature control or sparsity constraints to avoid degenerate solutions?
- Weighting of auxiliary losses: Report sensitivity to λPC, λVR, λRP beyond the provided ranges. Did you consider adaptive weighting (e.g., based on learning progress or gradient magnitudes)?
- Replay buffer design: The 2k buffer is relatively small. Did larger buffers help or hurt? For reward prediction, did you try other balancing schemes (e.g., stratifying by reward magnitude/sign) or curriculum strategies?
- Generalization: Any evidence that representations learned via auxiliary control transfer across tasks (e.g., between different Labyrinth levels) without fine-tuning, or speed up fine-tuning on new rewards?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, clearly executed, and empirically strong paper. The auxiliary control/prediction framework is likely to be influential and useful to the community. The requested clarifications would further strengthen the work, but I recommend acceptance.
