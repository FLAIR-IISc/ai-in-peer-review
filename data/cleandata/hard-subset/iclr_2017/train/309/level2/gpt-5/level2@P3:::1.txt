Summary and contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an actor-critic agent that augments on-policy A3C with off-policy auxiliary tasks to learn a richer representation that continues to improve even when extrinsic rewards are sparse. The auxiliary suite includes: (1) control tasks that learn Q-functions to maximize pseudo-rewards (notably pixel-control on an n×n grid, and preliminary results on feature-control of network activations) using n-step Q-learning on replayed experience; (2) reward-focused auxiliary tasks (immediate reward prediction trained with rebalanced replay); and (3) value function replay to accelerate and stabilize value learning. A single shared CNN+LSTM representation serves the main policy/value and all auxiliary heads, with a combined loss that balances on-policy A3C and off-policy auxiliary objectives.

Empirically, UNREAL delivers substantial gains on two challenging benchmarks. On 13 Labyrinth levels, UNREAL attains 87% mean human-normalized score (vs. 54% for A3C), improves robustness to hyperparameters, and achieves a mean 10× reduction in steps required to reach the A3C best performance. On 57 Atari games, UNREAL achieves 880% mean and 250% median human-normalized performance, surpassing A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN. Ablations indicate that each auxiliary component helps, and that learning to control pixel changes outperforms predicting input change and frame reconstruction.

Decision: accept
Key reasons: (1) A clear, well-motivated approach that unifies on-policy RL with off-policy auxiliary control and reward-focused prediction to address sparse reward and representation learning; (2) strong, broad empirical evidence with careful ablations and robustness analyses across 3D Labyrinth and Atari; (3) methodological novelty relative to prior work (e.g., Horde, UVFA, model-based prediction) in using many learned control tasks solely as auxiliary objectives for representation shaping, combined with an explicit mechanism to focus on extrinsic rewards.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles efficient representation learning under sparse and delayed rewards. The auxiliary control formulation aligns training signals with controllable aspects of future experience, which is better matched to agent goals than reconstruction objectives. Reward prediction with rebalanced replay addresses perceptual sparsity of reward without biasing the policy/value estimates.
- Placement in the literature: The distinction from options/pseudo-rewards for temporal abstraction and Horde (separate value functions with distinct weights) is well drawn; UNREAL uses shared representation and trains auxiliary policies solely to shape that representation. The approach complements UVFAs/successor features by not requiring parametric conditioning on goals and by leveraging off-policy replay to efficiently train many control tasks from a single behavior stream. The contrasts with reconstruction-based unsupervised auxiliary tasks and recent 3D auxiliary predictions are supported by ablations.
- Empirical support: Results span diverse domains, with comprehensive ablations (adding each auxiliary component, comparisons to reconstruction and immediate change prediction), hyperparameter sweeps, and learning-speed comparisons. The methodological details (replay buffer design, rebalancing for reward prediction, deconvolutional heads for spatial control) are sufficiently specified to assess plausibility. The magnitude and consistency of gains, plus observed robustness improvements, support the claims.

Suggestions to improve the paper (non-blocking)
- Clarify the stability and interaction of on-policy A3C with off-policy Q-learning heads in a recurrent architecture. For example, discuss whether auxiliary heads backpropagate through LSTM state and how truncated BPTT and stale hidden states in replay are handled, and provide any stabilizing tricks (target networks, gradient clipping, loss scaling).
- Provide a more systematic sensitivity analysis of the loss weights (λPC, λRP, λVR), including how they were selected and how performance varies near chosen values. The Atari-specific tuning of λPC due to pixel-intensity normalization deserves a brief quantitative justification.
- Report compute and wall-clock training time versus A3C and Dueling DQN to complement sample-efficiency gains. This helps situate the practical cost of the richer training signal.
- Expand Atari ablations beyond “without pixel control,” e.g., reward prediction only, value replay only, and their combinations, to mirror the Labyrinth analysis.
- Discuss limitations: potential reliance of pixel control on visually dynamic environments, possible diminishing returns in games with minimal motion, and whether auxiliary control can distract from the main objective if weights are poorly chosen.
- Representation analysis: include simple probes (e.g., linear classifiers for reward-relevant states, t-SNE/CCA comparisons) to support the claim that auxiliary control learns features more aligned with long-term goals than reconstruction.
- Connection to transfer: since auxiliary control tasks learn policies and values for many pseudo-rewards, briefly comment on the feasibility of reusing these for transfer or exploration, even though UNREAL does not exploit them directly.

Questions for clarification
- ALE protocol: Were standard evaluation protocols followed (action repeat, no-op starts, training/evaluation frames, life termination handling)? Are comparisons to Dueling DQN aligned in training budget and evaluation settings?
- Human-normalized scoring: Please specify the exact human reference scores and evaluation episodes used for normalization on both benchmarks.
- Replay and recurrence: When sampling sequences for off-policy updates, how is the initial LSTM state set? Is there any state estimation auxiliary loss or state-reset heuristic to mitigate mismatch between replayed observations and hidden state?
- Reward rebalancing: What is the empirical effect size of the 50/50 rebalancing on reward prediction and downstream policy/value performance? Does this introduce distributional shift that harms stability in any settings?
- Auxiliary grid design: How sensitive is performance to the pixel-control grid size/crop and to the feature-control layer choice?
- Variance and seeds: Robustness plots are helpful; could final-score distributions across seeds (with fixed hyperparameters) be reported on a few representative tasks?
- Sparse-reward benchmarks: Were very sparse games (e.g., Montezuma’s Revenge) evaluated? If excluded, please comment on expectations and any observed failure modes.
- Off-policy target networks: For the Q-learning auxiliary heads, what target network update schedule was used, and how sensitive is learning to this choice?

Overall, the paper presents a well-motivated, carefully evaluated approach that materially advances deep RL performance and data efficiency through auxiliary control and reward-focused tasks. The contributions are significant and of clear interest to the ICLR community.
