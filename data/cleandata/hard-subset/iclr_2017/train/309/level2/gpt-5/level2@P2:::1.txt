1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with auxiliary tasks designed to shape representations and improve data efficiency and robustness. The key ideas are: (i) auxiliary control tasks that maximize pseudo-rewards via off-policy n-step Q-learning, with a focus on pixel-control (per-cell frame-change) and (preliminary) feature-control; (ii) reward-focused auxiliary prediction (predicting immediate reward with a skewed sampling that balances zero/non-zero rewards) to densify the supervision for reward-relevant features without directly biasing the policy/value updates; (iii) value function replay to exploit newly discovered features; and (iv) an integrated architecture sharing a convolutional encoder and LSTM, with deconvolutional heads for spatial control tasks. Empirically, UNREAL improves A3C substantially on DeepMind Labyrinth (mean 87% human-normalized vs 54% for A3C; ~10× fewer steps to reach A3C’s best scores) and achieves strong results on 57 Atari games (reported mean 880% and median 250% of human). Ablations indicate benefits from each auxiliary component, and comparisons against reconstruction and input-change prediction baselines support the central claim that control-based auxiliary tasks yield more useful representations than pure prediction/reconstruction.

2. Decision
Accept. Reasons: (1) Strong empirical gains with careful ablations demonstrate clear practical value; (2) The approach is well-motivated, simple to implement on top of existing on-policy methods, and makes a convincing case that auxiliary control and reward-focused prediction improve representation learning for RL.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets representation learning for RL in sparse/complex visual domains. The motivation to use many pseudo-reward control problems (pixel/feature control) is sound and well connected to prior work (Horde, UVFA, successor features), with a distinctive emphasis on using control tasks exclusively as auxiliary objectives (not as options) to shape shared features.
- Novelty and relation to literature: While auxiliary tasks and off-policy replay are known, the particular combination—off-policy control tasks with deconvolutional spatial heads, reward prediction with rebalanced replay to focus on extrinsic rewards, and value replay integrated with A3C—is a compelling, coherent design that substantially advances performance. The comparison to reconstruction and simple input-change prediction is especially helpful; it isolates the value of control-based auxiliary tasks.
- Empirical support and rigor:
  - Labyrinth: The improvements in both final scores and learning speed are large and consistent across 13 levels. The ablation analysis (each auxiliary component), robustness to hyperparameters, and comparisons against baselines are appropriate and substantiate the claims. The “unsupervised RL” experiments directly test the hypothesis that control tasks produce better features than reconstruction.
  - Atari: Results are strong, with UNREAL outperforming A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN in mean/median normalized scores. The inclusion of “UNREAL without pixel control” is useful for attribution.
- Methodological clarity: The loss decomposition and training protocol (on-policy A3C vs off-policy replayed updates) are clearly described; implementation details (architectures, buffer size, task weights, sampling) are sufficient for replication by a well-resourced group.

Potential weaknesses and concerns (not fatal):
- Evaluation protocol fairness on Atari: The paper evaluates many hyperparameter settings per game and reports curves for the top-3 per game, which may inflate comparisons against prior work that used a single setting or minimal per-game tuning. While the authors also present distributions across all settings, claims of “state-of-the-art” should be calibrated to this caveat.
- Reproducibility and environment access: Labyrinth/DeepMind Lab is nonstandard; without public access, external verification of Labyrinth results is hard (though Atari results mitigate this).
- Computational cost: The paper emphasizes sample efficiency (environment steps), but auxiliary losses add compute per step. Wall-clock training time vs A3C is not reported; it would help practitioners evaluate trade-offs.
- Clarify “feature control”: It appears as a promising auxiliary task with preliminary results on a subset of Labyrinth levels; it is unclear if feature control is included in the final UNREAL experiments across all domains.
- Comparability of Atari settings: The agent uses RGB inputs (84×84) rather than the grayscale commonly used in ALE; clarify if reward clipping, evaluation with no-op/human starts, and other evaluation conventions match prior work to ensure comparability.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Report wall-clock speedups alongside sample efficiency. Include a breakdown of the extra computation (reward prediction network, deconv heads) and its impact on training time.
- Standardize the Atari evaluation protocol to match common baselines (clarify no-op starts/human starts, episode termination on life loss, reward clipping, frame-stacking, evaluation episodes). Consider reporting performance with a single global hyperparameter setting across games to complement the per-game tuning analysis.
- Expand Atari ablations: show the incremental effect of each auxiliary component (reward prediction, value replay, pixel control) separately on a representative subset of games.
- Clarify the role of feature control: Is it part of the final UNREAL agent? If not, present it as a separate exploratory result and discuss when it helps relative to pixel control.
- Analyze the interaction between reward-prediction oversampling and policy learning more explicitly (e.g., feature drift, interference). A small study measuring the effect of different reward-balancing ratios could be informative.
- Sensitivity to replay buffer size and recency: Given the small buffer (2k), provide sensitivity plots to buffer size and prioritization choices.
- Interpretation: The qualitative video is helpful; consider adding saliency/attention maps or probing of the learned representation to support the claim that auxiliary tasks focus features on reward-relevant aspects.

Questions for the authors
- On Atari, how many total environment frames were used for training and evaluation per game? What was the evaluation protocol (no-op/human starts, number of episodes, reward clipping, life-loss termination)?
- How does wall-clock training time compare to A3C for matching score levels on Labyrinth and Atari?
- Are the per-game hyperparameters selected using training validation on that game, and how does performance change if a single setting is used across all games?
- What is the exact composition of the final UNREAL agent in the reported Atari and Labyrinth results (pixel control only, or also feature control)?
- How sensitive are results to the reward prediction rebalancing ratio (0.5 non-zero vs zero)? Does a different ratio work better in denser/sparser reward games?
- Did pixel control ever conflict with optimal task behavior (e.g., tasks that require stillness)? If so, how was this mitigated?
- Can the auxiliary control tasks be disentangled to provide reusable behaviors (e.g., options), or are they strictly used for representation shaping in this work?
