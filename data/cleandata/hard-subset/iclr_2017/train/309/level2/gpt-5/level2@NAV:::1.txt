Summary and contributions: The paper proposes UNREAL, an auxiliary-task-augmented deep RL agent that learns many pseudo-reward control policies (pixel/feature control) and reward-focused predictions alongside a base A3C policy, all sharing a common representation. A skewed reward-prediction objective focuses features on reward-relevant signals, and off-policy replay enables efficient training of auxiliary objectives. Claimed contributions are: (1) a unified framework for auxiliary control and reward-prediction tasks that continue learning without extrinsic reward, (2) a mechanism to focus representations on extrinsic rewards via rebalanced reward prediction and value replay, and (3) state-of-the-art results with major gains in data efficiency and robustness on Labyrinth and Atari.

Decision: Accept. Key reasons: (1) Strong, broadly convincing empirical gains across two challenging domains with ablations that support the central claims; (2) A well-motivated and novel integration of auxiliary control and reward-focused prediction that is clearly positioned in the literature and practically useful.

Supporting arguments:
- Question/problem: How to learn richer representations for sparse/partial reward settings by exploiting abundant sensorimotor signals beyond extrinsic reward.
- Motivation/literature: Solid coverage and positioning relative to Horde, UVFA, successor representations, model prediction, prioritized replay, dueling networks, and A3C; the choice to use pseudo-reward control only as auxiliary training is a clear differentiator.
- Empirical support: On 13 Labyrinth levels, UNREAL improves final human-normalized mean from 54% to 87%, with ~10× speedups; on 57 Atari games, mean 880% and median 250% of expert human. Ablations (pixel vs prediction vs reconstruction; value replay; reward prediction) substantiate where gains originate.
- Usefulness: The approach is practical for improving data efficiency and hyperparameter robustness, especially in sparse-reward environments.
- Completeness: Architecture and training details are substantial (losses, λ ranges, replay design, heads). While code is not provided, the appendix gives many reproduction details.
- Limitations: Statistical significance reporting is absent; “mean 880%” may be skewed by outliers; compute budget and training-frame parity vs baselines need clearer guarantees.

Additional feedback (to help improve, not central to the decision):
- Provide per-game training frames, wall-clock, and compute budgets; ensure fair comparison to Dueling DDQN (frames, sticky actions).
- Report variance over seeds and significance tests; include median and interquartile metrics more prominently.
- Extend ablations on feature control and analyze representation (e.g., probing tasks).
- Discuss failure cases and the effect of λPC/λRP; add sensitivity plots beyond hyperparameters already shown.

Questions for the authors:
- How are “expert human” baselines defined across games; how many participants and trials?
- Are Atari evaluations using sticky actions and standardized no-op starts?
- What is the exact training-frame budget per method; were top-3 hyperparameters selected post hoc on test games?
- How sensitive is performance to the pixel grid size and to λPC across games?
- Does reward rebalancing (P(r≠0)=0.5) harm calibration or induce representation bias; have you tried alternative reweightings?
- What is the wall-clock and compute overhead of auxiliary tasks relative to A3C?
