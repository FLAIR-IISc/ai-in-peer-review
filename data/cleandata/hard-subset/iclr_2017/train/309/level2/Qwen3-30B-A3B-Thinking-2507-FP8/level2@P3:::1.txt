

The paper introduces UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that learns multiple auxiliary policies to control various aspects of the sensorimotor stream (pixel changes and network features) while also predicting reward onsets. These auxiliary tasks share a common representation with the main policy, enabling the agent to develop a more effective representation for the environment. The approach is motivated by the hypothesis that learning to control features—rather than simply reconstructing them—better aligns with long-term goals, as demonstrated through the analogy of a baby learning to maximize "redness" through various behaviors. The paper demonstrates UNREAL's effectiveness through extensive experiments on Labyrinth (a challenging 3D environment) and Atari games, showing significant improvements in both final performance and learning speed.

Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution by demonstrating that auxiliary control tasks combined with reward prediction substantially improve data efficiency and robustness in reinforcement learning, as evidenced by comprehensive empirical results across multiple challenging domains.

The claim is well-supported by rigorous experiments. On Labyrinth, UNREAL achieves 87% human-normalized performance (vs. 54% for A3C) across 13 diverse levels, with a 10× speedup in learning. The ablation studies confirm each auxiliary component contributes meaningfully: pixel control provides the largest boost, feature control offers substantial gains, and reward prediction enhances robustness. On Atari, UNREAL achieves 880% mean human performance (vs. previous state-of-the-art), with significantly improved hyperparameter robustness. The comparison to unsupervised baselines (reconstruction vs. prediction vs. control) provides compelling evidence that the proposed approach is more effective than prior methods for representation learning. The experiments are well-designed, with thorough evaluation across multiple hyperparameter settings and levels.

To further strengthen the paper, consider addressing the following points: (1) Provide a more detailed analysis of why feature control is slightly less effective than pixel control across Labyrinth levels, as the current explanation lacks depth; (2) Clarify the rationale for using a 3-class classification for reward prediction rather than regression, and discuss how this choice impacts performance; (3) Expand the ablation of feature control to include more Labyrinth levels beyond the single example presented; (4) Discuss potential biological plausibility connections beyond the introductory baby analogy, particularly regarding how auxiliary control tasks might relate to developmental learning in animals.

The paper's literature placement is strong, with clear differentiation from related work on Horde, UVFA, successor representations, and auxiliary prediction tasks. The motivation for using control rather than prediction/reconstruction is well-justified through both theoretical reasoning and empirical results. The UNREAL architecture effectively integrates auxiliary tasks with A3C, using experience replay to balance on-policy and off-policy learning while maintaining data efficiency.

The results are scientifically rigorous, with proper statistical evaluation across multiple environments, hyperparameters, and random seeds. The 10× learning speedup on Labyrinth and 880% Atari performance represent significant advances over prior work. The paper avoids overstating claims, with results presented transparently and comparisons to strong baselines.

The proposed approach is practical and could influence future work in reinforcement learning representation learning. The mechanisms for sharing representations between auxiliary tasks and the main policy are well-integrated, and the experience replay modifications for skewed sampling of reward events are elegantly implemented. The paper successfully demonstrates that learning to control features of the sensorimotor stream provides a more effective learning signal than traditional unsupervised methods.
