

This paper introduces UNREAL, a novel reinforcement learning agent that learns to maximize multiple pseudo-reward functions simultaneously while sharing a common representation. The authors propose an elegant framework combining auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) with reward prediction, enabling the agent to develop a more robust and efficient representation. The key claim is that this approach significantly improves learning speed, data efficiency, and robustness compared to previous methods.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field with well-supported claims. The experimental results on both Atari (880% human-normalized performance) and Labyrinth (87% human-normalized performance with 10× faster learning) are compelling and statistically significant. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the contribution of each component, and the thorough evaluation across multiple hyperparameter settings provides strong evidence for the robustness claims.

The theoretical foundation is sound, with thoughtful connections to related work on Horde, UVFAs, and successor representations. The architecture is well-motivated, and the paper clearly explains how the auxiliary tasks complement rather than replace the primary reward maximization objective. The implementation details provided in the appendix are sufficiently comprehensive for reproducibility.

For improvement, I suggest the authors clarify the relationship between their reward prediction task and standard value function learning. While the paper distinguishes between reward prediction and value learning, the connection to standard reinforcement learning theory could be more explicitly articulated. Additionally, the paper mentions that feature control was "only slightly worse than pixel control" but doesn't provide quantitative comparisons on the Labyrinth levels where feature control was tested. Including this data would strengthen the claims about the relative effectiveness of different auxiliary tasks.

I would also appreciate clarification on whether the auxiliary tasks are equally beneficial across all environments or if their effectiveness varies significantly depending on the task structure. This would help readers understand the broader applicability of the approach.

The paper's contributions are significant, well-executed, and supported by rigorous experiments. The results represent a clear advancement over previous state-of-the-art, and the methodological insights could prove valuable for future research in reinforcement learning.
