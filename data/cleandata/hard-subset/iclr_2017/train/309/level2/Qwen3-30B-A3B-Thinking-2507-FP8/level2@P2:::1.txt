

# Review of "Unreal: An Agent That Learns to Control Its Future Experiences"

## Summary
The paper introduces UNREAL, an agent that learns to predict and control multiple features of the sensorimotor stream by simultaneously maximizing many pseudo-reward functions through reinforcement learning. The authors propose that this approach develops more useful representations than traditional unsupervised learning (which typically reconstructs inputs), as it requires understanding how to influence features rather than just predicting them. The UNREAL architecture combines auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) with reward prediction and experience replay to improve learning efficiency and performance. The paper demonstrates significant improvements over A3C on both Atari (880% mean human-normalized performance) and Labyrinth (87% mean human-normalized performance), with a 10× speedup in learning.

## Decision
Accept with major revisions. The paper presents a compelling approach with strong empirical results, but requires clarification on several critical points to fully substantiate its claims.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution is well-motivated by the limitations of both traditional reinforcement learning (which relies on sparse extrinsic rewards) and unsupervised learning (which typically reconstructs inputs without learning to control them). The Labyrinth results are particularly impressive, showing a 10× speedup in learning and 87% human-normalized performance across 13 levels. The ablation studies provide convincing evidence for the necessity of each component (auxiliary control tasks, reward prediction, and experience replay).

However, the paper has several critical weaknesses that need addressing. First, the claim of "880% expert human performance" on Atari is highly unusual and lacks sufficient explanation. Standard human-normalized performance for Atari typically ranges from 100% (equal to human) to maybe 200-300% for the best agents. The paper should clarify exactly how human performance was measured and normalized, as this is fundamental to assessing the claim.

Second, the paper doesn't sufficiently establish what the previous state-of-the-art was on Labyrinth. The authors claim to "significantly outperform the previous state-of-the-art" but only compare to A3C. Given that Labyrinth is a new environment, it's unclear what prior work exists for comparison. The paper should provide a more thorough literature review and comparison to relevant prior work on similar 3D environments.

Third, the theoretical justification for why controlling features is better than predicting them is somewhat superficial. The baby example is intuitive but doesn't provide a rigorous basis for why this approach should generalize to complex environments. The paper would benefit from a more thorough theoretical analysis of why this representation learning approach might lead to better generalization.

## Additional Feedback
1. Clarify the human-normalized performance metric for Atari. Explain exactly how the 880% was calculated and provide context for how this compares to other Atari results in the literature.

2. Expand the related work section to include more recent work on auxiliary tasks in reinforcement learning, particularly the work by Lample & Chaplot (2016) and Mirowski et al. (2016) that the authors cite but don't sufficiently contrast with their approach.

3. Provide more detailed analysis of the computational cost of UNREAL compared to A3C. The paper mentions the 10× speedup in learning, but doesn't discuss whether UNREAL requires more compute per step or more total compute.

4. The paper claims "natural and artificial agents live in a stream of sensorimotor data" but doesn't clearly articulate how this relates to their specific approach. This could be strengthened with a more precise theoretical connection.

5. The ablation study in Figure 5 shows that "learning to control pixel changes is better than simply predicting immediate pixel changes," but doesn't provide sufficient explanation for why this is the case. The paper should include more analysis on how the control versus prediction distinction affects learning.

## Questions for Authors
1. How was human performance normalized for the Atari results? Specifically, what is the baseline human score used to calculate the 880% performance, and how does this compare to standard Atari performance metrics?

2. What was the previous state-of-the-art performance on Labyrinth before this work? The paper claims to outperform the previous state-of-the-art but only compares to A3C. Please provide a more thorough comparison to relevant prior work on similar environments.

3. Why did the authors choose to use Q-learning for the auxiliary tasks rather than policy gradient methods? The paper mentions that "any reinforcement learning method could be applied," but doesn't justify this specific choice.

4. The paper states that "learning to control pixel changes is indeed better than simply predicting immediate pixel changes," but the explanation in Figure 5 is somewhat superficial. Could the authors provide more detailed analysis on why this distinction matters for learning more useful representations?
