

Summary: This paper introduces the UNREAL agent, which enhances deep reinforcement learning by simultaneously learning multiple auxiliary policies for maximizing pseudo-reward functions (pixel control and feature control) in addition to the main extrinsic reward. The authors propose a novel reward prediction auxiliary task to focus the representation on relevant aspects of the environment. The paper demonstrates significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods on both Atari (880% human performance) and Labyrinth (87% human performance) benchmarks, with substantial gains in learning speed (10x faster) and robustness to hyperparameters.

Decision: Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to reinforcement learning with a well-motivated approach that is well-placed in the literature and supported by comprehensive experiments.

Supporting arguments: The paper's core contribution is well-motivated by the observation that reinforcement learning agents could benefit from learning to control various aspects of the environment, not just maximizing extrinsic rewards. The authors provide a thoughtful comparison with related work (Horde, UVFA, successor representation) and clearly articulate how their approach differs. The empirical results are compelling and well-structured, with thorough ablation studies demonstrating the value of each component (pixel control, feature control, reward prediction). The results on Labyrinth are particularly impressive, showing not just higher final performance but also significantly faster learning (10x speedup) and greater robustness to hyperparameter settings. The implementation details are sufficiently complete for reproducibility.

Additional feedback: The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how the auxiliary tasks specifically contribute to the agent's ability to generalize to new tasks. While the paper demonstrates performance improvements, it would strengthen the argument if the authors could provide evidence that the learned representations are indeed more useful for downstream tasks. For instance, could they show that the representation learned by UNREAL transfers better to new environments or tasks than representations from standard RL methods? 

The paper mentions that "learning to reconstruct only led to faster initial learning and actually made the final scores worse when compared to vanilla A3C," but it would be helpful to have more detailed analysis of why this is the case. Specifically, what aspects of the reconstruction loss lead to poor final performance compared to the control-based auxiliary tasks? A more in-depth discussion of the failure modes of reconstruction-based approaches would strengthen the paper.

The reward prediction task is a novel component that seems to contribute significantly to the results. The authors might consider elaborating on how the reward prediction task specifically shapes the representation to focus on relevant aspects of the environment, and how this differs from simply using the reward as a direct signal. It would be valuable to see some visualizations of how the representation changes when the reward prediction task is included versus when it's not.

Questions for authors:
1. The paper claims that "learning to control pixel changes is better than simply predicting immediate pixel changes." Could you provide more specific analysis of why the control-based approach works better than prediction? For instance, could you show the performance of the reward prediction task when trained to predict pixel changes versus when trained to control them?
2. In the Labyrinth experiments, the UNREAL agent achieved 116% human-normalized performance on lt hallway slope. Given that this exceeds human performance, could you elaborate on whether this indicates the agent has discovered a better strategy than humans, or if there might be some evaluation artifact that led to this result?
3. The paper states that "the representation learned by UNREAL is more robust to hyperparameter settings." Could you provide more insight into why this might be the case? Is it because the auxiliary tasks provide more consistent learning signals across different hyperparameter configurations, or is there another explanation?
