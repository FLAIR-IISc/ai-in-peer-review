- Introduces UNREAL agent that learns auxiliary control policies (pixel/feature control) plus a reward-prediction task alongside extrinsic reward.  
- Demonstrates large empirical gains: Atari (880% human), Labyrinth (87% human), ~10× faster learning and improved hyperparameter robustness.  
- Decision: Accept — strong contribution, well-motivated, well-placed in literature, and supported by comprehensive experiments.  
- Core idea motivated by learning to control environment aspects rather than only maximizing extrinsic reward.  
- Clear comparison to related work (Horde, UVFA, successor representation) and distinction of the approach.  
- Ablation studies show pixel control, feature control, and reward prediction each contribute meaningfully.  
- Labyrinth results particularly impressive: higher final performance, much faster learning, and greater robustness.  
- Implementation details are sufficiently complete for reproducibility.  
- Request clearer evidence/explanation that auxiliary tasks improve generalization and transfer to new tasks/environments.  
- Suggest experiments showing UNREAL-learned representations transfer better than standard RL representations.  
- Notes that reconstruction auxiliary helped early learning but hurt final performance; asks for deeper analysis of why reconstruction fails relative to control tasks.  
- Praises reward-prediction auxiliary as novel and impactful; requests elaboration and visualization of how it shapes representations vs. no reward prediction.  
- Question 1: Provide analysis comparing control-based pixel objectives versus pixel-prediction objectives to explain why control works better.  
- Question 2: Explain Labyrinth lt hallway slope result (116% human)—whether agent found a better strategy or an evaluation artifact.  
- Question 3: Clarify why UNREAL representations are more robust to hyperparameter changes—are auxiliary tasks providing more consistent learning signals or is there another reason?
