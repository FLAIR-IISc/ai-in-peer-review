This paper proposes augmenting neural architectures with recursion to improve generalization in neural program synthesis. The authors apply this concept to the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework on four tasks – grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort – demonstrating superior generalization and, crucially, a formal verification of perfect generalization. This is a compelling contribution, representing a significant step towards more robust and interpretable neural programs.

**Decision:** Accept with minor revisions.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of incorporating recursion is well-motivated and addresses a critical limitation of existing neural program synthesis approaches – poor generalization. The authors convincingly argue that recursion reduces complexity and facilitates reasoning about program behavior, ultimately enabling provable guarantees. The experimental results strongly support this claim. The observed improvements in generalization, particularly for bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, are impressive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

The most significant strength of this work is the formal verification of perfect generalization. This is a novel and highly valuable contribution, as it provides a rigorous guarantee of program correctness, something sorely lacking in the field. The detailed description of the verification procedure, along with the discussion of base cases and reduction rules, adds significant credibility to this claim. The authors correctly highlight the importance of recursion in making this verification tractable.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the concepts and experimental setup. The inclusion of topological sort and quicksort, in addition to the tasks from the original NPI paper, strengthens the generalizability of the findings. The analysis of why non-recursive approaches fail – learning spurious dependencies – is insightful.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong overall, a few areas could be improved.

*   **Clarify the training procedure:** The description of how the recursive traces are generated could be more detailed. While the authors mention modifying the training traces, providing concrete examples or pseudocode illustrating the process would be beneficial.
*   **Discussion of scalability:** The verification procedure, while impressive, might not scale well to more complex tasks with large state spaces. The authors acknowledge this but could expand on potential solutions or future research directions to address this limitation. Specifically, the comment about needing to cover infinite base cases for motor control is a point that could be expanded on.
*   **Ablation study on recursion depth:** For tasks like bubble sort, the authors explored partial recursion. A more comprehensive ablation study, systematically varying the level of recursion, could provide further insights into the optimal approach.
*   **Comparison to other generalization techniques:** While the authors position recursion as a key solution, a brief discussion of how it compares to other commonly used techniques like curriculum learning, data augmentation, or regularization would strengthen the argument.
*  **Environment encoding**: The description of the environment encoding for topological sort is somewhat dense. Consider expanding on why this encoding was chosen and how it facilitates learning.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed pseudocode example illustrating the process of generating the recursive training traces, particularly for the topological sort and quicksort tasks?
2.  Regarding scalability of the verification procedure, what are your thoughts on utilizing techniques like abstract interpretation or model checking to verify more complex programs?
3.  In the bubble sort experiments, were there any specific hyperparameter changes required when switching from non-recursive to fully recursive training?
4.  The paper states that the non-recursive NPI learns spurious dependencies. Could you elaborate on what specific types of dependencies were observed during training and how recursion mitigates them?
5.  Regarding the verification set, what criteria were used to determine the size and composition of the set, particularly for the tasks where it was generated randomly (topological sort and quicksort)?
