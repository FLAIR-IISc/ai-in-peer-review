## Review of MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION

**1. Summary of Claims**

This paper tackles the problem of poor generalization in neural networks designed to learn programs from data. The authors argue that incorporating recursion into neural architectures is crucial for improving generalization and interpretability, especially with limited training data. They demonstrate this by augmenting the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework with recursion and applying it to four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  The key contribution lies in showing that recursion enables provable guarantees of perfect generalization, a significant advance over previous approaches. The authors provide empirical evidence of superior performance and discuss the theoretical benefits of recursion for reasoning about program behavior.

**2. Decision**

**Accept.** The paper presents a compelling argument for the importance of recursion in neural program synthesis.  The empirical results are strong, demonstrating improved generalization across multiple tasks, and the theoretical contribution of enabling provable guarantees is significant. The clear presentation and connection to existing work (specifically the NPI framework) make the paper accessible and impactful. 

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The core motivation for this work is well-placed. Existing neural program synthesis methods suffer from a lack of robustness and difficulty in reasoning about their behavior, particularly when faced with inputs beyond those seen during training.  The authors correctly identify that this is often due to the networks learning spurious correlations rather than true program semantics. 

The proposed solution – augmenting architectures with recursion – is elegant and intuitively sound.  Recursion is a fundamental concept in programming that naturally promotes modularity and reduces complexity. The paper convincingly demonstrates how recursion can be implemented within the NPI framework.

The empirical results are a key strength. Across all four tasks, the recursive NPI models exhibit superior generalization compared to non-recursive approaches.  The results on the topological sort and quicksort tasks are particularly noteworthy, as these represent more complex algorithmic challenges.  

Most importantly, the ability to provide *provable* guarantees of generalization is a major advancement. This is a crucial step towards building trustworthy and reliable neural program synthesis systems. While the verification process itself is complex, the authors clearly outline the approach and demonstrate its effectiveness.

**4. Additional Feedback**

This is a strong paper, and the following feedback is intended to further enhance its impact.

*   **Generalization Proofs:** While the authors demonstrate *how* to verify generalization, the discussion of the proofs themselves could be more detailed. Providing a more concrete example of the verification process for one of the tasks (e.g., addition) with a simplified input set would be helpful.
*   **Scalability of Verification:** The authors acknowledge that the verification process may be intractable for tasks with very large or continuous input spaces. Discussing potential strategies for addressing this limitation (e.g., sampling-based verification, abstraction techniques) would be valuable.
*   **Source Code Release:** Given the complexity of the implementation and the potential for others to build upon this work, the authors should consider releasing their source code. This would greatly enhance the reproducibility and impact of the paper.
*   **Limitations:** It would be helpful to discuss the limitations of the approach more explicitly. For example, are there certain types of programs or tasks where recursion is less effective? What are the challenges of applying this approach to architectures other than NPI?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter choices would strengthen the paper.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a more detailed example of the verification process, perhaps for a simplified addition problem, illustrating how the base cases and reduction rules are checked?
*   What strategies are you considering for scaling the verification process to tasks with larger or continuous input spaces?
*   Do you plan to release the source code for your implementation?
*   Are there specific types of programs or tasks where you anticipate recursion being less effective? If so, can you explain why?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the learning rate and LSTM hidden state size?
