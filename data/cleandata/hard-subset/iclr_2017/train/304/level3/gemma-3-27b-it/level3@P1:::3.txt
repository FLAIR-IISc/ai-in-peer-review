## Review of MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION

**1. Summary of Claims**

This paper tackles the problem of poor generalization in neural networks learning programs from data. The authors argue that incorporating recursion into neural architectures is crucial for robust generalization and interpretability. They implement recursion within the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework on four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  Their experiments demonstrate significantly improved generalization performance, requiring far less training data than previous approaches. Notably, they provide a formal verification procedure, proving perfect generalization for the learned recursive programs given a complete verification set. This is a significant contribution, representing the first instance of provable generalization guarantees for neural programs.

**2. Decision**

**Accept.** The paper presents a compelling argument for the benefits of recursion in neural program learning, backed by strong empirical results and a novel, rigorous verification method. The demonstrated improvement in generalization and the ability to formally prove correctness are substantial contributions to the field.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The core strength of this paper lies in its insightful connection between recursion as a fundamental programming concept and its benefit in neural program synthesis. The authors convincingly argue that recursion addresses the limitations of existing neural program induction techniques, which often struggle with spurious correlations and lack interpretability. 

The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear advantage of the recursive approach across all four tasks. The authors thoughtfully address the challenges of generalizing to longer inputs, a common failing of previous methods, and show that their recursive models achieve perfect accuracy on significantly larger problem instances.  The addition of topological sort and quicksort provides valuable diversity in the experimental evaluation, going beyond the tasks presented in the original NPI paper. 

The most impactful contribution, however, is the formal verification procedure. The authors provide a clear and logical explanation of how recursion facilitates provable generalization, reducing the complexity of the proof to a manageable set of base cases and reduction rules. This level of rigor is unprecedented in this area of research and sets a new standard for evaluating neural program synthesis models. The detailed description of how the verification set is constructed is valuable and demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying semantics.

**4. Additional Feedback**

While the paper is strong, here are some suggestions for improvement:

* **Ablation Studies:** While the main result is impressive, more detailed ablation studies could further isolate the benefits of recursion. For example, a comparison between partially recursive models (as presented in the bubble sort experiment) and fully recursive models could offer more insights into the importance of different recursive aspects.
* **Scalability of Verification:** The paper acknowledges the limitations of the verification procedure for tasks with extremely large input domains. Exploring alternative verification techniques for such cases, even briefly, would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper could benefit from a discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity. How robust are the results to variations in learning rate, LSTM hidden state size, or other key hyperparameters?
* **Code Availability:** Making the code publicly available would greatly facilitate reproducibility and encourage further research in this area.
* **Comparison to Alternative Approaches:**  A brief discussion of how this approach compares to other techniques aimed at improving generalization in neural program synthesis (e.g., curriculum learning, data augmentation) would provide helpful context.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*  The paper states the training traces are constructed to explicitly contain recursive elements. Could you elaborate on the process of generating these traces and the criteria used to ensure they promote learning of recursive behavior?  Is it possible to automatically discover these traces or do they require substantial manual design?
*  The verification procedure relies on a correctly constructed verification set (V).  What mechanisms are in place to ensure the completeness of V, particularly for more complex tasks where manually identifying all base cases and reductions may be challenging?
*  You mention that the choice of domain-specific encoder influences the verification process. Could you discuss the tradeoffs between different encoder designs and their impact on the ease of verification?
*  The quicksort implementation seems more complex than the other tasks. Does this added complexity affect the training speed or require special considerations during the verification process?



Overall, this is a well-written, insightful, and technically sound paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neural program synthesis. The demonstrated improvements in generalization and the novel verification method are highly commendable.
