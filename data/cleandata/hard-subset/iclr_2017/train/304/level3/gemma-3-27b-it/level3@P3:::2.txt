This paper presents an interesting and accessible extension to Neural Programmer-Interpreters (NPIs) via the incorporation of recursion. The authors convincingly argue that recursion is a key abstraction for improving generalization and enabling provable guarantees in neural program learning. The experimental results on grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach, with the recursive NPI programs achieving perfect generalization on tasks where non-recursive models struggle. The reuse of notation from the Reed & de Freitas (2016) NPI paper aids readability, particularly for those familiar with that work.

**Accept** – This paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating a simple yet effective modification to a neural programming architecture that significantly improves generalization and allows for formal verification. The clear presentation and compelling results justify acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core strength of this paper lies in the insight that recursion addresses a fundamental limitation of previous neural program learning approaches: the difficulty of generalizing to inputs beyond the training complexity. By decomposing problems into smaller, self-similar subproblems, recursion reduces the complexity of the learning task and facilitates the discovery of robust program semantics. The authors’ demonstration of provable generalization is particularly noteworthy. This is a significant step towards building more reliable and trustworthy AI systems. The empirical results consistently show that the recursive NPI models outperform their non-recursive counterparts, particularly as input complexity increases. The thorough exploration of different tasks – from simple addition to more complex sorting algorithms – strengthens the claim that recursion is a broadly applicable technique.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

While the paper is strong, certain aspects could benefit from further elaboration. The discussion of handling continuous input spaces, such as those encountered in robotics (e.g., 3D car control), feels somewhat dismissive. While the authors acknowledge the intractability of current verification methods in such scenarios, a more detailed discussion of potential approaches—even if speculative—would be valuable. For example, could approximate verification techniques or sampling-based methods be employed? 

The generalization proofs, while impressive for the tested tasks, are described as "vague". More detail on how the verification sets were constructed and how they guarantee generalization would be helpful. Specifically, providing examples of the "base cases and reduction rules" for each task would enhance understanding. While Appendix A.6 attempts to address this, greater integration into the main body of the paper would be beneficial.

The authors repeatedly mention their intention to release the source code. This is crucial for reproducibility and further research in this area. A firm commitment to release, along with a timeline, would significantly strengthen the paper.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the limitations of verification in continuous input spaces: Are there any initial ideas or ongoing investigations into adapting the verification framework to handle such scenarios, even if it involves approximations or sampling techniques?
2.  Could you provide a more concrete example of a base case and a reduction rule for the topological sort task, and explain how verifying these components leads to a guarantee of perfect generalization?
3.  What is the anticipated timeline for releasing the source code for this project?
4.  The paper states that training sets are relatively small. Were any hyperparameter search or ablation studies performed to ensure robustness to variations in the training data, or is the performance highly sensitive to the specific examples chosen?
5.  The non-recursive bubble sort performance degraded around length 8. Is this a consistent finding across multiple random seeds, or is it a somewhat arbitrary result related to the specific training data?
