- Main claim: Introducing recursion into Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architectures significantly improves neural program generalization.  
- Empirical finding: Recursion-augmented NPI shows strong performance gains on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, generalizing to much larger inputs.  
- Theoretical contribution: The paper provides a formal proof of perfect generalization for the learned recursive programs.  
- Argument: Recursion helps avoid spurious correlations by decomposing problems into smaller subproblems, enabling provable guarantees.  
- Verification: The authors present a verification procedure based on base cases and reduction rules; a relatively small verification set can suffice.  
- Decision: Reviewer recommends acceptance.  
- Reproducibility request: Provide more detailed NPI Keras implementation details and ideally release code.  
- Ablation/sensitivity request: Discuss sensitivity to hyperparameters (learning rate, LSTM size, etc.).  
- Comparison request: Include a direct comparison between recursion and curriculum learning (or combine both).  
- Limitation noted: Verification procedure may not scale to tasks with very large input domains; suggest discussing approaches to mitigate this.  
- Clarification request: Further explain general principles for constructing recursive execution traces, especially for complex tasks like quicksort.  
- Question: What is the computational cost of the verification procedure as task complexity and verification set size grow?  
- Question: How might one train recursive neural programs without explicit execution traces—any preliminary ideas or directions?  
- Question: Why was NPI chosen and can recursion be integrated into other neural program induction architectures—what challenges arise?  
- Question: For bubble sort, does modifying the domain-specific encoder to focus on value comparisons affect learning performance or generalization across diverse data types?
