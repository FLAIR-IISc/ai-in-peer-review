## Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

This paper investigates the crucial role of recursion in enabling strong generalization and data efficiency for neural program synthesis. The authors build upon the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework, demonstrating that training with execution traces containing recursive calls allows the NPI to perfectly learn algorithms like Bubblesort and Tower of Hanoi from limited data, and importantly, provides a pathway to provable generalization. The core idea – modifying training data to explicitly include recursion – is remarkably simple yet appears highly effective.

I recommend **Reject** for now, with encouragement to resubmit after addressing key concerns. While the empirical results are impressive on the tested tasks, the paper doesn’t sufficiently convince me that this success demonstrates a fundamental breakthrough in neural program induction, or that the problems solved are representative of more challenging scenarios.

My primary concern stems from the nature of the tasks themselves and the availability of full execution traces. The authors acknowledge this, but it’s not adequately addressed. Providing complete execution traces arguably trivializes the problem, decoupling perception from program logic. The network essentially learns to *mimic* the trace rather than inferring the underlying program. In this setup, the task becomes less about program synthesis and more about trace reconstruction. This is particularly concerning given the perfect generalization results – a level of performance rarely seen in neural program learning. The paper doesn't offer convincing evidence that the learned programs would generalize to situations where the execution traces aren't directly available, or if the environment isn't perfectly defined. 

Furthermore, the experiments, while well-executed, are limited in scope. The paper highlights the importance of recursion for divide-and-conquer algorithms, but doesn't explore more complex problems where recursion alone might not be sufficient, or where the trace information is noisy or incomplete. The provided verification procedure is a strong contribution, but its applicability is limited to these carefully constructed, trace-available scenarios.

To strengthen the paper, I suggest the following:

*   **Ablation study:**  Systematically remove components of the recursive trace (e.g., the recursive calls themselves) to quantify the contribution of recursion to the final performance.
*   **Comparison to non-recursive baselines:** Provide a more thorough comparison against strong non-recursive baselines trained with comparable data and resources.
*   **Discussion of limitations:**  Expand the discussion on the limitations of trace-based learning and explore potential avenues for addressing them (e.g., incorporating reward shaping or curriculum learning).
*   **Explore more complex tasks:** Test the approach on tasks with larger state spaces, more complex logic, and/or partial observability.

I have several clarifying questions for the authors:

*   How sensitive is performance to the quality and completeness of the execution traces? Would the models still generalize with noisy or incomplete traces?
*   What happens when the problem domain is altered slightly? For example, for quicksort, would the model generalize to a different comparison operator?
*   What types of problems *still* pose a challenge for this approach, even with access to full execution traces? What are the inherent limitations of this method?
*   Could you elaborate on how the verification set size was determined, and how confident are you that it comprehensively covers all relevant base cases and reduction rules?
