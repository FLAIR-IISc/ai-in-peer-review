- Proposes incorporating recursion into neural programming (NPI) via modified training traces to improve generalization and interpretability.  
- Core idea is simple, well-motivated, and logically integrated with the NPI framework.  
- Training with recursive traces enables learning recursive programs that achieve strong generalization.  
- Empirical results are strong: perfect generalization on addition, improved sorting, and successful extensions to topological sort and quicksort.  
- Significant contribution: enables formal verification of program correctness by checking base cases and reduction rules.  
- Major concern: heavy reliance on full execution traces effectively decouples perception from program logic.  
- Scalability is questionable: detailed traces may be impractical or unavailable for complex problems.  
- Unclear how the method handles perceptual inputs (e.g., images) or tasks where traces exist but inference still fails.  
- Paper should include a more thorough discussion of limitations and avenues to reduce reliance on traces.  
- Additional ablation studies beyond partial vs full recursion would clarify the importance of components.  
- Need for discussion of computational cost of training recursive models vs non-recursive baselines.  
- Clarify the “provable generalization” wording: verification depends on the completeness of the verification set V.  
- Recommend concrete examples of spurious correlations learned by non-recursive NPI and how recursion avoids them.  
- Questions for authors: cases where traces exist but method fails; scaling to perceptual inputs; sensitivity of verification to V’s completeness; plans for learning recursion without explicit traces.  
- Recommendation: Accept with moderate confidence.
