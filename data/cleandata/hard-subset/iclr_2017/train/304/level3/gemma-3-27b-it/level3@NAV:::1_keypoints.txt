- Paper studies the role of recursion in enabling generalization and data efficiency for neural program synthesis (NPI framework).  
- Core method: modify training execution traces to explicitly include recursive calls.  
- Empirical claim: training with recursive traces yields near-perfect learning for Bubblesort and Tower of Hanoi from limited data.  
- Claim of provable generalization enabled by the recursive trace training.  
- Key strength: idea is simple yet appears highly effective on tested tasks.  
- Recommendation: Reject for now, with encouragement to resubmit after addressing concerns.  
- Main concern: availability of full execution traces may trivialize the task, causing the network to mimic traces rather than infer underlying programs.  
- Doubt that results demonstrate a fundamental breakthrough or generalize to scenarios without perfect trace availability or well-defined environments.  
- Experimental scope is limited; tested tasks may not represent more challenging or realistic problems.  
- Verification procedure is a strong contribution but its applicability is limited to carefully constructed, trace-available scenarios.  
- Suggested ablation: remove components of the recursive trace (e.g., recursive calls) to quantify recursion’s contribution.  
- Suggested baselines: more thorough comparison to strong non-recursive baselines trained with comparable data/resources.  
- Suggested discussion: expand on limitations of trace-based learning and potential remedies (e.g., reward shaping, curriculum learning).  
- Suggested extension: evaluate on more complex tasks with larger state spaces, complex logic, or partial observability.  
- Question: How sensitive is performance to the quality and completeness of execution traces (noisy/incomplete traces)?  
- Question: How well does the model generalize under domain changes (e.g., different comparison operator for quicksort)?  
- Question: What problem types remain challenging for this approach even with full traces; what are inherent limitations?  
- Question: How was the verification set size determined and how confident are authors that it covers all relevant base cases and reduction rules?
