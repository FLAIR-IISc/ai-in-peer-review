## Review of "Making Neural Programming Architectures Generalize via Recursion"

This paper tackles the critical problem of poor generalization in neural program synthesis. The authors propose augmenting neural architectures with recursion, demonstrating that training on recursive traces significantly improves generalization performance, and – crucially – allows for formal verification of correctness. They apply this approach to the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework on four tasks: addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, adding the latter two based on reviewer feedback. The core contribution is a demonstrably successful integration of recursion into a neural program learning architecture, coupled with a formal proof of perfect generalization – a first in this field.

**Decision: Accept** with enthusiasm. This paper presents a significant advance in neural program induction, addressing a core limitation of existing methods. The improvement in generalization performance, combined with the ability to *prove* correctness, is a compelling and important result. The addition of topological sort and quicksort demonstrably strengthens the paper, responding to previous concerns about the scope of experimentation.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The authors clearly articulate the problem of generalization in neural program learning and convincingly motivate the use of recursion as a solution. Their approach is well-placed within the existing literature, building upon the NPI framework and addressing its limitations. The empirical results are strong, consistently showing improved performance with the recursive approach across all four tasks. The theoretical result – a formal proof of perfect generalization – is particularly noteworthy and sets this work apart. The detailed description of the verification procedure and the thoroughness of the base case/reduction rule analysis inspire confidence in the claims. The experiments are well-designed, and the ablation experiments (comparing non-recursive, partially recursive, and fully recursive versions) offer valuable insight into the importance of full recursion.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, the detailed implementation aspects could benefit from further clarification. Specifically, providing the code for the NPI implementation used in the experiments would greatly enhance reproducibility. While the authors acknowledge the difficulty of verification with very large input domains, exploring potential strategies for scaling the verification procedure (e.g., sampling techniques, abstraction) would be a valuable direction for future work. Furthermore, a discussion on the computational cost of training recursive vs. non-recursive models would be useful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detail on the specific hyperparameters used during training for each task? Was hyperparameter tuning performed, and if so, what strategies were employed?
2.  The paper mentions the possibility of training recursive neural programs without explicit training execution traces. Could you elaborate on potential approaches to achieve this, beyond what is already mentioned in the conclusion?
3.  In the context of scaling the verification procedure, have you considered using techniques like symbolic execution or abstract interpretation to reduce the search space of possible inputs?
4. The modification to the domain-specific encoder for bubble sort seems to simplify the task substantially. Is there a risk this simplification impacts the generalizability of the findings to more complex programming tasks?
