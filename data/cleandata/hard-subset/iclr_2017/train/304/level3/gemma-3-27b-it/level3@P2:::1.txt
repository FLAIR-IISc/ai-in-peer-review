This paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures to improve generalization and interpretability, demonstrating this with the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) on sorting and addition tasks. The core idea is elegantly simple: modify training traces to explicitly include recursive calls, enabling the NPI to learn recursive programs that achieve perfect generalization and allow for formal verification.

**Accept** with a moderate confidence level. The paper tackles an important problem – the poor generalization of neural program synthesis models – and proposes a compelling and novel solution. The experimental results are strong, showing significant improvements in generalization and, crucially, enabling provable correctness. However, some concerns regarding the scope of applicability and the role of execution traces temper the enthusiasm.

The paper clearly identifies the problem of generalization in neural program learning. Existing approaches often struggle with inputs exceeding the complexity of those seen during training. The authors argue this stems from the models learning spurious correlations rather than true program semantics. Their solution – explicitly incorporating recursion – is well-motivated, drawing on the importance of recursion in classical programming. The connection to the NPI framework is logical, and the explanation of how recursion can be implemented within that architecture is clear. The idea of verifying program correctness by checking base cases and reduction rules is also a significant contribution, offering a path towards building more trustworthy AI systems.

The empirical results are impressive. The authors demonstrate that NPI, when trained on recursively formulated traces, can perfectly generalize to longer inputs in addition and correctly sort arrays of varying lengths, tasks where the original NPI struggled. The verification procedure confirms the perfect generalization claim, a strong result. The extension to topological sort and quicksort further strengthens the argument that recursion is a powerful tool for neural program synthesis. The analysis of partial versus full recursion in bubble sort is insightful.

However, the reliance on full execution traces raises concerns. As the reviewer notes, this approach effectively decouples perception from program logic. The model isn’t *learning* to interpret the input; it’s being *told* how to process it via the provided trace. While this is a valid approach, it’s unclear how well it will scale to more complex problems where obtaining such detailed traces is impractical or impossible. The reviewer’s question about problems where execution traces *are* available but the method still fails is critical. This limits the broader applicability of the proposed solution. For example, the experiments seem to focus on well-defined, symbolic inputs. How would this approach fare with more complex, perceptual inputs (e.g., sorting images of digits)?

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Discussion of limitations:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly the reliance on execution traces. Acknowledging this limitation and outlining potential avenues for addressing it (e.g., exploring methods for learning recursive abstractions without explicit traces) would strengthen the paper.
*   **Ablation studies:** While the comparison between partial and full recursion is helpful, further ablation studies could provide deeper insights into the importance of different components of the recursive formulation.
*   **Computational cost:** A brief discussion of the computational cost of training the recursive models compared to non-recursive models would be valuable.
*   **Clarify the “provable generalization” claim:** While the verification procedure is a significant accomplishment, the phrase "provable generalization" might be too strong. The verification only applies to the specific inputs covered in the verification set.  It doesn't guarantee generalization to *all* possible inputs.
*  **Expand on the claim that existing architectures struggle due to spurious correlations:** Provide a more concrete example of the types of spurious correlations the NPI models learn without recursion and how the recursive formulation avoids them.

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  Can you elaborate on the types of problems where execution traces are readily available but your method still struggles to infer the correct program? What characteristics of those problems contribute to the difficulty?
2.  What are your thoughts on scaling this approach to problems with more complex, perceptual inputs (e.g., images)? How could the reliance on execution traces be mitigated in such scenarios?
3.  The verification procedure relies on constructing the set V. How sensitive is the verification to the completeness of V? Are there cases where an incomplete V could lead to a false positive (i.e., incorrectly claiming perfect generalization)?
4.  The paper mentions future work exploring training recursive programs without explicit traces. Could you provide more details on your initial ideas for this research direction?
