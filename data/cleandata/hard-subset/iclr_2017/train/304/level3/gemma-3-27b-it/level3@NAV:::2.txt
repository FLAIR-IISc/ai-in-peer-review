## Review of “Making Neural Programming Architectures Generalize via Recursion”

This paper presents an interesting and accessible extension to Neural Programmer-Interpreters (NPIs) by incorporating recursion. The central claim – that adding recursion significantly improves generalization, particularly from limited training data – is compelling and well-motivated. The authors effectively leverage existing NPI notation, making the work readily understandable for those familiar with Reed & Freitas (2016). The demonstration of perfect generalization, backed by a formal verification procedure, is a strong contribution.

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. The key strength of this work lies in its ability to demonstrate a practical and effective modification to a complex model. The successful application of recursion to sorting and topological sort tasks, combined with the theoretical grounding provided by the provable generalization results, makes a convincing case for the benefits of this approach.

The supporting arguments are strong in terms of empirical results. The experiments clearly show improved generalization capabilities, especially compared to non-recursive counterparts. The verification procedure is a significant advancement, providing a level of confidence in the model’s correctness rarely seen in neural program synthesis. However, the proofs of generalization, while conceptually sound, feel somewhat vague in their presentation and reliance on covering base cases and reduction rules. Further elaboration on how these are rigorously identified and tested would be beneficial. Additionally, the scalability of this approach to continuous input spaces, such as 3D car environments, is a valid concern. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but it’s a significant one for broader applicability.

To further improve the paper, I suggest the following:

*   **Elaborate on the verification process:** Provide more detail regarding how the verification set is constructed for each task. While the general approach is outlined, concrete examples would strengthen the argument.
*   **Discuss limitations of verification:** Acknowledge the challenges of scaling the verification procedure to more complex domains and discuss potential future directions to address these limitations.
*   **Source Code Release:** Will the authors consider releasing the source code for their implementation? This would greatly benefit the research community and facilitate further exploration of this work.
*   **Clarify the relationship between recursion depth and generalization:** Is there a limit to the depth of recursion the model can effectively handle? Does increasing recursion depth always lead to improved generalization, or are there diminishing returns?

I would also like to ask the authors:

*   The paper highlights the advantage of recursive formulation to make the generalization problem tractable. Could the authors elaborate on how the addition of recursion affects the computational cost (training/inference) of the model?
*   The verification procedure depends on creating a finite set of tests. Could the authors clarify how the size of this set scales with problem complexity (e.g. for larger graphs in topological sort) and what practical limitations this poses?
*   The authors mention exploring alternative training methods beyond providing explicit execution traces. Could they provide a brief overview of potential avenues they are considering?
