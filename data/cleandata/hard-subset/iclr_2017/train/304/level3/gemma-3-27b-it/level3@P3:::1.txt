## Review of "Making Neural Programming Architectures Generalize Via Recursion"

This paper proposes incorporating recursion into Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architectures to improve generalization and enable provable guarantees of correctness. The core idea – augmenting training traces with recursive calls – is simple and demonstrably effective on tasks like grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, achieving perfect generalization with relatively little training data. The authors also present a verification procedure to confirm this generalization, a significant contribution.

**Decision: Accept with Minor Revisions.** The paper addresses a crucial issue in neural program synthesis – generalization – with a novel and surprisingly effective approach. The empirical results are strong, and the introduction of provable generalization is a valuable step forward. While questions remain about the scalability and broader applicability of the approach, the demonstrated success justifies acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper clearly identifies the problem of poor generalization in neural program synthesis, specifically highlighting the limitations of previous approaches when faced with increasing input complexity. The motivation for incorporating recursion – reducing problem complexity and enabling formal reasoning – is well-articulated and grounded in established principles of programming. The adaptation of the NPI architecture to support recursion is straightforward and well-explained, emphasizing that the key innovation lies in the training data, not a complex architectural change. 

The empirical results are compelling. Achieving perfect generalization on all four tasks, particularly with limited training data, is a significant achievement. The experiments demonstrate a clear advantage of the recursive approach over non-recursive methods, especially for tasks like bubble sort and quicksort where the non-recursive versions falter. The verification procedure is a major strength.  Successfully demonstrating provable generalization, even within the confines of these specific tasks, is a noteworthy contribution to the field.  It provides a framework for assessing the correctness of learned programs that is absent in most existing work.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper presents a strong argument, several areas could benefit from further elaboration.  The reviewer questions raised in the paper itself – whether the success is due to the simplicity of the tasks or a fundamental breakthrough – are valid and deserve more attention. The authors acknowledge the potential for traces to "decouple perception from program logic,” but further discussion is needed on how this might limit the applicability of the approach to more complex, real-world problems.  

The experiments, while convincing, could be expanded.  Exploring tasks with higher dimensionality or more complex state spaces would help assess the scalability of the approach. The authors briefly mention that the verification procedure may be infeasible for tasks with very large input domains; elaborating on potential solutions or alternative verification methods would be beneficial.

The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the choice of the NPI architecture. While it serves as a convenient platform for demonstrating the benefits of recursion, it isn’t clear whether the results are specific to NPI or generalize to other neural program synthesis frameworks. Investigating the applicability of the proposed approach to alternative architectures would strengthen the paper's impact.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. **Scalability & Perception Decoupling:** The paper acknowledges the risk of perception decoupling in larger domains. Could you provide concrete examples of tasks where this might be problematic and discuss potential mitigation strategies (e.g., incorporating perceptual constraints into the training data)?

2. **Architectural Independence:** To what extent do you believe the benefits of recursive traces are tied to the NPI architecture? Have you considered or explored applying this approach to other neural program synthesis frameworks (e.g., those based on transformers)?

3. **Verification Complexity:**  You mention the difficulty of verifying programs in large input domains.  Are there alternative, potentially less rigorous, verification techniques (e.g., property-based testing) that could provide a degree of confidence in generalization without requiring exhaustive verification?

4. **Beyond Execution Traces:**  The paper focuses on modifying execution traces. Could you elaborate on your "future directions" regarding training recursive neural programs *without* explicit traces? What alternative training signals or architectural modifications might be explored to achieve this?

5. **Failure Cases:** What types of problems do you anticipate *would* cause this method to fail, even with access to execution traces? What inherent limitations prevent the approach from scaling to all possible program synthesis tasks?
