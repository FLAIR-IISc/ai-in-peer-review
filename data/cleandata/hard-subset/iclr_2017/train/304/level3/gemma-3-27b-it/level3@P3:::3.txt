## Review of “MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION”

This paper presents a compelling argument for the importance of recursion in enabling neural programs to generalize effectively. The authors demonstrate that augmenting the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework with recursion leads to significant improvements in generalization performance on tasks like addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. Crucially, they provide a formal proof of perfect generalization for the learned programs, a notable achievement in the field of neural program induction.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper tackles a significant challenge in neural program synthesis – generalization – and proposes a well-motivated and effective solution. The empirical results are strong, and the formal proof of generalization is a particularly valuable contribution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core contribution – demonstrating that recursion drastically improves generalization – is convincingly supported. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of previous approaches that struggle with generalization beyond a certain complexity threshold. They persuasively argue that this stems from the models learning spurious correlations rather than the underlying program semantics. Introducing recursion elegantly addresses this issue by decomposing the problem into smaller, more manageable pieces, and providing a framework for provable guarantees.

The empirical results are compelling. The reported performance improvements on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort are substantial, particularly the ability to generalize to significantly larger input sizes than previously achieved. The inclusion of topological sort and quicksort, added based on reviewer suggestions, demonstrates the authors’ responsiveness and willingness to strengthen their work.

The most remarkable aspect of this paper is the formal proof of generalization. This is a groundbreaking result, as it provides a rigorous guarantee that the learned programs will behave correctly on all valid inputs, a capability absent in previous work. The detailed explanation of the verification procedure, focusing on base cases and reduction rules, is well-presented and facilitates understanding of the proof’s validity. The fact that a relatively small verification set suffices highlights the power of the recursive formulation.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong overall, a few points could further enhance its clarity and impact:

* **NPI Implementation Details:** A more detailed description of the Keras implementation of NPI would be beneficial for reproducibility. Providing the code or a link to it would be ideal.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions using standard hyperparameters. Discussing the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters (learning rate, LSTM hidden size, etc.) would be valuable.
* **Comparison with Curriculum Learning:** The paper positions recursion as a superior alternative to curriculum learning. A more direct comparison, perhaps with experiments where both techniques are applied to the same tasks, would strengthen this argument.
* **Limitations of Verification:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of their verification procedure for tasks with very large input domains. Expanding on potential approaches to address this challenge in future work would be helpful.
* **Clarification on Recursive Trace Construction:** While the examples provided for trace construction are helpful, further clarifying the general principles behind generating these traces, particularly for more complex tasks like quicksort, would be appreciated.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with the verification procedure, particularly as the complexity of the task and the size of the verification set increase?
2.  The paper highlights the potential for training recursive neural programs without providing explicit execution traces. Could you provide more details on potential approaches for achieving this, perhaps outlining preliminary investigations or future research directions?
3.  Regarding the choice of the NPI architecture, what were the key factors that led you to select this framework for demonstrating the benefits of recursion? Are there other neural program induction architectures where recursion could be similarly integrated, and what challenges might arise in those contexts?
4.  You mention the difficulty in analytically determining the verification set for tasks like topological sort. What heuristics or strategies did you employ to ensure the completeness and coverage of the empirically constructed verification set?
5.  The modification to the domain-specific encoder for bubble sort to focus on value comparisons simplifies verification. Does this modification impact the model’s learning performance or its ability to generalize to scenarios with a wider range of data types?
