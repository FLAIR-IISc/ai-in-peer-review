- Main contribution: incorporating recursion into neural architectures enables perfect generalization with minimal training data.  
- Method: explicitly integrate recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) via training on recursive execution traces.  
- Evaluation: demonstrated on four tasks—grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort (the latter two added after reviewer suggestions).  
- Key innovation: provide formal proofs that the learned recursive programs generalize perfectly (claimed first such result in neural program induction).  
- Motivation: addresses poor generalization in prior work by avoiding spurious input-complexity dependencies; recursion makes verification tractable.  
- Empirical result: perfect generalization across tasks with very few traces (e.g., 200 for addition, 6 for topological sort); non-recursive models fail on larger inputs.  
- Verdict: recommend acceptance—substantial theoretical and empirical contribution with rigorous results.  
- Improvement 1: explicitly discuss limits of the verification procedure for very large or infinite input domains (e.g., images, speech).  
- Improvement 2: add a more detailed analysis of when recursion is necessary versus merely beneficial for program learning.  
- Questions for authors: have you tested tasks where recursion is not a natural abstraction, and can the method be extended to learn recursion automatically rather than requiring recursive traces?
