

This paper tackles the critical problem of poor generalization in neural program learning, where models trained on small inputs fail to correctly handle inputs of greater complexity. The authors propose that incorporating recursion into neural architectures is necessary for robust generalization, demonstrating this through an extension of the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework. The key contribution is showing that recursive neural programs can achieve provably perfect generalization for multiple tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort), with the ability to verify this generalization through a finite set of base cases and reduction rules. The paper is well-motivated, building on the observation that existing neural programming approaches learn spurious dependencies on input characteristics like length, and that recursion naturally decomposes problems into manageable subproblems.

I recommend acceptance with a strong positive recommendation. The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that recursion enables provably perfect generalization, which is the first such result in neural program induction. The empirical results are compelling, showing that the recursive approach achieves perfect generalization with minimal training data (e.g., 200 examples for addition, 4 for quicksort), while non-recursive versions fail for inputs beyond certain complexity thresholds. The verification procedure provides a theoretically sound method to guarantee generalization without exhaustive testing.

The paper's strongest contribution is the formal verification framework for neural programs. By showing that recursion reduces the problem to verifying a finite set of base cases and reduction rules, the authors provide a method to prove perfect generalization. This is particularly significant for tasks like addition where the non-recursive version generalizes well empirically but lacks a theoretical guarantee. The verification set for addition (20,181 inputs) is analytically constructed, while for other tasks it's generated through careful analysis of environment observations, demonstrating the approach's practicality.

The paper's empirical results are scientifically rigorous. For bubble sort, the authors show that partial recursion (only one recursive call) is insufficient for perfect generalization, while full recursion (three recursive calls) achieves it. Similarly, for topological sort, the recursive version generalizes perfectly from a single training trace (5-node graph) to graphs of 70 nodes, while the non-recursive version fails at graph size 5. The quicksort results demonstrate that even for non-tail recursive algorithms, the approach works. The verification procedure is applied consistently across all tasks, with matching results between the neural program and the target program on the verification sets.

The paper also addresses reviewer suggestions by adding two new tasks (topological sort and quicksort) that demonstrate the approach's broader applicability beyond the original NPI tasks. The implementation details for these tasks are thorough, with complete program sets and trace-generation functions provided in the appendix.

For improvement, the paper could provide more detailed analysis of why recursion works better than other approaches to handling complexity. While the paper notes that recursion reduces problem complexity, a more formal analysis of the complexity reduction would strengthen the theoretical contribution. Additionally, the verification procedure becomes intractable for tasks with very large input domains (like MNIST), and the paper should discuss this limitation more explicitly, perhaps suggesting potential workarounds for such cases.

I would like to ask the authors to clarify whether the verification procedure could be automated or partially automated for more complex tasks. Specifically, how would the verification set be constructed for a task with an infinite input domain, and what would be the computational cost of this verification procedure for larger problems? Additionally, could the approach be extended to provide probabilistic guarantees for tasks where perfect generalization is not possible?

The paper's contribution is significant both practically and theoretically. It demonstrates a practical method for achieving perfect generalization in neural program learning and provides the first formal verification framework for neural programs. The results are compelling, the methodology is sound, and the paper makes a clear, important contribution to the field of neural program induction.
