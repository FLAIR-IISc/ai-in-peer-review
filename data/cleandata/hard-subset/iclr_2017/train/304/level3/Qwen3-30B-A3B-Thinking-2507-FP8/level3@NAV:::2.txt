

**Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"**

1. **Summary of Contribution:** This paper makes a compelling case for incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures, specifically Neural Programmer-Interpreters (NPI). The authors demonstrate that by modifying training traces to include recursive elements, NPI can learn recursive programs that achieve perfect generalization from extremely small training sets (e.g., 6 traces for topological sort). They introduce a verification procedure to formally prove perfect generalization by checking base cases and reduction rules, a novel contribution to the field. The paper is well-written, uses consistent notation from prior work, and clearly shows how a small, non-trivial change (recursion) significantly enhances practicality and generalization.

2. **Decision:** Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to neural programming by demonstrating how recursion enables provable generalization, a problem that has plagued the field. The empirical results are strong, showing perfect generalization where non-recursive approaches fail, and the verification procedure provides a new way to reason about neural program behavior.

3. **Supporting Arguments:** The paper successfully addresses a key limitation in neural programming—poor generalization beyond training complexity. The recursive approach elegantly decomposes problems, making verification tractable. The four task demonstrations (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) provide robust empirical evidence. The authors clearly position their work in the literature and show how their approach differs from prior neural programming methods.

4. **Additional Feedback:** The paper would benefit from more rigorous generalization proofs and a discussion of how to handle continuous input spaces (e.g., 3D navigation tasks), which the authors acknowledge as potentially intractable. Releasing the source code would greatly benefit the community and help validate results. The verification procedure's scalability to more complex domains should be explored.

5. **Questions for Authors:** 
- Could you provide more detail on how to make the generalization proofs more rigorous, particularly for tasks with complex reduction rules?
- How might this approach be extended to handle continuous input spaces, and what would be the theoretical and practical challenges?
- Will you release the source code for your implementation of recursive NPI?
