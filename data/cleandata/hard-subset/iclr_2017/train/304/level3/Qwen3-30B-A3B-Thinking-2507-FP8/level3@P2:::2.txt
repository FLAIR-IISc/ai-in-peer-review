

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of neural programming by introducing recursion as a key abstraction to address the well-known problem of poor generalization in neural architectures that learn programs from data. The authors demonstrate that by incorporating recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework, they can achieve perfect generalization on multiple programming tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort) from extremely small training sets. The paper is well-written, uses consistent notation from prior work, and clearly demonstrates how a small but crucial extension to existing architectures can significantly improve practical applicability. Most notably, the authors claim to provide the first provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs by verifying base cases and reduction rules, a significant advancement over previous approaches that relied on empirical generalization metrics.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses an important problem in neural programming: the inability of existing models to generalize beyond a certain input complexity threshold. The key insight—that recursion enables provable guarantees of perfect generalization—appears well-motivated and well-placed within the literature. The authors provide compelling empirical evidence across four diverse programming tasks, showing that recursive neural programs can achieve perfect generalization with minimal training data, whereas non-recursive versions fail dramatically on unseen input complexities. The paper's contribution is both novel and practically significant, as it demonstrates how a small architectural change (adding recursion) can transform a model from one that generalizes poorly to one that generalizes perfectly.

The empirical results are strong, with the recursive versions consistently outperforming non-recursive versions across all tasks, even when trained on very small datasets. For example, the recursive topological sort model achieves 100% accuracy across graph sizes from 5 to 70 vertices after training on just six traces, while the non-recursive version fails completely for graphs larger than size 8.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, I have a few suggestions to improve it:

1. The generalization proofs require more concrete detail. The paper claims "provably perfect generalization" but the verification procedure is somewhat vague. Please provide a more detailed description of how base cases and reduction rules are systematically determined and verified for different tasks. The paper should clarify how the verification set is constructed and why it's sufficient to guarantee generalization.

2. The paper acknowledges that handling continuous input spaces (e.g., 3D car) would be intractable with their approach. Please expand on this limitation and discuss potential directions for future work to address it.

3. The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of the relationship between the training traces and the recursive structure. Specifically, how do the recursive training traces differ from non-recursive ones in terms of the information they provide to the model?

4. Please consider releasing the source code for reproducibility, as this would significantly increase the paper's impact.

## 5. Clarification Questions

1. For the verification procedure, could you elaborate on how the set V (valid sequences of step inputs) is constructed for each task? What guarantees do you have that V is complete (i.e., contains all step input sequences that could arise during execution of any valid problem input)?

2. In the bubble sort task, the paper mentions that the verification set consists of one array of size 10, but the non-recursive version fails for arrays of length 8. Could you explain why the verification set for bubble sort is so small compared to the point where generalization fails?

3. Will you be releasing the source code for the implementation? If so, when can we expect it to be available?
