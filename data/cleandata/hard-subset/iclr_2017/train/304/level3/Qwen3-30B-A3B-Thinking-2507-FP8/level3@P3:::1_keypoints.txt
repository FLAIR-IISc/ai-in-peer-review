- Core claim: recursion is a crucial abstraction for neural programming, enabling strong out-of-domain generalization when training traces include recursive calls.  
- Method: modify NPI training traces to include recursion, producing models with provable perfect generalization.  
- Empirical validation: recursive NPI achieves perfect accuracy on four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort); non-recursive versions fail beyond complexity thresholds.  
- Verification contribution: introduce a procedure that proves correctness by testing base cases and reduction rules only.  
- Decision: Accept (with minor revisions).  
- Strength: identifies a simple, well-motivated extension of NPI that yields strong empirical results and the first provable guarantees for neural programs.  
- Concern: training on execution traces may make tasks easier—unclear how the method performs when traces are unavailable or inputs are more complex (e.g., image-based tasks).  
- Requested improvement: expand discussion of limitations and provide concrete examples where execution-trace training fails to infer correct programs.  
- Requested improvement: quantify and compare verification set sizes versus training set sizes across tasks.  
- Requested improvement: discuss scalability to continuous or high-dimensional input spaces (e.g., sorting images).  
- Requested improvement: analyze why partial recursion (e.g., bubble sort) doesn’t yield perfect generalization to clarify recursion requirements.  
- Question: how to handle verification when base cases and reduction rules are prohibitively large or continuous (e.g., sorting images)?  
- Question: provide specific programming tasks where execution traces exist but the method would fail and explain limiting factors.  
- Question: how to adapt the approach to very large input domains (e.g., arrays of size 100,000 when trained on size 10) without an exponentially larger verification set?
