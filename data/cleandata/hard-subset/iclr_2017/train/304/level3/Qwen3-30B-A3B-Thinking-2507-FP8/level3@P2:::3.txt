

# Review for "Making Neural Programming Architectures Generalize via Recursion"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

This paper addresses a fundamental limitation in neural programming: poor generalization beyond a certain input complexity. The authors propose that incorporating recursion into neural architectures is essential for achieving robust generalization. They demonstrate that by training Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) models on recursive execution traces (rather than non-recursive ones), the resulting neural programs can achieve perfect generalization on unseen inputs with remarkably small training sets. The authors showcase this approach on four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort), demonstrating significant improvements over non-recursive NPI models. Crucially, they provide a verification procedure to formally prove perfect generalization, which represents a major contribution as it is the first time such provable guarantees have been established for neural programs.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The key reasons for this decision are: (1) the paper addresses a significant, well-motivated problem in neural programming with a novel solution (recursion), and (2) the claims are strongly supported by both empirical results and a new verification procedure that provides formal guarantees of perfect generalization.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully tackles the problem of poor generalization in neural programming, which has been a persistent issue in the field. The authors clearly articulate that existing approaches learn spurious dependencies on training examples (such as input length) rather than the true program semantics, leading to generalization failure. Their solution—explicitly incorporating recursion into neural architectures—is well-motivated and well-placed within the literature, as they correctly cite and build upon prior work while identifying a clear gap in the literature (lack of provable generalization guarantees for neural programs).

The empirical results are compelling and well-documented. For all four tasks, the recursive versions achieve perfect generalization with minimal training data, while non-recursive versions fail for inputs beyond a certain complexity threshold. For example, in bubble sort, the non-recursive version fails for arrays longer than 8 elements, while the recursive version sorts all arrays perfectly. In topological sort, the recursive version achieves 100% accuracy across all graph sizes tested (5-70 vertices), while the non-recursive version fails for graphs larger than 8 vertices.

The most significant contribution is the verification procedure, which allows proving perfect generalization. The authors demonstrate this for all four tasks, showing that by verifying a finite set of base cases and reduction rules (which is tractable due to recursion), they can guarantee correct behavior on all possible inputs. This is a major theoretical advance, as it's the first time neural programs have been shown to have provable generalization guarantees.

## 4. Additional Feedback

The paper is well-written and makes a strong contribution to the field. To further improve it, I suggest:

1. The paper could provide more intuition about why recursion helps neural programs avoid learning spurious dependencies on input length. While the paper explains the verification procedure well, a more detailed discussion of how recursion changes the learning dynamics would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

2. The authors should discuss the limitations of their verification procedure more thoroughly. The paper acknowledges that the approach becomes infeasible for problems with very large input domains (e.g., MNIST digits), but it would be valuable to provide more concrete examples of such limitations and potential workarounds.

3. The paper mentions that they "add two new tasks—topological sort and quicksort—based on reviewer suggestions." It would be helpful to explain why these particular tasks were chosen to demonstrate the approach's applicability beyond the original NPI tasks.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "recursion makes it easier for the network to learn the right program and generalize to unknown situations." Could the authors provide more specific insight into why recursion leads to better learning of program semantics, particularly why the network avoids learning spurious length-dependent dependencies?

2. The verification procedure is described as being applicable to the four tasks presented, but the authors note it becomes infeasible for "very large input domains." Could the authors elaborate on how they would approach verification for a task like sorting MNIST digits, and what would need to change in the approach to make it feasible?

3. In the paper, the authors state that "the training set can often be considerably smaller than the verification set." Could they provide a specific example comparing the size of the training set and verification set for one of the tasks, to better understand the relationship between these two sets?
