

# Review of "Making Neural Programming Architectures Generalize via Recursion"

This paper tackles the critical problem of poor generalization in neural programming architectures. The authors argue that neural models often learn spurious dependencies rather than true program semantics, leading to failure on inputs beyond training complexity. They propose incorporating recursion as a key abstraction to enable provable perfect generalization, demonstrating that recursive neural programs can achieve 100% accuracy on unseen inputs after training on small datasets.

The approach is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors correctly identify the limitation of existing neural programming models (e.g., Reed & de Freitas, 2016) and build upon the Neural Programmer-Interpreter framework. Their insight that recursion naturally reduces problem complexity and enables verification through base cases and reduction rules is compelling and well-justified.

The paper supports its claims with empirical results showing recursive NPI models perfectly learn addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort with minimal training data, while non-recursive versions fail on larger inputs. The verification procedure they propose (testing only a small set of base cases) is novel and significant for providing provable guarantees.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's broader claim that "recursion solves neural program induction." The tasks tested (grade-school addition, bubble sort, etc.) are highly structured and small-scale. The critical assumption that execution traces are readily available for training is problematic for real-world applications. For instance, sorting MNIST digits would require the model to simultaneously learn perception and program logic, which execution traces would decouple, making the task trivial rather than challenging. The paper does not address what problems exist where execution traces are available but the method still fails.

Decision: Reject

Key reasons for rejection: (1) The experimental setup is too narrow to support the broad claim that recursion "solves" neural program induction, as it relies on synthetic execution traces that may not exist in real-world scenarios; (2) The paper fails to address how the method would perform on more complex problems where execution traces decouple perception from program logic, making the problem trivial rather than challenging.

Additional feedback: The paper's verification procedure is interesting, but the authors should explicitly acknowledge the limitation of requiring execution traces. Future work should explore how to induce recursion without explicit traces, which would be a more significant contribution. The authors should also address the reviewer's concern about the applicability to larger input domains like sorting MNIST digits.

Key questions for authors: 
1. How would your method handle a task where execution traces are not available (e.g., sorting images), and what would be the performance compared to non-recursive methods?
2. Can you provide a concrete example of a problem where execution traces are available but your method still fails to infer the correct program?
