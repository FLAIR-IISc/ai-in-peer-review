

# Review of "Making Neural Programming Architectures Generalize via Recursion"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contribution

This paper makes a compelling and well-executed contribution to the field of neural programming by introducing recursion as a key abstraction to improve generalization. The authors demonstrate that by incorporating recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework, neural programs can achieve perfect generalization from very small training sets (as few as 4-200 examples across different tasks), while also enabling provable guarantees about their behavior. The paper makes the important observation that recursion is a natural fit for the NPI architecture (which was not explicitly leveraged in the original NPI work) and shows how to modify training traces to enable the model to learn recursive programs. The authors apply their approach to four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, demonstrating superior performance compared to non-recursive versions, particularly for generalization to longer inputs or larger problem instances. The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations and consistent notation that makes the technical content accessible even to non-experts in neural programming.

## 2. Decision

I recommend accepting this paper. The key reasons for this recommendation are:
1) The paper successfully demonstrates that a small, conceptually simple modification (incorporating recursion) leads to significantly improved generalization performance from minimal training data.
2) The authors provide the first provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs, which represents a major theoretical advance in the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution is both conceptually simple and empirically compelling. The authors correctly identify that the core problem with existing neural programming approaches is their inability to generalize beyond a certain input complexity threshold due to learning spurious dependencies. By incorporating recursion, they decompose problems into smaller, manageable pieces, making it tractable to verify the behavior of the neural program across all possible inputs.

The experimental results are particularly strong: for addition, the recursive model generalizes perfectly to problems of arbitrary length after training on just 5 examples, while the non-recursive version fails. For bubble sort, the fully recursive version achieves 100% accuracy on arrays of any length (tested up to 10 elements), while the non-recursive version fails for arrays longer than 8 elements. The topological sort and quicksort results follow the same pattern, with the recursive versions achieving perfect generalization where non-recursive ones fail.

The paper's theoretical contribution of "provably perfect generalization" is significant. The authors demonstrate how recursion reduces the problem of verifying a neural program's behavior to verifying a finite set of base cases and reduction rules, rather than testing all possible inputs. This is the first time such provable guarantees have been established for neural programs.

## 4. Additional Feedback and Questions

While the paper is strong overall, I have a few suggestions to improve it:

1. The proof of generalization is compelling for the discrete, structured tasks presented (addition, sorting), but the paper acknowledges that the verification procedure becomes intractable for continuous input spaces. I'd appreciate more discussion on the limitations of this approach and whether the authors have considered potential extensions for continuous domains.

2. The paper mentions "a verification set that covers all base cases and reduction rules," but the description of how this set is constructed is somewhat vague. Could the authors provide more concrete details about the verification procedure for one of the tasks (e.g., addition) to help readers understand how to implement this for their own work?

3. The authors state that "this verification procedure cannot be performed for the non-recursive versions, since the propagation of the hidden state in the core LSTM module makes reasoning difficult." This is a strong claim that warrants more justification. Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of why the non-recursive version's hidden state propagation makes reasoning difficult, while the recursive version's structure enables tractable verification?

4. Will the authors release the source code for their implementation of the recursive NPI? Given the paper's focus on practical implementation details, having access to the code would greatly facilitate reproducibility and further research in this area.

This paper represents a significant step toward making neural programs more robust and interpretable. The authors have successfully demonstrated that a small but conceptually important change (recursion) can make neural programming approaches more practical and theoretically grounded. I believe this work will have a substantial impact on the field and would be valuable for both theoretical and applied researchers in neural programming.
