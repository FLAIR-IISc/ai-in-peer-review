

# Review of "Making Neural Programming Architectures Generalize Via Recursion"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a significant conceptual advancement in neural program induction by demonstrating that incorporating recursion into neural architectures enables perfect generalization, a property previously unattainable in the field. The authors show that by modifying the training traces to include recursive elements, the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework can learn recursive programs that generalize perfectly to inputs of arbitrary complexity. The paper makes four key contributions: (1) it demonstrates superior generalization for addition and bubble sort with minimal training data; (2) it introduces two new tasks—topological sort and quicksort—based on reviewer suggestions; (3) it provides the first formal proof of perfect generalization for neural programs; and (4) it establishes recursion as a necessary abstraction for robust neural program learning.

The paper's core insight—that recursion enables provable guarantees about neural program behavior—represents a fundamental theoretical advance. The authors carefully explain how recursion decomposes problems into smaller instances, making it tractable to verify base cases and reduction rules, which collectively guarantee perfect generalization. Their empirical results across four tasks consistently show that recursive programs achieve 100% accuracy on inputs of arbitrary size, while non-recursive versions fail at certain complexity thresholds, even with extensive training.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a compelling case for recursion as a necessary abstraction in neural program induction, with two key reasons supporting this decision: (1) it provides the first formal proof of perfect generalization for neural programs, a significant theoretical contribution; and (2) it demonstrates clear empirical improvements in sample efficiency and generalization across multiple tasks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper directly addresses a fundamental limitation in neural program induction: the inability to guarantee generalization beyond training complexity. Previous work has relied on empirical generalization metrics, but without theoretical guarantees, it remains unclear whether models learn true program semantics or spurious correlations. The authors convincingly argue that recursion resolves this issue by enabling decomposition of problems into smaller instances, which makes the generalization problem tractable.

The experimental results are particularly impressive. For addition, the recursive model generalizes perfectly to problems with 5000+ digits after training on just 200 traces, while the non-recursive version fails on problems constructed from training components. For bubble sort, the fully recursive version achieves perfect accuracy on arrays of any length, while non-recursive versions fail beyond length 8. The introduction of topological sort and quicksort tasks demonstrates the broader applicability of their approach.

Most significantly, the paper provides the first formal proof of perfect generalization for neural programs. By verifying base cases and reduction rules, the authors can prove that their learned programs will behave correctly for any valid input. This is a groundbreaking theoretical contribution that advances the field beyond empirical generalization metrics.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, a few enhancements would strengthen it further:

- The paper could provide more insight into why recursive training traces work so effectively. A deeper analysis of the learning dynamics, particularly how the model's internal representations change when learning recursive versus non-recursive programs, would be valuable.

- The verification procedure, while impressive for the tasks presented, becomes intractable for problems with continuous inputs (e.g., image processing). A brief discussion of potential extensions to handle such cases would strengthen the paper's broader impact.

- The paper mentions future work on training with partial or non-recursive traces. A short discussion of the challenges in this direction would help frame the current work's limitations and future potential.

## 5. Clarifying Questions

To better understand the paper's contributions and ensure confidence in the assessment, I would like to ask:

1. For topological sort, the paper modifies the domain-specific encoder to directly expose comparison results. How does this affect the model's ability to generalize to graphs with different value ranges or non-integer vertex labels?

2. The verification set for addition contains 20,181 input problems, which is larger than the training set of 200 traces. Can you explain why the verification set is larger in this case, and how the size of the verification set scales with problem complexity?

3. The quicksort implementation uses non-tail recursion, which the paper states is more complex to learn. How did this complexity affect the training process compared to the tail-recursive addition and bubble sort tasks?
