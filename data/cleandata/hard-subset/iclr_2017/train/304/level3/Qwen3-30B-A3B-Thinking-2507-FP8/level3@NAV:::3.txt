

**Review**

This paper makes a significant contribution to neural program induction by demonstrating how incorporating recursion into neural architectures enables perfect generalization with minimal training data. The authors propose a novel approach where recursion is explicitly integrated into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework through training on recursive execution traces. They demonstrate this approach across four tasks—grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort—with the latter two tasks added in response to reviewer suggestions. The key innovation is providing formal proofs that the learned recursive programs generalize perfectly, a result they claim is the first of its kind in neural program induction.

The paper clearly addresses the critical problem of poor generalization in neural program induction, where existing models learn spurious dependencies on input complexity rather than true program semantics. The approach is well-motivated by a thorough analysis of prior work and a compelling theoretical argument for why recursion enables tractable verification of program behavior. The empirical results are scientifically rigorous, showing perfect generalization across all tasks with minimal training data (e.g., 200 traces for addition, 6 for topological sort), while non-recursive versions fail on inputs beyond a certain complexity.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a substantial contribution through its formal proof of perfect generalization, which addresses a fundamental limitation in the field. The empirical results are convincing across multiple diverse tasks, and the approach is well-placed within the literature as a direct extension of NPI with clear theoretical justification.

For further improvement, the authors should explicitly discuss the limitations of their verification procedure for tasks with very large or infinite input domains (e.g., image processing or speech recognition), as mentioned in the paper. The paper would also benefit from a more detailed analysis of when recursion is necessary versus beneficial for program learning.

I would like to ask the authors whether they have tested their approach on tasks where recursion is not the most natural abstraction, to better understand the scope of applicability. Additionally, how might the approach be extended to learn recursion automatically rather than requiring recursive traces in training data?
