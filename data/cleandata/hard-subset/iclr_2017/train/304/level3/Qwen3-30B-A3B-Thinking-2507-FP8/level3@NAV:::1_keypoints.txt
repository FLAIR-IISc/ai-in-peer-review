- Tackles poor generalization in neural programming and proposes recursion to enable provable perfect generalization.  
- Builds on Neural Programmer-Interpreter; argues recursion reduces complexity and allows verification via base cases and reduction rules.  
- Empirical claim: recursive NPI models reach 100% accuracy on unseen inputs for addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort after minimal training.  
- Novel verification procedure: test a small set of base cases to obtain provable guarantees.  
- Strengths: well-motivated, well-placed in literature, and presents a compelling insight about recursion.  
- Major concern: broad claim that "recursion solves neural program induction" is unsupported by narrow experiments.  
- Experimental setup is synthetic and relies on availability of execution traces, which may not exist in real-world applications.  
- Paper does not address combined perception+logic tasks (e.g., sorting MNIST) where execution traces decouple learning and may trivialize the problem.  
- Missing analysis of cases where execution traces are available but the method still fails to infer correct programs.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Key rejection reasons: (1) reliance on synthetic execution traces undermines the broad claim; (2) lack of evaluation on more complex or realistic tasks where traces separate perception from program logic.  
- Suggested fixes: explicitly acknowledge the execution-trace limitation and investigate inducing recursion without explicit traces.  
- Recommendation to address applicability to larger input domains (e.g., sorting images) in future work.  
- Key questions for authors: How would the method perform when execution traces are not available? Can you give an example where traces exist but your method fails?
