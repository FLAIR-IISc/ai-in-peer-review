- Introduces recursion as a core abstraction in Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI), improving generalization and interpretability with limited data.  
- Evaluates recursive NPI on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) and adds two new tasks (topological sort, quicksort).  
- Demonstrates superior sample complexity and generalization compared to non-recursive baselines.  
- Provides a formal proof of perfect generalization for recursive neural programs — a first for the field.  
- Decision: Accept — novel, impactful contribution with rigorous evaluation and formal guarantees.  
- Well-motivated: addresses limitations of existing neural programming models (poor generalization, low interpretability) by leveraging recursion.  
- Empirical and theoretical rigor: combines experiments and a novel formal verification procedure demonstrating perfect generalization on tested tasks.  
- Verification procedure is valuable but currently dense and would benefit from simplification or illustrative examples.  
- Needs deeper analysis of why non-recursive models fail, particularly regarding learned spurious dependencies.  
- Scalability concern: experiments are on synthetic tasks; discussion needed on extension to real-world programming tasks (e.g., parsing, code generation).  
- Future directions: explore less supervised training (partial or non-recursive traces) and expand on these ideas as a research roadmap.  
- Question: Can the verification procedure be automated for very large or infinite input domains, and what are the challenges?  
- Question: How does recursive NPI handle noisy or incomplete training data — does recursion improve robustness?  
- Question: Can the approach be integrated with other architectures (e.g., Transformers) to enhance scalability and applicability?
