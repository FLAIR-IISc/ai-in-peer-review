## Review of "Making Neural Programming Architectures Generalize via Recursion"

This paper presents an interesting and well-written exploration of incorporating recursion into Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architectures to improve generalization. The core idea – that recursion provides a natural way to decompose problems and reduce the complexity for each neural network component – is compelling and intuitively sound. The authors successfully demonstrate this with experiments on grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, showing improved generalization, particularly with limited training data. Reusing the DeepMind paper notation is a smart choice, enhancing readability for those familiar with the field.

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. The primary reason for acceptance is the clear demonstration of a practical improvement to NPI through a relatively simple extension (recursion). The paper convincingly argues that this extension addresses a key limitation of existing neural program learning approaches – poor generalization – and offers a pathway towards more robust and interpretable models. The empirical results consistently show the benefits of recursion across diverse tasks.

The paper’s strength lies in its clarity and the tangible impact of the proposed method. The authors effectively motivate the use of recursion, grounding it in the challenges of generalization in neural program synthesis. The experimental setup is well-defined, and the results are presented in a clear and concise manner. The inclusion of provable generalization, even with the caveats discussed, is a significant contribution.

However, the proofs of generalization, while a noteworthy attempt, remain somewhat vague and rely heavily on the completeness of the verification set. The discussion of handling continuous input spaces (like 3D car environments) being intractable is a valid limitation, but could be expanded upon. It would be helpful to discuss potential strategies for addressing this challenge, even if only speculatively. 

I have several questions for the authors:

*   Regarding the verification procedure: How sensitive is the verification process to the completeness of the verification set? What steps were taken to ensure the verification set truly covers all base cases and reduction rules, particularly for the more complex tasks like topological sort and quicksort?
*   The paper mentions the possibility of training recursive neural programs without explicit training execution traces. Could you elaborate on potential approaches for achieving this?
*   The authors state that the recursive formulation of addition requires a smaller training set than the original NPI implementation. Could you quantify this difference and provide a more detailed analysis of the data efficiency gains achieved through recursion?
*   Will the source code for the experiments be made publicly available? This would greatly facilitate reproducibility and further research in this area.
