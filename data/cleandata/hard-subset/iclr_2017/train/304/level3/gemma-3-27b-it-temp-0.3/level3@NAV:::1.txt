## Review of "Making Neural Programming Architectures Generalize via Recursion"

This paper tackles the crucial problem of generalization in neural program synthesis, arguing that incorporating recursion is key to achieving strong out-of-domain performance and learning from limited data. The authors build upon the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework, focusing on modifying the training traces to include recursive calls. A significant strength is the proposed method for verifying correctness – testing only a small set of base cases and reduction rules – which is a novel and valuable contribution. The empirical results, demonstrating perfect learning of Bubblesort and Tower of Hanoi with recursive traces, are compelling.

I recommend **Reject** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the core idea is intriguing and the results promising, I am not fully convinced that the success demonstrated is a direct consequence of recursion, rather than the specific, relatively simple tasks chosen and the availability of complete execution traces. The paper feels somewhat limited in scope and doesn’t sufficiently address the potential limitations of this approach.

My primary concern revolves around the question of whether access to execution traces fundamentally *solves* neural program induction, or if the problems presented are too easy. The authors acknowledge this in their discussion, but don’t fully explore it. The success on sorting and Hanoi, while impressive, doesn’t necessarily translate to more complex, real-world scenarios. The paper briefly mentions the potential for trace decoupling in larger input domains (like sorting MNIST digits), but doesn’t delve into how this might be mitigated or whether the proposed method would even be applicable. The claim of "provable generalization" feels overstated without addressing this broader context. 

Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to existing approaches that attempt to improve generalization (e.g., curriculum learning, meta-learning) and a more detailed ablation study to isolate the impact of recursion. It's unclear how much of the performance gain is due to the recursive traces *specifically*, versus simply providing more informative training data.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the role of execution traces:** The paper needs to more explicitly discuss the limitations imposed by relying on complete execution traces. How does this approach scale to tasks where such traces are unavailable or prohibitively expensive to generate?
*   **Explore more challenging tasks:** Testing on more complex problems with larger state spaces and more intricate logic is crucial.
*   **Address the open question:** The authors pose the question of what problems would cause this method to fail. Providing some initial exploration of this question would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Expand the discussion of verification:** While the verification procedure is a valuable contribution, the paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of how the base cases and reduction rules are identified and how the verification set is constructed.



**Questions for the Authors:**

*   How would this approach perform on tasks where generating complete execution traces is impractical or impossible?
*   What modifications would be necessary to adapt this method to tasks with continuous state spaces (e.g., control problems)?
*   Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of generating the recursive training traces compared to non-recursive traces?
*   What specific characteristics of the Bubblesort and Hanoi tasks make them particularly well-suited to this approach? Would you expect the same level of success on a more complex sorting algorithm (e.g., Mergesort)?
