## Review of "Making Neural Programming Architectures Generalize via Recursion"

This paper tackles the critical problem of generalization in neural program synthesis, specifically addressing the limitations of existing approaches that struggle beyond a certain input complexity. The authors propose augmenting neural architectures with recursion, a key abstraction in programming, and demonstrate its effectiveness within the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework across four tasks: addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. A particularly strong contribution is the formal proof of perfect generalization for the learned algorithms, a significant first in this field.

I recommend **Accept** with enthusiasm. The paper presents a compelling argument for the importance of recursion in neural program induction, supported by both empirical results and a formal proof. The improvement in generalization, particularly for tasks like bubble sort and quicksort, is substantial and clearly demonstrates the benefits of the proposed approach. The addition of topological sort and quicksort, based on reviewer suggestions, strengthens the paper and broadens its scope. The formal proof of generalization is a landmark achievement, providing a level of theoretical grounding rarely seen in this area.

The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of recursion. The authors show that incorporating recursion allows the NPI model to achieve perfect generalization with a surprisingly small number of training examples. The comparison to non-recursive approaches highlights the significant improvement in performance and robustness. The verification procedure, while complex, is clearly explained and provides strong evidence for the theoretical claims. The inclusion of detailed program sets and trace-generating functions in the appendix further enhances the reproducibility and understanding of the work.

To further improve the paper, I suggest the following: 
* **Clarify the limitations of the verification procedure:** The authors acknowledge that the current verification method may not scale to tasks with very large input domains. Expanding on this limitation and discussing potential future directions for addressing it would be valuable.
* **Discuss the computational cost of recursion:** While recursion improves generalization, it can also introduce computational overhead. A brief discussion of the trade-offs between generalization and computational efficiency would be beneficial.
* **Explore alternative training methods:** The paper focuses on training with recursive traces. Investigating other training strategies, such as curriculum learning or reinforcement learning, could reveal further insights into the benefits of recursion.

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors:
*  For the quicksort task, you mention the earlier version required more functions and observations, making verification difficult. Could you elaborate on the specific changes made to simplify the verification process, and how these changes might affect the expressiveness of the learned program?
*  The verification process relies on constructing a set V that covers all base cases and reduction rules. How sensitive is the verification result to the completeness of V? Is there a risk of false positives if V is not perfectly comprehensive?
*  You mention the possibility of automating the generation of V from P in future work. Could you provide some initial thoughts on how this might be achieved?
