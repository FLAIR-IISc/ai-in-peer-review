This paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural architectures to improve their generalization capabilities, particularly in the context of neural programming. The authors argue that recursion is a crucial concept in programming languages and can be leveraged to reduce the complexity of problems, making it easier for neural networks to learn and generalize.

The authors build upon the Neural Programmer-Interpreters (NPI) framework, which is a type of neural programming architecture. They modify the training procedure by incorporating recursive calls into the execution traces, enabling the NPI model to learn recursive neural programs. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of recursion into neural programming architectures, which enables provably perfect generalization. The authors provide a verification procedure to prove that the learned recursive programs generalize perfectly, by testing on a set of problem inputs that cover all base cases and reduction rules.

The empirical results show that the recursive NPI models achieve perfect generalization on all tasks, outperforming non-recursive models. The authors also demonstrate that the recursive models can learn from a small number of training examples and generalize to unseen inputs.

I accept this paper because it presents a novel and well-motivated approach to improving the generalization capabilities of neural programming architectures. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their method, and the empirical results are convincing.

However, I have some concerns about the scope of the paper. The authors only demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a limited set of tasks, and it is unclear how well the method will generalize to more complex problems. Additionally, the authors rely on the availability of execution traces, which may not always be feasible in practice.

To strengthen the paper, I would like the authors to address the following questions:

* Can the authors provide more insight into why recursion is essential for neural programs to generalize? Is there a theoretical justification for this claim?
* How do the authors plan to extend their approach to more complex problems, where the availability of execution traces may be limited?
* Can the authors provide more details on the verification procedure, particularly on how to construct the set of problem inputs that cover all base cases and reduction rules?

Additional feedback:

* The paper could benefit from more visualizations, such as diagrams or flowcharts, to illustrate the recursive neural programs and the verification procedure.
* The authors may want to consider comparing their approach to other methods that improve generalization in neural programming architectures, such as curriculum learning or regularization techniques.
* The paper assumes a strong background in neural programming architectures and recursion theory. The authors may want to provide more context or explanations for readers who are not familiar with these topics.
