**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes augmenting neural architectures with recursion to improve generalizability and interpretability. The authors apply this idea to the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework and demonstrate its effectiveness on four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. The recursive neural programs learned by the NPI framework achieve superior generalizability and interpretability with small amounts of training data. The authors also provide a formal proof that the learned algorithms generalize perfectly, making this the first such result in neural program induction.

**Decision**

Based on my evaluation, I decide to **Accept** this paper. The key reasons for this choice are:

1. The paper tackles a specific and important problem in neural programming, namely, the lack of generalizability and interpretability of neural networks that attempt to learn programs from data.
2. The approach is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear and compelling argument for why recursion is a key abstraction that can help address these issues.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper provides strong evidence that the recursive neural programs learned by the NPI framework achieve superior generalizability and interpretability. The experimental results show that the recursive programs can generalize to longer inputs and more complex tasks, whereas the non-recursive programs fail to do so. The authors also provide a formal proof that the learned algorithms generalize perfectly, which is a significant contribution to the field of neural program induction.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach and results. The experiments are well-designed, and the results are presented in a clear and easy-to-understand manner.
