This paper proposes a novel approach to improve the generalization of Neural Programming Interpreters (NPI) by incorporating recursion. The authors demonstrate that by allowing the NPI to learn recursive programs, they can achieve superior generalization and interpretability with small amounts of training data.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to non-experts in the field. The authors reuse the notation from the DeepMind paper, which aids readability. The introduction provides a clear overview of the problem of generalization in neural programming architectures and the limitations of current approaches.

The key contribution of the paper is the introduction of recursion as a means to improve generalization. The authors show that by modifying the training procedure to include recursive elements, the NPI can learn recursive programs that generalize perfectly to unseen inputs. The paper provides a thorough analysis of the benefits of recursion, including its ability to reduce the complexity of problems and make them more tractable to reason about.

The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach on four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. The authors show that the recursive NPI outperforms the non-recursive version in terms of generalization, especially on longer inputs.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide provable guarantees about the behavior of the learned neural program. The authors propose a verification procedure that can be used to prove the correctness of the learned program on a given set of inputs. This is a significant contribution, as it addresses one of the major limitations of current neural programming architectures.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The generalization proofs are vague, and it is unclear how the approach would handle continuous input spaces, such as 3D car models. Additionally, the authors do not provide any information about the computational cost of the proposed approach or its scalability to larger problems.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper. The authors provide a novel approach to improving the generalization of NPI, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to non-experts in the field.
