This paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural architectures to improve their generalizability and interpretability. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by applying it to the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework on four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. The results show that the learned recursive programs achieve perfect generalization from a small number of training examples, outperforming previous results.

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of recursion into neural programming architectures, which enables provably perfect generalization. The authors provide a formal proof that the learned algorithms generalize perfectly, making this the first such result in neural program induction.

The approach is well-motivated, as the authors highlight the limitations of existing neural programming architectures in terms of generalization. They argue that the key to improving generalization is to incorporate recursion, which reduces the complexity of problems and makes it easier to reason about a program's behavior.

The paper supports its claims through extensive experiments on the four tasks mentioned above. The results demonstrate that the learned recursive programs achieve perfect generalization, whereas the non-recursive programs do not. The authors also provide a formal proof of perfect generalization for the recursive programs, which is a significant contribution to the field.

I accept this paper because it presents a novel and well-motivated approach to improving the generalizability of neural programming architectures. The results are impressive, and the formal proof of perfect generalization is a significant contribution.

However, I would like to see more discussion on the limitations of the approach and potential future work. For example, the authors mention that the verification procedure is inadequate for tasks with very large input domains. It would be helpful to discuss potential ways to address this limitation and extend the approach to more complex tasks.

Additionally, I would like to see more details on the implementation of the recursive NPI framework. The authors mention that they modified the training procedure to enable the NPI model to learn recursive programs, but they do not provide more details on how this was done.

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and well-motivated approach to improving the generalizability of neural programming architectures. The results are impressive, and the formal proof of perfect generalization is a significant contribution.

Questions for the authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the implementation of the recursive NPI framework? How did you modify the training procedure to enable the NPI model to learn recursive programs?
2. How do you plan to address the limitation of the verification procedure for tasks with very large input domains?
3. Can you discuss potential ways to extend the approach to more complex tasks, such as tasks involving MNIST digits or speech samples?
