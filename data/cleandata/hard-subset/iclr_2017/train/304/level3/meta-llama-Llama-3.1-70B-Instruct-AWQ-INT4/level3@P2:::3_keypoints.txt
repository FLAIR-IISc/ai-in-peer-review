- Proposes augmenting neural architectures with recursion to improve generalizability and interpretability.  
- Applies recursion within the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework and tests on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Reports recursive NPI programs achieve superior generalizability and interpretability with small training data.  
- Presents a formal proof that the learned algorithms generalize perfectly, claimed as a first in neural program induction.

- Decision: Accept.  
- Main reasons: addresses lack of generalizability and interpretability in neural program learning; approach is well-motivated with a clear argument for recursion.

- Experimental evidence shows recursive models generalize to longer inputs and harder instances while non-recursive models fail.  
- The formal proof of perfect generalization is a significant contribution.  
- Paper is well-written; experiments are well-designed and results clearly presented.

- Suggest exploring more complex tasks/domains (e.g., NLP, vision) to demonstrate broader applicability.  
- Recommend providing more technical details on training procedure and neural architecture.

- Questions for authors: provide training details (optimizer, hyperparameters), plans for extending to complex tasks/domains, and key steps/assumptions in the formal proof and how it ties to architecture/training.
