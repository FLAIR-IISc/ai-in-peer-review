- Proposes adding recursion to neural architectures to reduce problem complexity and improve generalization in neural programming.  
- Builds on the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) and modifies training by inserting recursive calls into execution traces.  
- Demonstrates the approach on four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Main contribution: introducing recursion into neural programming to enable provably perfect generalization.  
- Provides a verification procedure that tests a covering set of inputs for base cases and reduction rules to prove generalization.  
- Empirical results: recursive NPI models achieve perfect generalization, outperform non-recursive models, and learn from few examples.  
- Reviewer recommendation: accept — method is novel, well-motivated, and empirically convincing.  
- Concern: experiments are limited to a small set of tasks, so scalability to more complex problems is unclear.  
- Concern: approach depends on availability of execution traces, which may not be practical in many settings.  
- Ask for theoretical insight or justification for why recursion is essential for neural program generalization.  
- Ask how the method can be extended to more complex problems or settings with limited execution traces.  
- Ask for more detail on the verification procedure, especially constructing input sets that cover all base cases and reductions.  
- Suggest adding visualizations (diagrams/flowcharts) to illustrate recursive programs and the verification method.  
- Recommend comparing to other generalization-improving methods (e.g., curriculum learning, regularization).  
- Suggest providing more background on neural programming and recursion for non-expert readers.
