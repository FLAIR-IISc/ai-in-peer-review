**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes an extension to Neural Programming Interpreters (NPI) by incorporating recursion, a fundamental concept in computer science. The authors demonstrate that this small but effective modification improves the generalization capabilities of NPI from fewer execution traces. They apply this extension to four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, achieving superior generalizability and interpretability with small amounts of training data. The paper also provides a verification procedure to prove the perfect generalization of the learned recursive programs.

**Decision**

I recommend accepting this paper, with two key reasons for this choice. Firstly, the paper introduces a novel and effective extension to NPI, which improves its generalization capabilities. Secondly, the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach on four tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness and providing insights into the benefits of incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of current neural programming approaches and how recursion can address these limitations. The extension to NPI is simple yet effective, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach on four tasks. The paper is also well-written, making it easy to follow for non-experts.

The authors demonstrate that incorporating recursion into NPI improves its generalization capabilities, allowing it to learn from fewer execution traces. This is a significant contribution, as it has the potential to make neural programming more practical and widely applicable.
