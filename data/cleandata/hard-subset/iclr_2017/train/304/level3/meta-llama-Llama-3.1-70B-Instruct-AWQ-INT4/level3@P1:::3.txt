**Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions**

The paper proposes a novel approach to improve the generalizability of neural programming architectures by incorporating recursion. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by modifying the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework to learn recursive programs for four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. The results show significant improvement in generalization performance, especially when training on small amounts of data. Furthermore, the authors provide a formal proof that the learned algorithms generalize perfectly, which is a first in neural program induction.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper addresses a specific and well-defined problem in the field of neural programming architectures, namely the issue of poor generalizability. The approach proposed by the authors is well-motivated and grounded in the literature. The experiments are thorough and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach on multiple tasks. The formal proof of perfect generalization is a significant contribution to the field.

**Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

1. **Clear problem statement and motivation**: The paper clearly articulates the problem of poor generalizability in neural programming architectures and motivates the need for a solution.
2. **Well-designed experiments**: The experiments are well-designed, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach on multiple tasks.
3. **Significant improvement over baseline**: The results show significant improvement over the original NPI framework, especially when training on small amounts of data.
4. **Formal proof of perfect generalization**: The paper provides a formal proof that the learned algorithms generalize perfectly, which is a first in neural program induction.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

1. **Clarify the relationship between recursion and generalization**: While the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of recursion in improving generalization, it would be helpful to provide more insight into why recursion is particularly well-suited for this task.
2. **Discuss the limitations of the approach**: The paper mentions that the approach may not be applicable to tasks with very large input domains, but it would be helpful to discuss this limitation in more detail and provide guidance on how to address it.
3. **Provide more details on the implementation**: While the paper provides a high-level overview of the implementation, it would be helpful to provide more details on the specific architectures and hyperparameters used in the experiments.

**Questions for the Authors**

1. Can you provide more insight into why recursion is particularly well-suited for improving generalization in neural programming architectures?
2. How do you plan to address the limitation of the approach for tasks with very large input domains?
3. Can you provide more details on the specific architectures and hyperparameters used in the experiments?
