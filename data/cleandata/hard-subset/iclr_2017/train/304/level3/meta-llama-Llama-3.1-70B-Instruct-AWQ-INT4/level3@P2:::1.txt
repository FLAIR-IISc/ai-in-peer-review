**Summary**

This paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures to improve generalization and interpretability. The authors build upon the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework and modify the training procedure to include recursive calls. They demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. The paper also introduces a verification procedure to prove the correctness of the learned recursive programs.

**Decision**

I recommend accepting this paper, with one key reason being the novelty and significance of the proposed approach. The incorporation of recursion into neural programming architectures is a fresh idea that has the potential to improve the generalization and interpretability of neural programs. The empirical results, although limited to a few tasks, are promising and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.

**Supporting arguments**

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of current neural programming architectures. The introduction of recursion as a key abstraction to address these limitations is well-justified. The paper also provides a thorough analysis of the proposed approach, including a verification procedure to prove the correctness of the learned recursive programs.

The empirical results are promising, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach on four tasks. The results show that the recursive NPI models can generalize perfectly to unseen inputs, whereas the non-recursive models fail to do so.

**Additional feedback**

One potential concern is that the proposed approach may not be effective for more complex tasks or larger input domains. The authors acknowledge this limitation and propose it as future work. However, it is essential to investigate this concern further to ensure that the approach is scalable.

Another potential concern is that the verification procedure may not be feasible for more complex tasks or larger input domains. The authors propose an alternative method for constructing the verification set, but it is unclear whether this method is scalable.

**Questions for the authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the scalability concerns for more complex tasks or larger input domains?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the alternative method for constructing the verification set, and how it can be scaled up for more complex tasks?
3. How do the authors plan to extend the proposed approach to other neural programming architectures, such as the Neural Turing Machine or the Differentiable Neural Computer?

**Minor comments**

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the NPI framework and how it is modified to incorporate recursion.
* The empirical results could be improved by providing more details on the training procedure, such as the number of training iterations and the learning rate.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the time and space complexity of the proposed approach.
