**Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"**

This paper introduces the concept of recursion as a key abstraction for enabling neural programming architectures to generalize robustly from small datasets. The authors propose a simple but powerful modification to the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework, training it on execution traces that explicitly contain recursive calls. A significant contribution is the accompanying verification procedure, which allows for provable guarantees of perfect generalization by testing a finite set of base cases and reduction rules. The empirical results are impressive, demonstrating that this method enables the NPI to perfectly learn algorithms like Bubblesort and Quicksort, tasks on which non-recursive variants fail to generalize.

I recommend **Accepting** this paper. The primary reasons are: 1) The elegant and effective solution of using recursive traces to directly address the critical problem of out-of-domain generalization in program synthesis. 2) The introduction of a formal verification framework for neural programs, which represents a novel and important conceptual step for the field.

The paper’s main strength lies in its simplicity. By only altering the training data, the authors achieve a dramatic improvement in generalization, suggesting that providing the right structural priors is crucial. This contrasts sharply with efforts focused solely on architectural innovation. Furthermore, the ability to formally prove the correctness of a learned neural program is a powerful idea. While prior work evaluates generalization empirically on increasingly complex inputs, this paper shows how recursion makes the problem of verification tractable, shifting the goalposts toward creating truly reliable learned systems.

To further strengthen the paper, I would encourage the authors to address the following points, which are intended as constructive feedback:
The reliance on full, correct execution traces is a significant assumption. Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of this approach? For instance, in tasks where perception and logic are intertwined (e.g., sorting MNIST digits), the trace effectively decouples these problems, potentially trivializing the learning task. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion on how this method fares when traces are noisy or incomplete.

I would also be interested in the authors' perspective on the boundaries of this method. Could you characterize a class of problems where execution traces might be available, but learning the correct recursive program would still be exceptionally difficult or fail entirely? Answering this would help clarify the scope and limitations of the contribution.
