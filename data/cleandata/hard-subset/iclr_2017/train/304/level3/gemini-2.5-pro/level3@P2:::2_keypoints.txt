- Proposes adding recursion explicitly to neural programming (NPI) to improve generalization across algorithms.
- Extends NPI to learn recursive versions of addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort by modifying training execution traces.
- Shows recursive NPI models learn underlying recursive structure and generalize perfectly to much larger inputs from few examples.
- Introduces a verification procedure that leverages recursion to provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for learned programs.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason: Simple, well-motivated idea—using recursion is an elegant way to enforce compositional, length-agnostic solutions.
- Key reason: Strong empirical results—recursive models dramatically outperform non-recursive ones and generalize from very small training sets.
- Paper is clear, builds on NPI notation, and persuasively argues recursion prevents learning spurious, input-length correlations.
- Empirical evaluation is thorough; comparisons (non-recursive, partially recursive, fully recursive) show full recursion is essential for perfect generalization.
- Demonstrates success on non-tail-recursive algorithm (quicksort), achieving 100% accuracy on complex inputs after training on few examples.
- Main weakness: Claim of “provably perfect generalization” is overstated—verification procedure construction is vague and may be manual or intractable for complex tasks.
- Limitation acknowledged: Verification is impractical for large or continuous state spaces, reducing the formal guarantee’s scope.
- Suggestion: Provide a concrete walkthrough of the verification set construction (e.g., LSHIFT in bubble sort) to clarify methodology.
- Suggestion: Discuss scalability of the verification procedure and how verification-set size grows with control-flow/state complexity.
- Future direction: Investigate methods for the model to discover recursion from non-recursive traces or I/O examples rather than requiring recursive traces.
- Questions for authors: How is the verification set constructed for complex tasks (topological sort/quicksort) and how confident can one be it covers all states?
- Question: Will recursive-training generalization benefits hold in domains without feasible formal verification (e.g., images)?
- Question: Will the authors release NPI implementation and experiment code to the community?
