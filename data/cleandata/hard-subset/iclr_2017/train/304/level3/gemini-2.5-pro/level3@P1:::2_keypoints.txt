- Proposes incorporating recursion into Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) training traces to improve generalization of neural program synthesis models.  
- Training on explicitly recursive execution traces yields perfect generalization to much more complex inputs than seen during training.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical results on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort), often with very few training examples.  
- Recursive calls reset the LSTM controller’s hidden state, enabling decomposition of large problems into repeatable, length-agnostic steps.  
- Non-recursive NPI models fail to generalize as input complexity grows, while fully recursive models achieve perfect accuracy.  
- Introduces a verification framework aiming for “provably perfect generalization” by validating base cases and recursive reduction steps.  
- Recursion makes verification tractable by reducing infinite execution traces to a finite set of state transitions to check.  
- Reviewer accepts the paper, citing the elegant solution to generalization and the novel verification concept.  
- Notes the verification method is an important step but has limitations and may not be a formal mathematical proof.  
- Suggests reframing “provably perfect generalization” as a verification framework rather than a formal proof to manage expectations.  
- Points out the verification approach is inadequate for continuous or high-dimensional input domains and should be discussed more prominently.  
- Praises clarity and presentation, and reusing NPI notation to make the work accessible.  
- Asks for clarification on constructing the verification set V and whether a systematic/automated minimal set can be derived.  
- Requests more analysis on why non-recursive NPI succeeds on some tasks (addition) but fails on others (bubble sort).  
- Encourages releasing the NPI implementation and experiment code to benefit the research community.
