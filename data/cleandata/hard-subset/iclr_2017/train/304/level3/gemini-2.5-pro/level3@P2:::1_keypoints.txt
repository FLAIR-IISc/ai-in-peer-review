- Proposal: incorporate recursion into neural program induction to improve generalization.  
- Implementation: augment Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) training with recursive execution traces rather than changing the architecture.  
- Tasks: evaluated on grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Empirical claim: recursive traces enable perfect generalization from very small training sets; non-recursive models fail as input complexity grows.  
- Contribution: a verification framework giving provable perfect generalization by checking a finite set of base cases and reduction rules.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: significant, consistent improvement in generalization via recursion.  
- Key reason 2: novelty of enabling formal, provable correctness for learned programs.  
- Support: well-motivated hypothesis that recursion prevents models from learning spurious correlations.  
- Support: strong head-to-head experiments showing data efficiency and 100% accuracy (e.g., quicksort from 4 traces).  
- Support: recursion makes formal verification tractable by decomposing infinite inputs into finite verification sets.  
- Limitation: approach depends on complete, correct execution traces — a strong supervision assumption that may not hold in many real-world settings.  
- Concern: providing traces can decouple perception from logic (e.g., sorting MNIST images), potentially trivializing program induction.  
- Concern: core innovation is trace re-formulation while NPI’s explicit stack may be especially suited; applicability to architectures without such stack is unclear.  
- Question: how difficult/manual is constructing verification sets, and can this be automated from formal program specifications?  
- Question: characterize problem classes where full execution traces exist but the method would still fail.  
- Question: since non-recursive NPI generalized perfectly on addition in experiments, when is recursion actually necessary versus merely enabling provability?
