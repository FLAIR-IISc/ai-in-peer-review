This paper tackles the critical problem of poor generalization in neural program induction. The authors propose that explicitly incorporating the principle of recursion is a key solution to this longstanding issue. By training a Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) model on recursive execution traces, they demonstrate a dramatic improvement in generalization and sample efficiency across four algorithmic tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, and the newly introduced topological sort and quicksort. The paper’s most significant contribution is a formal verification procedure, made tractable by the recursive structure, which allows them to provide a proof of perfect generalization for the learned programs. To our knowledge, this is the first such result in this domain.

**Decision: Accept**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper for publication. The work makes two outstanding contributions: (1) it provides a simple yet empirically powerful method for achieving strong generalization in neural program synthesis from very few examples, and (2) it introduces a novel verification framework that brings a much-needed level of formal rigor to a field that has largely relied on empirical performance metrics.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The empirical results are clear and compelling. The recursive models achieve perfect accuracy on complex, unseen inputs after being trained on a remarkably small number of simple execution traces. The direct comparison with non-recursive baselines on the same tasks convincingly highlights the superiority of the proposed approach, particularly on the more complex topological sort and quicksort algorithms. Furthermore, the theoretical contribution of "provably perfect generalization" is a landmark achievement. By leveraging the structure of recursion to decompose the problem, the authors have developed a finite verification process that can guarantee correctness over an infinite input domain. This moves the field beyond simply testing on longer inputs and toward providing formal guarantees, a crucial step for building reliable systems.

**Additional Feedback and Questions:**

The paper is excellent, and the following points are intended to help strengthen it further. The reliance on hand-crafted recursive traces is a key component of the method. I would appreciate more discussion on the practicality and potential challenges of creating these traces for more complex algorithms.

I would also be interested in the authors' answers to the following questions:
1.  Your verification procedure is a standout contribution. Could you further clarify the boundaries of this method? For what class of problems, beyond the four presented, do you foresee this procedure being applicable, and where might it break down?
2.  Does the NPI architecture’s stack-based call structure give it an inherent advantage for learning recursion compared to other architectures like Neural Turing Machines? Have you considered how recursion might be implemented in those frameworks?
