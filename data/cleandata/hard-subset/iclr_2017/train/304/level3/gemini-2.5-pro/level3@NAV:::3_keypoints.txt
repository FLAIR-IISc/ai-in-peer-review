- Problem tackled: poor generalization in neural program induction.
- Proposal: explicitly incorporate recursion into training to improve generalization.
- Method: train a Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) on recursive execution traces.
- Empirical results: dramatic improvement in generalization and sample efficiency on grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.
- Major contribution: a formal verification procedure made tractable by recursion that allows proof of perfect generalization.
- Novelty claim: first result providing provable perfect generalization in this domain.
- Recommendation: Accept — strongly recommended for publication.
- Key contributions summarized: (1) simple, empirically powerful method for strong generalization from few examples; (2) a novel verification framework adding formal rigor.
- Supporting evidence: recursive models achieve perfect accuracy on complex unseen inputs from very few traces and outperform non-recursive baselines, notably on topo sort and quicksort.
- Theoretical impact: recursion enables a finite verification process guaranteeing correctness over an infinite input domain, moving beyond empirical testing.
- Concern: method relies on hand-crafted recursive traces — need discussion of practicality for more complex algorithms.
- Questions for authors: clarify boundaries/applicability of the verification procedure and whether NPI’s stack-based call structure gives an inherent advantage over other architectures (e.g., Neural Turing Machines) for learning recursion.
