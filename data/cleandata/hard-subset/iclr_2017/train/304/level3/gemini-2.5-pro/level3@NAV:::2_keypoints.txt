- Introduces a small extension to NPIs by incorporating recursion to improve program synthesis.
- Addresses poor generalization of neural program models from few examples to complex unseen inputs.
- Reformulates training traces for tasks (addition, sorting) to be recursive, enabling learning of robust program structure.
- Demonstrates that recursion enables perfect generalization from very few training examples, outperforming non-recursive baselines.
- Paper is well-written and reuses NPI notation, improving readability.
- Decision: Accept.
- Main reasons: elegant/practical solution to generalization and strong empirical support.
- Recursion prevents spurious dependence on input length via LSTM hidden state by enforcing base cases and reduction rules.
- Experiments (bubble sort, quicksort) show non-recursive models fail on longer inputs while recursive models achieve perfect accuracy.
- Caution: "provably perfect generalization" is overstated; verification is an exhaustive check over manually constructed base cases, not a formal proof.
- Scalability concern: verification approach may not scale to continuous or more complex programs; recommend tempering claims and discussing limitations.
- Request: clarify whether source code and execution traces will be released for reproducibility.
