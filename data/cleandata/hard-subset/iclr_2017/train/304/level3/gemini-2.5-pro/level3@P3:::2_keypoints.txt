- Paper proposes adding recursion to neural program learning by training NPI on recursive execution traces.
- Demonstrated on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) with strong empirical gains.
- Recursive NPI models achieve perfect generalization from very few examples (e.g., quicksort from 4, topo sort from 6).
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason 1: Simple, elegant, and effective idea—recursion markedly improves generalization in program synthesis.
- Key reason 2: Strong practical impact — state-of-the-art generalization on non-trivial algorithms with minimal data.
- Reuse of original NPI notation improves readability; paper is well-written and accessible.
- Core contribution: guiding existing architectures with recursion rather than designing new models.
- Empirical comparisons with non-recursive and partially-recursive baselines convincingly isolate the benefit of recursion.
- Claim of “provably perfect generalization” is overstated — the verification procedure is exhaustive testing, not a formal mathematical proof.
- The verification depends on a hand-constructed finite set covering base cases and reductions; its completeness is critical and may be fragile.
- Verification approach appears limited to discrete, well-structured domains and likely intractable for continuous or high-dimensional inputs.
- Suggestion: Tone down “provable” language and use terms like “verified” or “exhaustive verification.”
- Suggestion: Provide a more detailed, algorithmic description of how the verification set V is constructed from a reference implementation.
- Suggestion: Analyze which control-flow paths in the verification set were unseen during training to quantify generalization.
- Question: Will the authors release their NPI re-implementation for reproducibility?
- Question: Was verification set construction manual or automated, and how can 73 graphs be argued sufficient to cover all observation sequences for topo sort?
