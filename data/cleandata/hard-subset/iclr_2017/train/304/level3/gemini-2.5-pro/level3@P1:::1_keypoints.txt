- Introduces recursion as a key architectural principle for neural program synthesis and trains NPI with recursive execution traces to learn algorithms (addition, bubble sort, topo sort, quicksort) that generalize to much larger inputs.

- Proposes a novel verification procedure: recursion lets you prove perfect generalization by checking a finite set of base cases and reduction rules.

- Decision: Accept.

- Reason 1 for accept: Simple, elegant modification (only changing training traces) that yields strong empirical generalization from few examples.

- Reason 2 for accept: Provable generalization is a significant conceptual advance toward trustworthy neural program synthesizers.

- Correctly diagnoses prior failures (spurious correlations like input-length dependence) and argues recursion prevents hidden-state dependence by resetting the controller via recursive calls.

- Empirical claim: Recursive NPIs achieve 100% accuracy on long test cases after training on a handful of short examples, outperforming non-recursive counterparts.

- Verification contribution: First tractable verification framework for neural programs based on recursion, raising evaluation standards in the field.

- Main limitation: Heavy reliance on full, correct execution traces as supervision limits general applicability.

- Concern: For perception+logic tasks (e.g., sorting MNIST digits), execution traces decouple and simplify the problem by isolating algorithmic logic from perceptual grounding.

- Suggestion: Authors should explicitly discuss assumptions and position the work as focusing on learning control-flow/logic rather than integrated perception+reasoning.

- Future direction: Need to reduce supervision—investigate whether models can discover recursive abstractions from non-recursive traces or from input-output examples alone.

- Question 1 for authors: What failure modes remain even with perfect traces—are there recursive programs NPI/LSTM might fail to learn due to optimization or architecture limits?

- Question 2 for authors: How can integrated perception+logic tasks be learned recursively without traces, and how would verification handle base cases that depend on fallible perceptual modules?
