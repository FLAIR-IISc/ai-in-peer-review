This paper introduces a small but highly effective extension to Neural Programming Interpreters (NPIs) by incorporating recursion. The authors address a critical limitation in existing neural program synthesis models: poor generalization from a small number of training examples to more complex, unseen inputs. By reformulating training traces for tasks like addition and sorting to be recursive, the model learns a more robust and general underlying program structure. The work compellingly demonstrates that this change enables perfect generalization from very few training examples, a significant improvement over prior non-recursive baselines. The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and laudably reuses notation from the original NPI paper, which greatly aids readability for those familiar with the literature.

**Decision: Accept**

My decision is based on two key reasons. First, the paper presents an elegant and practical solution to the important problem of generalization in neural program induction. Second, the empirical results are strong and clearly support the central claim that recursion enables learning more generalizable programs from sparse data.

The supporting arguments are clear. The authors show that by making a program's training traces explicitly recursive, the NPI model is no longer forced to learn spurious dependencies on input length via its LSTM hidden state. Instead, it learns to solve problems by breaking them down into simpler base cases and reduction rules, a fundamental concept in computer science. The experiments on bubble sort and quicksort, where the non-recursive models fail on longer inputs while the recursive ones achieve perfect accuracy, provide convincing evidence for the approach's effectiveness.

While the core contribution is strong, the claims around "provably perfect generalization" should be tempered. The proposed method is a verification procedure based on an exhaustive check of a manually constructed set of base cases and reductions, rather than a formal proof. This approach seems tractable for the discrete tasks presented but would likely not scale to problems with continuous input spaces. I would encourage the authors to be more precise with this terminology in a final version. Further discussion on the limitations of constructing this verification set for more complex programs would also strengthen the paper.

Finally, to aid in reproducibility and further research, could the authors clarify if they intend to release the source code and the execution traces used for training and verification?
