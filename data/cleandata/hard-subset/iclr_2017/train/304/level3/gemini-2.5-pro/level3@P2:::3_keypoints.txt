- Paper targets neural program architectures’ failure to generalize to more complex inputs by explicitly incorporating recursion into training.
- Builds on the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) and trains on recursive execution traces to learn algorithmic structure rather than input-size correlations.
- Validated on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort), with topo sort and quicksort as non-trivial new demonstrations.
- Key technical contribution: a formal verification procedure that uses recursion to construct a finite verification set covering base cases and reduction rules.
- Verification procedure enables formal proofs of perfect generalization to inputs of arbitrary complexity.
- Decision: Accept.
- Main reason 1: Elegant and effective solution producing strong empirical results and perfect generalization from few training examples.
- Main reason 2: Novel and important method for proving perfect generalization, raising evaluation rigor in neural program synthesis.
- Argument: Prior models overfit to training data (e.g., input length) because hidden state must carry long-term information; recursion avoids this.
- Mechanism: Resetting the LSTM state at recursive calls enforces self-contained, modular functions that generalize independent of sequence length.
- Empirical strength: Learns complex divide-and-conquer (quicksort) and graph algorithms (topological sort) from only 4–6 traces, showing high sample efficiency.
- Claim of novelty: First work (to reviewer’s knowledge) to provide formal guarantees for a learned neural program.
- Concern: Construction of the verification set V may require significant manual effort; needs clarification on automation and concrete examples.
- Concern: Method relies on full, correct recursive execution traces for supervision, which may not be available for complex problems; discuss partial-trace or RL alternatives.
- Question: Are there theoretical/practical limits to recursion complexity the NPI can learn, e.g., mutual recursion or many interdependent recursive functions?
- Questions for authors: How much of verification set construction is manual vs automated (e.g., for topological sort)? How sensitive is learning to the specific recursive trace structure? Can the idea extend beyond NPI or is the stack-based architecture essential?
