1. Summary and contributions
- This paper is interesting, clearly written, and easy to follow. It proposes a small but effective extension to Neural Programming Interpreters (NPI): making recursion explicit in the execution traces so the model learns recursive programs. Because NPI already has a call stack and resets hidden state at subprogram boundaries, the change is minimal yet non-trivial in practice.
- The key claim is that recursion both improves generalization from few execution traces and enables provable guarantees by reducing reasoning to finite base cases and reduction rules. The paper demonstrates this on four tasks—grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort—showing strong generalization with very small training sets and constructing verification suites that, when passed, imply perfect generalization for the recursive models.
- The work reuses the DeepMind NPI notation and interfaces, aiding readability and making the paper accessible to non-experts.
- The broader message is compelling: a well-chosen programming abstraction (recursion) makes neural program learning more practical, interpretable, and verifiable.

2. Decision
Accept. Reasons:
- A simple, principled change (recursive traces) yields large gains in data efficiency and out-of-distribution generalization across multiple algorithmic tasks, and supports a verification protocol.
- The paper is well motivated, carefully executed, and easy to read; it is likely to influence how future neural program architectures incorporate classical programming abstractions.

3. Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? The poor generalization and opacity of neural program learners, especially beyond the lengths/complexities seen in training. The paper targets both empirical generalization and the ability to reason about correctness.
- Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. It situates NPI among neural program learners (NTM, DNC, Neural GPU, etc.) and identifies why non-recursive traces entangle hidden-state dynamics with input length. Leveraging NPI’s call stack to enforce fresh hidden states across calls is a crisp argument for recursion as the right abstraction. The link between recursion and tractable verification (finite base cases and reductions) is compelling and well explained.
- Do the results support the claims?
  - Empirical generalization: Strong improvements on bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort with very few traces (e.g., 6 traces for topological sort; 4 for quicksort). The recursive models succeed at lengths/graph sizes where non-recursive or partially recursive versions fail. Addition also shows that recursion enables learning from extremely limited traces that then compose to long examples.
  - Verification: For addition, the verification set is analytically constructed; for the others, an observation-space coverage procedure yields finite verification sets (e.g., 73 graphs for topsort, one length-10 array for the bubble-sort variant, one 10-element array for quicksort). Passing these suites provides evidence of “provably perfect generalization” under the paper’s definition.
  - Scientific rigor: The methodology is sound, reuse of NPI helps reproducibility, and the benefits of recursion are isolated via partial vs full recursion ablations on bubble sort. That said, the generalization “proofs” hinge on correct and complete construction of the verification set V; only for addition is this derivation fully analytic.

4. Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Clarify the proof/verification story. The paper’s guarantees are best described as verification-by-coverage of a finite observation set, not formal proofs of global correctness. For three tasks, V is derived partly empirically; please articulate the conditions under which the V-construction is complete and how one can detect/avoid missing cases. A more formal semantics for the observation-to-observation mapping and a completeness argument (even if conservative) would strengthen the claims.
- Discuss scalability to continuous/large input spaces. The paper notes that for high-dimensional continuous inputs (e.g., visual/motor control, 3D car domains) the base/reduction space can be huge or infinite, making V intractable. Please elaborate on how recursion could still help (e.g., symbolic interfaces, discretization, equivalence classes, or property-based testing) and what verification guarantees remain possible.
- Robustness and ablations. Report variance across random seeds and architectures; sensitivity to number/quality of traces; and ablations on encoder features (e.g., exposing absolute values vs relational predicates). Provide training details (LSTM sizes, learning rates, early stopping).
- Discovery of recursion. The current approach supervises recursive structure via traces. A discussion or small experiment on whether the model can discover recursion (from partial/non-recursive traces) would be valuable, even if negative.
- Practicality and efficiency. Include runtime and memory costs vs non-recursive NPI; max recursion depth; effects of tail recursion optimization; termination threshold sensitivity (α=0.5).
- Comparisons. Briefly compare to other call-structured or modular neural systems (e.g., neural module networks, stack-augmented RNNs) and to curriculum-trained baselines tuned for longer inputs.

Questions for the authors
- Will you release the Keras NPI reimplementation, training/verification code, and trace generators? This would materially aid reproducibility and adoption.
- How do you ensure completeness of V for topsort and quicksort? Is there an automated procedure to detect uncovered observation transitions, or a bound on the size of V?
- How sensitive are results to the exact domain-specific encoders (e.g., exposing relational predicates vs absolute values)? Could you provide a small study?
- What is the success rate across random seeds for each task? Do failures correlate with particular subprograms (e.g., COMPSWAP vs STACK)?
- Can the approach handle mutual recursion or recursion over deeper algebraic data types (e.g., trees) without handcrafting additional primitives?
- For continuous inputs, do you envision integrating learned symbolic interfaces (e.g., learned perception modules that emit discrete symbols) to keep V finite?
- In quicksort, how crucial are the specialized MOVE_* primitives? Could the model learn equivalent pointer-setting behavior without them?
- Could you quantify the minimal verification set sizes theoretically (upper bounds) given a program’s control structure?

Overall, this paper shows how a small, well-founded change—explicit recursion—can materially improve the generalization and verifiability of neural program learners. I recommend acceptance.
