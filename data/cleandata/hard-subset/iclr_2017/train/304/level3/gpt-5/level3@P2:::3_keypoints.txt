- Introduces recursion as a first-class abstraction in NPI via recursive training traces, improving generalization and enabling provable verification.

- Demonstrates recursion on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, achieving generalization far beyond training with very small datasets.

- Adds topological sort and quicksort to the NPI literature and claims the first formal generalization guarantees for learned neural programs.

- Decision: Accept.

- Conceptual novelty: Explicit recursion is a simple, well-motivated fix for generalization and interpretability failures.

- Empirical strength: Substantial gains in generalization and sample efficiency across diverse tasks.

- Methodological strength: Introduces a clear verification methodology that yields provable guarantees when base cases and reductions are covered.

- Problem framing: Hypothesis that spurious dependencies in long iterative executions harm generalization; recursion mitigates this by resetting context.

- Implementation: Recursion implemented in NPI by self-calls that reset hidden state; change is supervisory (recursive traces) and orthogonal to core architecture.

- Positioning: Properly situated relative to prior neural programming models; prior work used curricula and lacked provable guarantees.

- Empirical evidence: Recursive training achieves 100% generalization where non-recursive versions fail; very few traces needed for some tasks.

- Verification construction: Defines a finite verification set V covering base cases and reductions and checks learned step outputs against a reference program.

- Verification rationale: Recursion bounds per-call trace length, making the state space finite and tractable for exhaustive verification over V.

- Nature of proof: Verification is exhaustive over V rather than symbolic parameter proof, but logically sound given reduction to finite cases.

- Limitation — supervision: Requires full supervision via recursive execution traces and human-designed subprogram sets and encoders.

- Limitation — verification scope: Constructing a complete V is non-trivial for complex or continuous domains and may not scale.

- Limitation — task scope: Experiments limited to algorithmic/symbolic tasks; applicability to perception-heavy inputs remains open.

- Requested clarification: Precisely state preconditions for “provably perfect generalization” (completeness of V, exact match, encoder/primitive faithfulness).

- Suggestion — automate V: Provide methods/pseudocode or tools to systematically derive the verification set V from program specifications.

- Ablation requests: Test hidden-state reset policies, compare partial vs full recursion across tasks, and report sensitivity to return threshold and LSTM size.

- Baseline requests: Compare to strong non-recursive NPI with curricula and other architectures (e.g., DNC) to isolate recursion benefits.

- Scalability concerns: Discuss/empirically characterize stack depth, tail-call optimizations, and non-tail recursion behavior (quicksort).

- Reproducibility: Release code and verification sets; move key verification and program-set details from appendix to main text.

- Encoder clarity: Make explicit which encoders are used in training vs verification and justify that encoder choices do not invalidate guarantees.

- Questions for authors: How is V completeness certified for toposort/quicksort? How robust is learning to noisy/partial traces? Do benefits transfer to other NPAs? What recursion depths/stack issues arise? Do different trace styles produce distinct programs? How to scale verification for large alphabets/continuous observations?
