- Introduces recursion as a first-class abstraction in Neural Programming Architectures (instantiated on NPI) to improve generalization and interpretability.
- Key claim: recursive execution (via recursive training traces and stack-based context isolation) drastically improves generalization and interpretability.
- Key claim: recursion reduces the effective input domain per component, enabling a finite verification procedure and provable guarantees on algorithmic tasks.
- Key claim: small training sets suffice when program semantics are expressed recursively.
- Demonstrated on four tasks—grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort—with 100% generalization on long inputs and large graphs.
- Proposes a verification methodology that covers base cases and reduction rules to support provable generalization.
- Minimal change to training traces combined with NPI’s call stack and LSTM reset yields significantly more reliable behavior.
- Decision: Accept — idea is elegant, practically meaningful, and the verification angle is novel and useful.
- Significance: addresses a common failure mode—length- and structure-dependent overfitting in neural program learners.
- Positioning: recursion is a natural mechanism for compositional generalization and aligns well with NPI’s substrate.
- Empirical support: recursive variants generalize where non-recursive and partially recursive do not, often with far fewer traces.
- Quicksort result shows approach works beyond trivial tail-recursive/local problems.
- Verification practicality: construction of a finite verification set V is feasible and small for the tested discrete domains (e.g., a single 10-element array for quicksort).
- Clarity: paper is well written with clear descriptions of environments, encoders, and trace transformations.
- Improvement: strengthen and clarify “provable” claims—make assumptions explicit and distinguish formal proof from heuristic verification where applicable.
- Improvement: discuss scope and limitations for continuous or large state spaces and propose strategies (symbolic abstraction, interval reasoning, stochastic guarantees).
- Improvement: address supervision demands—how to handle partial traces, IO-only supervision, or discovery of recursion automatically.
- Improvement: include stronger baselines and ablations (e.g., NTM/DNC/Neural GPU, NPI with curriculum/regularization) and ablate LSTM reset, max call depth, stack implementation, and trace choices.
- Improvement: justify bubble sort’s verification-time encoder modification and report whether the learned model generalizes under the original encoder at test time.
- Improvement: release code, trained models, and scripts for trace and verification-set generation to support reproducibility.
- Question: can the V construction procedure be formalized to guarantee completeness for the discrete tasks?
- Question: for bubble sort, how do encoder changes used during verification affect the claim that the learned model generalizes under the original encoder?
- Question: can V be derived automatically from a declarative program specification or from learned traces?
- Question: can training with partial or non-recursive traces still induce recursive behavior; what failure modes occur?
- Question: how easily does this approach transfer to other architectures (DNC/NTM/Neural GPU); what architectural prerequisites beyond call stack and hidden-state reset are needed?
- Question: how is recursion depth and stack overflow handled in practice; are there limits or tail-call optimizations, and what happens on adversarial inputs that push depth?
- Question: how sensitive are results to trace noise, encoding variations, alternative pivot schemes, and adversarial perturbations?
- Question: can you quantify sample efficiency gains versus non-recursive training and provide evidence that recursion narrows the hypothesis space (training curves, seed variance)?
- Question: will you release source code and verification sets, or at least detailed pseudocode and environment specifications for reproduction?
