1. Summary of contributions
This paper tackles the persistent problem of poor and hard-to-reason-about generalization in neural program induction by adding a small but principled abstraction—recursion—to the NPI framework. The authors show that training on recursive execution traces yields dramatic gains in sample efficiency and generalization on four canonical tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort), and propose a verification procedure that reduces generalization proofs to checking base cases and reduction rules. The paper is clear, well organized, and reuses the original NPI notation, which greatly aids readability for non-experts. The main message—that a small, non-trivial architectural/training change can make neural programming more practical and analyzable—is compelling.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (i) A simple, well-motivated extension that substantially improves generalization from few traces across diverse tasks; (ii) A credible, if scoped, path toward verifiable generalization that advances rigor in neural programming.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: The work squarely addresses generalization and verifiability, two recognized bottlenecks in neural program learning.
- Motivation and placement: The paper is well positioned relative to NPI, DNC/NTM, Neural GPU, etc., and explains why recursion should reduce spurious length dependencies and hidden-state entanglement.
- Evidence: Strong empirical results show perfect or near-perfect generalization on longer inputs and more complex graphs/arrays, with very small training sets. Ablations (non-recursive vs partially vs fully recursive traces) are informative.
- Rigor: The verification framing (base cases + reductions) is sensible for discrete, finitely factored environments. However, parts of the “provably perfect generalization” claim rest on constructing a complete set V; the procedure currently relies on careful manual reasoning and problem-specific encodings, which weakens generality. The approach likely does not scale to continuous or high-dimensional input spaces (e.g., 3D control), as the authors note.

4. Feedback and questions to improve the paper
- Clarify and tighten the verification claims: precisely define assumptions ensuring V is complete; quantify effort to construct V; report coverage statistics and failure modes if V is incomplete.
- Automate where possible: can V be derived mechanically from a specification or reference interpreter? Any guarantees on minimality/complexity of V?
- Report resource metrics: stack depth distributions, inference latency/memory vs non-recursive NPI, and sensitivity to α and LSTM size.
- Discuss supervision: how robust is learning to partially recursive or noisy traces? Any progress toward discovering recursion without explicit traces?
- External validity: comment on adapting the method to continuous/perceptual domains and why that may be intractable; outline alternatives (symbolic interfaces, discretization).
- Will you release code and verification scripts to enable reproduction?

Specific questions:
- How do you detect and prevent spurious recursive calls that still pass the verification set but fail off-distribution?
- Can you quantify the size of V and SV per task and relate it to task primitives and observation dimensionality?
- How sensitive are results to the exact domain encoders (e.g., exposing comparisons vs values in bubble sort)?
- For quicksort (non-tail recursion), what training instabilities did you observe and how were they mitigated?
