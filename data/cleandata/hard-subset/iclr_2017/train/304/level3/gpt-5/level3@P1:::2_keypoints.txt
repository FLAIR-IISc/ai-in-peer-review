- Core claim: Explicitly incorporating recursion into neural program architectures yields robust generalization with minimal changes to NPI training traces.  
- Verification idea: A procedure based on base cases and reduction rules gives provable perfect generalization if the verification set covers all reachable configurations.  
- Empirical scope: Experiments on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort show large generalization gains from few traces.  
- Full recursion result: Fully recursive traces produce 100% accuracy on arbitrarily long inputs in these discrete tasks.  
- Partial recursion insight: Partial recursion (e.g., bubble sort) can be diagnostic and insufficient unless all relevant components are made recursive.  
- Practicality: The change is small but impactful due to NPI’s per-call hidden-state reset and call-stack support, improving interpretability and decomposition.  
- Addition finding: Recursive model generalizes from extremely few examples (e.g., five 1-digit sums) via composition; non-recursive models fail.  
- Bubble sort finding: Full recursion (making BSTEP and LSHIFT recursive) is necessary for perfect generalization; partial recursion gives modest gains.  
- Topological sort finding: Recursive model generalizes from very few traces (even one in an experiment) to much larger graphs; non-recursive fails.  
- Quicksort finding: Recursive NPI handles non-tail recursion and non-local pointer movement, generalizing beyond non-recursive limits.  
- Verification construction: Defining V (reachable per-call observation sequences) and a finite SV that covers V is central to the provable guarantee; SV is small in discrete domains.  
- Acceptance decision: Accept — reasons are the principled idea (recursion) improving generalization/interpretability and a credible verification pathway backed by experiments.  
- Request for clarity: The provable generalization section needs a more formal/algorithmic description of constructing V (pseudocode/figure recommended).  
- Complexity discussion: Authors should discuss complexity of V relative to observation cardinality and program structure, with bounds or empirical sizes.  
- Automation suggestion: Consider automating V generation from a formal specification of the reference program and methods to detect coverage gaps.  
- Failure-mode analysis: Provide ablations to quantify which recursive components matter, and analyze sensitivity to return-threshold α and stack depth limits.  
- Supervision reduction: Explore learning recursion from partial/non-recursive traces (hints, constraints, or search) to reduce supervision cost.  
- Broader applicability: Discuss adding recursion to other architectures (NTM/DNC/Neural GPU) and potential challenges/benefits.  
- Scalability caveat: Acknowledge impracticality of constructing V for high-dimensional/continuous observations and outline approximate verification strategies (abstraction/probabilistic).  
- Reproducibility: Release Keras implementation and verification scripts to aid community adoption.  
- Q1: Will you release code for recursive trace generators and verification tooling?

- Q5: Can the approach tolerate noisy or partial traces; have experiments trained with only top-level recursion supervision?  
- Q6: For large/continuous domains, which approximate verification abstractions (symbolic, interval, SMT) seem most promising?  
- Q7: How does recursive model time/space complexity at inference compare to non-recursive (wall-clock, calls, memory) for long inputs?  
- Q8: In quicksort, how critical are specific environment observations (comparators, stack peeks); could weaker encoders succeed?
