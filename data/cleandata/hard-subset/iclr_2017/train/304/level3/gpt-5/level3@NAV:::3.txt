1. Summary of contributions
The paper tackles the long-standing problem of poor out-of-distribution generalization in neural program induction. It proposes a simple but powerful idea: explicitly incorporate recursion into neural programming architectures by training on recursive execution traces, and exploit the call-stack reset semantics to bound per-call reasoning. Instantiated on the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI), the approach yields striking gains in sample complexity and generalization across four tasks: addition, bubble sort, and two new tasks (topological sort and quicksort). Beyond empirical results, the paper introduces a verification procedure that constructs a finite set V covering base cases and reduction rules, and proves that agreement with a reference program on V implies perfect generalization. To my knowledge, this is the first formal generalization guarantee for learned neural programs.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (a) Clear, novel contribution—turning recursion into a first-class training signal for neural program induction and showing it decisively improves generalization and sample efficiency. (b) A rigorous verification framework that upgrades empirical claims to provable guarantees.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem: Generalization beyond training lengths/sizes is the central challenge in neural programming; prior models (e.g., NPI, DNC, Neural GPU) degrade with size and lack provable guarantees.
- Motivation and positioning: Recursion aligns with how algorithms are specified and analyzed; resetting controller state at each call reduces the effective state space. The paper engages prior work fairly and pinpoints why length-based curricula fail.
- Evidence: Strong empirical results with tiny training sets (e.g., addition from 5 one-digit traces; bubblesort from 100 traces; topological sort from 6 traces to 120 nodes; quicksort from 4 traces), and clean ablation on bubblesort (non-recursive vs partial vs full recursion). The verification method is principled and, for these tasks, convincingly complete.

4. Feedback for improvement (helpful, not decision-critical) and questions
- Clarify the formal status of the “proof”: precisely state assumptions for constructing V, its completeness, and termination/stack-depth guarantees (especially for non–tail recursion in quicksort).
- Provide an ablation on the importance of call-state reset vs recursion, and sensitivity to encoder design (e.g., exposing comparisons vs raw values in bubblesort).
- Discuss scalability limits of the verification approach when V is large or continuous (acknowledged), and any heuristics to approximate V with guarantees.
- Compare against stronger baselines (e.g., Neural GPU variants, recent differentiable stacks) on the same tasks.
- Report training stability, variance over seeds, and failure modes when traces are noisy/partial; can partial/non-recursive traces still induce recursion?
- Release code and verification scripts to encourage adoption.

Questions:
- How is V proven complete for each task? Can you formalize the construction procedure and its correctness in the main text?
- What bounds exist on recursion depth and stack growth during inference for quicksort? Any guardrails to prevent divergence?
- How robust are results to removing hand-engineered observations (e.g., exposing ≤ instead of values)?
- Does the approach extend to other architectures (e.g., DNC) without trace supervision, and what modifications are needed?
