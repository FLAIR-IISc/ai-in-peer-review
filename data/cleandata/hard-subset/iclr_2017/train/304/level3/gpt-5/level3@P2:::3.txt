Summary of contributions
The paper tackles the poor and brittle generalization of neural program induction models by explicitly introducing recursion as a first-class abstraction. Concretely, it augments the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) with recursive training traces and shows that recursion (i) dramatically improves generalization on algorithmic tasks and (ii) enables a verification procedure that provides provable guarantees of perfect generalization. The authors demonstrate the idea on four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. With very small training sets, the recursive versions generalize to inputs far beyond the training regime (e.g., thousands of digits for addition, long arrays for sorting, large DAGs), and the paper introduces a principled method to construct a finite verification set that covers all base cases and reductions. The work adds two new tasks to the NPI literature (topological sort and quicksort) and provides what appears to be the first formal generalization guarantees for learned neural programs.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Conceptual novelty and importance: making recursion explicit in neural program architectures is a simple, well-motivated idea that addresses a core failure mode in generalization and interpretability.
- Strong empirical and methodological contributions: substantial gains in generalization and sample efficiency across diverse tasks, plus a clear verification methodology that yields provable guarantees when base cases and reductions are covered.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem and approach
- The paper squarely addresses the central question: why do neural program learners fail to generalize, and how can we make generalization provable? The hypothesis—that spurious dependencies arise in long, iterative executions—and the remedy—resetting context via recursive calls—are well-motivated both intuitively and from programming language principles.
- The approach is cleanly instantiated in NPI: recursion is implemented by having programs call themselves while resetting hidden state across calls (already supported by NPI’s call stack). The only required change is to the supervision (recursive traces), making the contribution orthogonal to the core architecture and potentially portable to other NPAs.

2) Positioning in the literature
- The paper situates itself with relevant neural programming models (NTM/DNC, Neural GPU, Pointer Nets, NRAM, NPI). It highlights that prior work focused on length generalization via curricula without explicit recursion and without provable guarantees. To the best of my knowledge, the claim of being first to give provable generalization for neural programs is accurate and significant.

3) Evidence and rigor
- Empirical: Across four tasks, recursive training leads to 100% generalization in regimes where non-recursive counterparts degrade, including a partially recursive ablation on bubble sort. The sample efficiency is impressive (e.g., 4–6 traces for quicksort/toposort).
- Verification: The paper defines a verification set V of step input sequences covering base cases and reduction rules and checks that the learned model’s step outputs match a reference program. This is convincing for the considered tasks because recursion bounds the length of per-call traces and reduces the state space to a finite, tractable set. The construction of V is explained, and for some tasks, its completeness is argued analytically (addition) or via systematic coverage reasoning (others). While the “proof” is a verification-by-exhaustion over V rather than a symbolic proof about the learned parameters, the logical structure is sound given the reduction to finite cases.

4) Scope and limitations
- The approach currently relies on full supervision via execution traces specifically crafted to be recursive. This is strong supervision and requires human-designed subprogram sets and domain encoders.
- The verification method depends critically on correctly and completely constructing V; this is non-trivial for complex domains and may not scale to tasks with large or continuous state spaces (as the authors note).
- Tasks are algorithmic and symbolic. Extending to perception-heavy inputs is an open question.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not decisive for acceptance)
- Clarify the guarantees: state precisely the preconditions under which “provably perfect generalization” holds (i.e., completeness of V, exact match to P on V, and faithfulness of the encoder/primitive operations to the reference semantics).
- Automating V: provide more detail on how one could systematically or automatically derive V from the program specification, and include pseudocode or a tool to generate V and a corresponding minimal verification set.
- Ablations:
  - Vary the hidden-state reset policy to directly test the hypothesis that spurious dependencies in long unrolled traces drive failures.
  - Compare partial vs full recursion across all tasks to assess how missing recursive calls affect failure modes.
  - Report sensitivity to the return threshold α and to LSTM size.
- Baselines: Where feasible, include comparisons to a strong non-recursive NPI with extensive curriculum training and to other architectures (e.g., DNC) on the new tasks, to isolate the benefit of recursion vs data scaling.
- Scalability: Discuss and, if possible, empirically characterize stack depth, tail-call optimization effects, and failure behaviors for non-tail recursion (quicksort).
- Clarity and reproducibility: Release code and the verification sets; fix minor reference formatting issues and move key details from the appendix into the main text (especially the verification construction and program sets).
- Encoder choices: Note that for bubble sort verification, the encoder exposing pairwise comparisons changes the observable state space. Make explicit which encoders are used in training vs verification and why this does not affect the validity of the guarantee.

Questions for the authors
- Completeness of V: For topological sort and quicksort, how do you certify that V is complete? Can you provide a formal argument or an algorithm that terminates with proof of coverage, beyond empirical saturation?
- Robustness to imperfect traces: How sensitive is learning to trace noise or partial traces? Can the model still discover recursion when only some calls are annotated as recursive or when supervising only with IO pairs?
- Generality beyond NPI: Have you attempted to incorporate recursion into other NPAs (e.g., DNC with a learned call/return interface)? Any empirical evidence that the benefits transfer?
- Non-tail recursion limits: In quicksort, what is the maximum observed recursion depth during training/testing, and did you encounter stack or stability issues? Is any form of call-stack regularization used?
- Ambiguity and overfitting: Do different recursive trace styles lead to distinct, functionally equivalent programs? If so, does verification still pass, and is there a preference bias?
- Verification at scale: For tasks with large alphabets or continuous observations, what strategies do you envision to avoid an explosion in V (e.g., symbolic abstractions, contracts, or SMT-backed reasoning)?

Overall, this is a timely, clear, and impactful paper that brings a principled PL-inspired abstraction into neural program induction and backs it with both strong empirical results and a verification methodology. I recommend acceptance.
