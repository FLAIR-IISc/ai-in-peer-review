- Proposes a small, effective extension to NPI: make recursion explicit in execution traces so models learn recursive programs.  
- Change leverages NPI’s call stack and hidden-state resets; minimal but non-trivial in practice.  
- Main claim: recursion improves generalization from few traces and enables provable guarantees via finite base cases and reductions.  
- Demonstrated on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topsort, quicksort) with strong generalization from very small training sets.  
- Constructs verification suites that, when passed, imply perfect generalization for the recursive models.  
- Reuses DeepMind NPI notation/interfaces, improving readability and accessibility.  
- Broader message: programming abstractions like recursion make neural program learning more practical, interpretable, and verifiable.  
- Decision: Accept — simple, principled change yields large gains in data efficiency, OOD generalization, and supports verification.  
- Paper is well motivated, carefully executed, easy to read, and likely influential for future architectures.  
- Problem targeted: poor generalization and opacity of neural program learners beyond training lengths/complexities.  
- Motivation/placement: clearly situated among neural program learners and argues why recursion prevents length-entangled hidden dynamics.  
- Empirical results: major improvements on bubble sort, topsort, quicksort (e.g., 6 traces for topsort, 4 for quicksort); addition composes to long examples.  
- Verification results: addition has analytic verification; others use observation-space coverage to produce finite verification sets (e.g., 73 graphs for topsort).  
- Caveat: verification proofs depend on correct/complete construction of verification set V; only addition is fully analytic.  
- Scientific rigor: methodology is sound, reproducible via NPI reuse, and ablations isolate benefits of recursion (partial vs full).  
- Clarify proof/verification story: describe verification-by-coverage, conditions for V completeness, and provide formal semantics/completeness arguments.  
- Discuss scalability: address large/continuous input spaces where V may be intractable and propose methods (symbolic interfaces, discretization, equivalence classes).  
- Report robustness/ablations: variance across seeds, sensitivity to number/quality of traces, encoder feature ablations, and full training details.  
- Explore discovery of recursion: discuss or experiment whether recursion can be learned from partial/non-recursive traces.  
- Practicality/efficiency: report runtime/memory vs non-recursive NPI, max recursion depth, tail-call effects, and termination threshold sensitivity.  
- Comparisons: relate to other call-structured/modular systems (neural module nets, stack-RNNs) and curriculum-trained baselines.  
- Reproducibility question: will the Keras NPI reimplementation, training/verification code, and trace generators be released?  
- Verification completeness question: how is V ensured complete for topsort/quicksort; is there automated detection of uncovered transitions or bounds on V size?  
- Encoder sensitivity question: how sensitive are results to domain-specific encoders (relational predicates vs absolute values)?  
- Randomness question: what is the success rate across random seeds and do failures correlate with particular subprograms?  
- Generality question: can the approach handle mutual recursion or recursion over complex ADTs (e.g., trees) without extra primitives?  
- Continuous input question: do authors envision learned symbolic interfaces to keep V finite for continuous domains?  
- Primitives question: in quicksort, how crucial are MOVE_* primitives — could pointer behavior be learned without them?  
- Theoretical question: can minimal verification set sizes be upper-bounded given a program’s control structure?
