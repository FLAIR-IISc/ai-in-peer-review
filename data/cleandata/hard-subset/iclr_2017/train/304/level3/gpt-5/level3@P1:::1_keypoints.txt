- Proposes explicitly incorporating recursion into Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) by changing training execution traces to include recursive calls.
- Claims recursion improves out-of-distribution generalization and enables verifiable correctness guarantees without changing the core architecture.
- Demonstrates on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) with strong generalization from very small training sets.
- Provides a verification procedure: cover base cases and reductions to build a finite verification set V whose matching implies perfect generalization.
- Argues recursion shrinks the effective state space via call-frame resets and removes spurious length dependencies, making behavior interpretable.
- Decision: Accept — idea is novel, well-motivated, easy to implement, with convincing empirical and verification evidence.
- Core idea is conceptually crisp and directly addresses generalization failures in neural program induction.
- Verification angle is original and valuable, yielding guarantees not possible in non-recursive formulations.
- Paper correctly diagnoses hidden-state entanglement across long iterations as a generalization failure mode and leverages call-stack isolation.
- Situates work relative to prior neural program models (NTM/DNC/Neural GPU/Pointer Networks/NPI) and experimentally contrasts with non-recursive NPI.
- Method is minimal intervention: only alter traces to include tail and non-tail recursion; domain encoders and call-stack semantics are explained.
- Empirically, recursive models generalize to very long inputs (thousands of digits for addition, large arrays/DAGs for sorting/toposort) from tiny training sets.
- Ablations (partial vs full recursion) support the central claim that full recursion is necessary in some tasks (e.g., bubble sort).
- Verification set V construction via base/reduction coverage is carefully argued and applied per domain, with exact trace matching to a reference program.
- Caveat: Heavy supervision via full execution traces narrows scope; guarantees depend on a known reference program and V.
- Caveat: Domain encoders sometimes leak privileged signals (e.g., comparisons, graph colors), decoupling perception from algorithmic reasoning.
- Caveat: Constructing V and automating verification is program-specific and non-trivial; completeness checks are challenging.
- Suggested clarification: Formalize assumptions behind “provably perfect generalization” and emphasize dependence on correctness/completeness of V and reference program P.
- Suggested experiments: More systematic ablations on training set size, trace noise, partial recursion, and less privileged encoders.
- Suggested comparisons: Evaluate non-NPI architectures with recursive traces or discuss adding recursion to them.
- Suggest automating verification: propose paths like symbolic execution or abstract interpretation and discuss complexity bounds.
- Suggest perception-program integration experiments using raw inputs (e.g., images) to study how recursion interacts with learned perception.
- Practical suggestions: discuss stack depth, tail-call optimization, parameter counts, training time, and inference speed impacts of recursion.
- Questions: Sensitivity of training to recursive trace design and whether recursion can be induced from non-recursive traces or weak supervision.
- Questions: Quantify sample efficiency between recursive and non-recursive variants, robustness to trace noise, completeness guarantees for empirically covered V, and encoder information leakage.
