

The paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures to address the problem of poor generalizability in neural programs. The authors demonstrate that by enabling recursion in the Neural Programmer-Interpreter framework, they can achieve perfect generalization from minimal training data and provide provable guarantees about the neural program's behavior for tasks including grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. The key insight is that recursion decomposes problems into smaller pieces, making it tractable to prove guarantees about the overall system's behavior without exhaustive testing of all possible inputs.

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution by introducing recursion as a key abstraction for neural programming, enabling provable generalization and providing a verification procedure for neural programs. The empirical results demonstrate clear improvements over non-recursive approaches across multiple tasks.

The paper's contribution is novel and well-motivated. The authors correctly identify that existing neural programming approaches fail to generalize because they learn spurious dependencies rather than true program semantics, and they convincingly argue that recursion is a necessary abstraction to address this limitation. The verification procedure they propose for proving perfect generalization represents a substantial advance, as it provides a method to ensure neural programs behave correctly on unseen inputs without exhaustive testing.

The empirical results are robust and well-supported. The recursive neural programs achieve perfect generalization on all tasks tested, while non-recursive versions fail for inputs of moderate complexity. The training data requirements are minimal (e.g., 200 traces for addition), which is impressive given the complexity of the tasks. The verification procedure successfully demonstrates that the recursive programs generalize perfectly by matching the behavior of the target program on the verification set, which covers all base cases and reduction rules.

The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of the limitations of the verification approach. While the authors acknowledge that the verification procedure may become infeasible for tasks with very large input domains, it would be valuable to explore potential extensions or alternative verification methods for more complex domains. The paper should also clarify how the verification set is constructed for tasks where analytical construction is difficult, as the authors mention they randomly generate input candidates to cover base cases and reduction rules.

The paper could strengthen its claims by providing more evidence that the recursive neural programs have learned the true program semantics rather than simply memorizing training examples. A more detailed analysis of the training dynamics, such as how the learning process differs between recursive and non-recursive formulations, would be valuable.

The paper would be improved by a more comprehensive comparison with other techniques for improving generalization in neural programs, such as curriculum learning or architectural modifications. While the paper briefly mentions curriculum learning, a more thorough comparison would help contextualize the contribution.

The verification procedure is the paper's most significant contribution, but the authors should clarify the conditions under which it can be applied and provide stronger guarantees about the completeness of the verification set. It would also be helpful to discuss how the verification procedure could be extended to handle more complex tasks.

Overall, the paper makes a strong contribution to the field of neural programming by introducing recursion as a key abstraction for generalizability and providing a verification procedure for neural programs. The results are compelling, the approach is well-motivated, and the verification framework represents an important advance for ensuring neural programs behave correctly on unseen inputs. The paper should be accepted for ICLR 2017.
