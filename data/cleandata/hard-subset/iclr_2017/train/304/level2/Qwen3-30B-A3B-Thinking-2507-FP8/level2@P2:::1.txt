

# Review

**Summary of Contributions**: The paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures to improve generalizability and enable provable guarantees about program behavior. The authors implement recursion in the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework for four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort), demonstrating that recursive neural programs achieve perfect generalization from very small training sets (as few as 4 examples for quicksort) and can be verified to generalize perfectly through a finite set of base cases and reduction rules.

**Decision**: Reject. The paper makes an interesting proposal but fails to provide sufficient evidence that recursion is necessary for the observed improvements, and the claims about "provably perfect generalization" are overstated for neural programs in general.

**Supporting Arguments**:
1. The paper's core claim—that recursion is "necessary" for neural programs to generalize—lacks proper justification. The authors don't provide evidence that non-recursive neural programs cannot achieve similar generalization with different architectures or training procedures. They simply compare their recursive approach to non-recursive versions of the same architecture, which doesn't prove that recursion is the essential factor.

2. The "provably perfect generalization" claim is misleading. The verification procedure only works for the specific, carefully constructed tasks with very limited input domains (e.g., addition of numbers with digits 0-9, small arrays of 0-9 values). The paper acknowledges this limitation (Section 4.2) but doesn't sufficiently emphasize that this approach is infeasible for realistic programming tasks with large input spaces. The verification sets they use (e.g., 20,181 addition problems) are still finite and task-specific, not generalizable to real-world programming problems.

3. The paper doesn't address whether the recursive programs are actually learning the true program semantics. They hypothesize that non-recursive programs learn "spurious dependencies," but provide no evidence beyond the fact that they fail on longer inputs. The recursive programs could simply be learning a different pattern that happens to work for the verification set, not necessarily the underlying program semantics.

4. The paper doesn't sufficiently compare with alternative approaches that might achieve similar results without explicit recursion (e.g., curriculum learning with more sophisticated input scheduling, or different model architectures). The claim that "this is the first time one can provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs" is questionable given that previous work (e.g., Graves et al. 2016) has demonstrated strong generalization on specific tasks, even if not with formal verification.

**Additional Feedback**:
1. The paper would benefit from a clearer theoretical justification for why recursion should improve generalization, rather than just empirical demonstrations. What specific properties of recursive programs make them more generalizable?

2. The authors should clarify the scope of their "provably perfect generalization" claim. It's crucial to specify that this only applies to the specific tasks with small, discrete input domains they consider, and not to neural programs in general.

3. The paper should provide more insight into why the recursive programs achieve better generalization. Is it the recursive structure itself, or simply the different training traces they use? A controlled experiment where the same training traces are used for both recursive and non-recursive versions would help isolate the effect of recursion.

4. The verification procedure described in Section 3.3 is interesting but limited. The authors should discuss how their approach might scale to more complex tasks, or acknowledge its limitations for real-world programming problems.

**Questions for Authors**:
1. The paper claims recursion is "necessary" for neural programs to generalize. Can you provide evidence that non-recursive neural programs cannot achieve similar generalization on these tasks with different architectures or training procedures?

2. How would you modify your verification procedure to handle a task like sorting arbitrary strings (not just digits 0-9), where the input space is much larger and more diverse?

3. The verification set for addition contains 20,181 examples. How does this compare to the size of the training set (200 examples)? Does the model's performance on the verification set depend on having seen similar patterns during training, rather than truly learning the underlying semantics?
