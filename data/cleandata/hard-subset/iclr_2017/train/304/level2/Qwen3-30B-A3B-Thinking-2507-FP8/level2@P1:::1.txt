

Review

This paper proposes a novel approach to improving generalization in neural programming architectures by incorporating recursion, a fundamental concept in programming languages. The authors argue that current neural programming models suffer from poor generalization beyond a certain input complexity due to spurious dependencies on training examples rather than learning the true program semantics. They demonstrate that by implementing recursion in the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework, they can achieve perfect generalization on four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort) from very small training sets. The key contribution is showing that recursion enables provable guarantees about neural programs' behavior through verification of base cases and reduction rules.

Decision: Accept

Key reasons: The paper makes a compelling contribution by demonstrating that recursion can enable provable perfect generalization for neural programs, which is a significant advancement in the field. The empirical results across four diverse tasks are convincing, showing that recursive programs achieve perfect generalization from minimal training data, while non-recursive versions fail on longer inputs. The verification procedure for proving generalization is novel and well-justified.

Supporting arguments: The paper's core claim is well-supported. For each task, the authors show that recursive programs outperform non-recursive ones in generalization, and they provide a clear verification procedure to prove perfect generalization. The experimental setup is sound, with careful attention to the training data size and verification set construction. The recursive formulations for each task are well-motivated and implemented consistently with the NPI framework.

The paper's main contribution is the conceptual insight that recursion is necessary for neural programs to generalize robustly, and they successfully demonstrate this through multiple examples. The verification procedure is particularly noteworthy as it provides a method to formally prove perfect generalization, which has not been done before for neural programs. The paper's approach is principled, as they show how recursion reduces problem complexity and makes it tractable to verify behavior.

Additional feedback: While the paper is strong overall, I suggest the authors could strengthen the discussion of related work. They mention curriculum learning but don't provide a direct comparison with other generalization techniques. A brief comparison with other methods for improving neural program generalization would help situate their contribution more clearly.

The paper could also benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of their verification procedure. While they acknowledge that it may not scale to tasks with infinite base cases (like MNIST or speech), it would be helpful to discuss potential directions for addressing this limitation. Additionally, the paper states that "the training set can often be considerably smaller than the verification set," but it would be useful to quantify this relationship for each task.

The authors should clarify why they needed to modify the domain-specific encoder for bubble sort (as mentioned in Section A.6) and how this affects the generality of their approach. This modification was necessary for verification but might limit the applicability of their method to other tasks.

Questions for authors:
1. For tasks like quicksort that require non-tail recursion, how would you modify the verification procedure to handle the additional complexity?
2. The paper mentions that "the training set can often be considerably smaller than the verification set." Could you provide specific numbers for the ratio between training and verification set sizes for each task?
3. How do you envision extending this approach to handle tasks where the verification set might be prohibitively large (e.g., problems with continuous input spaces)?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to neural programming. The idea of incorporating recursion to enable provable generalization is novel and well-executed, with compelling empirical results across multiple tasks. The verification procedure is particularly promising and could lead to a new paradigm in neural program verification. The paper is well-written and clearly presents its contributions and results. I believe this work will be valuable to the community and should be accepted for publication.
