

This paper makes a compelling case for incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures to achieve provably perfect generalization. The authors demonstrate that by explicitly training neural programs to use recursion (via modified training traces in the Neural Programmer-Interpreter framework), their models achieve 100% accuracy across various input sizes for addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort tasks, outperforming non-recursive baselines that fail at moderate input complexities. Crucially, they provide a verification procedure that proves perfect generalization by testing only finite base cases and reduction rules, which is a significant theoretical contribution to the field.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a strong, novel contribution by demonstrating how recursion enables provable generalization in neural programs—a capability that has been lacking in prior work. The empirical results are convincing, showing that recursive programs generalize perfectly from very small training sets (as few as 4 examples for quicksort), while non-recursive versions fail even on moderately complex inputs. The theoretical justification for why recursion enables this provable generalization is sound and well-motivated.

The verification procedure is particularly noteworthy as it provides a method for establishing formal guarantees about neural program behavior, which is a major advancement. While the approach is limited to tasks with finite base cases (as acknowledged by the authors), the framework they establish is general and could be extended to other domains.

For improvement, I suggest the authors:
1) More explicitly discuss the limitations of their verification procedure for tasks with infinite or very large state spaces (e.g., image processing tasks).
2) Provide a more detailed comparison of computational complexity between recursive and non-recursive approaches.
3) Explore whether the training traces could be generated automatically rather than manually constructed, as this would increase the practical applicability of their method.

The paper makes a significant contribution to neural programming by establishing a new paradigm for achieving provable generalization through recursion, which should be of great interest to the conference community.
