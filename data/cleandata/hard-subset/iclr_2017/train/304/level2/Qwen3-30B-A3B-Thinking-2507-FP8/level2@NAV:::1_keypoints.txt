- Paper shows incorporating recursion into neural programs yields provably perfect generalization.
- Training neural programs with recursive traces (in NPI framework) achieves 100% accuracy across input sizes for addition, bubble sort, topo sort, and quicksort.
- Recursive models outperform non-recursive baselines that fail on moderate input complexities.
- Authors provide a verification procedure proving perfect generalization by testing finite base cases and reduction rules.
- Theoretical justification for why recursion enables provable generalization is sound and well-motivated.
- Empirical results are convincing: recursive programs generalize from very small training sets (e.g., 4 examples for quicksort).
- Verification procedure is a significant advance for providing formal guarantees about neural program behavior.
- Method is limited to tasks with finite base cases, a limitation the authors acknowledge.
- Suggestion: more explicitly discuss limitations for tasks with infinite or very large state spaces (e.g., image processing).
- Suggestion: provide a detailed comparison of computational complexity between recursive and non-recursive approaches.
- Suggestion: explore automatic generation of recursive training traces to improve practical applicability.
- Overall recommendation: accept — significant, novel contribution establishing recursion as a path to provable generalization in neural programming.
