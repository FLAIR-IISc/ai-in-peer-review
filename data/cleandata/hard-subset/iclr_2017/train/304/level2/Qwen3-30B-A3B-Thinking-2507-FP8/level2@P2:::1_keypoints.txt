- Summary: Proposes adding recursion to Neural Programmer-Interpreter for four tasks, claiming perfect generalization from tiny training sets and verification via base cases and reduction rules.

- Decision: Reject — interesting idea but insufficient evidence and overstated claims about provable perfection.

- Supporting: Claim that recursion is "necessary" is unjustified; comparisons only to non-recursive versions of same architecture.

- Supporting: "Provably perfect generalization" is misleading because verification was done on small, task-specific finite input domains, not realistic large spaces.

- Supporting: No evidence that recursive models learn true program semantics; they may fit verification sets via other patterns.

- Supporting: Lacks comparison to alternative methods/architectures (e.g., curriculum learning); novelty of provable guarantees is questionable given prior work.

- Feedback: Provide clearer theoretical justification for why recursion should improve generalization.

- Feedback: Clarify scope of "provably perfect generalization" — emphasize limits to small discrete domains.

- Feedback: Determine whether gains come from recursion itself or different training traces; run controlled experiments with identical traces.

- Feedback: Discuss scalability and limitations of the verification procedure for more complex, real-world tasks.

- Question: Can you show non-recursive neural programs cannot achieve similar generalization with different architectures or training methods?

- Question: How would you adapt the verification procedure to large diverse domains (e.g., sorting arbitrary strings)?

- Question: How does the 20,181-example verification set compare to the 200-example training set, and could verification performance stem from seeing similar patterns during training?
