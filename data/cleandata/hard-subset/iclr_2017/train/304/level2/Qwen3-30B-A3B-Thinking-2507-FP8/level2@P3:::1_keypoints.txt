- Proposes incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures to improve generalizability.
- Implements recursion in the Neural Programmer-Interpreter framework and demonstrates tasks including grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.
- Key insight: recursion decomposes problems into smaller pieces, making provable guarantees tractable without exhaustive testing.
- Novel contribution: identifies recursion as a necessary abstraction to avoid spurious dependencies and capture true program semantics.
- Introduces a verification procedure to provide provable guarantees of neural program behavior.
- Empirical results show recursive models achieve perfect generalization while non-recursive versions fail on moderately complex inputs.
- Training data requirements are minimal (e.g., ~200 traces for addition), which is impressive.
- Verification demonstrates generalization by covering base cases and reduction rules that match target program behavior.
- Limitation: verification may become infeasible for tasks with very large input domains and needs further discussion.
- Needs clarification on how the verification set is constructed for tasks where analytical construction is difficult.
- More evidence required that models learn true program semantics rather than memorizing training examples.
- Recommend deeper analysis of training dynamics to contrast recursive vs. non-recursive learning behavior.
- Suggest a more comprehensive comparison with other generalization techniques (e.g., curriculum learning, architectural changes).
- Request clearer conditions and stronger guarantees about the completeness/applicability of the verification procedure.
- Overall recommendation: accept for ICLR 2017.
