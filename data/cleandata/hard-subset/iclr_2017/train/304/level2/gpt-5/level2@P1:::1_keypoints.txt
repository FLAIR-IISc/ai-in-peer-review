- Paper introduces recursion into neural programming (NPI) to improve out-of-distribution generalization and enable verification.
- Contributions: show NPI supports recursion; training with recursive traces yields far better generalization on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.
- Key conceptual claim: recursion constrains behavior to fixed-size subproblems with LSTM state reset, improving generalization and enabling verification.
- Verification proposal: enumerate finite set V of base cases and reduction-rule configurations; matching model outputs to reference on V proves generalization.
- Empirical claim: recursive traces produce perfect generalization on all four tasks with small training sets; non-recursive variants fail at larger scales.
- Verification implementation: concrete verification sets provided (e.g., 20k cases for addition) and a constructive procedure to derive V from reference code.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reasons for accept: simple, well-motivated idea with strong empirical gains and a novel verification perspective likely to influence the field.
- Motivation: recursion addresses spurious length/structure dependencies and hidden-state carry-over in non-recursive neural programs.
- Related work placement: well-situated relative to NPI, DNC/NTM, Neural GPU; focus is on provable generalization via explicit recursion.
- Empirical correctness: diverse tasks including non-tail recursion and graph structure validate the approach; small data budgets emphasize the inductive bias.
- Verification correctness: matching on finite V suffices given recursive call structure and state resets; approach is precise in spirit.
- Limitation: proof depends on correct and complete enumeration of V; derivation is semi-automated and task-specific.
- Scalability limitation: approach may be infeasible for tasks with very large or continuous observation spaces.
- Suggestion: formalize and exemplify the construction of V and the observation-to-observation mapping with a worked example.
- Suggestion: broaden empirical reporting—include full tables, error bars, multiple seeds, and ablations (hidden-state reset, partial vs full recursion, data sensitivity).
- Suggestion: report recursion/stack depth distributions and failure modes at large scales, especially for quicksort.
- Suggestion: compare to or discuss retrofitting recursion into other architectures (DNC/NTM) to strengthen generality claims.
- Suggestion: clarify environment design tradeoffs (e.g., bubble sort encoder exposing comparisons) and quantify effects on learnability and V size.
- Suggestion: release code, trace generators, and verification scripts to aid reproducibility.
- Suggestion: outline concrete future strategies for learning recursion with weaker supervision (search over call graphs, differentiable stacks, constraints).
- Question: how automated is V derivation—provide pseudo-code and guarantees on termination/coverage?
- Question: what procedures ensure V completeness and how are spurious/unreachable sequences handled?
- Question: how sensitive are results to initialization, LSTM size, hyperparameters, and what fraction of runs achieve perfect generalization?
- Question: what are maximum recursion/stack depths and how do runtime/memory scale for larger quicksort/topological sort instances?
- Question: for partial recursion cases (e.g., bubble sort), which missing components cause failures and how does length-dependent behavior manifest?
- Question: can abstract interpretation or learned state abstractions keep V finite for large/continuous observation spaces while preserving correctness?
- Question: were attempts made to learn recursion from non-recursive or partial traces (e.g., teacher forcing only on base cases), and what failed?
- Overall verdict: timely, insightful work introducing recursion and a verification lens to address neural program induction weaknesses; recommendation to accept.
