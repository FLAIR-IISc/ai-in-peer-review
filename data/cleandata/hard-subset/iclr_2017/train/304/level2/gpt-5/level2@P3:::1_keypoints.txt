- Paper proposes explicit recursion within the NPI framework to address neural program induction generalization failures.
- Recursion is implemented via training traces with recursive calls, using NPI’s call stack and hidden-state reset at subprogram boundaries.
- Empirical evaluation on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort shows strong generalization from very small training sets.
- The recursive models achieve perfect or near-perfect performance on inputs far beyond training lengths.
- A verification methodology is proposed: identify base cases and reductions, construct a finite verification set V, and prove perfect generalization by matching step outputs.
- Claims to provide the first provable generalization guarantees for learned neural programs on these tasks.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason: recursion materially improves generalization, interpretability, and enables tractable verification.
- Broad applicability demonstrated across multiple tasks including non-tail recursion (quicksort), with small training data.
- Motivation and placement: situates well in program induction literature and tests hypothesis that non-recursive models learn spurious length-dependent associations.
- Technical contribution: minimal architectural change but non-trivial training and verification formulation that constrains state per call and decouples behavior from input length.
- Verification construction of reachable step-input set V is carefully argued and instantiated with analytic coverage for addition and principled coverage for other tasks.
- Empirical evidence details: addition (data-efficient provable guarantees), bubble sort (only full recursion generalizes), topological sort (recursive generalizes from minimal traces), quicksort (recursive sustains correctness).
- Study isolates recursion role using matched environments/traces and large stress tests (e.g., 5000-digit addition, 120-node DAGs).
- Claims and rigor: provable generalization supported by matching learned step outputs to a reference program on V; limitations and assumptions are discussed.
- Feedback: need to formalize conditions guaranteeing V completeness and handle semantic-equivalence when multiple correct traces exist.
- Feedback: discuss generality beyond NPI and clarify essential architectural properties (e.g., call frames, hidden-state reinitialization).
- Feedback: analyze how environment encoders affect V size/tractability and provide design guidance.
- Feedback: include baselines and ablations (curriculum, non-trace-supervised variants, supervision level).
- Feedback: report robustness and scalability (stack-depth limits, noisy observations, sensitivity to return threshold α, recursion depths).
- Feedback: improve reproducibility by releasing code and verification set generators.
- Questions: How is V completeness established for tasks without analytic V (automated fixed-point or convergence criteria)?
- Questions: Does verification require exact trace matching or allow alternative correct sequences (e.g., different topological orders)?
- Questions: How critical is LSTM hidden-state reset at calls; what if partial carryover is allowed?
- Questions: Can recursion be discovered from partial/non-recursive traces (e.g., program synthesis over call graphs)?
- Questions: What recursion depths arise for non-tail recursion and are there stack/memory constraints or tail-call optimizations?
- Questions: For high-cardinality inputs (larger alphabets, images), are symbolic/abstract-interpretation methods planned to bound V without enumeration?
- Overall: simple, well-executed idea with clear benefits and a pragmatic verification step that meaningfully advances principled generalization of neural programs.
