Summary and contributions: The paper claims that augmenting neural programming architectures with explicit recursion dramatically improves generalization and interpretability, and uniquely enables provable guarantees of perfect generalization. Concretely, the authors modify training traces in the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) to induce recursive behavior and evaluate on four algorithmic tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort). They further introduce a verification procedure based on enumerating base cases and reduction rules to construct a finite verification set V and show learned models match a reference program P on V, implying perfect generalization.

Decision: Accept. Key reasons: (1) Novelty and clear motivation—explicit recursion as an architectural/training abstraction is a principled way to enforce program structure and mitigate length-based overfitting. (2) Strong support—compelling empirical results across diverse tasks and a concrete, reproducible verification methodology.

Supporting arguments: The work is well placed within the literature on neural programming (NPI, NTM/DNC, Neural GPU, pointer networks), identifying a recurrent failure mode: spurious dependencies on input length and opaque hidden states. The recursive reformulations (incl. tail recursion) reduce the domain each subprogram sees, resetting hidden state across calls, which is both conceptually sound and practically effective. Experiments are small-data and show stark gains: full recursion yields 100% accuracy on long inputs where non-recursive/partial-recursive fail (e.g., bubble sort, quicksort). The verification framework is a meaningful step toward rigor: for addition, V is analytically derived; for others, careful construction of V and matching traces to P substantiate the claims. The paper is useful for researchers building neural program learners and for those interested in verifiable ML.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical):
- Clarify the formal status of the “proof”: specify assumptions, soundness/completeness of constructing V, and failure modes if V is incomplete. A small formal lemma/theorem with conditions would strengthen rigor.
- Provide code and exact verification sets to aid reproducibility; quantify verification coverage (e.g., counts of observation transitions per subprogram).
- Add ablations: impact of hidden-state reset, tail-recursion optimization, and encoder design; sensitivity to stack depth limits.
- Compare against stronger non-NPI baselines (e.g., Neural GPU or DNC with curricula) and report sample efficiency more systematically.
- Discuss pathways to learn recursion with weaker supervision (partial/non-recursive traces) and prospects beyond toy algorithmic domains.

Questions for authors:
- How do you guarantee that the constructed V is complete for topological sort and quicksort? Can you bound V or automate its generation from P?
- What are the practical limits on recursion depth and stack usage at inference?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of environment observations (e.g., exposing comparisons vs raw values)?
- Could your approach transfer to other NPAs (e.g., DNC) without full execution-trace supervision?
