## Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

This paper tackles the critical problem of generalization in neural program synthesis, arguing that incorporating recursion is key to achieving robust performance and, crucially, enabling provable guarantees about program behavior. The authors demonstrate this by augmenting the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework with recursion on four tasks: addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, achieving superior generalization with limited training data.

I **accept** this paper. The central idea – that recursion provides a necessary abstraction for learning program semantics and enabling verification – is compelling and well-supported by the experimental results. The ability to *prove* generalization, even for a limited set of tasks, is a significant step forward in the field. The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches and convincingly demonstrates the benefits of their recursive approach.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation, approach, and results. The application of recursion to NPI is a logical and effective way to explore the idea. The experimental results consistently show improved generalization compared to non-recursive approaches, and the verification procedure is a novel and valuable contribution. The authors acknowledge the limitations of the verification procedure for very large input domains, which is a responsible and important inclusion.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification in a few areas. The connection between the specific training trace construction and the emergence of recursion could be more thoroughly explained. While the authors state they modify the training traces, a more detailed discussion of *how* these traces encourage the network to learn recursive behavior would be helpful. Additionally, the choice of tasks, while illustrative, seems somewhat limited. Exploring more complex algorithms or domains could further strengthen the claims. Finally, while the paper provides a detailed description of the verification process, a more accessible explanation of the underlying mathematical principles would be beneficial for a broader audience. 

I encourage the authors to consider these points during revision. Specifically, addressing the “how” of trace construction and expanding the scope of experiments would further solidify the impact of this promising work. The potential for provably correct neural programs is significant, and this paper represents a valuable contribution towards realizing that goal.
