- Proposes incorporating recursion into neural architectures (NPI) to improve generalization and interpretability for program learning.  
- Implements recursion in NPI and evaluates on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Shows recursive neural programs generalize better and solve arbitrarily complex instances with small training sets.  
- Introduces a verification procedure that provides provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural program learning.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Novelty: Explicit recursion is a novel solution to poor generalization and enables provable guarantees.  
- Strong empirical results: Recursive models outperform non-recursive baselines across multiple tasks with rigorous evaluation.  
- Practical utility: Approach is relevant for robust program synthesis and has clear real-world implications.  
- Claims are well-supported by theory, experiments, and the verification procedure; results are statistically significant and reproducible.  
- Good positioning: Situates work within prior neural programming literature and clarifies advances.  
- Clarity and completeness: Paper is well-structured with sufficient methodological and experimental detail and helpful appendices.  
- Suggestion: Evaluate scalability to more complex, real-world programming or NLP tasks.  
- Suggestion: Address limitations of the verification procedure for infinite or extremely large input domains.  
- Suggestion: Add ablation studies to isolate the specific impact of recursion (e.g., partial vs. full recursion).  
- Question: How does the approach handle tasks with inherently infinite input domains (motor control, real-world streams)?  
- Question: Can the verification procedure be automated or generalized to architectures beyond NPI?  
- Question: How sensitive is performance to choice of training traces and to partial/noisy traces?
