This paper tackles the problem of poor generalization in neural program synthesis, arguing that the lack of explicit recursive structure is a key limitation. The authors propose augmenting neural architectures with recursion, demonstrating its effectiveness on four tasks – addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort – within the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework. They achieve impressive results, including 100% accuracy on test inputs and, crucially, providing *provable* guarantees of generalization given verification sets covering base cases and reduction rules.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The work presents a compelling argument for the importance of recursion in neural program synthesis, supported by strong empirical results and, most notably, a novel contribution in the form of provable generalization guarantees – a significant step forward for the field.

The motivation is well-placed within the existing literature. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of prior approaches to neural program learning, specifically the tendency to overfit to training data and the difficulty in reasoning about behavior on complex inputs. The proposal to incorporate recursion addresses these limitations directly, drawing on established principles from programming language theory. The framing of the problem, and the connection made to the concept of recursion as a means of reducing complexity and enabling formal verification, is particularly strong.

The paper strongly supports its claims. The experimental results consistently demonstrate superior performance with the recursive NPI compared to previous approaches, and the detailed explanation of the verification procedure and the successful application to multiple tasks convincingly demonstrate the ability to provide provable guarantees. The analysis comparing the non-recursive, partially recursive, and fully recursive versions of bubble sort is particularly insightful.  The careful construction of training traces and verification sets, as described in the appendix, adds to the credibility of the results. The thoroughness of the experimental setup and the attention to detail in the verification procedure are commendable. The inclusion of topological sort and quicksort, more complex tasks than addition and bubble sort, further strengthens the arguments.

Additional feedback, aimed at improving the paper:

* **Clarify the connection to existing work on program synthesis with recursion.** While the paper cites relevant work in neural program learning, it could benefit from a more explicit discussion of how this work relates to traditional program synthesis techniques that leverage recursion. For example, discussing how this neural approach differs from, or complements, techniques like inductive program synthesis with recursive function definitions could be valuable.
* **Discuss limitations of the verification approach.** The authors acknowledge limitations regarding scalability to tasks with very large state spaces. Expanding on this limitation and discussing potential avenues for addressing it (e.g., approximate verification techniques, abstraction) would be helpful.
* **Elaborate on the choice of the NPI architecture.** While the authors justify using NPI due to its natural support for recursion, briefly discussing other potential architectures and why NPI was chosen over them would strengthen the paper. 
* **Training Data Size:** The paper mentions the small sizes of the training datasets. Providing a brief ablation study showing how performance changes with increasing training data size, even if performance plateaus quickly, would be valuable.
* **Code Availability:** While not a requirement, releasing the code would further enhance the reproducibility and impact of the research.

Questions for the authors:

*  The verification procedure seems highly dependent on carefully constructing the verification set V. What strategies do you employ to ensure that V is truly comprehensive and avoids false positives (i.e., a model passing the verification but failing on unseen inputs)?
*  You mention that training with only partial recursion (in bubble sort) is insufficient for perfect generalization. Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of *why* all three recursive calls are necessary, beyond just stating that it doesn't work without them?
*  How sensitive is the performance of the recursive NPI to the specific method used to generate the recursive training traces? Could variations in trace generation significantly impact the learned program’s generalization ability?
