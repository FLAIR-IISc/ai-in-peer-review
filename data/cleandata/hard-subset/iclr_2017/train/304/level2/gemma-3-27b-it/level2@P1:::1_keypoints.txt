- Problem: paper addresses poor generalization in neural program synthesis, blaming lack of explicit recursive structure.  
- Proposal: augment neural architectures with recursion within the NPI framework to improve generalization.  
- Tasks: evaluated on four tasks — addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Results: achieves impressive empirical performance, including 100% accuracy on test inputs.  
- Novelty: provides provable generalization guarantees via verification sets covering base cases and reduction rules.  
- Recommendation: reviewer recommends accept.  
- Motivation: strong framing linking recursion to reduced complexity and formal verification, grounded in prior literature.  
- Empirical support: thorough experiments show recursive NPI outperforms prior approaches and non-recursive variants.  
- Analysis: insightful comparison between non-recursive, partially recursive, and fully recursive bubble sort implementations.  
- Methodology: careful construction of training traces and verification sets (detailed in appendix) strengthens credibility.  
- Scope: inclusion of more complex tasks (topological sort, quicksort) bolsters the paper’s claims.  
- Feedback — related work: clarify connections to traditional program synthesis techniques that use recursion.  
- Feedback — verification limits: discuss scalability and limitations of the verification approach and possible remedies (e.g., abstraction, approximate verification).  
- Feedback — architecture choice: justify why NPI was chosen over other architectures that might support recursion.  
- Feedback — training data: include an ablation on training data size to show sensitivity and performance scaling.  
- Feedback — reproducibility: consider releasing code to aid reproducibility and impact.  
- Question — verification set: how do you ensure verification set V is comprehensive and avoids false positives?  
- Question — necessity of full recursion: can you provide an intuitive explanation why all recursive calls are required for bubble sort generalization?  
- Question — trace sensitivity: how sensitive is generalization to the method used to generate recursive training traces?
