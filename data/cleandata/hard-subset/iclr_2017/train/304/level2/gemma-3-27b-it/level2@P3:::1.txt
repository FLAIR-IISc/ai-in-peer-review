This paper tackles the challenging problem of generalization in neural program synthesis, arguing that incorporating recursion is crucial for achieving robust performance and enabling provable guarantees. The central claim is that recursion reduces the complexity of the learning problem, allowing the network to learn program semantics rather than spurious correlations. The approach is demonstrated by augmenting the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture with recursive calls on four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. The empirical results show superior generalization compared to non-recursive approaches, and importantly, the paper presents a verification procedure to *prove* perfect generalization under certain conditions.

The decision is to **accept** with minor revisions. The work addresses a significant issue in neural program synthesis – the lack of generalization and interpretability – with a compelling and novel approach. The key strength is the introduction of recursion as a fundamental building block for learning program semantics, and the demonstration of provable generalization is a substantial contribution. The experimental results support the claim, showing significant improvements in generalization performance across the tested tasks.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly explains the limitations of existing approaches. The connection to established concepts in programming languages (recursion, base cases, reduction rules) provides a solid theoretical foundation. The implementation details are sufficiently clear, allowing for potential reproduction, and the ablation studies (comparing recursive vs. non-recursive versions) are effective in demonstrating the benefits of the proposed approach. The verification procedure is a particularly strong aspect of the work, providing a level of confidence rarely seen in this field.

However, several aspects could be improved. First, the explanation of the verification procedure, while conceptually sound, could be more detailed, particularly concerning the construction of the verification set 'V'. Providing more concrete examples of how 'V' is determined for each task would strengthen the argument. Second, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach. For example, the verification procedure seems to be heavily reliant on the specific tasks and might not scale well to more complex problems with larger state spaces. It's not clear how the approach would handle tasks with continuous state spaces or those requiring more complex reasoning. Third, while the paper demonstrates that recursion *helps* generalization, it doesn't fully explore *why* it helps. A more detailed analysis of the learned representations in the recursive vs. non-recursive models could provide valuable insights. Finally, the ablation study on bubble sort ("partially recursive") suggests that some level of recursion is helpful, but not sufficient. A clearer explanation of what is missing from the "partially recursive" approach would be valuable.

Specific suggestions for improvement:

*   **Verification Procedure Details:** Expand the explanation of how the verification set V is constructed for each task. Include more concrete examples.
*   **Limitations Discussion:** Add a section discussing the limitations of the approach and potential challenges for scaling to more complex problems.
*   **Representation Analysis:** Include an analysis of the learned representations in the recursive vs. non-recursive models. Are the representations simpler, more interpretable, or more robust?
*   **Partial Recursion Explanation:** Elaborate on why the "partially recursive" version of bubble sort fails to generalize perfectly. What is missing compared to the fully recursive version?
*   **Clarify assumptions:** The use of the term "provable generalization" implies a strong statement. Clarify assumptions made during verification (e.g. input constraints).



Addressing these points would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. Overall, this is a significant contribution that offers a promising direction for improving generalization in neural program synthesis.
