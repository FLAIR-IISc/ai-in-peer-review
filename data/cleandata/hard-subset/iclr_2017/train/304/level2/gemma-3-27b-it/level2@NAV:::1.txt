## Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

This paper tackles the crucial problem of generalization in neural program synthesis, arguing that incorporating recursion is key to achieving robust performance and, importantly, provable guarantees. The authors propose augmenting neural architectures with recursion, demonstrating its effectiveness on four tasks – addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort – within the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework. The core claim is that recursion simplifies the problem space, facilitating learning and enabling formal verification of generalization.

I **accept** this paper. The approach is novel and addresses a significant limitation of existing neural program synthesis methods. The empirical results are compelling, showcasing superior generalization compared to non-recursive approaches, particularly for more complex tasks like quicksort. Crucially, the paper goes beyond empirical validation and presents a methodology for *proving* generalization, a significant contribution to the field.

The strength of this work lies in its insightful connection between recursion – a fundamental principle in programming – and improved generalization in neural networks. The authors convincingly demonstrate that recursion isn’t merely a performance enhancement, but a structural requirement for achieving provably correct behavior. The thorough experimental evaluation, coupled with the detailed description of the verification procedure, strengthens the validity of the claims.  The analysis of why previous approaches struggle due to spurious dependencies is also well-articulated.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification in several areas.  While the implementation details are provided, the process of constructing the recursive training traces could be elaborated on with more concrete examples, particularly for the topological sort and quicksort tasks. It would be helpful to discuss the sensitivity of the results to the size and composition of the training data. Additionally, the scalability of the verification procedure to larger and more complex problems beyond these four tasks needs to be addressed; the limitations acknowledged in the conclusion are important, but further discussion of potential solutions would be valuable.  Finally, the relationship to existing work on program synthesis and inductive programming, beyond the NPI framework, could be more explicitly discussed.

Overall, this is a strong and impactful paper. The authors present a compelling argument for the importance of recursion in neural program synthesis and provide a valuable methodology for achieving provable generalization. The work opens up exciting avenues for future research in this area and represents a significant step towards building more reliable and trustworthy AI systems.
