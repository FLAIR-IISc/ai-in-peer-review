This paper presents a novel and compelling approach to address the critical problem of generalization in neural program synthesis. The authors claim that explicitly incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures is essential for learning true program semantics and generalizing to inputs of arbitrary complexity. They demonstrate this concept by modifying the training procedure of the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework to learn recursive solutions for four tasks: addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. The core contributions are the successful application of recursion to achieve perfect generalization from very few training examples and, crucially, the introduction of a verification method that provides provable guarantees of this generalization.

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The decision is based on two key strengths: (1) the elegant and impactful idea of using recursion to achieve provable generalization, a significant conceptual advance for the field, and (2) the robust empirical and theoretical evidence that convincingly supports the paper's claims.

The paper’s core idea is well-motivated and executed. The authors correctly identify that existing models often fail by learning spurious correlations tied to training data characteristics, like input length. The proposed solution—using recursion to decompose problems into a finite set of base cases and reduction rules—is a natural fit. The experiments provide definitive validation. Across four distinct algorithmic tasks, the recursive models achieve 100% accuracy on complex, unseen inputs, while the non-recursive baselines systematically fail. The introduction of a verification procedure that makes formal guarantees tractable is a standout contribution, pushing the field towards a higher standard of rigor beyond simple test set evaluation.

For future improvement, I suggest a more detailed discussion of the supervision requirements. The method relies on manually providing recursive execution traces. While acknowledged as future work, a brief exploration of potential avenues for learning these recursive structures with less supervision (e.g., from I/O pairs or non-recursive traces) would strengthen the paper.

I have two questions for the authors:
1.  How complex is the manual process of constructing the verification set V for a new task? Could this process be automated?
2.  How robust is the learning process to variations in the training traces? For instance, if the traces contained minor inefficiencies or noise, would the model still learn the correct recursive program?
