- Introduces recursion as a core neural-programming abstraction to improve generalization and enable formal verification.
- Modifies Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) training traces to be recursive.
- Evaluates on grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.
- Recursive models achieve perfect or near-perfect generalization to much larger inputs from few examples; non-recursive baselines fail.
- Proposes a verification procedure: identify base cases and reduction rules to construct a finite verification set.
- Shows correctness on the verification set implies provable perfect generalization for these tasks.
- First demonstration of provably perfect generalization for a neural program synthesizer.
- Strong empirical support and clear motivation: recursion counters spurious, length-dependent correlations.
- Ablation (non-, partial-, full-recursive bubble sort) highlights the need for full recursion for generalization.
- Limitation: approach requires providing explicit recursive execution traces (strong supervision).
- Limitation: constructing the verification set V is manual and knowledge-intensive, risking human error; automation is future work.
- Open question: whether models can induce recursion from iterative traces/curricula of increasing lengths.
- Scope limitation: verification currently unsuitable for very large or continuous input domains; applies mainly to symbolic/algorithmic tasks.
