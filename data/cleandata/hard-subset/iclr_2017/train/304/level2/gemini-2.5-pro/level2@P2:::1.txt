**Summary**

This paper addresses the critical and well-documented problem of poor generalization in neural networks trained to learn algorithmic tasks. The authors propose a simple yet powerful solution: explicitly incorporating the concept of recursion into the learning process. They hypothesize that recursion is a necessary abstraction for robust generalization and, crucially, for enabling formal verification of the learned programs. As a proof of concept, they adapt the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework to learn recursive programs for four tasks: addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. They achieve this by providing training supervision in the form of recursive execution traces. The empirical results are compelling, showing that the recursive models achieve perfect generalization from very small training sets, significantly outperforming their non-recursive counterparts. The paper's most significant contribution is a method for providing provable guarantees of perfect generalization. By constructing a finite verification set that covers all base cases and reduction rules, the authors demonstrate for the first time that a neural program can be formally verified to work correctly on an infinite set of valid inputs.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that I recommend for acceptance. My decision is based on two key contributions:
1.  The paper introduces a novel and highly effective method for achieving strong generalization in neural program synthesis by explicitly teaching the model recursion. The idea is elegant, well-motivated, and supported by strong empirical evidence across a diverse set of algorithmic tasks.
2.  It pioneers a tractable approach to the formal verification of neural programs, offering "provably perfect generalization." This is a groundbreaking step towards building more reliable and trustworthy neural systems and opens up a significant new research direction.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's primary strength is its central idea. Connecting a fundamental computer science concept like recursion to a major weakness in modern machine learning (generalization on algorithmic tasks) is insightful. The authors clearly articulate why recursion helps: it breaks down problems into smaller, self-similar subproblems, effectively eliminating spurious dependencies on input characteristics like length, which plague non-recursive models.

The experimental results robustly support the paper's claims. On tasks like bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, the performance gap between the recursive and non-recursive models is stark. The ability of the recursive models to achieve 100% accuracy on complex inputs after training on just a handful of simple examples is a powerful demonstration. The ablation-like experiment with "partially recursive" bubble sort further strengthens the argument that learning the correct recursive structure is essential.

The most novel and exciting contribution is the verification procedure. While neural networks are often treated as black boxes, this work provides a concrete method to formally prove correctness. The insight that recursion reduces the verification problem from an infinite domain to a finite set of base cases and reduction rules is key. While the procedure itself is demonstrated on relatively simple algorithms, it represents a significant conceptual leap for the field. The paper is well-written, with the motivation, methods, and results presented clearly and logically.

The main weakness, which the authors rightly acknowledge, is the reliance on strong supervision in the form of full, explicitly recursive execution traces. This form of supervision is a significant "hint" to the model and may not be available for more complex tasks where the ideal recursive decomposition is unknown. The current work demonstrates that NPI *can* learn recursion if taught, but not that it can *discover* it.

Additionally, the process for constructing the verification set `V` seems to be a highly manual and task-specific process requiring expert knowledge of the algorithm's control flow. While effective for the chosen tasks, the scalability and generality of this verification procedure could be explored further. However, these limitations do not detract from the core contributions of the paper; rather, they lay the groundwork for exciting future research.

**Additional Feedback**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision:
*   It would be beneficial to more explicitly frame the contribution in the language of inductive bias. Providing recursive traces imposes a very strong and correct inductive bias on the model, which is the source of its impressive data efficiency and generalization.
*   In Appendix A.6, the domain-specific encoder for bubble sort is modified for the purposes of verification. Was the model reported in Table 1 (the main results) trained with the original encoder (exposing raw values) or this modified one (exposing comparisons)? Clarifying this would be helpful, as the representational change is significant.
*   The paper proves that a learned model matches a reference implementation on a verification set. It would be interesting to discuss the possibility of the model learning a *different*, but still correct, recursive algorithm from the one specified in the traces. Does the verification process guard against this?

**Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points in their rebuttal:
1.  The construction of the verification set `SV` is central to the claim of provable generalization. Could you provide more insight into the practical difficulty and effort required to construct this set for a task like quicksort? Do you see a clear path towards automating this process, for instance, by deriving `V` from a formal specification of the target program?
2.  While the paper focuses on teaching recursion via explicit traces, have you performed any experiments to see if the model can learn recursive solutions from weaker supervision? For example, could a model trained on non-recursive traces but evaluated in a setting that allows self-calls eventually converge to a recursive solution?
3.  In Section 4.1, you show that for addition, a non-recursive model trained on just 5 one-digit examples fails to generalize to longer sums that are compositions of the training examples. Could you elaborate on the specific failure mode? Does the model fail to handle the carry operation correctly over long distances, or does it get confused by the sequence length itself? This detail would further strengthen your hypothesis that non-recursive models learn spurious correlations.
