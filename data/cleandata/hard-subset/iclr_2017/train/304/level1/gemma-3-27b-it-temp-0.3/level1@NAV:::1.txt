## Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

This paper tackles the crucial problem of generalization in neural program synthesis. The authors argue that incorporating recursion into neural architectures is key to overcoming the limitations of existing approaches, which often struggle to generalize beyond a certain input complexity. They demonstrate this by augmenting the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework with recursion on four tasks: addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, achieving superior generalization and, importantly, enabling provable guarantees of correctness.

I **accept** this paper. The core idea of leveraging recursion to improve generalization in neural program synthesis is both insightful and well-supported. The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating perfect generalization on all tested tasks, and the introduction of a verification procedure to *prove* generalization is a significant contribution. This is a notable step towards building more reliable and interpretable neural programs.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly situating the work within the existing literature on neural program learning and highlighting the shortcomings of current methods. The authors convincingly argue that the inherent complexity of non-recursive approaches leads to spurious dependencies and hinders generalization. The choice of NPI as a base architecture is sensible, and the implementation of recursion within this framework is clearly explained. Crucially, the authors don’t just demonstrate improved performance; they provide a rigorous verification process that establishes provable guarantees, a rare and valuable achievement in this field. The experimental setup, while not extensive, is sufficient to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The inclusion of four diverse tasks strengthens the claims.

However, the paper could benefit from further discussion on the scalability of the verification procedure to more complex tasks and larger input domains, as acknowledged by the authors. While the current verification method is effective for the chosen tasks, its applicability to real-world problems with vast state spaces remains an open question. Further exploration of alternative training procedures beyond simply modifying the training traces would also be beneficial. Finally, providing more details on the hyperparameter tuning and the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters would strengthen the reproducibility of the work. 

I would appreciate clarification on the following: 
1.  How does the choice of LSTM as the core controller influence the effectiveness of the recursive approach? Would other recurrent architectures or even attention mechanisms yield similar results?
2.  The paper mentions the possibility of training recursive neural programs without explicit training execution traces. Could the authors elaborate on potential strategies for achieving this?
3.  What are the computational costs associated with the verification procedure, and how do they scale with the complexity of the task and the size of the input?
