- Claim: Neural program learners fail to generalize because they learn spurious correlations; explicitly incorporating recursion fixes this by decomposing problems into self-similar subproblems.  
- Contribution: Modify the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) to learn recursive programs for addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Empirical result: Recursive models trained on very few execution traces achieve perfect generalization on much larger/complex inputs, outperforming non-recursive baselines.  
- Verification: Introduce a method to formally verify learned recursive programs by checking a finite set of base cases and reduction rules, yielding provable guarantees of perfect generalization.  
- Decision: Accept — the paper presents a strong, foundational idea.  
- Key reason 1: Novel and powerful solution — recursion is an elegant, well-motivated programmatic inductive bias that significantly improves generalization.  
- Key reason 2: Provable guarantees — recursion makes verification tractable by bounding the state space to single recursive steps.  
- Motivation/positioning: Well-motivated critique of prior approaches; NPI’s call-stack is a natural substrate for recursion.  
- Empirical validation details: Clear experiments show non-recursive failures on sorting, 100% accuracy for recursive variants, and extreme data efficiency (e.g., single-trace topological sort).  
- Verification soundness: Convincing argument that resetting controller hidden state per recursive call reduces verification to a finite set; current SV construction is manual but serves as a proof-of-concept.  
- Feedback — automation: The verification-set (SV) construction is manual and task-specific; automating derivation from formal specs would improve scalability.  
- Feedback — supervision: Reliance on complete execution traces (NPI-style) is a practical limitation; discuss approaches for learning recursion from weaker supervision (e.g., IO pairs or RL).  
- Feedback — scope limits: Verification is inadequate for vast/continuous domains (e.g., vision); consider verifying recursive control-flow while treating perception modules as black boxes with assumed accuracy.  
- Question: Have you considered inducing recursion via architectural biases or loss regularizers that reward self-calls on smaller inputs instead of providing recursive traces?  
- Question: Why does grade-school addition generalize with a non-recursive baseline while bubble sort and quicksort require explicit recursion?  
- Question: How does a single 10-element array suffice for quicksort verification to cover base cases (empty/singleton) and all partitioning behaviors on arbitrary inputs?
