- Proposes explicitly incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures to improve generalization.
- Trains a Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) with recursive execution traces to solve tasks like addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.
- Shows recursive NPIs achieve perfect accuracy from very few training examples.
- Main contribution: a verification procedure that, using recursion, provides provable guarantees of perfect generalization.
- Strong accept recommendation based on a simple, well-motivated idea and the introduction of provable guarantees.
- Experiments convincingly show non-recursive NPIs fail on slightly more complex inputs while recursive versions generalize perfectly.
- Argues recursion enables learning length-invariant program semantics rather than spurious correlations.
- Recursive decomposition makes verification via finite base cases and reduction rules tractable, improving trustworthiness.
- Primary limitation: reliance on hand-crafted recursive execution traces for supervision.
- Suggestion: discuss future directions for learning recursion with less supervision (e.g., from non-recursive traces or input-output pairs).
- Limitation: constructing the verification set V appears to require significant domain expertise; discuss complexity and automation potential.
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to specific recursive formulations in traces (e.g., Hoare vs Lomuto quicksort) and why partial recursion in bubble sort fails while full recursion succeeds.
