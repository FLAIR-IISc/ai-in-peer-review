This paper presents a compelling and elegant approach to a central challenge in neural program synthesis: generalization. The authors propose that explicitly incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures is a key step towards learning robust algorithms. By modifying the training traces for a Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) to be recursive, they demonstrate that models can learn to solve tasks like grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort with perfect accuracy from a very small number of training examples. The paper's most significant contribution is a verification procedure that leverages the recursive structure to provide, for the first time, provable guarantees of perfect generalization for learned neural programs.

I strongly recommend to **Accept** this paper. My decision is based on two key strengths: 1) The work tackles a fundamental, well-known problem with a conceptually simple yet powerful idea that is well-motivated by principles of computer science. 2) The introduction of provable guarantees moves the field beyond purely empirical evaluation towards a more rigorous, formal understanding of learned programs.

The supporting evidence is persuasive. The experiments clearly demonstrate that while non-recursive NPI models fail on inputs even slightly more complex than the training data, the recursive versions generalize perfectly. This stark contrast provides strong validation for the central hypothesis that recursion helps models learn the true, length-invariant program semantics instead of spurious correlations. Furthermore, the ability to formally verify the learned program on a finite set of base cases and reduction rules—a process made tractable by the recursive decomposition—is a major step towards building trustworthy and reliable neural synthesizers.

I offer the following points as constructive feedback to help improve the paper:
*   The primary limitation is the reliance on hand-crafted recursive execution traces for supervision. The authors acknowledge this, and it would strengthen the paper to briefly discuss potential future directions for learning recursive structures with less supervision, for instance, from non-recursive traces or input-output pairs alone.
*   The process for constructing the verification set `V` appears to require significant domain expertise. A more detailed discussion of the complexity and potential for automating this step would be valuable for readers looking to apply this method.

Finally, I have a few questions for the authors to clarify my understanding:
*   How sensitive is the model's success to the specific recursive formulation provided in the traces? For example, would the model learn equally well if trained on traces for quicksort using the Hoare partition scheme instead of Lomuto?
*   The finding that a "partially recursive" bubble sort fails to generalize is very insightful. Could you elaborate on why a full recursive decomposition was necessary to achieve perfect generalization in this case?
