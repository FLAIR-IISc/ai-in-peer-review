## Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

This paper tackles the critical problem of poor generalization in neural program synthesis. The authors argue that incorporating recursion into neural architectures is key to improving generalization and enabling provable guarantees about program behavior. They demonstrate this by augmenting the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework with recursion on four tasks: addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, achieving superior generalization with small training datasets and, importantly, providing a method for formal verification.

I **accept** this paper. The core idea is insightful and well-motivated. The observed improvements in generalization, particularly the ability to *prove* generalization for the recursive models, are a significant contribution. The clear connection between recursion and tractability for formal verification is a strong point.  The experimental results across diverse tasks convincingly support the claim that recursion is a crucial component for robust neural program learning.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the problem, proposed approach, and experimental setup. The authors thoroughly explain how they modified the training procedure to encourage learning recursive programs. The inclusion of details regarding verification is commendable, as it directly addresses a significant limitation of existing work in this area.  The detailed appendix further enhances the reproducibility and understanding of the work. 

However, the paper could be strengthened by a more in-depth discussion of the limitations. While the success on the chosen tasks is encouraging, the generalization to more complex, real-world problems remains an open question.  Furthermore, the paper could benefit from a more extensive analysis of the learned recursive programs themselves – what specifically is learned, and how does the recursive structure manifest in the network’s internal representations? It would also be valuable to discuss potential challenges and alternative strategies for learning recursion without explicit modifications to the training traces.

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   The paper mentions that "recursion helps reduce the problem of generalization to these two aspects [base cases and reduction rules] and make the generalization problem significantly simpler to handle and reason about.” Could you elaborate on *how* the recursive structure specifically simplifies the verification process beyond just reducing the number of cases? What properties of recursion enable this simplification?
*   You mention that for tasks with very large input domains, the current verification procedure is infeasible. Do you have any preliminary thoughts on how to address this limitation, perhaps through sampling techniques or abstraction methods?
*   While you demonstrate the benefit of recursion, are there specific architectural constraints or hyperparameters that are particularly crucial for successful learning of recursive programs within the NPI framework?
*   The modification to the training traces seems critical. What were the results if you only added recursion but kept the original training traces? Would curriculum learning or other techniques help discover the recursive structure without explicitly altering the traces?
