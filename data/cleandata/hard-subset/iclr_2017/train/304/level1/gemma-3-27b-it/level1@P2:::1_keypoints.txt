- Problem: Addresses poor generalization in neural program synthesis and difficulty learning robust program semantics from limited data.  
- Proposed solution: Augment neural architectures with recursion as a key abstraction within the NPI framework.  
- Evaluation tasks: Demonstrates method on grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Core contribution: Shows recursion improves generalization and enables provable guarantees about program behavior.  
- Empirical evidence: Achieves 100% test accuracy after training on few examples across multiple tasks.  
- Verification: Provides a detailed verification procedure and carefully constructed verification sets covering base cases and reduction rules.  
- Reproducibility: Experimental setup and verification details are described sufficiently for replication.  
- Literature placement: Well-motivated and builds logically on prior neural program learning work (e.g., NPI, Vinyals et al., Kaiser & Sutskever).  
- Hypothesis rationale: Argues recursion reduces learning complexity and makes verification feasible.  
- Recommendation: Reviewer recommends accepting the paper.  
- Suggested improvement 1: Include a concrete, step-by-step verification example (e.g., simple addition) to aid clarity.  
- Suggested improvement 2: Expand discussion on scalability of the verification procedure to large input domains and potential remedies.  
- Suggested improvement 3: Add a detailed ablation study to isolate effects of different recursive formulations (e.g., fully vs. partially recursive bubble sort).  
- Suggested improvement 4: Provide stronger comparisons with other generalization methods (e.g., curriculum learning).  
- Suggested improvement 5: Clarify distinction between the NPI framework and the specific recursive implementation within it.  
- Questions for authors: Request a simple verification walkthrough, alternative verification techniques for large domains, evidence that non-recursive models learn spurious correlations, hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, and adaptability of the recursive approach to non-NPI architectures.
