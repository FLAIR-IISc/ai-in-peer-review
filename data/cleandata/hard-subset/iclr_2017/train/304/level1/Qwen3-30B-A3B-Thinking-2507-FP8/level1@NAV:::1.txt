

This paper addresses a critical challenge in neural programming: poor generalization of learned programs beyond training input complexity. The authors propose incorporating recursion as a key abstraction into neural architectures, demonstrating that recursive neural programs achieve perfect generalization on four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort) with minimal training data. The core contribution is the introduction of a verification procedure that provides provable guarantees of perfect generalization by verifying only base cases and reduction rules, rather than exhaustively testing all possible inputs.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution by showing that recursion is necessary for neural programs to generalize robustly and by introducing the first provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs. The empirical results are strong, with recursive versions achieving 100% accuracy across all tested inputs while non-recursive versions fail on longer inputs or larger graphs.

The paper supports its claims effectively through thorough empirical evaluation and a well-justified verification procedure. The recursive formulations for each task are carefully designed, and the verification process is clearly explained. The paper successfully demonstrates that recursion enables neural programs to learn true program semantics rather than spurious dependencies on training data characteristics.

For improvement, the authors should more thoroughly discuss the limitations of their verification approach, particularly for tasks with potentially infinite input domains. The claim of being "the first" to provide provable guarantees should be carefully contextualized against all relevant prior work on neural program verification.

I have two clarifying questions: (1) How would the verification procedure scale to tasks with infinite input domains (e.g., image classification), where the base cases might be unbounded? (2) Can the approach be extended to learn recursion automatically from partial or non-recursive traces, as mentioned in the future work section, without requiring explicitly recursive training traces?
