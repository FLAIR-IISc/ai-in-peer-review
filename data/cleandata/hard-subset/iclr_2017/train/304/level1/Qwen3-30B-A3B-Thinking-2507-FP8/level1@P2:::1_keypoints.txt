- Paper claim: Introducing recursion into neural programs (via NPI) yields robust, provable generalization to inputs of arbitrary complexity.
- Key technical contribution: Implement recursion in NPI by modifying training traces and introduce a verification procedure that proves perfect generalization by testing a finite set of base cases and reduction rules.
- Empirical results: Recursive models achieve perfect generalization on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) with very small training sets (4–200 examples).
- Verification example: For grade-school addition, proving generalization requires testing only 20,181 specific input problems rather than exhaustive enumeration.
- Contrast with non-recursive models: Non-recursive implementations fail dramatically on longer inputs and cannot be verified due to hidden state propagation.
- Motivation and theory: Paper clearly identifies poor generalization in neural programming and provides a compelling theoretical rationale for why recursion addresses it.
- Literature context: Well-situated relative to prior work and novel in providing provable generalization guarantees for neural programs.
- Rigor: Empirical comparisons between recursive and non-recursive models are thorough and scientifically rigorous across multiple tasks and input sizes.
- Suggested improvement 1: Provide more guidance on constructing recursive training traces for new tasks to aid reproducibility and adoption.
- Suggested improvement 2: Discuss limitations of the verification procedure explicitly, especially when verification sets may become prohibitively large for some tasks.
- Suggested improvement 3: Give examples of other neural programming architectures beyond NPI that could adopt this recursion approach.
- Clarification question 1: Why does the non-recursive topological sort fail on graph size 5 despite training including size-5 graphs?
- Clarification question 2: How does this verification approach compare to broader formal verification techniques for neural networks?
- Clarification question 3: How much harder is learning non-tail-recursive quicksort compared to tail-recursive tasks, and what does that imply about generality?
- Clarification question 4: Please elaborate on why verification is fundamentally impossible for non-recursive models rather than merely difficult.
