- Paper claim: Incorporating recursion into neural programming (e.g., NPI) yields provable perfect generalization from minimal training data by teaching true program semantics rather than spurious input correlations.  
- Empirical claim: Recursive neural programs achieve perfect accuracy on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) on much larger unseen inputs after training on very small trace sets.  
- Theoretical claim: Recursion enables verification of neural programs by reducing correctness to a finite set of base cases and reduction rules.  
- Decision: Accept — novel contribution providing the first provable generalization guarantees for neural programs.  
- Problem clarity: The paper clearly identifies poor generalization in neural program learning as due to spurious dependencies and shows recursion as a solution.  
- Positioning: Well-motivated and properly situated within neural programming literature (NPI, NTMs, Neural GPU); notes NPI naturally supports recursion.  
- Evidence: Theoretical justification and rigorous empirical results (e.g., addition up to 5000 digits, topo sort to 70 vertices) show recursive models generalize where non-recursive ones fail.  
- Data efficiency: Recursive models generalize perfectly after training on very small datasets (e.g., ~200 traces for addition, 100 for bubble sort).  
- Verification procedure: Thoroughly described and applied, testing only base cases and reduction rules instead of all inputs to prove generalization.  
- Additional feedback: Paper should more clearly explain why NPI supports recursion (LSTM hidden-state reset on subprogram calls) and discuss limits of verification scaling to complex domains (vision/speech).  
- Questions for authors: Clarify which specific NPI architectural features enable recursion and why overlooked previously; give a concrete example of verification requirements for large input domains (e.g., sorting MNIST); explain why partial recursion in bubble sort fails while full recursion succeeds.
