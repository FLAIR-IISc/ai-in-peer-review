

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

The paper proposes a novel approach to addressing the critical problem of poor generalization in neural programming architectures by incorporating recursion. The authors argue that recursion is necessary for neural programs to generalize robustly to inputs of arbitrary complexity, and they demonstrate this by implementing recursion in the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework. They show that recursive neural programs achieve perfect generalization on four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort) with remarkably small training sets (as few as 4-200 examples). Most significantly, they introduce a verification procedure that allows them to prove perfect generalization by testing only a finite number of base cases and reduction rules, rather than exhaustively testing all possible inputs. This represents the first time provable guarantees of perfect generalization have been provided for neural programs.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating that recursion enables provable generalization in neural programs, and the empirical evidence supporting this claim is strong and well-documented.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

**Problem and Motivation (Question 1):** The paper clearly identifies a fundamental problem in neural programming: existing approaches suffer from poor generalization beyond a certain input complexity threshold, learning spurious dependencies on training data characteristics rather than true program semantics. This problem has been well-documented in the literature (citing Vinyals et al., Kaiser & Sutskever, etc.), and the authors provide a compelling theoretical argument for why recursion should solve it—by decomposing problems into smaller instances, making it tractable to prove generalization properties.

**Approach and Literature Context (Question 2):** The approach is exceptionally well-motivated and properly situated in the literature. The authors correctly identify that recursion is a natural fit for neural programming (as it is for conventional programming), and they provide a clear explanation of how to implement recursion in the NPI framework without modifying the architecture itself—simply by changing the training traces to include recursive elements. The paper distinguishes itself from prior work by being the first to provide provable generalization guarantees for neural programs, which is a substantial theoretical contribution.

**Evidence and Scientific Rigor (Question 3):** The empirical results are scientifically rigorous and compelling. For each task, they carefully compare recursive and non-recursive implementations, demonstrating that the recursive models achieve perfect generalization on inputs of arbitrary complexity (up to 5000 digits for addition, 70 vertices for topological sort, etc.), while non-recursive models fail dramatically on longer inputs. Crucially, they provide a verification procedure that proves their recursive models generalize perfectly by testing only a finite set of base cases and reduction rules. For example, they show that for addition, they need to test only 20,181 specific input problems to prove generalization for all possible inputs. This verification procedure is sound, well-explained, and represents a major contribution to the field.

## 4. Additional Feedback and Questions

**To Improve the Paper:**
- The paper could provide more detailed guidance on how to construct recursive training traces for new tasks beyond the four examples provided. This would make the approach more accessible to other researchers.
- It would be helpful to discuss the limitations of the verification procedure more explicitly, particularly for tasks with larger input spaces where the verification set might become prohibitively large.
- The paper mentions that recursion can be implemented in any Neural Programming Architecture supporting a call structure, but it would strengthen the argument to provide examples of other architectures (beyond NPI) that could benefit from this approach.

**Clarification Questions:**
1. For the topological sort task, the non-recursive model fails at graph size 5, but the training set includes a graph of size 5. How is it possible that the model fails on the same graph size it was trained on, suggesting that the model didn't learn the correct semantics even for the training set size?
2. The paper claims this is the first time provable generalization guarantees have been provided for neural programs. How does this approach compare to formal verification techniques for neural networks in general (not just for programming tasks)?
3. For the quicksort task, the recursion is not purely tail recursive, which the authors note is harder to learn. How does the learning difficulty of this non-tail-recursive version compare to the tail-recursive examples (addition, bubble sort), and what does this suggest about the generalizability of the approach to different recursive structures?
4. The paper states that the verification procedure "cannot be performed for the non-recursive versions" due to hidden state propagation. Could you elaborate on why this is fundamentally impossible for non-recursive models, rather than just difficult?

This paper makes a significant contribution to neural programming by introducing a theoretically grounded approach to achieving perfect generalization, with strong empirical validation. The verification procedure represents a novel and valuable technique for reasoning about neural program behavior, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the ICLR 2017 program.
