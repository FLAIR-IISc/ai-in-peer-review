- Paper's main claim: recursion is a necessary abstraction for neural programs to achieve robust, provable generalization.
- Method: incorporate recursion into the NPI architecture via modified training traces to induce recursive behavior.
- Result: recursive NPI achieves provably perfect generalization on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) from very small training sets.
- Key insight: recursion decomposes problems into finite base cases and reduction rules, enabling finite verification instead of exhaustive testing.
- Novelty: first work claiming provable perfect generalization guarantees for neural programs.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.
- Major reason for acceptance: identifies a fundamental limitation in prior neural programming work and offers a theoretically grounded solution.
- Empirical support: recursive models generalize perfectly to much larger/longer inputs (e.g., 5000-digit addition, long arrays/graphs) where non-recursive models fail.
- Experimental rigor: clear comparisons among non-recursive, partially recursive, and fully recursive models with convincing task-specific results (e.g., bubble sort and topological sort).
- Verification contribution: proposes a novel, rigorous verification procedure based on testing base cases and reduction rules.
- Feedback — theory: request clearer theoretical analysis explaining why recursion makes verification tractable.
- Feedback — limitations: ask for deeper discussion and potential workarounds for tasks with infinite base cases.
- Feedback — supervision: ask to clarify what "decreasing supervision" entails for learning recursion without explicit traces.
- Feedback — positioning: suggest a brief comparison with formal program synthesis and theorem-proving approaches.
- Question: how does non-tail recursion in quicksort affect the verification procedure and what extra cases are needed?
- Question: verification set for addition contains ~20k inputs vs 200 training examples — how does verification size scale to harder tasks?
- Question: why couldn't the original NPI learn recursion via standard training — architecture limitation or training dynamics?
- Question: how would the verification procedure scale to continuous input spaces (e.g., sorting real numbers) with potentially infinite base cases?
