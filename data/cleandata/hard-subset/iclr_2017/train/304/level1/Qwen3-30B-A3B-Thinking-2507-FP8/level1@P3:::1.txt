

# Peer Review for "Making Neural Programming Architectures Generalize Via Recursion"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper presents a compelling argument that recursion is a necessary abstraction for neural programs to achieve robust generalization. The authors demonstrate that by incorporating recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture through modified training traces, they can achieve provably perfect generalization on four programming tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort) from extremely small training sets. Their key insight is that recursion decomposes problems into smaller instances with finite base cases and reduction rules, making it possible to verify perfect generalization by testing only the base cases and reduction rules, rather than exhaustively testing all possible inputs. This represents the first work to provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs, which is a significant theoretical contribution to the field.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper identifies a fundamental limitation in current neural programming approaches (poor generalization beyond training complexity) and provides a theoretically grounded solution (recursion) that enables provable generalization guarantees.
2. The empirical results are compelling, showing that recursive neural programs achieve perfect generalization on arbitrarily complex inputs (up to 5000 digits for addition, arbitrary-length arrays for sorting) from training sets as small as 4-200 examples, while non-recursive versions fail dramatically on longer inputs.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is exceptionally well-motivated and well-positioned in the literature. The authors correctly identify the gap in existing neural programming work: while all previous approaches have evaluated generalization on longer inputs, none have provided a method to prove perfect generalization. They establish a strong theoretical foundation by demonstrating that recursion enables a finite verification procedure (testing base cases and reduction rules), which is not possible with non-recursive programs due to the accumulation of hidden state dependencies.

The experimental results are scientifically rigorous and well-designed. For each task, they provide clear comparisons between non-recursive, partially recursive, and fully recursive versions. The bubble sort results (Table 1) are particularly convincing: the fully recursive version achieves 100% accuracy on arrays of length 10, while the non-recursive version fails completely at length 8. Similarly, for topological sort, the recursive model generalizes perfectly from a single training example (a 5-node graph) to graphs of size 70, while the non-recursive model fails even at size 5.

The verification procedure they propose (Section 3.3) is novel and rigorous. By constructing a verification set that covers all base cases and reduction rules, they can mathematically prove perfect generalization. This is a significant methodological contribution that sets their work apart from previous neural programming approaches.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- **Clarify theoretical justification**: While the paper demonstrates that recursion enables verification, it would strengthen the contribution to provide a brief theoretical analysis of why recursion makes verification tractable (e.g., by reducing the problem to a finite set of cases rather than requiring infinite verification).
  
- **Address limitations more thoroughly**: The paper acknowledges that verification becomes infeasible for tasks with infinite base cases (e.g., sorting arbitrary elements), but doesn't discuss potential workarounds. A brief discussion of how this limitation might be addressed for more complex domains would strengthen the paper.

- **Clarify "decreasing supervision"**: The authors mention future work on "decreasing supervision" to enable models to learn recursion without explicit training traces. This is a promising direction, but the paper would benefit from clarifying exactly what "decreasing supervision" means in this context.

- **Compare with formal program synthesis**: The paper claims to be the first to provide provable generalization guarantees for neural programs. A brief comparison with formal program synthesis approaches (e.g., using theorem provers to verify neural networks) would help position their contribution more precisely.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. For the quicksort task (Section 3.3), the recursion is not purely tail recursive. How does this affect the verification procedure compared to the other tasks, and what additional verification cases are needed?

2. The verification set for addition contains 20,181 input problems (Section A.6). How does this compare to the size of the training set (200 examples), and how does this verification set size scale to more complex tasks?

3. The paper states that the recursive NPI naturally supports recursion but the authors of NPI didn't consider it explicitly. Why couldn't the original NPI architecture learn recursive programs through standard training without modified traces? Is this a fundamental limitation of the architecture or a matter of training dynamics?

4. How would your verification procedure scale to tasks with continuous input spaces (e.g., sorting real numbers instead of discrete digits), where the base cases might be infinite?
