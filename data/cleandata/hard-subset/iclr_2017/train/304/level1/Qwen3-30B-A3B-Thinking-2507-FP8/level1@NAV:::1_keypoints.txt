- Addresses poor generalization of neural programs and proposes recursion as a key abstraction for improved generalization.  
- Introduces recursive neural programs that achieve perfect generalization on four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) with minimal training data.  
- Core contribution: a verification procedure giving provable guarantees of perfect generalization by verifying base cases and reduction rules instead of exhaustive testing.  
- Empirical results are strong: recursive models achieve 100% accuracy across tested inputs while non-recursive models fail on longer inputs or larger graphs.  
- Claims recursion is necessary for neural programs to generalize robustly and presents the first provable guarantees for neural program generalization.  
- Supports claims with thorough empirical evaluation, carefully designed recursive formulations, and a clearly explained verification process.  
- Demonstrates that recursion helps models learn true program semantics rather than spurious data-dependent patterns.  
- Recommendation: accept.  
- Suggested improvement: more thoroughly discuss limitations of the verification approach, especially for tasks with potentially infinite input domains.  
- Suggested improvement: contextualize the "first" claim against prior work on neural program verification.  
- Clarifying question 1: How does the verification procedure scale to tasks with infinite input domains (e.g., image classification) where base cases may be unbounded?  
- Clarifying question 2: Can the approach be extended to learn recursion automatically from partial or non-recursive traces without requiring explicitly recursive training traces?
