

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Paper Claims
This paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures as a means to achieve perfect generalization with minimal training data. The authors argue that existing neural program learning approaches suffer from poor generalization due to learning spurious dependencies on input characteristics rather than true program semantics. They demonstrate that by modifying training traces to include recursive elements, neural architectures like Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) can learn recursive programs that generalize perfectly to inputs of arbitrary complexity. The paper provides both theoretical justification (showing how recursion enables provable generalization) and empirical evidence across four programming tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort), demonstrating that recursive neural programs achieve perfect accuracy on unseen inputs after training on very small datasets. Crucially, the authors show that their recursive programs can be verified to generalize perfectly by testing on a finite set of base cases and reduction rules, which is a significant contribution to the field.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a novel contribution to neural programming by demonstrating that recursion enables provable perfect generalization, which is the first time such guarantees have been provided for neural programs. The approach is well-motivated and supported by both theoretical arguments and empirical results.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper successfully addresses the three key questions for a decision:

1. The specific problem tackled is clear: neural architectures for learning programming tasks exhibit poor generalization beyond training complexity levels due to learning spurious dependencies rather than true program semantics. The authors demonstrate that recursion resolves this problem.

2. The approach is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The paper properly situates itself among existing neural programming work (NPI, Neural Turing Machine, Neural GPU, etc.) and correctly identifies that NPI naturally supports recursion (though the original authors didn't exploit this). The authors provide a clear theoretical argument for why recursion should help (reducing problem complexity into base cases and reduction rules) and demonstrate this with concrete examples across four tasks.

3. The paper adequately supports its claims. The theoretical argument for how recursion enables verification is sound, and the empirical results are rigorous. The authors demonstrate that recursive programs generalize perfectly on inputs of arbitrary complexity (up to 5000 digits for addition, 70 vertices for topological sort, etc.) while non-recursive versions fail at certain complexity thresholds. The verification procedure is well-described and properly applied, demonstrating that the learned programs generalize perfectly by testing only on base cases and reduction rules rather than all possible inputs. The training data is small (200 traces for addition, 100 for bubble sort, etc.), yet the recursive models generalize perfectly, which is a strong empirical validation.

## 4. Additional Feedback
The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of why the NPI architecture naturally supports recursion, as this is a key insight but currently somewhat buried in the technical details. The authors could more explicitly state that the NPI's reset of the LSTM hidden state at each subprogram call (as mentioned in Section 3.1) is precisely what makes recursion possible without stack overflow. Additionally, while the paper demonstrates verification for four tasks, it would strengthen the argument if the authors could briefly discuss why their verification approach might not scale to more complex domains (like vision or speech), as they acknowledge in Section 4.2.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that NPI naturally supports recursion but the original NPI paper didn't consider this. Can you clarify what specific aspect of the NPI architecture (beyond the LSTM reset) enables recursion, and why the original authors might have overlooked this possibility?

2. For the verification procedure, you note that it's infeasible for domains with very large input spaces. Could you provide a more concrete example of what would be required to verify a neural program for a task like sorting MNIST digits, and what modifications might be needed to your verification approach for such domains?

3. In the bubble sort experiments, you mention that partial recursion (only one recursive call) is insufficient for perfect generalization, while full recursion (three recursive calls) works. Could you elaborate on why partial recursion fails to achieve perfect generalization, and how this relates to the specific structure of the bubble sort algorithm?
