

# Peer Review for "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of neural programming by proposing that recursion is a necessary abstraction for neural architectures to achieve robust generalization. The authors demonstrate that by incorporating recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework, they can achieve provably perfect generalization on four programming tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort) with minimal training data. The key insight is that recursion reduces problem complexity by decomposing it into smaller instances with base cases and reduction rules, making it tractable to verify correct behavior across all possible inputs. The paper is the first to provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs, which is a significant theoretical advance beyond the standard empirical generalization metrics used in the field.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by establishing recursion as a necessary component for neural programs to generalize perfectly, and provides empirical evidence that this approach outperforms non-recursive versions on multiple tasks. The verification procedure they introduce is novel and represents a major step toward making neural programs more interpretable and trustworthy.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper excels in addressing a fundamental limitation in neural programming: the inability of existing models to generalize beyond a certain complexity threshold. The authors clearly articulate that current approaches learn spurious dependencies rather than true program semantics, which is a critical insight that the field has overlooked. By introducing recursion as a core component, they provide both a practical solution and a theoretical framework for understanding generalization in neural programs.

The empirical results are compelling and well-structured. For each task, they show that recursive neural programs achieve perfect generalization with minimal training data (e.g., training on just 200 traces for addition), while non-recursive versions fail on longer inputs. The verification procedure they propose is particularly noteworthy - by demonstrating that they can prove perfect generalization through verification of base cases and reduction rules, they move beyond empirical generalization to provide mathematical guarantees. This verification process is feasible because recursion reduces the problem to a finite set of cases that can be explicitly tested.

The paper is well-placed in the literature. It properly situates itself within the neural programming literature, cites relevant work (Vinyals et al., Reed & de Freitas, etc.), and clearly distinguishes its contribution from previous approaches. The authors correctly note that while NPI naturally supports recursion, previous implementations did not exploit this capability, which is an important observation.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance its impact:

1. The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the limitations of the verification procedure. While the authors acknowledge it's infeasible for large input domains (like MNIST), they could provide more concrete guidance on when this approach is applicable and when it's not.

2. The training procedure relies on generating recursive execution traces, which may not be straightforward for all problems. The authors mention future work on decreasing supervision, but it would be helpful to discuss potential challenges and strategies for learning recursion from less structured data.

3. The theoretical justification for why recursion enables provable generalization could be strengthened. While the paper demonstrates it works empirically, a more formal argument about the relationship between recursion and the tractability of verification would add depth.

4. The paper claims to be the first to investigate provable generalization properties of neural programs, but it would strengthen the argument to briefly address whether any other approaches (e.g., symbolic methods or hybrid architectures) have attempted similar verification.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In the verification procedure, how do you ensure that the constructed verification set V is complete (i.e., covers all possible base cases and reduction rules)? The paper mentions this can be done by iterating through observation mappings, but how can one be certain this process doesn't miss any edge cases?

2. The paper states that the recursive formulation for quicksort is "not purely tail recursive" and "represents a more complex kind of recursion." Could you elaborate on how this complexity affects the model's ability to learn recursion, and whether this complexity could be mitigated through architectural modifications?

3. For tasks where the input domain is continuous or high-dimensional (e.g., sorting arrays of floating-point numbers rather than integers), how would the verification procedure need to be adapted, and what would be the computational cost of doing so?

4. The paper mentions that "the training set can often be considerably smaller than the verification set." How does this affect the practical utility of the approach when the verification set is large, and what strategies could be employed to reduce the size of the verification set for more complex tasks?
