- Proposes recursion as a necessary abstraction for neural architectures to achieve robust generalization, implemented within the NPI framework.
- Shows empirically and claims provably perfect generalization on four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) with minimal training data.
- Argues recursion reduces problem complexity by decomposing into base cases and reduction rules, making verification tractable.
- Introduces a verification procedure that can prove perfect generalization, moving beyond standard empirical metrics.
- Claims to be the first work providing provable generalization guarantees for neural programs.
- Decision: Accept — strong theoretical contribution backed by empirical results.
- Identifies a core limitation of prior neural program models: learning spurious dependencies rather than true program semantics.
- Demonstrates that recursive neural programs outperform non-recursive versions on longer inputs and generalize from very small training sets (e.g., 200 traces for addition).
- Notes verification is feasible because recursion reduces the infinite input domain to a finite set of cases to test.
- Situates the work well in related literature and highlights prior NPI implementations did not exploit recursion.
- Suggests the paper should more explicitly discuss limitations and applicability boundaries of the verification procedure.
- Recommends discussing challenges and strategies for generating or learning recursive execution traces with less supervision.
- Advises strengthening the theoretical argument linking recursion to the tractability of verification.
- Suggests addressing whether other approaches (symbolic or hybrid) have attempted similar verification to contextualize the claim of novelty.
- Asks how the verification set V is guaranteed complete and how to avoid missing edge cases.
- Asks how quicksort’s non-tail/complex recursion affects the model’s ability to learn recursion and whether architectural changes could help.
- Asks how verification would adapt to continuous or high-dimensional input domains and what the computational cost would be.
- Asks how large verification sets impact practical utility and what strategies could reduce verification set size for complex tasks.
