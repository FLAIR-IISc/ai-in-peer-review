- Addresses neural program generalization failures by explicitly adding recursion to an NPI-style architecture, claiming recursion constrains per-call state and enables provable generalization.
- Implements recursion by modifying execution traces (not architecture) and evaluates on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.
- Shows recursive variants trained on few traces generalize to much larger instances and can be verified given finite coverage of base cases/reductions.
- Proposes a verification framework: define V as valid step input sequences per function call and verify model outputs match reference P on V to guarantee generalization.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason 1: Conceptual/practical advance — recursion is a crucial inductive bias that yields strong empirical generalization with a simple training mechanism.
- Key reason 2: Novel verification perspective — recursion makes behavior tractable to reason about and offers a path to provable guarantees.
- Situates work well in literature and convincingly argues why non-recursive controllers accumulate spurious long-range dependencies.
- Approach is simple and sound: resetting LSTM state per call makes per-call behaviors finite and verifiable.
- Tasks cover increasing structural complexity and demonstrate consistent empirical gains for recursive variants.
- Empirical results: recursive models achieve 100% accuracy across wide input regimes where baselines fail, and show sample efficiency.
- Verification demonstrated: analytical verification for addition and constructed V/empirical coverage for other tasks, leveraging bounded per-call behavior.
- Limitation: Requires full execution traces and hand-crafted recursive traces, limiting applicability versus IO-only training.
- Limitation: Completeness of V is formally proven only for addition; for other tasks V’s completeness is argued/empirically validated but not fully proven.
- Limitation: Domain-specific encoders expose discrete observations, so generality to perceptual inputs is untested.
- Limitation: Comparisons are primarily within NPI variants rather than against other neural programming architectures.
- Suggestion: Strengthen formalism around V with proof sketches or systematic constructions for tasks beyond addition.
- Suggestion: Add ablations quantifying effects of hidden-state reset, recursion types, and specific environment observations.
- Suggestion: Evaluate robustness under noisy encoders or slight misspecifications to assess brittleness of guarantees.
- Suggestion: Compare with alternative controllers (e.g., DNC) using equivalent recursive traces to isolate recursion’s effect.
- Suggestion: Provide preliminary experiments toward weaker supervision (partial traces or IO pairs with inductive biases).
- Question 1: Can you formally prove V’s completeness for topological sort, quicksort, and bubble sort beyond empirical coverage?
- Question 2: How sensitive are results to encoder choices (e.g., raw values vs comparison predicates) and how does that affect V’s size/feasibility?
- Question 3: Is hidden-state reset per call strictly necessary, or can architectures retaining caller context still yield guarantees with interface restrictions?
- Question 4: Have you tried starting from non-recursive traces to let the model discover recursion automatically, and what were the results?
- Question 5: For quicksort, how does stack depth scale on long arrays, were there stability issues, and does the method extend to other divide-and-conquer algorithms?
- Question 6: Can you characterize task classes where base cases/reductions are small enough for practical verification versus tasks where this approach breaks down?
