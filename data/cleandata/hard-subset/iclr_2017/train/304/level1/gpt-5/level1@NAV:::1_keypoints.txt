- Tackles poor generalization and limited interpretability in neural program induction by making recursion a first-class abstraction.  
- Reformulates NPI execution traces to include recursive calls, leveraging call stack and hidden-state reset.  
- Recursion constrains hidden-state dependencies, decomposes problems into base cases and reductions, and enables verification-based proofs of perfect generalization.  
- Demonstrates recursive NPI on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort with strong generalization from small training sets.  
- Provides a verification procedure that proves correctness by covering all base cases and reduction rules.  
- Well situated in prior work (NTM, DNC, Neural GPU, NPI) and explains failures of non-recursive approaches.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Reason 1: Clear, well-motivated conceptual contribution—explicit recursion in neural program architectures with a principled verification method.  
- Reason 2: Convincing empirical results across diverse tasks and the first demonstration of provable generalization in this setting.  
- Supporting: Leverages NPI’s call stack and hidden-state reset to avoid spurious length dependencies.  
- Supporting: Verification methodology is sound for discrete algorithmic tasks by constructing reachable step-input sequences V.  
- Supporting: Empirical evaluations show consistent improvements (full recursion) and useful ablations (partial vs full recursion).  
- Suggestion: Clarify and formalize completeness conditions for constructing V, with a proof sketch or algorithmic guarantee.  
- Suggestion: Report sensitivity to mis-specified V and model variance (random seeds); include failure analyses.  
- Suggestion: Provide ablations separating supervision level from recursion (e.g., non-recursive traces with equivalent supervision).  
- Suggestion: Discuss generalization beyond discrete symbolic tasks and ways to auto-discover recursion without full traces.  
- Suggestion: Release code and verification artifacts to aid reproducibility.  
- Questions: How is completeness of V certified in practice, especially for topological sort and quicksort where V is empirically generated?  
- Questions: What is the impact of small omissions or noise in V on proofs and empirical behavior?  
- Questions: Can you quantify training-data efficiency gains attributable specifically to recursion versus trace richness?  
- Questions: How robust are results across random seeds and architectural variations (LSTM size, encoders)?  
- Questions: Could the verification pipeline be automated from a program specification P, and how would it scale to very large or continuous base cases?
