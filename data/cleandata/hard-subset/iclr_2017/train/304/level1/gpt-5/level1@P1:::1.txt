Summary and contributions
The paper tackles the poor and unpredictable generalization of neural program learning by explicitly incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures. Concretely, the authors show that the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) already provides the needed call/stack abstraction, and that by modifying training traces to be recursive, one can: (a) drastically improve generalization on algorithmic tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) from very small training sets; and (b) make it tractable to reason about, and even “prove,” perfect generalization via a finite verification set that covers base cases and reduction rules. The paper also describes a verification procedure to construct the set of reachable environment observations (V) and synthesize a corresponding finite verification input set Sv; validating the learned model on Sv constitutes a proof of perfect generalization under the model’s observation interface. Empirically, the recursive variants achieve 100% accuracy on much larger inputs than seen in training, and the verification passes for all four tasks.

Decision: Accept (weak accept)
Key reasons:
- Clear, well-motivated idea with significant impact: enforcing recursion aligns the learned control flow with true program semantics, yielding strong generalization and enabling tractable verification.
- Solid empirical evidence across four diverse algorithmic tasks and a principled verification methodology that is, to my knowledge, novel in neural program learning.

Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: Generalization of neural program learners to inputs far beyond the training regime is unreliable; current models learn spurious dependencies (e.g., input length) and offer no guarantees.
- Motivation and placement: Recursion is the canonical mechanism in programming languages to decompose problems via base cases and reductions. Bringing this abstraction into neural program learners is both conceptually sound and practically enabled by NPI’s call stack and reset of hidden state on subcalls. The paper situates itself well among NTM/DNC/Neural GPU/NPI/PTRNet literature and fills a clear gap: provable generalization.
- Method: No architectural change is needed; the key is to provide recursive execution traces for training. This reduces the state space each subprogram must handle (hidden state is reset per call), eliminates length dependence, and makes the observation domain finite and enumerable for verification. The verification procedure is reasonable: define the reachable observation set V for each subprogram (based on base cases and reduction rules), synthesize Sv to cover V, and check exact trace/output matches.
- Results: On addition, both non-recursive and recursive generalize to thousands of digits, but only the recursive variant can be verified. With minimal data (5 1-digit sums), the recursive model composes to long sums whereas the non-recursive fails. On bubble sort, full recursion yields perfect accuracy while non-recursive and partially recursive variants degrade beyond short arrays. On topological sort, the recursive variant trained on a single trace generalizes up to ≥70 nodes with 100% accuracy, while the non-recursive version fails from size 8; with more training traces both generalize up to 120, but only the recursive can be verified. On quicksort, the recursive variant remains perfect while the non-recursive degrades beyond length 11. The authors further verify perfect generalization via finite Sv (e.g., addition: 20,181 cases; topo: 73 graphs; quicksort: a single 10-element array covering V).

Caveats and limitations (do not outweigh the contributions, but should be acknowledged)
- The “provably perfect generalization” relies on correctness and completeness of the constructed V and Sv, which are hand-specified or empirically covered, and on the observation interface being sufficiently abstract to make V finite. The paper is transparent about this and discusses infeasibility for high-dimensional inputs.
- Supervision is strong: recursive traces are provided and essentially impose the recursive control flow; the model imitates rather than discovers recursion. Nonetheless, demonstrating that this inductive bias is key to generalization is valuable.
- Comparisons are limited largely to non-recursive NPI; broader baselines (e.g., DNC, Neural GPU) are absent.
- Some evaluation details are light (e.g., variance over random seeds, training stability, more extensive test suites).

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Clarify the two topological sort settings: the section reports both “non-recursive generalizes up to 120” and “non-recursive fails beyond 8 when trained on a single trace.” Make the training regimes explicit side by side.
- Strengthen baselines: include NPI with curriculum but no recursive traces, and (if feasible) DNC/Neural GPU on the same tasks.
- Provide ablations: effect of hidden-state reset removal; effect of making only some functions recursive (you partially do this for bubble sort); sensitivity to α (return threshold).
- Automate verification more: formalize/automate generation of V from the reference program P, and report coverage metrics to ensure Sv completeness.
- Report compute and parameter sizes, training time, and success rates over random initializations.
- Discuss recursion depth and stack behavior in practice (e.g., distribution of depths on long inputs; tail-call optimization details).
- Release code, traces, and verification scripts.

Questions for the authors
- How do you ensure completeness of V in practice when it is constructed empirically (e.g., for topo sort and quicksort)? Do you have a mechanical check that all observation-to-observation transitions implied by P are covered?
- For quicksort, you implement non-tail recursion with two recursive calls. How do you manage hidden-state dependencies across the two calls? Is resetting at each call sufficient to guarantee independence, and could this ever mask necessary cross-call information?
- In bubble sort, why does partial recursion (only the outer loop) not suffice, whereas making BSTEP and LSHIFT recursive yields perfect generalization? Can you quantify which unreachable observation sequences remain without full recursion?
- How robust are the models to slight changes in the domain-specific encoder (e.g., exposing absolute values vs relational predicates) beyond the verification-friendly variant? Does generalization or verification break under alternative encodings?
- Did you observe failures due to stack depth (e.g., quicksort on adversarial arrays)? Any mitigation beyond tail recursion (which does not apply to quicksort)?
- Can the approach discover recursion from non-recursive traces or partial traces (e.g., via differentiable call stacks or search over call graphs), and what failure modes do you anticipate?

Overall, the paper presents a simple, well-argued idea that materially advances generalization and verifiability in neural program learning. Despite strong supervision and limited baselines, the contribution is timely and significant.
