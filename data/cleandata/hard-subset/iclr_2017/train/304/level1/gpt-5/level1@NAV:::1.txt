Summary and contributions
This paper tackles the central problem of poor generalization and limited interpretability in neural program induction, proposing recursion as a first-class abstraction within neural programming architectures. By reformulating execution traces to include recursive calls in NPI, the authors demonstrate that recursion constrains hidden-state dependencies, decomposes problems into finite base cases and reductions, and enables verification-based proofs of perfect generalization. Across four algorithmic tasks—grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort—the recursive variants achieve strong generalization from small training sets and, crucially, are accompanied by a verification procedure that proves correctness by covering all base cases and reduction rules. The paper is well situated in the literature (NTM, DNC, Neural GPU, NPI, etc.) and clearly articulates why non-recursive approaches struggle.

Decision: Accept
Reasons: (1) Clear, well-motivated conceptual contribution—making recursion explicit in neural program architectures—to address generalization, with a principled verification procedure. (2) Convincing empirical results across diverse tasks, with small training data, and the first demonstration (to my knowledge) of provable generalization in this setting.

Supporting arguments
- The approach is well placed and motivated: it leverages NPI’s call stack and hidden-state reset to realize recursion naturally, directly addressing spurious length dependencies that plague non-recursive solutions.  
- The verification methodology is sound in scope: by constructing the set of reachable step-input sequences V and checking base cases/reductions, it provides a tractable proof strategy for discrete algorithmic tasks.  
- Empirical evaluations show consistent improvements (e.g., full-recursive bubble sort and quicksort) and parity where expected (addition), with ablations (partial vs full recursion) illustrating necessity of full recursive structure.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify and formalize the completeness conditions for constructing V; a brief proof sketch or algorithmic guarantee would strengthen the “provably perfect” claim.  
- Report sensitivity to mis-specified V and to model variance (seeds), and provide failure analyses.  
- Provide ablations disentangling supervision level vs recursion (e.g., non-recursive traces with equivalent supervisory signal).  
- Discuss generalization beyond discrete symbolic tasks, and pathways to automate discovery of recursion without full traces.  
- Release code and verification artifacts to aid reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- How do you certify that V is complete in practice, especially for topological sort and quicksort where V is generated empirically?  
- What is the impact of small omissions or noise in V on the proof and on empirical behavior?  
- Can you quantify training-data efficiency gains attributable specifically to recursion (vs trace richness)?  
- How robust are the results across random seeds and architectural variations (LSTM size, encoders)?  
- Could the verification pipeline be automated from a specification of P, and how might this extend to tasks with very large or continuous base cases?
