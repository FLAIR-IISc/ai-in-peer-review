- Claim: Explicit recursion is a key abstraction for neural program learning and generalization, implemented by adding recursive execution traces to NPI.  
- Empirical result: Recursive variants on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort generalize far beyond training regimes and outperform non-recursive baselines, often achieving 100% accuracy on much larger inputs.  
- Verification methodology: Define base cases and reduction rules, construct the finite set V of step-input sequences for recursive calls, and verify the learned model matches a reference program P on V to obtain provable generalization if V is complete.  
- Decision: Accept — primary reasons are a clear conceptual contribution (recursion + principled verification) and strong empirical demonstrations including non-tail recursion.  
- Problem addressed: Targets neural program learners’ failure to generalize to larger inputs caused by spurious training dependencies (e.g., length).  
- Motivation: Recursion restricts behavior, resets hidden state across calls, and decomposes problems into fixed-complexity subcalls that do not scale with input size.  
- Technical point: NPI already supports call stacks and hidden-state resets; adding recursive calls in supervision induces recursive behavior with reasonable use of tail recursion where applicable.  
- Verification argument: Coherent induction-based argument — if V captures all reachable step-input sequences per subprogram and the model matches P on V, correctness follows; depends critically on precise construction of V.  
- Empirical consistency: Results show recursive models achieve perfect generalization while non-recursive baselines degrade sharply; quicksort demonstrates handling of non-tail recursion and global pointer interactions.  
- Novelty: Training explicitly recursive neural programs and proposing a verification pathway for provable generalization is a novel, valuable conceptual/methodological contribution.  
- Clarity: Paper is generally clear with concrete recursive traces, domain-specific encoders, detailed appendices, and a useful description of the verification procedure.  
- Limitation — supervision: Approach requires full execution traces with explicit recursion; learning recursion from IO pairs or partial traces is left for future work.  
- Limitation — verification completeness: For some tasks V is derived analytically (addition) but for others coverage is argued via empirical saturation; guarantees hinge on V’s completeness.  
- Limitation — comparisons: Empirical baselines focus on non-recursive NPI variants; broader comparisons (e.g., DNC) are not included.  
- Suggested improvement: Formalize or algorithmically construct V from a specification of P and, if possible, prove completeness for tasks like topological sort and quicksort.  
- Suggested metrics: Report explicit coverage metrics — size of V, number of distinct step-input sequences tested per subprogram, and evidence that no new sequences appear as inputs scale.  
- Suggested ablations: Study the effect of hidden-state reset, sensitivity to recursive trace shapes, and dependence on domain-specific encoders.  
- Suggested experiments: Expand stress tests (longer arrays/graphs), report failure rates with confidence intervals, and discuss stack depth limits and any tail recursion optimizations.  
- Reproducibility request: Release code, traces, and verification sets and provide hyperparameters, training times, and seeds.  
- Question on V construction: For topological sort and quicksort, provide precise characterizations or proofs that constructed V includes all reachable step-input sequences and explain how coverage completeness is detected.  
- Question on encoder sensitivity: How sensitive is V to small changes in domain encoders and primitive sets, and would guarantees hold with original encoders?  
- Question on verification robustness: For quicksort, enumerate the distinct step-input sequences covered (e.g., why a single 10-element array suffices) and argue why no additional sequences arise for larger arrays or hidden-state interactions.  
- Question on learning recursion: Can the model discover recursion from IO pairs or weaker supervision, and how sensitive are results to the exact placement of recursive calls?  
- Question on scalability: How does the approach handle tasks with large or continuous observation spaces where base cases/reductions are large or infinite, and are approximate/symbolic abstractions possible?  
- Question on implementation limits: Were stack overflows or recursion-depth limits encountered during inference, and how were they handled?
