- Paper argues explicit recursion is a key abstraction for neural program learning to improve length/complexity generalization and enable post-training guarantees.
- Authors show how to train recursive programs within NPI by altering supervision traces to include recursive calls.
- Empirically, recursion drastically improves generalization on four tasks: addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, with small training sets.
- They propose a verification methodology that reduces correctness to checking a finite set of base cases and reduction rules using stack-based call frames and hidden-state resets.
- Central insight: recursion decomposes the input space into bounded subproblems, making learning and post hoc reasoning tractable.
- Empirical section is modest but diverse (arrays and graphs), and the verification angle is novel for neural programming.
- Decision: Accept (weak).
- Key reason: Conceptual clarity and practical efficacy—recursive traces are simple and yield strong generalization where prior methods fail with input size.
- Key reason: Verification contribution—reducing generalization guarantees to checking a finite set of environment-observation sequences is valuable.
- Problem targeted: poor length/structure generalization and opacity of neural program learners; reframes objective around recursive decomposition (base cases + reductions).
- Motivation and placement: strong motivation; recursion naturally aligns with NPI’s call-stack and addresses long-range hidden-state entanglement.
- Literature positioning: mostly adequate and plausibly first to study provable generalization for neural-network-based program representations.
- Empirical support: recursive traces consistently outperform non-recursive and partially-recursive variants, often achieving 100% on large inputs.
- Verification validity: convincing when observation space is finite and call-frame resets strictly bound per-call sequences; authors caution infeasibility for large/continuous observations.
- Limitation: Proof strategy depends on constructing the exact set V of reachable step-input sequences; for some tasks V was obtained by reasoning plus random generation, which is less formal and could miss corner cases.
- Limitation: Domain-specific encoders sometimes expose high-level predicates that simplify learning/verification, weakening architectural generality claims.
- Limitation: Supervision requires fully specified recursive traces, leaving open whether recursion can be discovered from IO pairs or partial traces.
- Suggested improvement: Calibrate “provably perfect generalization” claims; formalize V construction and provide reproducible procedures and coverage checks.
- Suggested improvement: Provide complete quantitative results, report means/variances across seeds, and clarify truncated tables.
- Suggested ablations: test effect of hidden-state reset, full vs partial recursion, encoder design (raw values vs predicates), and data-efficiency curves.
- Suggested improvement: Broaden baselines to other neural program learners (DNC/NTM/Neural GPU) on same tasks and budgets.
- Suggested improvement: Automate verification by constructing V algorithmically (state-transition closure) for at least one task to show practicality.
- Suggested improvement: Release code and verification scripts for reproducibility.
- Question: How do you certify completeness of V for topological sort and quicksort; provide exact cardinalities and evidence (e.g., transition-graph closure).
- Question: How sensitive are learning and verification results to encoder choice; what happens with raw-value-only encoders?
- Question: Within subcalls the LSTM maintains state—what is the maximum unrolled length per subcall and why does verifying over V suffice given internal dynamics?
- Question: Can recursion be discovered from non-recursive traces or IO pairs; any experiments or ideas to induce recursion automatically?
- Question: How robust is the method to noisy traces or distribution shifts in the observation encoder?
- Question: What recursion depths occur on largest inputs; any stack overflows or need for tail-call optimization in practice?
