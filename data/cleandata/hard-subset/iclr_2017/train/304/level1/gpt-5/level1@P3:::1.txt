Summary and contributions
The paper argues that explicit recursion is a key abstraction for neural program learning, both to improve length/complexity generalization and to enable post-training guarantees. Concretely, the authors (i) show how to train recursive programs within a Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) by altering supervision traces to include recursive calls, (ii) demonstrate that recursion drastically improves generalization on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) with small training sets, and (iii) propose a verification methodology that reduces correctness to checking a finite set of base cases and reduction rules by exploiting stack-based call frames and the reset of the core hidden state at call boundaries. The paper’s central insight—that recursion decomposes the input space into bounded subproblems, making both learning and post hoc reasoning tractable—is clear and compelling. The empirical section, while modest, is diverse (arrays and graphs), and the verification angle is novel in the neural programming literature.

Decision: Accept (weak)
Key reasons:
- Conceptual clarity and practical efficacy: Introducing recursion into NPI via recursive traces is simple yet effective, yielding strong generalization on tasks where prior works degrade sharply with input size.
- Verification contribution: The reduction of generalization guarantees to checking a finite set of environment-observation sequences is a valuable step toward scientifically rigorous claims about neural program behavior.

Supporting arguments
1) What problem is tackled? The paper targets the poor length/structure generalization and opacity of neural program learners. It reframes the learning objective around recursive decomposition (base cases + reductions), aiming for both robust generalization and tractable verification.

2) Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? The motivation is strong: most neural algorithm-learning papers report initial generalization gains followed by failures beyond a threshold; the authors identify the culprit as long-range hidden-state dependencies and entanglement. Recursion is a standard programming abstraction to control complexity and aligns naturally with NPI’s call stack and hidden-state reset. The literature positioning is mostly adequate (NTM/DNC/Neural GPU/NPI/Pointer Networks, etc.), and the claim of being first to study provable generalization of neural programs appears plausible for neural-network-based program representations (contrast with symbolic DSL-based program synthesis that can be verified, but is not directly comparable).

3) Do the results support the claims? Empirically, yes: recursive traces consistently outperform non-recursive and “partially recursive” variants, often achieving 100% over large input ranges (e.g., bubble sort and quicksort on much longer arrays; topological sort on graphs up to 120 vertices). The verification methodology is convincing in settings where (a) the environment observations are finite and fully captured by the chosen encoder and (b) call-frame resets strictly bound the step-input sequences per call. The paper is appropriately cautious that verification is infeasible when the observation space is very large/continuous.

That said, some aspects temper the strength of the “provably perfect generalization” claim:
- The proof strategy hinges on constructing the exact set V of reachable step-input observation sequences under the reference program P. For several tasks (e.g., topological sort, quicksort), V is obtained via “careful reasoning” plus random generation to cover cases, which is less formal than a constructive proof and could miss corner cases. The authors acknowledge this, but the abstract/intro language is stronger than the methodology warrants.
- Domain-specific encoders sometimes expose high-level predicates (e.g., bubble sort variant exposing ≤), which simplify verification and learning but bake in structure that may not be available more generally; this weakens claims about architectural generality.
- The supervision assumes fully specified recursive traces. This shows that NPI can execute recursion, but leaves open whether recursion can be discovered from IO pairs or partial traces.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all are decision-critical)
- Calibrate the “provably perfect generalization” claim: emphasize that guarantees hold relative to a finite observation abstraction and a constructed V that must be complete; tighten the formalization and provide a reproducible procedure (or proof) for V for each task. Include counts and coverage checks (e.g., graph properties needed to hit each observation transition).
- Provide all quantitative results clearly (some tables in the draft appear truncated). Report means/variances over seeds and failure modes.
- Ablations:
  - Effect of hidden-state reset: what happens if you do not reset h at subcall boundaries?
  - Full vs partial recursion (you partially report this for bubble sort; expand and analyze why partial recursion fails).
  - Impact of encoder design (raw values vs predicates; how does this affect both learning and verification effort)?
  - Data efficiency curves (accuracy vs number of traces).
- Broaden baselines: compare with other neural program learners (e.g., DNC/NTM/Neural GPU where applicable) on the same tasks and training budgets.
- Automating verification: sketch or implement an algorithmic construction of V from P (state-transition closure), even for one task, to demonstrate practicality beyond manual reasoning.
- Release code and verification scripts to support reproducibility and future work.

Questions for the authors
1) Completeness of V: For topological sort and quicksort, how do you certify that the constructed verification set SV covers all reachable step-input sequences? Can you provide the exact cardinalities of V per subprogram and evidence (e.g., transition-graph closure) that your SV induces them all?
2) Encoder dependence: How sensitive are results (both learning and verification) to the chosen environment observations? Have you tried encoders exposing raw values only for all tasks, and if so, what changes in V’s size and the model’s generalization?
3) Hidden state and recursion: Within a subcall, the LSTM still maintains state across steps. Can you quantify the maximum unrolled length per subcall across tasks and argue why verifying step outputs over V suffices despite the internal state dynamics?
4) Discovery of recursion: Have you attempted training on non-recursive traces or IO pairs and observed whether the model learns to emit recursive calls? Any ideas or preliminary results on architectural biases or training curricula that induce recursion automatically?
5) Robustness: How does the method handle noisy traces or minor distribution shift in the observation encoder (e.g., unseen numbers, different graph degree distributions)?
6) Resource usage: What are the recursion depths reached at test time on the largest inputs? Any stack overflows or need for tail-call optimization in practice?

Overall, this paper provides a clear and practically useful insight—make neural programs recursive—and backs it with evidence across multiple tasks plus a promising verification methodology. With improved formalization and ablations, it would make a strong contribution.
