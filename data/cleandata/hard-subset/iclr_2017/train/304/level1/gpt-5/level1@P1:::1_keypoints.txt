- Proposal: Incorporate recursion into neural program learning by training NPI with recursive traces to improve generalization and enable tractable verification.  
- Decision: Accept (weak accept).  
- Key reason: Enforcing recursion aligns learned control flow with true program semantics, improving generalization and enabling verification.  
- Empirical strength: Solid results across four algorithmic tasks and a novel, principled verification methodology.  
- Problem addressed: Neural program learners generalize poorly and learn spurious dependencies without guarantees.  
- Motivation: Recursion provides base-case/reduction decomposition; NPI’s call/stack and hidden-state reset naturally support it.  
- Method: No architecture change—train with recursive execution traces; define reachable observation set V and synthesize finite verification set Sv to prove generalization.  
- Verification idea: Enumerate finite observation domain V per subprogram, construct Sv covering V, and validate learned model on Sv as a proof.  
- Results summary: Recursive variants achieve 100% accuracy on much larger inputs and verification passes for addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Addition results: Both variants generalize to long sums, but only recursive is verifiable; minimal training data suffices for recursion to compose.  
- Bubble sort results: Full recursion yields perfect accuracy; non-recursive and partially-recursive variants break on longer arrays.  
- Topological sort results: Recursive model trained on a single trace generalizes to ≥70 nodes with 100% accuracy; non-recursive fails beyond size 8 with a single trace.  
- Quicksort results: Recursive variant remains perfect; non-recursive degrades past length 11.  
- Verification set examples: addition Sv=20,181 cases; topo Sv=73 graphs; quicksort verified with a single 10-element array.  
- Caveat—verification limits: Proofs depend on correctness/completeness of hand-specified or empirically-derived V/Sv and on having a finite observation interface.  
- Caveat—supervision: Training uses strong supervision (recursive traces), so the model imitates provided recursion rather than discovering it.  
- Caveat—baselines and eval: Comparisons focus on non-recursive NPI; broader baselines (DNC, Neural GPU) and evaluation details (seed variance, stability) are limited.  
- Suggested improvements: Clarify topo training regimes, add stronger baselines, provide ablations (hidden-state reset, partial recursion, α sensitivity), and report compute/params/training time and seed variance.  
- Suggested verification work: Automate synthesis of V from reference program P and report coverage metrics for Sv.  
- Suggested reproducibility: Release code, traces, and verification scripts.  
- Implementation question: How is completeness of V ensured when constructed empirically for topo and quicksort?  
- Implementation question: How are hidden-state dependencies managed across quicksort’s two recursive calls, and can resets mask needed cross-call info?  
- Implementation question: Why does partial recursion in bubble sort fail while making inner steps recursive yields perfect generalization?  
- Robustness question: How sensitive are results to domain encoder choices (relational vs absolute encodings)?  
- Reliability question: Any observed failures due to stack depth on adversarial inputs and mitigations beyond tail-call optimization?  
- Future direction: Can recursion be discovered from non-recursive/partial traces (e.g., via differentiable stacks or search), and what are expected failure modes?  
- Overall assessment: A simple, well-argued contribution that materially advances generalization and verifiability in neural program learning despite strong supervision and limited baselines.
