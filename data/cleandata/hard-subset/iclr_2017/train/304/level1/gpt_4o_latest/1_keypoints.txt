- Proposes incorporating recursion into neural architectures (NPI) to improve generalization and interpretability in program learning.  
- Implements recursive NPI and evaluates on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Demonstrates recursive models generalize better to more complex inputs and longer sequences than non-recursive models.  
- Introduces a verification procedure that provides provable guarantees of perfect generalization for learned programs.  
- Recommended for acceptance due to strong motivation, empirical and theoretical rigor, and novel provable guarantees.  
- Well-motivated framing: recursion decomposes problems and addresses known generalization limits of existing neural program learners.  
- Empirical rigor: comprehensive experiments showing recursive models achieve perfect generalization on tasks where non-recursive models fail.  
- Theoretical contribution: reduction to base cases and reduction rules makes verification tractable and groundbreaking for neural program synthesis.  
- Clarify verification procedure: explanation is dense and needs more examples or visual aids for how verification sets are constructed and used.  
- Compare with non-recursive models: provide deeper analysis of spurious dependencies causing their generalization failures.  
- Scalability concern: verification may not scale to large or infinite input domains; discuss potential extensions or mitigations.  
- Broader applicability: discuss how the recursive approach could extend to other domains like NLP or reinforcement learning.  
- Questions: how does recursion handle inherently iterative tasks, can verification be automated/integrated into training, and how sensitive are results to training data quality?
