**Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions**

The paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural architectures to improve generalization and interpretability. The authors argue that recursion is essential for neural programs to generalize and provide a framework for learning recursive neural programs using the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. The paper claims that the learned recursive programs achieve perfect generalization from a small number of training examples and provide a verification procedure to prove the correctness of the learned programs.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper provides a well-motivated approach to addressing the problem of poor generalization in neural programming architectures. The use of recursion is a key insight that enables the learning of more interpretable and generalizable programs. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach on a range of tasks.

**Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

1. **Well-motivated approach**: The paper provides a clear and well-motivated approach to addressing the problem of poor generalization in neural programming architectures. The use of recursion is a natural way to decompose complex problems into smaller sub-problems, making it easier to reason about the behavior of the learned programs.
2. **Effective experimental results**: The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach on a range of tasks, including grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. The learned recursive programs achieve perfect generalization from a small number of training examples, and the verification procedure provides a way to prove the correctness of the learned programs.
3. **Significant contribution**: The paper provides a significant contribution to the field of neural programming by introducing a new approach to learning recursive programs. The use of recursion enables the learning of more interpretable and generalizable programs, which is a key challenge in neural programming.

**Additional Feedback**

1. **Clarity and organization**: The paper is well-written and easy to follow. However, some sections could be improved for clarity and organization. For example, the section on the verification procedure could be expanded to provide more details on how the procedure works.
2. **Related work**: The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive review of related work on neural programming and recursion. This would help to situate the paper's contributions in the context of existing research.
3. **Future work**: The paper mentions several avenues for future work, including exploring new ways to train recursive neural programs and developing novel neural programming architectures integrated directly with a notion of recursion. It would be helpful to provide more details on these potential future directions and how they could build on the paper's contributions.

**Questions for the Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how the verification procedure works, including how the set of valid sequences of step inputs (V) is constructed and how the learned program is verified against this set?
2. How do you plan to address the challenge of constructing a verification set that covers all cases for tasks with very large input domains, such as those involving MNIST digits or speech samples?
3. Can you provide more details on how the learned recursive programs are used in practice, including how they are deployed and how they perform on real-world tasks?
