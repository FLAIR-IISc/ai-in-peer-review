- Paper argues recursion handling is crucial for neural programming architectures, enabling strong out-of-domain generalization and learning from less data.  
- Work builds on Reed & de Freitas (Neural Programmer-Interpreters), mainly changing training traces to include recursive calls.  
- Authors train NPI models on recursive traces and show how to verify correctness by testing only a small set of base cases and reduction rules.  
- Empirical result: NPI perfectly learns Bubblesort and Tower of Hanoi using recursive traces.  
- The idea is very simple—the only modification is to the execution traces presented during training.  
- Reviewer questions the takeaway: does access to execution traces effectively solve neural program induction, or was the problem too easy?  
- Example concern: in larger input domains (e.g., sorting MNIST digits), execution traces decouple perception from program logic, making the task trivialize into separate problems.  
- Open question posed: what problems exist where execution traces are available but the proposed method still fails to infer the program?
