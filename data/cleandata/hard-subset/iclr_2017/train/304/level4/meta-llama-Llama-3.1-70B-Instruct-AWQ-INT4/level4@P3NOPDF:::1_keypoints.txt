- Paper shows handling recursion in neural programming (NPI) enables strong generalization to out-of-domain cases and learning from less training data.
- Builds on Neural Programmer Interpreters (Reed & de Freitas 2016) by training NPI on execution traces that include recursive calls.
- Introduces a verification method using a small set of base cases and reduction rules to prove learned program correctness.
- Demonstrates impressive results: NPI perfectly infers Bubblesort and Tower of Hanoi when trained with recursive traces.
- Main modification is simple—adding recursive calls to training traces—highlighting the power of trace design.
- Raises concern that availability of execution traces may effectively simplify or trivialize the neural programming problem.
- Gives example (MNIST sorting) where traces decouple perception from program logic, questioning the generality of results.
- Asks for identification of problem domains where traces are available but the proposed method fails to infer correct programs.
- Calls for testing on more complex problems and scenarios where program logic is intricate despite trace availability.
- Recommends comparisons with other neural programming architectures to contextualize the method’s strengths and limitations.
- Concludes the work is a significant, simple contribution but requires broader evaluation to understand scope and limits.
