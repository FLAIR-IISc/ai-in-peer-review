This paper highlights the importance of handling recursion in neural programming architectures, demonstrating that it enables strong generalization to out-of-domain test cases and learning from smaller amounts of training data. The majority of the paper builds upon the Reed & de Freitas paper on Neural Programmer Interpreters (NPI) from ICLR 2016, which learns from program traces. The key innovation in this paper is training NPI models on traces that include recursive calls.

The authors present a method for verifying the correctness of the learned program by evaluating it on a small set of base cases and reduction rules. Notably, they show that the NPI architecture is capable of perfectly inferring the Bubblesort and Tower of Hanoi problems. The simplicity of the idea is a significant advantage, as acknowledged by the authors, with the only modification being the execution traces provided to the training pipeline.

A question arises regarding the implications of this work: does it effectively solve the neural programming problem when execution traces are available? This raises the possibility that the problem may have been less challenging than initially thought. To further investigate this, consider a more complex input domain, such as MNIST digits, where the NPI must infer how to sort MNIST digits from highest to lowest. In this scenario, having execution traces would essentially decouple the problem of recognizing digits from inferring program logic, making the task no more difficult than learning to recognize MNIST digits and bubble sort from symbols.

A more challenging problem would be one where execution traces are available, but the proposed method is unable to infer the program. Identifying such a problem would help to better understand the limitations and potential of the approach presented in this paper.
