This paper posits that the ability to handle recursion is crucial for neural programming architectures, as it enables strong generalization to out-of-domain test cases and learning from limited training data. The majority of the paper builds upon the Reed & de Freitas paper on Neural Programmer Interpreters from ICLR 2016, which learns from program traces, with the key difference being that this paper trains NPI models on traces containing recursive calls. The authors demonstrate how to verify correctness by evaluating the learned program on a small set of base cases and reduction rules, showcasing the NPI architecture's ability to perfectly infer Bubblesort and the Tower of Hanoi problems.

The simplicity of the idea is appealing, as acknowledged by the authors, with the only modification being the execution traces provided to the training pipeline. However, it raises questions about the implications of this finding. Does this mean that the neural programming problem is effectively solved when execution traces are available, or was the problem inherently less challenging? For instance, considering a larger input domain like MNIST digits, where the NPI must infer how to sort digits from highest to lowest, having execution traces would essentially decouple the problem of digit recognition from program logic inference, making it no more difficult than learning to recognize MNIST digits and bubble sort from symbols. A crucial question remains: what problem, with accessible execution traces, cannot be inferred using the proposed method?
