This paper argues that being able to handle recursion is very important for neural programming architectures — that handling recursion allows for strong generalization to out of domain test cases and learning from smaller amounts of training data. Most of the paper is a riff on the Reed & de Freitas paper on Neural Programmer Interpreters from ICLR 2016 which learns from program traces — this paper trains NPI models on traces that have recursive calls. The authors show how to verify correctness by evaluating the learned program on only a small set of base cases and reduction rules and impressively, show that the NPI architecture is able to perfectly infer Bubblesort and the Tower of Hanoi problems.

**Decision:** Accept

**Key reasons for this choice:** The paper proposes a simple yet effective approach to handling recursion in neural programming architectures, which allows for strong generalization to out of domain test cases and learning from smaller amounts of training data. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by training NPI models on traces that have recursive calls and verifying the correctness of the learned program on a small set of base cases and reduction rules.

**Supporting arguments for the reasons for the decision:**

* The paper presents a clear and well-motivated approach to handling recursion in neural programming architectures.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several tasks, including Bubblesort and the Tower of Hanoi problems.
* The paper provides a thorough analysis of the benefits of handling recursion in neural programming architectures, including improved generalization and learning from smaller amounts of training data.

**Additional feedback:**

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach, particularly in terms of the types of problems that can be solved using this method.
* The authors may want to consider exploring the application of their approach to more complex problems, such as those involving large input domains like MNIST digits.
* The paper could also benefit from a more detailed analysis of the relationship between the proposed approach and other methods for handling recursion in neural programming architectures.

**Questions for the authors:**
