- Argues recursion is crucial for neural programming, enabling strong generalization and learning from less training data.  
- Extends Neural Programmer-Interpreter (Reed & de Freitas) by training NPI on execution traces that include recursive calls.  
- Verifies correctness by evaluating learned programs on a small set of base cases and reduction rules.  
- Demonstrates NPI can perfectly infer BubbleSort and tasks like grade-school addition, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Method’s change is simple: only modify training traces to include recursion.  
- Raises the question whether having execution traces effectively solves neural programming or makes the task too easy.  
- Example concern: with traces, tasks like sorting MNIST digits decouple perception from program logic, simplifying the problem.  
- Asks what realistic problems have execution traces available but cannot be inferred by this method.  
- Reviewer accepts the paper, citing novel incorporation of recursion, improved generalization, and provable guarantees.  
- Provides strong empirical evidence across four tasks showing perfect generalization from few examples.  
- Verification procedure is an important contribution for proving learned program correctness.  
- Limitation: approach requires access to execution traces, which may not always be available.  
- Authors argue traces can often be obtained by running programs on small input sets.  
- Suggested additions: more training details (hyperparameters, iterations) and more analysis/visualizations of learned programs.  
- Claim that approach generalizes to other neural programming architectures lacks experimental examples.  
- Questions for authors: more details on the verification procedure and its applicability, plans to handle the trace-requirement limitation, and deeper analysis of learned program behavior.
