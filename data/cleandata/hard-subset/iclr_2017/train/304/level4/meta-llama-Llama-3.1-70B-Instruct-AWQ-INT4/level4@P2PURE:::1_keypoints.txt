- Paper argues that incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures yields strong out-of-domain generalization and reduced training-data needs.
- Work extends Reed & de Freitas’s Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) by training on execution traces that include recursive calls.
- Authors propose verifying learned programs by checking a small set of base cases and reduction rules, and report perfect inference for Bubblesort and Tower of Hanoi.
- Reviewer appreciates the simplicity: the only change is using recursive execution traces during training.
- Reviewer questions the significance: does this mean neural programming is effectively solved when execution traces are available, or was the task trivial?
- Gives the MNIST-sorting thought experiment: with execution traces, perception (digit recognition) is decoupled from program induction, making the problem easier and arguably not representative.
- Raises the critical open question: what tasks admit execution traces but still cannot be inferred by the proposed method?
