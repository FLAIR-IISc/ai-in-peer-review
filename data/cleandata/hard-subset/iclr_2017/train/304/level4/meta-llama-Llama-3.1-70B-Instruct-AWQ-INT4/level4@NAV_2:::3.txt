This paper improves significantly upon the original NPI work, showing that the model generalizes far better when trained on traces in recursive form. The authors show better sample complexity and generalization results for addition and bubblesort programs, and add two new and more interesting tasks - topological sort and quicksort (added based on reviewer discussion). Furthermore, they actually prove that the algorithms learned by the model generalize perfectly, which to my knowledge is the first time this has been done in neural program induction.

I accept this paper for the following reasons: (1) the authors demonstrate a significant improvement in the generalization capabilities of the NPI model by incorporating recursion into the training traces, and (2) they provide a rigorous verification procedure to prove the perfect generalization of the learned algorithms.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of their approach and results. The addition of topological sort and quicksort tasks is a significant improvement over the original NPI work, and the verification procedure is a major contribution to the field of neural program induction.

One minor suggestion I have is to consider adding more discussion on the limitations of the approach and potential future work. For example, the authors mention that the verification procedure may not be feasible for tasks with very large input domains, but they do not provide much discussion on how to address this limitation.

Overall, I think this paper is a significant contribution to the field of neural program induction, and I am happy to accept it for publication.

Additional feedback:

* The authors may want to consider adding more details on the implementation of the NPI model and the training procedure. While the paper provides a good overview of the approach, some readers may want more information on the specifics of the implementation.
* The authors may also want to consider adding more discussion on the relationship between the learned algorithms and the underlying programming concepts. For example, how do the learned algorithms relate to the traditional programming concepts of recursion and loops?
* The paper mentions that the authors plan to explore new ways to train recursive neural programs without providing explicit training execution traces or with only partial or non-recursive traces. This is an interesting direction for future work, and the authors may want to provide more discussion on how they plan to approach this problem.
