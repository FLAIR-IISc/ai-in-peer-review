This paper argues that the ability to handle recursion is crucial for neural programming architectures, as it enables strong generalization to out-of-domain test cases and learning from smaller amounts of training data. The majority of the paper builds upon the Reed & de Freitas paper on Neural Programmer Interpreters from ICLR 2016, which learns from program traces. The key difference lies in the training data: this paper trains NPI models on traces that include recursive calls. The authors demonstrate how to verify the correctness of the learned program by evaluating it on a small set of base cases and reduction rules, and impressively, show that the NPI architecture can perfectly infer the Bubblesort and Tower of Hanoi problems.

One aspect of the paper that I appreciate is the simplicity of the idea. As the authors themselves mention, the only modification is to the execution traces that the training pipeline receives. However, I am uncertain about the implications of this work. Does this mean that we have effectively solved the neural programming problem when execution traces are available? (And was the problem too straightforward to begin with?) For instance, a more extensive input domain, as one of the reviewers also mentions, is MNIST digits. We can imagine a problem where the NPI must infer how to sort MNIST digits from highest to lowest. In this setting, having execution traces would effectively decouple the problem of recognizing the digits from that of inferring the program logic – and thus the problem would be no more challenging than learning to recognize MNIST digits and learning to bubble sort from symbols. A more critical question is: what is a problem where we have access to execution traces but cannot infer it using the proposed method?
