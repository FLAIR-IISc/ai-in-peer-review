- Paper claims recursion capability is crucial for neural programming architectures, enabling strong out-of-domain generalization and learning from less data.
- Builds on Reed & de Freitas (NPI) and differs mainly by training on execution traces that include recursive calls.
- Presents a verification method using a small set of base cases and reduction rules to prove learned program correctness.
- Demonstrates that the NPI architecture can perfectly learn Bubblesort and Tower of Hanoi with the recursive traces.
- Reviewer appreciates the simplicity: only the training traces are modified.
- Reviewer questions the broader implications: does having execution traces effectively solve neural programming in those settings?
- Gives an example (sorting MNIST digits) where traces would decouple perception from program inference, making the task easier.
- Raises the critical question of what classes of problems would still be unsolvable by the proposed method despite having execution traces.
