- Proposes incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures to improve generalization.  
- Modifies the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) to learn recursive programs.  
- Demonstrates perfect generalization on grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Argues recursion is a key abstraction enabling strong generalization even with small training data.  
- Paper is well-motivated, clearly organized, and experiments are thorough and well-executed.  
- Provides strong empirical evidence and a novel explanation for provably perfect generalization via recursion.  
- Main weakness: approach depends on availability of explicit execution traces for training.  
- Authors acknowledge the trace limitation and suggest future work to learn without traces.  
- Reviewer asks for more discussion of limitations and potential future directions.  
- Specific questions: how to extend to more complex tasks (e.g., large input domains like MNIST).  
- Specific questions: provide deeper insight into why the method generalizes so well with little data.  
- Specific questions: how to address the requirement for explicit execution traces during training.
