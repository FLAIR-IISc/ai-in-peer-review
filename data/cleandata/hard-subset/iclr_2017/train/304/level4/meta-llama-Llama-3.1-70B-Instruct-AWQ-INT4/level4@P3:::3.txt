This paper improves significantly upon the original NPI work, showing that the model generalizes far better when trained on traces in recursive form. The authors demonstrate better sample complexity and generalization results for addition and bubblesort programs, and introduce two new and more interesting tasks - topological sort and quicksort. Furthermore, they actually prove that the algorithms learned by the model generalize perfectly, which to my knowledge is the first time this has been done in neural program induction.

I decide to accept this paper with the key reasons being: (1) the authors propose a novel approach to neural program induction by incorporating recursion, and (2) they provide a rigorous verification procedure to prove the generalization of the learned algorithms.

The paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature, as it addresses the significant issue of poor generalizability in neural program induction. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the problem and their approach, making it easy to follow their reasoning. The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach on various tasks.

The verification procedure is a significant contribution of the paper, as it provides a rigorous way to prove the generalization of the learned algorithms. The authors demonstrate the applicability of this procedure to various tasks, including addition, topological sort, and quicksort.

One potential concern is that the verification procedure may not be scalable to more complex tasks or larger input domains. However, the authors acknowledge this limitation and propose it as future work.

Additional feedback:

* The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the recursive formulations for each task, especially for topological sort and quicksort.
* The authors could provide more insights into why the recursive approach leads to better generalization results, and how it relates to the underlying structure of the tasks.
* The paper could include more discussions on the potential applications of the proposed approach, especially in areas where provable generalization is crucial.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on how you constructed the verification sets for each task, especially for topological sort and quicksort?
* How do you plan to address the scalability issue of the verification procedure for more complex tasks or larger input domains?
* Can you discuss the potential limitations of the proposed approach, especially in terms of the types of tasks it can be applied to?
