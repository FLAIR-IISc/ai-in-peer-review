This is a very interesting and fairly easy to read paper. The authors present a small, yet nifty approach to make Neural Programming Interpreters significantly more powerful. By allowing recursion, NPI generalizes better from fewer execution traces.

I decide to accept this paper with one key reason for this choice: the authors provide a well-motivated approach that effectively tackles the problem of poor generalizability in neural programming architectures.

The authors' approach is well-placed in the literature, as they build upon existing neural programming architectures and identify a key limitation - the lack of recursion - that hinders their ability to generalize. By incorporating recursion, the authors demonstrate significant improvements in generalization performance on various tasks.

The paper supports its claims through empirical results and theoretical analysis. The authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, including the use of recursive formulations for NPI programs and the construction of verification sets to prove perfect generalization. The results are impressive, with the recursive NPI model achieving perfect generalization on tasks such as addition, topological sort, and quicksort.

One potential critique is that the generalization proofs are a bit vague, particularly when considering continuous input spaces. However, this is a minor point, and the authors do provide a clear explanation of their approach and results.

Additional feedback to improve the paper:

* Consider providing more details on the construction of the verification sets, particularly for tasks with large input domains.
* Discuss potential limitations of the approach, such as the need for careful design of the recursive formulations and the potential for increased computational complexity.
* Provide more context on the relationship between the proposed approach and other neural programming architectures, such as the Neural Turing Machine and the Differentiable Neural Computer.

Questions to the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the construction of the verification sets, particularly for tasks with large input domains?
* How do you plan to address the potential limitations of the approach, such as the need for careful design of the recursive formulations and the potential for increased computational complexity?
* Are you planning on releasing the source code for the proposed approach?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neural programming architectures.
