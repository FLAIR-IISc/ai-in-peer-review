This paper argues that the ability to handle recursion is crucial for neural programming architectures, as it enables strong generalization to out-of-domain test cases and learning from smaller amounts of training data. The majority of the paper builds upon the Reed & de Freitas paper on Neural Programmer Interpreters (NPI) from ICLR 2016, which learns from program traces; the key difference here is that the authors train NPI models on traces that include recursive calls. They demonstrate how to verify correctness by evaluating the learned program on a small set of base cases and reduction rules, and impressively show that the NPI architecture can perfectly infer Bubblesort and the Tower of Hanoi problems.

One of the strengths of this paper is its simplicity; as the authors acknowledge, the only modification is to the execution traces that the training pipeline receives. However, this raises questions about the broader implications of this work. Does this mean that we have effectively solved the neural programming problem when execution traces are available? Was the problem too easy to begin with? For instance, a more challenging input domain, as one of the reviewers also mentions, is MNIST digits. We can imagine a problem where the NPI must infer how to sort MNIST digits from highest to lowest. In this setting, having execution traces would effectively decouple the problem of recognizing the digits from that of inferring the program logic, making the problem no harder than learning to recognize MNIST digits and learning to bubble sort from symbols. A critical question remains: what is an example of a problem where we have access to execution traces, but the proposed method fails to infer the correct solution?
