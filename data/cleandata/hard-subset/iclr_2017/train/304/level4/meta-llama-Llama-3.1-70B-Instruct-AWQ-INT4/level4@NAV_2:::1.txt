This paper proposes a novel approach to improve the generalization of neural programming architectures by incorporating recursion. The authors argue that recursion is a key abstraction that enables neural programs to generalize well, even with small amounts of training data. To demonstrate this, they modify the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture to learn recursive programs and show that it achieves perfect generalization on several tasks, including grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.

I accept this paper because it presents a well-motivated approach to improving the generalization of neural programming architectures. The authors provide a clear explanation of the problem of poor generalization in current neural programming architectures and propose a simple yet effective solution by incorporating recursion. The paper is well-organized, and the experiments are thorough and well-executed.

The main reason for my acceptance is that the paper provides strong evidence that recursion is a crucial component for neural programs to generalize well. The authors demonstrate that their approach can learn recursive programs that achieve perfect generalization on several tasks, which is a significant improvement over previous results. Additionally, the paper provides a clear explanation of how recursion enables provably perfect generalization, which is a novel contribution to the field.

One potential weakness of the paper is that it relies on the availability of execution traces for training. However, the authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest that future work could explore ways to learn recursive programs without explicit execution traces.

To improve the paper, I suggest that the authors provide more discussion on the limitations of their approach and potential future directions. For example, they could discuss how their approach could be applied to more complex tasks or how it could be integrated with other neural programming architectures.
