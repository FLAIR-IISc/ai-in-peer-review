This paper emphasizes the importance of handling recursion in neural programming architectures, arguing that it enables strong generalization to out-of-domain test cases and learning from smaller amounts of training data. The majority of the paper builds upon the Reed & de Freitas paper on Neural Programmer Interpreters (NPI) from ICLR 2016, which learns from program traces, by training NPI models on traces that include recursive calls. The authors demonstrate how to verify correctness by evaluating the learned program on a small set of base cases and reduction rules, and impressively, show that the NPI architecture can perfectly infer Bubblesort and the Tower of Hanoi problems.

What I appreciate about this work is its simplicity; as the authors themselves note, the only modification is to the execution traces that the training pipeline is exposed to. However, I am unsure what the key takeaway is – does this imply that we have effectively solved the neural programming problem when execution traces are available? (And was the problem too straightforward to begin with?) For instance, considering a larger input domain, such as MNIST digits, we can envision a problem where the NPI must infer how to sort MNIST digits from highest to lowest. In this setting, having execution traces would effectively decouple the problem of recognizing the digits from that of inferring the program logic – making the problem no more challenging than learning to recognize MNIST digits and learning to bubble sort from symbols. A critical question arises: what is a problem where we have access to execution traces but cannot infer it using the proposed method?
