This paper significantly improves upon the original Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) work by demonstrating that the model generalizes substantially better when trained on recursive traces. The authors show improved sample complexity and generalization results for addition and bubble sort programs, and introduce two new and more complex tasks - topological sort and quicksort. Furthermore, they provide formal proofs that the algorithms learned by the model generalize perfectly, which, to my knowledge, is the first time this has been achieved in neural program induction.

I decide to accept this paper, primarily due to its innovative contribution to the field of neural program induction and its rigorous evaluation methodology. The authors' use of recursion to improve the generalization capabilities of the NPI model is a significant advancement, and their provision of formal proofs for the learned algorithms' perfect generalization is a notable achievement.

The paper's key strengths lie in its clear motivation, thorough literature review, and well-designed experiments. The authors convincingly argue that the original NPI model's lack of recursion is a significant limitation, and their introduction of recursive traces effectively addresses this issue. The experimental results demonstrate substantial improvements in generalization performance, and the formal proofs provide a high degree of confidence in the learned algorithms' correctness.

One potential area for improvement is the exploration of alternative methods for training recursive neural programs, as mentioned in the paper's future work section. The authors' current approach relies on providing explicit recursive execution traces, which may not be feasible in all scenarios. Investigating methods to learn recursive programs from partial or non-recursive traces could further enhance the model's applicability.

Additional feedback:

* The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the authors' results, particularly in the context of broader neural program induction research. How do the findings relate to other approaches in the field, and what potential avenues for future research do they open up?
* The authors may consider providing more information about the computational resources required to train and evaluate their model, as well as the time complexity of the training and inference processes. This would help readers better understand the model's scalability and practicality.
* In the appendix, the authors provide detailed descriptions of the program sets and trace-generating functions for each task. While this information is valuable for reproducibility, it may be helpful to include a brief summary or overview of these components in the main text to provide a clearer understanding of the model's architecture and functionality.
