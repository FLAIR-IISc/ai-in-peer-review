- Paper claims recursion is crucial for neural programming architectures, enabling strong out-of-domain generalization and learning from limited data.
- Work builds on Reed & de Freitas (NPI, ICLR 2016), with the main change being training on execution traces that include recursive calls.
- Authors show correctness can be verified by testing learned programs on a small set of base cases and reduction rules.
- Empirical results: the NPI architecture perfectly infers Bubblesort and the Tower of Hanoi when trained with recursive traces.
- The idea’s simplicity is appealing since the only modification is the form of execution traces provided to training.
- Raises a concern about implications: does access to execution traces essentially solve neural programming, or was the task inherently easier?
- Example concern: for larger input domains (e.g., MNIST digits), execution traces could decouple perception from program logic, making the task trivially separable.
- Key open question: what problems cannot be inferred by this method even when execution traces are available?
