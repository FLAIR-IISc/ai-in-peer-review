This paper argues that being able to handle recursion is very important for neural programming architectures — that handling recursion allows for strong generalization to out of domain test cases and learning from smaller amounts of training data.  Most of the paper is a riff on the Reed & de Freitas paper on Neural Programmer Interpreters from ICLR 2016 which learns from program traces — this paper trains NPI models on traces that have recursive calls.  The authors show how to verify correctness by evaluating the learned program on only a small set of base cases and reduction rules and impressively, show that the NPI architecture is able to perfectly infer Bubblesort and other tasks such as grade-school addition, topological sort, and quicksort.  
What I like is that the idea is super simple and as the authors even mention, the only change is to the execution traces that the training pipeline gets to see.  I'm actually not sure what the right take-away is — does this mean that we have effectively solved the neural programming problem when the execution traces are available? (and was the problem too easy to begin with?).    For example, a larger input domain (as one of the reviewers also mentions) is MNIST digits and we can imagine a problem where the NPI must infer how to sort MNIST digits from highest to lowest.  In this setting, having execution traces would effectively decouple the problem of recognizing the digits from that of inferring the program logic — and so the problem would be no harder than learning to recognize MNIST digits and learning to bubble sort from symbols.  What is a problem where we have access to execution traces but cannot infer it using the proposed method?

I decide to accept this paper with one key reason being that the authors have demonstrated a novel approach to incorporating recursion into neural architectures, which leads to improved generalization performance and the ability to provide provable guarantees about the behavior of the learned programs. The simplicity of the approach is also a significant advantage, as it only requires modifying the training traces to include recursive calls.

The authors provide strong evidence to support their claims, including experiments on four different tasks that demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach. The results show that the learned recursive programs can generalize perfectly to unseen inputs, even when trained on a small number of examples. The authors also provide a verification procedure that can be used to prove the correctness of the learned programs, which is an important contribution to the field of neural programming.

One potential limitation of the approach is that it requires access to execution traces, which may not always be available. However, the authors argue that this is not a significant limitation, as execution traces can often be obtained through other means, such as by running the program on a small set of inputs.

Overall, I believe that this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural programming and demonstrates the potential of incorporating recursion into neural architectures. I would like to see the authors explore more complex tasks and domains in future work, such as the example mentioned above involving MNIST digits.

Additional feedback:

* The authors may want to consider adding more details about the training procedure, such as the hyperparameters used and the number of iterations.
* The authors may also want to consider adding more analysis of the learned programs, such as visualizations of the program structure or analysis of the program's behavior on different inputs.
* The authors mention that the approach can be applied to other neural programming architectures, but they do not provide any examples or experiments to support this claim. It would be interesting to see the authors explore this idea further in future work.

Questions for the authors:

* Can the authors provide more details about the verification procedure and how it can be applied to other tasks and domains?
* How do the authors plan to address the limitation of requiring access to execution traces in future work?
* Can the authors provide more analysis of the learned programs and their behavior on different inputs?
