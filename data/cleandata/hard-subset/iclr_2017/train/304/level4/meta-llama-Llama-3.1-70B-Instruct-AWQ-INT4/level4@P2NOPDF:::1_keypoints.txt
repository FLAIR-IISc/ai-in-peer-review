- Handling recursion in neural programming architectures enables strong out-of-domain generalization and learning from less training data.
- The work extends Reed & de Freitas’ Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) by training on execution traces that include recursive calls.
- The authors propose verifying learned program correctness using a small set of base cases and reduction rules.
- Empirically, the NPI architecture can perfectly infer Bubblesort and Tower of Hanoi when trained with recursive traces.
- The approach’s simplicity is a strength—only the execution traces provided to training are modified.
- Raises the question whether neural programming is effectively solved when execution traces are available, suggesting the problem may be easier than assumed.
- Example critique: in tasks like sorting MNIST digits, execution traces would decouple perception from program logic, reducing the challenge to digit recognition plus symbolic sorting.
- A more insightful evaluation would identify tasks where traces are available but the method fails, to delineate its limitations.
