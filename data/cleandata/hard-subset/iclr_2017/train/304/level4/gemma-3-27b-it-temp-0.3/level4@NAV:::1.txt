This paper investigates the importance of recursion for neural programming, positing that it enables improved generalization and data efficiency. The work builds directly upon the Neural Programmer Interpreter (NPI) framework introduced by Reed & de Freitas (ICLR 2016), extending it to handle recursive program traces. The authors demonstrate that training NPI models with these traces allows for accurate program inference; notably, the architecture successfully learns both Bubblesort and the Tower of Hanoi problems with perfect accuracy when verified against base cases and reduction rules. 

A key strength of this work lies in its simplicity – the core modification is solely within the training data, specifically the inclusion of recursive execution traces. However, this raises questions about the broader implications. It’s unclear whether this result signifies a substantial advance in neural programming, particularly given the availability of execution traces. One wonders if the problems addressed were inherently limited in complexity. To illustrate, consider a task like sorting MNIST digits: access to execution traces would effectively separate digit recognition from program logic, reducing the challenge to digit recognition *and* learning a symbolic bubblesort. This prompts the question of identifying problems with available execution traces that remain intractable for the proposed method, highlighting the limitations of this approach.
