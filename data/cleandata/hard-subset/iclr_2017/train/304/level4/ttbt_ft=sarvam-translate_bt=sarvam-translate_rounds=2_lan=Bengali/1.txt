This paper argues that managing recursion is crucial for neural programming architecture. Managing recursion allows for powerful generalization in testing outside the domain and helps to learn from a small amount of training data. Much of this paper is based on Reed and De Freitas's "Neural Programmer Interpreters" from ICLR 2016," where the program learns from traces. This paper trains the NPI model on traces containing recursive calls. The authors demonstrate how to evaluate learned programs using only a small number of base cases and reduction rules to verify accuracy, and impressively show that the NPI architecture is capable of accurately inferring problems like bubble sort and the Tower of Hanoi.

I liked that the concept is very simple, and as the authors note, the only change is the training pipeline, which changes the execution traces it sees. I'm not really sure what to learn from this – does it mean that once the execution traces are available, we have effectively solved the neural programming problem? (And the problem was too easy to start with?). For example, a larger input domain (as one reviewer mentioned) is MNSSTIs digits, and we can imagine a problem where NPI has to infer a method to sort MNSSTIs digits from highest to lowest. In this situation, if there are execution traces, the problem of identifying digits and inferring program logic will be different – and therefore the problem will not be more difficult than learning to identify MNSSTIs digits and sort bubbles from symbols. What are the problems where we have access to execution traces but cannot infer using the proposed method?
