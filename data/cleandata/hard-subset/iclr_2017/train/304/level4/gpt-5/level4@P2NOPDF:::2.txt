This is an interesting and accessible paper. The authors propose a concise yet effective extension—adding recursion—to the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI), which substantially improves generalization from fewer execution traces. It is a compelling example of how a small but nontrivial modification can make a machine learning method more practical. I also appreciate the consistent notation with the original DeepMind NPI paper; as a non-expert, this made it straightforward to read the two papers in parallel.

My main concern is that the generalization proofs are somewhat vague. For the discrete, numerical tasks presented, one can in principle enumerate all possible execution paths up to the next recursive call, which supports finite verification. However, how does this approach extend to continuous input spaces (e.g., the 3D car example from the original paper)? In such settings, exhaustive enumeration is infeasible, and it seems that a proof of generalization would remain intractable without additional assumptions or structure. Clarifying the theoretical scope—what kinds of tasks admit the proposed guarantees and under what conditions—or providing alternative forms of assurance (e.g., PAC-style bounds, robustness analyses, or assumptions like Lipschitz continuity) would strengthen the contribution.
