This paper argues that recursion is crucial for neural programming architectures: enabling recursion yields strong generalization to out-of-domain test cases and allows learning from smaller amounts of training data. The work largely builds on Reed & de Freitas (ICLR 2016) on Neural Programmer-Interpreters (NPI), which learn from program traces; here, the NPI is trained on traces that include recursive calls. The authors show how to verify correctness by evaluating the learned program on only a small set of base cases and reduction rules, and, impressively, demonstrate that the NPI architecture can perfectly infer Bubble Sort and the Tower of Hanoi.

What I like is the simplicity of the idea; as the authors note, the only change is to the execution traces provided to the training pipeline. However, I am not sure what the main takeaway should be—does this imply we have effectively solved the neural programming problem when execution traces are available? (And was the problem perhaps too easy to begin with?) For example, as another reviewer mentions, consider a larger input domain such as MNIST digits, where the NPI must learn to sort MNIST digits from highest to lowest. In this setting, having execution traces would effectively decouple digit recognition from program logic—reducing the task to standard MNIST recognition plus learning to bubble sort from symbols. What is an example of a problem where we have access to execution traces but cannot infer it using the proposed method?
