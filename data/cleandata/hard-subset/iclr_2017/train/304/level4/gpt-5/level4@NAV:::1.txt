This paper contends that the ability to handle recursion is crucial for neural programming architectures—specifically, that recursion enables strong generalization to out-of-domain test cases and learning from relatively small training sets. Much of the work builds on Reed & de Freitas’s Neural Programmer-Interpreter (ICLR 2016), which learns from program traces; here, the NPI is trained on traces that include recursive calls. The authors show that one can verify correctness by evaluating the learned program on only a small set of base cases and reduction rules, and, impressively, they demonstrate that the NPI architecture can perfectly infer Bubble Sort and the Tower of Hanoi.

What I like is the striking simplicity: as the authors themselves note, the only change is in the execution traces fed to the training pipeline. I’m still unsure what the correct takeaway is—does this imply that, when execution traces are available, we have essentially solved the neural programming problem? (And was the problem perhaps too easy to begin with?) For example, consider a richer input domain, such as MNIST digits, and imagine asking an NPI to sort MNIST digits from highest to lowest. In that setting, access to execution traces would effectively decouple digit recognition from inferring the program logic—so the task is no harder than learning MNIST classification plus symbol-level bubble sort. What is a problem where execution traces are available but the proposed method still cannot infer the solution?
