- Main claim: Recursion is crucial for neural programming architectures, enabling strong out-of-domain generalization and learning from less training data.
- Method: Builds on Reed & de Freitas (NPI) by training the NPI on execution traces that include recursive calls.
- Verification: Correctness can be verified by evaluating the learned program on a small set of base cases and reduction rules.
- Empirical result: The NPI architecture can perfectly infer Bubble Sort and the Tower of Hanoi using recursive traces.
- Positive note: The idea is simple and minimal—the only change is to the execution traces provided during training.
- Unclear takeaway: The reviewer questions whether this implies the neural programming problem is effectively solved when execution traces are available.
- Concern about difficulty: The reviewer suggests the tasks might be too easy when execution traces are provided.
- Counterexample concern: In larger input domains (e.g., sorting MNIST digits), execution traces could decouple perception from program logic, simplifying the task.
- Open question: What is an example of a problem where execution traces are available but the proposed method cannot infer the program?
