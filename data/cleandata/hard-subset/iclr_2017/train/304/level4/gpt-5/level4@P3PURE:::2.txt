This is a very interesting and fairly easy-to-read paper. The authors present a small yet effective approach to make Neural Programming Interpreters (NPI) significantly more powerful: by allowing recursion, NPI generalizes better from fewer execution traces. It is a compelling example of how a small but nontrivial extension can make a machine learning method substantially more practical.

I also appreciate the consistent notation with the original DeepMind paper. As a non-expert in this area, this made it straightforward to read the two papers in tandem.

My main critique is that the generalization proofs are somewhat vague. For the numerical examples in the paper, one can iterate over all possible execution paths until the next recursive call. However, how does this approach generalize to a continuous input space (e.g., the 3D car example in the original paper)? It seems that a proof of generalization would still be intractable in the continuous case.
