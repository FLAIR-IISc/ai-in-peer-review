Summary:
This paper presents a simple yet effective extension to Neural Programmer-Interpreters (NPI): enabling recursion within the model. The core claim is that recursion allows NPIs to generalize substantially better from fewer execution traces, particularly to longer or more complex instances than seen during training. The paper maintains the original NPI formalism and notation, which makes the contribution easy to follow and to relate directly to prior work.

Strengths:
- Clarity and readability. The exposition is concise, and reusing the original NPI notation lowers the barrier to understanding for readers familiar with the DeepMind paper.
- Conceptual contribution. The work demonstrates that a small, well-motivated architectural/methodological change—adding recursion—can yield significant gains in practical generalization, which is both interesting and broadly relevant.
- Empirical evidence. The experiments on discrete, algorithmic tasks convincingly show improved generalization from fewer traces and to longer inputs, aligning well with the intuition behind recursion.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Theory and generalization proofs. The generalization arguments feel underspecified. For the discrete tasks considered, it is feasible to enumerate all execution paths between recursive calls and argue about correctness for these segments. However:
  - The assumptions underlying the proofs are not fully explicit (e.g., bounded recursion depth, deterministic subroutines, finite symbol/state spaces, exact execution vs. approximation).
  - It is unclear how the proof techniques extend beyond discrete, finite settings. In particular, the approach appears to rely on enumerability of call-free segments, which does not hold for continuous input spaces or tasks with uncountably many states.
  - As written, it seems that guarantees in continuous domains (e.g., the 3D car navigation example from the original NPI paper) would remain intractable, or at least would require additional assumptions (e.g., Lipschitz continuity, discretization schemes, or state abstraction) that are not discussed.
- Scope of evaluation. The experiments are persuasive for symbolic/algorithmic tasks but do not explore settings where perception or continuous control are involved. This leaves open whether recursion confers similar benefits when state representations are learned from raw inputs or when dynamics are continuous/noisy.
- Practical details. The paper would benefit from more implementation specifics (e.g., how stack frames are represented; maximum recursion depth and how termination is handled; training regimen, curriculum, and use of teacher forcing), along with ablations quantifying the contribution of recursion relative to more data or alternative training strategies for non-recursive NPI.

Questions for the authors:
- What exact assumptions are required for the generalization guarantees (finite state/action spaces, deterministic transitions, bounded recursion depth, perfect subroutine identification)? Please enumerate and state the theorems precisely.
- Can you clarify how your proof strategy would—or would not—extend to continuous domains? Are there conditions (e.g., Lipschitz continuity, discretization granularity, or stability margins) under which analogous guarantees could be obtained?
- How are stack frames encoded and passed during recursive calls? Is mutual recursion supported? Is there any mechanism akin to tail-call optimization to avoid excessive depth?
- What safeguards exist for non-termination or excessive recursion depth? Do you impose a hard cap during training and evaluation?
- How sensitive is performance to the coverage and quality of the execution traces? Do you observe failure modes where recursion leads to compounding errors across call boundaries?
- Are you planning to release the source code and training scripts?

Suggestions:
- Strengthen the theory by making assumptions explicit and delimiting the scope: discrete vs. continuous, deterministic vs. stochastic, bounded vs. unbounded recursion depth. Where possible, provide formal statements and proofs; otherwise, clearly mark results as conjectural or empirical.
- Discuss the continuous case explicitly. Even a negative result (e.g., that generalization guarantees do not readily extend without strong regularity assumptions) would be valuable, as would potential paths forward (e.g., discretization error bounds, abstraction-based verification).
- Provide more implementation details and ablations (e.g., removing recursion, increasing training traces, or alternative curricula) to isolate the contribution of recursion.
- If feasible, add a small experiment that interfaces recursion with learned perception or a discretized control task to probe robustness outside purely symbolic domains.
- Release code to facilitate reproducibility and adoption.

Overall assessment:
A clear and compelling paper showing that adding recursion to NPI yields substantial practical benefits for algorithmic tasks. The idea is elegant and the empirical results are convincing in their domain. The main limitation is the theoretical treatment of generalization, which currently appears confined to discrete, enumerable settings and does not convincingly address continuous domains. With clarified assumptions, a more precise theoretical framing, and public code, this work would make a strong contribution.

Minor editorial notes:
- “Generalization proves” → “generalization proofs.”
- “A prove of generalization” → “a proof of generalization.”
