Summary of contributions
The paper proposes adding recursion as an explicit abstraction in neural programming architectures to improve out-of-distribution generalization and interpretability. Concretely, the authors modify Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) training to include recursive calls in execution traces, leveraging NPI’s call stack and hidden-state reset across subprograms. They demonstrate the idea on four tasks—grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort—showing strong generalization from very small training sets to much longer or larger inputs. A key contribution is a verification procedure: by testing a finite set of base cases and reduction rules, the authors argue the learned programs generalize perfectly, providing (to their knowledge) the first provable generalization guarantees for neural programs in this setting.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) The idea is simple, well-motivated, and cleanly integrated into an existing architecture, yet yields large gains in generalization. (2) The combination of empirical results and a verification procedure for provable generalization is novel and impactful.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem and significance: Prior neural program learners often fail to generalize beyond training regimes (e.g., longer sequences, larger graphs), with opaque hidden-state dependencies. The paper targets this limitation directly by introducing recursion to constrain program structure and simplify reasoning.

2) Approach and relation to literature: The method is well placed in the literature. It builds on NPI, which already executes from traces and resets hidden state at subprogram boundaries, making recursion natural. The authors’ contribution is to modify traces to include recursive calls and show how this reduces the state space, enabling tractable verification. The positioning relative to other neural program architectures (NTM/DNC/Neural GPU/Pointer Networks/HAM) is appropriate; the case that recursion can be broadly implemented in such architectures is plausible.

3) Claims and evidence: Empirically, the recursive traces lead to robust generalization on all four tasks, often with perfect accuracy far beyond training regimes. The ablation on bubble sort (non-recursive, partially recursive, fully recursive) is particularly informative: full recursion is needed for perfect generalization. The verification methodology—constructing a finite set that covers base cases and reductions, then checking the learned program’s behavior—substantiates the claim of provable generalization where applicable. While the verification relies on careful construction of the valid observation sets, the paper explains the procedure and demonstrates feasibility on multiple tasks, including non-tail recursion (quicksort).

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Scope and limits: The paper should more clearly delineate when verification is tractable. For perception-heavy inputs (e.g., MNIST digits or speech), the base-case/reduction observation space may be large or infinite. A more explicit discussion of the boundary between “verifiable” and “intractable” settings would help, along with guidance on automating or approximating V.
- Supervision requirements: The method relies on full recursive execution traces. Please discuss how much supervision can be relaxed (partial traces, noisy traces, or input-output pairs only), and whether the model can discover recursion autonomously. Even preliminary results or a roadmap would strengthen the contribution.
- Generality beyond NPI: The paper argues recursion is broadly applicable. A short section on how to adapt the approach to other architectures (e.g., DNC/NTM/HAM) would broaden impact, even if only at the design/principle level.
- Verification details: Provide more algorithmic detail or pseudo-code for constructing the verification set V and the mapping from observations to observations. Clarify the complexity and completeness guarantees, and discuss failure modes if V is incomplete.
- Empirical breadth: Include more diagnostics/ablations (e.g., effect of hidden-state reset, stack depth limits, return threshold α). Report sensitivity to the domain-specific encoder choices, especially for topological sort and quicksort.
- Correctness of task list: The paper’s contributions center on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort; ensure consistency throughout (e.g., avoid references to tasks not studied).

Questions for the authors
- Supervision and discovery: Can the model learn recursive structure from input-output pairs alone, or from non-recursive/partial traces? What training signals or architectural biases would help it discover the recursion?
- Robustness to trace issues: How sensitive is training to errors or noise in traces? Can the method tolerate partial or missing calls?
- Verification scalability: How large were the verification sets (V and SV) for each task, and how were you sure they were complete? Can the process be automated from a formal specification of P? What is the computational cost?
- Limits of the approach: Can you provide example tasks where execution traces are available yet the proposed method fails (e.g., due to very large or continuous observation spaces, or complex control flow beyond recursion)?
- Perception-program coupling: In problems mixing perception and program induction (e.g., sorting MNIST digits), how would you structure the encoder so that recursion remains verifiable? Can the verification isolate the program logic from perception errors?
- Recursion mechanics: For non-tail recursion (quicksort), what ensures correct stack discipline and termination in learned programs? Did you observe any failure modes related to deep call stacks?
- Generalization without verification: For tasks where V is too large to enumerate, do you have alternative approximate verification schemes or statistical guarantees?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, clear, and impactful paper. The core idea is straightforward yet powerful, and the verification component is a notable advance. Strengthening the discussion of limits, supervision requirements, and verification automation would further improve the work.
