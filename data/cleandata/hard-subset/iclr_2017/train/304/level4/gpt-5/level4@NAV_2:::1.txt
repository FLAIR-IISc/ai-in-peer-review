1. Summary of claims and contributions
- The paper posits that explicit recursion is crucial for neural programming architectures to achieve strong out-of-distribution generalization and data efficiency.
- Building on Neural Programmer-Interpreter (Reed & de Freitas, 2016), the authors train NPI models on execution traces that include recursive calls.
- They propose a verification strategy that checks correctness on a small set of base cases and reduction rules, arguing this yields provable generalization.
- Empirically, they report that, with this change to the traces, NPI can learn programs that generalize perfectly (e.g., Bubble Sort and Tower of Hanoi), from small training sets, with significantly improved interpretability.

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The central contribution is essentially a change in supervision (making traces recursive), which, while elegant, leaves the broader take-away unclear: if execution traces with recursion are available, is the neural programming problem effectively solved for the presented class of tasks?
- The scope seems limited to symbolic settings where traces neatly decouple perception from algorithmic structure; it is not established where the approach fails or what classes of problems resist this recipe.

3. Supporting arguments
- The idea is refreshingly simple—the only modification is to the training traces—and the empirical results are compelling for the chosen problems. However, this raises a concern about problem difficulty under the supervision regime: when high-quality recursive traces are provided, NPI may be learning a straightforward mimicry of the execution, rather than discovering algorithmic structure under weaker supervision.
- As another reviewer notes, in richer input domains (e.g., sorting MNIST digits), execution traces would separate digit recognition from program logic. In that setup, the challenge collapses to standard perception (recognize digits) plus symbolic sorting given the recognized symbols. This suggests the proposed method does not address the harder integrated setting where perception and algorithm induction interact without strong trace supervision.
- The paper does not delineate boundaries: What tasks, given access to execution traces, cannot be learned with this approach? Without such a boundary, it is hard to assess the generality and scientific contribution beyond demonstrating that NPI can replay well-structured recursive traces.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect the decision)
- Clarify the primary take-away. Is the claim that “with recursive traces, NPI (or similar NPAs) can provably generalize on a broad class of algorithmic tasks”? If so, characterize that class more precisely and articulate assumptions on environments, encoders, and trace availability.
- Make the limitations explicit. Provide at least one concrete task where recursive execution traces exist but the method does not succeed, or explain why such a counterexample would be hard to construct.
- Expand on the verification story. The base-cases/reductions argument is compelling; it would help to quantify verification set sizes relative to training sets across tasks and to discuss failure modes if the verification set is incomplete.
- Discuss the supervision gap. How does performance degrade when traces are partial, noisy, or non-recursive? This would situate the contribution relative to settings where only I/O pairs are available.
- Consider a “perceptual” variant (e.g., sorting MNIST digits) to demonstrate whether the approach scales beyond purely symbolic inputs even when traces are provided, or to make clear why this is outside scope.

Questions for the authors
- If high-quality recursive traces are available, do you believe the neural programming problem (for the family of tasks you consider) is essentially solved with your method? If not, what are the remaining hard cases?
- Can you provide an example of a problem where execution traces are available but your approach does not succeed (and explain why)?
- How sensitive is verification to coverage of the base cases and reduction rules? Can you detect undercoverage automatically?
- What happens when only non-recursive traces (or partial/noisy traces) are available? Can the model still discover recursion, or does performance collapse to the non-generalizing regime?
