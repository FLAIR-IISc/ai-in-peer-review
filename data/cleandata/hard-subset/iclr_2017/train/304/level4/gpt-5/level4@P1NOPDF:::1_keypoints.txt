- Paper claims training neural program interpreters on traces that include recursion enables strong out-of-domain generalization and learning from less data.
- Method closely follows Reed & de Freitas (NPI); the sole modification is adding recursive calls to execution traces.
- Authors propose verifying learned programs by testing a small set of base cases and reduction rules.
- Empirical results show the NPI architecture can perfectly learn Bubble Sort and Tower of Hanoi from recursive traces.
- Reviewer praises the simplicity: only the training traces change.
- Reviewer questions the main takeaway: does this effectively solve neural program induction whenever execution traces are available?
- Concern that with richer input domains (e.g., sorting MNIST digits), traces simply decouple perception from program induction, reducing the task to two easier subproblems.
- Asks about the method's scope: what problem classes admit traces yet remain nontrivial for this approach?
- Requests examples or clarification of settings where traces exist but the method fails or correctness cannot be certified using only base cases and reduction rules.
