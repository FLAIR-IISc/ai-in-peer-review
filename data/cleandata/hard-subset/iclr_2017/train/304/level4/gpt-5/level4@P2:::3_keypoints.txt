- Decision: Accept.

- Contribution: Introduces explicit recursion into Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) via recursive execution traces without changing core architecture.

- Demonstrations: Shows improved generalization and sample efficiency on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.

- Key novelty: Presents a verification procedure that, given inputs covering all base cases and reductions, yields provable guarantees of perfect generalization for learned neural programs.

- Conceptual significance: Recursion as an explicit abstraction reduces hidden-state entanglement, aligns with classic semantics, and enables provable guarantees.

- Empirical strength: Consistent wins across four tasks including nontrivial graph traversal and divide-and-conquer sorting.

- Sample-efficiency evidence: Models train from very few traces (e.g., 4 for quicksort, 1–6 for topo sort, 200 for addition), with recursive addition generalizing from five 1-digit examples.

- Full vs partial recursion: Fully recursive models achieve perfect generalization (e.g., bubble sort), while partial recursion helps but can still fail.

- Verification tractability: Recursion bounds per-call behavior so correctness reduces to testing a finite set V of step inputs, making verification tractable.

- Rigor: Verification methodology is precise and well-motivated though not mechanized formal proofs; construction of V is discussed with concrete examples.

- Positioning: Well situated relative to NPI, DNC/NTM, Neural GPU; novelty lies in trace design and recipe for enabling recursion in neural program architectures.

- Limitation: Verification currently assumes a finite, reasonably small set of base cases and reductions.

- Limitation: Verification as described is likely infeasible for high-dimensional perceptual inputs (e.g., MNIST, speech).

- Request: Clarify automation of verification set (V) construction and feasibility of generating V from formal specs.

- Ablation request: Provide ablations on encoder design (e.g., exposing comparisons vs raw values) to quantify effects on learnability and verification complexity.

- Ablation request: Report impact of tail recursion and stack depth on training stability and memory.

- Analysis request: Analyze failure modes for partial recursion (bubble sort) to illustrate where generalization breaks.

- Comparison request: Compare against strong non-NPI baselines (e.g., DNC/NTM, Neural GPU) on at least one task.

- Reproducibility request: Release code, hyperparameters, training/verification traces, wall-clock training times, and model sizes.

- Question — V construction: How much of V construction was automated vs manual, effort per task, and path to automation from program specs?

- Question — V completeness: How do you ensure completeness of empirically generated V for tasks like topo sort and quicksort?

- Question — Generality: Beyond NPI, what architectural requirements are necessary (hidden-state reset, explicit stacks), and have you tried other controllers?

- Question — Robustness to supervision: How robust is the approach to noisy/partial traces, and can models infer recursion from non-recursive supervision?

- Question — Recursion depth and stability: What are observed limits on recursion depth at inference, were tail-call optimizations used, and did quicksort training show instabilities or credit-assignment issues?

- Question — Encoder sensitivity: How sensitive are results to encoder choices, and can the method work with more generic encoders?

- Question — Verification scope: How does V change when relaxing assumptions (e.g., leading zeros, unequal-length inputs) for addition, and does the learned program still verify?

- Question — Quicksort specifics: Does the verified quicksort handle duplicates and worst-case pivot behavior uniformly?

- Question — Empirical details: Please report training time, compute budget, variance across seeds, and provide finer-grained accuracy vs input-size curves near failure thresholds.
