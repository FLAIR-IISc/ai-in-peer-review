- Proposes augmenting NPI architectures with recursion to improve generalization and interpretability.  
- Trains with recursive execution traces and resets hidden state at each subcall to split problems into smaller, tractable contexts.  
- Demonstrates approach on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort with strong empirical generalization from very small training sets.  
- Introduces a verification procedure using finite base cases and reduction rules to provide provable perfect-generalization guarantees in discrete settings.  
- Uses consistent notation with original NPI work, aiding clarity.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Reason: Explicit recursion substantially improves generalization and interpretability with compelling empirical results.  
- Reason: Work advances toward provable guarantees by framing verification around base cases and reductions.  
- Problem addressed: Neural program learners often fail to generalize due to spurious hidden-state dependencies.  
- Motivation: Recursion naturally reduces problems to smaller instances and aligns with NPI call/return semantics.  
- Evidence: Empirical results show perfect accuracy far beyond training regimes (e.g., thousands of digits, much longer arrays/graphs).  
- Key empirical insight: Full recursion (vs. partial) is necessary for perfect generalization (illustrated in bubble sort).  
- Verification: Testing base cases and reductions convincingly yields provable guarantees for the discrete tasks studied.  
- Main concern: Verification/generalization claims are unclear for continuous or infinite input spaces where the set V of step sequences is infinite.  
- Recommendation: Make formal claims precise with an explicit theorem, assumptions, and proof sketch.  
- Recommendation: Provide algorithmic details/pseudocode and complexity discussion for constructing the verification set V, plus coverage diagnostics.  
- Recommendation: Discuss scalability, stack depth, tail recursion handling, and resource limits for very large inputs.  
- Recommendation: Comment on whether recursion can be discovered from weaker supervision (I/O only or partial traces) and degradation in that regime.  
- Recommendation: Include ablations comparing recursive vs non-recursive training under identical data budgets and curriculum; report sensitivity to encoders and return threshold α.  
- Recommendation: Release code to aid reproducibility and adoption.  
- Question: How to extend verification to continuous or very large input spaces (e.g., symbolic over-approximation, Lipschitz bounds, discretization)?  
- Question: Can construction of V be automated from a formal program specification, and when is finiteness guaranteed?  
- Question: Do you rely on tail-recursion optimization or bounded stacks in practice, and how does this affect non-tail-recursive tasks like quicksort?  
- Question: Any preliminary results on learning recursive structure from partial/non-recursive traces or I/O pairs only?  
- Question: How do training time and sample complexity scale with problem size in the recursive setup versus non-recursive NPI?  
- Question: Will the authors release their implementation/source code?
