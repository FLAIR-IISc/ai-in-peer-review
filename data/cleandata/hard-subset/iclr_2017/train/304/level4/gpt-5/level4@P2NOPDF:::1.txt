This paper argues that explicit support for recursion is crucial in neural program induction, enabling strong generalization to out-of-distribution inputs and learning from limited data. The work builds directly on Reed & de Freitas (ICLR 2016) on Neural Programmer-Interpreters (NPI), but trains NPI models on execution traces that include recursive calls. The authors further propose a simple correctness check: evaluate the learned program on a small set of base cases and reduction rules. Empirically, they show that the NPI architecture can learn correct programs for Bubble Sort and the Tower of Hanoi.

I appreciate the simplicity and clarity of the idea: the only substantive change is to the training traces shown to the model. The results are compelling, and the verification approach is elegant.

However, I am unsure about the broader takeaway. If full execution traces are available, does this essentially solve neural program induction for the considered domains? Was the original problem perhaps too easy under this supervision regime? As another reviewer noted, consider extending the input domain to MNIST digits and asking the model to sort images from highest to lowest. With execution traces, the task may decompose into two largely independent parts: (1) recognizing the digits and (2) learning the symbolic sorting procedure from traces. In that scenario, the overall challenge reduces to standard digit recognition plus learning Bubble Sort over symbols, which is arguably not substantially harder than the symbolic case.

This raises a key question for the paper’s scope and impact: What is an example task where execution traces are available, yet the proposed method cannot learn the program or fails to generalize as claimed? Clarifying the limits of this approach—e.g., tasks with perceptual grounding, noisy or partial traces, longer-range dependencies, or recursion with complex state—would help position the contribution. More broadly, it would be useful to discuss whether the gains come from introducing recursion per se, from the structure of the traces, or from the verification strategy, and how these factors scale to richer domains.

Overall, the paper presents a neat and well-executed idea with impressive empirical demonstrations. A sharper articulation of the problem setting in which this approach is both applicable and nontrivial, along with examples that stress its limitations, would strengthen the contribution and clarify its significance for neural program induction.
