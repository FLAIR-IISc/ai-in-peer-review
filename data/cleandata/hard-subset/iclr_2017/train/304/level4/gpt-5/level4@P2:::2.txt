Summary
This paper proposes augmenting Neural Programming Architectures—instantiated with the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI)—with explicit recursion. The key claim is that making programs recursive simplifies the learning problem, improves generalization from very few traces, and enables formal verification of perfect generalization by testing a finite set of base cases and reduction rules. The authors implement recursive versions of four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort), report strong empirical generalization (often 100% on much larger inputs than seen in training), and present a verification procedure that certifies correctness for all valid inputs once the base cases and reductions are covered. The idea is simple yet impactful, and the paper is clearly written, reusing the original NPI notation, which makes it easy to follow.

Decision: Accept
- Reason 1: A well-motivated, minimal architectural shift (explicit recursion) yields substantial gains in generalization and interpretability, with a credible verification story.
- Reason 2: The approach is clearly positioned, empirically validated on diverse tasks, and likely to influence how neural program learning systems are designed.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses the notorious generalization failure of neural program learners when extrapolating to longer/complex inputs. The motivation to leverage recursion—decomposing problems into base cases and reductions—is well grounded in programming languages and aligns with how classical algorithms achieve scalability and analyzability.
- Approach and placement in literature: The method fits naturally within NPI’s call/stack semantics and should transfer to other modular program-execution architectures. The literature review is appropriate; the novelty lies not in inventing recursion, but in demonstrating that explicit recursive structure (and resetting hidden state across calls) dramatically reduces spurious dependencies and enables verification.
- Evidence and rigor: Empirically, recursive models generalize consistently across tasks, including non-tail recursion (quicksort). The verification methodology is sensible: construct the finite set of reachable step-input observations for each subprogram and confirm model outputs match the reference implementation. This supports the claim of provable perfect generalization for the presented discrete tasks. While the proofs rely on a reference program and handcrafted encoders, the logic is coherent and practically useful.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Clarify the verification set construction. The paper outlines a procedure to derive V (reachable observation sequences) from the reference implementation. Please formalize this mapping more explicitly (state transition relation), specify when it terminates, and provide complexity/size statistics for V per task.
- Discuss limitations in continuous or very large state spaces. The paper notes that verification may be infeasible when base cases/reductions are large or infinite (e.g., motor control). Please delineate what aspects of your method break down, and outline approximate or probabilistic verification strategies (e.g., abstraction, interval analysis, concolic testing).
- Ablations. Provide evidence that recursion itself, rather than other changes, drives the gains. Examples: partial vs full recursion (you include this for bubble sort—great); effect of resetting the LSTM state at subcalls; sensitivity to encoder design (e.g., exposing absolute indices vs relative comparisons).
- Non-uniqueness of “correct” programs. Since multiple recursive decompositions may be valid, discuss whether your verification certifies equivalence to a specific reference P or to the desired input-output semantics irrespective of implementation details.
- Scalability of stack depth. Report maximum observed recursion depth and any mitigation (e.g., tail-call optimization), and analyze failure modes when depth exceeds training or memory limits.
- Reproducibility. Please release code, training/verification traces, and scripts to construct V and the verification sets.

Questions for the authors
- Verification scope: How large are the V sets (and resulting verification sets) for each task, and how long does verification take? Can the V-construction procedure be automated from a formal specification of P?
- Continuous domains: For tasks with continuous observations/actions (e.g., the 3D car control example in NPI), how could your approach provide useful guarantees? Would abstraction, discretization, or Lipschitz bounds help, and what errors would they introduce?
- Encoder design: Some encoders expose length or boundary flags. Could this reintroduce length-dependent shortcuts? Did you test encoders that hide absolute positions to confirm the benefit truly comes from recursion?
- Learning without full traces: You mention future work on weaker supervision. Do you see a path to discover recursion (calls and base cases) from I/O pairs or partial traces (e.g., via program induction with a recursion prior)?
- Non-tail recursion: Quicksort involves two recursive calls. Did you observe training instabilities or compounding errors there? Any special techniques needed beyond your listed primitives?
- Code release: Are you planning to release the implementation and verification harness?
