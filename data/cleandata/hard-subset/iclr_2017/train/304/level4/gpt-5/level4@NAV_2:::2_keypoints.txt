- Paper augments NPI with explicit recursion, yielding markedly better generalization from few execution traces.
- Recursion decomposes tasks into base cases and reductions, constraining behavior and enabling tractable reasoning about execution.
- Authors demonstrate empirical gains and provable generalization procedures for discrete tasks.
- Instantiated on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, showing strong generalization and improved interpretability.
- Exposition is clear and reuses original NPI notation for easy comparison to prior work.

- Recommendation: Accept.
- Key reasons: a simple, well-motivated recursion abstraction yields substantial improvements in generalization and interpretability.
- Key reasons: the verification methodology provides credible, practically useful guarantees for discrete tasks.

- Recursion is a natural abstraction missing from prior neural programming work and fits cleanly into NPI via existing call stack and hidden-state reset.
- Prior architectures (NTM/DNC/Neural GPU) struggle with out-of-distribution generalization; recursion addresses this gap.
- Empirical results show recursive traces generalize far beyond training regimes; ablations clarify limits (e.g., tail recursion insufficient for bubble sort).
- The verification framework for base cases and reductions is well-argued and practically instantiated for the discrete settings considered.
- Notation and training-trace modifications are concrete and reproducible in principle.

- Verification scope should be more explicitly delimited: current arguments apply mainly to discrete, finitely enumerable observation spaces.
- Provable generalization for continuous or very large state spaces is under-specified; clarify required approximations, discretizations, or alternative reasoning.
- The procedure for constructing the valid step-input set V needs a more formal specification, complexity analysis, and guidance on (partial) automation.
- Additional ablations recommended: sensitivity to number of traces, necessity of hidden-state reset at recursive calls, stability with non-recursive or partial traces.
- Discuss practical stack-depth management, tail recursion optimizations, failure modes, and the computational cost of verification relative to training.

- How can verification be extended to continuous or high-dimensional observation spaces—via discretization, symbolic bounds, or other abstractions—and what guarantees remain?
- How do you ensure V is complete for nontrivial tasks beyond addition without exhaustive search; can you provide algorithmic criteria or tooling to detect coverage gaps?
- To what extent can V generation be automated from a formal specification of the reference program P (e.g., symbolic execution or model checking), and any preliminary results?
- How sensitive is generalization to mismatches between the trace generator and learned primitives/encoders (e.g., noisy observations or slight architectural changes)?
- Results for training with fewer or partially non-recursive traces, or with mixed trace types—how gracefully does performance degrade?
- What empirical stack depths are observed for quicksort and topological sort, and are stack overflows encountered without tail-call optimization?
- Will you release source code and trace generators to facilitate reproducibility and further exploration?
