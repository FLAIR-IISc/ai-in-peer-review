- Paper argues recursion is crucial for neural programming architectures, enabling strong out-of-domain generalization and learning from small training sets.
- Builds on Reed & de Freitas’s Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) and trains the NPI with execution traces that include recursive calls.
- Authors show correctness can be verified by evaluating the learned program on a small set of base cases and reduction rules.
- Empirically, the NPI with recursive traces can perfectly learn Bubble Sort and the Tower of Hanoi.
- Strength is striking simplicity: the only change is the inclusion of recursion in execution traces fed to training.
- Reviewer is uncertain about the broader takeaway: does availability of execution traces mean neural programming is essentially solved?
- Raises concern that tasks with richer input domains (e.g., sorting MNIST digits) decouple perception from program induction, making the problem easier and limiting the claim’s significance.
- Asks for examples of problems where execution traces are available but the proposed method would still fail to infer the solution.
