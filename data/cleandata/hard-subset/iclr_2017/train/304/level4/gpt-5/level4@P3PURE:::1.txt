This paper argues that the ability to handle recursion is crucial for neural programming architectures, enabling strong generalization to out-of-domain test cases and learning from smaller amounts of training data. The work builds on Reed & de Freitas (ICLR 2016) on Neural Programmer-Interpreters (NPI), which learn from program execution traces; here, the authors train NPI models on traces that include recursive calls. They further show how to verify correctness by evaluating the learned program on a small set of base cases and reduction rules, and they demonstrate that the NPI architecture can perfectly infer Bubblesort and the Tower of Hanoi.

I appreciate the simplicity of the idea; as the authors note, the only change is to the execution traces used in training. That said, I am unsure about the broader takeaway. Does this imply that, when execution traces are available, the neural programming problem is essentially solved? If so, was the underlying problem too easy to begin with? For instance, as another reviewer notes, a more complex input domain is MNIST digits. Consider a task where the NPI must sort MNIST digits from highest to lowest. With execution traces, the problem effectively decomposes into recognizing the digits and then applying bubble sort over the resulting symbols. In this setup, the overall challenge seems no harder than standard MNIST recognition plus learning bubble sort from symbolic inputs.

This leads to a key question for the paper: What is an example of a problem for which we have access to execution traces but for which the proposed method cannot successfully infer the program? Clarifying the limits of the approach with such a counterexample would sharpen the contribution and help delineate when recursion in traces is sufficient versus when additional architectural or training innovations are needed.
