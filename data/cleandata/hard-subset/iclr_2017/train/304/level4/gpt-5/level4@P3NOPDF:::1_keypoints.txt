- Paper claim: Supervising NPI models with recursive execution traces improves out-of-distribution generalization and reduces data needs, plus a lightweight correctness check via base cases and reduction rules.
- Empirical result: Achieves perfect generalization on Bubble Sort and Tower of Hanoi with recursive supervision.
- Strength: Conceptually simple and powerful—no architecture changes, only recursive trace supervision.
- Strength: Clear empirical wins on canonical recursive tasks demonstrating benefits of recursion-aware supervision.
- Strength: Verification framing (base cases + reduction rules) is intuitive and aligns with recursive reasoning.
- Strength: Promises strong extrapolation to much larger input sizes than seen in training.
- Weakness: Novelty unclear—main change is supervision; need to clarify differences from Reed & de Freitas (ICLR 2016) and any other training/termination/call-stack changes.
- Weakness: Method depends on high-quality, fully specified execution traces—practicality is questionable when traces are unavailable, costly, or noisy.
- Weakness: Unclear limits—if recursive traces suffice here, does this effectively “solve” neural program induction for many problems or do fundamental challenges remain?
- Weakness: Decoupling perception from algorithmic reasoning may mask real-world difficulties (e.g., sorting MNIST with perception errors feeding the controller).
- Weakness: Verification via base cases/reduction rules resembles unit tests; unclear how base cases are chosen and what guarantees/coverage they provide.
- Weakness: Evaluation breadth is limited—needs tests on mutual/nested/data-dependent recursion and much larger generalization ranges.
- Weakness: Missing comparative baselines and ablations—non-recursive trace supervision, memory-augmented models, sequence models, and recursion-depth/stack-limit studies.
- Weakness: Lacks resource and training details (time, compute, memory/stack usage, curriculum/sampling strategies).
- Suggestion: Extend experiments to perception-based tasks (e.g., sorting MNIST) to test robustness to imperfect perception.
- Suggestion: Stress-test supervision by evaluating robustness to partial, noisy, or pruned traces and the model’s ability to infer missing calls.
- Suggestion: Provide a taxonomy of problem classes where recursive traces help and counterexamples where they fail (stochastic, side-effect-heavy, ambiguous decompositions).
- Suggestion: Precisely define the verification protocol, enumerate assumptions/limitations, and discuss bounding error probability or targeted test generation.
- Suggestion: Quantify sample efficiency with learning curves and sensitivity analyses for trace length and recursion depth.
- Suggestion: Open-source code, traces, and evaluation scripts for reproducibility and further analysis.
- Overall assessment: Clean, well-motivated idea with strong initial results, but impact depends on clarifying trace availability/quality, broadening empirical evaluation, and adding rigorous ablations and baselines.
