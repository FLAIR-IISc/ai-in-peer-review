- Introduces recursion as an explicit architectural/training abstraction for neural program induction by converting NPI traces to recursive calls that reset hidden state and decompose into base cases and reductions.
- Shows large gains in sample efficiency and generalization on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort), achieving 100% accuracy far beyond training regimes from small trace sets.
- Proposes a verification procedure that, given finite base cases and reductions, can provably guarantee perfect generalization of the learned neural programs.

- Decision: Accept — recursion is a simple, principled mechanism that materially improves generalization and interpretability and is supported by strong empirical and verification results.

- Problem addressed: Prior neural program learners overfit spurious correlations (e.g., input length) and fail to generalize; recursion enforces structure aligning with true program semantics.
- Motivation: Recursion naturally matches classical algorithm structure (base cases + reductions); NPI’s call/stack semantics make recursion a minimal, well-justified extension.
- Addition results: Recursive traces generalize to thousands of digits from few 1-digit training examples; non-recursive models fail on long compositions.
- Bubble sort results: Full recursion yields perfect generalization; non-recursive and partially recursive variants fail as length grows.
- Topological sort results: Recursive model solves large DAGs from minimal traces; non-recursive models degrade with graph size.
- Quicksort results: Recursive variant generalizes despite non–tail-recursive structure; non-recursive baseline degrades at moderate sizes.
- Verification evidence: Constructing a finite set V of step-inputs covering base cases and reductions plus S_V enables post-training proof that model matches specification for all inputs.
- Rigor/clarity: Paper details domain encoders, interfaces, trace procedures, partial vs. full recursion distinctions, and how recursion bounds verification state space; some details are deferred to appendix.

- Clarify and potentially automate completeness of V and provide a worked example of V construction in the main text.
- Discuss strategies for large or infinite base-case/reduction spaces (e.g., approximation, symbolic abstraction, property testing).
- Provide more discussion or experiments on learning recursion with weaker supervision and portability to other NPAs (DNC/NTM).
- Ablate the importance of hidden-state reset, return-threshold α, and stack-depth constraints.
- Report variance across random seeds and analyze failure modes of non-recursive and partially recursive baselines.
- Where possible, compare against architectures trained on IO pairs with curriculum to isolate the effect of recursion.
- Release code, traces, and verification sets and move key appendix elements (V construction, representative traces) into the main paper.
- Fix reference formatting inconsistencies.

- How is completeness of V (and S_V) guaranteed for each task and can the mapping-from-observations procedure be formalized/automated?
- How sensitive is learning and verification to imperfect or omitted recursive traces?
- Does resetting controller hidden state matter for verification tractability and generalization—any ablation if not reset?
- Has the recursive-trace strategy been tested on other NPAs (DNC/NTM/pointer networks), and what obstacles are anticipated?
- For non–tail recursion (quicksort), how are stack usage, termination, and scaling with input size ensured and are there failure cases?
- For domains with very large/infinite base cases (e.g., vision/speech), what concrete verification relaxations or abstractions do the authors envision?
- Is there a path to induce recursion from IO-only supervision (e.g., search over call graphs or differentiable stacks), and which signals were critical for discovering recursion?
