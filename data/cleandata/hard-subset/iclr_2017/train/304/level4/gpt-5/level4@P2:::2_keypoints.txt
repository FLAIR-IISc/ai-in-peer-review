- Proposes adding explicit recursion to Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) to simplify learning, improve generalization, and enable formal verification.
- Claims recursive programs generalize from few traces and allow certifying perfect generalization via finite base cases and reduction rules.
- Implements recursive versions of addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, reporting strong empirical generalization (often 100% on larger inputs).
- Presents a verification procedure that tests a finite set of reachable observations per subprogram to certify correctness for all valid inputs.
- Decision: Accept.
- Reason 1: Minimal architectural change (explicit recursion) yields large gains in generalization and interpretability, with a credible verification story.
- Reason 2: Clear positioning and empirical validation across diverse tasks, likely to influence neural program learning design.
- Problem/motivation: Addresses poor extrapolation of neural program learners by leveraging recursion’s base/reduction structure.
- Approach/literature placement: Fits naturally with NPI call/stack semantics; novelty is demonstrating the impact of explicit recursion and hidden-state reset across calls.
- Evidence/rigor: Empirical results show consistent generalization including non-tail recursion (quicksort), and verification logic is coherent though reliant on a reference program and handcrafted encoders.
- Request to clarify verification set construction: formalize mapping from reference implementation to V, termination conditions, and size/complexity stats per task.
- Limitation note: Verification may be infeasible for continuous or very large state spaces; suggest outlining approximate/probabilistic verification strategies (abstraction, interval analysis, concolic testing).
- Ablations needed: Provide evidence recursion drives gains (partial vs full recursion, LSTM state reset effect, encoder sensitivity).
- Non-uniqueness concern: Discuss whether verification certifies equivalence to a specific reference program or to input-output semantics regardless of implementation.
- Stack depth/scalability: Report max recursion depth, mitigation strategies (e.g., tail-call optimization), and failure modes when depth exceeds training/memory limits.
- Reproducibility: Release code, training/verification traces, and scripts to construct V and verification sets.
- Question: How large are V and verification sets per task, and how long does verification take; can V be automated from a formal spec of P?
- Question: For continuous observation/action tasks, how can the approach provide guarantees—abstraction/discretization/Lipschitz bounds and their errors?
- Question: Could encoders that expose length/boundary flags reintroduce length-dependent shortcuts; were encoders hiding absolute positions tested?
- Question: Is there a path to discover recursion from I/O pairs or partial traces (program induction with a recursion prior)?
- Question: Did non-tail recursion (quicksort) cause training instabilities or compounding errors; any special techniques used?
- Question: Will the authors release the implementation and verification harness?
