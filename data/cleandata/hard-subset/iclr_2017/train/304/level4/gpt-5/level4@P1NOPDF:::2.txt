This is a very interesting and quite easy-to-read paper. The authors propose a small but effective extension that makes Neural Programmer-Interpreters (NPI) substantially more capable: by enabling recursion, NPI generalizes better from fewer execution traces. It is a compelling example of how a modest, nontrivial change can make a machine learning method significantly more practical.

I also appreciate the consistent notation with the original DeepMind paper; as a non-expert in this area, it made it straightforward to read the original work in parallel.

My main critique concerns the generalization proofs, which feel somewhat vague. For the numerical examples in the paper, it is feasible to enumerate all possible execution paths up to the next recursive call. However, how would this approach generalize to a continuous input space (e.g., the 3D car example in the original paper)? It seems that a proof of generalization would still be intractable in the continuous case.
