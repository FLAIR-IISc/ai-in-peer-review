Summary of contributions
The paper proposes augmenting Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architectures with recursion to improve generalization and interpretability in program-learning tasks. By training with recursive execution traces and leveraging the call stack (with hidden state reset at each subcall), the approach splits problems into smaller subproblems with fixed, tractable contexts. The authors demonstrate this on grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, showing strong empirical generalization from very small training sets. They further introduce a verification procedure: given a finite set of base cases and reduction rules, they validate that the learned program matches the reference on all corresponding step input-output sequences, thereby offering provable guarantees of perfect generalization in these discrete settings. I also appreciate the consistent notation with the original NPI work, which makes the paper easy to follow.

Decision: Accept
Reasons: (1) A simple, well-motivated idea—explicit recursion—substantially improves generalization and interpretability for neural programs, with compelling empirical results. (2) The work takes a first step toward provable guarantees in this area by framing verification around finite base cases and reductions.

Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: Neural program learners often fail to generalize beyond training regimes (e.g., longer inputs), partly due to spurious dependencies (such as input length) captured in hidden states. The paper seeks both better generalization and a way to reason formally about model behavior.
- Motivation and placement in the literature: The paper situates itself among models like NTM, DNC, Neural GPU, Pointer Networks, and NPI, highlighting that prior methods generally lack mechanisms for provable generalization. Recursion is a natural programming abstraction that reduces problems to smaller instances and is especially well-suited to NPI’s call/return semantics and hidden-state reset.
- Evidence for claims: On four tasks, the recursive traces lead to strong empirical generalization, including perfect accuracy far beyond training regimes (e.g., thousands of digits for addition; much longer arrays/graphs for sorting tasks). The partial-versus-full recursion comparison (bubble sort) is particularly illuminating: only full recursion yields perfect generalization, supporting the central claim. The verification procedure—test all base cases and reductions—provides provable guarantees for the discrete settings considered, and is executed convincingly for the presented tasks.

Main concern (clarity of proofs/generalization scope): While the verification approach is clear for discrete spaces with finite base cases and reduction rules, the generalization argument becomes vague for continuous input spaces (e.g., the continuous-control “3D car” setting from prior work). In such cases, the set V of step input sequences is potentially infinite, making exhaustive verification intractable. The paper acknowledges this limitation; still, the current framing might overstate the generality of the guarantees beyond the finite/discrete contexts studied.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not essential to the accept decision)
- Make the formal claims precise: clearly delimit the conditions under which “provably perfect generalization” holds (finite observation/state partitions, finite base cases/reductions, exact trace matching). An explicit theorem with assumptions and proof sketch would strengthen the contribution.
- Elaborate on constructing the verification set V: provide a more algorithmic description or pseudo-code, complexity considerations, and a discussion of when V is guaranteed finite. If possible, include coverage diagnostics showing that V indeed captures all reachable step input sequences for each task.
- Discuss scalability: clarify how stack depth and tail recursion are handled in practice, and whether stack-related resource limits affect generalization on very large inputs.
- Learning supervision: since traces are provided, please comment on whether recursion could be discovered from weaker supervision (e.g., I/O only, partial traces), and whether your approach degrades gracefully in that regime.
- Empirical details: include ablations comparing recursive vs. non-recursive training under identical data budgets and curriculum; report sensitivity to the domain-specific encoders and to the return threshold α.
- Code release would substantially aid reproducibility and adoption.

Questions for the authors
1. Continuous domains: How do you envision extending your verification framework to continuous or very large input spaces where V is infinite (e.g., the 3D car example)? Are there relaxations—such as symbolic over-approximations, Lipschitz bounds, or discretization schemes—that could yield meaningful guarantees without exhaustive enumeration?
2. Automation of V: Can construction of V from a formal program specification P be automated (e.g., via static analysis or model checking), and under what conditions is finiteness guaranteed?
3. Stack limits and tail recursion: In practice, do you rely on tail recursion optimization or bounded stacks? How does this affect tasks like quicksort, which are not purely tail recursive?
4. Supervision and discovery of recursion: Do you have preliminary results on learning recursive structure from partial or non-recursive traces, or from I/O pairs only?
5. Scalability: How do training time and sample complexity scale with problem size in your recursive setup compared to non-recursive NPI?
6. Source code: Are you planning to release your implementation? This would be very valuable for the community.
