This paper argues that the ability to handle recursion is crucial for neural programming architectures, enabling strong generalization to out-of-domain test cases and learning from smaller amounts of training data. The approach closely follows Reed & de Freitas (ICLR 2016) on Neural Programmer-Interpreters (NPI), which learn from program execution traces; here, the key change is to train NPI models on traces that include recursive calls. The authors further show how to verify correctness by evaluating the learned program on a small set of base cases and reduction rules, and they demonstrate that the NPI architecture can perfectly infer Bubble Sort and Tower of Hanoi.

What I like most is the simplicity: as the authors themselves note, the only modification is to the execution traces that the training pipeline observes. However, I am unsure of the main takeaway. Does this result imply that we have effectively solved neural program induction when execution traces are available? Was the original problem perhaps too easy once traces are provided?

For example, consider a larger input domain, such as MNIST digits (as another reviewer also notes). One could pose a task where the NPI must learn to sort MNIST digits from highest to lowest. In this setting, access to execution traces would effectively decouple digit recognition from program induction: the difficulty reduces to (1) recognizing MNIST digits and (2) learning Bubble Sort from symbolic inputs. If so, the overall task is no harder than these two subproblems in isolation.

This raises a scope question for the proposed method: What class of problems admits execution traces yet remains nontrivial for your approach? Can you identify a setting where traces are available but the method cannot infer the correct program, or where correctness cannot be certified using only base cases and reduction rules? Clarifying such boundaries would help position the contribution and articulate when recursion in traces is both necessary and sufficient for strong generalization.
