- Adds explicit recursion to NPI via modified training traces and call-stack/hidden-state resets to improve generalization and interpretability.  
- Introduces a verification procedure that proves perfect generalization by checking only base cases and reduction rules instead of all inputs.  
- Empirical results show perfect or near-perfect generalization on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort with very small training sets.  
- Recursive traces outperform non-recursive or partially recursive traces, which fail to generalize to longer inputs.  
- Recursion reduces the size of the verification problem, enabling formal guarantees on nontrivial tasks.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Training and executing neural programs recursively is simple, well-motivated, and broadly applicable.  
- Key reason 2: Combining recursion with verification is novel in neural program induction and yields strong empirical generalization plus formal guarantees.  
- Addresses a recognized weakness: poor extrapolation and lack of interpretability/guarantees in neural programming architectures.  
- Positions work relative to NPI, DNC, NTM, Neural GPU, Pointer Networks; differs by explicitly incorporating recursion into learned programs.  
- Technical approach is minimal (trace modification) yet effective; hidden-state resets at call boundaries explain improved generalization and tractable verification.  
- Verification method (constructing set V of step-inputs for base cases and reductions and checking equivalence to a reference program) is clearly described and demonstrated.  
- Experiments include informative ablation: partial recursion in bubble sort reveals failures, validating the necessity of full recursive structure.  
- Verification results substantiate claims of provable perfect generalization under a well-defined V.  
- Limitation: Requires strong supervision in the form of recursive execution traces and engineered domain encoders that may expose helpful abstractions.  
- Limitation: Constructing a complete verification set V can be difficult or infeasible in perceptual or high-dimensional domains.  
- Suggestion: Tighten novelty claims (e.g., “first provable guarantees”) to precise scope and cite related program synthesis/verification work.  
- Suggestion: Move more verification detail into main paper, provide pseudocode for constructing V, report V sizes, and discuss completeness guarantees.  
- Suggestion: Include ablations varying recursion degree, hidden-state reset behavior, and tail recursion to isolate sources of improvement.  
- Suggestion: Test robustness to less-engineered encoders and report trade-offs between learnability and generality.  
- Suggestion: Report training time, parameter counts, max recursion depth, and memory/compute behavior as input sizes grow.  
- Suggestion: Explore generalization to perceptual/noisy inputs (e.g., sorting MNIST digits) to clarify verification limits.  
- Question: How is completeness of V ensured for topological sort and quicksort, and what's the probability of missing reachable step-input sequences under the generation strategy?  
- Question: How sensitive are results to return threshold α, LSTM capacity, and recursive factoring (tail vs non-tail); what fails first with reduced capacity?  
- Question: Were attempts made to discover recursion automatically from non-recursive traces, and if so, why did they fail?  
- Question: To what extent do results depend on engineered encoders (e.g., comparison outcomes or stack-empty flags) versus raw values?  
- Question: In partially recursive bubble sort, what specific errors occur as length increases and how do they relate to missing recursive reductions?  
- Question: How would the verification approach adapt to perceptual inputs, and can errors be bounded by composing recognition accuracy with the verified program?
