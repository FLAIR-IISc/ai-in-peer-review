

This paper proposes a novel approach to improving the generalization capabilities of neural programming architectures by incorporating recursion. The authors demonstrate that by modifying training execution traces to include recursive calls, neural programs can achieve perfect generalization on several tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort) from extremely small training sets. The key insight is that recursion enables the decomposition of complex problems into smaller, manageable subproblems, allowing for provable guarantees about the system's behavior through verification of base cases and reduction rules. The authors show that neural programs trained with recursive traces consistently outperform non-recursive counterparts, achieving 100% accuracy on previously unseen inputs after training on just a few examples.

Decision: Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to neural programming by establishing recursion as a critical component for achieving perfect generalization. The core idea is well-motivated, addressing a clear limitation in existing approaches, and the authors provide rigorous empirical evidence supporting their claims. The verification procedure they introduce represents a novel contribution to the field, enabling the first provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs.

Supporting arguments: The paper effectively addresses the three key questions for review. First, it clearly identifies the problem: neural programs generally fail to generalize beyond a certain input complexity due to spurious dependencies on training examples. Second, the approach is well-motivated by the authors' thorough analysis of why existing methods fail and why recursion is a natural solution for program decomposition. Third, the claims are strongly supported by both empirical results and the verification procedure they introduce. Their verification method is particularly compelling, as it demonstrates that recursion reduces the verification problem to checking a finite number of base cases and reduction rules rather than testing an infinite number of inputs.

The paper's strengths include:
1. A clear, well-structured argument for why recursion is necessary for neural programs to generalize
2. The elegant demonstration that recursion can be implemented within existing neural programming architectures (specifically NPI) through simple modifications to training traces
3. The novel verification procedure that enables provable guarantees of perfect generalization
4. Strong empirical results showing perfect generalization on all tasks from minimal training data

Additional feedback to improve the paper:
1. While the verification procedure is compelling for the specific tasks studied, the paper should more clearly address the limitations of this approach for more complex domains. The authors mention this briefly in the conclusion but should elaborate on the challenges of applying verification to domains like MNIST digit sorting or speech processing where the input space is continuous and potentially infinite.

2. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the verification set is constructed for tasks beyond addition. The appendix provides some details, but a more comprehensive explanation would help readers understand the practicality of the verification procedure.

3. The paper states that "the training set can often be considerably smaller than the verification set," but it would be helpful to provide a concrete comparison of the sizes for at least one task to illustrate this point more clearly.

4. The authors should more explicitly address why their approach doesn't solve the broader neural programming problem. While the method works for the specific tasks studied, it's important to clarify the scope of applicability.

Clarifying questions for the authors:
1. The paper demonstrates perfect generalization for several programming tasks, but what would be the minimal verification set for a more complex task like sorting MNIST digits? How would the verification set scale with problem complexity?

2. In the introduction, the authors state that "recursion is the first time one can provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs." However, previous work on neural programs has sometimes claimed perfect generalization for specific problems. How does this work differ from those claims in terms of rigor and generality?

3. The paper focuses on tasks where the program structure is known (e.g., bubble sort, quicksort). For more complex or less structured programming tasks, how would the authors propose constructing the recursive traces needed for training? What would be the limitations of their approach in such cases?

4. The authors note that "the NPI model naturally supports recursion (even though the authors of NPI did not consider this aspect explicitly)." Can you elaborate on what specific aspects of the NPI architecture enable recursion, and whether other neural programming architectures would require substantial modifications to support recursion?
