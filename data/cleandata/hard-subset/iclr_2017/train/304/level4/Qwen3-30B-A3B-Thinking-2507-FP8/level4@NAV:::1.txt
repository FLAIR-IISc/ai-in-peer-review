

This paper posits that recursion handling is critical for neural programming architectures, enabling strong generalization to out-of-domain test cases and efficient learning from limited training data. The work largely builds upon Reed & de Freitas' 2016 ICLR Neural Programmer Interpreter (NPI) framework by training NPI models on execution traces containing recursive calls. The authors demonstrate a verification approach that evaluates learned programs solely on base cases and reduction rules, and compellingly show the NPI architecture can perfectly infer solutions for Bubblesort and the Tower of Hanoi problems.  

I appreciate the concept's conceptual straightforwardness, as the authors note the sole modification is providing the training pipeline with recursive traces. However, I question the primary takeaway: does this imply the neural programming problem is effectively solved when execution traces are available—potentially because the problem was overly constrained? For instance, as another reviewer noted, a more challenging input domain like MNIST digits would present a scenario where the NPI must infer sorting digits from highest to lowest. Here, execution traces would decouple digit recognition from program logic inference, reducing the task to learning digit recognition and symbolic bubble sort—neither inherently harder than the individual components. What is a problem requiring execution traces where the proposed method fails to generalize?
