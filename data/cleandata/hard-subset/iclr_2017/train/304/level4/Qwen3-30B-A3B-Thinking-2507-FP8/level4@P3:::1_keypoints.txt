- Main contribution: Incorporating recursion into NPI lets neural programs achieve provably perfect generalization with very small training sets.  
- Method: Modify execution traces to include explicit recursive calls so NPI learns recursive program structure.  
- Theoretical insight: Recursion reduces generalization to verifying a finite set of base cases and reduction rules.  
- Empirical evaluation: Demonstrated on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Results: Recursive models achieved 100% accuracy across tested inputs, sometimes after only a few training examples (e.g., 4 for quicksort).  
- Verification: Introduced and implemented a procedure that proves perfect generalization by checking base cases and reductions.  
- Decision: Reviewer recommends accept.  
- Acceptance rationale: Approach is theoretically sound, practically effective, and addresses provable generalization in neural programming.  
- Novelty: Verification procedure for neural programs is new and enables trustworthy program learning.  
- Motivation: Correctly identifies poor generalization in prior work as due to lack of structural decomposition.  
- Argument: Recursion provides the needed decomposition and avoids spurious dependencies on input characteristics.  
- Strong empirical support: Non-recursive models fail on larger inputs (e.g., bubble sort fails >8 elements; topological sort fails at size 5) while recursive models generalize to much larger instances.  
- Implementation quality: Traces and experiments are carefully executed and results are thoroughly validated.  
- Limitation 1: Verification can become infeasible for tasks with very large or continuous input domains (example mention: MNIST-like tasks).  
- Limitation 2: Approach relies on supervised execution traces; extension to learning from input-output pairs is not addressed.  
- Clarification needed: Explain why NPI “naturally supports recursion” (stack-based execution) in more technical detail.  
- Practicality concern: Generating recursive traces requires knowing the problem’s recursive structure; automation of trace generation is not discussed.  
- Open question: How to apply verification to infinite/very large domains (e.g., sorting real numbers) and potential remedies.  
- Open question: Scalability of verification when base cases and reduction rules grow large — computational tractability concerns.  
- Request for data: Provide concrete sizes of verification sets vs training sets for each task to illustrate data efficiency.  
- Future direction: Explore reducing supervision so models can discover recursive abstractions without explicit recursive traces, and discuss expected trade-offs.
