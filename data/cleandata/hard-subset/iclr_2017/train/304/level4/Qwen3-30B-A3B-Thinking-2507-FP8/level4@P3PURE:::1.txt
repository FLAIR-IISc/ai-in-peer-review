

This paper argues that recursion handling is crucial for neural programming architectures, enabling strong generalization to out-of-domain test cases and efficient learning from limited training data. The work builds upon Reed & de Freitas' 2016 ICLR Neural Programmer Interpreter (NPI) framework, extending it to train NPI models on execution traces containing recursive calls. The authors demonstrate a novel verification method: evaluating the learned program solely on a small set of base cases and reduction rules, and they show the NPI architecture can perfectly infer solutions for Bubblesort and the Tower of Hanoi problems.  

A clear strength is the conceptual simplicity of the approach—the only modification required is providing the training pipeline with traces that include recursive calls. However, the central question remains: does this effectively solve the neural programming problem when execution traces are available, or was the problem inherently too constrained? For instance, consider a larger input domain like MNIST digits, where the task is sorting digits from highest to lowest. In this setting, execution traces would decouple the digit recognition task from the sorting logic, making the problem equivalent to separately learning digit recognition and symbolic bubble sort—effectively reducing it to a trivial combination of known subtasks. Crucially, the paper does not identify a meaningful problem where execution traces are accessible yet the proposed method fails to infer the solution. This gap undermines the claimed generality of the approach.
