- Argues that incorporating recursive execution traces is crucial for neural programming, enabling strong generalization and efficient learning from limited data.
- Builds on Reed & de Freitas' NPI framework by training on traces containing recursive calls.
- Introduces a verification method that needs only evaluation on base cases and reduction rules, reconstructing Bubble Sort and Tower of Hanoi with perfect accuracy.
- Method’s simplicity is notable: only training-trace modifications are required, no architectural changes.
- Empirical results are convincing for the paper’s well-defined algorithmic tasks.
- Broader significance is uncertain because evaluations use small, structured tasks with explicit execution traces that may not reflect real neural programming challenges.
- Core concern: the approach depends on availability of traces that explicitly decompose problems into recursive steps, which may be an artificial constraint.
- Example: sorting MNIST with explicit traces decouples perception and symbolic sorting, making the problem trivial and not testing algorithm discovery.
- The paper should clarify whether the method can recover recursion from traces that do not explicitly reveal recursive structure or only show final input-output pairs.
- If NPI cannot infer recursion from output-only traces, that is a significant limitation that needs discussion.
- Claiming the method "solves" neural programming is premature; it advances the trace-based paradigm but does not address learning algorithms from unstructured inputs.
- Recommend tempering conclusions, explicitly discussing limitations, and proposing a harder benchmark where traces exist but do not encode the recursive logic.
