

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a compelling contribution by demonstrating that incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures enables provably perfect generalization with minimal training data. The authors build upon the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework from Reed & de Freitas (2016) and show that by modifying training traces to include explicit recursive elements, NPI can learn recursive programs that generalize perfectly to inputs of arbitrary complexity. The paper's key insight is that recursion fundamentally changes the problem structure, reducing it to verifying a finite set of base cases and reduction rules rather than testing on increasingly complex inputs. The authors demonstrate this approach on four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, showing that recursive implementations achieve 100% accuracy on all tested inputs after training on extremely small datasets (as few as 4 examples for quicksort). Crucially, they provide a verification procedure that proves perfect generalization for these tasks by checking only the base cases and reduction rules, which is a novel contribution to the field.

## 2. Decision

**Decision: Accept**

**Key reasons for acceptance:** (1) The paper presents a theoretically sound and practically effective approach to a significant problem in neural programming - the lack of provable generalization. (2) The verification procedure for recursive neural programs is novel and provides a concrete path toward trustworthy neural program learning.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution is well-motivated by the limitations of current neural programming approaches. Existing methods (e.g., Vinyals et al., 2015; Reed & de Freitas, 2016) demonstrate poor generalization beyond training complexity thresholds due to spurious dependencies on input characteristics like length. The authors correctly identify that these issues stem from the lack of structural decomposition in neural program learning, and recursion provides exactly this decomposition. 

The empirical results are strong and clearly demonstrate the advantage of recursion. For example, on bubble sort, the non-recursive model fails completely on inputs longer than 8 elements, while the fully recursive model achieves 100% accuracy on all tested lengths. Similarly, the recursive topological sort model generalizes perfectly to graphs of up to 70 vertices after training on just 6 examples, while the non-recursive model fails at graphs of size 5. These results are not merely incremental improvements but represent a fundamental shift in the generalization capability of neural programs.

The verification procedure is particularly impressive. By showing that the neural program's behavior can be proven correct through a finite set of tests covering base cases and reduction rules, the authors provide a path toward provable guarantees for neural programs - something no prior work has achieved. This verification procedure is not just a theoretical exercise; it's implemented and successfully applied to all four tasks.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, acknowledging prior work while clearly articulating how recursion addresses limitations of existing approaches. The technical execution is sound, with careful implementation of recursive traces for each task and thorough empirical validation.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance its impact:

1. The paper mentions that the verification procedure becomes infeasible for tasks with large input domains (e.g., MNIST digits), but it would be helpful to provide a more concrete example of a task where this limitation is problematic. This would better frame the scope of the approach.

2. The paper focuses on learning from execution traces, which is a significant constraint. It would strengthen the paper to discuss how this approach might be extended to scenarios where execution traces are not available, or to contrast the approach with other methods that learn from input-output pairs (like the Neural Turing Machine).

3. The paper states that the NPI architecture "naturally supports recursion" but doesn't provide a clear explanation of why this is the case. A brief technical discussion of how the stack-based execution model enables recursion would strengthen the argument.

4. The paper claims that "the only change is to the execution traces," but it's worth noting that this requires understanding the recursive structure of the problem to generate appropriate training traces. A discussion of how this would be automated for new problems would be valuable.

## 5. Clarifying Questions

To fully assess the paper's contribution and limitations, I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1. For tasks with infinite or very large input domains (e.g., sorting arbitrary real numbers), what specific limitations would prevent the verification procedure from being applied, and how might these be addressed?

2. How would the approach scale to more complex recursive programs where the number of base cases and reduction rules grows significantly? Would the verification procedure become computationally intractable?

3. The paper states that "the training set can often be considerably smaller than the verification set." Could you provide specific examples of the verification set sizes relative to the training set sizes for each task, to better understand the data efficiency?

4. The paper mentions that "in future work, we would like to decrease supervision and construct models that are capable of coming up with recursive abstractions themselves." How might this be achieved without providing explicit recursive traces, and what would be the expected trade-offs?
