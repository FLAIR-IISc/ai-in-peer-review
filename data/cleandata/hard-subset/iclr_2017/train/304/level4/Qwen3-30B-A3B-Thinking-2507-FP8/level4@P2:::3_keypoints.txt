- Introduces recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework to improve generalization in neural program induction.
- Shows that training on recursive traces enables neural programs to achieve provable perfect generalization with very small training sets.
- Applies the approach to addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, with recursive models achieving 100% accuracy on unseen inputs while non-recursive models fail on longer inputs.
- Key novelty: a formal verification procedure that proves learned programs generalize by verifying base cases and reduction rules instead of relying solely on empirical tests.
- Claims this is the first demonstration of provable perfect generalization in neural program induction, representing a major theoretical advance.
- Recommends acceptance based on addressing a core limitation (poor generalization), strong empirical results, and a novel verification method.
- Paper is well-motivated and clearly explains why recursion removes spurious input-complexity dependencies; strong connection to programming-language concepts.
- Suggestion: clarify how training on recursive traces leads the model to learn the recursive structure that enables verification.
- Suggestion: provide a more detailed comparison with other generalization techniques (e.g., curriculum learning) and argue why recursion is superior.
- Suggestion: discuss limitations of the verification procedure for tasks with very large input domains and propose possible extensions.
- Question: why did original NPI work not consider/expose recursion, and could recursion have been learned inadvertently in prior experiments?
- Question: how does the verification procedure handle non-tail recursion complexities (e.g., quicksort) versus tail-recursive tasks?
- Question: how was the verification set size determined (e.g., smaller than training for quicksort), and is it generally smaller than the training set for recursive programs?
- Question: how might reducing supervision (partial or non-recursive traces) be done in future work, and would provable generalization guarantees be retained?
