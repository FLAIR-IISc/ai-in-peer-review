- Introduces recursion in neural program induction to enable robust, provable generalization.  
- Shows NPI with recursive training traces naturally learns recursive programs that decompose problems.  
- Provides first provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs via base cases and reduction rules.  
- Verification: proving a finite set of base cases and reductions suffices to guarantee generalization to arbitrary inputs.  
- Empirical results: recursive models generalize perfectly on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort with high sample efficiency.  
- Addition: perfect generalization after 200 traces (max length 3); non-recursive fails on longer inputs.  
- Bubble sort: full recursion achieves 100% up to length 100; non-recursive fails by length 8; partial recursion insufficient.  
- Verification set example: addition verification used 20,181 inputs to prove generalization without exhaustive testing.  
- Decision: reviewer recommends accept based on theoretical novelty and strong empirical gains with minimal data.  
- Strength: addresses prior generalization limits (curriculum learning) by enforcing true program semantics via recursion.  
- Suggestion: discuss limitations and scalability of the verification approach for large input domains (e.g., MNIST, speech).  
- Suggestion: discuss challenges and methods for reducing supervision (partial or non-recursive traces) in future work.  
- Suggestion: clarify why original NPI did not learn recursion and why their implementation enables it.  
- Clarifying question: why are three recursive calls necessary for bubble sort and why does partial recursion fail at certain sizes?  
- Clarifying question: how does verification scale to more complex recursive programs and are there bounds on verification set size?  
- Clarifying question: compare and contrast this provable generalization with other formal verification approaches for neural networks.
