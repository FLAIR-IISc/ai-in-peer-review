- Paper proposes adding recursion to neural programming architectures and training traces to achieve strong generalization.
- Authors modify training traces to include recursion and show NPI can learn recursive programs with provable generalization guarantees.
- Experiments on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort achieve 100% accuracy on tested inputs after training on small datasets.
- Reviewer recommends rejection primarily because the main contribution is a simple modification to training traces.
- Second rejection reason: the proposed verification procedure applies only to problems with finite base cases and reduction rules.
- Approach depends on access to recursive execution traces, limiting applicability where such traces are unavailable.
- Method does not address tasks requiring joint inference of perception and program logic from raw input-output pairs (e.g., sorting MNIST digits).
- Verification via exhaustive base-case/reduction testing becomes computationally intractable for large or infinite input domains.
- Authors acknowledge limitations but do not adequately analyze scaling to problems with very large or infinite base cases.
- Paper lacks sufficient comparison to alternative methods for learning recursive programs without explicit recursive traces.
- Review suggests strengthening the paper by clearly articulating verification limits and analyzing when the approach fails.
- Reviewer recommends adding experiments on tasks without obvious recursive structure to delineate method boundaries.
- Questions posed: how to handle missing execution traces, detailed limits of verification for infinite/large base cases, and comparisons to other non-trace methods.
- Overall: interesting core insight but narrow scope, strong limitations, and insufficient evidence of broader practical applicability.
