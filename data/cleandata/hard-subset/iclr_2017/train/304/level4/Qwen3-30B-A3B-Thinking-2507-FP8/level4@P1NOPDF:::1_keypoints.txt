- Paper claims recursion in neural-programming architectures enables strong out-of-distribution generalization and learning from limited data.  
- Extends Reed & de Freitas’ NPI framework by training on execution traces that include recursive calls.  
- Introduces a verification method that tests learned programs using only a small set of base cases and reduction rules.  
- Empirical results: NPI perfectly infers solutions for Bubblesort and Tower of Hanoi.  
- The modification is conceptually simple—train with traces containing recursion—and the simplicity is a strength.  
- Central claim is unclear: does this actually solve neural programming when execution traces are available, or are the benchmarks too simple?  
- Example critique: tasks like sorting MNIST digits would decouple perception from program logic, making the problem trivial (digit recognition + symbolic sorting).  
- The paper lacks a non-trivial benchmark where execution traces exist but the method fails; scope and limits of applicability need clarification.  
- Authors should identify concrete challenging cases where traces are available but NPI cannot generalize, to justify the method’s significance.
