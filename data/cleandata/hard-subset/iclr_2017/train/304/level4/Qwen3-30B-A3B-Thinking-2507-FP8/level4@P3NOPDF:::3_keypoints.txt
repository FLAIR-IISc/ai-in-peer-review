- Core contribution: training on recursive execution traces instead of linear traces yields substantially better generalization.  
- Empirical gains: marked sample complexity reductions (up to 10× for bubblesort) and consistent improvements across addition, bubblesort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Added tasks: inclusion of topological sort and quicksort (suggested during review) strengthens evaluation by testing more complex algorithmic reasoning.  
- Theoretical contribution: formal proof of exact generalization under recursive trace training—the first such proof in neural program induction to the reviewer’s knowledge.  
- Proof significance: moves beyond empirical observation to mathematically guarantee robustness of the learned algorithms.  
- Experimental results: topological sort generalizes perfectly to unseen graphs; quicksort is robust to varying input sizes and partition strategies.  
- Ablations: studies isolate recursive trace representation as the primary cause of the improvements.  
- Writing and reproducibility: generally clear, but some methodological details (e.g., recursive trace construction for quicksort) and a brief inductive sketch of the proof would aid accessibility.  
- Comparison to prior work: focused and highlights the novelty relative to the original NPI framework.  
- Limitation: evaluation confined to sorting and graph algorithms; broader algorithmic domains (e.g., string manipulation, other graph traversals) remain to be tested.  
- Overall judgment: well-executed, high-impact work that bridges empirical and theoretical understanding; recommend acceptance without reservation.
