- Proposes incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures to improve generalization by modifying training execution traces to include recursive calls.  
- Shows that recursive training traces yield perfect generalization on tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) from very small training sets.  
- Key insight: recursion decomposes complex problems into smaller subproblems, enabling provable guarantees via verification of base cases and reduction rules.  
- Empirical claim: recursive neural programs consistently outperform non-recursive counterparts, achieving 100% accuracy on unseen inputs after minimal training.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Supporting argument 1: Clearly identifies the generalization problem as spurious dependencies on training examples.  
- Supporting argument 2: Well-motivated solution with analysis of why existing methods fail and why recursion is appropriate.  
- Supporting argument 3: Strong empirical results plus a novel verification procedure that reduces verification to a finite check of base cases and reductions.  
- Strength 1: Clear, well-structured argument for recursion as necessary for neural program generalization.  
- Strength 2: Demonstrates recursion can be implemented within existing architectures (NPI) via simple trace modifications.  
- Strength 3: Introduces a novel verification procedure enabling provable guarantees of perfect generalization.  
- Strength 4: Strong empirical evidence of perfect generalization on all tasks from minimal data.  
- Feedback 1: Need clearer discussion of verification limitations for more complex or continuous domains (e.g., MNIST, speech).  
- Feedback 2: Provide a more detailed explanation of how verification sets are constructed for tasks beyond addition.  
- Feedback 3: Give concrete comparisons of training set vs. verification set sizes for at least one task.  
- Feedback 4: Explicitly clarify why the approach does not solve the broader neural programming problem and its scope of applicability.  
- Question 1: What would the minimal verification set look like for a more complex task like sorting MNIST digits, and how would it scale with complexity?  
- Question 2: How does this claim of provable perfect generalization differ from prior neural program works that claimed perfect generalization?  
- Question 3: For tasks without known program structure, how would recursive traces be constructed and what are the limitations?  
- Question 4: Which specific aspects of the NPI architecture enable recursion, and would other neural programming architectures need substantial modifications?
