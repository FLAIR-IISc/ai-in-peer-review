

This paper makes a compelling contribution to neural programming by demonstrating that incorporating recursion as a key abstraction significantly improves generalization capabilities. The authors show that recursive neural programs can achieve perfect generalization from minimal training data, with the added benefit of providing provable guarantees about their behavior. The work is well-motivated and well-placed within the existing literature on neural programming architectures.

The core contribution is the demonstration that recursion can be effectively integrated into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter framework, enabling neural programs to generalize perfectly to inputs of arbitrary complexity for discrete programming tasks. The authors provide strong empirical evidence across four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort), showing that recursive neural programs outperform non-recursive versions by achieving 100% accuracy on unseen inputs after training on very small datasets. Crucially, they also introduce a verification procedure that allows for formal proofs of perfect generalization, a significant methodological advance.

I recommend acceptance. The paper addresses a fundamental limitation of neural programming architectures (poor generalization) with a practical solution that provides provable guarantees. The empirical results are convincing, and the theoretical framework for verification is well-developed. The paper is clearly written, with excellent consistency to the original Neural Programmer-Interpreter paper, making it easy for readers to follow the relationship between this work and prior research.

However, I have one important concern regarding the scope of the verification procedure. The paper demonstrates provable generalization for discrete, structured inputs like addition and sorting. The authors should clarify how their approach would handle continuous input spaces, such as those encountered in the 3D car example from the original Neural Programmer-Interpreter paper. While the paper focuses on discrete programming tasks, it would be valuable to address this limitation explicitly and discuss whether the verification procedure could be extended to continuous domains.

Additionally, I would appreciate clarification on how the authors' modified domain-specific encoder for topological sort (mentioned in the appendix) affects the model's ability to generalize to arbitrary comparable elements. The paper notes that this modification makes verification tractable, but it's important to understand how it impacts the generalization capabilities of the model.

The authors should also consider releasing the source code to facilitate reproducibility and further research in this area.

To clarify my understanding, I have two questions:
1. How would the verification procedure be adapted to handle continuous input spaces, such as those in the 3D car example from the original NPI paper?
2. Could you elaborate on how the modified domain-specific encoder for topological sort affects the model's generalization capabilities when applied to arrays containing arbitrary comparable elements?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The incorporation of recursion as a key abstraction is both novel and effective, and the ability to provide provable guarantees about neural program behavior represents a significant methodological advance.
