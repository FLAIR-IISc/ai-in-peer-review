

1. The paper makes a significant contribution by proposing that recursion is a necessary abstraction for neural programming architectures to achieve robust generalization. It demonstrates that by incorporating recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter framework, the resulting models achieve perfect generalization with minimal training data across four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. Most notably, the authors provide the first provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs, showing that with verification of base cases and reduction rules, the learned programs can be mathematically proven to work correctly for all valid inputs.

2. I recommend acceptance. The paper successfully demonstrates that recursion fundamentally enables neural programs to generalize perfectly, and it provides the first formal proof of perfect generalization for neural programs, which is a major theoretical contribution.

3. The paper addresses the key question of what the paper claims to do by clearly showing that recursion enables provable generalization. The approach is well motivated through a thorough analysis of the generalization problem in neural programming and how recursion solves it by decomposing problems into base cases and reduction rules. The claims are strongly supported by empirical results showing perfect generalization from minimal training data across multiple tasks, and the authors provide a novel verification procedure that proves perfect generalization without exhaustive testing. The experiments demonstrate superior performance compared to non-recursive approaches, with the recursive versions achieving 100% accuracy on unseen inputs after training on very small datasets.

4. To further strengthen the paper, consider expanding the discussion of the limitations of the verification procedure for large input domains (e.g., MNIST or speech). While the authors note this as future work, a more detailed analysis of when the verification approach becomes infeasible and potential alternative approaches would be valuable. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with recent neural programming approaches that incorporate some form of hierarchical structure.

5. Could the authors clarify how their verification procedure would scale to more complex tasks with larger input domains, such as those involving image or audio data? Additionally, how might the recursive approach be adapted to handle tasks where the base cases or reduction rules are not easily analytically defined, as might be the case for more complex program synthesis problems?
