

This paper argues that handling recursion is crucial for neural programming architectures, enabling strong generalization to out-of-distribution test cases and learning from limited training data. The work builds directly on Reed & de Freitas’ 2016 ICLR Neural Programmer Interpreter (NPI) framework, extending it to train on execution traces containing recursive calls. The authors demonstrate a verification method that evaluates learned programs solely on a small set of base cases and reduction rules, and impressively show the NPI architecture perfectly infers solutions for Bubblesort and the Tower of Hanoi problems.  

The core idea is conceptually straightforward: the only modification required is providing training pipelines with traces that include recursive invocations. This simplicity is commendable, as the authors note. However, the central claim remains unclear—does this approach effectively solve neural programming *when execution traces are available*? If so, was the benchmark problem inherently too simple?  

Consider a more substantial challenge: sorting MNIST digits from highest to lowest. Here, execution traces would decouple the digit recognition task from program inference. The NPI would only need to learn the sorting logic (e.g., bubble sort) applied to symbolic representations of digits, while digit recognition could be handled separately by a pre-trained classifier. This reduces the problem to two trivial subtasks—recognizing digits and sorting symbols—making the overall challenge no harder than standard MNIST classification and symbolic sorting.  

The critical question is: what constitutes a non-trivial problem where execution traces are available, but the proposed method fails to infer the correct program? Without such a benchmark, it remains uncertain whether the approach addresses a meaningful gap in neural programming or merely solves a problem whose structure was artificially simplified by the trace-based training paradigm. The authors should clarify the scope of the method’s applicability and identify a concrete challenge where execution traces exist but the NPI cannot generalize.
