- Claims recursion handling is crucial for neural programming, enabling strong out-of-domain generalization and efficient learning from limited data.
- Builds on Reed & de Freitas' NPI framework by training NPI models on execution traces that include recursive calls.
- Introduces a verification method that evaluates learned programs using only a small set of base cases and reduction rules.
- Demonstrates perfect inference by the NPI architecture on Bubblesort and Tower of Hanoi.
- Strength: approach is conceptually simple—only requires providing recursive traces during training.
- Raises concern whether the method truly solves neural programming when execution traces are available or merely exploits an overly constrained problem setup.
- Example critique: in larger domains (e.g., MNIST digit sorting), execution traces decouple perception from algorithmic reasoning, reducing the task to trivial subproblems.
- Paper does not identify a meaningful real-world problem where execution traces are available but the method fails, undermining its claimed generality.
