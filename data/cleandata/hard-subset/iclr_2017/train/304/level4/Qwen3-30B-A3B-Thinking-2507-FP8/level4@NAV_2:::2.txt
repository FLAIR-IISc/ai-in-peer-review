

1. The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures, specifically the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI), enables provable perfect generalization across multiple programming tasks. The authors show that recursive neural programs can achieve 100% accuracy on unseen inputs after training on a very small number of examples, with the ability to formally verify this generalization through a verification procedure that tests base cases and reduction rules. This represents a novel approach to neural program learning that addresses a critical gap in the field.

2. Accept. The paper makes a clear, novel contribution to the field of neural program learning by demonstrating that recursion enables provable generalization properties, which has not been previously addressed. The empirical results across four distinct tasks provide strong evidence for the approach's effectiveness.

3. The authors successfully implement recursion in the NPI framework and demonstrate its effectiveness across four different programming tasks. Their verification procedure for proving perfect generalization is a substantial methodological contribution that addresses a critical limitation in current neural program learning approaches. The results show clear improvements over non-recursive versions, with the recursive approach achieving perfect generalization on all test cases while non-recursive versions fail on longer inputs. The paper's theoretical foundation for how recursion enables provable guarantees is sound and well-supported by the experimental results.

4. To further strengthen the paper, the authors could provide more detailed discussion about the limitations of their approach when applied to continuous input spaces. While the paper focuses on discrete, structured problems, it would be valuable to address how the verification procedure might be adapted or extended for continuous domains. Additionally, including a more thorough comparison of the computational complexity of the verification procedure across different problem sizes would be helpful for understanding the practical limitations of the approach.

5. Could you elaborate on how the verification procedure would be adapted for continuous input spaces, such as those encountered in the 3D car example from the original NPI paper? What specific challenges would arise in extending the current verification approach to continuous domains, and are there potential strategies to address these challenges? Will the authors be releasing the source code to facilitate reproduction of results and further research?
