

The paper proposes that incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures, specifically the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI), enables provably perfect generalization across multiple programming tasks with minimal training data. The authors demonstrate this by modifying training traces to include recursive elements, enabling the NPI to learn recursive programs for grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. Crucially, they establish a verification procedure that confirms perfect generalization by testing only base cases and reduction rules, rather than exhaustively evaluating all possible inputs.

I recommend acceptance with the following key reasons: (1) The paper introduces a novel and theoretically grounded approach to neural program generalization through recursion, which addresses a fundamental limitation in current neural programming research; (2) The verification procedure provides the first provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs, a significant theoretical contribution.

The paper effectively addresses the three key questions: (1) It clearly tackles the problem of poor generalization in neural programs when faced with inputs beyond training complexity; (2) The approach is well-motivated by the natural role of recursion in programming languages and its potential to reduce problem complexity, placing the work within the relevant literature on neural programming; (3) The claims are strongly supported by empirical results demonstrating perfect generalization across multiple tasks with minimal training data, complemented by the novel verification procedure that proves generalization properties without exhaustive testing.

To further strengthen the paper, consider adding a brief discussion of how the verification procedure scales with increasingly complex tasks, and whether the method could be applied to non-recursive tasks to demonstrate its limitations. Additionally, explicitly comparing the verification set size to the training set size for each task would clarify the efficiency gains.

Please clarify: How would the verification procedure adapt when the base cases or reduction rules involve continuous domains (e.g., sorting images by brightness) rather than discrete symbolic inputs? Additionally, could you elaborate on why the non-recursive NPI implementation from Reed & de Freitas (2016) failed to learn recursive programs despite the architecture's inherent capability for recursion?
