

**Summary of Contribution:**
This paper makes a compelling case for incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures to achieve provable generalization. The authors demonstrate that recursive neural programs, implemented within the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework, can generalize perfectly to inputs of arbitrary complexity for discrete tasks with finite base cases, after training on very small datasets. They provide a verification procedure to formally prove this generalization by testing only the base cases and reduction rules (a finite set), representing a significant theoretical advance in neural programming.

**Decision:**
Accept. The paper makes a substantial contribution to neural programming by demonstrating how recursion enables provable generalization. The theoretical framework for verification is novel and well-motivated, and the empirical results are strong and well-documented for discrete tasks.

**Supporting Arguments:**
The paper successfully addresses a critical gap in neural programming: the inability to provide provable guarantees about generalization. The authors' key insight—that recursion reduces the problem complexity to smaller, finite cases that can be verified—provides a clear path to proving generalization. Their verification procedure, which requires testing only the base cases and reduction rules (a finite set), is a significant theoretical contribution, as previous neural programming approaches could not provide such guarantees.

The empirical results are compelling and well-executed. For each of the four discrete tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort), the recursive NPI model achieves perfect generalization (100% accuracy) on inputs of arbitrary size after training on minimal data (e.g., 200 traces for addition, 6 traces for topological sort), while non-recursive versions fail to generalize beyond certain input complexities. For example, the recursive topological sort model generalizes to graphs of up to 70 vertices after training on just 6 traces, while the non-recursive model fails for graphs of size 8 and beyond. The verification step confirms that these results stem from the model learning the correct program semantics rather than spurious dependencies.

The paper's theoretical contribution is particularly significant because it provides a path to moving neural programming from empirical results to formal guarantees. The verification procedure demonstrates how neural programs can be designed to have provable properties, a critical step toward making neural programming more reliable and trustworthy for real-world applications. The paper also makes a practical contribution by showing that recursion can be implemented simply by modifying the training traces in the NPI framework, making it accessible to practitioners without requiring major architectural changes.

**Additional Feedback:**
The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the scope and limitations of the verification procedure. While the authors note it is infeasible for continuous input spaces (e.g., MNIST, speech), it would be helpful to clearly state the types of problems for which provable generalization is achievable. A precise characterization of the problem class for which the approach works would help readers understand the boundaries of the contribution.

The paper could also more clearly distinguish between the theoretical contribution (recursion enables provable generalization for discrete tasks with finite base cases) and the practical contribution (a method to implement recursion in NPI). This would help clarify the novelty of the work.

**Questions for Authors:**
1. How would you extend the verification framework to handle tasks with continuous input spaces, such as the 3D car example mentioned in the original NPI paper? What theoretical and practical challenges would arise?

2. The paper states that the NPI architecture naturally supports recursion due to the LSTM hidden state being reset at each subprogram call. Could you elaborate on why this reset is critical for enabling recursion, and whether this property is generalizable to other neural programming architectures?

3. For tasks where base cases might be extremely large or infinite (e.g., certain motor control tasks), how would you adapt the verification procedure? What would be the practical limitations of the current approach in such cases?
