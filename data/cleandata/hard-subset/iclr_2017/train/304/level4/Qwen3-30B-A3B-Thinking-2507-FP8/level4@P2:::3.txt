

This paper makes a significant contribution to neural program induction by introducing recursion as a key abstraction to improve generalization. The authors demonstrate that incorporating recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework through recursive training traces enables neural programs to achieve provable perfect generalization with minimal training data. Their approach is applied to four tasks—grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort—where recursive neural programs consistently outperform non-recursive versions, achieving 100% accuracy on unseen inputs with remarkably small training sets (as few as 4 examples for quicksort).

The paper's key innovation is showing that recursion enables provable guarantees about neural program behavior, which to the best of my knowledge has never been achieved before in neural program induction. The authors provide a formal verification procedure that demonstrates learned programs generalize perfectly to inputs of arbitrary complexity by verifying base cases and reduction rules, rather than relying on empirical generalization tests. This represents a major theoretical advance over previous work in the field.

I recommend acceptance for the following reasons:
1. The paper addresses a fundamental limitation of current neural program induction approaches—poor generalization to unseen inputs—and provides a theoretically grounded solution through recursion.
2. The experimental results are compelling, with recursive versions achieving perfect generalization across all four tasks, while non-recursive versions fail on longer inputs.
3. The verification procedure is novel and represents the first time neural programs can be proven to generalize perfectly, not just empirically tested.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly explaining why existing approaches fail (learning spurious dependencies on input complexity) and why recursion solves this problem (reducing complexity through base cases and reduction rules). The connection to programming language concepts is strong and well-explained.

To further improve the paper, I suggest:
1. Clarifying the relationship between the verification procedure and training process—how exactly does training on recursive traces enable the model to learn the recursive structure that makes verification possible?
2. Providing a more detailed comparison with other approaches to improve generalization (like curriculum learning), explaining why recursion is superior to these alternatives.
3. Discussing the limitations of the verification procedure for tasks with very large input domains, and suggesting potential extensions.

I have a few questions to help clarify my understanding:
1. The paper states that "the NPI model naturally supports recursion" but didn't explicitly consider it. Can the authors elaborate on why the original NPI authors overlooked this, and whether the recursive structure was accidentally learned in some prior experiments?
2. For tasks like quicksort with non-tail recursion, how does the verification procedure handle the more complex recursive structure compared to tail-recursive tasks?
3. The paper claims the verification set can be smaller than the training set (e.g., 4 training examples vs. a verification set for quicksort). How was the verification set size determined, and is it always smaller than the training set for recursive programs?
4. The paper mentions future work on decreasing supervision (training with partial or non-recursive traces). Could the authors elaborate on how this might work, and whether it would maintain the provable generalization guarantees?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The combination of empirical results showing improved generalization and theoretical verification of perfect generalization represents a major advance over current approaches. The paper is well-written and the contributions are clearly articulated. I believe this work will have a significant impact on neural program induction and related areas of program synthesis.
