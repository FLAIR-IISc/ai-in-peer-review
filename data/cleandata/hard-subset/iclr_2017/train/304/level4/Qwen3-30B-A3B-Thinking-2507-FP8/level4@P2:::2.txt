

This paper makes a compelling contribution by introducing recursion as a key abstraction to enable neural programs to generalize perfectly from minimal training data. The authors demonstrate that by incorporating recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework, they achieve provably perfect generalization on four programming tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort) with significantly smaller training sets than previous approaches. The core insight—that recursion decomposes problems into manageable subproblems with finite base cases and reduction rules, making generalization guarantees tractable—is novel and well-motivated. The paper's empirical results show that recursive neural programs achieve 100% accuracy on all test inputs after training on just a few examples, whereas non-recursive variants fail at moderate input complexities.

I recommend acceptance with the following key reasons: (1) The paper introduces a fundamentally new approach to neural program generalization through recursion, which enables provable guarantees about program behavior—a significant advancement over previous work that could only demonstrate empirical generalization; (2) The authors provide a clear verification procedure that demonstrates perfect generalization by testing only a finite set of base cases and reduction rules, rather than exhaustively evaluating all possible inputs.

The paper's strengths include its clear connection to prior work (using consistent notation with the original NPI paper), the well-structured empirical evaluation across multiple tasks, and the elegant demonstration that recursion enables the model to learn true program semantics rather than spurious correlations with input length. The verification procedure is particularly impressive, as it transforms a typically intractable generalization problem into one that can be rigorously verified with a finite number of test cases.

For improvement, the authors should strengthen their discussion of how their verification approach extends to continuous input spaces (such as those in the original NPI paper's 3D car example). While the paper demonstrates the approach works for discrete, combinatorial problems, the generalization proofs for continuous domains remain unclear. The authors should explicitly address whether their verification procedure is applicable to continuous inputs, or if additional techniques would be required.

I also recommend expanding the discussion of the verification set construction. While the paper describes how they construct verification sets for discrete tasks, the procedure for continuous domains would require additional explanation. The authors could include a brief analysis of the computational complexity of verification for different problem types.

To help readers understand the scope of their contribution, the authors should more clearly distinguish between their approach and existing work that uses recursion in neural architectures. While the paper mentions that recursion is a standard programming concept, it doesn't sufficiently contrast their neural program verification approach with prior work on recursive neural networks.

Regarding the generalization proofs, the authors should provide more concrete examples of how the verification procedure would work for a continuous input space. The paper currently assumes discrete inputs, but neural programming often deals with continuous data. The authors should either extend their framework to handle continuous domains or explicitly state the limitations of their approach.

I have two specific questions for the authors:
1. How would your verification procedure handle continuous input spaces, such as the 3D car navigation task described in the original NPI paper? Would the approach require modification, or is it inherently limited to discrete domains?
2. Will the authors release their implementation code? Given the paper's focus on a concrete implementation (NPI with recursion), providing code would significantly help other researchers reproduce and build upon this work.

Overall, this is an important contribution that addresses a fundamental limitation in neural program learning. The paper's core insight—that recursion enables provable generalization—has significant implications for the field. The empirical results are compelling, and the verification procedure represents a novel methodological contribution. With minor clarifications about the scope of their verification approach, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ICLR 2017.
