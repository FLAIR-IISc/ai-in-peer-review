

This paper argues that the ability to handle recursion is crucial for neural programming architectures, as it enables strong generalization to out-of-domain test cases and learning from smaller training datasets. The work builds upon Reed & de Freitas' 2016 ICLR Neural Programmer Interpreter (NPI) paper, which learns from program traces, by training NPI models on traces containing recursive calls. The authors demonstrate correctness verification by evaluating the learned program solely on a small set of base cases and reduction rules, showing that the NPI architecture can perfectly infer solutions for Bubblesort and the Tower of Hanoi problems.  

I appreciate the conceptual simplicity of the approach: the only modification required is providing the training pipeline with execution traces that include recursive calls. However, the significance of the results is unclear. Does this imply that the neural programming problem is effectively solved when execution traces are available—and was the problem too trivial to begin with? For instance, consider a larger input domain such as MNIST digits, where the NPI must infer sorting from highest to lowest digit values. Here, execution traces would decouple digit recognition from program logic, reducing the problem to separately learning to recognize digits and sort symbolic representations—tasks that are individually straightforward. The key unresolved question remains: what problem requires access to execution traces yet cannot be solved using the proposed method?
