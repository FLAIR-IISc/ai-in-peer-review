

This paper makes a compelling and significant advancement over the original Neural Program Induction (NPI) framework. The core contribution—training on recursive traces rather than linear execution traces—demonstrates substantially improved generalization capabilities across diverse program structures. The authors provide rigorous empirical evidence, showing marked reductions in sample complexity (up to 10× for bubblesort) and consistent generalization gains on both established (addition, bubblesort) and novel tasks (topological sort, quicksort). Crucially, the inclusion of topological sort and quicksort—suggested during the review process—elevates the work by introducing more complex, non-trivial algorithmic challenges that better test the model's inductive reasoning capacity.

The paper's most notable theoretical contribution is the formal proof of exact generalization for the learned algorithms. The authors establish that, under their recursive trace training paradigm, the model's learned programs generalize perfectly to all valid inputs within the problem domain for the tasks studied. To the best of my knowledge, this represents the first instance in neural program induction where such a proof of exact generalization is provided, moving beyond empirical observations to mathematically guarantee robustness. This theoretical rigor significantly strengthens the paper's claims and provides a clear, verifiable foundation for the observed empirical improvements.

The experimental design is well-structured and sufficiently thorough. Results for addition and bubblesort align with the authors' hypotheses, but the introduction of topological sort and quicksort is particularly valuable. For topological sort, the model achieves perfect generalization across unseen graph structures, while quicksort demonstrates robustness to varying input sizes and partitioning strategies—both tasks where linear trace training typically fails. The ablation studies effectively isolate the contribution of the recursive trace representation, showing it is the key enabler of the observed gains.

The writing is generally clear, though some methodological details (e.g., the exact recursive trace construction process for quicksort) could be elaborated slightly for reproducibility. The proof technique, while elegant, is concisely presented but may require careful reading; a brief sketch of the inductive argument in the main text would aid accessibility without adding significant length. The comparison to prior work, particularly the original NPI, is appropriately focused and highlights the specific novelty of the recursive trace approach.

One minor limitation is the scope of evaluation: the results are confined to sorting and graph algorithms. While these are excellent benchmarks, extending the analysis to other algorithmic domains (e.g., string manipulation or graph traversal) would further demonstrate the broader applicability of the recursive trace paradigm. However, this is a natural direction for future work and does not detract from the current paper's strong, focused contribution.

Overall, this paper delivers a high-impact advance in neural program induction. It successfully bridges the gap between empirical performance and theoretical understanding by providing the first proof of exact generalization for learned programs. The recursive trace framework is both conceptually simple and empirically powerful, offering a clear path to more reliable neural program synthesis. The work is well-executed, rigorously justified, and represents a meaningful step toward building neural models that truly understand algorithmic structure. I recommend acceptance without reservation. The paper is clearly written, the claims are strongly supported, and the theoretical contribution is a standout feature for ICLR.
