

This paper presents a compelling and well-written extension to Neural Programming Interpreters (NPIs) that significantly enhances their practicality through a simple yet effective addition: recursion. The authors demonstrate that enabling recursive execution allows NPIs to generalize more robustly from limited training examples, a substantial improvement over prior work. The contribution is particularly noteworthy for its elegance—showing how a non-trivial architectural modification can yield meaningful gains in model capability without introducing excessive complexity. The consistent use of notation with the original DeepMind NPI paper greatly aids comprehension, allowing non-experts to readily cross-reference both works. This thoughtful design choice enhances the paper's accessibility and impact.

The core technical contribution is clearly articulated and empirically validated across multiple tasks. The results convincingly show that recursive NPIs outperform their non-recursive counterparts, especially when training data is scarce. The method’s simplicity and effectiveness make it a valuable addition to the literature on neural program synthesis.

My sole substantive critique concerns the theoretical justification for generalization. The paper provides proofs for discrete numerical examples by enumerating execution paths until recursion terminates. However, the extension to continuous input domains—such as the 3D car navigation task referenced in the original NPI work—raises significant questions. In continuous spaces, exhaustive path enumeration is infeasible, and the tractability of formal generalization proofs remains unclear. The authors should address whether their approach inherently supports generalization in such domains or if additional assumptions or approximations are required. A more detailed discussion of this limitation would strengthen the paper’s theoretical foundation.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-motivated, but the paper would benefit from a brief comparison to recent work on neural program synthesis with recursion (e.g., from ICML 2016 or NeurIPS 2016) to better contextualize the novelty. Additionally, while the 3D car example is mentioned, the paper does not explicitly demonstrate how recursion improves performance on continuous tasks—this would be a valuable addition.

Finally, as is standard practice for reproducibility, I recommend the authors release the source code upon publication. This would enable the community to validate results and build upon the work more effectively.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that makes a meaningful step toward more practical neural program interpreters. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and addresses an important gap with a minimal yet powerful modification. The minor theoretical gap noted above is easily resolvable with additional discussion, and the work is suitable for acceptance at ICLR 2017.
