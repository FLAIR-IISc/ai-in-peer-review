

This paper proposes that incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures is essential for achieving strong generalization capabilities. The authors demonstrate that by modifying training traces to include recursive elements, the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) can learn recursive programs that generalize perfectly to inputs of arbitrary complexity, with provable guarantees. They apply this approach to four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, showing that the recursive NPI models achieve 100% accuracy on all tested inputs after training on very small datasets.

I recommend rejection with two key reasons: (1) The paper's core contribution is a relatively simple modification to training traces, and (2) The verification procedure they propose is limited to problems with finite base cases and reduction rules, making it inapplicable to many real-world problems.

The paper makes a straightforward observation: when training traces explicitly include recursive calls, the NPI learns recursive programs that generalize perfectly. This is a valuable insight, but it's important to note that this approach fundamentally depends on having access to execution traces containing recursive elements. The paper demonstrates impressive results on their specific tasks, but the approach doesn't generalize to scenarios where such execution traces aren't available. For instance, the paper doesn't address how their method would work for a problem like sorting MNIST digits from highest to lowest, where execution traces would be difficult or impossible to obtain. In such cases, the neural program would need to infer both the digit recognition and sorting logic from raw input-output pairs, which is a fundamentally different challenge.

The verification procedure the authors propose is another significant limitation. They claim to provide "provable guarantees of perfect generalization" by testing on a verification set covering base cases and reduction rules. However, this verification approach only works for problems with finite base cases, which excludes many practical applications. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't adequately address how their method would scale to problems with potentially infinite input domains. For example, the verification set for addition contains 20,181 input problems, but for more complex tasks with larger input spaces, this approach would become computationally intractable.

The paper also doesn't sufficiently compare their approach to alternative methods for learning recursive programs that don't rely on explicit recursive training traces. While the authors mention "future work" on decreasing supervision, they don't provide evidence that the current approach is necessary or superior to other techniques.

Additional feedback: To strengthen the paper, the authors should more clearly articulate the limitations of their verification procedure and provide a more detailed analysis of when their approach would and wouldn't work. It would also be valuable to include experiments on a task that doesn't have an obvious recursive structure to demonstrate the boundaries of their method.

Questions for authors:
1. How would your approach handle problems where execution traces are not available, but the program has recursive structure?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the limitations of your verification procedure for problems with infinite or very large base cases?
3. How does your method compare to other techniques for learning recursive programs that don't require access to recursive execution traces?

The paper's core insight is interesting, but the limitations of the approach and the narrow scope of their experiments make it difficult to see how this would generalize to practical programming tasks where execution traces aren't readily available. The verification procedure they propose is impressive for their specific tasks but has clear limitations that aren't sufficiently addressed.
