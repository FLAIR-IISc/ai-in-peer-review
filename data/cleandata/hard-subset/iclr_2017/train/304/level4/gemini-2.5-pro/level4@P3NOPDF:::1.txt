This paper argues that the ability to handle recursion is a critical feature for neural programming architectures, enabling strong generalization to out-of-distribution test cases while learning from smaller amounts of training data. The proposed method builds directly upon the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework (Reed & de Freitas, ICLR 2016), which learns from program execution traces. The primary contribution is the introduction of recursive calls into these training traces, which allows the NPI model to learn recursive programs. The authors also propose a method for verifying program correctness by evaluating the learned program on a small set of base cases and reduction rules. The effectiveness of this approach is impressively demonstrated by showing that the NPI can perfectly learn complex algorithms like Bubblesort and the Tower of Hanoi.

A significant strength of this work is its simplicity and elegance. As the authors themselves note, the core NPI architecture remains unchanged; the key innovation lies in augmenting the training data with execution traces that exhibit recursion. This makes the contribution easy to understand and highlights the power of providing the right training signal to these models.

However, the impressive success of the method also raises important questions about the problem formulation and the scope of the contribution. My main concern revolves around understanding the implications of having access to ground-truth execution traces. The paper's results are so strong that one might wonder: have we effectively solved the neural programming problem for any task where execution traces are available? Or, conversely, does the availability of these traces make the problem significantly easier than general program synthesis from input-output examples?

To illustrate this point, consider a more complex task, such as sorting a sequence of MNIST digits from highest to lowest value. In a setting where execution traces are provided, the problem appears to decouple into two more manageable, and largely separate, sub-problems: 1) a perception task of recognizing the digits, and 2) a symbolic reasoning task of learning a sorting algorithm (e.g., Bubblesort) on abstract symbols. The NPI would not need to jointly learn the program logic and the perception model from scratch; the traces provide the ground truth for the program's control flow, effectively separating the "what" (perception) from the "how" (algorithmic logic). This suggests that the core challenge of inferring the program logic might be less formidable when the intermediate execution steps are given.

This leads to my primary question for the authors: What are the limitations of this approach? Could you describe a class of problems where execution traces are available, yet the proposed method would still struggle or fail to learn the correct program? Understanding such failure modes is crucial for delineating the boundaries of this contribution and for guiding future research in this area. Clarifying the conditions under which this method excels, and where it might falter, would significantly strengthen the paper and provide a clearer perspective on the remaining challenges in neural program synthesis.
