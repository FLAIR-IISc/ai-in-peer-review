- Proposes recursion as a key architectural element to enable strong generalization in neural program models.  
- Shows that providing recursive execution traces lets the existing NPI learn recursive programs without changing its architecture.  
- Applies the approach successfully to grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Introduces a verification procedure that uses recursion structure to give provable guarantees of perfect generalization by testing base cases and reduction rules.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Two main reasons for acceptance: an elegant/simple solution to generalization and a novel formal verification method for neural programs.  
- Strength: demonstrates that structured supervision (recursive traces) can be more effective than more complex architectures.  
- Empirical claim: recursive NPI models achieve perfect generalization from very few training examples.  
- Question: Does providing full recursive traces sidestep the core challenge of program synthesis by handing over the recursive structure?  
- Question: How does the method extend to tasks that combine perception and algorithmic reasoning (e.g., sorting MNIST digits) where traces decouple perception from algorithms?  
- Question: What are the method’s failure modes—e.g., tasks with complex memory management, non-obvious recursive decompositions, or high-dimensional/base-case generalization?
