- Problem: Neural program synthesizers generalize poorly by learning spurious correlations instead of true algorithms.
- Contribution: Proposes incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures to improve generalization.
- Method: Augments Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) by training on explicitly recursive execution traces.
- Implementation detail: Resets the LSTM controller's hidden state at each recursive call to prevent counting and enforce modularity.
- Tasks evaluated: Grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.
- Results: Recursive models achieve perfect generalization on complex unseen inputs after training on very few simple examples.
- Verification: Introduces a method to provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization by testing a finite set of base cases and reduction rules.
- Novelty claim: First work to provide formal verification guarantees for learned neural programs in this setting.
- Supporting evidence: Empirical and conceptual arguments show recursion prevents reliance on input length and yields robust performance (including non-tail-recursive quicksort).
- Readability: Paper is well-written and reuses NPI notation to aid clarity; recursive reformulations are clearly explained.
- Limitation/question 1: Scope and limitations of verification for continuous or high-dimensional state spaces need deeper discussion.
- Limitation/question 2: Current approach relies on human-provided recursive traces; unclear if models can discover recursion from non-recursive traces or I/O pairs.
- Practicality question: Authors should clarify plans to release source code to benefit the research community.
