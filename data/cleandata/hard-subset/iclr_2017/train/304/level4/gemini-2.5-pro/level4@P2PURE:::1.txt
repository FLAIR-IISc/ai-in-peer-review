This paper posits that recursion is a critical capability for neural programming architectures, enabling strong generalization to out-of-distribution examples and learning from less training data. The proposed method builds upon the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) of Reed & de Freitas (ICLR 2016), which learns from program execution traces. The key innovation is to train the NPI model on traces that contain recursive calls. The authors also introduce a method for verifying program correctness using a small set of base cases and reduction rules. Using this framework, they impressively demonstrate that the NPI can perfectly infer algorithms such as Bubblesort and the Tower of Hanoi.

A key strength of this work lies in its elegant simplicity. As the authors acknowledge, the only required change to the original NPI framework is the content of the execution traces provided during training.

This simplicity, however, raises questions about the problem's formulation and the significance of the results. If providing execution traces is sufficient to solve these tasks, have we effectively solved neural programming, or was the problem simplified to the point of being trivial? For instance, consider a task requiring the NPI to sort MNIST digits. In this setting, providing execution traces would effectively decouple the perceptual problem of digit recognition from the algorithmic problem of learning the sorting logic. The challenge would then be reduced to two separate, and arguably simpler, problems: learning to classify MNIST digits and learning Bubblesort from symbolic representations.

This raises a fundamental question about the limitations of the approach: Can the authors describe a problem where full execution traces are available, yet this method would still be unable to infer the underlying program?
