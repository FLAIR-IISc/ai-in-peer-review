- Proposes recursion as a core abstraction for neural programming to improve generalization from limited data.  
- Integrates recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework and evaluates on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Empirically shows recursive NPI models achieve perfect generalization from very few training examples, outperforming non-recursive baselines.  
- Demonstrates that recursion makes formal verification tractable by reducing correctness to a finite set of base cases and reduction rules.  
- Highlights that recursive calls reset the controller hidden state, limiting temporal dependencies and simplifying verification.  
- Recommends acceptance due to the method’s simplicity, elegance, and effectiveness.  
- Strength: addresses reliability and interpretability by providing a verification procedure for learned programs.  
- Strength: compelling empirical evidence, notably 100% accuracy on bubble sort and quicksort after minimal training.  
- Claims novelty as the first instance of provable guarantees for a learned neural program in this context.  
- Paper is well-written and accessible, using original NPI notation for clarity.  
- Suggests strengthening discussion of verification limitations for continuous or extremely large state spaces.  
- Asks how the verification approach scales to continuous domains (e.g., visual reasoning, robotics) and whether recursion aids tractable verification there.  
- Requests whether the authors will release their NPI implementation to facilitate reproducibility.
