This paper introduces a compelling and valuable extension to the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework by incorporating recursion. The core idea is to enable the NPI model to call itself, thereby allowing it to learn generalizable, recursive algorithms from a limited number of execution traces. This is a significant step forward, as it enhances the model's expressive power and improves its data efficiency on problems with inherent recursive structures.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The authors present their contribution in an intuitive and accessible manner. A particular strength is the adherence to the notation established in the original DeepMind NPI paper. As someone not deeply embedded in this specific subfield, I found this consistency extremely helpful, as it allowed me to read the original paper in tandem with this submission to quickly grasp the key differences and contributions.

The proposed extension, while conceptually elegant, represents a non-trivial and impactful improvement. It serves as an excellent case study in how a targeted architectural modification can unlock a new level of capability in a neural model, making it significantly more practical. By addressing the lack of recursion, a fundamental tool in classical programming, the authors move the NPI framework closer to its goal of emulating a truly general programming interpreter.

My main point of critique, and an area where I would appreciate clarification, concerns the arguments for generalization. The paper provides a convincing rationale for how the model generalizes on the discrete numerical tasks presented. The argument appears to rely on the ability to iterate over all possible execution paths between recursive calls to prove that the learned sub-program is correct. However, the extension of this proof technique to continuous input spaces is not immediately obvious.

For example, considering a task like the 3D car navigation example from the original NPI paper, the state space is continuous, and the number of possible execution paths would be infinite. It seems that proving generalization by enumerating execution paths would be intractable in such a case. Could the authors elaborate on how their generalization claims might extend to continuous or high-dimensional state spaces? Is there an alternative method for analyzing generalization in these scenarios, or does this remain a key challenge for the recursive NPI framework?

Finally, to facilitate reproducibility and encourage further research in this promising direction, are there plans to release the source code associated with this work?
