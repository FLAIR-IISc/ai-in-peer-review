- Paper claims recursion is critical for neural programming architectures, enabling strong OOD generalization and efficient learning from limited data.  
- Builds on Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) by training on execution traces that include recursive calls.  
- Proposes a verification method that checks learned programs via a small set of base cases and reduction rules.  
- Empirically shows perfect learning for algorithms like Bubblesort and Tower of Hanoi.  
- Strength: solution is simple—core change is confined to the execution traces supplied during training.  
- Fundamental concern: if full execution traces are available, the problem setup may be overly constrained and less representative of program synthesis challenges.  
- Example critique: supplying traces for tasks like sorting MNIST digits decouples perception from algorithmic learning, reducing the task to two simpler problems.  
- Suggestion: authors should discuss or demonstrate problem classes where the approach fails despite trace availability to clarify the method’s limitations and boundaries.
