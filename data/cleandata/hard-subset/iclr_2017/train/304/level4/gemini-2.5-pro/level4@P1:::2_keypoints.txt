- Proposes improving generalization of neural programming architectures by explicitly incorporating recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework.  
- Applies the recursive NPI to four algorithmic tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Shows that recursion enables learning true program semantics from very few training examples.  
- Demonstrates perfect generalization on complex inputs after minimal training.  
- Introduces a verification procedure that provides provable guarantees of correctness for learned programs.  
- Recommends accepting the paper based on the simplicity and effectiveness of the recursion principle and the novel verification framework.  
- Argues recursion forces models to learn base cases and reduction rules, preventing spurious, length-dependent feature learning.  
- Empirical results strongly support the claims, and keeping NPI notation improves readability.  
- Highlights provable generalization as a major advance because a finite verification set can cover all base cases and recursive steps.  
- Suggests the paper should more explicitly discuss limitations of the verification method, especially for continuous or high-dimensional inputs where enumeration may be intractable.  
- Asks whether the authors will release source code and training data to support reproducibility.
