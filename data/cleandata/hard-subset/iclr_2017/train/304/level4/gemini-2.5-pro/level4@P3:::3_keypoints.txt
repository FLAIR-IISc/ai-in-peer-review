- Introduces recursion as a key abstraction to improve generalization of neural program architectures by avoiding spurious, length-dependent correlations.  
- Augments the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) with recursive execution traces for addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Empirical results show perfect/generalizable behavior on much larger inputs after training on very few recursive traces.  
- Proposes a novel verification procedure that, via recursion, enables formal proofs of perfect generalization across all valid inputs.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Reason 1: Significant conceptual contribution—recursion enforces modular, context-independent logic and grounds algorithmic learning in classical CS abstractions.  
- Reason 2: Novel and powerful results—high sample efficiency plus a verification framework that provides provable guarantees.  
- Implementation detail: Resetting the LSTM hidden state on each recursive call enforces contextual isolation and drives learning of core steps.  
- Empirical contrast: Non-recursive baselines fail to scale with input size, whereas recursive NPI achieves 100% accuracy on much larger instances.  
- Concern: Approach depends on strong supervision via hand-crafted recursive traces; needs discussion on learning recursion from weaker signals.  
- Concern: Implementation is tied to NPI; clarify how recursion could be integrated into other architectures (e.g., DNC, Transformer) and which components are essential.  
- Concern: Constructing the verification set V appears complex and manual; include a concrete walkthrough (e.g., for addition) to clarify the process.  
- Question: How critical is the LSTM reset mechanism, and can other architectures achieve similar contextual isolation?  
- Question: What is the path and key challenges for inducing recursive programs from weaker supervision (e.g., iterative traces or I/O pairs)?  
- Question: Can the construction of the verification set V be automated, perhaps by analyzing provided program primitives?
