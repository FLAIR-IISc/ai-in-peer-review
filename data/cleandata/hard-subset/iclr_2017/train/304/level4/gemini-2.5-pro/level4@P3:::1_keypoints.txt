- Addresses poor generalization in neural algorithm learners by explicitly incorporating recursion into training.  
- Proposes using recursive execution traces within the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework.  
- Evaluated on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, achieving perfect generalization from few examples.  
- Introduces a verification procedure that leverages recursion to provably guarantee correctness over all valid inputs by testing finite base cases and reductions.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Simple, elegant, and effective method enabling perfect generalization via recursion.  
- Key reason 2: First method (to reviewer’s knowledge) for formally verifying a learned neural program, providing provable guarantees over infinite input domains.  
- Supporting argument: Recursive traces guide models away from spurious, length-dependent correlations and decompose problems into learnable base cases and reductions.  
- Supporting argument: Empirical results show recursive models generalize flawlessly while non-recursive baselines fail as complexity increases.  
- Additional feedback: Need more discussion of dependence on high-quality, fully supervised recursive traces and their creation cost.  
- Additional feedback: Clarify scope of applicability, especially limitations for large or continuous state spaces like images or speech.  
- Additional feedback: Expand discussion on future work toward learning recursion from partial or non-recursive traces and automating discovery of recursive structure.  
- Question: How would the framework handle tasks combining perception and algorithmic reasoning (e.g., sorting MNIST digits), and does supervision effectively decouple perception from symbolic reasoning?  
- Question: What classes of problems or trace structures would this method fail on even with correct recursive traces, and can the authors characterize such limitations?  
- Question: How difficult is authoring recursive traces for new tasks, and can this process be automated in future work?
