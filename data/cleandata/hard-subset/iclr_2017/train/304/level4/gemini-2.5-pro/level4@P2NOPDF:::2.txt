This paper presents an elegant and significant extension to the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework. The central contribution is the introduction of recursion, a seemingly small modification that substantially improves the model's ability to generalize from fewer execution traces. The work serves as an excellent example of how a well-chosen architectural prior—in this case, recursion—can lead to significant gains in sample efficiency and practical applicability.

The paper is clear, well-written, and accessible. I particularly commend the authors for maintaining notational consistency with the original DeepMind paper on NPIs. For readers not deeply familiar with this specific subfield, this choice greatly facilitates understanding the contribution in the context of the original work.

My primary concern lies with the discussion of generalization. The generalization proofs for the presented numerical examples rely on iterating through all possible execution paths until the subsequent recursive call. It is unclear how this proof strategy extends to tasks with continuous input spaces, such as the 3D car manipulation task mentioned in the original NPI paper. In such cases, enumerating all possible execution paths seems intractable. The authors should provide more detail on the scope of their generalization claims or discuss the challenges of proving generalization in continuous domains.

Finally, will the source code be made available? Releasing the code would be a valuable contribution to the community and would aid in the reproducibility of these interesting results.
