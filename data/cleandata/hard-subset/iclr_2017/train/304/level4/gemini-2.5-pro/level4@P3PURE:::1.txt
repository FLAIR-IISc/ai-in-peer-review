This paper argues for the importance of recursion in neural programming architectures, positing that this capability enables strong generalization to out-of-domain test cases and learning from smaller training sets. The work builds directly upon the Neural Programmer Interpreters (NPI) framework by Reed & de Freitas (ICLR 2016), which learns from program execution traces. The central contribution of this paper is to train NPI models on execution traces that include recursive calls. The authors demonstrate a method for verifying the correctness of the learned program by evaluating it on a small set of base cases and reduction rules. Impressively, they show that this approach allows the NPI architecture to perfectly infer algorithms such as Bubblesort and the solution to the Tower of Hanoi problem.

A key strength of this work is the simplicity of the core idea. As the authors note, the only modification required is to the execution traces provided during training. This simplicity, however, raises a fundamental question about the broader implications of the work: Has the neural programming problem been effectively solved for any task where execution traces are available? Or, does this success suggest that the problem formulation, given access to traces, is less challenging than initially perceived?

To illustrate this point, consider a more complex input domain, such as sorting MNIST digits from highest to lowest. In such a scenario, providing execution traces would effectively decouple the perceptual task (recognizing digits) from the algorithmic task (inferring the sorting logic). The problem would then reduce to two separate, and arguably simpler, sub-problems: learning an MNIST classifier and learning to sort symbolic representations. This leads to a crucial question for the authors: what is an example of a problem where execution traces are available, yet the proposed method would fail to learn the correct program?
