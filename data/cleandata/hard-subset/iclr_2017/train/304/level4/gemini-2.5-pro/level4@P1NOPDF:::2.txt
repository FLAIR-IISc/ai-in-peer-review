This is a well-written and accessible paper that presents an interesting contribution to the field of Neural Programming Interpreters (NPIs). The authors propose a simple yet effective extension—the incorporation of recursion—that significantly enhances the power of the NPI framework. By allowing for recursive calls, the model can generalize more effectively from a smaller number of execution traces. This work is an excellent example of how a conceptually small, yet non-trivial, modification can lead to significant practical improvements in a machine learning method.

I also commend the authors for using notation consistent with the original DeepMind paper on NPIs. For readers less familiar with this specific area, this consistency greatly improves readability and facilitates understanding the current work in the context of the original.

My primary critique concerns the generalization proofs, which I found to be somewhat vague. For the numerical examples presented in the paper, the generalization claim is plausible because one can iterate over all possible execution paths up to the next recursive call. However, it is not clear how this reasoning extends to continuous input spaces, such as the 3D car manipulation task from the original NPI paper. It appears that a formal proof of generalization would remain intractable in such continuous cases. I would appreciate the authors' comments on this potential limitation.

Finally, do the authors plan to release their source code?
