- Proposes integrating recursion into neural programming architectures to improve generalization and enable formal reasoning about correctness.
- Argues existing neural program synthesis models learn spurious correlations rather than true program semantics.
- Trains the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) with execution traces containing explicit recursive calls to decompose problems into simpler instances.
- Demonstrates the approach on four algorithmic tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.
- Claims superior generalization from very little data compared to non-recursive models.
- Introduces a method for provably perfect generalization by verifying behavior on a finite set of base cases and reduction rules.
- Recommends acceptance because recursion is a simple yet powerful idea addressing poor generalization and verification is a significant advance.
- Leverages NPI’s inherent stack-based mechanism and only modifies training data rather than designing a new architecture.
- Empirical results show recursive models achieve perfect accuracy on complex inputs after training on a handful of simple examples, unlike non-recursive counterparts.
- Reviewer believes this is the first demonstration of a correctness guarantee for a neural program synthesizer (to their knowledge).
- Constructive feedback: reliance on full recursive execution traces is strong supervision—need discussion of applicability and trace availability.
- Constructive feedback: address tasks where perception and logic are entangled (e.g., sorting MNIST digits) and whether approach still applies.
- Constructive feedback: discuss potential failure modes and problems where recursive traces may not suffice to learn correct programs.
