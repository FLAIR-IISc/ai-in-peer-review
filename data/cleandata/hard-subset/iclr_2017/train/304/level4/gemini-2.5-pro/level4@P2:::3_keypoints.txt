- Proposes explicitly incorporating recursion into neural program architectures to improve generalization.  
- Instantiates the idea within the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework.  
- Evaluates on four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Recursive models achieve dramatically better sample efficiency and perfect generalization, outperforming non-recursive variants.  
- Introduces a verification framework that uses recursion to reduce correctness checking to finite base cases and reduction rules.  
- Claims to be the first work in neural program synthesis to provide provable generalization guarantees.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Novel and significant insight that recursion addresses a primary weakness of prior models.  
- Key reason 2: Formal verification is a major conceptual advance for trustworthy neural programs.  
- Recursion forces state resets per subproblem, preventing learning spurious input-size correlations and promoting true algorithmic semantics.  
- Experimental choices are strong and diverse, and the bubble sort ablation effectively isolates recursion’s impact.  
- Verification framework makes neural program behavior tractable and addresses interpretability concerns.  
- Concern: Method depends on high-quality, fully recursive execution traces—questionable practicality for weaker supervision or I/O-only training.  
- Concern: Constructing the verification set SV may be nontrivial; ask about automating SV generation from implementations or specs.  
- Concern: Unclear how approach extends to continuous or high-dimensional domains where base cases and reductions are less defined.  
- Reproducibility request: Clarify any NPI implementation differences or key details in the appendix.
