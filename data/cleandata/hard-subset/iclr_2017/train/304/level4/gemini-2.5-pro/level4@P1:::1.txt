### Review

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces recursion as a key architectural element for neural programming models to achieve strong generalization. The authors argue that explicitly incorporating recursion allows models to learn robust program semantics from very few training examples and generalize perfectly to inputs of unseen complexity. The core idea is demonstrated by augmenting the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework. Rather than altering the NPI architecture itself, the authors show that simply providing it with training data consisting of recursive execution traces is sufficient for it to learn recursive programs. The paper successfully applies this method to several algorithmic tasks, including grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. A significant contribution is a proposed verification procedure, which leverages the structure of recursion to provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization by testing the learned model on a finite set of base cases and reduction rules.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a well-executed paper with a clear and compelling core idea. My decision to accept is based on two key reasons:
1.  The paper presents an elegant and remarkably simple solution to the critical problem of generalization in neural program synthesis.
2.  It introduces a novel method for formally verifying neural programs, a significant step towards building more reliable models that can provide performance guarantees.

**Supporting Arguments**

The strength of this work lies in its simplicity. The authors demonstrate that a complex problem—poor generalization in neural program synthesizers—can be effectively addressed not by designing more intricate architectures, but by providing the model with the right kind of structured supervision. The finding that an existing architecture like NPI can learn recursive programs simply by observing recursive traces is a powerful and insightful result.

Furthermore, the concept of "provably perfect generalization" is a crucial contribution. While neural networks are often treated as black boxes, this paper provides a concrete methodology for verifying the correctness of a learned neural program over an infinite domain of inputs. This moves the field forward by showing a path toward building more trustworthy and reliable program synthesis systems. The empirical results are impressive, showing that the recursive NPI models achieve perfect generalization on complex tasks from a very small number of training examples.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision. The paper's success raises important questions about the scope and limitations of this approach. The results are so strong that it prompts a deeper reflection on the nature of the problem being solved.

*   Does this work suggest that the main challenge in neural programming is not learning the program's logic, but rather discovering the appropriate recursive structure? If full, correct recursive execution traces are provided, has the core difficulty of program synthesis been sidestepped?

*   To probe the boundaries of this method, consider tasks with more complex, high-dimensional inputs, such as sorting MNIST digits. In such a scenario, providing execution traces seems to decouple the perceptual problem (recognizing digits) from the algorithmic problem (learning to sort). This suggests that the remaining challenge would be equivalent to learning a sorting algorithm on symbolic inputs, plus a standard image classification task. I would appreciate the authors' perspective on this and a discussion of how their framework applies to problems where perception and algorithmic reasoning are more deeply intertwined.

*   This leads to a final, crucial question: What are the failure modes of this approach? Could the authors describe a class of problems where recursive execution traces are available, yet this method would still fail to learn a generalizable program? For example, would this approach struggle with programs that require more complex memory management, non-obvious recursive decompositions, or where the base cases are themselves high-dimensional and require generalization?
