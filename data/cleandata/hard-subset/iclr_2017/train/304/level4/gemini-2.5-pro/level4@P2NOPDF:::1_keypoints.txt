- Paper claims recursion handling is critical for neural programming architectures to generalize from limited/out-of-domain data.
- Method: a direct extension of Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) that trains from program execution traces.
- Key modification: include recursive calls in training traces.
- Verification: correctness checked by testing a small set of base cases and reduction rules.
- Experiments: modified NPI perfectly learns classic recursive algorithms (Bubblesort, Tower of Hanoi).
- Strength: simplicity and elegance—the only change is trace content, yet it enables learning complex recursion.
- Concern: providing full execution traces may trivialize program induction by removing the core difficulty of discovering algorithms.
- Example: sorting MNIST with traces would separate perception (digit recognition) from algorithmic learning, making the task easier.
- Implication: traces can offload the hardest part—algorithm discovery—leaving simpler subproblems.
- Request: authors should clarify limitations and identify problem classes where access to full traces would still fail to yield correct learned programs.
