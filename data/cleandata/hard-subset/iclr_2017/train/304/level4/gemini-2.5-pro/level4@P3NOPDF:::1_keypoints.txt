- Claim: Recursion is critical for neural programming architectures, enabling strong out-of-distribution generalization and learning from less training data.  
- Method: Builds on Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) by adding recursive calls into execution traces so the NPI can learn recursive programs.  
- Verification: Introduces a correctness-checking method that evaluates learned programs on a small set of base cases and reduction rules.  
- Results: Demonstrates impressive success—NPI perfectly learns complex algorithms like Bubblesort and Tower of Hanoi.  
- Strength: Work is simple and elegant—the NPI architecture is unchanged; the key contribution is supplying recursive execution traces as the training signal.  
- Concern: Having access to ground-truth execution traces may substantially simplify the problem—raising whether this effectively solves neural programming when traces exist.  
- Example/implication: With traces, tasks like sorting MNIST digits decouple perception from algorithm learning, so the model need not jointly learn perception and control flow.  
- Request/limitation: Reviewer asks authors to characterize failure modes and problem classes where traces are available but the method would still struggle, to clarify the scope and boundaries of the contribution.
