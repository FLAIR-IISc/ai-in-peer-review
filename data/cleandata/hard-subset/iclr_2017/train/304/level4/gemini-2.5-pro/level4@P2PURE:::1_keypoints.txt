- Paper claims recursion is a critical capability for neural programming architectures, enabling strong OOD generalization and learning from less data.
- Method builds on Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) and trains it on execution traces containing recursive calls.
- Authors propose a verification method for program correctness using a small set of base cases and reduction rules.
- Empirical result: NPI trained this way can perfectly infer algorithms such as Bubblesort and Tower of Hanoi.
- Strength: elegant simplicity — the only change to NPI is the content of the execution traces.
- Concern: simplicity raises doubts about problem formulation and result significance—providing traces may trivialize the task.
- Example critique: with tasks like sorting MNIST digits, execution traces decouple perception from algorithm learning, reducing the challenge to two simpler problems.
- Open limitation: unclear whether there exist problems where full execution traces are available yet this method would fail to infer the underlying program.
