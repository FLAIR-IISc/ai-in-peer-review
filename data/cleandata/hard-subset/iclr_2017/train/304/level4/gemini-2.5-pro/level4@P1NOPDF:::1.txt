This paper argues that the ability to handle recursion is a critical capability for neural programming architectures, enabling strong generalization to out-of-distribution test cases and more efficient learning from limited training data. The work builds directly upon the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework of Reed & de Freitas (ICLR 2016), which learns from program execution traces. The primary contribution of this paper is to extend that framework by training NPI models on traces that include recursive calls. The authors introduce a method for verifying the correctness of the learned program by evaluating it on a small set of base cases and reduction rules. Impressively, they demonstrate that this approach enables the NPI architecture to perfectly learn algorithms like Bubblesort and the Tower of Hanoi.

A strength of this work is its compelling simplicity; as the authors note, the core modification is confined to the execution traces provided during training. This success, however, raises a fundamental question about the problem formulation: if complete execution traces are available, does this effectively solve the core challenge of neural program synthesis, or is the problem setup itself overly constrained?

For instance, consider a more complex task, such as sorting MNIST digits by their numerical value. In such a scenario, providing execution traces would effectively decouple the perceptual challenge of digit recognition from the algorithmic task of learning the sorting logic. The problem would then reduce to two separate, and arguably simpler, sub-problems: learning an MNIST classifier and learning to sort abstract symbols.

This raises the question of the method's limitations. It would be valuable for the authors to discuss or demonstrate a class of problems where, even with access to execution traces, the proposed approach would struggle or fail. Clarifying the boundaries of this technique would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.
