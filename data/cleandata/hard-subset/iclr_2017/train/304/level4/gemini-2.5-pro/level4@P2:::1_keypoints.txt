- Introduces recursion as an abstraction to improve generalization in neural program synthesis by decomposing problems into self-similar subproblems.  
- Modifies NPI training traces to include explicit recursive calls rather than iterative traces.  
- Demonstrates recursive NPI learns recursive programs for addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Recursively-trained models achieve near-perfect generalization from very small training sets.  
- Proposes a verification framework enabling formal proof of correctness on an infinite input set via a finite set of base cases and reduction rules.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Novelty/significance: Leveraging recursion is a simple, elegant, and impactful idea that enables provable generalization and improves reliability/interpretability.  
- Strong empirical support: Clear experimental evidence shows recursive traces vastly outperform non-recursive training, especially on sorting tasks.  
- Recursive formulation removes model dependence on input length by enforcing fixed-length controller behavior per subproblem.  
- Verification is tractable because recursion reduces verification to testing a finite set of cases.  
- Primary weakness: Heavy reliance on strong supervision—full, correct, explicit recursive execution traces limits real-world applicability.  
- Concern that providing recursive traces sidesteps the hard problem of discovering recursion from raw data (e.g., perception + algorithm separation).  
- Request to clarify scope: whether contribution is showing NPI's latent recursion capability or advocating a general training paradigm for neural algorithmic tasks.  
- Question about constructing the verification set SV: manual/semi-manual process, scalability, and potential automation from formal specifications.  
- Question about applicability to other architectures: can recursive-trace training transfer to models like DNCs or Neural GPUs, or is NPI uniquely suited due to its stack-based calls?  
- Clarifying questions: whether classes of problems (mutual recursion, complex data structures) might resist this approach; and whether all iterative loops must be converted to recursion for provable generalization given partial-recursive models fail.
