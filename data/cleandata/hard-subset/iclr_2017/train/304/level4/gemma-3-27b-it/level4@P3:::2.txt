## Review of “Making Neural Programming Architectures Generalize via Recursion”

This paper presents a compelling and well-written approach to improving the generalization capabilities of neural program synthesis, specifically within the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework. The authors identify a key limitation of existing methods – poor generalization beyond a certain complexity threshold – and propose incorporating recursion as a crucial abstraction to address this issue. The empirical results on grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort demonstrate significant improvements in generalization performance with remarkably small training sets. The connection to provable generalization, even if limited in scope currently, is a particularly exciting contribution. The consistent notation with the original NPI paper is also a significant aid to understanding.

**Decision:** Accept with minor revisions. The paper addresses a significant challenge in neural program synthesis and proposes a novel and effective solution. The experimental results are convincing, and the potential for provable generalization is a promising direction.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper clearly articulates the problem of generalization in neural program synthesis. Existing methods struggle to scale to inputs beyond those seen during training, often exhibiting brittle behavior due to spurious correlations. The authors convincingly argue that recursion offers a way to mitigate this by breaking down complex problems into smaller, more manageable subproblems, reducing the domain of each neural network component and enabling more robust learning of program semantics. 

The empirical evaluation is well-executed and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The results across four diverse tasks – including sorting and graph manipulation – highlight the broad applicability of recursion. The reported 100% accuracy on test sets after training on limited data is particularly impressive. 

The most significant contribution is the initial exploration of *provable* generalization. While the current verification procedure is limited to discrete cases, it establishes a crucial first step towards formally guaranteeing the correctness of learned programs. This is a significant departure from the typical empirical evaluation in this field and opens up exciting new avenues for research.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

While the discussion of provable generalization is a major strength, the limitations are acknowledged but could be further elaborated. Specifically, the paper briefly mentions the difficulty of extending the current verification approach to continuous input spaces (e.g., robotic control). Expanding on this limitation and outlining potential future directions for addressing it (e.g., formal methods for continuous systems, sampling-based verification) would strengthen the paper.

The description of the verification procedure could be slightly more detailed. While the core idea is clear, providing a more concrete example of how the base cases and reduction rules are identified and verified for one of the tasks (e.g., addition) would improve clarity.

Regarding the implementation details, providing information about the specific hyperparameters used for training (e.g., learning rate, batch size, LSTM hidden size) would enhance reproducibility.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. The paper states that the recursive trace generation “is one way to train a recursive neural program.” What other approaches to training recursive neural programs are you considering or planning to explore? Specifically, could recursion be induced *without* explicitly providing recursive training traces, perhaps through reward shaping or curriculum learning?

2. The verification procedure relies on identifying the “set of valid sequences of step inputs.” How sensitive is this procedure to the completeness of this set? In other words, what is the impact of missing a valid sequence in V on the guarantee of perfect generalization?

3. You mention that the complexity of verification might preclude its use for tasks with very large or infinite state spaces. Could techniques like abstract interpretation or compositional verification be explored to address this limitation in future work?

4. Will the source code for your implementation be made publicly available? This would greatly facilitate reproducibility and further research in this area.
