This paper presents an interesting and readily accessible approach to enhancing Neural Programming Interpreters (NPIs). The authors demonstrate that incorporating recursion significantly improves generalization from limited execution traces. This is a compelling example of how a focused, yet non-trivial, extension can substantially increase the practicality of a machine learning method. I particularly appreciated the consistent notation used between this work and the original DeepMind paper, which facilitated understanding for a non-expert like myself.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper addresses a significant limitation of neural program synthesis – poor generalization – with a simple and effective solution. The empirical results are convincing, and the introduction of provable generalization, even in a limited context, is a valuable contribution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of augmenting NPIs with recursion is well-motivated. Existing neural program learning models struggle to generalize beyond a certain complexity, often learning spurious correlations rather than the underlying program semantics. Recursion provides a natural way to decompose problems and reduce the complexity of individual neural network components, enabling more robust learning and generalization. The experimental results on grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort consistently demonstrate superior performance with recursive NPIs compared to their non-recursive counterparts, especially when training data is scarce. 

The novelty of providing *provable* generalization guarantees, even if currently limited to discrete tasks, is noteworthy. This is a significant step towards building more trustworthy and reliable neural program synthesis systems. The authors clearly articulate the conditions under which these guarantees hold (correctness of base cases and reduction rules) and provide a verification procedure.

**Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

While the generalization proofs are a strong point, the discussion of their limitations could be expanded. The paper acknowledges the difficulty of applying this approach to continuous input spaces, but a more detailed explanation of the challenges and potential avenues for addressing them would be beneficial. For instance, discussing techniques for approximating or discretizing continuous spaces, or exploring alternative verification methods, could strengthen the argument. 

The paper would benefit from discussing the computational cost of the recursive approach. Does recursion significantly increase training or inference time? A brief analysis of this aspect would provide a more complete picture of the method’s practicality.

Finally, the availability of source code would greatly enhance the reproducibility and impact of this work.  Please consider releasing the code to the research community.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational overhead introduced by recursion (both during training and inference)?
2.  The verification procedure relies on explicitly defining base cases and reduction rules. How scalable is this approach to more complex programs with a larger state space? Are there strategies to automate or simplify the definition of these elements?
3.  You mention exploring alternative ways to train recursive neural programs without explicit execution traces. Could you provide more detail on these future directions? What challenges do you foresee in such an approach?
4.  Are you planning on releasing the source code for this work?
