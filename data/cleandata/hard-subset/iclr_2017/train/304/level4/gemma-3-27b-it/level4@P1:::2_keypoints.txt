- Overall positive: interesting, readable paper presenting a small but effective improvement to Neural Programmer-Interpreters.  
- Main contribution: incorporating recursion into NPI significantly improves generalization from few execution traces.  
- Practical impact: small non-trivial extension makes the method substantially more practical.  
- Notation: authors maintained consistency with the original DeepMind paper, aiding readability.  
- Primary critique: generalization proofs are limited in scope and tailored to discrete examples.  
- Unclear scaling: it’s unclear how the verification approach extends to continuous input spaces (e.g., 3D car example).  
- Request: authors should consider releasing source code to aid reproducibility.  
- Summary of experiments: evaluated on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort with strong generalization results.  
- Provable guarantees: ability to provide formal verification of correctness is a notable contribution.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Strengths: clear problem statement, well-motivated recursion approach, strong empirical results (100% with small training sets), and good connections to prior work.  
- Suggested additions: elaborate limitations of verification for continuous inputs and discuss potential extensions (abstraction/sampling).  
- Suggested experiments: include ablation studies on partial recursion across tasks.  
- Scalability concern: discuss computational cost and scaling of the verification procedure.  
- Questions for authors: feasibility of extending verification to continuous inputs, challenges for more complex tasks (dynamic data structures/APIs), verification resource requirements/scaling, and willingness to release code.
