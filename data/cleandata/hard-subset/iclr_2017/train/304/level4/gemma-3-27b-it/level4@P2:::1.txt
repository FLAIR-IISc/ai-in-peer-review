This paper proposes that incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures is crucial for achieving strong generalization and learning from limited data. The work builds upon the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework from Reed & de Freitas (ICLR 2016), modifying the training data to include recursive calls within program traces. The authors demonstrate that NPI models trained with these recursive traces achieve perfect performance on tasks like bubble sort and topological sort, and importantly, present a method for *verifying* this correctness by evaluating only base cases and reduction rules.

I recommend **rejecting** this paper. While the idea of leveraging recursion is intuitively appealing and the experimental results are promising, the paper feels incomplete and the significance of the contribution remains unclear. Specifically, the strong results appear to be heavily reliant on the availability of execution traces, and the paper doesn’t adequately address the limitations of this setup.

My primary concern is the dependence on complete execution traces. The authors acknowledge this, but downplay its significance. The proposed method effectively solves the programming problem *given* the traces, which shifts the focus from program synthesis to trace learning. The paper doesn't sufficiently grapple with scenarios where obtaining such traces is difficult or impossible. The example of MNIST digit sorting illustrates this point – the trace-based approach decouples digit recognition from sorting logic, trivializing the problem.  A convincing argument needs to be made about the practical relevance of this trace-based learning paradigm, or the limitations explicitly acknowledged and framed as future work.

Secondly, the verification procedure, while impressive, reinforces the reliance on the trace-based approach. The ability to verify correctness with a small set of base cases and reduction rules is a direct consequence of the recursive structure *introduced by the traces*, not necessarily a breakthrough in neural program verification itself. The paper needs to better articulate what specifically about recursion enables this verification process.

Finally, while the experimental results on bubble sort and topological sort are compelling, the paper lacks a thorough comparison to non-recursive approaches trained with more sophisticated techniques (e.g., curriculum learning, data augmentation).  Demonstrating a clear advantage over existing methods, beyond simply showing that recursion *can* work, is crucial.

Here are some suggestions for improvement:

*   **Expand the discussion of limitations:** More thoroughly address the scenarios where obtaining execution traces is impractical or impossible. Explore alternative training paradigms that don't rely on complete traces.
*   **Clarify the contribution:** Explicitly state whether the paper aims to solve the neural programming problem *given* traces, or whether it provides a more general approach to program synthesis.
*   **Strengthen the comparison to existing methods:** Include a more comprehensive comparison to non-recursive approaches, employing state-of-the-art training techniques.
*   **Elaborate on the verification procedure:** Provide a more detailed explanation of how recursion simplifies the verification process, and why it's difficult to apply to non-recursive models.
*   **Consider more complex tasks:** Evaluate the approach on more challenging problems that go beyond simple sorting and graph traversal. Perhaps tasks that require reasoning about more complex data structures.
*   **Discuss the scalability of the verification method:** How does the verification procedure scale to larger and more complex programs? The current description suggests it might become intractable.

I have several questions for the authors:

*   How does the performance of the recursive NPI model compare to a non-recursive NPI model trained with curriculum learning?
*   What are the challenges of applying this approach to problems where obtaining execution traces is difficult or impossible?
*   Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of why recursion simplifies the verification process?
*   How does the size of the verification set scale with the complexity of the program?
*   Are there any theoretical guarantees on the generalization ability of the learned recursive programs? (Beyond the verification on the test set.)
