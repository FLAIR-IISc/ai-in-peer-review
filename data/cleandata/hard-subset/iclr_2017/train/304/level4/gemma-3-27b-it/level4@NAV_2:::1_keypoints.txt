- Recommendation: accept.
- Core claim: incorporating recursion into neural programming is crucial for strong generalization and learning from limited data.
- Main contribution: augmenting the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) with recursive calls by modifying training traces.
- Key benefit: recursion yields substantial performance gains and enables formal verification of correctness.
- Demonstrations: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort achieve perfect generalization after small-data training.
- Provable guarantees: verification via testing base cases and reduction rules is provided and effective.
- Method appeal: simple, practical approach that alters training data rather than complex architectures.
- Positioning: clearly situated within NPI literature, addressing prior generalization shortcomings.
- Experimental strength: convincing results and a detailed, well-justified verification procedure.
- Insightful analysis: study of different recursion levels in bubble sort clarifies the impact of recursion abstraction.
- Limitation noted: discussion on applicability to larger input domains (e.g., MNIST) should be expanded, including input representation complexity.
- Suggested extensions: explore learning recursion without explicit execution traces (e.g., reinforcement or curriculum learning).
- Suggested analysis: deeper examination of how the LSTM hidden state encodes recursive structure.
- Clarifying questions for authors: whether prior attempts encouraged recursion in original NPI; sensitivity and confidence metrics for the verification set; whether additional traces yield diminishing returns for topo/quicksort; and what problem characteristics make tasks hard even with full execution traces.
