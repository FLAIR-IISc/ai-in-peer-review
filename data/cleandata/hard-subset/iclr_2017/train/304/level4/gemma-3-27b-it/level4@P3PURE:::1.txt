This paper argues for the importance of recursion in neural programming architectures, positing that the ability to handle recursive calls leads to stronger generalization to out-of-domain test cases and improved learning efficiency with smaller training datasets. The work builds heavily on the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework introduced by Reed & de Freitas at ICLR 2016, which learns from program traces. Specifically, the authors train NPI models using traces that include recursive calls, and demonstrate a method for verifying correctness through evaluation on a limited set of base cases and reduction rules. Remarkably, the NPI architecture achieves perfect inference of both Bubblesort and the Tower of Hanoi problems.

A key strength of this work is the simplicity of the core idea – as the authors note, the sole modification lies in the composition of the training execution traces. However, this simplicity raises questions about the broader implications. It’s unclear whether this result indicates a resolution to the neural programming problem *given* access to execution traces, or if the selected problems were unduly constrained. For instance, extending the input domain to something like MNIST digits presents a challenge: consider a task requiring the NPI to sort MNIST digits from highest to lowest. In this scenario, access to execution traces would effectively separate the digit recognition problem from the program logic inference, reducing the overall difficulty to learning digit recognition alongside a symbolic Bubblesort. This prompts the question of identifying a problem with available execution traces that remains intractable for the proposed method – what limitations still exist?
