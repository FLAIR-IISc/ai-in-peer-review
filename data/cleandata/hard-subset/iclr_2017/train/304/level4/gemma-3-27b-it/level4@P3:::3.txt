## Review of “MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION”

This paper presents a compelling argument for the importance of recursion in enabling neural networks to learn programs that generalize effectively. The authors augment the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework with recursion and demonstrate substantial improvements in generalization performance on four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. A particularly noteworthy contribution is the demonstration of *provable* generalization – a significant step forward in the field of neural program induction.

**Decision:** **Accept** with enthusiasm. The paper addresses a critical limitation of existing neural program synthesis techniques – poor generalization – and offers a clear, well-motivated, and empirically validated solution. The theoretical contribution of demonstrating provable generalization is particularly strong and sets this work apart.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper clearly articulates the problem of generalization in neural program learning. Existing approaches often struggle to perform reliably on inputs beyond a certain complexity threshold, due to spurious correlations learned during training. The authors convincingly argue that recursion provides a crucial abstraction for mitigating this issue by reducing the complexity of the learning problem and fostering the development of more robust and interpretable program representations. 

The experimental results are impressive. The recursive NPI models consistently outperform non-recursive counterparts across all four tasks, achieving 100% accuracy on test inputs after training on relatively small datasets. The inclusion of topological sort and quicksort adds significant value, extending the evaluation beyond simpler problems. 

Crucially, the authors go beyond empirical demonstration and provide a rigorous verification procedure to *prove* that the learned recursive programs generalize perfectly given a defined verification set. This is a groundbreaking achievement and addresses a major challenge in the field – establishing confidence in the correctness and reliability of neural program synthesizers. The detailed explanation of the verification procedure, including the construction of the verification set, is commendable.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong overall, here are a few suggestions for improvement:

*   **Clarify the connection to curriculum learning:** The paper briefly mentions curriculum learning but could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how recursion differs from, and potentially complements, this approach.
*   **Discussion of limitations:** The paper acknowledges limitations in the verification procedure for tasks with very large input domains. Expanding on these limitations and discussing potential future directions for addressing them would be valuable.
*   **Ablation Study:** A focused ablation study investigating the impact of different components of the recursive training traces would be beneficial. For example, how much does the full recursion in bubble sort contribute versus the partial recursion?
*   **Code Release:** Releasing the code for reproducing the experiments would greatly enhance the impact of this work and facilitate further research in the area.
*   **Hyperparameter details**: Providing a more complete table of hyperparameters used in the experiments would increase reproducibility.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions future work exploring alternative training methods for recursive neural programs without relying on explicit execution traces. Could you elaborate on potential approaches you are considering?
2.  In the verification process, constructing the set 'V' is crucial. How sensitive is the verification to the completeness of 'V'? Could a slightly incomplete 'V' lead to a false positive verification result?
3.  The paper demonstrates perfect generalization on the tasks presented. Do you anticipate challenges in applying this approach to more complex programming tasks where the program semantics may be more nuanced or ambiguous?
4.  The quicksort implementation, while functional, appears more complex than the other examples. Could you comment on the trade-offs between expressiveness and the complexity of verification when designing recursive programs?
