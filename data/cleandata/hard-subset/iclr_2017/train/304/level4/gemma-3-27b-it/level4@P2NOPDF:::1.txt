This paper investigates the importance of recursion for neural programming architectures, arguing that the ability to handle recursive calls leads to improved generalization and data efficiency. The work builds directly upon the Neural Programmer Interpreter (NPI) framework introduced by Reed & de Freitas (ICLR 2016), which learns programs from execution traces. The key contribution of this paper is training NPI models using traces that *include* recursive function calls. 

The authors demonstrate a compelling ability to learn complex algorithms—specifically, Bubblesort and the Tower of Hanoi—to perfect accuracy. This is verified through evaluation on a limited set of base cases and reduction rules, a surprisingly robust approach. The simplicity of the core idea is a significant strength; as the authors note, the only modification to the original NPI framework lies in the composition of the training data – the inclusion of recursive trace data.

However, this simplicity also raises questions about the broader implications of the results. The success achieved on these tasks leads one to wonder if the problem was, in some sense, too easily addressed given the availability of complete execution traces. A crucial question remains: does this work represent a significant step towards solving the neural programming problem *in general*, or only in the specific scenario where full execution traces are provided?

To illustrate this concern, consider extending the problem domain to something more complex, such as sorting MNIST digits from highest to lowest. In this case, access to execution traces would effectively separate the task into two sub-problems: digit recognition and sorting. The NPI would then only need to learn to recognize the digits and apply a bubble sort operation to symbolic representations of those digits. This decoupling would arguably trivialize the programming aspect of the problem. 

Therefore, a crucial direction for future work is to identify tasks where the presence of execution traces *does not* fully simplify the programming challenge to this degree. It would be beneficial to explore scenarios where the execution traces themselves require complex interpretation, or where the underlying problem necessitates reasoning beyond simple algorithmic application. Defining such a challenging problem, and demonstrating the limitations of the proposed method on it, would provide a more complete understanding of the approach's capabilities and guide future research in neural programming.
