## Review of “Learning to Execute Recursive Programs”

This paper presents a compelling advancement in Neural Program Induction (NPI), building substantially upon the authors’ prior work. The central contribution lies in demonstrating that training the NPI model on recursively-defined program traces dramatically improves generalization performance and sample complexity. Through rigorous experimentation on addition, bubblesort, topological sort, and quicksort – the latter two tasks added in response to reviewer feedback, which is commendable – the authors convincingly illustrate the benefits of this recursive training approach. 

The core strength of this work is the significant empirical improvement over existing NPI methods. The results on addition and bubblesort are noteworthy, but the extension to topological and quicksort presents a considerably more challenging and interesting benchmark. These new tasks represent a leap in complexity and demonstrate the scalability of the proposed method to algorithms commonly encountered in introductory computer science curricula. The empirical validation is comprehensive, with clear comparisons to baselines and careful analysis of performance across varying dataset sizes. 

However, the most significant and novel contribution of this paper is the *proof* of perfect generalization for the learned algorithms. This is a critical result. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first instance where formal guarantees of generalization have been provided in the context of neural program induction. The proof itself appears sound and builds upon the properties of the recursive training regime, providing strong theoretical backing for the observed empirical benefits. The clarity with which the authors present this proof is also appreciated.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the model and training procedure. The motivation for the work is clearly stated, and the connection to existing NPI literature is well-defined. The inclusion of an appendix with additional experimental details and proofs is a positive aspect, enhancing reproducibility.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Empirical Improvement:** Demonstrates substantial gains in sample complexity and generalization compared to previous NPI approaches.
* **Challenging Benchmarks:** Successfully extends NPI to more complex and realistic algorithms (topological sort and quicksort).
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** Provides a formal proof of perfect generalization, a novel contribution to the field.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and the technical details are presented in a clear and accessible manner.
* **Responsiveness to Feedback:** The authors incorporated reviewer suggestions to strengthen the work, notably by adding the quicksort and topological sort tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

While the results are very strong, a potential area for further exploration is the practical limitations of applying this approach to even more complex programs. The tasks explored, while significantly more challenging than prior work, still represent a relatively constrained domain. Investigating the scalability of the method to programs with larger state spaces or more intricate control flow would be a valuable extension. Furthermore, while the proof of generalization is impressive, understanding the *conditions* under which this generalization holds (e.g., specific properties of the recursive structure) could provide further insights. 

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Neural Program Induction. The combination of compelling empirical results, theoretical guarantees, and a clear presentation makes it a valuable addition to the ICLR conference. The authors have successfully demonstrated that training NPI models on recursive traces leads to substantial improvements in generalization and sample complexity, and their proof of perfect generalization is a landmark achievement. 

**Recommendation:**

Strong Accept.
