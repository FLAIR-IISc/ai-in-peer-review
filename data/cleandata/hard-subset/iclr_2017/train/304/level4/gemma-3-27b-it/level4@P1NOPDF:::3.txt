This paper represents a significant improvement over the original Neural Program Induction (NPI) work, demonstrating that the model generalizes far more effectively when trained on traces represented in recursive form. The authors present compelling results showing improved sample complexity and generalization performance on addition and bubblesort programs, and importantly, introduce two more challenging and interesting tasks – topological sort and quicksort (added following discussion with reviewers). Furthermore, they provide a formal proof demonstrating that the algorithms learned by their model generalize perfectly – to my knowledge, this is the first instance of such a result in the field of neural program induction. 

The core idea of recasting program execution traces into a recursive structure is insightful and well-motivated. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with careful consideration given to both simple and more complex algorithmic tasks. The addition of topological and quicksort demonstrates a responsiveness to feedback and a willingness to tackle more substantial problems, strengthening the paper considerably. 

However, I do have some concerns. While the theoretical guarantee of perfect generalization is a major strength, the practical implications of this guarantee are not fully explored. The tasks considered, while illustrative, are still relatively constrained. It remains unclear how well this approach would scale to programs with significantly larger state spaces or more complex control flow. A discussion of these limitations would be beneficial.

Specifically regarding the experiments, the comparison to the original NPI model is strong, but a comparison to other relevant baselines – such as those based on sequence-to-sequence models or reinforcement learning – would further contextualize the improvements achieved. The choice of hyperparameters, particularly the size of the LSTM, feels somewhat arbitrary. A brief ablation study investigating the sensitivity of performance to these parameters would be valuable. 

The presentation is generally clear, though some sections could benefit from further clarification. For example, the explanation of the recursive trace representation could be expanded with a more detailed example. Similarly, the connection between the recursive structure and the generalization guarantee is not entirely obvious and could be elaborated upon.

Despite these minor points, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of neural program induction. The combination of empirical results and theoretical guarantees is particularly impressive. I believe that addressing the concerns outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.

**Strengths:**

* Significant improvement over previous NPI work.
* Insightful use of recursive trace representation.
* Strong experimental results on multiple tasks.
* Formal proof of perfect generalization.
* Responsiveness to reviewer feedback.

**Weaknesses:**

* Limited discussion of scalability to more complex programs.
* Lack of comparison to a broader range of baselines.
* Hyperparameter choices lack justification.
* Some sections could benefit from further clarification.



I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the points raised above.
