## Review of “Neural Programmer-Interpreters with Recursion”

This paper presents an interesting and readily accessible contribution to the field of Neural Program Interpreters (NPIs). The authors demonstrate a surprisingly effective, yet conceptually simple, extension to the original NPI framework – the introduction of recursion. This addition demonstrably improves generalization performance, particularly when training data is limited, enabling NPIs to learn more complex programs from fewer execution traces. This work serves as a compelling illustration of how a small, non-trivial modification can significantly enhance the practical applicability of a machine learning technique.

A significant strength of this paper lies in its consistency with the original DeepMind NPI publication regarding notation and terminology. This consistency greatly aided my understanding as a reviewer less familiar with this specific area. The ability to readily cross-reference the two papers facilitated a smoother learning process and allowed for a quicker grasp of the innovation presented here. 

The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the benefits of recursion across several tasks. The ablation studies effectively highlight the impact of this extension, and the comparisons to non-recursive baselines clearly illustrate the improvements achieved. The examples provided are well-chosen and help to solidify the understanding of how recursion allows the NPI to learn more generalizable programs.

However, the theoretical justification for the improved generalization capabilities, specifically the proofs provided, feel somewhat incomplete. While the authors accurately point out that exhaustive iteration over execution paths is feasible for the discrete examples presented, the applicability to continuous input spaces remains unclear. The original NPI paper demonstrated capabilities with continuous inputs (e.g., the 3D car example), and it’s not immediately obvious how recursion extends the generalization guarantees in these scenarios. Specifically, the argument doesn’t address how the recursive calls are bounded or how the search space for possible execution paths remains tractable in a continuous domain. A more detailed discussion of the theoretical limitations and potential approaches to analyze generalization in continuous spaces would be valuable. Is a formal proof of generalization still considered intractable in the continuous case, and if so, what alternative analyses could be performed to provide further insight?

Finally, the availability of source code would significantly benefit the research community. Reproducibility is crucial, and releasing the implementation would allow others to build upon this work and explore its potential further. A statement regarding the authors' plans for code release would be appreciated.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with a clear presentation and a compelling technical contribution. While the theoretical discussion regarding generalization could be strengthened, the empirical results are convincing and the approach is well-motivated. I believe this work would be a valuable addition to ICLR 2017, pending clarification on the generalization proofs and confirmation regarding code availability.



**Strengths:**

*   Clear and accessible writing.
*   Significant improvement in NPI generalization with a simple extension.
*   Consistent notation with the original NPI paper.
*   Convincing experimental results and ablation studies.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Generalization proofs lack detail, especially regarding continuous input spaces.
*   Lack of a statement regarding source code availability.



**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the theoretical limitations of the generalization arguments in the continuous input space scenario?
*   Do you have plans to release the source code for this work? If so, when?
