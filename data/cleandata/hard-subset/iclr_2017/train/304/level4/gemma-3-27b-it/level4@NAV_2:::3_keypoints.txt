- Paper advances neural program synthesis by showing that adding recursion to neural architectures (NPI) improves generalization and interpretability.  
- Demonstrates superior performance with smaller training datasets on four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Introduces a method to prove perfect generalization of learned programs—a novel and significant theoretical contribution.  
- Reviewer recommends rejection with strong encouragement to resubmit after minor revisions (core idea/expts strong, presentation needs clarity).  
- Strength: elegant solution showing recursion is crucial for learning robust program semantics.  
- Strength: empirical results consistently show recursive NPI outperforms prior approaches, especially on generalization to larger inputs.  
- Strength: inclusion of topological sort and quicksort increases practical relevance and difficulty of evaluation.  
- Strength: successful verification of perfect generalization sets a new evaluation standard for neural program synthesis.  
- Weakness: verification process description lacks sufficient detail and examples.  
- Weakness: construction and completeness justification of the verification set (V) needs clearer explanation.  
- Weakness: scalability of the verification procedure to more complex tasks and larger domains is not addressed.  
- Weakness: reliance on explicit execution traces—alternative training methodologies for discovering recursion are not explored.  
- Suggestion: provide detailed walkthroughs and examples of V construction and justify its completeness for each task.  
- Suggestion: discuss scalability limits of verification and potential optimizations or approximations.  
- Suggestion: explore and discuss alternative training strategies (e.g., curriculum learning, reinforcement learning) to learn recursion without explicit traces.  
- Suggestion: include a concise summary of program sets in the main paper or appendix for readability.  
- Clarifying Q: What heuristics ensured complete coverage of base cases and reduction rules when constructing V for topological sort and quicksort?  
- Clarifying Q: What are the computational costs of verification for larger, more complex programs, and are there optimizations or approximations?  
- Clarifying Q: Have the authors tried training recursive programs without explicit traces, and what were the outcomes?  
- Clarifying Q: Why were these four tasks chosen and how representative are they of broader programming problem classes?
