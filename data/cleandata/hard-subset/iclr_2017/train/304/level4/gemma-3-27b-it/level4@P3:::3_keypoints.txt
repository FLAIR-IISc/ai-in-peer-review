- Paper augments Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) with recursion to enable neural programs that generalize better.
- Authors demonstrate substantial generalization improvements on four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.
- Recursive NPI models achieve 100% test accuracy after training on relatively small datasets, outperforming non-recursive counterparts.
- Recursion is argued to reduce complexity and prevent spurious correlations, yielding more robust and interpretable program representations.
- A rigorous verification procedure is provided to prove perfect generalization given a defined verification set.
- The construction and explanation of the verification set and procedure are detailed and noteworthy.
- Decision: Accept enthusiastically — the provable generalization is a significant theoretical and practical contribution.
- Suggest clarifying how recursion relates to and complements curriculum learning.
- Note limitations of the verification procedure for tasks with very large input domains and recommend expanding that discussion.
- Recommend an ablation study to quantify the contribution of different recursive trace components (e.g., full vs partial recursion).
- Request release of code to improve reproducibility and foster follow-up work.
- Ask for a more complete hyperparameter table to increase experimental reproducibility.
- Questions for authors: potential training methods without explicit execution traces; sensitivity of verification to completeness of V; applicability to more complex tasks; trade-offs between expressiveness and verification complexity (e.g., quicksort).
