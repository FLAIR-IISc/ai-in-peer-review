This is a very interesting and fairly easy to read paper. 
The authors present a small, yet nifty approach to make Neural Programming Interpreters significantly more powerful. By allowing recursion, NPI generalizes better from fewer execution traces.
It's an interesting example of how a small but non-trivial extension can make a machine learning method significantly more practical.
I also appreciate that the same notation was used in this paper and the original Deepmind paper. As a non-expert on this topic, it was easy to read the original paper in tandem. 
My one point of critique is that the generalization proves are a bit vague. For the numerical examples in the paper, you can iterate over all possible execution paths until the next recursive call. However, how would this approach generalize a continuous input space (e.g. the 3D car example in the original paper). It seems that a prove of generalization will still be intractable in the continuous case? 
Are you planning on releasing the source code?

This paper presents a compelling approach to improving generalization in neural program synthesis by incorporating recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework. The authors clearly articulate the problem of poor generalization in existing neural program learning methods and convincingly demonstrate that recursion addresses this limitation across several tasks. The empirical results are strong, showing superior performance and, crucially, the ability to provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization – a significant contribution.

**Decision:** Accept with minor revisions. The work is novel, well-motivated, and supported by solid experimental evidence. The addition of recursion is a valuable contribution to the field of neural program synthesis.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1.  **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper identifies a critical challenge in neural program learning – the lack of generalization – and provides a strong rationale for why recursion is a suitable solution. The connection to the limitations of previous approaches, such as the failure of curriculum learning beyond a certain complexity, is well explained.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors demonstrate improved performance and perfect generalization across four diverse tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. The consistently positive results build confidence in the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3.  **Provable Generalization:** The key strength of this work lies in the ability to provide formal guarantees of generalization. This is a significant advancement, as it addresses a major concern with neural program synthesis – the lack of reliability and interpretability.
4.  **Well-Placed in Literature:** The work builds directly upon the Neural Programmer-Interpreter framework and clearly references relevant prior work in neural program synthesis.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   The discussion of the verification procedure could be expanded. While the core idea is presented, providing more detail on how the base cases and reduction rules are identified and covered would be helpful. Specifically, how does the choice of verification set ensure complete coverage, particularly in cases with potentially infinite state spaces?
*   The paper acknowledges the limitations of the verification procedure for continuous input spaces. It would be beneficial to briefly discuss potential future directions for addressing this limitation, such as exploring alternative verification techniques or focusing on tasks with more discrete input domains.
*   A discussion of the computational cost of training recursive NPIs compared to non-recursive NPIs would be valuable. Does the introduction of recursion significantly increase the training time or resource requirements?
*   The authors mention exploring new training methods beyond execution traces. A brief elaboration on potential avenues for such research would be interesting.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on how the verification set is constructed for each task to ensure complete coverage of base cases and reduction rules?  Specifically, for tasks like topological sort with potentially large graph spaces, how do you ensure the verification set is representative and sufficient to prove generalization?
*   What is the computational overhead of training recursive NPIs compared to their non-recursive counterparts?
*   You mention exploring training methods beyond execution traces. Could you provide some preliminary thoughts on alternative training strategies?
*   In Section 3.2, you mention tail recursion optimization. Was this optimization actually implemented, and if so, did it have a significant impact on performance or memory usage?
