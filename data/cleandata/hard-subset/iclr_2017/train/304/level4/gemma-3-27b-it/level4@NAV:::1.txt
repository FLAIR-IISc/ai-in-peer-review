This paper investigates the importance of recursion for neural programming, positing that it enables stronger generalization and improved sample efficiency. The work builds heavily upon the Neural Programmer Interpreter (NPI) framework from Reed & de Freitas (ICLR 2016), extending it to learn from program execution traces containing recursive calls. The authors demonstrate a verification method relying on evaluating correctness against base cases and reduction rules, and achieve compelling results – perfectly inferring both Bubblesort and Tower of Hanoi.

The strength of this work lies in its simplicity; the core innovation is solely within the training data – the inclusion of recursive traces. However, this success raises questions about the broader implications. Specifically, it’s unclear whether this represents a significant advance in neural programming, or if the problem was fundamentally simplified by the availability of execution traces. For instance, scaling to a more complex input domain like MNIST, and framing a task such as sorting MNIST digits, highlights a potential decoupling of perception and program logic. In this scenario, the task reduces to digit recognition combined with a simple sorting algorithm. Crucially, the paper does not address limitations; what problems with access to execution traces remain beyond the reach of this approach?
