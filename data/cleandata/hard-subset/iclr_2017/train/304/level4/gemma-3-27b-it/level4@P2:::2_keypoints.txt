- Paper proposes augmenting Neural Programming Interpreters (NPIs) with recursion to improve generalization from limited execution traces.
- Incorporating recursion is a simple, practical extension that substantially increases NPI usability and generalization.
- The authors maintain notation consistent with the original DeepMind NPI paper, aiding clarity for non-experts.
- Decision: Accept — addresses a major limitation (poor generalization) with a simple, effective solution and convincing experiments.
- Empirical results show recursive NPIs outperform non-recursive ones on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, especially with scarce training data.
- The work provides provable generalization guarantees (under certain conditions), a notable step toward trustworthy neural program synthesis.
- The paper clearly states the conditions for guarantees (correct base cases and reduction rules) and supplies a verification procedure.
- Limitation: proofs are currently restricted to discrete tasks; discussion of these limits could be expanded.
- Suggestion: expand on challenges and potential approaches for handling continuous input spaces (e.g., discretization or alternative verification).
- Missing analysis: computational cost of recursion during training and inference; a brief runtime/complexity evaluation is recommended.
- Reproducibility: releasing source code would strengthen impact and enable validation by the community.
- Question 1: Please quantify the computational overhead introduced by recursion in training and inference.
- Question 2: How scalable is the verification procedure when programs have larger state spaces, and can base cases/reduction rules be automated or simplified?
- Question 3: What are the proposed directions and anticipated challenges for training recursive neural programs without explicit execution traces?
- Question 4: Are there plans to release the source code for this work?
