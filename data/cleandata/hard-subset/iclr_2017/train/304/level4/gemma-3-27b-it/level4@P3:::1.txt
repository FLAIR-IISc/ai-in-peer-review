## Review of “Making Neural Programming Architectures Generalize via Recursion”

This paper tackles the problem of poor generalization in neural program synthesis, specifically when learning from data. The authors argue that incorporating recursion into neural architectures is key to achieving robust generalization and interpretability, even with limited training data. They demonstrate this by augmenting the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework with recursion on four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, achieving superior results and, crucially, enabling formal verification of correctness.

I recommend **Accept** with enthusiasm. The core idea is elegant and impactful, the empirical results are strong, and the introduction of provable generalization guarantees is a significant contribution to the field. This work addresses a fundamental limitation of existing neural program synthesis approaches and offers a promising path forward.

The paper is well-motivated. The limitations of existing methods – particularly their poor generalization beyond a certain complexity threshold – are clearly articulated. The authors correctly identify that current approaches often learn spurious correlations rather than true program semantics. The choice to build upon the NPI framework is logical, as it provides a strong baseline and a natural structure for incorporating recursion. The connection to recursion as a core concept in programming and its potential for simplifying program complexity is also well-established and serves as a solid foundation for the proposed approach.

The paper provides compelling evidence to support its claims. The empirical results consistently show that recursive NPI models outperform their non-recursive counterparts, achieving perfect accuracy on a variety of tasks, even with small training sets. The most significant contribution, however, is the ability to *prove* generalization by verifying the model's behavior on base cases and reduction rules. This is a groundbreaking result, demonstrating that neural programs can, in principle, be formally verified and guaranteeing their correctness. The authors meticulously detail the verification procedure and provide evidence of its successful application to the tested tasks. The inclusion of results across multiple tasks – including the more complex topological sort and quicksort – strengthens the validity of the findings.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

While the paper is strong, a few areas could benefit from further clarification. 

*   **Scalability and Applicability:** The discussion surrounding the limitations of applying this verification approach to very large state spaces (e.g., MNIST) is important. Expanding on potential strategies for addressing this limitation would be valuable. Could techniques like abstract interpretation or symbolic execution be integrated with the recursive NPI framework to enable verification of more complex programs?
*   **Trace Generation:** The reliance on specifically crafted training traces is a key aspect of this work. A more detailed discussion of the process of creating these traces, and the potential challenges associated with doing so for more complex problems, would be helpful. How sensitive are the results to the quality and coverage of the training traces? 
*   **Comparison to Curriculum Learning:** The paper acknowledges curriculum learning but doesn't fully contrast the benefits of recursion *versus* a well-designed curriculum. A more direct comparison, perhaps with experiments varying curriculum complexity alongside the recursive augmentation, could further highlight the advantages of the proposed approach.
*   **Beyond NPI:** While leveraging NPI is effective, exploring the generality of the recursion concept across *different* neural architectures would strengthen the argument that recursion is a fundamental requirement for robust neural program synthesis.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  You mention the potential for future work to explore training recursive neural programs without explicit execution traces. Could you elaborate on potential strategies for achieving this? For example, would reinforcement learning or generative models be suitable approaches?
2.  The verification procedure relies on carefully constructing the set of base cases and reduction rules. How would this process be automated or made more scalable for more complex tasks where manually identifying these elements is impractical?
3.  How does the choice of LSTM as the core controller impact the performance and verifiability of the recursive NPI model? Would other recurrent architectures or attention mechanisms be equally suitable, or potentially more effective?
4.  In the discussion of topological sort, you mention the need to consider the fact that graphs can have more than one connected component. How does the recursive implementation specifically address this challenge?
