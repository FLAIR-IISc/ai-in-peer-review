- Paper is interesting, easy to read, and uses familiar notation matching the original DeepMind NPI paper.  
- Main contribution: adding recursion to NPI significantly improves generalization from fewer execution traces.  
- Small, non-trivial extension makes the method more practical for neural program synthesis.  
- Empirical results are strong across four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort).  
- Key theoretical claim: ability to provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Clear problem definition and strong motivation linking recursion to generalization limits of prior methods.  
- Work is well-situated in the literature and builds directly on NPI.  
- Critique: generalization proofs are vague, especially unclear for continuous input spaces (e.g., 3D car example).  
- Suggest expanding the verification procedure description, including how base cases and reduction rules are identified and covered.  
- Recommend discussing potential future directions for handling continuous input spaces and alternative verification techniques.  
- Request discussion of computational/training cost overhead introduced by recursion compared to non-recursive NPIs.  
- Interest in exploring training methods beyond execution traces; authors should elaborate potential approaches.  
- Questions for authors: how verification sets are constructed (esp. for large/complex spaces like topological sort), what the computational overhead is, any preliminary alternative training strategies, and whether tail recursion optimization was implemented and its impact.  
- Asks whether the authors plan to release source code.
