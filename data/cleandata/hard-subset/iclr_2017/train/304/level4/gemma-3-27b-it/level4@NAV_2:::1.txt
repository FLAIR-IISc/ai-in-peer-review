This paper argues that incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures is crucial for achieving strong generalization and enabling learning with limited training data. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that augmenting the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework with recursive calls – achieved simply by modifying the training traces – leads to substantial improvements in performance and, critically, allows for formal verification of correctness. The authors showcase this on four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, achieving perfect generalization after training on small datasets and providing provable guarantees through testing base cases and reduction rules.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The work presents a compelling argument for the importance of recursion in neural program learning and offers a novel, practical approach to realizing this benefit. The ability to *prove* generalization, even for relatively complex tasks like quicksort, is a significant accomplishment and a noteworthy contribution to the field.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of existing neural program learning approaches – specifically, their poor generalization beyond a certain complexity threshold. The authors effectively position their work within the relevant literature, building upon the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (Reed & de Freitas, 2016) and highlighting how their recursive approach addresses its shortcomings. The proposed method is elegant in its simplicity, focusing on modifying the training data rather than complex architectural changes. 

The experimental results are convincing. The authors demonstrate superior performance compared to previous approaches and, importantly, provide formal verification of correctness. The detailed explanation of the verification procedure and the reasoning behind the choice of verification sets strengthen the validity of their claims. The analysis of different levels of recursion in bubble sort provides insightful understanding of the impact of this abstraction.

While the paper is strong, several points could further enhance its impact. Firstly, the discussion on the limitations of this approach with larger input domains (e.g., MNIST) is valuable but could be expanded. Exploring how the complexity of the input *representation* affects the applicability of this method would be beneficial. Secondly, it would be helpful to investigate alternative training procedures beyond providing explicit execution traces. Could the model learn to identify and implement recursive abstractions on its own, perhaps through reinforcement learning or curriculum learning? Finally, a more detailed analysis of the learned representations – specifically, how the LSTM’s hidden state captures the recursive structure – could offer further insights.

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors:

1.  The paper mentions that the initial NPI architecture did not consider recursion explicitly. Were there any attempts to encourage recursion in the original NPI training procedure (e.g., through reward shaping or data augmentation) that were unsuccessful? If so, what were the key challenges?
2.  The verification procedure relies on a carefully constructed verification set. How sensitive is the verification process to the completeness of this set? Is there a metric to quantify the confidence level based on the coverage of the verification set?
3.  For the topological sort and quicksort tasks, you state that the number of traces was small (6 and 4, respectively). Was there a point of diminishing returns with respect to the number of training traces? Would increasing the number of traces further improve generalization performance, or did the model quickly saturate its learning capacity?
4.  Regarding the claim that the problem might be “too easy” given the availability of execution traces, could you elaborate on what characteristics of a problem would make it harder to solve, even with full execution traces? Specifically, are there limitations regarding the expressiveness of the program set used, or the complexity of the underlying problem domain?
