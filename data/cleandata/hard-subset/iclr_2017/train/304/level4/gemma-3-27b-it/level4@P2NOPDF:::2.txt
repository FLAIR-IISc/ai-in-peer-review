This paper presents a compelling and readily accessible contribution to the field of Neural Program Interpreters (NPIs). The authors demonstrate a surprisingly effective, yet concise, extension to the original NPI framework – the incorporation of recursion – which notably improves generalization performance, particularly when learning from limited execution data. This work provides a valuable illustration of how a carefully considered, non-trivial addition can significantly enhance the practical applicability of a machine learning technique.

A particular strength of this paper is its consistency with the notation used in the foundational DeepMind paper on NPIs. This consistency greatly facilitated understanding for a reader less familiar with the topic, enabling seamless reference between the two works. I found it particularly helpful to consult the original paper alongside this one.

My primary concern lies with the generality of the provided generalization arguments. While the proofs appear sound for the discrete examples presented – where all possible execution paths leading up to a recursive call can be exhaustively enumerated – their applicability to continuous input spaces is less clear. For instance, the original DeepMind paper featured a 3D car example with continuous state variables. It is not immediately obvious how the proposed approach, and its accompanying generalization analysis, would scale to such scenarios. It appears that establishing formal generalization guarantees for continuous input spaces may remain computationally intractable. Further clarification on this point, or a discussion of the limitations in continuous domains, would be beneficial.

Finally, it would be extremely valuable for the research community if the authors were to release their source code. This would facilitate reproducibility and encourage further exploration of this promising approach.
