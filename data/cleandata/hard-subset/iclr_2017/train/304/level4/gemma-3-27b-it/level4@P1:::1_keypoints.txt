- Paper proposes adding recursion to Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) training traces to improve generalization and learning efficiency.  
- Core contribution: train NPI models on traces that explicitly include recursive calls.  
- Introduces a verification procedure that tests learned programs on a limited set of base cases and reduction rules to guarantee correctness.  
- Empirical result: NPI with recursion can perfectly infer programs for tasks like bubble sort and quicksort on tested domains.  
- Recommendation: Reject — paper promising but incomplete in demonstrating broader impact and limitations.  
- Strength: simplicity — the main change is modifying execution traces, yet yields strong gains.  
- Strength: verification method offers a rigorous route to provable program behavior.  
- Main weakness: lacks critical analysis of assumptions and failure modes where the approach wouldn’t work.  
- Scalability concern: verification may not scale to very large or high-dimensional input domains (e.g., MNIST).  
- Open question: whether availability of execution traces oversimplifies the neural programming problem and limits generality.  
- Needs clearer scope: clarify whether goal is solving neural programming with traces or broader generalization improvements.  
- Needs more detail on verification: how the verification set is constructed and why it guarantees correctness.  
- Suggestion: include ablation studies to isolate effects of different recursive-training components.  
- Suggestion: discuss alternative training methods (e.g., training without explicit traces) and computational costs of verification.  
- Presentation: improve clarity and conciseness in places (e.g., environment encoder descriptions).
