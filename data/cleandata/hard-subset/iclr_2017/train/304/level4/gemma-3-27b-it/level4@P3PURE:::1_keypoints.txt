- Paper argues recursion is important in neural programming, enabling better out-of-domain generalization and efficiency with smaller training sets.  
- Work builds on the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework (Reed & de Freitas, ICLR 2016) that learns from program execution traces.  
- Authors train NPIs using traces that include recursive calls and present a verification method using a limited set of base cases and reduction rules.  
- NPI achieves perfect inference on Bubblesort and Tower of Hanoi under this approach.  
- Main strength is simplicity: the only modification is how training execution traces are composed.  
- Simplicity also raises questions about broader implications and whether this actually resolves neural programming given access to traces.  
- Unclear if selected problems were too constrained, limiting conclusions about general applicability.  
- Example critique: extending inputs to perceptual domains (e.g., MNIST digits) would decouple perception from program logic when execution traces are available, reducing difficulty.  
- Open question: identify problems with available execution traces that remain intractable for the proposed method — what limitations persist?
