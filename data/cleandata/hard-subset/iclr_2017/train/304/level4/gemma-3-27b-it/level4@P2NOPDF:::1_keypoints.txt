- Paper studies the role of recursion in neural programming, claiming recursion improves generalization and data efficiency.  
- Builds on the Neural Programmer Interpreter (NPI) framework and trains NPI using traces that include recursive calls.  
- Main contribution: showing that including recursive function calls in training traces enables learning of recursive algorithms.  
- Empirical result: NPI learns Bubblesort and Tower of Hanoi to perfect accuracy.  
- Evaluation verified using a small set of base cases and reduction rules, demonstrating strong generalization from limited examples.  
- Simplicity is a strength: the only change to NPI is the composition of training data (recursive traces).  
- Major concern: success may be limited to scenarios where full execution traces are available and thus the problem is effectively simplified.  
- Counterexample: tasks like sorting MNIST would decouple perception and algorithmic parts if traces are provided, trivializing the programming challenge.  
- Suggested future work: identify tasks where traces do not simplify the problem, explore traces requiring complex interpretation, and demonstrate method limitations on harder domains.
