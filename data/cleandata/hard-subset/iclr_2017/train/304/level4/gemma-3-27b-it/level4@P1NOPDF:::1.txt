This paper argues that the ability to handle recursion is crucial for neural programming architectures—specifically, that it enables strong generalization to out-of-domain test cases and facilitates learning with smaller training datasets. The work builds heavily upon the Reed & de Freitas paper on Neural Programmer-Interpreters (NPI) from ICLR 2016, which learns from program traces. This paper extends that approach by training NPI models on traces that include recursive calls. The authors demonstrate a method for verifying correctness by evaluating the learned program on a limited set of base cases and reduction rules, and impressively show that the NPI architecture can perfectly infer solutions to both Bubblesort and the Tower of Hanoi problems. 

A key strength of this work is the simplicity of the core idea; as the authors note, the only modification is to the execution traces used during training. However, I'm uncertain about the overall significance of these results. Does this suggest we’ve effectively solved the neural programming problem *when* execution traces are available? (And was the original problem formulation simply too constrained?). For example, consider a more complex input domain like MNIST digits. Imagine a task requiring the NPI to infer a program that sorts MNIST digits from highest to lowest. In this scenario, access to execution traces would effectively decouple the digit recognition problem from the program logic inference—making the task no more difficult than learning to recognize MNIST digits and applying a bubblesort algorithm to symbolic representations. Crucially, what problems, even with access to execution traces, would remain intractable for the proposed method?
