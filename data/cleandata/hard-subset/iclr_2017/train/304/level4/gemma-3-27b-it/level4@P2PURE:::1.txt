This paper demonstrates the importance of recursion for neural programming architectures, arguing that it enables stronger generalization to unseen test cases and facilitates learning with limited training data. Building upon the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework from Reed & de Freitas (ICLR 2016), which learns from program traces, this work trains NPI models using traces that include recursive calls. The authors present a method for verifying correctness by evaluating the learned program on a small set of base cases and reduction rules, and notably achieve perfect inference of both Bubblesort and the Tower of Hanoi problems. 

A key strength of this work is its simplicity; as the authors point out, the only modification is to the training data – the execution traces themselves. However, this raises questions about the broader implications. Does the availability of execution traces effectively resolve the neural programming problem, or were the chosen tasks overly simplistic? For instance, expanding the input domain to MNIST digits – requiring the NPI to sort digits from highest to lowest – highlights a potential decoupling of concerns. In this scenario, execution traces would separate digit recognition from program logic, reducing the problem to digit recognition coupled with learning to bubble sort symbols. This prompts the question of what problems, even with access to execution traces, would prove intractable for the proposed method.
