This paper argues that incorporating recursion is crucial for neural programming architectures – enabling strong generalization to out-of-domain test cases and learning from smaller amounts of training data. The core contribution builds upon the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework from Reed & de Freitas (ICLR 2016), which learns from program traces. The authors train NPI models on traces that explicitly include recursive calls. They demonstrate a method for verifying correctness by evaluating the learned program on a limited set of base cases and reduction rules, and impressively show that the NPI architecture can perfectly infer programs for tasks like bubble sort and quicksort.

**Reject.** While the idea is intuitively appealing and the results are promising on the tested tasks, the paper feels incomplete in demonstrating the broader impact of this approach. The key limitation is that the paper doesn’t fully address the scenarios where this method *wouldn’t* work well, or what the limitations are.

The strength of this work lies in its simplicity; the core change is modifying the execution traces used during training. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate that recursion improves generalization and allows for provable correctness.  The verification procedure is a valuable contribution, offering a rigorous way to guarantee program behavior. However, the discussion lacks a critical examination of the assumptions underlying this success. The authors acknowledge this briefly, mentioning that the verification procedure might not scale to very large input domains (e.g., MNIST), but this concern deserves more in-depth analysis.

The paper raises a valid question: Does the availability of execution traces simplify the neural programming problem to the point where it becomes less challenging? The success in tasks like sorting is impressive, but it's unclear whether this approach would generalize to more complex problems where creating comprehensive execution traces is impractical or impossible. The authors touch upon this with the MNIST example, but further exploration of such scenarios would strengthen the paper.  What types of problems, even with available traces, would remain intractable for this approach? What are the inherent limitations of using recursion in this context?



**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not assessment):**

*   **Clarify the Scope:** The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of the scope of the problem being addressed. Is the goal to solve neural programming with access to traces, or to improve generalization more broadly?
*   **Elaborate on Verification:** The verification procedure is a significant contribution, but the explanation could be more detailed. Specifically, explaining how the verification set is constructed and why it guarantees correctness would be helpful. The description in Section 3.3 is a bit abstract.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** A more thorough discussion of the limitations of this approach is needed. What types of programs are difficult to learn recursively? What are the challenges of scaling this approach to more complex problems?
*   **Ablation Studies:** While the paper shows the effect of adding recursion, it would be beneficial to include ablation studies to isolate the specific contributions of different aspects of the recursive training process. For example, what is the effect of only adding recursive calls to some functions but not others?
*   **Alternative Training Methods:** The authors mention exploring new ways to train recursive neural programs without explicit execution traces. Discussing potential avenues for this research, even briefly, would add value.
*   **Formatting/Clarity:** In a few places, the writing is somewhat dense and could be clarified. For example, the description of the environment encoders could be more concise and easier to follow.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the types of problems where you anticipate this approach would *not* be effective, even with access to execution traces?
2.  Can you provide more details on the construction of the verification set, specifically how you ensure that it covers all necessary base cases and reduction rules?
3.  Have you experimented with different methods for generating the training traces, such as automatically synthesizing them or using a curriculum learning approach?
4.  What are the computational costs associated with verifying the correctness of a learned program, and how does this scale with the complexity of the program?
5.  The paper briefly mentions future work exploring training without explicit traces. Could you provide a bit more detail on potential strategies for achieving this?
