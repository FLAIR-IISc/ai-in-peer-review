1. Summary of what the paper claims to do/contribute
- The paper argues that explicit recursion is key to making neural program synthesis generalize beyond training distributions and to enabling provable guarantees. 
- Concretely, it shows how to train recursive variants of the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) by modifying execution traces to include recursive calls, without changing the core architecture. 
- It applies this to four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort), reporting perfect generalization from small training sets, often far beyond lengths seen in training. 
- It proposes a verification procedure: construct a finite set V of all reachable step-input sequences corresponding to base cases and reduction rules of the recursive specification P; if the learned model M matches P on V, then M is said to provably generalize to all valid inputs.
- It provides task-specific domain encoders, recursive traces, and verification sets; for some tasks, it claims formal coverage of all base cases/reductions and empirical validation that M’s traces match P on the verification sets.

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The main technical contribution is limited to crafting recursive supervision (execution traces) in an architecture (NPI) that already supports recursion; there is no method for discovering recursion, and little architectural novelty.
- The “provable generalization” claim hinges on manual, task-specific construction of V (and, in one case, altered observations for tractability), with no general, sound, or automated procedure ensuring completeness. The guarantees are therefore narrow and not convincingly established beyond the instantiated tasks.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses a clear and important problem—poor out-of-distribution generalization of neural program learners and lack of guarantees. Positioning recursion as the right abstraction is well motivated and well-placed in the literature on algorithm learning.
- Approach: Recursion is enabled by inserting recursive calls into training traces; the LSTM hidden state is reset at each call (as in NPI), effectively bounding the per-call temporal horizon. This is a reasonable mechanism to reduce spurious length dependencies. However, it is primarily a training supervision change, not a new model or learning principle for discovering recursive structure from data.
- Claims vs. support:
  - Empirical generalization: The experiments show strong generalization on the chosen tasks, with ablations (non-recursive, partially recursive) supporting the claim that full recursion helps. Nonetheless, test sets per length are small (30 problems), and the training supervision is heavy (full traces), which limits conclusions about robustness and learnability at scale.
  - Provable guarantees: The verification procedure is task-specific. For addition, the construction of V is analytic; for others, V is indirectly covered through random generation until coverage is believed complete. For bubble sort, the encoder was modified (exposing only comparisons) to make verification tractable, changing the task semantics and limiting the scope of the guarantee. There is no general algorithm to derive a minimal complete V from P, nor a proof that the constructed V is complete for all valid inputs. Thus, the “first provable guarantees for neural programs” claim is overstated; at best, the paper provides finite, task-specific model checking under strong abstraction choices.
- Relation to prior work: The paper correctly contrasts with NPI and related models that lack guarantees and often fail to generalize. However, since NPI already supports call stacks, showing that recursive traces help generalization is incremental. The novelty lies more in the verification framing than in learning or architectural advances.
- Clarity: The paper is generally clear and detailed. The descriptions of environments, encoders, and traces are thorough. The verification section is conceptually clear but lacks formalization and automation details.
- Scope and generality: The approach hinges on (i) an architecture that resets hidden state at call boundaries, (ii) tasks whose recursive structure yields a small V under chosen observations, and (iii) curated traces. It is unclear how it extends to settings with large or continuous observations (the paper acknowledges this) or to other neural program architectures without similar call/stack semantics.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all part of the decision)
- Sharpen and formalize the verification story:
  - Provide a formal definition of V and an algorithm to derive it from P (or sufficient conditions for completeness), with proofs where feasible.
  - Avoid changing the encoder for verification; instead, show guarantees under the same observation model used in training/inference, or clearly delimit the scope of the guarantee.
  - Report the size of V and SV, coverage statistics, and verification logs for each task.
- Strengthen empirical evaluation:
  - Increase test diversity and sizes, and report failure analyses. Include more baselines (e.g., other algorithm-learning models) and ablations (e.g., with/without hidden state reset across calls).
  - Report full hyperparameters, training stability, and code to ensure reproducibility.
- Advance beyond supervised traces:
  - Explore partial supervision or IO-pair training with mechanisms that bias toward recursion discovery, and quantify when the model induces recursive structure.
- Generality:
  - Demonstrate the approach with at least one alternative architecture that supports function calls, or clarify constraints under which the guarantees hold.
  - Analyze scalability: how does |V| grow with task complexity and observation design?

Questions for the authors
- How do you certify that V is complete for topological sort and quicksort? Can you provide a formal argument or procedure that guarantees completeness, rather than empirical coverage via random generation?
- For bubble sort, does the verification guarantee still hold under the original encoder that exposes raw values? If not, what exactly is guaranteed by the modified encoder verification?
- How sensitive is training to the exact recursive traces? Would small deviations or partial traces still lead to the same learned recursive behavior?
- Your guarantees rely on the hidden state reset on subcalls. If the architecture retained hidden state across calls, would the verification approach still be valid?
- What are the recursion depth and stack size limits observed in practice (especially for non-tail recursion in quicksort), and how do they affect generalization?
- Can you quantify the size of V and SV for each task, and provide evidence (e.g., exhaustive state transition coverage) that all reachable step-input sequences are included?
- Do you plan to release code and verification artifacts to allow independent validation?
