1) Summary of contributions (positive and generous)
The paper tackles the persistent generalization failures of neural program learning, arguing that explicit recursion is an essential inductive bias to learn the right program semantics and to reason about out-of-distribution inputs (e.g., longer lengths or larger structures). Concretely, the authors show that (i) standard Neural Programming Architectures (NPAs), instantiated via NPI, already support recursion through a call stack and hidden state reset, (ii) providing recursive training traces enables the network to learn recursive programs for four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort), and (iii) recursion makes verification tractable: by checking a finite set of base cases and reduction rules, the authors claim “provably perfect generalization.” Empirically, the recursive variants exhibit striking generalization (often 100% on very large inputs) from very small training sets, including a nice ablation on “partial vs full” recursion for bubble sort. The paper is well written, provides clear task definitions and encoders, and offers a thoughtful verification narrative tying observed step inputs to correctness.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Limited novelty: NPI already supports recursion; the main technical change is to craft recursive traces. The core conceptual claim (recursion improves generalization) is convincing, but the methodological novelty is incremental.
- Insufficient rigor behind “provably perfect generalization”: beyond addition, the verification sets V are not derived with formal completeness guarantees; they are partially constructed empirically until “coverage,” which weakens the proof claims.

3) Supporting arguments
- On novelty/positioning:
  - The architecture-level support for recursion (stack + hidden-state reset at calls) is inherited from NPI. The key contribution is using recursive supervision to elicit recursive behavior. While valuable, this is a relatively small step relative to the strong claim of enabling generalization “via recursion.”
  - The paper does not compare against alternatives that also control hidden-state propagation (e.g., modular calls without self-recursion, segmentation with resets, or stack-augmented RNNs). This makes it hard to isolate recursion per se from the benefit of limiting temporal credit assignment and context entanglement.

- On the verification/proof story:
  - The formal claim requires that V include all reachable step input sequences. For addition, the construction is analytic; for the other tasks, V is built through reasoning plus empirical generation “until covered.” Without a proof of completeness (or an automated procedure with guarantees), the “provably perfect generalization” claim is not fully substantiated.
  - For bubble sort, the verification uses a modified encoder (exposing only comparisons rather than values) to keep V tractable. This changes the problem and potentially conflates the contribution of recursion with changes in observability.
  - For quicksort, new primitives were introduced to simplify pointer movement and verification. These problem-specific choices reduce the generality of the claim and blur whether gains stem from recursion, from stronger supervision, or from tailored primitives.

- On empirical evaluation:
  - Results are promising but limited to four synthetic algorithmic tasks under heavy supervision (full execution traces, often tailored). Non-recursive baselines are only within the same architecture; no comparisons to other neural programming systems or training regimes (e.g., curriculum with resets, iterative variants with bounded unroll).
  - Reported test sizes are small per length/graph size (n=30), and there is no analysis of robustness (noisy inputs, off-policy errors in traces).
  - The training data are very small (sometimes only a handful of traces), which is impressive, but the tasks and supervision are also highly structured.

4) Additional feedback to help improve the paper (not all part of the acceptance decision)
- Strengthen the verification section:
  - Provide a sound and complete algorithm for constructing V (or give conditions under which the iterative observation-to-observation mapping converges and is complete), including a proof sketch.
  - For topological sort, bubble sort, and quicksort, formalize why certain observation combinations are unreachable; provide invariants and show closure under your step transition mapping.
  - Report verification set sizes and coverage certificates, and release the code to reproduce V and the checks.

- Tighten causal claims about recursion:
  - Add baselines that restrict hidden-state propagation without recursion (e.g., iterative decomposition with stack-like frame resets but no self-calls).
  - Ablate tail-recursion vs. non-tail recursion; show sensitivity to stack depth and to disabling the hidden-state reset at calls.
  - Use consistent encoders across variants to avoid conflating benefits with changes in observables.

- Reduce supervision or move toward discovery:
  - Explore weaker supervision (partial traces, non-recursive traces) and test whether the model can induce recursive structure.
  - Consider training-time regularizers or architectural biases that encourage recursive calls without explicitly supervising them.

- Expand empirical scope:
  - Include more diverse algorithmic tasks and richer data types without tailoring (e.g., larger integer alphabets without encoder simplification).
  - Provide learning curves and sample complexity analyses; report failure modes.

Questions for the authors
- How do you guarantee completeness of V for topological sort, bubble sort, and quicksort? Can you formalize the observation transition system and prove that your construction reaches a fixed point that includes all reachable step input sequences?
- To what extent do your results depend on hidden-state reset at subprogram calls versus recursion itself? Have you tried a non-recursive but modular baseline with resets?
- How sensitive are the results to LSTM size, number of training traces, and optimization hyperparameters? Please provide ablations or at least ranges.
- For bubble sort, why change the encoder for verification (comparison-only) rather than verify the original setting? Can you provide a proof-oriented construction of V for the original encoder?
- For quicksort, do the added primitives materially simplify the task? Could your approach verify a version with more generic primitives?
- Can you release code, trained models, and your V-construction scripts so the community can build upon the verification methodology?

Overall, the paper makes a clear, appealing case that recursion is a powerful inductive bias for neural program learning, and the empirical results are strong. With a more rigorous and general verification pipeline and stronger baselines, this work could have significant impact.
