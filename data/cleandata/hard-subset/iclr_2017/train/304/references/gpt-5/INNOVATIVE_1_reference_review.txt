Summary and contributions
- This paper argues that explicit recursion is a key abstraction for neural program induction, both to improve out-of-distribution generalization and to enable provable guarantees. Concretely, the authors adapt the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) to learn recursive traces (programs calling themselves with a call stack that resets the controller state at each call).
- The central technical contribution is a verification procedure: by characterizing a finite set of base cases and reduction-step input sequences V induced by a recursive program, the authors show that matching the model’s step outputs to a reference implementation on V implies perfect generalization to inputs of unbounded size/complexity for that task.
- They instantiate this idea on four tasks—grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort—showing strong empirical generalization from very small training sets and, for the recursive variants, verification-based “provably perfect generalization.”
- A partial-recursion ablation highlights that making all relevant loops recursive is necessary for perfect generalization in bubble sort.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel and bold idea: treat recursion as an explicit inductive bias for neural programming and couple it with a principled verification procedure to obtain provable guarantees. This is a substantive conceptual step beyond prevailing “longer input” generalization tests.
- Compelling evidence across multiple algorithmic domains (string/array operations, graphs, divide-and-conquer) with extremely small supervision, showing both qualitative and quantitative gains over non-recursive NPI training.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The paper clearly identifies why length-based curricula fail (spurious dependencies in long recurrent traces) and why recursion helps (fixed-size subproblems with hidden-state reset per call). It relates to NPI and broader neural programming literature; to my knowledge, prior work did not explicitly exploit recursion for provable generalization.
- Methodological clarity: While the architecture remains NPI-like, the contribution lies in the training trace design (recursive calls) and the verification technique. The procedure to construct V via observation-to-observation mappings is well articulated, and the authors discuss its tractability and limits.
- Empirical support: Across tasks, recursive training achieves 100% accuracy far beyond training regimes; partial recursion fails where expected; verification sets are small (e.g., one 10-element array suffices for quicksort), and addition’s V is derived analytically. This triangulates the claim that recursion simplifies both learning and reasoning.
- Scope and honesty: The paper is forthright about limitations: reliance on execution traces, domain-specific encoders, and challenges extending verification to tasks with very large or continuous input domains.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Formal status of “proof”: The guarantee hinges on constructing V exactly. For the tasks where V was covered via random generation plus checking, please strengthen the description of how completeness was certified. If possible, provide a mechanically checkable specification-to-V pipeline (or proof obligations/invariants) to reduce human error.
- Broaden applicability: While NPI naturally supports call stacks and hidden-state resets, discuss what is required to port this approach to other neural programming architectures (e.g., DNC/NTM). A small experiment demonstrating recursion + reset vs no-reset would clarify the key ingredient.
- Encoder design: In some variants (e.g., bubble sort for verification), you expose comparison outcomes instead of raw values. Please analyze how much this simplifies the problem and whether the verification result still holds if the encoder provides raw values (or provide a path to derive the same V).
- Reproducibility: Release code, trained models, and verification scripts, including auto-generation of verification sets from specifications where possible.
- Supervision reduction: Since trace supervision is heavy, include a preliminary experiment or analysis showing whether partial traces or non-recursive traces can still lead to recursive program induction (e.g., via learned subroutine discovery).
- Robustness and scaling: Discuss stack depth limits, tail recursion optimization in practice, sensitivity to the return threshold α, and failure modes with longer or adversarial inputs.

Questions for the authors
- Construction and completeness of V: For topological sort and quicksort, how do you certify that the derived V is complete? Beyond empirical coverage, can you provide a formal argument or tool that guarantees no reachable observation transitions are missing?
- Verification costs: What are the sizes of V and the wall-clock time required for verification per task? How does this scale with program branching factor and arity of observations?
- Architectural dependence: Is hidden-state reset at call boundaries strictly necessary for the proof strategy? If the controller state persists (as in many seq2seq settings), can your approach still produce a finite V?
- Trace supervision: Can the model discover recursion when trained on non-recursive traces or only I/O examples? Any evidence (even small-scale) that subroutine boundaries/recursive calls can be inferred?
- Encoder ablations: For each task, how do results change if the encoder reveals raw symbols rather than precomputed booleans (e.g., comparisons)? Does verification remain tractable?
- Quicksort specifics: You simulate recursion with explicit argument stacks in the environment. How critical are these stacks vs the call stack? Could the verification work with genuine recursive calls only?
- Non-recursive infeasibility: Can you quantify the explosion of the state space for non-recursive variants to concretely show why verification is impractical there (e.g., length-dependent step sequences)?

Overall, this paper introduces a timely and original direction—using recursion to turn generalization into a finite verification problem. Despite relying on trace supervision and careful encoders, the conceptual advance and practical demonstrations warrant acceptance.
