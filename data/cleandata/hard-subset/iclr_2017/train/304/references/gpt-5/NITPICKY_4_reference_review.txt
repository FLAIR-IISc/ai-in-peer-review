Summary
- The paper argues that explicitly incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures improves generalization and enables provable guarantees. As a concrete instantiation, the authors modify the training procedure of Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) to supply recursive execution traces for four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort). They claim empirical perfect generalization from small training sets and introduce a verification procedure: if a finite set of base cases and reduction rules is covered by a verification set, then the learned program “provably” generalizes to all valid inputs by matching a reference program P on all reachable step-input sequences V.
- Contributions claimed: (i) first exploration of recursion in neural programs to enforce generalization; (ii) a verification methodology for “provably perfect generalization”; (iii) strong empirical results on four algorithmic tasks.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Overstated claims and insufficient formalism for “provably perfect generalization.” The verification procedure is ad hoc, lacks a formal proof that V is complete/closed under transitions for all valid inputs, and is conditional on exact trace matching and staying on-distribution. This falls short of a rigorous proof.
2) Limited novelty primarily in engineering recursive traces atop an architecture (NPI) that already supports recursion/call stacks; the main idea is supervision design rather than a new model or training principle. 
3) Experimental and presentation issues: missing/partial tables and figures, broken references, inconsistencies across sections, and modified encoders for verification that undermine comparability.

Supporting arguments
- Proof/verification rigor: The “theorem” reduces to checking M(i)=P(i) for i in a constructed set V. There is no formal demonstration that V is complete (i.e., all reachable step-input sequences for all valid inputs) nor that the iterative mapping procedure terminates with the exact set. If the learned model deviates even slightly from P, it may enter states outside V, invalidating guarantees. The paper acknowledges such pitfalls but does not address them. The result is closer to property-based testing than a proof.
- Novelty and positioning: NPI already has call/return and resets hidden state at calls, making recursion natural. The core contribution is crafting recursive traces. The claim of being the “first” to present neural recursion is too broad; recursive computation and call stacks have appeared in several neural program induction frameworks, and recursive neural networks (albeit in different domains) are long-standing. Related work discussion is thin and misses prior models with function calling/stack-like behavior (e.g., variants of stack-RNNs, modular networks).
- Supervision strength: The approach relies on full execution traces—now made recursive—and a reference program P both for training and verification. This is heavy supervision and does not demonstrate learning to discover recursion; the model essentially imitates P with strong guidance. The paper mentions this as future work but the current claims read stronger than what is shown.
- Experimental clarity and consistency:
  - The text claims both non-recursive and recursive topological sort generalize to 120 vertices, yet later reports non-recursive fails with a single-trace training setup. These setups should be clearly separated and labeled; otherwise results appear contradictory.
  - For bubble sort verification, the encoder is changed to expose a comparator rather than values to keep V small. This changes the problem and makes results not comparable to the earlier experiments; the paper mixes results across variants.
  - Reported test sizes (30 per length) are small; many tables/figures are missing in the provided text (e.g., “Length of Array Non-Recursive Partially Recursive Full Recursive” without numbers), making it hard to assess rigor.
- Scalability and practicality: The construction of V and SV is manual/problem-specific and can become intractable; the paper concedes this for perceptual tasks. The claims of “first provable guarantees” should be qualified as limited to small, discrete, fully specified program domains with carefully designed interfaces/encoders.
- Implementation details: The paper suggests tail recursion optimization to avoid deep stacks but does not clarify whether it is implemented. For addition with thousands of digits, naive recursion implies large call depth; how is stack growth handled in practice?
- Presentation/formatting: Numerous LaTeX artifacts, truncated algorithms, footnote placements, and broken references (e.g., “? \Q2015\E”) detract from clarity and polish.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Formalize the verification story: precisely define the semantics, state space, and transition relation for step inputs/outputs; prove that V is the set of all reachable observations from all valid inputs under P; show that the learned M matching P on V implies correctness for all valid inputs. Consider model-checking or abstract interpretation style arguments.
- Strengthen evaluation: provide complete quantitative tables/figures, larger test suites, variance across seeds, and failure analyses. Report stack depth, memory usage, and runtime. Release code and verification generators.
- Clarify experimental setups: cleanly separate regimes (e.g., multiple-trace vs single-trace training). Avoid changing encoders between training/evaluation and verification unless clearly justified and reported as separate tasks.
- Ablations: show the impact of recursion in traces vs non-recursive traces under identical supervision budgets; test partial recursion variants across all tasks; analyze sensitivity to trace errors.
- Related work: expand and temper novelty claims; discuss prior neural architectures with recursion/stack-like mechanisms and prior attempts at formal guarantees in neural program induction.
- Discuss robustness: what happens if the model deviates mid-execution? Can you design verification that is robust (e.g., inductive invariants) rather than enumerating V?
- Clarify tail recursion optimization and stack handling.

Questions for the authors
1) How do you prove that your constructed V is complete for each task? Can you provide a formal proof (not just a procedure) that all reachable step-input sequences from any valid input under P are in V?
2) Do you implement tail recursion optimization in NPI? What is the maximum recursion depth observed for addition with 5000 digits and quicksort on your longest arrays? Any stack limits or failures?
3) Please reconcile the topological sort results: when do non-recursive models “generalize up to 120” vs “fail beyond 8”? What are the exact training setups and data sizes?
4) For bubble sort, how do results differ when keeping the original encoder in verification? Can you verify without changing the problem interface?
5) How sensitive is verification to minor model errors? If M deviates from P for a few steps but returns to P’s states, does the guarantee still hold?
6) Will you release code, traces, and SV generators to reproduce V and the verification procedure?
