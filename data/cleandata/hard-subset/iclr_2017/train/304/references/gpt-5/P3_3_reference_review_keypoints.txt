- Paper claims explicit recursion is key for neural program induction and shows NPI can learn recursive programs from hand-crafted recursive traces to generalize to unbounded input sizes.
- Authors propose a verification procedure: checking the learned model’s step outputs on a finite set V of reachable step-inputs implies provable generalization under the observation interface.
- Empirical results on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort show recursive traces greatly improve generalization versus non-recursive traces; some tasks have verification sets that certify correctness.
- Decision: Reject.
- Main critique: central contribution is the supervision protocol (handcrafted recursive traces and simplified observation interfaces), not learning or inducing recursion from data.
- Main critique: claims of “provably perfect generalization” rely on task-specific encoders and a hand-constructed V, undermining generality and rigor.
- Empirical shortcoming: insufficient baselines and ablations (no comparisons to other neural program architectures, no non-trace supervision, no models that discover recursion).
- Verification not formalized end-to-end: completeness of V is not guaranteed for most tasks and sometimes requires changing the observation interface to make V tractable.
- Related work under-positioned: limited discussion of contemporaneous approaches for learning recursion, differentiable interpreters, neural stacks/queues, and compositional generalization.
- Methodology note: insight that NPI’s call/return and hidden-state reset enable fixed-size subproblems is useful but more of an instructional reframing than a new architecture or objective.
- Verification practices vary: addition’s V is analytic, but others use heuristic/random generation or simplified interfaces, reducing the strength of verification claims.
- Experiments use very strong supervision (full recursive traces); no experiments show learning from I/O pairs or partial traces, nor comparisons to sequence models with curriculum learning.
- Problem interfaces are sometimes modified (e.g., exposing comparator booleans) to make verification feasible, reducing comparability to prior work.
- Evaluation relies on small random test sets per length; “100%” generalization claims are not stress-tested across distributions or implementations.
- Missing ablations: sensitivity to hidden-state reset, recursion depth, tail vs non-tail recursion, noisy traces, and whether controller memorizes traces vs applying rules.
- Suggested improvements: formalize V construction and completeness proofs or automated certified procedures for at least one nontrivial task without interface changes.
- Suggested improvements: reduce supervision — show learning from I/O pairs or partial traces and compare to models with built-in stacks/calls that might discover recursion.
- Suggested improvements: add broader baselines and ablations (NPI without reset, Transformers/LSTMs with curriculum, noisy traces, branching factor effects).
- Suggested improvements: clarify limitations of V, dependence on the observation interface, failure modes, and release code/data and verification toolchain for reproducibility.
- Key questions for authors include how completeness of V is ensured for topo sort/quicksort, sensitivity to removing hidden-state reset, learning from non-recursive traces/I-O pairs, dependence on exposing booleans vs raw values, robustness to observation-interface shifts, and scaling with recursion depth/branching.
