Summary and contributions
- The paper targets the poor out-of-distribution generalization of neural program induction models and argues that explicit recursion is the right abstraction to obtain both improved empirical generalization and verifiable guarantees. 
- Concretely, the authors show that the NPI architecture already supports recursion (via call-stack semantics and LSTM hidden-state reset on calls) and that training on recursive execution traces suffices to induce recursive neural programs.
- They introduce a verification procedure: if one can enumerate base cases and reduction rules (encoded as reachable step-input sequences V for each subprogram), then checking model outputs against a reference program P over a finite “verification set” SV implies “provably perfect generalization.”
- Empirical demonstrations on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort show strong generalization from very small training sets; the recursive variants greatly outperform non-recursive ones. The paper claims verified perfect generalization for all tasks given appropriately constructed SV.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) The “provably perfect generalization” claim rests on ad-hoc, partially heuristic verification sets and task/encoder modifications that undermine formal guarantees; the construction of V is insufficiently formalized and, for multiple tasks, is only partially derived and then covered by random search. 
2) The technical novelty is limited: the core architectural capability (recursion) is already inherent in NPI; the main contribution is to craft recursive supervision and environment encodings. The paper does not demonstrate learning to discover recursion, only to imitate provided recursive traces.

Supporting arguments
- Formal claims: The central theorem requires that V equals the set of all reachable step-input sequences for all valid inputs. For addition, the authors provide an analytical construction; for other tasks, they acknowledge difficulty and fall back to random generation “until complete coverage,” which is not a proof of completeness. For topological sort they say V is analytically derived but then covered by randomized graphs; for bubble sort they alter the encoder to expose comparisons directly to shrink V; for quicksort they revise the primitive set to make verification tractable and claim a single 10-element array suffices. These choices weaken the generality and rigor of the guarantee and blur the line between proving model correctness and constraining the problem until verification is easy.
- Over-claiming: The paper repeatedly states “provably perfect generalization,” yet the verification is empirical against SV and depends critically on the correctness and completeness of V. There is no mechanized proof, no formal bound on missed cases, and no accounting for floating-point or model nondeterminism. The proposal is a testing methodology with hand-constructed coverage criteria, not a proof framework.
- Task and encoder design: Several tasks are simplified by injecting high-level observations (e.g., bubble sort variant encoder returns Q(1,i1) ≤ Q(1,i2)), which substantially reduces the learning burden and affects comparability with prior work. Quicksort uses specialized MOVE_* primitives to make learning and verification easier. These choices confound the claim that the model learned the “true semantics” rather than exploiting engineered supervision and observations.
- Limited novelty relative to NPI: The paper’s main mechanism is to rewrite traces with recursive calls—NPI already supports recursive calling semantics and LSTM reset-on-call. The contribution is thus largely about supervision design; there is no new architecture or training algorithm that induces recursion automatically or encourages compositional inductive biases.
- Incomplete empirical reporting and formatting issues: Critical tables are missing concrete numbers in the provided manuscript (e.g., “Length of Array Non-Recursive Partially Recursive Full Recursive” without entries; similar omissions for topological sort and quicksort tables). Algorithms and equations are truncated/garbled (Algorithm 1, footnotes, line numbers, broken references), and several references are malformed. This significantly impedes assessment and suggests insufficient polish.
- Experimental rigor: 
  - Small evaluation sets (30 instances per length/graph size) and limited range of lengths/graph sizes reported (and not visible in the manuscript) make it hard to assess robustness. 
  - No comparisons to alternative architectures (DNC, NTM, Neural GPU, modern transformers with call stacks), nor ablations on supervision strength.
  - No analysis of runtime/memory/stack depth limits; tail-call optimization is mentioned but not specified/validated.
  - Reproducibility is unclear; the NPI reimplementation details (hyperparameters, training schedule, random seeds) are sparse, and no code release is indicated.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all decisive for my recommendation)
- Formalization: Precisely define valid inputs, the subprogram semantics P, and an algorithm to construct V exhaustively from P (ideally mechanized). Provide proofs that V is complete for each task under stated assumptions. If random generation is used to cover V, give a correctness criterion and termination proof or a coverage certificate.
- Verification: Provide the complete V and SV for each task (or a generator and proof), and release code to reproduce verification. Consider SMT/abstract-interpretation-based checks of the neural controller’s decisions over symbolic input spaces to bridge from testing to proof.
- Generality: Avoid task-specific encoder shortcuts (e.g., exposing ≤) when claiming generalization; show results with raw observations comparable to prior work, and separate “verification-friendly” encoders as a distinct study.
- Baselines and ablations: Compare against strong non-recursive curricula, other neural program architectures, and variants with/without hidden-state reset-on-call. Ablate supervision (full recursive traces vs. partial traces vs. I/O only) to demonstrate the specific contribution of recursion beyond supervision engineering.
- Reporting: Fix missing tables/algorithms, provide full hyperparameters and training details, report sensitivity to α, depth limits, and numerical stability. Quantify sample efficiency improvements and error analysis on failure cases (if any).
- Scope the claims: Recast “provably perfect generalization” to “verified correctness under a finite coverage set derived from base cases and reduction rules” unless a complete formal proof is provided.

Questions for the authors
1) For each task, can you formally specify V (as a set or as a symbolic characterization) and prove that it includes all reachable step-input sequences from any valid input? How is completeness ensured when random generation is used?
2) What are the exact contents of Tables 1–4? Please provide the missing numbers and full experimental ranges.
3) Did you implement tail-call optimization or any stack compression? What are the maximum observed recursion depths (e.g., 5000 for addition), and how do memory/stack limits affect correctness?
4) How sensitive are results to the choice of encoder? Do the recursive models still “verify” when using the original bubble sort encoder (without ≤ exposure)?
5) Can the approach discover recursion from non-recursive traces or I/O-only supervision? If not, what inductive biases would be needed?
6) Are the verification sets and code publicly available, including scripts that certify coverage of V and check trace equality?
