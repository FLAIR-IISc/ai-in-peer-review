Summary and contributions
- The paper argues that explicit recursion is a key abstraction for making neural program executors generalize beyond training regimes that rely on iteration or length-based curricula. Concretely, the authors modify the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) training traces to be recursive (programs can call themselves, with hidden state reset at each call as in NPI), and show this yields strong generalization on algorithmic tasks.
- They also propose a verification procedure that, for recursive programs, reduces generalization to checking correctness on a finite set of base cases and reduction rules. By constructing a set V of all possible step-input observation sequences reachable within one call frame and verifying that the learned model agrees with a reference program P on V, they claim “provably perfect generalization.”
- Empirically, with very small numbers of training traces, the recursive variants achieve 100% generalization on long inputs for addition, bubble sort (with “full” recursion), topological sort, and quicksort, while non-recursive or partially recursive variants degrade beyond modest sizes.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) The main technical contribution is a supervision/training-trace reformulation of an existing architecture (NPI) that already supports recursion, rather than a new model or learning method that induces or discovers recursive structure. The recursion is effectively hard-coded via traces and program sets.
2) The “provably perfect generalization” claim rests on hand-crafted, problem-specific verification sets and environment encodings; completeness of V is not formally guaranteed in general, and for several tasks is obtained heuristically (random generation until “coverage”), undermining the strength of the proof claim.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The idea that recursion reduces problem complexity and yields length-independent execution is well motivated; NPI’s call stack and hidden-state reset make it a natural setting. However, the paper repeatedly frames recursion as a novel architectural contribution, while the architectural capability already exists in NPI; the new ingredient is providing recursive traces and arguments. This is more a training curriculum/data design than a modeling advance.
- Empirical support: Results on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort are compelling within the NPI setting: small amounts of trace supervision, 100% generalization to much larger instances for the fully recursive variants, and clear failures for non-/partially-recursive variants. However:
  - Tasks are classic toy algorithmic benchmarks with full execution-trace supervision. No exploration of robustness to noisy traces, partial supervision, or input-output-only settings.
  - Baselines are limited to non-recursive and partially recursive versions within the same architecture; no comparison to other algorithmic models (e.g., DNC/NTM/Neural GPU) or to stronger NPI training curricula.
  - For bubble sort verification they modify the encoder to expose pairwise comparisons directly, which simplifies verification but deviates from the original environment and may inflate claims of generalization/verification.
  - The number of test instances per length (30) is modest; while acceptable for deterministic algorithms, broader stress tests (adversarial inputs, deeper recursion) would strengthen claims.
- Verification/proofs: The central theorem reduces correctness to matching P on all step-input sequences i in V. This is reasonable and insightful—essentially model checking over a finite abstraction enabled by recursion. However:
  - Constructing V precisely is non-trivial. For addition they analytically derive a large verification set; for others, they “carefully reason” about reachable observations and then use random generation to cover V. There is no formal proof of completeness of V for those tasks, so the guarantee is contingent on the correctness of this construction.
  - The guarantee is to match a particular deterministic reference program P, not necessarily to satisfy the task specification. For example, topological sort has multiple valid orders; the method verifies agreement with one DFS variant, not correctness under the broader spec.
  - The approach depends critically on hand-designed program sets, argument spaces, and observation encoders chosen to make V finite and tractable. This limits generality and obscures how to apply the method to richer domains.

Constructive feedback to improve the paper
- Clarify and temper claims: Reframe “provably perfect generalization” as a task- and encoding-dependent model-checking guarantee contingent on a complete V; distinguish verification against P from spec-level correctness (e.g., non-unique outputs).
- Formalize V: Provide a formal, general procedure (and proof) for constructing a complete V from P for each task, or characterize conditions under which V is finite and computable. Automating this would substantially strengthen the contribution.
- Broaden evaluation: 
  - Include comparisons to other neural algorithm learners and to NPI trained with stronger curricula or more traces.
  - Report stability across seeds, training success rates, and sensitivity to trace noise/partial traces.
  - Quantify sizes of V, verification sets, and runtime of verification; report maximum recursion depth handled.
- Reduce supervision: Explore learning recursive structure from IO pairs or partial/non-recursive traces, even if success is partial; ablate which supervision signals are necessary.
- Avoid environment tweaks for verification unless also evaluated in the original setting; if modified, clearly separate results and discuss trade-offs.
- Release code and verification harness.

Questions for the authors
1) For topological sort and quicksort, how do you establish that V is complete? Can you provide a formal construction or proof that your observed set of step-input sequences covers all reachable cases?
2) What is the exact size of V and of the verification sets for each task? How long does verification take?
3) How robust is training to random initialization and small perturbations in traces? What is the success rate across seeds?
4) For topological sort, since multiple valid orders exist, is your verification ensuring agreement with a fixed DFS variant or correctness w.r.t. any valid order? Can your framework verify a relational spec rather than a fixed P?
5) Can you report results without the modified encoder in bubble sort verification? Does verification still succeed and what is the size of V then?
6) To what extent can the model discover recursive calls if traces are not explicitly recursive, or if only IO supervision is available?
7) What are the limits on recursion depth/stack size in practice, and how does tail recursion optimization manifest in your NPI implementation?
