- Paper claims explicit recursion enables neural program executors to generalize beyond training distributions and admit provable guarantees.  
- Shows NPI already supports recursion via call/return stack and hidden-state reset; recursive execution traces suffice to learn recursive neural programs.  
- Proposes verification: build finite set V of step-input sequences covering base cases and reductions of reference P; matching M on V implies correctness by induction.  
- Applies method to addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, reporting strong generalization and providing verification sets claiming “provably perfect” models.  

- Decision: Weak accept.

Strengths:
- Identifies length/complexity-driven generalization failure and proposes recursion/resetting hidden state as a principled fix.  
- Method is simple and general—no architectural changes, just recursive supervision—applicable to other stack-based NPAs.  
- Verification idea reduces infinite domains to finite checks; construction of V and pitfalls are discussed.  
- Empirical results show dramatic gains for recursive traces, often achieving 100% accuracy on much larger inputs.  

Weaknesses/limitations:
- Supervision strength: model imitates recursive teacher traces rather than discovering recursion autonomously.  
- Verification is conditional on a hand-specified P and complete V; guarantees are about a trained instance relative to P, not a model class.  
- For bubble sort they change the encoder (exposing comparisons) to make V tractable, weakening generality.  
- Tasks are symbolic, hand-engineered benchmarks with limited perceptual variability, limiting applicability to vision/audio or very large base cases.  
- Missing comparisons to other architectures that might learn recursion or modularity, and to program-synthesis baselines.  
- The formal theorem is informal; lacks invariants or automated completeness checks for V and relies on manual reasoning.

Suggestions to improve:
- Clarify novelty: emphasize recursive supervision + verification-by-coverage vs. NPI’s existing capabilities.  
- Provide a more formal or automated algorithm to derive V from P, with soundness/completeness discussion.  
- Broaden baselines: include curriculum NPI, periodic reset variants, DNC/Neural GPU, and ablations of hidden-state reset.  
- Run robustness/ablation studies: sensitivity to trace noise, partial traces, different recursive factorizations, tail vs non-tail recursion.  
- Avoid changing domain encoder for verification; if done, report both and discuss trade-offs.  
- Release code, trained models, reference programs P, and verification sets for reproducibility.

Questions for authors:
- How sensitive is learning to the exact recursive factorization and trace style; do equivalent traces yield the same generalization?  
- What is the quantified role of hidden-state reset at call boundaries; what if hidden state is carried across calls?  
- For quicksort, what maximum call depth occurs at test time and how does it affect memory/compute?  
- How do you ensure completeness of V in practice, especially when derived empirically from runs of P? Any diagnostics for incompleteness?  
- Can partial-supervision settings (only top-level I/O or partial traces) still induce recursive structure?  
- Do guarantees survive small implementation/platform perturbations (e.g., floating-point tolerances)?
