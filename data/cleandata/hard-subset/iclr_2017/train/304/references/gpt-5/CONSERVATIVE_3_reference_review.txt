Summary
The paper argues that explicitly incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures improves generalization and enables provable guarantees. Concretely, the authors modify training traces for the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) so that programs call themselves recursively. They claim two benefits: (i) empirical generalization from very small training sets on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, and (ii) “provably perfect generalization” by verifying correctness on a finite “verification set” that purportedly covers all base cases and reduction rules implied by the recursive decomposition. They outline how to construct such verification sets and report 100% accuracy for the recursive variants, including for larger inputs than seen in training.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The core contribution is incremental—NPI already supports call stacks and the main change is to hand-craft recursive traces. (2) The evidence for “provably perfect generalization” relies on strong supervision, task-specific encoders, and hand-engineered verification sets whose completeness is not convincingly established; baselines are weak and comparisons limited.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and positioning
  - The architectural claim is modest: NPI inherently supports recursion via its call stack and hidden-state reset. The main technical step is providing recursive supervision in the traces. This is a reasonable engineering choice but not a substantial architectural or algorithmic innovation. The paper’s framing (“first to investigate provable generalization of neural programs”) is overstated given that guarantees here are contingent on hand-specified observation sets, reference programs, and finite verification coverage.
  - Related work coverage is incomplete and baselines are narrow. There is no quantitative comparison against other neural program induction systems (e.g., Neural GPU, DNC, Pointer Networks) or recent alternatives trained from IO pairs, only to non-recursive/partially recursive NPI variants.

- “Provable” generalization and verification methodology
  - The guarantee reduces to checking that the learned model reproduces the reference program’s step outputs for all sequences in a set V. For most tasks, V is not derived formally but via ad hoc reasoning plus sampling “until coverage” is achieved. For topological sort and quicksort in particular, the procedure to prove completeness of V is not rigorous or machine-checked; the paper asserts completeness but provides neither formal proofs nor constructive algorithms that can be independently verified.
  - The approach relies on heavy task-specific choices that weaken the generality of the claim. For bubble sort, the encoder is altered for verification to expose a ≤ predicate and pointer-range flags rather than the raw values, which materially changes the problem and can trivialize parts of the reasoning. Using different encoders between training/evaluation variants undermines the comparability of results and the claimed general principle.
  - The “proof” is only as strong as the correctness of the hand-coded reference program and the completeness of V. There is no formal semantics for the network behavior nor any automated verification; it is essentially test-based validation on a finite constructed set. The authors also acknowledge that for tasks with large or continuous input spaces the procedure is infeasible, limiting the scope of the contribution.

- Empirical rigor
  - The experimental setup is under-specified (no code, limited hyperparameters, number of epochs, initialization, or seeds). No variance across runs is reported.
  - Evaluation breadth is limited: only 30 test instances per length/graph size; “up to 5000 digits” or “120 vertices” is reported without systematic stress testing, error bars, or larger-scale sweeps. For quicksort, lengths beyond 11 are tested but not far beyond; recursion depth/generalization is not probed at much larger scales (e.g., length 1k arrays, graphs with 1k nodes).
  - The supervision is very strong: full execution traces with recursive calls. It is unclear whether similar gains could be achieved by non-recursive NPI with improved curricula or alternative training regimes. No ablation disentangles the effects of recursive decomposition from the effect of resetting the hidden state at subcalls (a known NPI behavior).
  - The training data are tiny (e.g., 4 traces for quicksort), amplifying concerns about instability and the need to report multiple seeds.

- Scope and practicality
  - The approach is tailored to discrete, highly structured tasks with hand-designed encoders and scratchpads. The paper asserts generality but concedes the verification story does not extend to richer domains (vision, speech). A clearer boundary of applicability is needed.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide a formal, constructive method for generating V from the reference program, with a proof of completeness, or a certified checker. For topological sort and quicksort, include the exact sizes of V and the verification sets, and a reproducible procedure to confirm coverage.
- Use consistent encoders across training, evaluation, and verification; justify any changes and report their effects. Evaluate bubble sort without swapping the encoder to “≤” for verification.
- Strengthen baselines: compare to established architectures trained on IO pairs and to stronger NPI baselines (e.g., enhanced curricula, alternative supervision). Include symbolic program baselines where appropriate.
- Report comprehensive experimental details, multiple random seeds, and variance. Scale evaluations (e.g., quicksort length 1k, topological sort 1k nodes) to test deep recursion.
- Release code and verification scripts.
- Analyze sensitivity to hidden size and stack depth; quantify failures if recursion depth exceeds training.

Questions for the authors
1. How exactly is V constructed and how do you certify its completeness for topological sort and quicksort? Can you provide a formal algorithm and proof, or a tool that verifies coverage?
2. Are you releasing code and the verification artifacts? Without them, reproducibility and trust in the verification claims are limited.
3. Why change the bubble sort encoder for verification? Do your guarantees hold with the original encoder? What is the size of V in each case?
4. How do results compare to strong IO-trained baselines (Neural GPU, DNC, Transformer) on the same tasks and train/test splits?
5. How far do the recursive models scale in practice (e.g., arrays of length 1k–10k, graphs with 1k nodes)? Any stack depth limitations or failures?
6. To what extent is recursion per se responsible for gains vs. NPI’s hidden-state reset at subcalls? Can you provide ablations or controls?
7. For the addition experiment trained on five 1-digit sums, did traces include carry cases? How is coverage of carry transitions ensured?
