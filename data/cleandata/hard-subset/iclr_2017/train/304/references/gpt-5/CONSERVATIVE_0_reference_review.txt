Summary and contributions
- The paper argues that explicit recursion is a crucial abstraction for neural program induction to generalize beyond training lengths. It instantiates this idea in the NPI framework by modifying supervision traces to include recursive calls and stack-based context resets, and claims that this yields “provably perfect generalization.”
- Empirically, the authors demonstrate strong length generalization on four algorithmic tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort), often outperforming a non-recursive NPI baseline, with very small training sets. They also propose a verification procedure: construct a finite set of step-input sequences V (covering base cases and reduction rules) from a reference implementation P, and check that the learned model M matches P on all elements of V.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The core novelty is limited to modifying training traces to make programs tail-recursive in an existing architecture (NPI). The architectural claim that “NPAs support recursion” is not substantiated beyond NPI.
- The “provable generalization” claim is, in its present form, not rigorous. The verification set V is derived from the reference program’s reachable states, not from the model’s reachable states. This does not prevent M from deviating into states outside V; thus it is not a proof of equivalence of M and P on all valid inputs.
- Empirical evaluation is on toy tasks with very heavy supervision (full recursive traces), limited baselines, and small test samples per configuration. Many critical implementation choices (e.g., modified encoders) weaken the comparability and significance of results.

Supporting arguments
- Scope and baselines: The comparison is limited to variants of NPI (non-recursive, partially recursive, recursive). There is no evaluation against established neural algorithm learning systems (e.g., Neural GPU, DNC/NTM, stack-augmented RNNs, NRAM/HAM), nor against non-neural program-synthesis methods with formal guarantees. This makes it hard to judge the practical advantage of the proposed idea.
- Supervision and practicality: The approach depends on providing fully recursive execution traces. This is stronger supervision than commonly assumed in program induction and diminishes the claim that recursion “enables” generalization; rather, the supervision enforces the abstraction. The paper acknowledges this, but does not quantify how much supervision can be relaxed before performance drops.
- “Provable perfect generalization”: The verification theorem ∀i∈V M(i) ⇓ P(i) is a finite conformance check on a hand-constructed set V. Two core issues remain:
  1) Completeness: V is constructed using P’s transitions. The proof requires that all step-input sequences encountered during M’s execution from any valid input lie in V. This is not established. If M errs on any step, it may enter states outside V.
  2) Circularity/assumptions: Some tasks required changing the encoder (e.g., exposing a comparison bit in bubble sort) to keep V small. This changes the problem and bakes in structure that aids both learning and verification, weakening the claim of generality.
- Experimental rigor:
  - Reported test sizes are small (e.g., 30 arrays/graphs per length), and some statements conflict (e.g., topological sort up to 70 vs. 120 vertices). Tables lack full numbers in the provided text.
  - Addition is known to generalize for NPI without recursion; the benefit here is only verification, not performance. For other tasks, the gap to non-recursive NPI is unsurprising given the supervision.
  - No analysis of sensitivity to recursion depth, stack limits, or tail-call optimization stability. No ablations on encoder design, argument discretization, or hidden state reset policy. No runtime/memory complexity measurements relative to input size.
- Positioning: The paper does not sufficiently engage with literature on verification of neural systems, equivalence checking, or certified program synthesis. The claim of being “first to provide provable guarantees” seems overstated given that the guarantee hinges on conformance over a finite set V and strong modeling assumptions (e.g., exact stack semantics, hidden-state reset).

Suggestions to improve the paper (not necessarily affecting the decision)
- Formalize the guarantee. Provide a proof that (i) the set V is closed under the model’s transition relation induced by M when starting from any valid input; or (ii) establish a bisimulation or inductive invariant that ensures M cannot leave V. Alternatively, adopt a CEGAR-like loop to discover counterexample states reachable under M and prove absence thereof.
- Strengthen baselines and tasks. Compare against Neural GPU, DNC/NTM, and stack-augmented RNNs trained with curricula; include non-neural baselines with formal methods where applicable. Evaluate on more diverse algorithmic tasks and larger, systematically generated test sets. Report statistical confidence.
- Reduce supervision. Explore learning recursion from partial or non-recursive traces, and quantify the tradeoff between supervision strength and generalization/verification success.
- Avoid changing encoders in ways that leak oracle signals (e.g., exposing “≤” directly). If such changes are necessary, separate these settings clearly and justify their relevance.
- Provide architectural generality. Demonstrate the approach on at least one other NPA besides NPI, or provide a clear formal abstraction and recipes to retrofit common architectures with recursive calling and context isolation.
- Reproducibility and clarity. Release code and full verification sets; include complete tables, hyperparameters, and training details. Clean up references and ensure consistency in reported results.

Questions for the authors
1) How do you guarantee that all step-input sequences encountered under M’s execution from any valid input are in V? If M makes an early mistake, could it transition to an unseen observation outside V? How is this prevented or detected?
2) For bubble sort, how do results change when using the original encoder (without exposing the comparison bit) during both training and verification? Please report full tables.
3) How sensitive are results to recursion depth and stack capacity? Do you impose limits, and what happens near those limits?
4) Can you quantify performance when traces are only partially recursive or when only I/O supervision is provided? What fraction of calls must be labeled to recover perfect generalization?
5) Beyond NPI, can your approach be applied to a DNC/NTM-style controller or Neural GPU? What changes are required?
6) How large are the verification sets V for each task, and what is the verification time? Do you have an automated completeness check for V?
7) Could you provide ablations isolating the effect of hidden-state reset at call boundaries versus recursion per se?

Overall, the high-level insight that recursion aids generalization and verification is appealing, but the current evidence and rigor are insufficient to support the strong claims.
