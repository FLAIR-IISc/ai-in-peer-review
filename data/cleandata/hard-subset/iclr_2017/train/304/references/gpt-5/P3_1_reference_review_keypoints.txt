- Paper shows adding explicit recursion via supervised recursive traces to NPI yields strong length generalization and enables post-training verification for some tasks.
- Authors induce recursion by modifying supervision traces so subprograms call themselves and use NPI’s hidden-state reset across calls.
- Empirically demonstrate perfect generalization on four algorithmic tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) often with few training traces.
- Propose verification: define finite set V of reachable step-inputs (base cases/reductions); if model matches reference P on V, execution on any valid input is correct.
- Decision: Reject.
- Main novelty limited: NPI already supports recursion; contribution mainly supplying recursive supervision traces rather than a new model or general learning principle.
- “Provably perfect generalization” claim depends on hand-crafted encoders and manually constructed finite abstraction V, limiting scope and novelty.
- Verification overstated: guarantees rely on complete/correct V and engineered encoders; for several tasks V is not formally derived but constructed via reasoning and random generation.
- Methodological insight is sound: recursion plus call-frame resets confines temporal dependencies and aids modular reasoning; the tail-recursive rewrites explain performance differences.
- Novelty is incremental: the paper emphasizes recursive traces and argues they make generalization/verification tractable, but this is not a major architectural advance.
- Completeness of V is inconsistent: analytically derived for addition but task-specific and not formally guaranteed for topological sort and quicksort.
- Heavy domain engineering: encoders expose abstract booleans/conditions rather than raw observations, enabling tractable V and driving learning; generality without such encoders is unclear.
- Scalability concerns: approach requires finite V and does not apply to tasks with huge/infinite V (e.g., vision, speech).
- Empirical scope limited to synthetic algorithmic tasks with trace supervision and abstraction; baselines are limited and sample sizes per length are small (30).
- Lack of comparisons to alternative generalization strategies or stronger baselines, and absence of adversarial/robustness stress tests.
- Claim that this is the first provable guarantee for neural programs is too strong; better framed as finite-state model checking under a hand-crafted abstraction.
- Suggestion: formalize the verification story with definitions of P, the abstraction function, and proofs/automated checks that V is complete and closed under transitions.
- Suggestion: release code and V sets with scripts to verify coverage and equality of step outputs for auditability.
- Suggestion: run ablations to test sensitivity to encoder design and evaluate with less engineered observations (raw values vs booleans).
- Suggestion: compare to baselines that reset hidden state across loop iterations to isolate recursion vs state-reset effects.
- Suggestion: test robustness to noisy traces, distribution shifts, degenerate inputs, and different quicksort pivot schemes.
- Suggestion: explore learning recursion from weaker supervision (I/O pairs or partial traces) and quantify gap to fully supervised traces.
- Suggestion: connect verification to program logics/model checking and explore automated construction of V from P or abstraction refinement for large/infinite V.
- Question: How do you certify completeness of V for topological sort and quicksort—formal argument or automated enumeration of reachable step-inputs?
- Question: How sensitive are results to domain encoders—does exposing raw values (not booleans) cause V explosion or degrade verification?
- Question: Can non-recursive variants match performance by resetting hidden state at loop boundaries without recursion?
- Question: For quicksort (non-tail recursion), what ensures termination and stack safety in the neural setting; were there failures from mispredicted return probabilities?
- Question: Can you provide adversarial tests (arrays with many duplicates, worst-case pivots; DAGs with high fan-out) and larger-scale stress tests?
- Question: Is there a path to automating V construction from a formal specification of P and handling tasks with large/infinite V via abstraction refinement?
