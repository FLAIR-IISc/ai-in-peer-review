1. Summary of claims and contributions
- The paper argues that explicit recursion is a key abstraction for neural program learning, enabling both improved generalization and tractable verification. The authors show that Neural Programming Architectures (NPAs) such as the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) already support recursion via stack-based subroutine calls with hidden state reset, but prior work did not exploit this.
- They modify training traces to include recursive calls, yielding recursive versions of four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. With very small training sets, recursive models generalize to arbitrarily larger inputs, whereas non-recursive baselines degrade beyond a length/complexity threshold.
- They propose a verification procedure: for a recursive program, generalization can be proven by verifying correctness on all base cases and reduction rules. They formalize this as equality of step outputs between the learned model M and the reference program P over the set V of all reachable step-input sequences within a function call; recursion ensures V is finite. They describe a constructive procedure for V and a corresponding verification set of concrete inputs SV that exercises all elements of V. They report that the learned recursive models produce exact traces matching P on SV, thus providing provable generalization for the four tasks.
- Empirically, recursive models achieve 100% accuracy on large-scale instances (e.g., up to 5000-digit addition; large DAGs for topological sort; longer arrays for sorting), often outperforming non-recursive models trained with curriculum.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (i) Conceptual novelty and clarity of the central idea that explicit recursion induces finite verifiable state spaces within function calls, enabling provable generalization; (ii) Solid empirical evidence across diverse algorithmic tasks with minimal supervision, showing clear generalization gains over non-recursive counterparts.

3. Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement in literature: The paper clearly articulates why non-recursive neural programs often overfit to spurious factors (e.g., input length) and why recursion provides a principled decomposition with base cases and reductions. Positioning relative to NPI and other memory-augmented models is appropriate; the core idea (recursion as an architectural/training abstraction plus verification) is new in this line of work.
- Technical soundness: The use of stack frames with hidden-state reset per call in NPI is correctly identified as enabling recursion. The training modification—providing recursive execution traces—is straightforward and effective. The verification framework is sound in spirit: if V (the set of all reachable step-input sequences within any function call) is complete and finite, and M’s outputs match P’s on V, correctness on all valid inputs follows by induction on the recursion depth. The paper gives a constructive approach to derive V via observation-to-observation mappings from P and demonstrates that for the tasks studied, V is small enough to be covered by a finite SV.
- Empirical rigor: The experiments are compelling across four tasks, with tiny training sets and large generalization windows. The comparison against non-recursive and partially recursive variants (e.g., bubble sort) isolates the effect of full recursion. The claims of perfect generalization for the recursive models are backed by the verification step for each task (with details in the appendix).
- Limitations are acknowledged: The approach needs recursive supervision (explicit traces) and the verification procedure may be infeasible when base cases/reductions induce very large or infinite state spaces (e.g., perceptual tasks).

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily affecting the decision)
- Formalization of V and completeness: The verification section would benefit from a more formal specification of the observation state space per function and a proof sketch of completeness of V (e.g., fixed-point argument akin to value iteration), including sufficient conditions under which V is finite. Currently, parts rely on “careful reasoning” and manual curation.
- Automation: Consider tooling that, given a formal specification of P, automatically constructs V and generates a minimal SV. This would strengthen the claim of “provably perfect generalization” by reducing manual steps.
- Consistency in environments: For bubble sort verification, the encoder was altered to expose comparisons instead of raw values to keep V tractable. Please clarify the implications of verifying a slightly different environment than the one used in the main experiments, and whether verification can be done without changing the encoder.
- Robustness and reproducibility: Report multiple training runs/seeds and variance, since small-data training can be brittle. Release code, trained models, and verification scripts for community use.
- Baselines and ablations: Include additional baselines (e.g., non-recursive with stronger curricula; architectures with explicit loops) and ablations (e.g., hidden-state reset on/off; effect of tail recursion optimization) to more fully attribute gains to recursion.
- Scope of “first provable guarantees”: Calibrate claims relative to contemporaneous work that couples learning with verification/spec checking in program synthesis. The contribution here is distinct (neural program execution with recursion and trace-level verification), and precise positioning will avoid overclaiming.

Questions for the authors
- Construction and completeness of V: For each task, can you provide a precise definition of the observation lattice and a proof (or algorithmic certificate) that your constructed V includes all reachable step-input sequences for each function? How do you detect if V is incomplete?
- Soundness across recursion: You verify “within one function call.” Can you formalize the inductive argument that composition of verified calls preserves correctness across arbitrarily deep recursion (including mutual recursion)? Are there any hidden-state dependencies across calls that could violate the assumption (e.g., side effects outside the encoded environment)?
- Bubble sort verification: Why is the encoder changed for verification, and can you verify the original encoder? If not, what prevents it (state explosion, or an actual mismatch in reachable observations)?
- Quicksort branching: Since quicksort is not tail-recursive and makes two recursive calls, how do you ensure the stacks/arguments cover all interleavings in V? Is the single 10-element array guaranteed to exercise every observation transition in both branches?
- Generality and automation: Do you foresee a way to learn recursion (i.e., discover the recursive structure) without providing recursive traces? Can your verification approach guide learning (e.g., counterexample-guided refinement)?
- Resource bounds: What are the memory/stack depth constraints at inference? Are there formal bounds relating maximum input size to stack usage under your implementation?

Overall, this work advances the field by showing how a classical programming abstraction—recursion—can make neural programs both generalize and be verifiable. The core idea is clear and impactful, the experiments are convincing, and the limitations are well scoped.
