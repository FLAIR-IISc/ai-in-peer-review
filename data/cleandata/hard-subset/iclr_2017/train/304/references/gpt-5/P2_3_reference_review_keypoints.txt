- Proposes recursion as key abstraction to make neural program learners generalize beyond training complexities and shows NPI supports call stacks; supplying recursive execution traces enables learning recursive programs.
- Introduces a verification procedure: identify base cases and reductions to build a finite set V of step-input observations and a verification set SV; matching the reference program P on SV implies claimed perfect generalization.
- Empirical results: recursive traces achieve strong generalization (100%) on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, often with fewer traces than non-recursive training; partial-recursion ablations fail.
- Decision: Reject (borderline).
- Limited novelty: main technical contribution is handcrafted recursive traces and task-specific encoders; NPI already supports recursion, so work is closer to supervised engineering than learning recursion.
- Provable-generalization claim is contingent: it requires manual construction of V and assumes completeness; for most tasks V is not formally proven exhaustive but supported by random coverage and reasoning.
- Motivation and placement are sound: recursion is a natural tool for compositional generalization and related work is appropriately cited.
- Empirically convincing for chosen tasks; partial-recursion ablation is informative about recursion’s importance.
- Verification rigor is weak for complex tasks: only addition has an analytic V; others rely on informal reasoning, random instance coverage, and sometimes modified encoders to aid verification.
- Method depends on a reference program P and exact execution-trace supervision, so it does not address learning recursion from I/O pairs or discovering recursion automatically.
- Unclear robustness to supervision or model noise; small deviations could lead to unverified behavior.
- Experimental scope limited: comparisons confined to NPI variants; no baselines from other neural algorithmic models (DNC, Neural GPU, Transformers).
- Missing analyses: sensitivity to trace noise, training stability, failure modes, and sample efficiency are not reported.
- Reproducibility concerns: no public NPI implementation provided and no statement about code release.
- Paper clarity is good with detailed appendices; role of hidden-state reset at subcalls and its effect on verification is well explained.
- Suggestion: make verification fully rigorous for at least one nontrivial task by specifying P, proving V’s completeness, and proving matching on V prevents exiting V.
- Suggestion: reduce supervision — demonstrate learning recursion from I/O examples or non-recursive/partial traces.
- Suggestion: add broader baselines (DNC, Neural GPU, Transformers) and analyze robustness and sample efficiency.
- Suggestion: ablate NPI design choices (hidden-state reset, stack mechanism, encoder features) to show necessity.
- Suggestion: clarify scalability and practical cost of constructing V and SV and whether verification can be automated.
- Suggestion: release code and verification scripts to aid reproducibility.
- Question: For tasks beyond addition, can you formally prove V is complete for all reachable step-input observations under P?
- Question: How do you guarantee that equality with P on V prevents the model from emitting step outputs outside P’s transition system?
- Question: How sensitive are results to minor errors in supervision traces; can small deviations lead to unverified regions?
- Question: Can the approach learn recursion from I/O examples or non-recursive traces, e.g., via search over call structures?
- Question: How large can V become for more complex recursive programs and what is the practical overhead of constructing and checking SV?
- Question: Will you release your NPI re-implementation and verification tooling?
