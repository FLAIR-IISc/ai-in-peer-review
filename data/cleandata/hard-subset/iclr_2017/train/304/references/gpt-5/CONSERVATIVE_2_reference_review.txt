1) Summary
The paper tackles the longstanding generalization failures of neural program induction models on algorithmic tasks. The core claim is that making recursion an explicit first-class abstraction in neural programming architectures leads to (i) dramatically improved extrapolation to larger/longer inputs and (ii) a pathway to “provably perfect generalization” by verifying correctness on a finite set of base cases and reductions. Concretely, the authors instantiate this in NPI by modifying execution traces to be recursive, and they provide a verification procedure that reasons over step-input sequences. Experiments on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort suggest that recursive traces yield much stronger generalization than non-recursive traces, sometimes perfect, and that a suitably constructed verification set suffices to certify correctness.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The central “provably perfect generalization” claim rests on hand-engineered encoders and verification sets whose completeness is not formally established for the harder tasks; the argument amounts to exhaustive testing over a finite, engineered state space rather than a rigorous proof. (2) The technical novelty is modest: NPI already supports recursion via call stacks; the contribution is primarily to train with recursive traces. (3) Evaluation is narrow and lacks strong baselines and ablations; the setup is heavily engineered and supervised, making it unclear how broadly the conclusions transfer.

3) Supporting arguments
- On “provable guarantees.” The paper’s theorem reduces to showing M(i) matches P(i) for all step inputs i in a set V. However:
  - V is derived using a reference implementation plus reasoning and, for several tasks, completed by random generation “until coverage.” There is no formal proof that V is complete for those tasks—i.e., that no step-input sequence can arise outside V at test time. This is especially concerning for topological sort and quicksort where the combinatorics are richer.
  - The bubble sort variant replaces the encoder with a direct comparator to make V tractable. This materially changes the problem and weakens the generality of the claim.
  - The guarantees thus hold only relative to the engineered observation space and the assumed completeness of V. This is much closer to exhaustive test coverage in a finite abstraction than a formal generalization proof.

- Limited novelty and strong supervision. The main mechanism is to feed recursive traces into an architecture (NPI) that already has a call stack and resets hidden state on calls. The model does not discover recursion; it is taught it. The domain-specific encoders and primitives are tailored (e.g., exposing “is stack empty,” next child’s color, direct ≤ comparisons), and the traces precisely implement known algorithms. This makes the result plausible but reduces the conceptual leap.

- Evaluation gaps.
  - Baselines: The paper compares to non-recursive traces within NPI but not to other established algorithm-learning models (e.g., Neural GPU, DNC/NTM, NRAM, or even more recent neural execution models), nor to stronger non-recursive curricula or larger non-recursive training sets. It is therefore unclear whether recursion per se is the key factor versus data/trace sufficiency.
  - Ablations: No analysis of the role of hidden-state reset, tail recursion vs. non-tail recursion (beyond one partial recursion case), sensitivity to supervision strength (partial traces), or robustness to noise/misaligned traces.
  - Reporting: Tests often use only 30 random instances per size, which is small for claiming perfect generalization, and there is no statistical analysis. Details like LSTM sizes, training hyperparameters, and thresholds are only partially specified.
  - Engineering for verification: For bubble sort, changing the encoder to expose comparisons makes verification easy but deviates from the original setting; the paper’s strongest claim relies on such modifications.

- Scope and claims. The tasks are standard synthetic algorithmic benchmarks with small alphabets and heavily instrumented environments. The paper itself acknowledges that for richer input spaces (e.g., images, speech) verification becomes infeasible. The “first provable guarantees” claim should be substantially tempered to “provable within a designed, finite observation abstraction when a complete V can be constructed.”

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Reframe the main claim: emphasize a verification methodology for recursive neural programs under finite, explicit observation abstractions, rather than universal “provably perfect generalization.”
- Provide a formal construction and proof of completeness for V for at least one complex task (e.g., topological sort) without resorting to random coverage. Alternatively, supply an automated model-checking procedure with soundness/completeness guarantees relative to the abstraction.
- Strengthen baselines and ablations:
  - Compare against stronger non-recursive training regimes (more traces, curriculum, different α thresholds), and other architectures.
  - Ablate hidden-state reset on subcalls to show its necessity.
  - Study partial/noisy traces to quantify supervision needs.
- Report comprehensive experimental details: LSTM sizes, training epochs, optimizer hyperparameters, stopping criteria, and variance across multiple runs.
- Release code, traces, and verification sets to enable reproduction and scrutiny.
- Attempt verification with the original bubble sort encoder (no direct comparator), or clearly separate “verification-friendly” settings from standard ones.

Questions for the authors
- How do you establish that V is complete for topological sort and quicksort? Is there a formal argument that no unseen observation sequences can arise at larger sizes?
- What happens if the hidden state is not reset at subcalls? Does the “proof” still go through? Empirically, how does generalization change?
- Can the model learn to introduce recursive calls from non-recursive or partial traces, or is explicit recursive supervision essential?
- How sensitive are results to LSTM size, the return threshold α, and trace noise?
- For bubble sort, can you verify the non-modified encoder setting? If not, what is the gap?
- If you increase non-recursive training data or use a stronger curriculum, how close does it get to the recursive variant on generalization?
