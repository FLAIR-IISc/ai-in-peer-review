Summary
The paper tackles generalization in neural program learning by explicitly incorporating recursion into a Neural Programming Architecture (instantiated with NPI). The main claims are: (1) adding recursion (via recursive execution traces) leads to substantially better out-of-distribution generalization on algorithmic tasks; and (2) recursion enables provable guarantees of perfect generalization by verifying correctness on a finite “verification set” that covers base cases and reduction rules. The authors re-implement NPI, modify training traces to be recursive for four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort), show strong empirical generalization from very small training sets, and provide a verification procedure that argues perfect generalization once all base cases/reductions are covered.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The central “provably perfect generalization” claim is not rigorously supported: the verification procedure depends on hand-crafted observation abstractions, reference-program semantics, and ad hoc coverage guarantees (often via random generation), without a formal completeness argument for most tasks.
- The technical novelty is limited: NPI already supports recursion; the contribution is primarily to provide recursive traces and a verification workflow, under heavy supervision and task-specific encoders, with limited baselines and potential confounds.

Supporting arguments
- On motivation and placement: The motivation—that recursion provides the right inductive bias for algorithmic generalization—is compelling and well articulated. Positioning relative to NPI, NTM/DNC, Neural GPU, etc. is adequate, but the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contribution from prior work beyond altering supervision to make recursion explicit.
- Technical contribution: Recursion is “enabled” by providing recursive traces and relying on NPI’s call stack and hidden-state reset. There is no architectural or training-method innovation to discover recursion or reduce supervision; the work shows that if you supervise recursion, NPI can use it. This is valuable empirically, but incremental.
- Claims and rigor of verification: The paper’s theorem reduces generalization to verifying all base cases and reductions. However:
  - For addition, the authors enumerate a large verification set and provide some reasoning; acceptable.
  - For bubble sort, they change the encoder to expose the comparison result directly to make V manageable. This simplifies the task and compromises comparability to the non-recursive variant; the verification guarantee then applies only to this altered setting.
  - For topological sort and quicksort, V is constructed via “careful reasoning” plus random generation until “coverage,” but the completeness of V is not formally established. The procedure relies on a manually specified observation abstraction and a reference program; any mismatch or incompleteness undermines the claimed guarantee.
  - The notion of “proof” here reduces to testing step outputs against a reference program over a finite set derived from designer-chosen observations. This is closer to conformance testing than a formal proof, and its validity is contingent on the soundness and completeness of the abstraction—largely unproven for most tasks.
- Empirics: Results are promising—100% generalization reported for recursive variants on long inputs, with ablation showing partial recursion is insufficient. However:
  - Baselines are limited to the authors’ own non-recursive and partially recursive traces, with small training sets and small test samples per length (30). No comparisons to other architectures (e.g., Transformers with pointers or DNC) or stronger training curricula.
  - Some evaluations alter the domain-specific encoder (e.g., bubble sort via exposing comparisons), making fairness unclear.
  - The “perfect generalization” claim is not demonstrated empirically across exhaustive inputs (impractical) nor proven generally—only the verification-set-based argument is offered.
- Scope and limitations: The paper acknowledges the verification approach does not scale to tasks with large or continuous observation spaces (e.g., vision), which limits impact. The supervision level (full execution traces) is heavy; learning recursion discovery is left to future work.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Clarify and formalize the verification framework. Provide a precise definition of the observation abstraction, a formal proof (or mechanized procedure) that the computed V is sound and complete with respect to all valid inputs and program semantics, at least for one nontrivial task (e.g., topological sort). Avoid reliance on random generation for coverage guarantees or provide a certifier demonstrating closure under the transition relation.
- Separate the contributions: (a) empirical demonstration that recursive traces improve generalization; (b) a verification methodology. Strengthen each with cleaner experimental controls (no encoder changes between compared variants) and stronger baselines.
- Report ablations: effect of hidden-state resetting on recursion, depth of recursion, tail vs non-tail recursion, size of training traces, and robustness to noise in traces.
- Reduce supervision: explore learning recursive structure from partial traces or I/O only; or show that the method scales to weaker supervision regimes.
- Release code/traces and verification tooling to enable reproducibility and inspection of V.

Questions for the authors
1. For topological sort and quicksort, how do you guarantee completeness of V? Can you provide a formal argument or algorithm that, given P and the observation abstraction, constructs V exactly (not heuristically)?
2. How sensitive are the verification guarantees to the choice of observation abstraction? Could two different abstractions yield different V and different conclusions?
3. In bubble sort, exposing only the comparison result changes the task. Can you verify the original encoder setting without altering the observation space?
4. Did you attempt stronger non-recursive baselines (e.g., with curriculum, more training traces, or different architectures)? How do results change?
5. Can the model learn recursion without explicit recursive traces (e.g., from non-recursive traces or pure I/O)? Any preliminary results?
6. Are the verification sets and code available? If not, please provide them to substantiate the claims.
