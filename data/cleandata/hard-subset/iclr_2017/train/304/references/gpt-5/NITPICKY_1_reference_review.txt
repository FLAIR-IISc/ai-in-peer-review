Summary and contributions
The paper argues that explicitly incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures (NPAs) is key to reliable generalization on algorithmic tasks and enables provable guarantees. Concretely, the authors (i) show how to express recursion within the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) by using recursive execution traces; (ii) present a verification procedure that, given base cases and reduction rules, reduces “perfect generalization” to finite testing over a set V of reachable step-input sequences; and (iii) empirically demonstrate strong generalization on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort), with “provably perfect” guarantees when the verification set covers all base cases and reductions. The paper emphasizes that recursion reduces hidden-state entanglement across long horizons (since NPI resets hidden state on subcalls), thereby improving interpretability and tractability of verification.

Decision: Reject. Key reasons: (1) The main technical contribution is largely in the supervision (handcrafted recursive traces and engineered encoders), not in model design; the novelty over NPI is modest. (2) The “provably perfect generalization” claim rests on a manual, task-specific construction of V and verification sets (sometimes via random generation until “coverage”), with crucial assumptions and changes to encoders that weaken the claim; many methodological details are missing or fragile. (3) The paper has substantial presentation and formatting issues (missing numbers in tables, broken references, incomplete pseudocode), which preclude careful evaluation and reproducibility.

Supporting arguments
- Conceptual novelty and scope:
  - NPI already supports a call stack; enabling recursion via program self-calls and hidden-state resets is natural. The core contribution is modifying training traces to be recursive, plus task-specific encoders and program sets. This is valuable but incremental relative to prior NPI, and the paper overstates novelty (“first to investigate provable generalization”) given that the proof is via finite testing under heavily engineered environments and supervision.
  - The approach depends on full execution-trace supervision; there is no demonstration that recursion is discovered from I/O or partial traces, limiting broader impact.

- Verification methodology:
  - The theorem ∀i∈V, M(i) ⇓ P(i) is “proven” by testing on a finite V that the authors construct via a manual mapping of possible observations. This hinges on (i) a correct, complete characterization of V and (ii) stable model behavior under continuous outputs and thresholds (e.g., α=0.5 return). The paper acknowledges that for several tasks V is obtained by random generation “until coverage,” which is not a proof. Failure modes in constructing V are not quantified.
  - For addition, the “provable” claim holds under restrictive assumptions (equal length, no leading zeros), and the extension is asserted but not shown.
  - For bubble sort, to make verification tractable, the encoder is changed to expose a comparator and bound flags directly. This meaningfully changes the task and reduces state-space complexity; the proof then depends on this engineered observation space. The verification set is a single array claimed to cover all of V, which seems brittle.
  - For quicksort, the encoder exposes boolean comparisons (≤, ==) and stack conditions; again, substantial domain engineering is embedded into the observation space. An earlier version “needed more functions and observations” and was abandoned due to verification complexity, underscoring sensitivity to design choices.

- Empirical evaluation:
  - Tables are incomplete in the submission (e.g., Table 1/2/3/4 headings without values). This prevents verifying claimed accuracies. Claims like “100% accuracy on all valid inputs” are not supported by visible numbers or confidence intervals.
  - Only 30 random test problems per length/size are used; no variance across random seeds or training runs is reported. Success under small samples and high supervision may not reflect robustness.
  - Training sets are tiny and heavily supervised (full traces); no comparison to weaker supervision (I/O pairs) or to curriculum baselines is shown for the same tasks with matched encoders.
  - No ablations quantifying the contributions of recursion vs. other factors (e.g., hidden-state reset, encoder design, tail-recursion vs. non-tail) are provided.

- Missing details and reproducibility:
  - Model sizes, core LSTM dimensions, MLP layer sizes, number of programs per task, loss weighting (return/program/args), training epochs, early stopping, and decoding choices (argmax vs. sampling) are not sufficiently specified.
  - Stack depth limits, tail recursion optimization details, and memory constraints for long inputs (e.g., 5000-digit addition or deep recursion in quicksort) are not discussed.
  - No code or verification scripts are provided.

- Presentation quality:
  - Numerous formatting/reference issues: broken citations with placeholders (e.g., “? \Q2015\E”), incomplete bibliographic entries, footnote markers misplaced, and inconsistent notation.
  - Algorithms and figures are incomplete or malformed (e.g., Algorithm 1’s recursive call syntax, missing “end” statements; Figure/Table content missing).
  - Notation is occasionally undefined or imprecise (e.g., the big-step semantics symbol without formal context, environment tensor shapes varying across sections).

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Provide complete quantitative results in the main text (all tables filled, with standard deviations over multiple seeds), and release code plus the exact verification artifacts.
- Tighten the verification section: formally define the semantics (state, observation, transition) and give a constructive, automated procedure to derive V from P (or a mechanized proof/SMT check). Avoid random generation for coverage claims; if unavoidable, provide coverage metrics and guarantees.
- Clarify the exact decision rule at inference (argmax vs. thresholding), and establish robustness margins (e.g., margins from α) to ensure that the finite test covers numerical variation.
- Report ablations: (i) non-recursive vs. partially vs. fully recursive traces; (ii) encoder with/without comparator exposure; (iii) hidden-state reset ablation; (iv) effect of tail recursion. Include numbers on generalization and verification feasibility.
- Reduce domain engineering in encoders where possible to avoid trivializing the tasks; or clearly separate results where such engineering is used and temper claims accordingly.
- Broaden baselines to include non-NPI architectures equipped with call stacks/recursion and compare under matched supervision.
- Substantially clean up the writing, references, algorithms, and figures; ensure all assumptions (e.g., equal-length addition) are prominently stated with their impact on claims.

Questions for the authors
1. How is V constructed precisely for each task? Can you provide a mechanized derivation or checker that proves completeness of V relative to P’s semantics?
2. For bubble sort and quicksort, what are the exact contents of Tables 1 and 3? Please include full numerical results and multiple runs.
3. How sensitive are the “proofs” to the α threshold and to small perturbations in logits? Do you have margin analyses to certify stability?
4. What are the LSTM/MLP sizes and training details (epochs, losses, beam/greedy decoding)? What is the variance across seeds?
5. What is the maximum recursion/stack depth encountered, and how is stack overflow avoided for large inputs?
6. Can your approach learn recursion from I/O pairs or partial traces? If not, what proportion of supervision can be removed while maintaining generalization?
7. For addition, can you verify without the equal-length/no-leading-zero assumption, and what is the size of V then?
