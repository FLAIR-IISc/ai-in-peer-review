- Paper claims explicit recursion is a key abstraction for neural program learning, improving out-of-distribution generalization and enabling provable guarantees.
- Method: train recursive programs in the NPI framework by modifying execution traces to include recursive calls.
- Tasks: evaluated on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, showing strong generalization from small training sets.
- Main technical contribution: a verification procedure that identifies base cases and reduction rules by constructing V (reachable step-input observations) and a finite verification set S_V.
- Empirical claim: recursive models achieve “provably perfect generalization” for the considered tasks by checking model outputs on S_V.
- Decision: Accept (weak accept).
- Strength: conceptual clarity and simplicity—isolates recursion as a missing abstraction and leverages NPI’s existing call mechanism with minimal changes.
- Strength: strong empirical evidence across diverse tasks, including divide-and-conquer and graph algorithms, with generalization from tiny datasets.
- Strength: principled verification methodology exploiting recursion to make coverage finite and practicable, with small S_V in examples.
- Supporting point: well-motivated placement relative to prior neural programming work and grounded in programming language theory.
- Technical detail: training traces encode recursion and leverage LSTM hidden-state reset per call; progression from non-recursive to fully recursive traces is demonstrated.
- Verification detail: V and S_V formulation makes concrete checks possible; S_V sizes cited (e.g., single length-10 array for quicksort, 73 DAGs for topological sort).
- Empirical result: non-recursive baselines fail beyond modest sizes while recursive traces generalize perfectly across tested scales.
- Concern: limited novelty—NPI already supports a call stack; main novelty is curated recursive traces and the verification procedure.
- Concern: scope of “provable” claims depends on correct/complete V, fidelity of reference program P, and compactness of observation spaces; not general-purpose for high-dimensional observations.
- Concern: supervision burden—requires full execution traces with explicit recursion; need to quantify supervision cost versus trace-free methods.
- Concern: engineering choices (e.g., encoder changes, pointer primitives) affect verifiability and raise generality questions.
- Suggestion: clarify and formalize verification—define valid inputs and V per task, prove completeness, provide algorithms/pseudocode, and report sizes of V and S_V.
- Suggestion: strengthen empirical reporting—add quantitative tables, variance across seeds, failure modes, model/training details, and ablations (hidden-state reset, partial recursion, noisy traces).
- Suggestion: calibrate claims regarding “first to provably generalize” and relate to program synthesis and certified components; release code and traces for reproducibility.
- Questions: how is V constructed and guaranteed complete; can a mechanical procedure be given and can completeness be formally argued for a nontrivial task beyond addition?
- Questions: how robust is verification to architectural changes (different encoders); can the original bubble sort encoder be verified without observation changes?
- Questions: can the approach handle other non-tail-recursive algorithms (e.g., merge sort) or branching recursion to stress coverage construction?
- Questions: can recursive behavior be discovered from non-recursive traces or I/O-only training, or with partial hints?
- Questions: what are practical limits on recursion depth and stack usage; were there stack overflows or hidden-state drift issues?
- Questions/suggestion: could you release a tool that, given P and primitives, constructs V and a minimal S_V automatically?
- Overall: paper makes a clear, timely contribution showing recursion improves generalization and enables task-specific verification, despite overclaiming and reliance on strong supervision.
