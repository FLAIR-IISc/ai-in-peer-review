1) Summary of contributions
The paper tackles the long-standing generalization problem in neural program induction: models trained on short/simple instances often fail on longer/more complex inputs, and there is no way to certify behavior beyond finite tests. The authors argue that explicit recursion is the key abstraction for both improving generalization and enabling provable guarantees. They instantiate this idea in the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) by modifying execution traces to introduce recursive calls, leveraging NPI’s stack and hidden-state reset across calls. They propose a verification procedure: if a learned recursive program matches a reference program on a finite set that covers all base cases and reduction rules (i.e., all reachable step-input sequences), then the model provably generalizes to all valid inputs. On four tasks—grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort—the recursive versions generalize far beyond training distributions and, for three tasks, are “verified” to generalize perfectly. The paper highlights that recursion reduces the effective state space to a tractable set of step-level configurations and provides clear empirical wins over non-recursive traces, often with very small training sets.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The core technical contributions rely on heavy supervision (explicit recursive traces) and a verification procedure that depends on a reference implementation to enumerate all reachable step inputs, limiting both novelty and scalability.
- The claims of “provably perfect generalization” are contingent on manual, task-specific construction of the verification set and even changes to task encoders to make verification tractable, which undermines the generality and practical impact.

3) Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The insight that recursion can improve inductive bias and interpretability is well-motivated and timely. The paper situates itself among NTM/DNC/Neural GPU/NPI and prior curriculum-learning failures. However, the related work could better connect to broader literature on structured inductive biases, neurosymbolic program synthesis, and formal verification of neural systems; the “first to provide provable guarantees” claim is too strong given existing work on certified properties and program-equivalence-style reasoning (albeit often in different settings).
- Methodological substance: The main mechanism is to hand-craft recursive traces for NPI (which already supports a call stack) and to verify equivalence to a known program by enumerating all reachable step input sequences V. This is a compelling perspective, but:
  - Learning is close to imitation of a known recursive reference program rather than discovering recursion autonomously. The supervision burden is high and limits applicability.
  - Constructing V requires the reference implementation and careful reasoning per task. For some tasks the authors altered the encoder (e.g., bubble sort exposing a direct comparison predicate) to avoid a blow-up in V. This weakens the claim of a general, scalable verification recipe.
  - The notion of “provable” generalization is conditional on the accuracy and completeness of V. The paper acknowledges that for richer inputs (e.g., vision, speech), V may be prohibitively large or infinite, leaving the central claim inapplicable to many realistic domains.
- Empirical support: Results clearly demonstrate that recursive traces improve generalization on the chosen algorithmic tasks, including quicksort (non-tail recursion). However:
  - Baselines are limited to non-recursive or partially recursive traces in NPI, without comparisons to other architectures or to regularizers/inductive biases that might mitigate length overfitting (e.g., explicit call stacks but iterative traces, state-reset ablations, stronger curricula).
  - The number of random test instances per length (30) is small; while verification mitigates this for some tasks, the verification itself is task-specific and sometimes relies on a very small set (e.g., a single 10-element array for quicksort).
  - There is no analysis of robustness to noise, threshold sensitivity (α = 0.5), or stack depth/overflow, which are relevant to the claimed guarantees in practice.

Overall, the paper offers a clear and valuable intuition with convincing demonstrations on classic algorithmic tasks, but the reliance on handcrafted supervision and non-general verification hinders its suitability for ICLR.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Clarify the boundary between “proof” and “test generation”: Formalize the conditions under which V is complete and reachable. Can you provide an algorithm (and complexity guarantees) to construct V from a high-level specification P, rather than from hand reasoning?
- Automating recursion discovery: Explore learning to insert recursive calls from partial or non-recursive traces (as suggested in future work), and include preliminary empirical evidence or constraints that make this feasible.
- Stronger baselines and ablations:
  - Compare against NPI variants with state reset across loop iterations (no recursion), explicit symbolic stacks, or regularizers discouraging length-dependent hidden states.
  - Ablate the effect of hidden-state reset across calls: is recursion beneficial beyond the reset?
  - Compare with Neural GPU/DNC or recent neurosymbolic methods on these tasks.
- Generality of verification: Avoid modifying encoders to ease verification, or at least quantify how such changes alter difficulty and realism. Provide metrics on the size of V, time to construct V, and time to verify per task.
- Robustness and stability: Examine sensitivity to α, decoding errors, and slight perturbations to inputs or environments.
- Reproducibility: Release code, data, and verification sets. Include exact architectures, hyperparameters, and training times.

Questions for the authors
- Construction of V: Can you precisely define the procedure and its completeness guarantees? Under what assumptions does it terminate, and what is its worst-case complexity relative to the program size?
- Dependency on the reference program: If one does not have a complete reference implementation P, can V still be constructed reliably? How much partial specification is sufficient?
- Encoder choices: For bubble sort, exposing Q(1,i1) ≤ Q(1,i2) greatly simplifies verification. How do results and verification scale without this shortcut? Can you quantify the blow-up in V?
- Role of hidden-state reset: If you remove the reset across subcalls, does recursion still yield strong generalization? Conversely, if you keep non-recursive traces but reset every loop iteration, how close do you get?
- Quicksort specifics: Verification on a single 10-element array is claimed sufficient to cover V. Can you provide a proof sketch of why that array induces all necessary step input sequences? How sensitive is this to pivot scheme or array distribution?
- Practicality: How does verification scale to larger graphs/arrays (e.g., topo sort with hundreds of nodes, quicksort with thousands of elements)? What are the time/memory costs?
- Beyond discrete algorithmic tasks: Do you see a path to applying this to perceptual inputs where base cases/reductions are not finite? Any intermediate domains (e.g., structured NLP) where V remains finite?

I believe addressing these points—especially automating V, reducing supervision, and strengthening baselines—would substantially improve the paper’s impact.
