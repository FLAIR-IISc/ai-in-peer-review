- Paper claims explicit recursion enables neural program induction to generalize beyond training and provides provable guarantees via recursive NPI and a verification procedure.
- Authors train recursive variants of NPI by modifying execution traces to include self-calls and evaluate on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.
- Empirical results show recursive variants generalize to much larger inputs (often 100% accuracy) and a verification check over finite step configurations is proposed.
- Decision: Reject.

- Major critique 1: Provable generalization claims rely on heavy task-specific engineering (encoders, primitives, fully supervised recursive traces) and incomplete verification for some tasks.
- Major critique 2: Methodological novelty is limited—NPI already supports call stacks; contribution is mainly recursive supervision and conformance testing, not discovery of recursion or broader applicability.

- Motivation: Correctly identifies spurious dependencies and motivates recursion, but related work on differentiable stacks, interpreters, and neural verification is under-discussed.
- Methodology: Main technical step is rewriting traces to be recursive; no mechanism for discovering recursion from weaker supervision.
- Verification approach: Relies on exhaustive testing over a finite set V, but V is not always analytically derived and sometimes obtained by random generation, undermining completeness.
- Verification dependency: Construction of V depends critically on domain-specific encoder and primitives, and encoders were sometimes modified to make verification tractable.
- Hidden-state concern: Assumes observation vector determines next action and that within-call hidden states are captured by V; no formal boundedness of within-call execution is provided.

- Empirical evaluation: Uses few, fully supervised traces with engineered encoders, making tasks akin to deterministic interpreters rather than algorithm discovery.
- Evaluation limitations: Test sizes per length are small, confidence intervals absent, and analysis of depth/stack usage or return-probability failures is missing.
- Baseline fairness: Comparisons may be unfair when encoders differ; key ablations (state reset, tail vs general recursion, trace length impact) are missing.

- Scope: Approach acknowledged not to scale to tasks with large/infinite base case spaces (e.g., perceptual inputs), limiting applicability.
- Claim conditioning: “Provably perfect generalization” is conditional on DSL, encoder, exact traces, and exhaustive coverage of V; where V isn't proven complete, claim should be softened.

- Requested verification improvements: Provide formal completeness proofs for V for at least one nontrivial task or an automated procedure to construct and prove closure of V.
- Reporting improvements: Report exact sizes of V and SV for each task and justify sufficiency or minimality by construction.
- Assumption transparency: Explicitly state assumptions under which step outputs are determined by observations and bound max steps per recursive call.

- Fairness and ablation suggestions: Use same encoder for recursive and non-recursive comparisons, and include ablations (recursion vs iterative with reset, recursion discovery, LSTM depth, hidden-state reset).
- Generality suggestions: Demonstrate approach on other neural programming architectures (e.g., DNC/NTM) and explore partial supervision or learning recursion insertion from non-recursive traces.
- Reproducibility: Release code, training/verification sets, hyperparameters, training curves, and report stack depth and runtime/memory.

- Key questions for authors include proving completeness of V for topological sort/quicksort, effect of original encoder on bubble sort generalization, bounds on within-call steps and LSTM hidden-state influence, frequency and handling of return-probability errors, detection of spurious unreachable behaviors, and stack overflow/tail recursion handling.

- Overall: Idea is important and promising but current work overstates provability and generality, relies on heavy supervision and engineering, and lacks rigorous verification beyond simplest tasks; addressing these would strengthen the contribution.
