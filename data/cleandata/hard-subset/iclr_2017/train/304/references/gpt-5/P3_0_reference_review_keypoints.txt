- Core claim: Explicit recursion in neural program induction yields strong out-of-distribution generalization and enables provable guarantees.
- Contribution: Show NPI naturally supports recursion via call stacks and hidden-state resets at subprogram boundaries.
- Contribution: Train NPIs with recursive execution traces (including tail recursion) to learn addition, bubble sort, DFS-based toposort, and quicksort.
- Contribution: Propose a verification procedure that constructs reachable step-input sets V per subprogram and checks step-wise equivalence on a finite S_V to yield provable generalization.
- Empirical result: Recursive models achieve 100% correctness on large inputs with very small training sets, while non-recursive variants degrade.
- Decision: Accept (weak) — idea is simple, powerful, well executed despite reliance on strong supervision and tailored encoders.
- Motivation: Clear argument that recursion + hidden-state reset reduces spurious length dependencies and long-range hidden-state opacity.
- Methodology: Careful NPI instantiation with supervision-only change; principled verification recognizing V construction as key challenge and offering analytic vs sampling paths.
- Empirical support: Diverse tasks and instructive ablations (e.g., partial vs full recursion in bubble sort); verification backed by task-specific S_V constructions.
- Scope caveats: Verification tractable mainly in symbolic/discrete environments with small observation alphabets; recursion imposed via traces, not discovered.
- Limitation: “Provably perfect” guarantees are conditional on completeness/correctness of V, encoder choice, and deterministic decoding; V construction sometimes relied on manual reasoning and sampling.
- Limitation: Approach depends on strong supervision (provided recursive traces and fixed subprogram set) and does not demonstrate discovering recursion from I/O alone.
- Limitation: Baselines limited to non-recursive NPI; lacks comparisons to other neural algorithm learners or verifiable program-synthesis methods.
- Reproducibility concern: Uses an NPI reimplementation; code and verification artifacts not reported as released; some reference formatting incomplete.
- Suggested improvement: Provide a formal, step-by-step procedure or tool to derive V, report sizes of V and S_V, and detail manual vs sampling steps and stopping criteria.
- Suggested improvement: Report verification robustness to decoding choices, numeric nondeterminism, and tolerances in comparisons.
- Suggested improvement: Add ablations on hidden-state reset, tail recursion vs loops, and partially recursive subroutines to quantify necessary recursive boundaries.
- Suggested improvement: Compare to at least one alternative recursion-capable architecture (e.g., stack-RNN, Neural GPU) on shared tasks.
- Suggested improvement: Discuss scaling and memory (recursion depth, stack limits, tail-call optimization, worst-case inputs) and release code/verification generators.
- Questions for authors: How is V constructed and certified complete for toposort/quicksort, and what are sizes of V and S_V?
- Questions for authors: How sensitive are training and verification to the domain-specific encoder; could verification be tractable with the original bubble-sort encoder exposing values?
- Questions for authors: Does the guarantee hold under stochastic decoding or numeric perturbations; any verification failures across seeds?
- Questions for authors: Any preliminary results where recursion is inferred from I/O pairs or partial traces via biases/regularizers?
- Questions for authors: Have you tried recursive-trace supervision on other NPAs (e.g., DNC, Neural GPU) and does verification transfer?
- Questions for authors: Practical limits on recursion depth in your NPI implementation and handling of stack overflow or tail-call optimization?
