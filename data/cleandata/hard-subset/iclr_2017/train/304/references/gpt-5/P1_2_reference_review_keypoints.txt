- Claim: Explicit recursion enables neural program learning to generalize beyond training regimes and admit provable guarantees.
- Method: Induce recursion in NPI by modifying execution traces to include recursive calls without changing the core architecture.
- Evaluation: Tested on grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort with strong empirical generalization from very small training traces.
- Technical contribution: A verification procedure that builds a finite verification set V from recursion’s base cases and reduction rules to prove generalization.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason 1: Clear, well-motivated insight that enforcing recursion yields better generalization and tractable proofs.
- Key reason 2: Strong empirical evidence across multiple, non-trivial recursive tasks, often achieving perfect generalization.
- Key reason 3: Novel verification methodology for neural program learning leveraging recursion.
- Supporting argument: Resetting hidden state at each recursive call reduces spurious dependencies on sequence length/structure.
- Supporting argument: The modification is simple and leverages NPI’s existing call stack and frame-local hidden states.
- Supporting argument: Verification reduces correctness to checking base cases and reduction rules over a finite set V, constructed analytically or via semantics.
- Supporting argument: Recursive models outperform non-recursive and partially recursive variants, with the architecture unchanged.
- Positioning: Distinct from prior NPI and neural programming work by using explicit recursion and providing provable generalization guarantees.
- Limitation: Requires full execution traces with explicit recursive calls; the method does not discover recursion autonomously.
- Limitation: Domain-specific encoders expose high-level predicates that simplify learning and verification, limiting generality.
- Limitation: Verification completeness for non-addition tasks is argued via careful reasoning and sampling rather than a full formal/automated proof.
- Limitation: Comparisons are primarily to NPI variants; broader neural-programming baselines are not included.
- Suggested improvement: Clarify and formalize construction of V for non-addition tasks and provide a completeness proof sketch or clearly state assumptions.
- Suggested improvement: Report stack depth limits, memory footprint, and whether tail-call optimization is implemented.
- Suggested improvement: Provide ablations on hidden state reset, partial recursion effects, and sensitivity to trace noise/incomplete supervision.
- Suggested improvement: Strengthen empirical comparisons by including other neural-programming baselines under identical encoders.
- Suggested improvement: Discuss generality beyond NPI and how recursion/verification could port to other architectures (DNC/NTM).
- Suggested improvement: Release code and exact verification sets V for reproducibility.
- Question: How is completeness of V for topological sort and quicksort guaranteed—formal criterion or convergence proof for the fixed-point procedure?
- Question: What are practical stack limits during inference; were stack overflows encountered; is tail-call optimization used?
- Question: How sensitive is learning to correctness/completeness of recursive traces—can partially recursive or partially observed traces suffice?
- Question: How much do domain-specific encoders influence difficulty—does raw-value exposure keep verification tractable and models generalize?
- Question: Can you provide runtime/step complexity comparisons between recursive and non-recursive executions on long inputs?
- Question: Is there a path to learning recursion discovery without explicit supervision, and would the verification pipeline still apply?
