Summary
The paper argues that explicitly incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures improves generalization and enables “provably perfect” generalization via verification on a finite set of base cases and reduction rules. Concretely, the authors modify training traces for NPI to induce recursive calls (no new architecture), and evaluate on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. They report 100% accuracy on held-out instances far beyond training ranges and propose a verification procedure that constructs a finite set V of step-input sequences covering base cases and reductions; matching the learned model’s step outputs to a reference program P on V is claimed to imply perfect generalization.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Limited novelty: the core architecture remains unchanged and NPI already supports recursion; the contribution is primarily hand-crafting recursive traces. (2) The “provably perfect generalization” claim relies on hand-constructed (and sometimes randomized) verification sets derived from a reference program P and domain-specific encoders that simplify the state space; this is not a general, automated, or scalable proof method and seems brittle. The empirical evidence is limited to toy tasks with small training sets and small random test suites, without broader or stronger baselines.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and positioning: The paper’s main mechanism—recursion—is already implicitly supported by NPI’s call stack and LSTM reset; the work shows that changing supervision to recursive traces improves generalization. This is interesting but incremental. The claim that this is the “first investigation” of provable generalization for neural programs overlooks adjacent literature in formal verification and certified properties of neural networks; regardless, the proposed verification here is not a general proof framework but a task-specific enumeration.

- Verification claims: The proof concept hinges on constructing V, the set of all possible step input sequences reachable under P for all valid inputs. For several tasks, the paper admits V is obtained by “randomly generating input candidates until they completely cover the base cases and reduction rules.” This cannot provide a provable guarantee of completeness, and thus does not justify “provably perfect generalization.” Even when V is constructed analytically (e.g., addition), the guarantee is with respect to a specific environment abstraction and reference semantics P; it does not translate to general settings or to other encodings.

- Task design and encoders: For bubble sort, the domain-specific encoder is changed to expose only a comparison outcome rather than values, dramatically shrinking the observation space and making verification tractable. This change undermines comparability to prior baselines and arguably simplifies the task to the point that verification is feasible but less meaningful. More broadly, the approach relies heavily on hand-crafted program sets, environment encoders, and traces; the supervision is strong and specialized.

- Experimental rigor: Results are reported on small synthetic tasks with very small training sets and only 30 random test instances per length/size. The non-recursive baselines are restricted to NPI variants; there is no comparison to other established algorithm-learning models (Neural GPU, DNC, NRAM, etc.) under comparable supervision and curricula. No ablations analyze whether gains arise from recursion per se versus other trace differences (e.g., shorter horizons, LSTM resets per call). The partial recursion study is suggestive but limited. Claims of “100% accuracy” on “all inputs we tried” are not a substitute for rigorous stress testing or statistical robustness.

- Generality and scalability: The method hinges on tasks where base cases and reduction rules can be finitely enumerated under a chosen observation model. The paper acknowledges that for realistic domains (e.g., vision, speech), V becomes huge or infinite and the procedure is inadequate. There is no pathway proposed to address this, nor automation for building V from P beyond informal reasoning.

- Reproducibility and clarity: Code is not provided; many details are relegated to the appendix, and fairness of baselines (e.g., identical supervision strength) is not fully established. The construction of V for topological sort/quicksort is not formalized and depends on manual reasoning or random coverage.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Temper the “provable” claims: be precise that guarantees hold only under a given program set, environment abstraction, and a complete V derived from P. Avoid implying general proof of generalization for neural programs broadly.

- Provide stronger baselines and ablations: compare against other neural algorithm learners; include curriculum learning with non-recursive traces; equalize supervision; test sensitivity to noise in traces; quantify the effect of tail recursion and call-stack resets; report performance under longer horizons without recursion.

- Automate verification: formalize the construction of V (or sound over-approximations) from P; provide proof obligations and completeness checks rather than random coverage; discuss complexity and completeness guarantees.

- Use original encoders: report results and verification under the original bubble sort encoder (values exposed), or justify the modified encoder as a principled abstraction; otherwise, clearly separate the simplified verification task from the original.

- Robustness: evaluate over much larger and adversarial test suites; report error rates, variance, and failure cases; analyze stack depth behavior and resource usage.

- Reduce supervision: explore whether recursion can be discovered from IO pairs or partial traces, or via program-structure priors, to strengthen the contribution beyond trace engineering.

Questions for the authors
1. How do you guarantee V is complete for tasks where you “randomly generate” inputs? Without completeness, how can you claim a proof?
2. How would you construct V without access to a reference program P? If P is required, what is the practical value of learning M rather than executing P?
3. For bubble sort, how do results and verification change under the original encoder that exposes values? Can you verify under that setting?
4. Can you disentangle the effect of recursion from shorter effective horizons and LSTM resets across calls (already present in NPI)? Any ablations?
5. How sensitive are results to errors in program or argument selection at inference (given stochastic outputs)? Are the guarantees robust to small prediction noise?
6. Can your approach scale to tasks with richer observation spaces (e.g., strings over large alphabets) without bespoke encoders or hand-crafted V?
7. Will you release code and the verification harness to enable reproducibility and independent assessment?
