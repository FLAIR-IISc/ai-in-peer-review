Summary and contributions
- The paper argues that explicit recursion is a key abstraction for neural program induction to generalize beyond short training instances. Concretely, it shows that the NPI architecture already supports recursion (via a call stack and hidden state reset), and that by supplying recursive execution traces at training time one can learn recursive neural programs that generalize to inputs of unbounded size.
- The authors further propose a verification procedure: if one can construct a finite set V of all step-input configurations reachable within each recursive call (i.e., covering base cases and reduction rules), then checking equivalence of the learned model’s step outputs to a reference program P on V implies “provably perfect generalization.”
- Empirical demonstrations on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort show that recursive traces dramatically improve generalization relative to non-recursive (and partially recursive) traces; in some cases, verification sets are constructed that certify correctness on all valid inputs under the assumed observation interface.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons
1) The central contribution is largely in the supervision/training protocol (handcrafted recursive traces and observation interfaces) rather than in learning recursion or inducing recursive structure from data. The claims of “provably perfect generalization” rely on task-specific, sometimes simplified encoders and a hand-constructed V, calling into question generality and rigor.
2) The empirical evaluation, while suggestive, lacks sufficient baselines and ablations to support strong claims: e.g., comparisons to alternative neural programming architectures, to non-trace supervision, or to architectures that discover hierarchical calls/recursion; the verification procedure is not formalized end-to-end (completeness of V is not guaranteed for most tasks) and sometimes requires modifying the problem interface to make V tractable.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement. The case that current neural program induction models overfit to length and fail to extrapolate is well known. Highlighting recursion as the right abstraction for generalization is sensible and timely. However, related work is not fully positioned: there is limited discussion of contemporaneous efforts on learning recursion/program structure (e.g., differentiable interpreters, neural stacks/queues, modular neural programs, or later works showing systematic generalization via compositionality) and how this proposal differs in principle beyond supplying recursive supervision.
- Methodology. The NPI already supports function calls and resets the controller state on calls. The paper’s technical contribution is to construct recursive traces and show that, with the call/return mechanism, the controller faces only fixed-size subproblems. This is a compelling insight, but it is an instructional re-framing rather than a new architecture or training objective that encourages recursion to be discovered. The authors acknowledge this and list it as future work; nonetheless, it limits novelty.
- Verification. The verification story is the most ambitious claim. In practice:
  - For addition, V is derived analytically, which is convincing.
  - For other tasks, V is assembled by reasoning about reachable observations and then “ensuring” coverage via random instance generation, or by simplifying the observation interface (e.g., bubble sort exposes only a comparator and pointer-bound booleans) to reduce the state space. This undermines generality: verification depends critically on a bespoke, low-entropy observation interface and complete coverage of V, neither of which is guaranteed for realistic settings.
  - The result is test-based verification against a reference implementation P under an abstracted observation model. It is useful, but falls short of a formal proof or scalable, automated method to generate V with correctness guarantees. Claims of “provably perfect generalization” should be qualified accordingly.
- Experiments. The tasks are standard algorithmic benchmarks; the reported improvements with recursive traces are credible. However:
  - Supervision is very strong (full, recursive execution traces). No results show learning recursion from I/O pairs or partial traces, and no comparisons to sequence models (e.g., Transformers) with curriculum learning.
  - In places, the interface is changed to make verification feasible (e.g., exposing A[pj] ≤ A[phi] instead of raw values), blurring comparability to prior results.
  - Evaluation relies on small random test sets per length; the “100%” claims outside the verification setup are not stress-tested across distributions or implementations.
  - Missing ablations: sensitivity to hidden state reset, depth of recursion, tail vs non-tail recursion, noise in traces, and the extent to which the controller memorizes traces versus applying reduction rules.

Constructive feedback to improve the paper
- Substantially strengthen the verification section:
  - Formalize the construction of V and give completeness proofs (or automated procedures with certificates) for at least one nontrivial task beyond addition, without modifying the observation interface.
  - Clearly separate “test-based certification under a fixed observation abstraction” from “proof of generalization,” and temper claims accordingly.
- Reduce reliance on heavy supervision. Include experiments that:
  - Learn from I/O pairs (no traces), or from partial/non-recursive traces, showing the method can induce recursion.
  - Compare to models with built-in differentiable stacks/call abstractions that may discover recursion.
- Provide broader baselines and ablations:
  - NPI without hidden-state reset on calls (to isolate the benefit).
  - Sequence models (Transformer/LSTM) with curriculum and/or algorithmic supervision.
  - Effects of noisy traces, alternate recursive decompositions, and increasing branching factor.
- Clarify limitations. Discuss when V becomes intractable, the dependence on the observation interface, and failure modes (e.g., quicksort with different partition schemes, adversarial inputs, or off-by-one pointer errors).
- Reproducibility. Release code, data, and the verification toolchain; provide exact definitions of P for each task.

Questions for the authors
1) How is completeness of V ensured for topological sort and quicksort? Can you provide a constructive algorithm that, given P and the observation interface, terminates with a verified-complete V, or a certificate of completeness?
2) How sensitive are results to removing hidden-state reset on subprogram calls? Does generalization degrade, and by how much?
3) Can the model learn recursion when trained on non-recursive traces or I/O pairs only? Any preliminary evidence?
4) For bubble sort and quicksort, how much of the success hinges on exposing boolean comparisons rather than raw values? If you expose raw values, can you still construct V feasibly and verify?
5) Do the learned programs remain correct under distribution shifts in the observation interface (e.g., larger value alphabets, different pointer initializations) or alternative but equivalent recursive decompositions?
6) How does performance scale with recursion depth and branching (e.g., mergesort, DFS on larger graphs with varying degree distributions)?
