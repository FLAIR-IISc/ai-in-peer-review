### **Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"**

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper addresses the problem of poor generalization in neural networks trained for program synthesis tasks. The authors observe that existing models, even when trained with curriculum learning, fail to generalize to inputs of complexity beyond what is seen during training. The central hypothesis is that these models fail because they do not learn the true, underlying semantics of the program, instead learning spurious correlations from the training data. The authors propose that explicitly incorporating the concept of recursion is the key to achieving robust generalization. The paper's primary contribution is to demonstrate that by providing a Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) with training data consisting of explicitly recursive execution traces, the model can learn programs that achieve perfect, and even provably perfect, generalization. The method is evaluated on four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. The authors claim this is the first work to provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs, enabled by a verification procedure that checks the model's behavior on a finite set of base cases and reduction rules.

**Decision: Reject**

**Key Reasons for Decision:**

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Idea:** The paper’s central claim is that recursion enables generalization. However, the proposed method does not involve the model *learning* a recursive abstraction. Instead, the model is provided with explicit, step-by-step recursive execution traces and trained to imitate them. The NPI architecture already possesses a stack-based mechanism suitable for recursion; the contribution here is to change the supervision signal. The finding that a model trained to perfectly imitate a recursive algorithm then behaves recursively is not surprising. The work is more a demonstration of imitation learning on recursive programs than an advance in enabling neural networks to discover generalizable algorithmic structures.

2.  **Overstated Claims of "Provable Generalization":** The "proof" of generalization presented is not a formal proof derived from the properties of the learned model. Rather, it is an empirical verification on a hand-crafted, finite set of inputs (the "verification set"). The construction of this set requires a complete, a priori understanding and enumeration of all base cases and recursive reduction steps of the target algorithm. This procedure is highly manual, task-specific, and relies on the existence of a reference implementation. If one already possesses a formal understanding of the program sufficient to construct this verification set, the role and utility of training a neural network to approximate it becomes questionable. The claim of providing a "proof" is misleading; it is a thorough, but ultimately empirical, test suite.

**Supporting Arguments**

The core of my skepticism lies in the framing of the contribution. The paper presents itself as solving the generalization problem in neural programming, but the solution sidesteps the hardest part of the problem: the discovery of algorithmic abstractions. The model is not learning to be recursive; it is learning to follow a recursive script. This significantly diminishes the impact of the work. While demonstrating that NPI can successfully follow these traces is a valuable result, it does not support the broader claim of "making neural programming architectures generalize." A more accurate title would reflect the method of training on recursive traces.

Furthermore, the verification procedure, while interesting, feels circular. It aims to prove that the model `M` has learned the correct program `P`. To do this, one must first use the specification of `P` to construct a comprehensive verification set `V`. The "proof" then consists of running `M` on inputs that generate the sequences in `V` and checking that its outputs match `P`'s. This procedure verifies that the imitation is correct on the carefully selected examples, but the guarantee of generalization comes from the human-constructed completeness of `V`, not from any inherent property of the learned neural network. The paper acknowledges that constructing `V` can be difficult and sometimes relies on random generation until coverage is met, which further weakens the claim of a formal, provable guarantee. For complex programs, this manual verification would be intractable, limiting the applicability of the proposed method to the simple, well-understood algorithms presented.

The experimental results, while showing superior performance for the recursive versions, primarily serve to validate the premise that imitating a correct, general algorithm leads to better generalization than imitating a brittle, length-dependent one. The comparison is between two forms of supervision for the same NPI model, rather than a comparison of the proposed recursive NPI against other established program synthesis architectures. Without such comparisons, it is difficult to assess the broader significance of the results in the context of the field.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   **Reframing the Contribution:** The paper would be stronger if the contribution was framed more precisely around the methodology of using recursive traces for imitation learning and the corresponding challenges and benefits. The broad claims about solving generalization should be toned down.

*   **Ablation Studies:** The argument for recursion relies heavily on the NPI's architecture, specifically the resetting of the LSTM hidden state on each subprogram call. An ablation study where this state is *not* reset would provide strong evidence for the necessity of this architectural feature. This would help disentangle the benefits of the recursive traces from the architectural priors of the NPI model.

*   **Baseline Comparisons:** To situate the work better, comparisons against other neural programming architectures (e.g., DNC, Neural GPU) on the same tasks would be beneficial. Even if these models are not designed for recursion, their failure or success on these generalization benchmarks would provide important context.

*   **Clarity on Verification Set Construction:** The process of creating the verification set `V` is central to the "provability" claim but is described at a high level. More detail, perhaps with a concrete, step-by-step example for a simple case, would be necessary for the reader to understand the complexity and limitations of this procedure. The current description makes it seem more like an art than a systematic method.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  The verification procedure appears to require a complete specification of the target program `P` to construct the verification set `V`. Could you elaborate on the practical advantage of learning a neural network `M` in this setting, as opposed to using the formal specification of `P` directly?

2.  Could you quantify the generalization that occurs between the training set and the verification set? Specifically, what percentage of the state-action pairs (step inputs and outputs) in the verification set are unseen in the training set? This would help clarify whether the model is truly generalizing or mostly interpolating between memorized trace fragments.

3.  How robust is the learning process to the quality of the recursive traces? For example, if trained on traces from a less efficient or slightly incorrect recursive algorithm, how does the model perform? Does it successfully learn the flawed program, or does learning break down entirely?
