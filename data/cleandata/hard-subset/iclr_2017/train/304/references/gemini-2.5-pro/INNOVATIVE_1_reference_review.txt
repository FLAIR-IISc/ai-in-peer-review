### Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

**Summary of Contributions:**

This paper introduces the fundamental computer science concept of recursion as a core principle for training neural program synthesis models. The central thesis is that the endemic problem of poor generalization in neural programmers—their failure to handle inputs of greater complexity than seen in training—stems from their inability to learn the true, underlying algorithmic semantics. The authors argue that by explicitly structuring programs recursively, models can learn these semantics, breaking down complex problems into a finite set of base cases and reduction rules.

To demonstrate this, the paper adapts the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture, showing that by providing it with recursive execution traces, it can learn perfectly generalizable programs for tasks like addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. The empirical results are striking, with recursive models succeeding on complex inputs where non-recursive baselines fail completely, often after training on a very small number of examples.

Most significantly, the paper claims that this recursive structure makes the learned programs amenable to formal verification. By constructing a finite "verification set" that covers all base cases and reduction rules, the authors demonstrate a procedure to prove that the learned neural program will generalize perfectly to all valid inputs of arbitrary complexity. This represents a groundbreaking step towards creating neural systems with provable correctness guarantees.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This is an excellent paper that introduces a bold, novel, and highly significant idea to the field of neural program synthesis. My recommendation for acceptance is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Conceptual Contribution:** The paper's core contribution is not an incremental improvement to an existing architecture, but a fundamental conceptual shift. By importing the principle of recursion, it provides a powerful and elegant explanation for the generalization failures of prior models and offers a compelling solution. This work moves the conversation from simply adding more memory or attention to thinking about the crucial control-flow abstractions needed to learn true algorithms.

2.  **Groundbreaking Work on Verifiability:** The claim of "provably perfect generalization" is profound. While the field has been content with empirical validation, this paper opens the door to formal verification of learned neural programs. The insight that recursion makes verification tractable by reducing an infinite input space to a finite set of conditions is brilliant. This is precisely the kind of high-impact, direction-setting research that should be highlighted.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper's strength lies in its clear, powerful, and well-motivated central idea. While previous work has struggled with generalization, often resorting to curriculum learning with limited success, this paper correctly diagnoses the problem: the models learn superficial correlations (like input length) instead of the underlying algorithm. The proposed solution—recursion—is a perfect fit for this problem, as it is the canonical method in computer science for defining procedures that operate on inputs of arbitrary size.

The empirical support for this idea is strong and convincing. The head-to-head comparisons between recursive and non-recursive NPI models on tasks like bubble sort and quicksort clearly demonstrate the superiority of the recursive approach. The experiments where models are trained on extremely limited data (e.g., a single graph for topological sort) and the recursive version still achieves perfect generalization are particularly compelling evidence that the model is learning the correct algorithmic abstraction.

I am especially enthusiastic about the verification aspect. This is a significant step towards building more reliable and trustworthy AI systems. While the current procedure for constructing the verification set appears to be partially manual and task-specific, I view this not as a fatal flaw but as an acceptable limitation for a foundational paper. The authors have demonstrated the *possibility* of verification, and this conceptual breakthrough is far more important than the perfection of its initial implementation. It lays the groundwork for future research on automating this process. This willingness to tackle a difficult and crucial problem like verification, even with an imperfect initial method, is exactly the kind of bold research I look for.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not central to my decision to accept.

*   **Supervision Level:** The current method relies on providing complete, explicitly recursive execution traces. This is a very strong form of supervision. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion on the path towards reducing this supervision. For instance, could a model learn a recursive policy from non-recursive traces, or perhaps even from input-output pairs alone, if endowed with the right inductive biases (e.g., a built-in program stack)? The authors mention this as future work, but expanding on potential approaches would be valuable.
*   **Formalism of Verification:** The description of the verification procedure is largely intuitive. It could be beneficial to formalize it slightly more, perhaps by drawing an explicit analogy to proof by structural induction, which seems to be the underlying mathematical principle. This would help connect the work more strongly to the formal methods community.
*   **Scope of Applicability:** The paper rightly notes that the verification procedure may be infeasible for tasks with infinite base cases (e.g., involving continuous perception). A brief, speculative discussion on what "recursion" or "verification" might mean in these domains could be inspiring. For example, could it relate to learning equivariant/invariant representations that are provably stable under certain transformations, analogous to how a recursive call handles a transformed subproblem?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide more detail on the complexity and effort involved in manually constructing the verification set `V` for a task like topological sort or quicksort? Is this process more of an art, or did you develop a systematic methodology that could be a precursor to automation?
*   The NPI's architectural feature of resetting the LSTM state upon a subprogram call is highlighted as critical. How do you see the core idea of learning recursive programs being implemented in other popular architectures, such as the Transformer, which lack this specific mechanism? Would an attention mask that enforces locality be a potential analogue?
*   The paper experiments with both tail-recursive (addition) and non-tail-recursive (quicksort) algorithms. Was there a noticeable difference in the ease of training or the amount of data required for the model to learn these different recursive patterns?
