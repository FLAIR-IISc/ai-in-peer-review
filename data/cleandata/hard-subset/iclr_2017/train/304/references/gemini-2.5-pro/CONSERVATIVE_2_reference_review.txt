### Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper addresses the problem of poor generalization in neural networks trained to synthesize programs. The authors argue that existing models, even when trained with curriculum learning, fail to learn the true program semantics and instead learn spurious correlations tied to characteristics of the training data, such as input length. The central proposal is to explicitly incorporate recursion into the training process to overcome this limitation. By providing a Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) model with execution traces that follow a recursive structure—breaking a problem down into base cases and reduction rules—the authors demonstrate that the model can learn to generalize perfectly from a very small number of training examples. The paper presents empirical results on four algorithmic tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort), showing that the recursively-trained models achieve 100% accuracy on inputs of arbitrary complexity, significantly outperforming non-recursive baselines. A key contribution is the claim of "provably perfect generalization," where a finite verification set covering all base cases and reduction rules can be used to formally verify that the learned program will execute correctly on all valid inputs.

**2. Recommendation and Key Reasons**

**Recommendation:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **The contribution is primarily in data engineering, not architectural innovation.** The paper claims to "incorporate recursion into neural architectures," but the NPI architecture is used off-the-shelf and already possesses the stack mechanism necessary for recursion. The novelty lies entirely in hand-crafting highly structured, recursive execution traces for supervision. The model does not *learn* a recursive abstraction but is simply forced to mimic one provided in the training data. This overstates the contribution and frames a supervision technique as a fundamental architectural advance.
2.  **The experimental validation is insufficient and lacks comparison to established methods.** The only baselines are "non-recursive" versions of the same NPI model. The paper fails to compare its approach against any other prominent neural program synthesis models that it cites as suffering from poor generalization (e.g., DNC, Neural GPU). Furthermore, the performance of the non-recursive baseline for bubble sort appears to be significantly worse than what was reported in the original NPI paper, which calls the validity of the comparisons into question.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The core idea of leveraging recursion to promote generalization is intuitive. However, the method by which this is achieved is a significant weakness. The authors do not propose a new model or a new learning algorithm that discovers recursive structure. Instead, they rely on providing the model with complete, step-by-step execution traces of a pre-defined recursive algorithm. This is an extremely strong form of supervision that essentially reduces the learning problem to mimicry. While the results are impressive, they feel less like a success of machine learning and more like a demonstration that if you tell a model exactly how to execute a recursive program, it can do so. The generalization is a direct consequence of the structure of the supervision, not an emergent property learned by the model.

The claim of "provably perfect generalization" is also framed in a way that is potentially misleading. What is presented is not a formal proof in the typical sense but rather an exhaustive verification procedure. This procedure itself relies on a manually-constructed "verification set" that must cover all base cases and reduction rules. The paper is vague on the process of constructing this set, referring to "careful reasoning" and iterating through observation mappings. This process appears to be highly manual, requiring significant domain expertise for each new task, and its scalability to more complex programs is doubtful. The authors concede it is infeasible for tasks with large input domains, which severely limits the applicability of this "provable guarantee" to a very narrow class of algorithmic problems.

The empirical evaluation is the paper's most significant weakness. A central motivation is that prior methods fail to generalize. Yet, none of these prior methods are included as baselines. To be convincing, the authors should have demonstrated that established models like the DNC or Neural GPU fail on these tasks under standard training and that only their recursive-trace approach succeeds. Instead, they compare only against an iterative-trace version of their own NPI implementation. For bubble sort, their non-recursive model fails for arrays of length > 8, whereas the original NPI paper reported successful generalization up to length 20. This discrepancy suggests that their non-recursive baseline may be sub-optimally implemented or trained, thus inflating the apparent benefits of the recursive approach. Without stronger, more representative baselines, the paper's empirical claims are not well-supported.

**4. Additional Feedback**

*   The framing of the contribution should be more precise. The work is about a supervision scheme for teaching existing architectures recursive algorithms, not about a new architecture itself. The title and introduction should reflect this more accurately.
*   The discussion of the verification procedure should be more transparent about the degree of manual effort and domain knowledge required. An example walking through the construction of the verification set `V` for a simple case (e.g., addition) would greatly improve clarity.
*   The paper would be substantially stronger if it explored methods to reduce the reliance on perfect, recursive traces. For instance, could the model learn a recursive policy from iterative traces, or from input-output examples alone? Acknowledging this as future work is good, but demonstrating even a preliminary step in this direction would elevate the contribution significantly.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you clarify how your contribution is architectural? The NPI model already supported the necessary stack-based function calls. It seems your work is about designing a new form of supervision (recursive traces) for this existing architecture. Is that a fair characterization?
2.  Your non-recursive bubble sort baseline appears to underperform relative to the results in the original NPI paper by Reed & de Freitas (2016). Can you explain this performance gap? How can we be confident that the superior performance of your recursive model is not simply due to a weak non-recursive baseline?
3.  Regarding the verification procedure: how practical is the manual construction of the verification set `V` for novel tasks not discussed in the paper? Could you provide a quantitative estimate of the human effort required, for instance, for the topological sort task? Is any part of this process automated?
