

# Review for "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures to achieve "provably perfect generalization." The authors argue that existing neural programming approaches fail to generalize beyond training complexity thresholds due to learning spurious dependencies, and claim that recursion enables neural programs to learn true program semantics. They demonstrate this by modifying training traces to include recursive elements for the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture, showing perfect generalization on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort) with small training sets. The authors claim to provide "provable guarantees of perfect generalization" through verification on finite sets of base cases and reduction rules.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper's claims of "provably perfect generalization" are not supported by the evidence provided, and the verification procedure does not establish the claimed theoretical guarantees. The paper presents a modification to training traces rather than a novel architectural innovation, and the empirical evidence is insufficient to support the strong claims made.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's fundamental flaw lies in conflating verification on a finite set of inputs with proof of generalization to all possible inputs. The authors claim "provably perfect generalization" but their verification procedure only tests on a finite, constructed set of inputs (e.g., 20,181 for addition), not all possible inputs. This is not a proof of generalization to arbitrary-length inputs as claimed, but rather verification on a specific set of inputs. The paper's Section 3.3 states: "we can prove that the learned neural programs achieve perfect generalization after verifying its behavior on the verification input set." This is misleading—verifying on a specific set does not prove generalization to all possible inputs, which is the core claim.

The paper's empirical evidence is also insufficient. For example, on addition, the paper claims the recursive approach "solves all valid inputs with 100% accuracy after training on a very small number of examples," but the verification set for addition contains 20,181 inputs, which is not "very small" in absolute terms. Moreover, the paper does not compare against strong baselines (e.g., the original NPI paper's results) in a statistically rigorous manner. The paper's Table 1 for bubble sort shows the fully recursive version achieves 100% accuracy on arrays of length up to 10, but the paper doesn't provide statistical significance testing or error bars for these results.

The paper's claim that recursion is "the first to investigate the important problem of provable generalization properties of neural programs" is questionable. The authors acknowledge that the NPI architecture naturally supports recursion (Section 2.2), yet they claim to be the first to investigate it. This suggests the paper is more about modifying training procedures than introducing a novel architectural concept, which contradicts the paper's emphasis on "recursion" as a fundamental innovation.

The paper's verification procedure is also not scalable. The authors themselves state: "For tasks with very large input domains, such as ones involving MNIST digits or speech samples, the state space of base cases and reduction rules could be prohibitively large, possibly infinite." This directly undermines their claim of "provably perfect generalization" for arbitrary inputs, as the verification approach becomes infeasible for more complex tasks.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Clarify the distinction between verification on a finite set and proof of generalization to all inputs. The paper's terminology is misleading and needs revision to avoid overclaiming.

2. Provide more rigorous empirical comparisons against established baselines, including statistical significance testing and error analysis. The paper should demonstrate that the recursive approach is consistently better than non-recursive approaches across multiple metrics and datasets.

3. Address why previous neural programming works (e.g., NPI) did not incorporate recursion despite the architecture supporting it. The paper's claim that "the authors of NPI did not consider this aspect explicitly" is insufficient justification for the novelty claim.

4. Expand the verification to include more complex tasks beyond the four examples, or provide a clear theoretical analysis of the scalability limitations of their verification procedure.

5. Address the paper's claim that "recursion is an essential element for neural programs to generalize" with evidence beyond the four specific tasks they tested. The paper should demonstrate that recursion provides a general solution to the generalization problem, not just a solution for specific tasks with particular structures.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims "provably perfect generalization" but only verifies on a finite set of inputs. Can you provide a formal proof showing how verification on a finite set implies generalization to all possible inputs, rather than just the specific inputs tested?

2. For the addition task, the verification set contains 20,181 inputs. How does this compare to the size of the input space (which is infinite for arbitrary-length addition)? What is the theoretical guarantee that these 20,181 inputs cover all possible cases that could arise in addition problems?

3. The paper states that the NPI architecture naturally supports recursion, yet previous work with NPI did not incorporate it. Why did previous NPI work fail to leverage this natural support for recursion, and what specific change in training procedure (beyond just "providing recursive traces") enabled the model to learn recursive programs?

4. For tasks where the verification set becomes prohibitively large (e.g., sorting arrays of size 100+), what alternative verification procedure would you propose that maintains the "provably perfect generalization" claim?
