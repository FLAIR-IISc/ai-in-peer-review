

# Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

The paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures to achieve provably perfect generalization. The authors argue that current neural programming models (like Neural Programmer-Interpreter) fail to generalize beyond a certain complexity threshold because they learn spurious dependencies on training examples rather than the true program semantics. They claim that by modifying training traces to include recursive elements, their approach enables neural programs to learn the correct program semantics, achieve perfect generalization on tasks like addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, and provide provable guarantees of this generalization through verification. The paper claims this is the first time such provable guarantees have been provided for neural programs.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper's claim of "provably perfect generalization" is not supported by sufficient evidence, and the paper contains multiple technical inconsistencies that undermine its central contribution.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's central claim of "provably perfect generalization" is unsupported. The authors describe a verification procedure but fail to provide the actual proofs that would substantiate their claim. As stated in Section 3.3: "we can provide proofs that these learned programs generalize perfectly," yet the paper contains no actual proofs. This is a fundamental contradiction - if the paper claims to provide provable guarantees, it must provide the proofs. The absence of these proofs is a major flaw that cannot be overlooked.

The paper contains multiple technical inconsistencies that undermine confidence in the methodology:

1. The description of the domain-specific encoder for addition has a dimensionality mismatch. The paper states "Q ∈ R4×N×K" but then uses "Q(1, i1), Q(2, i2), Q(3, i3), Q(4, i4)" in the MLP, which would require Q to be R4×N×K, not R4×N×K as stated.

2. The paper states "K is set to 11" for addition to represent digits plus an end token, but then says "the original paper uses K = 10," creating confusion about the actual implementation.

3. The paper claims "the NPI model naturally supports recursion" but provides no evidence that previous NPI work didn't consider this possibility. The authors should clarify whether this was an oversight in prior work or a genuine novelty.

4. The paper states that for addition, "the verification set for addition contains 20,181 input problems," but doesn't explain how they derived this number or why it's sufficient to prove generalization for all possible inputs.

The paper also fails to properly position its work against prior literature. While it claims to be the first to investigate "provable generalization properties of neural programs," it doesn't adequately contrast with other work that might have provided similar guarantees (e.g., using formal methods or symbolic reasoning). The claim of novelty is overstated.

The paper's empirical results are promising but insufficient to support the strong theoretical claims. For instance, when demonstrating generalization, they state "the recursive version can generalize perfectly to long problems constructed from these components (such as the sum '822+233', where '8+2' and '2+3' are in the training set)" but don't provide evidence that the non-recursive version fails on these specific examples, only that it fails on longer inputs.

## 4. Additional Feedback

1. **Formal Proof Requirement**: The paper's central claim of "provably perfect generalization" requires actual proofs. The authors should provide the formal proofs in the main text or in a supplementary material, not just describe a verification procedure.

2. **Clarify Technical Details**: Resolve the dimensionality inconsistencies in the domain-specific encoder descriptions. Provide clear and consistent definitions of all model parameters (e.g., K values).

3. **Position Against Literature**: Better position the work against prior neural programming approaches. Clarify why previous work didn't incorporate recursion (if it wasn't considered) or why it's novel to apply it in this specific way.

4. **Verification Set Justification**: Explain how the verification sets for each task were derived and why they are sufficient to prove generalization. For addition, provide the mathematical derivation of the 20,181 input problems.

5. **Address Limitations**: Acknowledge that their verification procedure would be infeasible for most practical applications (e.g., with large input domains like images), and discuss how their approach might be extended to such cases.

6. **Provide Actual Tables and Figures**: The paper references multiple tables and figures that are missing from the provided text. These are essential for evaluating the claims.

7. **Clarify Training Data Size**: Provide a clear comparison of the training set sizes used (e.g., 200 traces for addition with max length 3) to previous work to substantiate the claim of "very small number of examples."

The paper presents an interesting idea with promising empirical results, but the lack of formal proof for their central claim and the technical inconsistencies undermine the paper's contribution. For a paper claiming "provably perfect generalization," the absence of actual proofs is a fatal flaw. The authors should either provide the formal proofs or significantly temper their claims about provability.
