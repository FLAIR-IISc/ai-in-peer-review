

# Peer Review: "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural programming by addressing a fundamental challenge: the poor generalization of neural programs to inputs beyond a certain complexity threshold. The authors propose that incorporating recursion into neural architectures is key to achieving "provably perfect generalization" - meaning the model will behave correctly for *any* valid input, not just those seen during training.

The key contributions are:
- Identifying that current neural programming approaches fail to generalize because they learn spurious dependencies on training examples (e.g., input length) rather than the true program semantics
- Proposing that recursion is a necessary abstraction for neural programs to achieve perfect generalization
- Demonstrating that the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture naturally supports recursion, but previous work didn't train it to learn recursive programs
- Showing that modifying training traces to include recursive elements enables NPI to learn recursive programs
- Providing a verification procedure to prove perfect generalization by checking only a finite number of base cases and reduction rules
- Demonstrating empirical success across four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort) where recursive neural programs achieve 100% accuracy on inputs of arbitrary size after training on very few examples

The paper is well-motivated, builds on relevant prior work (particularly the NPI architecture), and provides strong empirical evidence for its claims. The verification procedure is particularly novel and useful for establishing generalization guarantees.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper tackles a fundamental and important challenge in neural programming - generalization beyond training complexity.
2. The approach is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature, with clear connections to prior work.
3. The empirical results are strong and demonstrate clear advantages of the recursive approach over non-recursive baselines.
4. The verification procedure provides a novel and useful framework for establishing generalization guarantees in neural programs.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

The paper makes a compelling contribution to neural programming by addressing a critical limitation of current approaches. The hypothesis that neural programs fail to generalize because they learn spurious dependencies rather than true program semantics is well-supported by prior work (Zaremba et al., 2016, etc.) and the empirical results in the paper.

The core insight that recursion enables provable generalization is significant and well-justified. The paper correctly identifies that recursion reduces the generalization problem to verifying a finite set of base cases and reduction rules, which is a powerful theoretical advantage. The empirical results across four different tasks provide strong evidence for this claim.

The verification procedure described in Section 3.3 is particularly valuable. It's rare for neural program papers to provide such concrete evidence of generalization, rather than just reporting accuracy on test sets of limited complexity. This is a major contribution to the field.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of their approach. The experiments are well-designed, with appropriate baselines and sufficient statistical analysis (30 test examples per input length).

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, these suggestions would further improve it:

1. **Clarify "provable" generalization**: The term "provable" might be misleading. The paper provides verification on a finite set of inputs, not a formal mathematical proof. The authors should be more precise about what "provable" means in this context to avoid confusion with formal verification methods.

2. **Expand on the relationship between recursion and verification**: The paper states that recursion "makes it easier to reason about a program's behavior," but doesn't explicitly connect this to the verification procedure. A more detailed explanation of how recursion enables the verification to be tractable would strengthen the argument.

3. **Discuss limitations more thoroughly**: The paper acknowledges that verification is infeasible for tasks with large input domains, but doesn't discuss how this limitation affects the applicability of the approach. The authors should provide a more nuanced discussion of when their approach is applicable and when it might not work.

4. **Provide more theoretical justification**: While the empirical results are strong, the paper would benefit from more theoretical discussion about *why* recursion helps with generalization. What specific properties of recursive programs make them more generalizable than iterative ones?

5. **Compare with other recursive neural approaches**: The paper doesn't discuss how their approach differs from other attempts to incorporate recursion into neural networks (e.g., Recursive Neural Networks, Tree-LSTM). A brief comparison would help situate their contribution more clearly.

6. **Clarify the verification set construction**: For tasks other than addition, the paper states they "randomly generate input candidates until they completely cover the base cases and reduction rules." This is vague. They should provide more details on how they determined their verification set was complete.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. For the addition task, you claim to cover all base cases and reduction rules with 20,181 input problems. How did you mathematically prove this number is sufficient to cover all possible cases? What would happen if there was a case not covered in your verification set?

2. The paper states "recursion makes it easier for the network to learn the right program and generalize to unknown situations." Can you elaborate on why recursion specifically enables better learning compared to other structural constraints on the neural network?

3. For the bubble sort task, you report that the fully recursive version achieves perfect generalization while the partially recursive version does not. What specific aspect of the recursion (the additional recursive calls to BSTEP and LSHIFT) is critical for perfect generalization?

4. The paper mentions "the first time one can provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs." How does your approach differ from the guarantees provided by symbolic program synthesis methods, which can provide formal guarantees of correctness?

5. The verification procedure requires constructing a verification set that covers all base cases and reduction rules. For tasks with continuous inputs (e.g., sorting real numbers rather than integers), how would you adapt this procedure? What would the verification set look like in such cases?

## Final Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to neural programming. The key insight that recursion enables provable generalization is novel and well-supported by the empirical results. The verification procedure is particularly valuable and provides a new direction for establishing generalization guarantees in neural programs.

While there are some minor issues to address (primarily around clarifying the meaning of "provable" and discussing limitations more thoroughly), these do not undermine the paper's main contribution. The paper provides clear evidence that recursion is a key abstraction for neural programs to achieve perfect generalization, and this is a substantial advance in the field. I recommend acceptance with the suggested revisions.
