

# Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## Summary
The paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures to achieve provable generalization guarantees. The authors argue that current neural programs (e.g., Neural Programmer-Interpreter) fail to generalize beyond certain input complexities because they learn spurious dependencies on training examples rather than true program semantics. They demonstrate that the NPI architecture inherently supports recursion (through its stack-based call structure), and by modifying training traces to include recursive elements, they can train neural programs that learn recursive abstractions. The authors show that their recursive neural programs achieve 100% accuracy on unseen inputs of arbitrary complexity for four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort), and they provide a verification procedure to prove perfect generalization by testing only a finite set of base cases and reduction rules.

## Decision
Reject. While the paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution regarding recursion and provable generalization, the experimental evaluation is limited to trivially small-scale tasks where the verification procedure is feasible, and the paper fails to demonstrate scalability to more complex programming problems.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution is theoretically sound: recursion does provide a structured way to decompose problems and potentially enable provable generalization. The connection to programming concepts is well-motivated, and the NPI architecture is correctly identified as inherently supporting recursion.

However, the paper's experimental evaluation is severely limited. The verification procedure they propose is only feasible for the specific tasks they study (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) because these tasks have small, well-defined input spaces where base cases and reduction rules can be enumerated. The paper acknowledges this limitation (Section 3.3), but fails to address whether the approach can scale to more complex tasks with larger input spaces. For example, the verification set for addition contains 20,181 input problems, which becomes intractable for more complex problems like sorting natural language inputs or processing images.

Furthermore, the training setup is problematic. The paper trains on extremely small examples (e.g., addition trained on 3-digit numbers) but claims generalization to 5000-digit numbers. It's unclear whether the model is learning a recursive abstraction or simply memorizing the limited training examples. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the model would generalize to arbitrary-length inputs if trained on even smaller examples (e.g., 1-digit numbers). The training data size (200 traces for addition) is so small that the model may not be learning a true recursive pattern but rather a pattern specific to the limited training inputs.

## Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Demonstrate the approach on a more complex task where the verification set would be prohibitively large (e.g., sorting real-world data or processing natural language), and discuss how the verification procedure would need to be modified. This would provide evidence of scalability beyond trivial tasks.

2. Provide evidence that the model is learning a true recursive abstraction rather than memorizing training examples. This could include showing the model's behavior on intermediate-sized inputs during training, or testing generalization from training on 1-digit numbers to 1000-digit numbers.

3. More thoroughly address the limitations of the verification procedure for complex tasks. The paper mentions this as a limitation but doesn't provide a clear path forward for more complex scenarios.

## Questions for Authors
1. How would you apply your verification procedure to a more complex task, such as sorting a list of words alphabetically? What would the verification set look like, and how large would it be?

2. The paper trains on very small examples (max length 3 for addition), but claims to generalize to 5000-digit numbers. How do you know the model isn't just memorizing the training examples rather than learning a recursive abstraction? Could you provide results for training on even smaller examples (e.g., 1-digit numbers) and testing on longer numbers?

3. The paper mentions that the recursive program for bubble sort requires three recursive calls for perfect generalization, but only one was used in the partially recursive version. How did you determine the number of recursive calls needed for each task, and how would you automatically determine this for new tasks?
