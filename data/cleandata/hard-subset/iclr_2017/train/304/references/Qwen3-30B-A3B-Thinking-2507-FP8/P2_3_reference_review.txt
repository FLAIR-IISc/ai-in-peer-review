

# Review: MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural programming by addressing a fundamental limitation in current approaches: poor generalization beyond the complexity seen in training data. The authors correctly identify that existing neural programming models (like Neural Programmer-Interpreter) fail to generalize because they learn spurious dependencies on specific characteristics of training examples (like input length) rather than the true program semantics. 

The key contribution is the proposal to explicitly incorporate recursion into neural programming architectures, which the authors argue is a necessary abstraction for neural programs to generalize. They demonstrate that recursion enables "provably perfect generalization" for specific tasks, meaning the neural programs can be formally verified to work correctly for all possible inputs after testing only a finite number of base cases and reduction rules. The authors implement this approach in the NPI architecture, showing empirical success on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort) where recursive programs achieve 100% accuracy on all test inputs after training on a very small number of examples, while non-recursive versions fail for longer inputs.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper addresses a well-identified and important problem in neural programming with a novel and well-motivated solution.
2. The empirical results are strong, clearly demonstrating the advantage of the recursive approach over non-recursive baselines.
3. The verification procedure they propose for proving perfect generalization is sound and represents a significant contribution to the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The authors correctly identify that current neural programming approaches suffer from poor generalization beyond training complexity, which is a well-documented limitation in the field. The connection to recursion as a fundamental programming concept is particularly strong and well-justified. The problem statement is clearly articulated, and the literature review properly positions their work in the context of prior neural programming approaches.

The empirical results are compelling and well-presented. For example, in the addition task, the recursive model generalizes perfectly from a small number of 1-digit examples to arbitrarily long additions, while the non-recursive model fails. The same pattern holds for the other tasks. The verification procedure they describe is a major contribution in itself, as it provides a way to formally verify neural program behavior without needing to test infinitely many inputs.

The theoretical justification for why recursion helps with generalization is sound. By decomposing problems into base cases and reduction rules, recursion creates structure that makes verification tractable. The paper correctly explains how this reduces the verification problem from an infinite to a finite one, which is the key insight enabling their provable guarantees.

The paper's contribution is novel and significant. While recursion has been used in programming for decades, this is the first time it has been explicitly incorporated into neural programming architectures with the goal of enabling provable generalization. The authors' claim that this is the first time provable guarantees of perfect generalization have been provided for neural programs appears well-supported by their experimental results.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend addressing the following points:

1. **Clarify the scope of "provably perfect generalization."** The paper states this applies to "all valid inputs," but it's important to be precise about what constitutes a "valid input" for each task. For example, in addition, they assume no leading zeros (except for single digits), which is a specific constraint. Clarifying these constraints would help readers understand the boundaries of the claim.

2. **Explain the NPI's natural support for recursion more clearly.** The paper states "the NPI model naturally supports recursion" but doesn't provide a clear explanation of why this is the case. A brief clarification of the NPI architecture's recursive capabilities would help readers better understand the foundation of their approach.

3. **Discuss limitations more thoroughly.** While the paper acknowledges that verification may be infeasible for tasks with very large input domains (like MNIST), it would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical boundaries of their approach. What are the specific conditions under which their verification procedure works well, and when might it fail?

4. **Provide more insight into why recursion works better.** The paper mentions that non-recursive models learn spurious dependencies on input length, but it would be valuable to see more concrete evidence of this. Perhaps a small analysis of the learned models' behavior on different input lengths would strengthen their argument.

5. **Detail the verification set construction process.** For tasks like topological sort and quicksort, the paper states they "randomly generate input candidates until they completely cover the base cases and reduction rules." A more detailed explanation of this process would help readers understand how they ensured comprehensive coverage.

## Questions for Authors

1. In the verification procedure, you state "V should also contain the necessary reductions, which corresponds to making the recursive calls at the correct time, as indicated by P." How do you ensure that your verification set covers all possible reduction cases, especially for more complex tasks like quicksort where the reduction rules are less straightforward?

2. For the bubble sort task, you modified the domain-specific encoder to make verification feasible. How general is this modification? Would your approach work with the original NPI domain-specific encoder, or does it require this specific design of environment observations?

3. The paper claims that the recursive approach "out-performing previous generalization results." Could you provide a more specific comparison to prior work, such as the exact generalization performance of the best previous approach versus your recursive method on the same tasks?

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neural programming. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and the results are compelling. With minor revisions to clarify the scope of the claims and strengthen the discussion of limitations, this paper would be an outstanding contribution to ICLR.
