

# ICLR Peer Review

## Summary of Claims/Contribution
This paper addresses a critical limitation in neural programming: the inability of existing architectures to generalize robustly beyond a certain input complexity threshold. The authors propose that explicitly incorporating recursion into neural architectures enables provable perfect generalization, a novel contribution to the field. They demonstrate this by modifying the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture to learn recursive programs for four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort). Their key claims are: (1) recursion decomposes problems into manageable base cases and reduction rules, (2) this enables verification of perfect generalization with finite testing, and (3) minimal training data suffices for perfect generalization on arbitrary input sizes. The paper provides empirical evidence across four tasks and a verification procedure that formally proves generalization for these specific cases.

## Decision
**Accept with minor revisions.** This paper makes a significant contribution to neural programming by addressing a fundamental limitation (generalization) through a novel and well-motivated approach (recursion). The empirical results are compelling, and the verification procedure represents a notable theoretical advance that has not been achieved before for neural programs.

## Supporting Arguments

The paper's motivation is strong and well-placed in the literature. It correctly identifies the key limitation in existing neural programming work (Vinyals et al., 2015; Reed & de Freitas, 2016; Zaremba et al., 2016) where models fail to generalize due to learning spurious dependencies on training set characteristics rather than true program semantics. The authors make a compelling case that recursion—fundamental to programming languages—provides the necessary abstraction for neural programs to generalize correctly.

The methodology is well-executed. The authors demonstrate that by modifying training traces to include recursive elements, the NPI model naturally learns recursive programs without architectural changes. Their verification procedure is particularly impressive: by focusing on base cases and reduction rules (which are finite for their tasks), they can formally prove perfect generalization. For example, for addition, they show that verifying 20,181 specific inputs (covering all relevant base cases and reduction rules) guarantees generalization to any input size. This is a significant theoretical contribution that goes beyond empirical generalization claims.

The empirical results are convincing across all four tasks. For addition, the recursive model generalizes from 1-digit examples to 5000-digit problems, while non-recursive models fail. For bubble sort, the fully recursive version sorts arrays of arbitrary length, while non-recursive versions fail beyond length 8. The verification procedure is correctly applied, and the paper provides sufficient evidence that their recursive programs achieve perfect generalization.

## Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Clarify limitations of the verification approach:** The paper rightly acknowledges that their verification method is limited to tasks with finite base cases (like addition, sorting), but doesn't adequately address how this would scale to more complex domains (e.g., image classification). The authors should discuss potential extensions or approximations for continuous input spaces and explicitly state the boundaries of their approach.

2. **Strengthen theoretical justification:** While the intuition about recursion decomposing problems is sound, the paper lacks formal analysis explaining *why* recursion specifically leads to better generalization. A brief theoretical argument (e.g., relating to inductive bias or problem decomposition) would strengthen the contribution.

3. **Provide comparative data efficiency metrics:** The paper mentions "very small number of training examples" but doesn't quantify this relative to other approaches. Including a table comparing training examples needed for their recursive approach versus non-recursive baselines would clarify the sample efficiency advantage.

4. **Address potential implementation differences:** For topological sort, the paper states the non-recursive model has "low accuracy, beginning from size 5," but Reed & de Freitas (2016) reported generalization to larger graphs. The authors should clarify if there are implementation differences affecting this comparison.

## Questions for Authors

1. For tasks with potentially infinite base cases (e.g., sorting arbitrary real numbers), how might your verification procedure be adapted or what theoretical guarantees could be provided?

2. The paper claims "the non-recursive programs do not generalize well because they have learned spurious dependencies." What specific evidence do you have for this beyond the fact that they fail on longer inputs?

3. For the quicksort task, you mention the recursion "is not purely tail recursive and therefore represents a more complex kind of recursion." How does this complexity affect the learning process compared to the other tasks, and what specific challenges did it present?

4. You state that "the training set can often be considerably smaller than the verification set" (e.g., 200 traces for addition vs. 20,181 verification inputs). Could you elaborate on why the verification set is larger than the training set in this case, and whether the verification set size is directly related to the complexity of the task?

This paper makes a significant contribution to neural programming by providing a path to provable generalization through recursion. With minor clarifications on the scope and limitations of their verification procedure, it will be a valuable addition to the field.
