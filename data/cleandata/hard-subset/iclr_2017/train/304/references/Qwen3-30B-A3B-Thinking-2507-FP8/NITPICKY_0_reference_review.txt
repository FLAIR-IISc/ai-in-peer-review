

# Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

The paper makes a compelling claim that incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures enables "provably perfect generalization" for neural programs. The authors argue that current neural programming models fail to generalize beyond a certain complexity threshold due to learning spurious dependencies on training data characteristics (like input length) rather than true program semantics. They propose that by explicitly incorporating recursion—through modifying training traces to include recursive elements—neural programs can achieve perfect generalization with provable guarantees. The paper demonstrates this approach on four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort), claiming that their recursive neural programs generalize perfectly to inputs of arbitrary complexity after training on very small datasets, and that they can provide "provable guarantees" of this perfect generalization through verification on a finite set of base cases and reduction rules.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper's central claim of "provable guarantees of perfect generalization" is fundamentally misrepresented, as the verification procedure described does not constitute a formal proof. This misrepresentation of the paper's contribution is a critical flaw that undermines the entire paper's significance.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Rejection

The paper's most serious flaw is the misrepresentation of its verification procedure as a "proof" when it is merely empirical testing on a finite set of inputs. The paper repeatedly claims "provable guarantees" and "provable perfect generalization" (e.g., "we can provide proofs that these learned programs generalize perfectly," "this is the first time one can provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs"), but the verification procedure described in Section 3.3 is not a proof—it's a finite empirical test. 

The paper states: "if we can prove that (1) it performs correctly on the base cases; (2) it learns the reduction rules correctly" then "we can provide proofs that these learned programs generalize perfectly." However, the paper doesn't provide any formal proof of correctness for the base cases and reduction rules—they simply test on a finite set of inputs and claim this constitutes a proof. This conflates empirical verification with formal proof, which is a serious misrepresentation of what constitutes a proof in computer science.

For example, the paper claims for addition: "we have proven that any member of F will be added correctly, thus eliminating an infinite family of inputs that need to be tested." But they haven't proven this—they've empirically verified a finite subset of F. The paper never demonstrates that the verification set they construct (20,181 inputs for addition) actually covers all possible cases, nor do they provide a formal proof that the recursive structure ensures generalization to all inputs.

The paper also makes a significant oversight by failing to engage with the literature on formal verification of neural networks. If they truly intended to claim "provable generalization," they should have cited and engaged with work on formal verification of neural networks (e.g., Sankaranarayanan et al., 2013; Huang et al., 2017; similar to how they engage with the neural programming literature).

The paper's claim that "this is the first time one can provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs" is also misleading. The verification procedure described is not a proof—it's empirical testing. The paper has not demonstrated any provable guarantees in the mathematical sense.

## 4. Additional Feedback

1. **Clarify the meaning of "provable"**: If the authors wish to claim "provable" generalization, they must provide a formal proof, not just empirical verification. They should either:
   - Revise their claims to accurately reflect that they have demonstrated empirical generalization with a verification procedure that covers a finite set of cases
   - Provide a formal proof of generalization for their recursive neural programs

2. **Engage with formal verification literature**: The paper should cite and discuss relevant work on formal verification of neural networks to properly situate their contribution in the literature.

3. **Clarify the contribution**: The paper's contribution is better described as "training neural programs to use recursion" rather than "incorporating recursion," since the NPI architecture they use naturally supports recursion (as they acknowledge in Section 2.2). The authors should clarify this distinction.

4. **Address the verification set construction**: The paper claims that "recursion drastically reduces the number of configurations we need to consider during the verification phase," but doesn't provide evidence that their verification sets are complete. They should provide a more rigorous argument for why their verification sets cover all necessary cases.

5. **Fix minor formatting inconsistencies**: The paper inconsistently uses "NPI" and "Neural Programmer-Interpreter." The paper should maintain consistent terminology throughout.

6. **Clarify the relationship to the NPI paper**: The paper acknowledges that the NPI model naturally supports recursion (even though the original authors didn't explicitly consider it), but doesn't clearly articulate how their work differs from simply retraining the NPI model with recursive traces.

**Questions for authors:**

1. Can you provide a formal proof that your recursive neural programs generalize perfectly for all inputs (beyond the empirical verification on the finite verification set)?
2. How do you reconcile your claim of "provable guarantees" with the fact that your verification procedure is empirical testing on a finite set of inputs?
3. How does your verification procedure differ from standard empirical evaluation, and why does it constitute a "proof" rather than just a test?
4. How do you ensure that your verification sets (e.g., 20,181 inputs for addition) truly cover all possible base cases and reduction rules, rather than just a representative sample?

The paper presents an interesting approach to improving generalization in neural programming, but the misrepresentation of their verification procedure as "provable" rather than empirical is a critical flaw that cannot be overlooked. Without a formal proof of generalization, the paper's central claim is unsubstantiated.
