

# Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures to achieve provably perfect generalization on programming tasks. The authors claim that by modifying training traces to include recursive elements, neural programs can learn true program semantics rather than spurious dependencies on input characteristics (like length), enabling perfect generalization to inputs of arbitrary complexity. They demonstrate this approach on four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort) using the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework, claiming their recursive programs achieve 100% accuracy on all inputs after training on a small number of examples, with formal proofs of generalization via verification sets.

## Decision: Reject

**Key reasons for rejection:**
1. The paper contains multiple significant technical inconsistencies and unsupported claims that undermine the core contribution
2. The verification procedure described does not actually provide "provably perfect generalization" as claimed, but rather verification on a finite (though large) set of inputs

## Supporting Arguments

The paper's core claim of "provably perfect generalization" is fundamentally flawed. The authors claim to provide "provably perfect generalization" but their verification procedure only tests on a finite set of inputs (20,181 for addition, 10 for bubble sort, etc.). This is not a proof of generalization to all possible inputs, as the paper states, but rather verification on a specific finite set. The paper states "we can provide proofs that these learned programs generalize perfectly" but the actual procedure only verifies on the verification set, not on all possible inputs. This is a critical misrepresentation of what constitutes a proof of generalization.

The paper makes several unsupported claims about the NPI architecture and prior work:
- "The NPI model naturally supports recursion (even though the authors of NPI did not consider this aspect explicitly)" - This is not demonstrated; the paper merely states it as fact without evidence that the original NPI could have learned recursion without modification.
- "The only metric that has been used is generalization of expected behavior to inputs of greater complexity" - This is factually incorrect; prior works use metrics like accuracy on test sets, training time, and model size.
- "the non-recursive NPI program fails" - The original NPI paper (Reed & de Freitas, 2016) reports good generalization on addition (up to 3000 digits), contradicting this claim.

There are multiple technical inconsistencies:
- In the bubble sort section, the paper claims "the partially recursive version is able to sort every array given to it" but Table 1 shows it fails for lengths 9-10.
- The paper states "the recursive version can generalize perfectly to long problems constructed from these components" for addition, but the verification set size (20,181) is much larger than the training set (200 traces), making the claim of "small number of training examples" misleading.
- The paper claims "recursion makes it easier for the network to learn the right program," but provides no evidence that the recursive training traces cause the model to learn recursion rather than just learning a different non-recursive program.

The paper also contains numerous formatting inconsistencies: inconsistent citations (e.g., "Reed & de Freitas (2016)" vs "Reed & de Freitas (2016)"), incorrect references (e.g., "Q2015E" instead of "2015"), and missing figure references (Figure 1 is referenced but not included).

## Additional Feedback

1. The paper's core claim of "provably perfect generalization" needs complete reworking. If the authors mean "verified generalization on a finite set," they should be precise about this. If they truly want to claim a proof of generalization to all inputs, they need a theoretical argument, not just verification on a finite set.

2. The paper should provide statistical significance testing for the results. The differences in accuracy between recursive and non-recursive versions could be due to chance, especially with small test sets (30 examples per length).

3. The paper should clarify why the verification set for addition is 20,181 inputs, while the training set is only 200 traces. This large discrepancy undermines the claim of "very small number of examples."

4. The paper should provide ablation studies to isolate the effect of recursion versus other potential changes in the training procedure.

5. The paper should correct the numerous formatting inconsistencies, inconsistent citations, and technical inaccuracies that undermine credibility.

6. The paper should properly contextualize its contribution relative to existing work on neural program verification, which does exist (e.g., work on formal verification of neural networks).

The paper presents an interesting idea, but the technical inconsistencies and misleading claims about "provably perfect generalization" are too significant to overlook. The paper would benefit from substantial revision to clarify its claims and provide a more accurate assessment of its contribution before it could be considered for publication.
