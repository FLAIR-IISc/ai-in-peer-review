- Introduces recursion into neural programming (NPI) to enable provable generalization beyond training distributions.
- Contribution: provides a theoretical justification for why recursion enables provable generalization.
- Contribution: proposes a training method that modifies execution traces to explicitly contain recursive elements.
- Contribution: empirically demonstrates recursive models achieve 100% accuracy on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort while non-recursive models fail on longer inputs.
- Contribution: presents a verification procedure that proves perfect generalization by testing a finite set of base cases and reduction rules.
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.
- Recursion reduces generalization to verifying base cases and reduction rules, making proofs tractable vs. prior empirical-only measures.
- Empirical evidence: recursive addition generalizes to 5000-digit numbers from tiny training sets; recursive bubble sort/topological sort/quicksort generalize to much larger inputs than non-recursive versions.
- Verification example: addition generalization can be proven by verifying 20,181 specific cases instead of infinitely many inputs.
- Paper situates itself well in related work, highlighting the lack of prior provable generalization guarantees for neural programs.
- Suggestion: provide evidence for the “spurious dependencies” claim via analysis of hidden states or attention patterns.
- Suggestion: acknowledge and expand discussion of practical limitations where verification sets may be prohibitively large (e.g., continuous or infinite state spaces).
- Suggestion: clarify how recursive training traces were generated for tasks beyond addition, especially for topological sort and quicksort.
- Suggestion: compare with alternative generalization-improving approaches (e.g., meta-learning, data augmentation).
- Question: how could the verification procedure be adapted for tasks with infinite base/reduction cases (e.g., real-valued arrays)?
- Question: provide concrete training set size vs. verification set size for the four tasks to quantify the relationship.
- Question: how does the approach handle problems without natural recursive formulations—would it fail or be inapplicable?
- Question: explain why preserved LSTM hidden state in non-recursive addition makes generalization hard to prove and how recursion avoids this.
