- Proposes recursion as a key abstraction enabling provable perfect generalization for neural programs.  
- Introduces a verification procedure that proves perfect generalization by covering finite base cases and reduction rules.  
- Demonstrates perfect generalization on four algorithmic tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort).  
- Achieves strong empirical results with very small training sets (e.g., 200 traces for addition, 100 for bubble sort; addition generalizes after 5 examples).  
- Uses the Neural Programmer-Interpreter architecture to implement recursive neural programs.  
- Argues recursion reduces generalization to a tractable finite verification problem.  
- Claims novelty as the first work to investigate provable generalization properties of neural programs.  
- Positions contributions clearly within prior neural programming literature and connects to traditional programming concepts.  
- Decision: Accept — paper is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated.  
- Verification procedure is a major methodological advance over empirical generalization metrics.  
- Acknowledges limitation: verification only applies to tasks with clear recursive structure and not to continuous input spaces (e.g., MNIST).  
- Recommends deeper discussion of verification limits, including how verification set size scales with task complexity.  
- Requests clearer theoretical explanation for why verification works for recursive but not non-recursive programs.  
- Suggests exploring training methods that learn recursion without explicit recursive execution traces.  
- Advises comparing computational cost of verification versus training, especially for larger tasks.  
- Asks specific author questions about non-recursive verification feasibility, verification set scaling (e.g., 20,181 cases for addition), theoretical bounds for topological sort, sensitivity to training example choice, and prior related works.
