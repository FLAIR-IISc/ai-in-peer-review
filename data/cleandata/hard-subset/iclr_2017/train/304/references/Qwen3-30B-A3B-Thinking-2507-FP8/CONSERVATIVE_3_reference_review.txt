

# Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

The paper claims to address the critical problem of generalization in neural programming architectures. The authors argue that existing approaches fail to generalize beyond a certain complexity threshold because they learn spurious dependencies on training data characteristics (like input length) rather than the true program semantics. They propose incorporating recursion into neural architectures, specifically the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI), to enable provable perfect generalization. The key contribution is the claim that recursive neural programs can be verified to generalize perfectly on any input, with the paper presenting empirical results on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort) showing the recursive versions achieve perfect generalization from very small training sets. The authors assert this is the first time "provably perfect generalization" has been achieved for neural programs.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes strong claims about "provably perfect generalization" that are not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented, and the validation methodology lacks rigor for real-world applicability.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main weakness lies in the mismatch between the claimed contribution and the actual evidence provided. The authors claim to provide "provably perfect generalization," but this proof is only applicable to their specific task formulations with carefully constructed verification sets that cover all base cases and reduction rules. The paper fails to address two critical limitations:

First, the verification procedure is only tractable for their specific, highly constrained tasks. For example, the verification set for addition contains 20,181 input problems (as they explicitly state), and for other tasks they resort to randomly generating verification sets. For more complex programming tasks with larger input domains (e.g., sorting arbitrary arrays or graphs), the verification set would likely become prohibitively large, rendering their "provable generalization" claim meaningless in practice. The paper acknowledges this limitation in Section 4.2 but fails to provide a convincing solution.

Second, the empirical validation lacks proper comparison to established baselines. The paper compares recursive vs. non-recursive versions of their own architecture but doesn't compare against other state-of-the-art methods that have been designed specifically for generalization (e.g., curriculum learning, differentiable programming approaches, or more recent neural programming architectures). The paper claims their recursive approach "out-performs previous generalization results," but without proper comparison to the best existing methods, this claim is unsubstantiated.

The paper also makes the questionable assertion that "the memory updates of these neural programs are so complex and interdependent that it is difficult to reason about the behaviors of the learned neural program under previously unseen situations." This is an overstatement - many neural network architectures (including those with recursive structures) have well-studied generalization properties, and the authors provide no evidence to support this claim.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct a more rigorous comparison against established baselines for neural programming, not just their own non-recursive versions. This would provide context for how much improvement their approach actually delivers.

2. Address the scalability of their verification procedure to more complex programming tasks. The paper's "provably perfect generalization" claim is only meaningful if it can be applied to a broad range of tasks, not just the specific ones they chose.

3. Provide a more nuanced discussion of the relationship between their method and existing work. The paper claims their approach is "the first time one can provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs," but the NPI architecture naturally supports recursion (as they acknowledge), so this isn't truly a new architectural innovation. They should clarify whether the novelty lies in the training procedure or the verification method.

4. Provide a more detailed analysis of why recursion specifically helps with generalization, rather than just claiming it does. The paper doesn't establish a causal link between the recursive structure and improved generalization beyond the empirical results on their specific tasks.

## Questions for Authors

1. How would you scale your verification procedure to a programming task with an input domain that's not easily parameterized (e.g., sorting arbitrary images or natural language processing tasks)? What would be the computational cost?

2. Can you provide evidence that your recursive approach outperforms other generalization techniques (like curriculum learning or better data augmentation) on the same tasks, rather than just comparing it to non-recursive versions of the same architecture?

3. The paper states that "the memory updates of these neural programs are so complex and interdependent that it is difficult to reason about the behaviors of the learned neural program under previously unseen situations." Can you provide evidence for this claim, rather than simply stating it?

4. The verification set for addition contains 20,181 input problems. How does the size of this verification set scale with more complex tasks, and how does this affect the practicality of your approach?
