

# Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## Summary of Contribution
This paper presents a bold and novel approach to addressing a fundamental limitation in neural programming: the inability of current models to generalize beyond a certain input complexity threshold. The authors make the compelling argument that the lack of recursion in neural programming architectures is the root cause of this generalization failure, and they propose incorporating recursion to enable provably perfect generalization. The paper demonstrates that by modifying training traces to explicitly include recursive elements, neural programming architectures (specifically NPI) can learn recursive programs that achieve perfect generalization on unseen inputs. Crucially, the authors provide a verification procedure that allows them to prove perfect generalization for the learned programs, which is a significant theoretical contribution. The paper is the first to investigate "provable generalization properties of neural programs" and demonstrates this approach on four diverse tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.

## Decision
Accept

## Key Reasons for Acceptance
1. **Novel and Bold Concept**: The paper introduces a truly novel idea—explicitly incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures—which is a fundamental concept in programming languages but has been overlooked in neural program learning. This is a significant conceptual leap that addresses a core limitation in the field.

2. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The paper demonstrates that recursive neural programs achieve perfect generalization from extremely small training sets (e.g., 200 traces for addition with max length 3), outperforming all previous approaches. The verification procedure provides a rigorous way to prove perfect generalization, which is a major theoretical contribution.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's core contribution—using recursion to enable provable generalization—is both novel and impactful. The authors correctly identify that current neural programming approaches fail to generalize because they learn spurious dependencies rather than true program semantics. Their solution is elegant: by incorporating recursion (which naturally exists in the NPI architecture but was not explicitly used), they enable the model to learn the true program semantics, which generalizes perfectly.

The verification procedure described in Section 3.3 is particularly impressive. By showing that they can prove perfect generalization through verification of base cases and reduction rules (instead of testing on infinite input sets), the paper provides a theoretical foundation for neural program generalization that has been missing from the field. This is a significant theoretical contribution that goes beyond the typical empirical results in neural programming.

The empirical results across four diverse tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) are compelling. For example, the recursive quicksort version generalizes perfectly to arrays of any length (verified up to length 100), while the non-recursive version fails at length 11. The fact that the recursive approach generalizes from just 4 training examples (for quicksort) is particularly striking.

The paper's approach is well-motivated and correctly positioned in the literature. It builds on the Neural Programmer-Interpreter architecture while identifying a critical limitation that has been overlooked in prior work. The authors correctly cite relevant literature (Vinyals et al., Kaiser & Sutskever, Reed & de Freitas, Graves et al., Zaremba et al.) and clearly articulate how their work differs from and improves upon these approaches.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the verification procedure. The authors note it's infeasible for tasks with "very large input domains" like MNIST, but it would be helpful to more clearly articulate what specific characteristics make verification feasible (finite base cases) versus infeasible (infinite input spaces).

2. The paper mentions "future work" to decrease supervision and train with partial or non-recursive traces. A brief discussion of how this might be achieved (even speculatively) would strengthen the paper's forward-looking perspective.

3. The paper claims to be "the first to investigate the important problem of provable generalization properties of neural programs." While this is likely true, a more thorough comparison to other approaches that claim to provide guarantees (e.g., symbolic methods, program synthesis) would help position this contribution more precisely.

4. For the bubble sort task, the authors note that "partial recursion" (only one recursive call) is insufficient for perfect generalization. It would be interesting to see a more detailed analysis of why one recursive call is insufficient while three are needed.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that "recursion enables provable guarantees on neural programs' behavior without needing to exhaustively enumerate all possible inputs." Could you elaborate on how this differs from traditional program verification techniques?

2. For the verification procedure, how much does the size of the verification set grow with the complexity of the task? Is there a theoretical bound on the verification set size based on the recursion structure?

3. The paper mentions "in future work, we seek to enable more tasks with recursive structure." Could you provide examples of tasks that might be challenging to incorporate with recursion, and why?

This paper makes a significant contribution to neural programming by introducing a novel approach that fundamentally changes how we think about generalization in neural programs. The combination of theoretical insight and strong empirical validation makes this a valuable contribution to the field.
