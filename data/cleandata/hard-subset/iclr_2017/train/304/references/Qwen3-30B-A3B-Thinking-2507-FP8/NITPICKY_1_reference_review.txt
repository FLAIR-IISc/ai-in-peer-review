

# Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures to achieve provable perfect generalization. The authors argue that current neural programming approaches suffer from poor generalization due to learning spurious dependencies on training examples, and they claim that recursion provides a way to learn the true program semantics with provable guarantees. They demonstrate their approach on four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort) by modifying training traces to include recursive elements, showing empirical perfect generalization from very small training sets. The paper claims this is the first time provable perfect generalization can be demonstrated for neural programs.

## Decision

Reject

## Key Reasons for Rejection

1. **Misrepresentation of "provable generalization"**: The paper claims "provably perfect generalization" but only describes a verification procedure that tests on a finite set of inputs (e.g., 20,181 for addition), not a formal mathematical proof. The verification set is large but finite, and the paper fails to demonstrate that the model generalizes to all possible inputs in an infinite input space. This misrepresents the meaning of "provable" in mathematical terms.

2. **Conceptual confusion between recursion and training traces**: The paper claims "recursion is an essential element for neural programs to generalize" but fails to distinguish between the concept of recursion as a programming paradigm and their specific training method (modifying training traces to include recursive elements). Their approach is not about incorporating recursion into the architecture but rather about changing the training data. The claim that "NPI naturally supports recursion" is misleading, as the architecture itself doesn't change—only the training traces do.

3. **Critical gap in explanation of generalization**: The paper claims the recursive model generalizes from "8+2" and "2+3" to "822+233" but fails to explain *how* the model achieves this generalization. The paper states "the recursive version can generalize perfectly to long problems constructed from these components" but provides no mechanism for how the model composes these smaller additions into a larger solution. This is a fundamental gap in the explanation of their claimed contribution.

## Supporting Arguments

The paper's strongest claim—that they provide "provably perfect generalization"—is undermined by the lack of formal mathematical proof. The verification procedure described in Section 3.3 is essentially a large-scale empirical test, not a proof. For example, for addition, they verify on 20,181 specific input problems but make no claim about inputs outside this set. This is not "provable" generalization in the mathematical sense.

The paper also fails to clarify the relationship between their training approach and the concept of recursion. In Section 3.2, they state "We found that changing the NPI training traces is a simple way to enable this," yet they frame this as "enabling recursion" rather than "enabling learning of recursive patterns through specific training traces." This conceptual confusion misrepresents the contribution.

The paper's claim that "recursion is an essential element for neural programs to generalize" is not supported by evidence. They show that their specific training method (using recursive traces) works, but they don't prove that recursion itself is necessary or that other approaches couldn't achieve the same results. They also don't provide a formal argument for why recursion would be necessary for generalization, which is a critical gap in their motivation.

The paper's description of the verification procedure contains inconsistencies. In Section 3.3, they state "Recursion drastically reduces the number of configurations we need to consider during the verification phase," but for addition, they still require 20,181 verification inputs. This contradicts their claim of drastic reduction in verification complexity.

## Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Clarify the core contribution**: The paper should explicitly distinguish between the concept of recursion as a programming paradigm and their specific training approach. The title and claims should be revised to reflect that they are proposing a training method that incorporates recursive elements, not that they are adding recursion to the architecture.

2. **Provide a formal proof**: To support their claim of "provably perfect generalization," the paper should include a formal mathematical proof that their approach guarantees generalization to all inputs, not just a verification on a finite set. This would require a formal definition of the program semantics and a proof by induction over the input space.

3. **Explain the generalization mechanism**: The paper must explain how the model generalizes from small components to larger problems. For the addition example, they should describe the model's internal mechanism for composing "8+2" and "2+3" into "822+233."

4. **Address the verification set size**: For tasks like addition with a 20,181 verification set, the paper should address whether this is truly tractable and how it compares to the training set size. The claim that "the verification set can often be considerably smaller than the training set" is contradicted by the addition example.

5. **Clarify the NPI architecture's relationship to recursion**: The paper should provide a clear technical explanation of how the NPI architecture supports recursion, rather than simply stating it "naturally supports recursion."

6. **Improve consistency in terminology**: The paper uses "NPI" without defining it as an acronym for "Neural Programmer-Interpreter" in the first usage, and the term "recursive neural program" is used inconsistently.

## Questions for Authors

1. How does the paper's verification procedure constitute a "proof" of perfect generalization, given that it tests on a finite set of inputs rather than providing a mathematical proof?

2. The paper claims that "recursion is an essential element for neural programs to generalize," but demonstrates that a specific training method (with recursive traces) works. What evidence do you have that recursion itself (as a concept) is necessary, rather than just the specific training traces?

3. For the addition task, how exactly does the model generalize from "8+2" and "2+3" to "822+233"? Can you provide a detailed explanation of the model's internal mechanism for this composition?

4. Why is the verification set for addition so large (20,181 inputs) when the paper claims recursion "drastically reduces" the number of configurations to verify? What is the baseline verification set size without recursion?

5. The paper states "the NPI model naturally supports recursion," but doesn't explain how this is possible given the architecture described in Section 3.1. Can you provide a clear technical explanation of how the architecture supports recursion?
