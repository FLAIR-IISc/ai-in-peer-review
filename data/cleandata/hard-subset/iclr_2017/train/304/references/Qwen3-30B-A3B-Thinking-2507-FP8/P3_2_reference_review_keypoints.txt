- Paper addresses poor generalization in neural programs and proposes recursion as key to achieving "provably perfect generalization" to arbitrary inputs.
- Contributions: identifies spurious dependency learning, argues recursion is necessary, shows NPI supports recursion, trains NPI with recursive traces, and gives a verification procedure for generalization.
- Empirical results: recursive NPI achieves 100% accuracy on arbitrary-size inputs for addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort after training on few examples.
- Verification procedure: proves generalization by checking a finite set of base cases and reduction rules, providing stronger guarantees than typical test-set evaluation.
- Decision: recommend accept with minor revisions.
- Reasons for acceptance: tackles important problem, well-motivated, strong empirical gains, and a novel verification framework.
- Supporting argument: recursion reduces generalization to a finite verification problem and mitigates learning spurious correlations.
- Experiments: well-designed with appropriate baselines and statistical analysis (30 test examples per input length).
- Suggestion: clarify meaning of "provable" generalization to avoid confusion with formal proofs or formal verification.
- Suggestion: better connect how recursion specifically makes verification tractable and provide more detailed explanation.
- Suggestion: discuss limitations more thoroughly, especially where verification is infeasible due to large input domains.
- Suggestion: add more theoretical justification for why recursion aids generalization compared to iterative or other structures.
- Suggestion: compare to other recursive neural approaches (e.g., Recursive Neural Networks, Tree-LSTM) to situate the contribution.
- Suggestion: clarify how verification sets are constructed (e.g., random generation until coverage) and justify completeness.
- Questions for authors: how was the addition task's 20,181 verification examples proven sufficient and what if coverage missed a case?
- Questions for authors: why does recursion improve learning vs. other structural constraints, which recursive calls enabled perfect generalization in bubble sort, how this compares to symbolic synthesis guarantees, and how to adapt verification for continuous inputs.
