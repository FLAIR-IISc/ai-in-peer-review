

# Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contribution
The paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures to achieve provable perfect generalization. The authors identify a key limitation in existing neural programming approaches: models learn spurious dependencies on training examples (e.g., input length) rather than the true program semantics, leading to poor generalization beyond a certain complexity threshold. They argue that recursion, a fundamental concept in programming, enables decomposition of problems into base cases and reduction rules, making it tractable to prove generalization properties. The authors demonstrate that by modifying training traces to include recursive elements, the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture can learn recursive neural programs that generalize perfectly to inputs of arbitrary complexity (e.g., addition with 5000+ digits, sorting arrays of arbitrary length). Crucially, they introduce a verification procedure that can prove perfect generalization by testing a finite set of base cases and reduction rules, which is the first such provable guarantee for neural programs.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by introducing recursion as a mechanism for achieving provable perfect generalization in neural programs. The empirical results demonstrate clear superiority of the recursive approach over non-recursive variants across multiple tasks, with the key strength being the ability to provide formal guarantees of perfect generalization.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's contribution is well-justified and well-supported. First, the problem is clearly identified and well-motivated: existing neural programming models fail to generalize beyond certain input complexities, and no existing approach provides provable guarantees about generalization. The authors correctly position their work within the literature by referencing key neural programming architectures (NPI, Neural Turing Machines, etc.) and identifying the gap in provable generalization.

The theoretical justification for recursion's role in enabling provable generalization is sound. The authors correctly argue that recursion reduces problem complexity to finite base cases and reduction rules, making verification tractable. This is supported by their formal verification procedure that can prove generalization by testing only a finite set of inputs, rather than requiring infinite testing.

The empirical results are compelling and well-executed. For four distinct tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort), the recursive neural programs achieve perfect generalization (100% accuracy) on inputs of arbitrary complexity after training on very small datasets (e.g., 200 traces for addition, 6 traces for topological sort), while non-recursive versions fail for inputs beyond a certain size. The verification procedure is rigorously applied, with the authors demonstrating that the learned recursive programs match the behavior of the true programs on the verification sets, thus proving perfect generalization.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
- The paper could provide a more detailed discussion of how the verification set size scales with task complexity. For the tasks presented, verification sets are relatively small, but for more complex tasks, the verification set might become prohibitively large.
- A comparison with other generalization techniques (like curriculum learning) would strengthen the paper's position, particularly since the authors cite curriculum learning as a common approach that ultimately fails.
- The paper mentions future work on decreasing supervision (training with partial or non-recursive traces), but including preliminary results or a more concrete roadmap would enhance the discussion of future directions.
- The limitations section could be expanded to discuss scenarios where the verification procedure might not be feasible (e.g., for tasks with infinite or extremely large state spaces).

## 5. Clarifying Questions
- For the quicksort task, the paper mentions that the recursion is not purely tail recursive. How does this affect the verification procedure compared to the other tasks, which use tail recursion?
- The paper claims the NPI naturally supports recursion but notes that the original NPI authors didn't consider this aspect. Could the authors provide more specific details on why the original authors didn't explore this, and whether there were any implementation challenges in the NPI that prevented them from using recursion?
- The verification procedure requires constructing a verification set that covers all base cases and reduction rules. For tasks with very large input domains (e.g., arbitrary graphs for topological sort), how would the authors suggest constructing this verification set in practice?
