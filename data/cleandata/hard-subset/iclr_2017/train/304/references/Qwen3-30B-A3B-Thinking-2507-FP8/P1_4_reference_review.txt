

# Peer Review: "Making Neural Programming Architectures Generalize Via Recursion"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural programming by proposing recursion as a key abstraction to achieve provable perfect generalization. The authors argue that current neural programming approaches suffer from poor generalization due to learning spurious dependencies on training data characteristics rather than true program semantics. They demonstrate that by incorporating recursion into neural architectures (specifically the Neural Programmer-Interpreter), neural programs can learn the true program semantics and generalize perfectly to inputs of arbitrary complexity. 

The paper provides a novel verification procedure that, given a verification set covering all base cases and reduction rules, can prove perfect generalization. The authors show this approach successfully enables perfect generalization on four algorithmic tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort) with remarkably small training sets (e.g., 200 traces for addition, 100 for bubble sort). The key insight is that recursion reduces the generalization problem to verifying a finite number of base cases and reduction rules, making the verification tractable.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field with a novel and well-motivated approach to neural program generalization. The core idea of using recursion as a fundamental abstraction for neural programs is both theoretically sound and empirically validated. The verification procedure provides a significant methodological advancement that enables provable guarantees for neural programs.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in several key areas:

First, the motivation is exceptionally strong. The authors clearly articulate the fundamental limitation of current neural programming approaches (poor generalization beyond training complexity) and provide a compelling theoretical argument for why recursion is the necessary abstraction to address this problem. They correctly position their work as the first to investigate provable generalization properties of neural programs.

Second, the empirical results are robust and compelling. The recursive programs achieve perfect generalization on all tested tasks with very small training sets (e.g., the recursive addition program generalizes perfectly after training on just 5 examples), while non-recursive versions fail on longer inputs. The verification procedure is rigorously applied to all tasks, demonstrating that the recursive programs indeed generalize perfectly.

Third, the verification procedure itself is a major contribution. The paper provides a clear method for proving perfect generalization by verifying a finite set of base cases and reduction rules. This is a significant advancement over current approaches that rely on empirical generalization metrics. The authors correctly note that this verification is only possible for tasks with well-defined recursive structures (like sorting algorithms), and they appropriately discuss the limitations of their approach for more complex tasks.

The paper's contributions are well-placed in the literature. The authors thoroughly review prior work on neural programming and clearly distinguish their approach from existing methods. The connection to traditional programming concepts (recursion as a fundamental abstraction) is particularly strong and well-explained.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. A more detailed discussion of the limitations of the verification procedure. While the paper mentions that the approach doesn't scale to continuous input spaces (like MNIST), it would be helpful to discuss how the verification set size scales with task complexity and what the practical limits of this approach might be.

2. A clearer explanation of why the verification procedure works for recursive programs but not for non-recursive ones. The paper states this is because "the propagation of the hidden state in the core LSTM module makes reasoning difficult," but a more detailed explanation would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

3. A discussion of how the training procedure could be modified to learn recursive structures without requiring explicit recursive execution traces. The paper mentions this as a future direction but doesn't explore the challenges or potential approaches.

4. A comparison of the computational cost of the verification procedure versus the cost of training the neural program. For larger tasks, the verification might become expensive, and it would be helpful to know if this is a practical limitation.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims that recursion "makes it tractable to prove the generalization of neural programs." Could you elaborate on why non-recursive neural programs cannot be verified in the same way? Is this a fundamental limitation of neural networks, or could other techniques (like formal methods) be applied to non-recursive programs?

2. For the addition task, the verification set contains 20,181 input problems. How does this verification set size scale with the complexity of the task? For more complex tasks (like quicksort), how large might the verification set become?

3. The paper states that the recursive version of topological sort generalizes perfectly for graphs up to 120 vertices. What is the theoretical upper bound on the size of graphs that could be handled with this approach, and how does it relate to the verification set?

4. The paper trains on very small datasets (e.g., 5 examples for addition). How sensitive is the training to the specific examples in the training set? Would the recursive programs still generalize perfectly if the training examples were different but still covered the base cases?

5. The paper claims to be the first to provide provable guarantees for neural programs. Are there any prior works that implicitly achieved similar results but without the explicit verification framework? If not, why didn't prior work consider this approach?

This paper makes a substantial contribution to neural programming that addresses a fundamental limitation of the field. The insight that recursion enables provable generalization is both novel and impactful, and the empirical results strongly support the claims. The verification procedure provides a new methodological framework that could significantly influence how neural programs are developed and evaluated. I believe this paper will be of significant interest to the ICLR community and should be accepted.
