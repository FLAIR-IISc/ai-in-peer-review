

# Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## Summary of Contributions
This paper makes a bold and novel contribution to neural programming by introducing recursion as a fundamental abstraction for enabling provable perfect generalization. The authors correctly identify that current neural programming architectures (like Neural Programmer-Interpreter) fail to generalize beyond certain complexity thresholds due to learning spurious dependencies on training data characteristics (like input length) rather than the true program semantics. Their key insight is that recursion—fundamental to programming languages—enables neural programs to decompose problems into base cases and reduction rules, making it tractable to prove perfect generalization. They demonstrate this by modifying training traces to enable recursion in the NPI architecture, achieving 100% accuracy on inputs of arbitrary complexity for addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort tasks after training on extremely small datasets. Crucially, they provide a verification procedure to formally prove perfect generalization by checking only a finite set of base cases and reduction rules, which is a significant theoretical contribution.

## Decision
**Accept**

## Key Reasons for Acceptance
1. **Novel Core Insight**: The paper introduces a fundamentally new perspective on neural program generalization by recognizing recursion as a necessary abstraction. This is a bold, high-impact idea that hasn't been explored before in neural programming literature. The theoretical argument that recursion enables provable generalization is compelling and well-motivated.

2. **Strong Evidence for the Claim**: The empirical results across four different programming tasks demonstrate that recursive neural programs achieve perfect generalization from minimal training data, while non-recursive versions fail on longer inputs. The verification procedure they propose—proving perfect generalization by checking only finite base cases—represents a significant theoretical advance, making this the first paper to provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's core contribution is sufficiently novel and impactful to outweigh minor limitations. The authors correctly acknowledge that their verification procedure applies only to tasks with finite base cases (like programming tasks), not to more general domains like image recognition. This limitation is reasonable given the paper's specific focus on neural programming. 

The empirical validation is strong for the specific tasks tested. For example, their recursive topological sort model trained on just 6 examples (from graphs of size 5 and 7) generalizes perfectly to graphs of size 70, while the non-recursive model fails even at size 8. Similarly, recursive addition trained on 5 examples generalizes perfectly to arbitrarily long addition problems, while non-recursive fails. The verification procedure they propose is not just a theoretical curiosity—it's actually implemented and verified for each task.

The paper is well-placed in the literature. It correctly identifies the gap in current neural programming research (lack of provable generalization properties) and positions itself as the first to address this. The authors thoroughly engage with prior work, acknowledging that while NPI naturally supports recursion, previous work didn't explicitly leverage it for generalization.

## Additional Feedback for Improvement
1. **Clarify the Scope of Verification**: The paper could better articulate the specific conditions under which the verification procedure is applicable. A brief discussion of how to identify tasks with finite base cases versus those with infinite state spaces would strengthen the paper.

2. **Address Scalability of Verification**: While the authors acknowledge verification is limited to specific tasks, they could briefly discuss potential directions for extending verification to more complex domains (e.g., using symbolic execution techniques).

3. **Clarify the "Recursion" in NPI**: The paper states NPI naturally supports recursion, but doesn't fully explain why previous NPI work didn't discover this. A brief comparison of how their training traces differ from prior NPI work would help readers understand the specific novelty.

4. **Expand on the "Why"**: While the paper explains how recursion helps, a slightly deeper theoretical discussion of why recursion (as opposed to other program structures) is uniquely suited for neural program generalization would strengthen the contribution.

## Clarifying Questions for Authors
1. How would you extend your verification procedure to handle tasks where the base cases might be infinite (e.g., certain forms of program synthesis with symbolic inputs)?

2. In the verification procedure, you state "V should also contain the necessary reductions, which corresponds to making the recursive calls at the correct time." Could you elaborate on how you ensure the model learns the correct timing for recursive calls during training, especially for non-tail-recursive cases like quicksort?

3. Your paper states this is "the first time one can provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs." How do you reconcile this with the fact that some prior work (e.g., on neural symbolic execution) might have implicitly achieved similar guarantees for specific tasks?

This paper makes a significant and novel contribution to neural programming by introducing recursion as a fundamental abstraction for achieving provable generalization. The theoretical insight, combined with strong empirical validation and the novel verification procedure, makes it a compelling contribution to the field that deserves publication at ICLR.
