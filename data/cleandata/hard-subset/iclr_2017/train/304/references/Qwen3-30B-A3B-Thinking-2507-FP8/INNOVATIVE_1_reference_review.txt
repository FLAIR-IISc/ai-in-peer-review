

# Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## Summary of Contributions
This paper makes a bold and novel contribution to the field of neural programming by proposing that recursion is a fundamental abstraction necessary for neural programs to achieve perfect generalization. The authors demonstrate that by incorporating recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter architecture through modified training traces, they can learn neural programs that achieve provably perfect generalization on several tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) from extremely small training sets. Crucially, they introduce a verification procedure that proves neural programs generalize perfectly by testing only a finite set of base cases and reduction rules, which is the first time such provable guarantees have been provided for neural programs. This represents a significant conceptual shift from current approaches that rely on empirical generalization testing without formal guarantees.

## Decision
Accept. The paper introduces a highly novel and theoretically grounded approach to a fundamental problem in neural programming. The core idea of using recursion to enable provable generalization is bold, conceptually important, and addresses a critical limitation of current neural programming architectures.

## Supporting Arguments
This paper excels in novelty and conceptual contribution, which I prioritize over methodological completeness. The central insight—that recursion is necessary for neural programs to generalize well and that it enables provable guarantees—is both novel and profound. The authors convincingly demonstrate this through multiple tasks with small training sets, showing that recursive neural programs achieve perfect generalization where non-recursive versions fail completely.

The verification procedure they propose is particularly significant—it transforms the field from empirical generalization testing to formal verification. While the approach has limitations (e.g., verification set size may be prohibitive for very complex tasks), the paper's core contribution of enabling provable generalization is transformative. The paper's claims are well-supported by empirical results showing perfect generalization on all tested tasks from minimal training data, and the theoretical justification for why recursion enables this is sound.

This is a paradigm shift in how we think about neural program generalization, moving from "does it work on longer inputs?" to "can we prove it works on all inputs?" This conceptual advance outweighs the paper's minor methodological limitations, such as the reliance on explicitly constructed recursive traces for training.

## Additional Feedback
1. The claim of being "the first" to investigate provable generalization properties of neural programs needs careful verification against the literature. The authors should explicitly address prior work on formal verification of neural networks (e.g., using symbolic methods) to clarify their novelty claim.

2. While the verification procedure is elegant, the paper should discuss the practical limitations more thoroughly. For tasks with very large input domains (e.g., image classification), the verification set might become prohibitively large. The authors should discuss how this approach might scale to more complex domains.

3. The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of how the recursive training traces were generated. The authors mention "future work" to decrease supervision, but it's unclear how the current approach could be extended to learn recursion without explicit recursive traces. A brief discussion of potential mechanisms for learning recursion from non-recursive traces would strengthen the paper.

4. The claim that "NPI naturally supports recursion" needs more justification. The paper states this as fact but doesn't demonstrate how the original NPI architecture would have naturally supported recursion without the modified training traces. A brief explanation of why the original NPI didn't learn recursion despite this architectural capability would be valuable.

## Questions for Authors
1. How would you extend this verification procedure to handle tasks with continuous input spaces (e.g., image classification), where the base cases might be infinite?

2. Could you provide more detail on the relationship between the size of the verification set and the complexity of the program being verified? For instance, how does the verification set size scale with the depth of recursion?

3. The paper claims that the recursive NPI "naturally supports recursion" even though the original NPI didn't use it. What specific architectural feature of NPI makes recursion possible without modification, and why didn't the original authors exploit this?

This paper introduces a genuinely novel and important direction for neural programming research, with the potential to significantly impact how we design and verify neural programs. The core idea of using recursion to enable provable generalization is sufficiently bold and novel to warrant acceptance, even with minor methodological limitations.
