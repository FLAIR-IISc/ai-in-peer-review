- Paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural architectures to enable provable perfect generalization.  
- Authors modify the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) to learn recursive programs for addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Key claims: recursion decomposes problems into base cases/reduction rules, enables finite verification of perfect generalization, and requires minimal training data.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Motivation: Correctly identifies existing neural programming generalization failures and argues recursion as a principled remedy.  
- Methodology: Training traces augmented with recursion let NPI learn recursive programs without architectural changes.  
- Verification: By verifying finite base cases and reduction rules, the authors formally prove perfect generalization for their tasks.  
- Empirical results: Recursive models generalize from tiny training instances to arbitrarily large inputs (e.g., 1-digit to 5000-digit addition), while non-recursive models fail.  
- Example verification scale: addition required verifying 20,181 inputs to guarantee generalization.  
- Limitation: Verification approach is restricted to tasks with finite base cases and may not scale to continuous or more complex domains.  
- Request for stronger theory: Paper lacks formal analysis explaining why recursion yields better generalization (inductive-bias justification).  
- Data-efficiency request: Authors should quantify training-set size advantages versus baselines (comparative metrics/table).  
- Clarify implementation differences: Need to reconcile topological sort results with prior work reporting better generalization.  
- Question: How to adapt verification for tasks with infinite base cases (e.g., real-valued sorting)?  
- Question: What direct evidence shows non-recursive models learn spurious dependencies beyond failure on larger inputs?  
- Question: How did quicksort’s non-tail recursion affect learning difficulty and what challenges arose?  
- Question: Why is the verification set larger than the training set (e.g., 20,181 vs. 200 traces) and how does verification size relate to task complexity?
