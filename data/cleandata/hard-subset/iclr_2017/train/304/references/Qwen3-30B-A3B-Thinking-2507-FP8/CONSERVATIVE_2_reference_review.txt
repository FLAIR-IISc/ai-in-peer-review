

# Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

The paper proposes that incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures enables "provably perfect generalization" for specific tasks. The authors claim that by modifying training traces to include recursive elements, their Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) model can learn recursive programs that generalize perfectly to inputs of arbitrary complexity for four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. They argue that recursion allows them to verify base cases and reduction rules, providing a finite set of inputs to prove perfect generalization. The paper claims this is the first time "provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs" have been achieved.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes an overstated claim of "provably perfect generalization" without sufficient theoretical grounding or empirical validation against established baselines. The verification procedure described is limited to specific tasks with small input domains, not a general theoretical guarantee.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's central claim of "provably perfect generalization" is misleading and not rigorously supported. The authors verify a finite set of inputs (20,181 for addition) to claim generalization across all possible inputs, but this verification is limited to specific tasks with manageable input spaces. As the paper acknowledges, this approach is "infeasible" for most real-world problems involving continuous or large input domains. Their verification procedure is essentially a standard testing approach, not a new theoretical guarantee as claimed.

The paper lacks thorough empirical validation against established neural programming approaches. The authors only compare against non-recursive versions of the same NPI architecture, not against state-of-the-art neural programming models like Neural Turing Machines, Differentiable Neural Computers, or Neural GPUs. Without these comparisons, it's impossible to determine whether recursion is truly superior or if other approaches could achieve similar results.

The paper also fails to explain why the original NPI authors didn't incorporate recursion, given that the architecture "naturally supports recursion" as claimed. This oversight weakens the novelty claim, as it suggests recursion was an obvious consideration that was previously overlooked.

The verification procedure for bubble sort relies on a modified domain-specific encoder that exposes comparison results directly, simplifying the problem significantly. This modification reduces the problem's realism and makes verification easier, but it's not clear how this would translate to real-world applications.

## 4. Additional Feedback

1. **Clarify the "provably perfect generalization" claim**: The paper should explicitly state that this is limited to specific tasks with small input domains, not a general theoretical guarantee. The current phrasing is misleading and overstates the contribution.

2. **Expand empirical validation**: Compare against multiple state-of-the-art neural programming architectures, not just non-recursive versions of NPI. This would provide stronger evidence that recursion is truly necessary for perfect generalization.

3. **Address scalability**: The paper should discuss how the verification procedure scales with problem complexity. For example, how large would the verification set need to be for sorting arrays of 100 elements versus 1000 elements?

4. **Explain the NPI oversight**: Provide a clear explanation for why the original NPI authors didn't incorporate recursion, given the architecture "naturally supports recursion" as claimed. This is a significant gap in the literature review.

5. **Discuss limitations more thoroughly**: The paper acknowledges that verification is infeasible for tasks with large input domains, but this limitation should be discussed more prominently as a fundamental constraint of the approach.

**Questions for Authors**:
1. How does your verification procedure scale with problem complexity? What would be the verification set size for sorting arrays of 100 elements versus 1000 elements?
2. Can you provide evidence that recursion is necessary for perfect generalization, or is it possible that other approaches (like better curriculum learning) could achieve similar results?
3. Why didn't the original NPI authors incorporate recursion, given the architecture "naturally supports recursion" as claimed?
4. How would your approach handle a task like sorting images or processing natural language, where the input domain is continuous and potentially infinite?
5. Can you provide evidence that your recursive training procedure generalizes to other neural programming architectures beyond NPI, or is it specific to this particular architecture?

The paper presents an interesting idea that recursion might help with generalization in neural programming, but the claims are overstated and the evidence is insufficient to support the central contribution of "provably perfect generalization." Without stronger empirical validation against established baselines and a more nuanced understanding of the limitations of their approach, this work does not meet ICLR's standards for novelty and rigor.
