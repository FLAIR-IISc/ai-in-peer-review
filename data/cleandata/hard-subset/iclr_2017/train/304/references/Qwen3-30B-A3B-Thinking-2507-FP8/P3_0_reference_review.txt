

# Peer Review: MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper tackles the critical problem of generalization in neural program learning. The authors argue that current neural programming architectures (like Neural Programmer-Interpreter) fail to generalize beyond a certain input complexity because they learn spurious dependencies on training examples (such as input length) rather than the true program semantics. 

The key contribution is proposing to explicitly incorporate recursion into neural architectures as a solution. The authors demonstrate that by modifying training traces to include recursive elements, the NPI model can learn recursive programs that achieve provably perfect generalization. They show this with four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort), where the recursive versions achieve 100% accuracy on inputs of arbitrary complexity after training on a very small number of examples. Crucially, they claim to be able to provide "provably perfect generalization" by verifying the model on a finite set of base cases and reduction rules.

The paper is well-written, makes a clear connection to prior work, and presents a novel verification procedure that allows them to formally prove generalization properties for their recursive neural programs. This is indeed a significant contribution to the field of neural programming, particularly for tasks with recursive structure.

## 2. Decision

**Decision: Reject**

**Key reasons for rejection:**
1. The "provably perfect generalization" claim is significantly overstated and limited to a narrow class of problems with well-defined base cases and reduction rules.
2. The approach requires explicit recursive traces during training, which is a form of strong supervision that limits its applicability to tasks where such traces can be constructed.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

### The "Provably Perfect Generalization" Claim is Misleading

The paper's most ambitious claim is that they can provide "provably perfect generalization" for neural programs. However, this claim is only valid for tasks with a small, finite number of base cases and reduction rules. The paper itself acknowledges this limitation: "For tasks with very large input domains, such as ones involving MNIST digits or speech samples, the state space of base cases and reduction rules could be prohibitively large, possibly infinite."

This is a critical limitation that the paper fails to adequately address. The term "provably perfect generalization" suggests a general method applicable to any neural program, but in reality, the verification procedure only works for problems with a small, well-defined verification set. For the vast majority of real-world programming tasks (where input domains are large and continuous), this approach is not feasible.

The authors present the verification procedure as a novel contribution, but it's essentially a manual process of constructing a verification set that covers all possible base cases and reduction rules. This is not a general verification method but rather a task-specific verification that only works for problems with small verification sets.

### The Training Approach Requires Explicit Supervision

The paper states: "we found that changing the NPI training traces is a simple way to enable this" (Section 3.2). This means the authors are providing explicit recursive execution traces to the model during training, which is a form of strong supervision. The model isn't discovering recursion on its own; it's being explicitly shown how to implement recursion.

This is a significant limitation because it means the approach is only applicable to tasks where we can construct these recursive traces. The authors acknowledge this: "In future work, we would like to decrease supervision and construct models that are capable of coming up with recursive abstractions themselves."

The paper doesn't demonstrate that the model can learn recursion without explicit recursive traces, which is a crucial aspect of the claim that recursion enables generalization. The empirical results are impressive, but they're only possible because the training data already contains the recursive structure.

### Limited Scope of Demonstration

The paper demonstrates the approach on four specific tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) that happen to have natural recursive structures. However, the paper doesn't show that the approach works for tasks without natural recursive structure (e.g., image processing tasks, natural language processing tasks, or other programming tasks that don't decompose naturally into recursive subproblems).

The authors claim this is the first time "provably perfect generalization" has been achieved for neural programs, but this is only true for these specific tasks. For most programming tasks, this approach would not work at all.

### Inadequate Comparison to Prior Work

The paper compares its recursive NPI to non-recursive NPI, but doesn't compare to other neural programming approaches that have been specifically designed for better generalization. For example, the Neural GPU (Kaiser & Sutskever, 2015) was explicitly designed to generalize to longer inputs through its architecture. The paper also doesn't compare to the Neural Programmer (Neelakantan et al., 2015), which uses a different approach to program learning.

Without these comparisons, it's hard to assess whether the gains from recursion are truly significant compared to other approaches that might already handle generalization better.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of neural program learning, it could be significantly improved by:

1. **Clarifying the scope of the "provably perfect generalization" claim**: The paper should explicitly state that this only applies to tasks with a small, finite verification set (i.e., problems with a well-defined recursive structure where base cases and reduction rules can be enumerated). The current wording suggests a general solution that doesn't exist.

2. **Discussing limitations more thoroughly**: The paper should dedicate a section to discussing the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding tasks without natural recursive structure. The authors should acknowledge that this is not a general solution to the neural program generalization problem.

3. **Exploring less supervised approaches**: The authors should include experiments showing how the model might learn recursion from non-recursive traces or with partial supervision, as they mention as future work. This would strengthen the claim that recursion enables better generalization.

4. **Comparing to other neural programming approaches**: The paper should include a comparison to Neural GPU (Kaiser & Sutskever, 2015) and other approaches that have demonstrated better generalization properties, to provide context for the significance of their results.

5. **Clarifying the relationship to formal verification**: The paper claims this is the first time "provably perfect generalization" has been provided for neural programs. The authors should clarify how this differs from formal verification techniques for neural networks (e.g., for safety-critical systems), which have been studied for some time.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding of the paper and assess its claims:

1. How would you apply this approach to a programming task that doesn't have a natural recursive structure (e.g., a task that requires a for-loop over a fixed-size array)? Would it be possible to force a recursive formulation for such a task?

2. What are the specific conditions that make a programming task amenable to this approach (i.e., when can we construct a finite verification set)? Can you provide a precise characterization of the class of problems for which this approach works?

3. The paper claims this is the first time "provably perfect generalization" has been provided for neural programs. How does this differ from formal verification of neural networks in safety-critical systems (e.g., for autonomous vehicles), where similar verification techniques have been applied?

4. How would you handle a task where the base cases are infinite (e.g., sorting arrays of arbitrary length, where the base case is a single element array)? The paper states that "for problems where the base cases may be extremely large or infinite, recursion can still help reduce the problem of generalization to these two aspects," but doesn't explain how this works in practice for infinite base cases.

5. The paper trains on very small datasets (e.g., 200 traces for addition with max length 3). How would the model's performance change if you trained it on a larger dataset without the recursive traces? Would the non-recursive model still fail to generalize, or would it learn the recursive structure on its own with more data?

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents an interesting and well-executed approach for neural program learning on specific tasks with natural recursive structure. The verification procedure is novel and valuable for understanding neural program behavior. However, the paper overstates its contribution by presenting the verification procedure as a general solution to the neural program generalization problem when it's actually limited to a narrow class of problems. The "provably perfect generalization" claim is misleading and needs significant qualification. Without addressing these limitations and clarifying the scope of the contribution, the paper's impact is overstated and the claim of being the first to provide provable guarantees for neural programs is inaccurate.
