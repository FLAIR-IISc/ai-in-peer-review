

# Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a compelling contribution by addressing a fundamental limitation in neural programming: poor generalization beyond training complexity. The authors propose that explicitly incorporating recursion into neural architectures enables provable perfect generalization, which they demonstrate across four programming tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort). Their key insight is that recursion reduces problem complexity to base cases and reduction rules, making verification tractable. The paper provides a novel verification procedure that, given coverage of base cases and reduction rules, proves perfect generalization for the learned neural programs. This is the first time such provable guarantees have been established for neural programs, with the recursive NPI model achieving 100% accuracy on arbitrarily long inputs after training on extremely small datasets.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes an interesting contribution with a clear theoretical insight, but the empirical claims are not sufficiently supported by rigorous evaluation, and the limitations of the approach are not adequately addressed.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The key reason for rejection is that the paper's empirical results lack scientific rigor in several critical areas. While the recursive models achieve perfect generalization on the specific tasks tested, the verification procedure is limited to problems with very small input spaces (e.g., addition with small digit values, small sorting arrays). The paper acknowledges this limitation (Section 5), noting the verification approach is infeasible for large input domains like MNIST, which significantly restricts the applicability of the claimed "provably perfect generalization" across realistic programming tasks.

Furthermore, the paper does not adequately address why the non-recursive models fail to learn the true program semantics. The claim that non-recursive models learn "spurious dependencies" lacks direct evidence. The paper shows that non-recursive models fail on longer inputs, but doesn't demonstrate that these failures stem from learning incorrect program semantics rather than simply insufficient training data or architectural limitations.

The paper also overstates the novelty of its verification procedure. While the specific application to neural programs is novel, the general approach of verifying recursive programs by checking base cases and reductions is standard in program verification (e.g., mathematical induction). The paper should more clearly position this work within the broader context of program verification.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen this work for future submission, I recommend:

1. **Expand the evaluation to more challenging tasks** that demonstrate the approach's utility beyond small, structured problems. The current results only cover problems with small input domains where verification is trivially feasible.

2. **Provide stronger evidence for the "spurious dependencies" hypothesis**. Instead of merely showing that non-recursive models fail on longer inputs, include analysis of what the models actually learn (e.g., by examining the hidden states or attention patterns).

3. **Clarify the novelty claim** by more thoroughly comparing with existing program verification approaches and explaining how this specifically advances the field of neural program verification.

4. **Address the supervision requirement** more directly. The paper notes that recursive traces are needed for training, but doesn't discuss how to learn recursion without explicit recursive traces. This is a significant limitation that should be acknowledged and addressed in future work.

5. **Provide a more detailed analysis of the verification set construction**. The paper mentions it's "difficult to analytically determine" verification sets for most tasks, yet it claims perfect generalization. A clearer discussion of the relationship between verification set size and the scope of provable generalization would strengthen the claims.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How would your verification procedure scale to a problem like sorting a 1000-element array? What would be the size of the verification set required to prove generalization for all possible inputs?

2. Could you provide evidence that the non-recursive models fail to learn the true program semantics, rather than simply being unable to handle longer inputs due to architectural limitations?

3. How does your verification procedure differ from standard approaches to verifying recursive programs (e.g., mathematical induction or Hoare logic), and why is it specifically suited for neural programs?

4. What would be the minimal amount of supervision required to train a recursive neural program without providing explicit recursive traces? Can you propose a strategy for learning recursion from non-recursive traces?

The paper presents an interesting and potentially important insight about the role of recursion in neural programming, but the current evaluation does not sufficiently support the broad claims about provable generalization. With the suggested improvements, this could become a strong contribution to the field.
