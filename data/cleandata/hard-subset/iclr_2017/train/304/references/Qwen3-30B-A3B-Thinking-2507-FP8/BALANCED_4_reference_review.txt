

# Review of "Making Neural Programming Architectures Generalize via Recursion"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper tackles the critical problem of poor generalization in neural programming architectures, where models trained on simpler inputs fail to generalize to more complex inputs beyond a certain threshold. The authors propose incorporating recursion into neural architectures as a solution, arguing that recursion enables provable guarantees of perfect generalization. They demonstrate this approach by modifying the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture to learn recursive programs for four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. The paper claims to be the first to provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs, which they achieve through a verification procedure that tests base cases and reduction rules.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes an interesting contribution by connecting recursion to neural program generalization, but the claims of "provably perfect generalization" and the novelty of the approach are overstated, and the empirical evidence does not sufficiently support these claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's key weakness is its overstatement of the novelty and scope of its contribution. While recursion is indeed a powerful concept in programming, the paper fails to establish why recursion is fundamentally necessary for generalization rather than just being a useful technique. The authors claim this is "the first time one can provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs," but they don't adequately explain why this wasn't possible before with non-recursive approaches, or why the verification procedure they propose isn't applicable to other neural program architectures.

The empirical results show that recursive versions generalize better than non-recursive versions on specific tasks, but the paper doesn't adequately address why the non-recursive versions fail. The claim that non-recursive programs learn "spurious and overly complex representations" isn't sufficiently supported by evidence—there's no analysis of what the non-recursive models actually learned or why they failed on longer inputs.

Most significantly, the paper's verification procedure is limited to very specific tasks with small input spaces. For example, the verification set for addition contains only 20,181 input problems, and the paper acknowledges that "for tasks with very large input domains, such as ones involving MNIST digits or speech samples, the state space of base cases and reduction rules could be prohibitively large." This severely limits the applicability of the "provably perfect generalization" claim to real-world problems, which the paper doesn't adequately address.

The paper also lacks a clear theoretical justification for why recursion leads to better generalization. The argument is largely empirical, with limited explanation of the underlying principles.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. More clearly distinguish between what is truly novel (the specific application of recursion to NPI) versus what is a general principle (recursion as a tool for program generalization). The paper overstates the novelty.

2. Provide a more detailed analysis of what the non-recursive models actually learned, including why they failed on longer inputs. This would strengthen the motivation for recursion.

3. Discuss the limitations of the verification procedure more thoroughly, particularly for tasks with larger input spaces. The paper should acknowledge that the "provably perfect generalization" claim is limited to specific task domains with small input spaces.

4. Compare with alternative approaches to improving generalization (beyond curriculum learning), such as more sophisticated architectures or training procedures, to better establish the value of recursion.

5. Provide a clearer explanation of how the training traces were constructed to incorporate recursion, including whether this approach is generally applicable to other neural programming architectures.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims that "none of the current approaches to neural programming provide a method or even aim to enable provable guarantees about generalization." Can you provide a more nuanced discussion of why this is the case, and how your verification procedure differs from previous work on neural program verification (if any)?

2. The verification procedure for addition requires 20,181 input problems. For a task like sorting, where the input size could be arbitrarily large, how would you scale this verification procedure? What would be the verification set size for sorting arrays of length 100, for example?

3. The paper states that "the NPI model naturally supports recursion (even though the authors of NPI did not consider this aspect explicitly)." How do you reconcile this claim with the fact that the original NPI paper didn't use recursion, and the authors didn't claim to have discovered this capability?

4. The paper claims that recursive programs "learn the true program semantics" while non-recursive programs learn "spurious and overly complex representations." Can you provide evidence or analysis showing that the recursive programs are learning the true semantics, rather than just a different kind of representation that happens to generalize better on these specific tasks?
