

# Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

This paper tackles the critical problem of generalization in neural programming architectures. The authors identify that existing neural program learning models (including the Neural Programmer-Interpreter, NPI) suffer from poor generalization beyond a certain complexity threshold, even when using curriculum learning. They argue this occurs because these models learn spurious dependencies on training examples (like input length) rather than the true program semantics.

The paper's key contribution is proposing to incorporate recursion into neural architectures as a solution to this problem. The authors claim that recursion enables neural programs to learn the true program semantics, generalize perfectly to inputs of arbitrary complexity, and provide provable guarantees about their behavior. They demonstrate this by modifying the NPI architecture to learn recursive programs for four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. Crucially, they show that their recursive neural programs achieve perfect generalization from a very small number of training examples and can be verified to generalize perfectly by checking only a finite number of base cases and reduction rules.

The paper makes the strong claim that this is the first time one can provide "provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs," which represents a significant conceptual advance in the field.

## 2. Decision

**Accept.** The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural programming with a well-motivated approach that addresses a critical limitation of existing methods. The empirical results are compelling and the verification procedure represents a novel contribution.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

### Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Statement
The paper clearly identifies a fundamental problem in neural programming: the inability of existing models to generalize beyond a certain complexity level. The authors provide strong evidence from prior work (Vinyals et al., Kaiser & Sutskever, Reed & de Freitas, Graves et al., Zaremba et al.) that this is a widespread limitation. The problem statement is well-justified and the authors effectively demonstrate the limitations of existing approaches through their analysis of why curriculum learning fails.

### Novel and Well-Justified Approach
The core idea of using recursion to enable provable generalization is novel and well-justified. The authors provide a strong theoretical foundation for why recursion should help (reducing the problem to base cases and reduction rules that can be verified), and they correctly identify that NPI naturally supports recursion but wasn't previously used for this purpose. The paper makes a clear connection between programming language theory (recursion as a fundamental concept) and neural program learning, which is a compelling interdisciplinary insight.

### Strong Empirical Results
The empirical results are impressive and directly support the paper's claims. The recursive models generalize perfectly to inputs of arbitrary complexity from very small training sets:
- Addition: Trained on 3-digit numbers, generalizes to 5000-digit numbers
- Topological sort: Trained on a single 5-vertex graph, generalizes to graphs of size 70
- Bubble sort: Full recursive version sorts all arrays, while non-recursive fails beyond length 8
- Quicksort: Recursive version sorts all arrays, while non-recursive fails beyond length 11

These results are much stronger than prior work, which typically showed degradation in performance beyond certain input lengths.

### Novel Verification Procedure
The paper's verification procedure is a significant contribution. By showing that recursion reduces the generalization problem to a finite verification set (covering all base cases and reduction rules), the authors provide a practical way to prove perfect generalization. This is a major step beyond previous work that couldn't provide such guarantees.

### Clear Technical Contributions
The paper makes several concrete technical contributions:
1. A clear specification of how to make NPI learn recursive programs (by modifying training traces)
2. A verification procedure for proving perfect generalization
3. Demonstrations on four different programming tasks
4. The insight that recursion is essential for provable generalization

### Addressing Potential Weaknesses
The paper acknowledges limitations of their verification procedure (not feasible for tasks with very large input domains like MNIST), and they appropriately focus on tasks where the verification set can be constructed. They also properly cite prior work and position their contribution within the literature.

## 4. Additional Feedback

### Strengths to Highlight
- The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation for why recursion should help with generalization
- The empirical results are convincing and directly address the problem statement
- The verification procedure is a genuinely novel contribution that opens up new research directions
- The paper makes the important distinction between learning the true program semantics versus spurious dependencies

### Areas for Improvement
1. **Clarify the Verification Procedure**: The paper states they "randomly generate input candidates until they completely cover the base cases and reduction rules." It would be helpful to provide more detail on how they ensure completeness. For tasks like topological sort, where they claim to have constructed a verification set of size 73, it would be valuable to explain how they know this set covers all necessary cases.

2. **More Discussion on Verification Complexity**: While the paper acknowledges that verification is infeasible for large input domains, it would strengthen the paper to provide a more thorough discussion of the computational complexity of the verification procedure and how it scales with task complexity.

3. **Comparison to Alternative Recursive Approaches**: The paper focuses on modifying NPI training traces to enable recursion. It would be valuable to briefly discuss how this approach compares to other potential ways to incorporate recursion (e.g., architectural changes to the neural network itself).

4. **Theoretical Justification for Verification**: While the empirical verification is compelling, it would strengthen the paper to provide a more rigorous theoretical justification for why verifying the base cases and reduction rules guarantees perfect generalization for all inputs.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. For the bubble sort task, the paper states that partial recursion (only adding a tail recursive call to BUBBLESORT) is insufficient for perfect generalization, while full recursion (adding recursive calls to BSTEP and LSHIFT as well) is required. Can you provide more insight into why exactly the partial recursion isn't sufficient? Is it because the non-recursive versions of BSTEP and LSHIFT have spurious dependencies on input length?

2. For the addition task, the paper claims to analytically construct a verification set of 20,181 input problems. Could you provide more detail about how this verification set was constructed and why it's sufficient to guarantee perfect generalization for all possible addition problems?

3. The paper states that "the training set can often be considerably smaller than the verification set." For the addition task, the training set has 200 traces (max length 3), while the verification set has 20,181 problems. How do you reconcile this with the claim that the model generalizes perfectly from a small training set? What is the relationship between the training set size and the verification set size?

4. The paper acknowledges that verification is infeasible for tasks with large input domains. For tasks like sorting where the input is a sequence of numbers, how would you envision extending this approach to handle larger input spaces? Would it require additional architectural changes or different verification strategies?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neural programming. The approach is well-motivated, the empirical results are compelling, and the verification procedure represents a novel and valuable contribution. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to ICLR.
