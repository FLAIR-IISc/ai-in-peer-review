

# Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims
The paper proposes that incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures enables provably perfect generalization for neural programs. The authors argue that current neural program learning approaches fail to generalize beyond certain complexity thresholds due to learning spurious dependencies on training data characteristics (like input length). They claim their approach—modifying training traces to explicitly include recursive elements—enables the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) model to learn recursive programs that achieve perfect generalization with formal guarantees. They demonstrate this on four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort), claiming their method is the first to provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs.

## 2. Decision
Reject

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision
The paper makes several overstated claims that are not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented. The primary concerns are:

First, the paper's claim of "provable guarantees of perfect generalization" is misleading. While the authors verify their model on carefully constructed verification sets for each task (e.g., 20,181 inputs for addition), these verification sets are tiny compared to the infinite input space of the tasks. The verification procedure only confirms correctness for a finite number of inputs, not for all possible inputs as claimed. For example, the addition task verification set (20,181 inputs) represents a minuscule fraction of all possible addition problems. The paper fails to demonstrate how their verification procedure would scale to more complex programming tasks with vastly larger input spaces.

Second, the experimental setup is problematic. The training sets are extremely small (200 traces for addition, 100 for bubble sort), yet the authors claim their recursive approach "out-performs previous generalization results" without proper comparison to strong baselines. They don't compare against other methods that might achieve good generalization (e.g., curriculum learning with appropriate architectures), making their "out-performing" claim unsubstantiated. The paper also fails to address how their approach would scale to more complex programming tasks beyond the four simple, highly structured tasks tested.

Third, the paper's claim of novelty is overstated. The assertion that this is "the first time one can provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs" ignores relevant prior work on neural program verification, which the authors don't properly address. The paper's approach of modifying training traces to include recursion is relatively simple, and the authors don't sufficiently demonstrate why this approach couldn't be applied to other architectures or why it represents a fundamental advance rather than a minor modification.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
The paper would benefit from several key improvements to strengthen its claims:

1. Provide a more rigorous comparison with strong baselines, including recent approaches to neural program learning that might already achieve good generalization. The paper's claim of "out-performing previous generalization results" is unsubstantiated without such comparisons.

2. Clarify the limitations of the verification procedure. Explicitly state that the "provable" generalization is limited to the specific input domains covered by the verification set, not to all possible inputs as the paper suggests.

3. Address relevant prior work on neural program verification more thoroughly. The paper should acknowledge and distinguish its approach from existing verification techniques to properly establish its novelty.

4. Demonstrate how the approach would scale to more complex programming tasks. The four tasks tested are highly structured with limited input spaces; the paper doesn't show how their method would work for more challenging programming problems.

5. Consider whether the verification set construction process could be automated or made more robust for larger tasks. The current approach relies on manual construction of verification sets, which isn't scalable to real-world programming tasks.

Questions for the authors:
1. How do you justify the claim of "provable guarantees of perfect generalization" when your verification set for addition contains only 20,181 inputs, while the input space is infinite (all possible addition problems)?

2. Why do you claim your approach is "the first" to provide provable guarantees when there are other works on neural program verification (e.g., [cite specific papers]) that might have similar claims?

3. How would your verification procedure scale to a task like sorting arbitrary arrays of 100+ elements, where the verification set would need to be exponentially larger?

4. What evidence do you have that your approach would generalize to more complex programming tasks beyond the four simple ones tested in this paper?
