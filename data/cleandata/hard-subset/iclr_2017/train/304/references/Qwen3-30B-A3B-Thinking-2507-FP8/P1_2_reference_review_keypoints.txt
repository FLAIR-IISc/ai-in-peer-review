- Paper argues recursion is necessary for provable generalization in neural program synthesis.  
- Authors modify Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) training traces to implement recursion.  
- Theoretical claim: recursion decomposes problems into finite base cases and reductions enabling verification.  
- Empirical claim: recursive NPI achieves perfect generalization on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort with very small training sets.  
- Reported empirical highlights: addition generalizes to 5000-digit numbers from 200 traces; bubble sort succeeds beyond length 8 where non-recursive fails; topological sort works up to 70 vertices from a 5-vertex example.  
- Introduces a verification procedure that can prove perfect generalization by testing a finite set of base cases and reductions.  
- Reviewer recommends acceptance due to novelty (first provable guarantees for neural programs) and strong empirical results.  
- Theoretical justification and empirical evidence are judged compelling and well-supported.  
- Verification procedure is framed as a major advancement over prior empirical-only generalization metrics.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and situates itself appropriately in prior work.  
- Request clarification why the verification set for addition (20,181 inputs) is larger than its 200-example training set.  
- Ask for a deeper explanation of why the NPI architecture “naturally supports recursion” beyond having a stack.  
- Suggest discussing limitations and possible extensions/approximations of the verification procedure for large input domains (e.g., MNIST, speech).  
- Recommend analysis of learned model internals (attention, hidden states) to explain why recursion improves generalization.  
- Question: why is the addition verification set so large relative to training—task-specific or general?  
- Question: how does the verification set ensure coverage of all possible timings of recursive calls (e.g., pivot-dependent quicksort behavior)?  
- Question: beyond adding recursive traces, how is training modified so the model learns when to make recursive calls without explicit timing supervision?
