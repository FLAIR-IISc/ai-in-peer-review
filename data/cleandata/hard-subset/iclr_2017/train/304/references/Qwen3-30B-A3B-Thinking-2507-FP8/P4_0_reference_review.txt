

1. Summary: This paper proposes a novel approach to enable neural programming architectures to achieve provable perfect generalization by incorporating recursion. The authors demonstrate that recursive neural programs, trained on appropriately modified traces containing recursive elements, can generalize perfectly to inputs of arbitrary complexity for several programming tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort). They provide a verification procedure that proves generalization by testing base cases and reduction rules, which is feasible due to the recursive structure. The paper claims this is the first time provable guarantees of perfect generalization have been provided for neural programs, and they empirically demonstrate that their recursive programs outperform non-recursive counterparts by achieving 100% accuracy on inputs far beyond training complexity.

2. Decision: Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural programming by introducing recursion to enable provable generalization, which is well-motivated and technically sound.

3. Supporting arguments: The paper clearly identifies a fundamental limitation in neural programming (poor generalization beyond certain complexity thresholds due to spurious dependencies) and proposes a well-motivated solution (recursion). The verification procedure is theoretically sound and enables provable generalization guarantees, which is a substantial contribution. The empirical results are convincing: for all four tasks, the recursive programs achieve perfect generalization on inputs of arbitrary complexity (e.g., addition with 5000+ digits, sorting arrays beyond length 100) while non-recursive versions fail. The training data is extremely small (e.g., 200 traces for addition), yet the recursive programs generalize perfectly. The verification procedure is well-explained and provides a clear path to proving generalization for recursive neural programs, which has not been possible before.

4. Additional feedback: The paper would benefit from more explicit discussion of the limitations of the verification procedure, particularly regarding scalability to larger tasks. It would be helpful to quantify how the size of the verification set grows with problem complexity. The paper should also clarify the trade-offs between the supervision required to generate recursive traces and the benefits of the resulting provable generalization. The modification to the bubble sort domain-specific encoder (exposing comparisons directly) is an important detail that should be more thoroughly justified, as it affects the generalizability to arbitrary comparable elements.

Questions for authors: 
1. How would your verification procedure scale for tasks with very large or infinite base cases (e.g., sorting arrays with arbitrary elements, rather than just numbers 0-9)?
2. Can you provide more details on how the verification set size scales with problem complexity for your tasks, and what computational resources are required for this verification process?
3. For the bubble sort task, you modified the domain-specific encoder to expose comparisons directly. How does this affect the model's ability to generalize to inputs outside the specific numeric range used in training (e.g., negative numbers or non-integer elements)?
