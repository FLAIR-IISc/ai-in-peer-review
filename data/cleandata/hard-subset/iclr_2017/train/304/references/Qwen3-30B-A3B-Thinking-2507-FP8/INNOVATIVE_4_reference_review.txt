

# Review for "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper makes a bold and novel contribution to the field of neural programming by introducing recursion as a key mechanism for achieving provable generalization in neural programs. The authors correctly identify a fundamental limitation in existing neural programming approaches: they fail to generalize beyond a certain complexity threshold due to learning spurious dependencies rather than the true program semantics. The paper's core insight is that recursion, a fundamental concept in programming, can enable neural programs to generalize perfectly to inputs of arbitrary complexity and provide provable guarantees about their behavior. The authors demonstrate this by incorporating recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture through modified training traces, showing that recursive neural programs achieve perfect generalization across four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort) with minimal training data. Crucially, they introduce a verification procedure that proves perfect generalization by testing only a finite set of base cases and reduction rules, which is a significant methodological contribution previously absent in neural programming.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a highly novel contribution with significant potential impact, and the empirical results strongly support the core claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's contribution is fundamentally novel in the neural programming literature. To my knowledge, this is the first work to explicitly incorporate recursion into neural architectures for programming tasks and to provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization. This is a bold and important conceptual leap that addresses a core limitation in the field.

The authors provide strong empirical evidence for their claims across multiple tasks, showing that recursive neural programs generalize perfectly to inputs far beyond the training set complexity, while non-recursive versions fail. The verification procedure they introduce is particularly compelling—it demonstrates that the recursive neural programs generalize perfectly by testing only a finite set of cases, rather than needing to test an infinite number of inputs. This verification approach represents a significant methodological advance that could enable more reliable neural program synthesis in the future.

The paper is well-motivated and properly situated in the literature. It correctly identifies the limitations of current approaches (including the common use of curriculum learning, which ultimately fails for the same reason) and provides a compelling rationale for why recursion should solve the generalization problem. The connection to formal program verification is particularly strong and well-explained.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong overall, there are a few points where the authors could improve clarity and completeness:

1. The paper mentions "the first time one can provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs" multiple times. To strengthen this claim, it would be helpful to explicitly state why previous approaches couldn't provide such guarantees, beyond just saying "the memory updates are so complex and interdependent." A brief explanation of why recursion specifically enables this (beyond just "reduces complexity") would be valuable.

2. The paper notes that the verification procedure becomes infeasible for tasks with very large input domains (e.g., MNIST). It would be helpful to discuss the conditions under which this verification procedure is tractable, and perhaps provide a more concrete characterization of the input domain size where the verification procedure becomes impractical.

3. The paper states that "any general Neural Programming Architecture supporting such a call structure can be made to support recursion." It would strengthen the paper to provide a more concrete characterization of what constitutes "such a call structure" and how common this is across existing neural programming architectures.

4. The paper mentions that "in future work, we would like to decrease supervision and construct models that are capable of coming up with recursive abstractions themselves." It would be helpful to provide a brief sketch of what this might look like, as this represents a significant direction for future work.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper demonstrates perfect generalization for addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. Could the authors provide more specific insight into why recursion works particularly well for these tasks, and whether there are fundamental properties of these tasks that make them amenable to recursive neural programming?

2. The verification set for addition contains 20,181 input problems. For a task like addition with numbers up to 5000 digits, this verification set size seems surprisingly small. Could the authors explain why this verification set is sufficient to guarantee generalization for all possible inputs, and how they determined this set size?

3. The paper mentions that the NPI model naturally supports recursion, but the authors of NPI didn't explicitly consider it. How might the authors' approach change if they were to reimplement NPI with recursion as a core design principle from the beginning, rather than as a modification to the training procedure?
