

# Peer Review: MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to neural programming by introducing recursion as a key mechanism for achieving provable generalization. The authors argue that existing neural programming models fail to generalize beyond certain complexity thresholds because they learn spurious dependencies on training data characteristics (like input length) rather than the true program semantics. They propose incorporating recursion into neural architectures, specifically the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI), to enable provable perfect generalization.

The key contributions include: (1) a theoretical justification for why recursion enables provable generalization; (2) a method to train recursive neural programs by modifying training traces to explicitly contain recursive elements; (3) empirical demonstration across four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) showing that recursive models achieve 100% accuracy on all tested inputs, while non-recursive versions fail on longer inputs; and (4) a verification procedure that proves perfect generalization by testing only a finite set of base cases and reduction rules, which the authors claim is the first such provable guarantee for neural programs.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

Key reasons for acceptance: The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in neural programming with a novel and well-motivated solution. The theoretical justification for recursion's role in enabling provable generalization is sound, and the empirical results are strong and clearly presented.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully demonstrates that recursion fundamentally changes how neural programs generalize. The authors provide a compelling theoretical argument that recursion reduces the problem to base cases and reduction rules, making generalization tractable to prove. This is a significant conceptual advance over previous work that could only measure generalization empirically.

The empirical results are particularly strong. For addition, the recursive model generalizes perfectly to 5000-digit numbers after training on just 200 examples (with max length 3), while the non-recursive model fails on long problems constructed from training components. For bubble sort, the full recursive version sorts all arrays perfectly (up to 100+ elements), while non-recursive versions fail beyond length 8. Similar results are shown for topological sort and quicksort.

The verification procedure is a major contribution. By constructing a verification set that covers all base cases and reduction rules, the authors can prove perfect generalization without testing all possible inputs. For example, for addition, they can prove generalization to arbitrarily long numbers by verifying only 20,181 specific cases (vs. testing infinitely many inputs). This is a novel approach that provides mathematical guarantees previously unavailable in neural programming.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with a thorough discussion of prior work (NPI, Neural Turing Machine, etc.) and their limitations regarding generalization. The authors correctly identify the gap in the literature: no existing neural programming work provides provable generalization guarantees.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance its impact:

1. **Clarify the "spurious dependencies" claim**: The paper states that non-recursive models learn "spurious dependencies" but doesn't provide evidence beyond the generalization failure. Adding a brief analysis of hidden state representations or attention patterns could strengthen this claim.

2. **Discuss practical limitations**: The verification procedure works well for the four tasks, but the paper should acknowledge that for tasks with continuous inputs or infinite state spaces (e.g., MNIST digit classification), the verification set might be prohibitively large. The authors note this briefly in Section 4.2 but could expand on the practical limitations of their approach.

3. **Explain the training procedure more clearly**: The paper mentions that the recursive training traces are modified but doesn't fully explain how they were generated for tasks beyond addition. A more detailed description of the trace generation process for topological sort and quicksort would help readers understand the scope of the approach.

4. **Compare with alternative approaches**: While the paper discusses curriculum learning, it doesn't compare with recent approaches that use different training strategies (e.g., meta-learning, data augmentation) to improve generalization. A brief comparison would help position the work more precisely.

## 5. Questions for Authors

To help clarify my assessment and strengthen the paper, I'd appreciate answers to these questions:

1. For tasks where the base cases and reduction rules might be infinite (e.g., sorting arbitrary real-valued arrays), what would be the most promising path to adapt the verification procedure? Could the authors speculate on how this might be extended?

2. The paper states that "the training set can often be considerably smaller than the verification set" (Section 4.2). Could the authors provide specific numbers for the four tasks (e.g., training set size vs. verification set size) to quantify this relationship?

3. The paper mentions that "recursion is an essential element for neural programs to generalize" (Section 2.2). How would the authors respond to the counterargument that some problems don't have natural recursive formulations, and whether their approach would fail for such problems?

4. In Section 3.3, the authors state that "the core LSTM module's hidden state is preserved over all these ADD1 calls" for non-recursive addition. Could they provide a brief explanation of why this preservation makes generalization difficult to prove, while the recursive formulation avoids this issue?

This paper makes a substantial contribution to neural programming by establishing recursion as a mechanism for provable generalization. The empirical results are convincing, the theoretical justification is sound, and the verification procedure represents a novel approach to understanding neural program behavior. With the minor clarifications suggested above, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
