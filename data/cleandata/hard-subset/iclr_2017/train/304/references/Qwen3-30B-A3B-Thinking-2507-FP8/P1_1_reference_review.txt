

# Peer Review: "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of neural programming by addressing a fundamental limitation in current neural program synthesis approaches: poor generalization to inputs of greater complexity than seen during training. The authors propose that explicitly incorporating recursion into neural architectures is a key to enabling perfect generalization. They demonstrate that by modifying training traces to include recursive elements, the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture can learn recursive programs that achieve 100% accuracy on inputs of arbitrary complexity (e.g., addition of numbers with 5000 digits, sorting arrays of arbitrary length) after training on a very small number of examples.

The key contributions include:
- Proposing recursion as a necessary abstraction for neural programs to generalize well
- Demonstrating that recursion enables provable guarantees of perfect generalization
- Providing a verification procedure to prove generalization by testing on a finite set of base cases and reduction rules
- Implementing recursive versions of four programming tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) that outperform non-recursive versions
- Showing that the verification procedure only needs to test a small number of inputs (much smaller than the infinite space of possible inputs)

The paper is well-structured, clearly motivated, and makes a strong case for the importance of recursion in neural programming. The authors successfully demonstrate that their approach achieves perfect generalization where previous methods fail, and they provide the first provable guarantees for neural programs.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The key reasons for acceptance are:
1. The paper addresses a fundamental and important problem in neural programming that has been overlooked in previous work.
2. The approach is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and the experimental results clearly demonstrate the advantages of recursion for generalization.
3. The paper provides the first provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs, which is a significant theoretical contribution.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper makes a strong case for the importance of recursion in neural programming. The authors correctly identify that current neural programming models fail to generalize beyond a certain complexity threshold because they learn spurious dependencies on training examples rather than the true program semantics. Their hypothesis that recursion can solve this problem is well-supported by both theoretical reasoning and empirical results.

The empirical results are convincing: for addition, the recursive model generalizes perfectly to 5000-digit numbers after training on just 200 examples, while the non-recursive model (though it may generalize to long inputs) lacks a provable guarantee. For bubble sort, the fully recursive model sorts all arrays tested, while the non-recursive and partially recursive versions fail for longer arrays. The same pattern holds for topological sort and quicksort.

The most significant contribution is the verification procedure that allows proving perfect generalization. The authors correctly argue that recursion reduces the generalization problem to verifying base cases and reduction rules, which is tractable for problems with discrete, structured inputs. The paper demonstrates this with concrete examples for each task, showing that the verification set is orders of magnitude smaller than the infinite space of possible inputs.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, carefully citing and contrasting with previous work on neural programming. The authors clearly distinguish their contribution from prior approaches, which didn't explicitly incorporate recursion or provide provable generalization guarantees.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper makes a strong contribution, there are several areas where the authors could improve clarity and rigor:

1. **Clarify the training trace construction**: The paper states that "changing the NPI training traces is a simple way to enable this" but doesn't provide sufficient detail on how exactly these recursive traces were generated. A more detailed explanation of the procedure for constructing recursive traces (beyond just saying "we added recursive calls") would be valuable for reproducibility and understanding.

2. **Expand on the limitations of the verification procedure**: The paper notes that the verification procedure is infeasible for tasks with large input domains (like MNIST or speech), but doesn't explore how to address this limitation. It would be helpful to discuss potential extensions or alternative verification approaches for continuous or high-dimensional input spaces.

3. **Provide more insight into why recursion works**: While the paper correctly argues that recursion decomposes problems into base cases and reduction rules, it could provide more specific analysis of what the network learns in the recursive version versus the non-recursive version. For instance, do the learned reduction rules match the intended ones? How do the hidden states differ between the two approaches?

4. **Clarify the relationship between recursion and the NPI architecture**: The paper states that "the NPI model naturally supports recursion" but doesn't fully explain why this is the case. A more detailed discussion of how the NPI's stack-based execution model enables recursion (compared to other neural programming architectures) would strengthen the paper.

5. **Address the potential for overfitting to verification set**: The paper states that the verification set is much smaller than the training set, but doesn't explicitly address whether the model could be overfitting to the verification set. It would be helpful to discuss how the authors ensured that the verification set was representative of the problem space.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that "the NPI model naturally supports recursion" but doesn't explain why this is the case. Could you elaborate on what specific architectural properties of NPI enable recursion, and how this might differ from other neural programming architectures?

2. For the verification procedure, you note that "for tasks with very large input domains, such as ones involving MNIST digits or speech samples, the state space of base cases and reduction rules could be prohibitively large." How might you adapt the verification procedure for continuous input spaces, or what alternative approaches would you propose for such tasks?

3. In your experiments, you trained on very small datasets (e.g., 200 traces for addition). How do you ensure that the recursive traces aren't simply memorizing the specific examples in the training set, rather than learning a generalizable recursive program? Could you provide some analysis of the model's behavior on inputs not in the training set but in the verification set?

4. The paper claims that "the recursive learned program has a provable guarantee of perfect generalization" but this guarantee only applies to the specific verification set you constructed. How would you characterize the scope of this guarantee, and what limitations does it have for inputs beyond the verification set?

5. For the bubble sort task, you mention that "the partially recursive version is not enough for perfect generalization." Could you explain why only full recursion (with recursive calls for all four functions) was necessary, and what the intermediate results were for the partially recursive version?
