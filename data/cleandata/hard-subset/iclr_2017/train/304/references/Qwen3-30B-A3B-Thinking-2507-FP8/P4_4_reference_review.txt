

# Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper makes a significant contribution by introducing recursion as a key element for neural programming architectures to achieve provable generalization. The authors demonstrate that by explicitly incorporating recursion into neural architectures (specifically the Neural Programmer-Interpreter, NPI), neural programs can learn true program semantics rather than spurious dependencies on training data characteristics. The paper shows that recursive neural programs achieve perfect generalization on unseen inputs (e.g., addition with 5000-digit numbers, topological sort on 70-vertex graphs) after training on very small datasets. Crucially, the authors provide a verification procedure to prove perfect generalization by checking only a finite number of base cases and reduction rules, which is the first time such provable guarantees have been provided for neural programs.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a novel contribution with strong empirical validation of its claims. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the paper introduces recursion as a fundamental concept for neural programming to enable provable generalization, which is a significant theoretical advancement; (2) the empirical results clearly demonstrate superior generalization capabilities of recursive neural programs across multiple tasks, with verification procedures to prove perfect generalization.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper is well-motivated and clearly places its contribution in the literature. It correctly identifies that current neural programming approaches fail to generalize beyond certain complexity thresholds due to learning spurious dependencies, and that none provide provable generalization guarantees. The authors' insight that recursion (a core concept in programming) is missing from neural architectures is well-founded and well-justified.

The theoretical claim that recursion enables provable perfect generalization is rigorously supported. The paper provides a clear explanation of how recursion reduces the generalization problem to verifying a finite number of base cases and reduction rules, making the verification tractable. The verification procedure described in Section 3.3 is logically sound and well-justified.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous. The authors train on very small datasets (e.g., 200 traces for addition, 4 for quicksort) and demonstrate perfect generalization to inputs orders of magnitude larger than the training set. The comparison between recursive and non-recursive versions across four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of their approach. The paper also correctly notes that the non-recursive versions cannot be verified due to the hidden state propagation making reasoning about unseen inputs difficult.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance clarity and impact:

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the verification procedure, particularly for tasks with continuous or high-dimensional inputs (e.g., images or speech), which the authors briefly mention but do not address in depth.

2. The paper states that "the training set can often be considerably smaller than the verification set," but it would be helpful to quantify this relationship for each task to better understand the sample efficiency of the approach.

3. The paper could more explicitly discuss how the recursive training traces were generated (beyond the "constructing new training traces which explicitly contain recursive elements" statement), as this is a crucial aspect of the methodology.

4. The paper claims to be the first to investigate "provable generalization properties of neural programs," but it would strengthen the claim to explicitly contrast with related work on formal verification of neural networks, which has been explored in other contexts (e.g., for safety-critical systems).

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. For the bubble sort task, you mention that the non-recursive version failed for arrays of length 8, while the recursive version generalized perfectly. Could you clarify whether the non-recursive version was trained on arrays of length up to 7, and if so, how this specific length threshold was determined?

2. The verification set for addition contains 20,181 input problems. Could you explain why this specific number was chosen, and whether it's possible to construct a smaller verification set while still guaranteeing perfect generalization?

3. The paper states that the recursive quicksort implementation is "not purely tail recursive." How does this affect the verification procedure compared to the other tasks, and would the verification set need to be larger to account for this non-tail recursion?
