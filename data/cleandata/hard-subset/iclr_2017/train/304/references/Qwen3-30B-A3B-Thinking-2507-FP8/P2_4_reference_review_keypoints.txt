- Paper claim: recursion is a necessary abstraction for perfect generalization in neural programs; propose recursive NPI variant and a verification procedure.  
- Implemented recursive training traces for addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, claiming 100% accuracy on arbitrary-length inputs from small training sets.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Reason for acceptance 1: identifies a major limitation (spurious dependencies) in current neural programming approaches.  
- Reason for acceptance 2: experiments convincingly show recursive models generalize much better than non-recursive versions.  
- Reason for acceptance 3: using recursion to enable (empirical) provable generalization is novel and important.  
- Main revision required: the phrase “provably perfect generalization” is overstated because the verification is empirical testing on finite sets, not a formal proof.  
- Core insight supported: recursion helps models learn true program semantics rather than spurious training correlations.  
- Empirical support: recursive addition generalizes to 5000 digits from 200 examples; recursive bubble sort and quicksort outperform non-recursive baselines.  
- Verification critique: for addition authors claim an analytic verification set of 20,181 examples but don’t provide a mathematical proof of completeness; other tasks use random generation that may miss edge cases.  
- Clarification requested: explicitly state verification provides empirical evidence rather than a mathematical guarantee.  
- Need more detail on how verification sets were constructed and how completeness was ensured (e.g., derivation of V for topological sort).  
- Request quantitative comparisons to prior work’s generalization performance on the same tasks.  
- Discuss why NPI’s original work didn’t exploit recursion and why recursion won’t be learned without specialized training traces.  
- Discuss limitations more fully: extra supervision required, scalability to more complex tasks, and applicability to non-recursive problems.  
- Question 1: provide a mathematical proof that the addition verification set of 20,181 examples is complete.  
- Question 2: for other tasks, what criterion ensures the randomly generated verification set is complete and free of missed edge cases?  
- Question 3: why didn’t original NPI authors use recursion, and why can’t NPI learn recursion without modified traces?  
- Question 4: provide quantitative comparisons of maximum input lengths/generalization vs. previous methods.  
- Question 5: how does verification set size scale with task complexity (e.g., for merge sort)?
