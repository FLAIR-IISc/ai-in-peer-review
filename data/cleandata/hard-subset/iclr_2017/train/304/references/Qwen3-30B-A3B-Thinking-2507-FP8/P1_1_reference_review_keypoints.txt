- Paper addresses poor generalization in neural program synthesis and proposes explicit recursion to achieve perfect generalization.
- Authors modify NPI training traces to include recursion and claim recursive NPI learns programs that generalize to arbitrarily large inputs (e.g., 5000-digit addition, arbitrary-length sorting).
- Key contributions: proposing recursion as necessary for generalization, enabling provable perfect generalization, giving a verification procedure, implementing recursive versions of four tasks, and showing small verification sets suffice.
- Decision: Accept.
- Reasons for acceptance: addresses an important overlooked problem, approach is well-motivated/theoretically sound with strong empirical results, provides first provable guarantees for neural programs.
- Core supporting argument: recursion prevents learning spurious dependencies and decomposes tasks into base cases and reduction rules, enabling provable generalization.
- Empirical evidence: recursive models generalize perfectly (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) where non-recursive/partially recursive variants fail.
- Verification procedure argument: recursion reduces infinite generalization to checking a small finite set of base cases and reduction rules, demonstrated practically for tasks.
- Literature placement: paper is well-situated and clearly differentiates from prior neural programming work lacking recursion or provable guarantees.
- Improve clarity: provide detailed procedure for constructing recursive training traces to aid reproducibility.
- Improve discussion: expand on limitations and possible extensions of the verification procedure for large or continuous input domains.
- Deeper analysis: give more insight into what the network learns in recursive vs non-recursive versions (e.g., whether learned reduction rules match intended ones, hidden-state differences).
- Architectural clarity: explain why NPI "naturally supports recursion" and how its stack-based execution enables recursion relative to other architectures.
- Robustness concern: address potential overfitting to the verification set and justify that the verification set is representative.
- Question 1: What specific architectural properties of NPI enable recursion compared to other neural programming architectures?
- Question 2: How could the verification procedure be adapted or replaced for continuous or high-dimensional input spaces (e.g., MNIST, speech)?
- Question 3: How do you ensure recursive traces trained on small datasets (e.g., 200 examples) aren’t just memorizing examples rather than learning general recursive programs?
- Question 4: How should we interpret the scope and limitations of the claimed "provable guarantee" given it applies to the constructed verification set?
- Question 5: Why was full recursion necessary for bubble sort (all four functions) and what were the intermediate results for the partially recursive variant?
