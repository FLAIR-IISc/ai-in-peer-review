

# Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## 1. Summary of Claims
The paper claims to address the critical challenge of generalization in neural program synthesis by incorporating recursion into neural architectures. The authors argue that recursion enables "provably perfect generalization" for neural programs, which they claim has never been achieved before. They demonstrate their approach on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort) using the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture, showing that their recursive formulations achieve 100% accuracy on all tested inputs after training on very small datasets. They further claim to provide "provably perfect generalization" by verifying the neural program on a finite set of base cases and reduction rules, which they argue makes the generalization guarantee tractable.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper makes overly strong claims about provable generalization that are not adequately supported by the evidence, and the approach lacks sufficient novelty compared to existing work.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main claim—that their method provides "provably perfect generalization" for neural programs—is fundamentally flawed in its scope and applicability. The verification procedure they describe only works for very specific, artificially constrained tasks with small input spaces. For example, their verification set for addition contains 20,181 input problems, but this is limited to a specific digit representation with no leading zeros and equal-length inputs. This is a far cry from the "infinite space of possible inputs" they claim to handle.

The paper incorrectly claims to be the first to incorporate recursion into neural architectures for generalization, when the NPI architecture itself naturally supports recursion (as they acknowledge in Section 2.2). The authors simply changed the training traces to include recursive elements, which is a minor modification rather than a fundamental innovation. Previous work (e.g., Reed & de Freitas, 2016) already used a recursive-like structure in their architecture but did not explicitly train for recursion.

The paper's experiments are highly misleading. While they claim to train on "very small number of examples" (e.g., 200 traces for addition), their verification sets are often larger than their training sets. For bubble sort, they train on 100 traces but verify on a set of one array of size 10. This suggests they're not learning generalization from small examples but rather memorizing specific patterns that match the verification cases. Their "provably perfect generalization" claim only holds for the very specific inputs they verify, not for arbitrary inputs.

The paper fails to demonstrate why their approach is superior to existing methods that could achieve similar results with more standard techniques. For instance, they don't compare their method to carefully designed curriculum learning approaches that might achieve similar generalization without the need for explicit recursion.

Most critically, the paper overstates the significance of their verification procedure. They acknowledge that for "tasks with very large input domains, such as ones involving MNIST digits or speech samples, the state space of base cases and reduction rules could be prohibitively large." This admission undercuts their main claim of "provably perfect generalization" for neural programs, as it reveals their method is only applicable to a tiny subset of possible programming tasks.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To improve the paper, the authors should:
1. Correctly contextualize their work within existing literature on recursion in neural programming. The NPI architecture already supports recursion, so the contribution is more about training procedures than architectural innovation.
2. Provide a more rigorous analysis of when their verification procedure is applicable and when it fails. The paper should acknowledge that their approach is limited to tasks with small, analytically tractable input spaces.
3. Conduct more comprehensive comparisons against established methods (e.g., curriculum learning, better data augmentation) that could achieve similar generalization without requiring explicit recursion.
4. Clarify the relationship between their training traces and the recursive structure they claim is learned. Is the recursion truly learned, or is it simply imposed through the training data?

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper claims their method is "the first time one can provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs." However, they acknowledge the verification procedure is infeasible for most real-world tasks. How do you reconcile this claim with the limitations you acknowledge in Section 3.3?
2. For tasks where the verification set is prohibitively large (e.g., sorting arrays of arbitrary size), how would your method be applied in practice? What would be the "base cases and reduction rules" in such cases?
3. The paper states "the NPI model naturally supports recursion (even though the authors of NPI did not consider this aspect explicitly)." If the architecture naturally supports recursion, why did the original NPI paper fail to learn recursive programs? Was it purely a training data issue, or were there architectural limitations?
4. How does your method scale to tasks with continuous input spaces (e.g., sorting numbers with arbitrary ranges, not just digits 0-9)? The verification procedure described would be infeasible for such cases.
