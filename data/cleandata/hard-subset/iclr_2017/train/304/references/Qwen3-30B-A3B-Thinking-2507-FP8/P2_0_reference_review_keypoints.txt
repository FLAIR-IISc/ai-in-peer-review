- Paper proposes adding recursion to neural programming architectures to enable provable generalization.  
- Recursion reduces generalization to verifying a finite set of base cases and reduction rules.  
- Authors modify Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) training traces to teach recursive programs.  
- Evaluated on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort with strong empirical gains.  
- Recursive models achieve 100% accuracy on very large or long inputs where non-recursive models fail.  
- Addition: generalizes to 5000-digit numbers from 200 short traces; non-recursive fails on longer numbers.  
- Bubble sort: recursive version sorts all arrays perfectly; non-recursive fails beyond length 8.  
- Topological sort: recursive works up to 70 vertices; non-recursive fails beyond 7 vertices.  
- Quicksort: recursive version succeeds for all tested lengths; non-recursive fails beyond length 10.  
- Paper introduces a verification procedure to prove perfect generalization for recursive neural programs.  
- Claim this is the first work to investigate provable generalization properties of neural programs.  
- Decision: recommend acceptance due to novel, theoretically sound solution and strong empirical evidence.  
- Paper is well-written and provides sufficient implementation and experimental detail.  
- Suggestion: provide clearer, step-by-step pseudocode or flowchart for the verification procedure.  
- Suggestion: discuss limitations of verification for large input domains (e.g., MNIST, speech) and possible mitigations.  
- Suggestion: quantify “very small number of examples” with concrete metrics across tasks.  
- Suggestion: explain why original NPI training did not discover recursive programs and how recursion interacts with NPI internals.  
- Question: how do you ensure the verification set truly covers all base cases and reduction rules for complex tasks?  
- Question: how was the 20,181 verification-set size for addition derived and what are the specific base/reduction cases?  
- Question: how do you formally prove generalization beyond the finite verification set—does the set provably cover all cases?  
- Question: why is a single array of size 10 sufficient for bubble sort verification and how was that validated?  
- Question: what are the main challenges and possible approaches for enabling models to discover recursive abstractions with less supervision?
