

# Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of neural programming by addressing a fundamental limitation of current approaches: poor generalization to inputs of greater complexity than those seen during training. The authors correctly identify that existing neural programming architectures (like Neural Programmer-Interpreter) learn spurious dependencies on characteristics like input length rather than the true program semantics, leading to poor generalization beyond a certain complexity threshold.

The paper's key contribution is the proposal to incorporate recursion into neural programming architectures, which the authors argue is a critical abstraction for enabling perfect generalization. They demonstrate that recursion reduces the generalization problem to verifying a finite number of base cases and reduction rules, which can be done with a verification procedure. This is the first paper to investigate provable generalization properties of neural programs.

The authors implement their approach by modifying the training traces of the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture to include recursive elements, enabling NPI to learn recursive programs. They evaluate their approach on four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. Their results show that the recursive versions achieve 100% accuracy on all tested inputs (up to very large inputs like 5000-digit numbers for addition), while non-recursive versions fail for longer inputs. Crucially, they provide a verification procedure that can prove perfect generalization for their recursive programs.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

**Key reasons for acceptance:**
1. The paper addresses a significant, well-motivated problem in neural programming (generalization beyond training complexity) with a novel and theoretically sound solution.
2. The empirical results are strong, demonstrating perfect generalization from very small training sets across multiple challenging tasks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of neural programming. The problem of poor generalization is well-documented in the literature (citing Vinyals et al., Kaiser & Sutskever, Reed & de Freitas, Graves et al., Zaremba et al.), and the authors correctly identify that existing approaches lack provable guarantees about generalization.

The theoretical insight that recursion can reduce the generalization problem to verifying a finite set of base cases and reduction rules is compelling and novel. The paper correctly points out that this is a significant advance over previous approaches, which require testing on arbitrarily long inputs to verify generalization.

The empirical evidence is robust:
- For addition, the recursive version generalizes perfectly to 5000-digit numbers from training on just 200 traces (max length 3), while the non-recursive version fails on longer numbers.
- For bubble sort, the fully recursive version sorts all arrays perfectly, while the non-recursive version fails for arrays longer than 8 elements.
- For topological sort, the recursive version works on graphs up to 70 vertices, while the non-recursive version fails for graphs larger than 7 vertices.
- For quicksort, the recursive version works perfectly for all tested array lengths, while the non-recursive version fails for arrays longer than 10 elements.

The verification procedure they propose is particularly valuable as it provides the first method to prove perfect generalization for neural programs, addressing a critical gap in the literature.

The paper is well-written, clearly explains the problem and solution, and provides sufficient detail about their implementation and experiments. The connection between recursion and provable generalization is well-motivated and supported by both theory and empirical results.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, the following suggestions would further improve it:

1. **Clarify the verification procedure**: The paper mentions the verification procedure multiple times but lacks a clear, step-by-step explanation of how to implement it. A pseudocode description or more detailed flowchart would help readers understand how to apply this to other tasks.

2. **Address limitations of the verification approach**: The paper acknowledges that verification becomes infeasible for tasks with very large input domains (e.g., MNIST or speech). It would be valuable to discuss potential approaches to handle such cases or to more explicitly acknowledge the limitations of the verification procedure.

3. **Quantify "very small number of examples"**: The paper states that the recursive programs generalize perfectly from "a very small number of examples." It would strengthen the paper to provide more concrete metrics (e.g., 200 traces for addition with max length 3, which is much smaller than the number of possible addition problems of length 3).

4. **Explain why NPI didn't previously learn recursive programs**: While the paper mentions that NPI naturally supports recursion but didn't explicitly leverage it, it would be helpful to provide more insight into why NPI's original training procedure failed to learn recursive programs.

5. **Discuss the relationship between the recursive formulation and the NPI architecture**: The paper could provide more detail on how the recursive formulation specifically interacts with NPI's architecture, particularly the LSTM controller and stack-based execution model.

## Questions for the Authors

1. In the verification procedure, how do you ensure that the verification set truly covers all base cases and reduction rules? For tasks like topological sort and quicksort, which are more complex than addition, what specific criteria did you use to determine that you had covered all necessary cases?

2. For the addition task, you analytically determined a verification set of 20,181 input problems. Could you provide more detail on how you derived this number? Specifically, what are the base cases and reduction rules that you identified, and how did you ensure you covered all of them?

3. The paper claims "provably perfect generalization" but the verification set is finite. How do you formally prove that the model will generalize to all possible inputs (not just those in the verification set)? Is there a formal proof that the verification set covers all relevant cases?

4. For the bubble sort task, the paper states that the verification set consists of one array of size 10. How did you determine that a single array of size 10 was sufficient to cover all base cases and reduction rules, and what criteria did you use to validate this?

5. The paper mentions that "in future work, we would like to decrease supervision and construct models that are capable of coming up with recursive abstractions themselves." What are the main challenges you foresee in developing such a model, and how might they be addressed?

This paper makes a significant contribution to neural programming by introducing recursion as a key mechanism for enabling provable generalization. The combination of theoretical insight and strong empirical results, along with the verification procedure, makes it a valuable addition to the field. I believe it would be a strong contribution to ICLR.
