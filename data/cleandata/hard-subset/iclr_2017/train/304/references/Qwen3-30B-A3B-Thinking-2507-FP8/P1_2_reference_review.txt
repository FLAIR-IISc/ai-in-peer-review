

# Peer Review for "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural programming by addressing the critical problem of generalization in neural program synthesis. The authors propose that recursion is a necessary abstraction for neural programs to achieve perfect generalization, and they demonstrate that by incorporating recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture, they can achieve provable guarantees of perfect generalization for several programming tasks.

The key contributions are:
- A theoretical argument that recursion enables provable generalization by decomposing problems into finite base cases and reduction rules
- A practical implementation of recursion in NPI by modifying training traces to include recursive elements
- Empirical demonstration that recursive neural programs achieve perfect generalization on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort tasks after training on very small datasets (200 traces for addition, 100 for bubble sort, 6 for topological sort, and 4 for quicksort)
- A verification procedure that can prove perfect generalization by testing on a finite set of base cases and reduction rules, which is the first time such provable guarantees have been provided for neural programs

The paper is well-motivated and clearly positions itself in the literature, correctly identifying the limitations of current neural programming approaches (spurious dependencies on training data characteristics rather than true program semantics) and explaining why recursion can solve this problem.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The key reasons for this decision are:

1. The paper makes a novel and important contribution to the field by introducing recursion as a necessary component for neural program generalization, with the first provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs.

2. The empirical results are strong, clearly demonstrating that the recursive approach outperforms non-recursive approaches across multiple tasks, achieving perfect generalization with minimal training data.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's claims are well-supported by both theoretical justification and empirical results. The theoretical argument that recursion enables provable generalization is compelling: by decomposing problems into finite base cases and reduction rules, the authors can verify a finite set of inputs that guarantees perfect generalization for all inputs.

The empirical results are particularly strong. For example:
- In addition, the recursive version generalizes perfectly to 5000-digit numbers with just 200 training examples, while the non-recursive version fails on longer numbers.
- For bubble sort, the fully recursive version sorts all arrays of any length (tested up to length 10), while the non-recursive version fails for arrays longer than 8.
- For topological sort, the recursive version works on graphs with up to 70 vertices with just a single training example (from a 5-vertex graph), while the non-recursive version fails for graphs of size 5 and beyond.

The verification procedure is a major advancement over prior work. The authors demonstrate how to verify a finite set of inputs that guarantees perfect generalization for all inputs, which is a significant step forward from the empirical generalization metrics used in previous work.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail about the implementation and experiments. The connection to prior work is appropriate, and the authors correctly identify how their approach differs from and improves upon existing methods.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

I have a few suggestions to strengthen the paper further:

- The paper mentions that the verification set can be "considerably smaller than the training set" in some cases, but for addition, they report a verification set of 20,181 input problems compared to a training set of 200 traces. This seems counterintuitive and requires clarification. It would be helpful to explain why the verification set is larger for addition, and whether this is a general pattern or specific to this task.

- The paper states that "the NPI model naturally supports recursion" but doesn't fully explain why this is the case. A more detailed explanation of how the NPI architecture's design enables recursion (beyond the simple fact that it has a stack) would strengthen the theoretical foundation of the paper.

- The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of the limitations of the verification procedure. The authors note that for tasks with very large input domains (like MNIST or speech), the verification procedure would be infeasible. It would be helpful to discuss potential extensions or approximations to the verification procedure for these cases.

- Including some analysis of what the model actually learns (e.g., attention patterns, hidden state representations) would provide deeper insight into why recursion enables better generalization. This could help readers understand the mechanism by which recursion improves performance.

## 5. Questions for Authors

To help clarify my understanding and ensure confidence in the assessment, I have the following questions:

1. For the addition task, the verification set has 20,181 input problems while the training set has only 200 traces. This seems counterintuitive since the paper claims "the verification set can often be considerably smaller than the training set." Could you clarify why the verification set is larger for addition, and whether this is a general pattern or specific to this task?

2. In the verification procedure, the authors state that "V should also contain the necessary reductions, which corresponds to making the recursive calls at the correct time, as indicated by P." How do they ensure that the verification set covers all possible timing of recursive calls, particularly for tasks like quicksort where the timing depends on the pivot position (which varies with the input)?

3. The paper mentions that the recursive version of NPI learns recursive programs "by modifying the training procedure." Could you provide more detail on how the training procedure is modified beyond simply adding recursive traces? Specifically, how does the model learn to make the recursive calls at the correct times without explicit supervision for those timing decisions?
