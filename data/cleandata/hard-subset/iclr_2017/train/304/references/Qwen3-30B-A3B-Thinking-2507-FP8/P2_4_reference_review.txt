

# Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of neural programming by identifying recursion as a necessary abstraction for achieving perfect generalization in neural programs. The authors argue that current neural programming models (including NPI) fail to generalize beyond a certain complexity threshold because they learn spurious dependencies on training data characteristics rather than the true program semantics. They propose that incorporating recursion into neural architectures enables the model to learn the true program semantics and achieve provably perfect generalization. The authors demonstrate this by modifying the NPI architecture to learn recursive programs through carefully constructed training traces for four tasks: grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. They claim that their recursive neural programs achieve 100% accuracy on inputs of arbitrary length from very small training sets (e.g., 200 examples for addition), and they propose a verification procedure to prove perfect generalization by testing on a finite set of base cases and reduction rules.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.

**Key reasons for acceptance:**
1. The paper identifies a significant and well-documented limitation in current neural programming approaches (poor generalization due to spurious dependencies) and proposes a compelling solution (incorporating recursion as a core architectural component).
2. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that recursive neural programs generalize better than non-recursive versions on the tasks tested, achieving perfect accuracy on inputs of arbitrary length from very small training sets.
3. The idea of using recursion to enable provable generalization is novel and important for the field of neural programming.

**Key reason for requiring revisions:**
The paper's claim of "provably perfect generalization" is overstated. The verification procedure described is empirical (testing on a finite set), not a mathematical proof. The paper needs to more carefully qualify this claim.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core insight is significant: recursion is a key abstraction that enables neural programs to learn the true program semantics rather than spurious dependencies. This is well-motivated by the literature they cite (Vinyals et al., Kaiser & Sutskever, Reed & de Freitas, Graves et al., Zaremba et al.), which all report poor generalization beyond certain complexity thresholds.

The experiments are well-designed and provide strong empirical evidence that recursive neural programs generalize better than non-recursive versions. For example, their recursive addition model generalizes to inputs with 5000 digits from just 200 training examples, while the non-recursive model fails for longer inputs. Similarly, their recursive bubble sort model generalizes perfectly to arrays of length 10 (while non-recursive fails beyond length 8), and their recursive quicksort model handles all array lengths tested.

However, the claim of "provably perfect generalization" requires clarification. The paper states: "This is the first time one can provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs." But the evidence provided is empirical verification on a finite set (the verification set), not a mathematical proof. For addition, they claim to have analytically determined a verification set of 20,181 examples, but they don't provide the mathematical proof that this set is complete (i.e., that it covers all possible base cases and reduction rules). For other tasks, they rely on random generation of verification sets, which may miss edge cases.

The verification procedure described is promising, but it's an empirical verification, not a mathematical proof. The paper should clearly state that the verification procedure provides empirical evidence for perfect generalization, not a mathematical proof. The claim of "provably perfect generalization" should be qualified to reflect that the verification set is constructed to cover all base cases and reduction rules, but this is an empirical verification rather than a mathematical proof.

## 4. Additional Feedback

**1. Clarify the "provably perfect generalization" claim:**
The paper needs to more carefully qualify the "provably perfect generalization" claim. It should explicitly state that the verification procedure provides empirical evidence for perfect generalization, not a mathematical proof. The paper should state that the verification set covers all base cases and reduction rules, but that this is an empirical verification.

**2. Provide more details on verification set construction:**
For tasks other than addition, the paper says they "randomly generate input candidates" to cover all base cases and reduction rules. It would be helpful to provide more details on how they ensured the verification set was complete. For example, for topological sort, they say they constructed a verification set of size 73 by ensuring randomly generated graphs cover the analytically derived V. How did they derive V analytically? What is the process for ensuring completeness?

**3. Quantitative comparison to previous work:**
The paper claims their method "out-performs previous generalization results" but doesn't provide quantitative comparisons to previous methods on the same tasks. Adding a table showing the generalization accuracy of previous methods vs. their method would strengthen the claims.

**4. Address NPI's natural support for recursion:**
The paper states that "the NPI model naturally supports recursion (even though the authors of NPI did not consider this aspect explicitly)." It would be helpful to explain why the original NPI authors didn't use recursion, and why it's not obvious that recursion would be learned without modified training traces.

**5. Discuss limitations more thoroughly:**
The paper acknowledges that the approach requires more supervision (explicit recursive training traces), but it would be helpful to discuss other limitations as well, such as scalability to more complex tasks and applicability to tasks without clear recursive structure.

## Questions for the Authors

1. For addition, you claim to have analytically determined a verification set of 20,181 examples. Can you provide the mathematical proof that this set is complete (i.e., that it covers all possible base cases and reduction rules)?

2. For tasks other than addition, you say you "randomly generate input candidates" until they cover all base cases and reduction rules. What is your criterion for determining when the verification set is complete? How do you ensure you haven't missed any edge cases?

3. The paper states that "the NPI model naturally supports recursion." Why didn't the original NPI authors use recursion, and why did they fail to learn recursive programs without the modified training traces?

4. Can you provide a quantitative comparison of your method's generalization performance to previous methods on the same tasks? For example, what is the maximum input length your recursive model can handle compared to previous methods?

5. How does the size of the verification set scale with the complexity of the task? For example, how large would the verification set need to be for a more complex recursive algorithm like merge sort?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural programming, and with these clarifications, it would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
