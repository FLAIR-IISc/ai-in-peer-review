

# Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in neural programming: the inability of current neural architectures to generalize well to inputs of greater complexity, even when trained with curriculum learning. The authors make a compelling case that the root issue is that neural networks learn spurious dependencies on specific characteristics of training examples (like input length) rather than the true program semantics.

The paper's main contributions are:
- Proposing that recursion is a necessary abstraction for neural programs to generalize well
- Demonstrating that recursion enables provable guarantees of perfect generalization
- Showing that the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture naturally supports recursion, but previous implementations did not explicitly leverage it
- Providing a verification procedure to prove perfect generalization by checking base cases and reduction rules
- Empirically demonstrating perfect generalization on four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort) with very small training sets

The paper is notable for being the first to provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs, which is a significant theoretical contribution to the field. The authors make a strong case that recursion fundamentally changes the generalization behavior of neural programs by reducing the problem to a finite set of verifiable cases.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing recursion as a necessary abstraction for provable generalization in neural programs, which is novel and important.
2. The empirical results are strong, demonstrating perfect generalization on multiple tasks with very small training sets, where previous approaches failed.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

### Theoretical Contribution

The paper's theoretical contribution is substantial. The authors correctly identify that the core limitation of current neural programming approaches is the inability to provide provable guarantees of generalization. Their argument that recursion enables such guarantees is well-motivated: by decomposing problems into base cases and reduction rules, the verification problem becomes tractable (finite) rather than intractable (infinite). This is a novel and important insight that addresses a fundamental limitation in the field.

The verification procedure they propose is elegant and well-justified. They correctly note that for recursive programs, the verification set only needs to cover the base cases and reduction rules, which is a finite set, rather than testing all possible inputs. This is a crucial theoretical advance that has not been previously demonstrated in neural programming.

### Empirical Evidence

The empirical results are convincing. For each task, they show that recursive versions of the neural programs achieve perfect generalization from very small training sets (e.g., 4 examples for quicksort), while non-recursive versions fail completely on longer inputs. The results are consistent across four different tasks with different complexities, which strengthens the generalizability of their claim.

The paper's claim that "this is the first time one can provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs" appears to be well-founded. While the NPI architecture has been previously proposed, the authors correctly note that previous work did not explicitly incorporate recursion, and therefore did not provide any guarantees about generalization.

### Comparison with Literature

The paper is well-placed in the literature. The authors correctly cite and build upon the relevant work in neural programming (Vinyals et al., Kaiser & Sutskever, Reed & de Freitas, Graves et al., Zaremba et al.). The paper addresses a clear limitation in this line of work that has been acknowledged but not solved by previous approaches.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these points would help improve it:

1. **State-of-the-art comparison**: The paper compares only to the original NPI work, but there have been several neural programming approaches published since 2016 (e.g., Neural GPUs, Neural Turing Machines, etc.). A brief comparison to more recent approaches would strengthen the paper's positioning.

2. **Verification set size**: The paper mentions that verification sets can be very small (e.g., 10 elements for quicksort), but it would be helpful to explicitly state how small the verification set is relative to the training set. This would better demonstrate the efficiency of their approach.

3. **Limitations section**: The paper mentions that the verification procedure is infeasible for tasks with very large input domains (like MNIST or speech), but it would be helpful to discuss more concretely what the limitations are and whether there might be ways to address them (e.g., through approximation or sampling).

4. **Training procedure details**: The paper states that they "change the NPI training traces" to enable recursion, but doesn't provide enough detail on how they construct these recursive traces. A brief description of the trace construction process would be helpful for reproducibility.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper claims this is the first time provable guarantees of perfect generalization have been provided for neural programs. Could you clarify whether any prior work has made similar claims for specific, limited cases (e.g., for very simple programs or specific architectures)?

2. For the verification procedure, how do you ensure that the verification set truly covers all base cases and reduction rules? In the case of topological sort, you mention constructing a verification set of size 73 - could you provide more detail on how this was constructed?

3. The paper states that the training set can be "considerably smaller than the verification set," but in the experiments, the training sets are quite small (4-200 examples) while the verification sets appear to be larger (e.g., 20,181 examples for addition). Could you clarify the relationship between the training set size and verification set size in your experiments?

4. For tasks like quicksort that use non-tail recursion (as mentioned in Section 3.2), how does the verification procedure handle the more complex recursive structure compared to tail-recursive tasks like addition?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural programming by establishing recursion as a necessary abstraction for provable generalization. The theoretical insight is strong, the empirical results are convincing, and the verification procedure is elegant and well-justified. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the ICLR proceedings.
