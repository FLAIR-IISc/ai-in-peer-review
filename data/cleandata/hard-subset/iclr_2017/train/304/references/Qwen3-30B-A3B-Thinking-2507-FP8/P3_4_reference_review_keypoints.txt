- Proposal: Use recursion in neural programming (via modified NPI training traces) to obtain provable generalization; tested on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Decision: Reject — theoretically interesting with promising empirical results but lacks sufficient novelty and generalizability.  
- Novelty concern: Recursion is a long-standing CS idea; main contribution is applying it to NPI via trace modification rather than introducing a new architecture or concept.  
- NPI already "naturally supports recursion"; paper’s innovation is primarily in training supervision, not architecture.  
- Verification limits: The verification procedure only works for small, analytically tractable input spaces and does not scale to large or complex tasks.  
- Empirical scope: Results are confined to simple tasks, so “provably perfect generalization” claims are limited to those cases.  
- Baseline comparisons: Insufficient comparison to other generalization techniques (e.g., curriculum learning) or alternative architectures.  
- Supervision requirement: Method relies on explicitly provided recursive training traces, a strong and potentially impractical supervision signal.  
- Improvement suggestion: Clearly distinguish what is novel (trace-based recursion training) versus established knowledge (recursion concept).  
- Improvement suggestion: Evaluate on broader and more complex programming tasks to test scalability and generality.  
- Improvement suggestion: Compare against alternative generalization methods and architectural modifications beyond recursion.  
- Improvement suggestion: Discuss limitations of the verification procedure more thoroughly, especially for real-world large input spaces.  
- Clarification request: Explicitly qualify “provable generalization” as applying only to tasks with small, verifiable input sets.  
- Question: How would the approach handle tasks with large input domains (e.g., arrays of length 1000+, graphs with 100+ vertices) where verification is infeasible?  
- Question: Beyond supplying recursive traces, what specific architectural or training modifications enabled NPI to learn recursion?  
- Question: How can one automatically determine the necessary level/depth of recursion for a given task?  
- Question: How does the small training set size (e.g., 200 traces) relate to the much larger verification set (e.g., 20,181 problems), and why does the model generalize so well from few examples?
