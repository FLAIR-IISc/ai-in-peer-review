

# Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

## 1. Summary
The paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures to enable provable generalization. The authors argue that existing neural programming models fail to generalize beyond certain complexity thresholds because they learn spurious dependencies on training examples rather than the true program semantics. They demonstrate that recursion, a fundamental concept in programming, enables neural programs to learn the correct semantics and achieve perfect generalization. The authors instantiate this idea in the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture by modifying training traces to include recursive elements. They show that recursive neural programs achieve perfect generalization on four tasks (grade-school addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort) from very small training sets, and provide a verification procedure to formally prove perfect generalization by testing only a finite set of base cases and reduction rules. This is the first time provable guarantees of perfect generalization have been provided for neural programs.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural programming by demonstrating how recursion enables provable generalization. The approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and supported by both empirical results and a novel verification procedure.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses a clear gap in the literature: existing neural programming models fail to generalize beyond certain complexity thresholds, and there are no provable guarantees about their generalization. The authors correctly identify recursion as a natural fit for programming tasks that can reduce problem complexity and make generalization tractable.

The technical contribution is strong and well-executed. The paper provides a clear description of how to implement recursion in NPI, with concrete examples for four different programming tasks. The empirical results are compelling: recursive neural programs achieve perfect generalization on all tested tasks from very small training sets (200 traces for addition, 100 for bubble sort, etc.), while non-recursive versions fail for longer inputs. The verification procedure is novel and well-described, allowing the authors to prove perfect generalization by testing only a finite set of base cases and reduction rules rather than exhaustively testing all possible inputs.

The paper is technically sound with clear descriptions of the architecture, training procedures, and verification process. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, noting that the approach works well for tasks with well-defined base cases and reduction rules but might not be applicable to tasks with very large input domains (like MNIST digits or speech).

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach. While the paper mentions that the verification procedure becomes infeasible for tasks with very large input domains, a more detailed analysis of which types of tasks are amenable to this approach would strengthen the paper.
- The paper could provide deeper insight into why recursive formulations work better than non-recursive ones. A more detailed analysis of the learned representations or attention patterns would help explain why recursion leads to better generalization.
- The paper mentions that the NPI model naturally supports recursion but the authors didn't explicitly consider it. A more detailed explanation of what specific aspects of NPI enabled recursion would be valuable for future work.
- The comparison to prior work could be strengthened with more specific numbers on how much better the recursive models perform compared to previous state-of-the-art generalization results.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- How would you expect your verification procedure to scale to larger and more complex tasks? What specific modifications would be needed for it to work for tasks like sorting images or processing natural language?
- The paper mentions that for tasks with very large input domains, the verification procedure becomes infeasible. Could you provide a more concrete example of such a task and explain why the verification procedure would fail?
- In the verification procedure, you state that "it is critical to construct V correctly." What specific challenges did you face in constructing V for each of the four tasks, and how did you overcome them?
- The paper claims that recursion "makes it tractable to prove the generalization of neural programs." Could you elaborate on what specifically makes it tractable (e.g., the structure of the problem, the way recursion decomposes the problem) and how this differs from non-recursive approaches?
- The paper mentions that the training set can often be considerably smaller than the verification set. How do you expect this relationship to change as the complexity of the tasks increases?
