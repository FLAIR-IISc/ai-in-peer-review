This paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures to improve generalization. The core idea is that recursion helps the network learn true program semantics rather than spurious correlations, leading to provable guarantees of generalization. The authors demonstrate this by applying their approach to the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture on tasks like addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, achieving 100% accuracy and providing formal proofs of generalization.

**Reject.** While the idea of incorporating recursion into neural programs is interesting and potentially impactful, the paper lacks sufficient clarity and rigor in its presentation. The empirical results, while promising, are not presented convincingly, and the claim of "provable generalization" feels overstated given the limited scope of verification.

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of detail and justification regarding the construction of the training traces. While the authors state they modify the NPI training traces to include recursive elements, the specifics of *how* this is done are often glossed over. For instance, the description of the recursive topological sort is somewhat convoluted, and it's unclear how the recursive calls are strategically placed to facilitate learning. The appendix attempts to provide more detail, but it still feels incomplete and requires significant effort from the reader to fully understand. 

Secondly, the claim of "provable generalization" is weakened by the fact that the verification sets are quite small and tailored to the specific recursive formulations. The authors acknowledge limitations for problems with very large or infinite base cases, but the verification procedure's applicability even within the demonstrated tasks feels restricted and doesn't scale well. The justification that the recursive structure *automatically* simplifies the verification process needs further elaboration and formalization.  The paper does not clearly explain how they ensure the verification set truly covers all necessary base cases and reduction rules, beyond stating they followed a procedure described in Section 3.3.

Supporting Arguments:

*   **Clarity:** The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in explaining the methodology, particularly the creation of recursive training traces. The explanation often feels like a high-level description without enough concrete details.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The claim of "provable generalization" requires significantly more rigorous justification. The verification process is described but the reasoning why this covers all necessary cases is not clear. The connection between recursion *guaranteeing* generalization is not fully explained; it's more that recursion makes verification *tractable*.
*   **Novelty:** While the idea of applying recursion to neural programming is novel, the paper doesn’t sufficiently contextualize its contribution within the broader landscape of program synthesis and neural-symbolic integration. Are there related efforts in program verification or static analysis that could be leveraged?
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical results are presented without sufficient comparative analysis. It’s not clear how much the performance gains are due to recursion specifically, versus other factors like architectural choices or hyperparameter tuning. The tables in Section 4 simply state “Non-Recursive”, “Partially Recursive”, and “Full Recursive” without indicating which training procedure was used for each.

Additional Feedback:

*   **Explain the Training Trace Generation Process:** Provide a more detailed algorithm or pseudocode for generating the recursive training traces for each task. This is crucial for reproducibility and understanding.
*   **Formalize the Verification Procedure:** Develop a more formal description of the verification process, including a clear definition of the base cases, reduction rules, and how the verification set is constructed to ensure complete coverage.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** Include more comprehensive comparisons to existing neural programming models (e.g., the original NPI without recursion) on the same tasks, with consistent training budgets and hyperparameters.
*   **Scalability Analysis:** Discuss the limitations of the approach in terms of scalability to more complex tasks with larger state spaces.
*   **Generalization Discussion:** While the paper focuses on generalization to longer inputs, also discuss generalization to *different* types of inputs, especially for tasks like topological sort and quicksort.

Questions for the Authors:

1.  Can you provide a complete and executable script or pseudocode that generates the recursive training traces for each task?
2.  What guarantees do you have that the verification set truly covers all base cases and reduction rules for each task, especially considering the potential complexity of the state space?
3.  How does the performance of your recursive NPI compare to the original NPI, trained with the same hyperparameters, on the same tasks, but using non-recursive traces?
4.  How does the computational cost of training and verification scale with the complexity of the task and the size of the input?
5.  What are the practical limitations of this approach in terms of applying it to real-world programming tasks with more complex semantics and larger state spaces?
