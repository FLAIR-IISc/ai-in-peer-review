This paper tackles the problem of generalization in neural program synthesis, specifically addressing the limitations of existing models to perform well on inputs exceeding the complexity seen during training. The authors propose incorporating recursion into neural architectures as a means to improve generalization and, importantly, enable provable guarantees of correct behavior. They demonstrate this approach by integrating recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture for tasks including addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, achieving 100% accuracy on test inputs and providing formal proofs of generalization.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The core idea of using recursion to enhance generalization in neural program synthesis is novel and well-motivated. The empirical results are strong, demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods, and the ability to provide formal guarantees of correctness is a major contribution.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the problem, the proposed solution, and the experimental results. The motivation for using recursion – to reduce program complexity and facilitate reasoning about behavior – is compelling. The authors provide a clear explanation of how recursion is implemented within the NPI architecture and describe a verification procedure to prove generalization. The experimental setup is well-defined, and the results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The inclusion of four diverse tasks strengthens the conclusions. The detailed explanation of the verification process is particularly commendable, outlining how the base cases and reduction rules are identified and tested. The appendix provides valuable details regarding the program sets and trace generation, increasing the reproducibility of the work.

Several aspects could be improved. While the authors acknowledge limitations related to verification on tasks with extremely large or infinite base cases (e.g., motor control), a more detailed discussion of potential strategies for addressing these scenarios would be beneficial. The rationale behind the specific training trace construction for each task isn’t fully explored. While the paper mentions modifying training traces to enable recursion, a deeper investigation into different training strategies and their impact on learning recursive programs could strengthen the work. Expanding the discussion of related work to include more recent advances in program synthesis and neural-symbolic approaches would provide greater context. It would be helpful to have a more detailed discussion of the computational cost associated with the verification procedure, particularly as the complexity of the tasks increases. Finally, the paper heavily relies on the NPI architecture. Investigating the application of recursion to other neural program synthesis frameworks could reveal broader applicability and limitations of the approach.

Specific questions for the authors:

1.  Could you elaborate on the challenges of scaling the verification procedure to tasks with very large or infinite base cases, such as those found in motor control or game playing? Are there alternative verification techniques or approximation methods that could be employed in such scenarios?
2.  What principles guided the construction of the training traces for each task? Were any attempts made to learn recursive programs from non-recursive traces, and if so, what were the results?
3.  How does the computational cost of the verification procedure scale with the complexity of the task (e.g., the size of the graph in topological sort or the length of the array in quicksort)?
4.  Are there any known limitations to the applicability of recursion in neural program synthesis? For example, are there certain types of problems where recursion is unlikely to be effective?
5.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the modifications made to the original NPI implementation, beyond simply stating that it was made as faithful as possible? Were any significant architectural changes required to support recursion effectively?
