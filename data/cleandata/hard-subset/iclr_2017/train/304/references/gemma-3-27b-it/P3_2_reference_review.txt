## ICLR 2024 Review: Making Neural Programming Architectures Generalize via Recursion

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the crucial problem of generalization in neural program learning. The authors argue that existing approaches struggle to generalize beyond a certain complexity due to spurious dependencies learned by the network. They propose incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures as a key abstraction to improve generalization and, importantly, enable provable guarantees about program behavior. They demonstrate their approach by integrating recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) and showing perfect generalization on tasks like addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, backed by formal verification. This is a potentially significant contribution, as it moves beyond purely empirical evaluation towards a more rigorous understanding of neural program learning.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling argument, a sound methodology, and strong empirical results coupled with formal verification—a rare and valuable combination in this field. The idea of leveraging recursion for generalization and verifiability is novel and well-executed.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the generalization problem in neural program learning and provides a strong justification for exploring recursion as a potential solution. The limitations of existing approaches are well-highlighted.
* **Novelty & Significance:** The introduction of recursion as a core principle for designing generalizable neural programs is a novel contribution.  The ability to *prove* generalization, rather than simply demonstrating it empirically, is a significant advancement. This moves the field closer to building truly reliable and trustworthy AI systems.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments across four different tasks consistently demonstrate the superior generalization performance of the recursive NPI models compared to non-recursive counterparts. The results are convincing, especially considering the relatively small training sets used.
* **Rigorous Verification:** The proposed verification procedure is a key strength.  The authors don't just claim generalization; they provide a formal argument supported by a defined verification process.  The detailed explanation of the verification process and how recursion simplifies it is particularly insightful.
* **Well-Written & Structured:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Related Work Expansion:** While the paper cites relevant work, expanding the discussion of related work in areas like program synthesis, inductive programming, and formal methods could further strengthen the paper’s positioning. A deeper dive into how recursion interacts with techniques like curriculum learning would also be valuable.
* **Ablation Studies:** While the paper presents comparisons between recursive and non-recursive models, conducting more focused ablation studies to understand the impact of different aspects of the recursive implementation (e.g., the specific tail recursion optimization) would be insightful.  How does *partial* recursion compare to *full* recursion across all tasks?
* **Scalability Discussion:** The paper acknowledges limitations in verification for large input domains. A more in-depth discussion of potential strategies for scaling the verification procedure (e.g., using abstraction or sampling techniques) would be valuable. This doesn't need to be implemented, but outlining potential future directions would be beneficial.
* **Clarification of the Training Procedure:** The paper mentions modifying the training procedure to enable recursion. Providing more details about these modifications beyond simply using recursive traces would be helpful. Were any specific loss functions or regularization techniques employed?
* **Code Availability:** Making the code publicly available would greatly enhance the reproducibility and impact of this work.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the Verification Set Construction:** You mention analytical derivation for addition and empirical construction for other tasks. What are the key challenges in analytically deriving the verification set for topological sort and quicksort? Is it simply a combinatorial explosion, or are there specific complexities in those tasks that make analytical derivation difficult?
* **Training Trace Generation:** The training traces appear critical. Could you elaborate on the process of generating recursive traces? Is it a fully automated process, or does it require manual intervention? How sensitive are the results to the specific choice of training traces?
* **Computational Cost of Verification:**  What is the typical runtime for the verification process on the tasks presented? As you scale the problem size, how does the verification time grow?
* **Generalizability Beyond NPI:** You mention the general applicability of recursion to other Neural Programming Architectures.  Could you discuss potential challenges in applying this approach to architectures that don't have a natural stack-based calling convention like NPI? Would alternative implementations of recursion be required?
* **Robustness to Noise:**  How robust are the learned programs to noisy input data? Would the verification still hold in the presence of small perturbations in the input?




In conclusion, this is a high-quality paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and well-supported solution. The combination of strong empirical results and formal verification makes a compelling case for the benefits of incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the feedback provided.
