This paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural program synthesis architectures to improve generalization, arguing that recursion allows for provable guarantees of generalization. The authors demonstrate this on four tasks – addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort – using the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture. They achieve perfect generalization on these tasks with a small number of training examples and present a verification procedure to prove this generalization.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper addresses a significant challenge in neural program synthesis – generalization – with a novel and compelling approach. The authors convincingly demonstrate the benefits of incorporating recursion, achieving perfect generalization on multiple tasks and providing a framework for verifying this property.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty:** The explicit incorporation of recursion into neural program synthesis architectures and the focus on provable generalization are novel contributions. Previous work primarily focused on improving generalization empirically, without formal guarantees.
* **Technical Soundness:** The authors provide a clear explanation of how recursion can be implemented in the NPI architecture and demonstrate its effectiveness through experiments. The proposed verification procedure, while requiring careful construction of the verification set, is a sound approach to establishing generalization guarantees. The connection between the two properties of recursion (base cases and reduction rules) and the verification process is well-articulated.
* **Empirical Results:** The results are strong. Achieving 100% accuracy on the four tasks with a relatively small training set is impressive. The comparison with non-recursive approaches further highlights the benefits of the proposed method.  The ablation with partially recursive bubble sort provides insight into the necessity of full recursion for perfect generalization.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the concepts and methodology. The inclusion of example traces helps illustrate the approach.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Verification Set Construction:** While the authors acknowledge the difficulty of constructing the verification set for complex tasks, a more detailed explanation of the procedure would be beneficial. Specifically, how do they ensure the completeness of V for tasks like topological sort and quicksort?  Providing more details, perhaps with pseudocode, on the verification set generation process would strengthen the paper.
* **Scalability of Verification:** The authors acknowledge that the verification procedure may not be scalable to very large input domains. It would be valuable to briefly discuss potential future directions for addressing this limitation. Could techniques like symbolic execution or abstract interpretation be applied?
* **Choice of NPI:**  The paper heavily relies on the NPI architecture.  While a natural choice given the initial work on neural programming, briefly discussing the generalizability of the proposed approach to other architectures (e.g., those with different memory mechanisms) could broaden the impact of the work.
* **Training Data Size:** The paper uses a small number of training examples. While this demonstrates the efficiency of the approach, it would be helpful to investigate the performance with larger training sets. Does the performance plateau, or does it continue to improve with more data?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the process for ensuring the completeness of the verification set, particularly for tasks like topological sort and quicksort where analytical determination is difficult?  Could you provide more details on the heuristics used to randomly generate candidate inputs?
2. What are the limitations of the proposed verification procedure, and what future research directions could address the scalability issues for tasks with very large input domains?
3. Do you anticipate similar benefits from incorporating recursion into other neural program synthesis architectures beyond NPI? Are there specific architectural features that might enhance or hinder the effectiveness of this approach?
4.  The authors state that partially recursive bubble sort performs slightly better than non-recursive bubble sort, but still doesn’t achieve perfect generalization. Can you analyze why partially recursive is better, but not sufficient? What specific part of the program still relies on spurious dependencies?
