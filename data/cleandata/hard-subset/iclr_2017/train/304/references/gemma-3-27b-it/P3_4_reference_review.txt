## ICLR 2024 Review: Making Neural Programming Architectures Generalize via Recursion

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of generalization in neural program learning. The authors argue that existing approaches struggle to generalize beyond a certain complexity threshold due to spurious correlations learned by the network. They propose incorporating recursion into neural architectures as a key mechanism to overcome this limitation, providing a structured way to reduce complexity and improve generalization. They demonstrate this by modifying the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture to support recursive calls, and show empirically that the resulting programs achieve perfect generalization on sorting, addition, and graph traversal tasks, accompanied by *provable* guarantees of correctness via verification. This is a significant step forward, as it's rare to see formal verification applied to neural program learning.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** (with minor revisions).  The paper presents a compelling argument and demonstrates a significant improvement in generalization capabilities through a well-motivated and implemented approach. The addition of provable guarantees is a particularly strong contribution, setting this work apart from much of the existing literature.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Motivation:** The problem of generalization in neural program learning is well-established, and the authors clearly articulate the limitations of current approaches. The hypothesis that recursion can address these limitations is logical and well-grounded in the principles of program design.
* **Novelty & Significance:**  While NPI itself is not new, applying the concept of recursion *explicitly* within this framework, and demonstrating the resulting gains in generalization *with verification*, is novel and significant.  The ability to formally prove generalization is a major accomplishment.
* **Empirical Results:** The experiments across multiple tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the recursive approach. The authors show that recursive programs achieve 100% accuracy on test sets, while non-recursive models struggle.
* **Clear Explanation:** The authors explain the concept of recursion within the context of neural program learning clearly and provide a detailed explanation of their implementation in NPI.  The description of the verification process is also thorough and well-motivated.
* **Well-defined Verification Procedure:** The verification procedure is a key strength. The authors successfully bridge the gap between empirical performance and formal guarantees. Their discussion of the challenges and limitations of the verification process for larger state spaces is also appreciated.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Related Work:** The related work section could be expanded.  While the cited papers are relevant, a broader discussion of work on program synthesis, inductive programming, and formal methods could provide more context for the contribution.  Specifically, mentioning recent work on neural-symbolic program synthesis could be valuable.
* **Scalability & Computational Cost:** The paper focuses on demonstrating the principle. A discussion of the computational cost associated with training recursive programs and the scalability of the approach to larger, more complex problems would be beneficial. How does the recursive approach impact training time compared to non-recursive methods?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  Were any hyperparameter sweeps conducted?  A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter choices (e.g., learning rate, LSTM hidden size) would strengthen the empirical analysis.
* **Ablation Studies:**  While the comparison to the original NPI is valuable, more targeted ablation studies could further elucidate the contribution of recursion. For example, comparing the performance of partially recursive vs. fully recursive models across different tasks.
* **Generalization Beyond the Verification Set:** While the paper proves generalization *within the verification set*, discussing potential limitations of the verification approach and the possibility of generalization failing *outside* of the verified space would be prudent.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding Verification Scalability:**  You acknowledge the limitations of the verification procedure for very large state spaces.  Do you envision any techniques (e.g., abstract interpretation, compositional reasoning) to address this issue and enable verification of more complex programs?
* **The Role of the Training Trace:** The construction of the recursive training traces seems critical. How sensitive is the performance to the specific structure of these traces? Could a curriculum learning approach be used to *discover* recursive patterns from non-recursive traces, rather than explicitly encoding them?
* **Alternative Architectures:** You mention that NPI naturally supports recursion. Are there other neural programming architectures that would be more or less amenable to the recursive approach, and why?
* **Connection to Formal Programming Languages:** Do you see a direct correspondence between the learned recursive programs and equivalent programs written in a traditional formal programming language? Could the learned programs be automatically translated into such languages?
* **Generalization to other tasks**: Have you experimented with tasks beyond sorting, addition and graph traversal? Are there certain types of problems where the recursive approach is particularly beneficial?




Overall, this is a strong and impactful paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neural program learning. The combination of empirical results and formal verification is particularly compelling, and I believe it has the potential to inspire further research in this area.
