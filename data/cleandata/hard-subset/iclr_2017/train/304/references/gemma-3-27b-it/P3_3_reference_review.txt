## ICLR 2024 Review: Making Neural Programming Architectures Generalize via Recursion

**1. Summary**

This paper tackles the critical challenge of generalization in neural program learning. The authors argue that current approaches struggle to generalize beyond a certain complexity due to spurious dependencies learned by the network. They propose incorporating recursion into neural architectures, arguing it's a fundamental concept for program abstraction and generalization. They demonstrate this by applying recursion to the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort), achieving perfect generalization and, crucially, providing *provable* guarantees of this generalization via formal verification. This is a significant contribution, as it offers a path towards building more reliable and interpretable neural programs.

**2. Decision**

**Accept (with minor revisions)**. The paper presents a compelling argument for the benefits of recursion in neural program learning. The empirical results are strong, and the formal verification of generalization is a particularly noteworthy achievement – a rare and valuable contribution in this field. The approach is well-motivated, and the experiments demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Strong Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing neural program learning approaches concerning generalization. The hypothesis that spurious correlations hinder performance is well-supported by the observations in the introduction.
* **Novelty & Significance:** The explicit incorporation of recursion into neural program learning, and the demonstration of its impact on generalization, are genuinely novel. The key contribution is not merely *using* recursion, but framing it as a key abstraction for generalization *and* developing a verification method to formally prove it.
* **Empirical Results:** The experiments across four diverse tasks consistently demonstrate the superior performance of the recursive NPI models. Achieving 100% accuracy on unseen inputs is a strong result, especially when contrasted with the limitations of non-recursive approaches.
* **Formal Verification:** This is the most compelling aspect of the paper. The ability to *prove* generalization, even within the confines of the chosen tasks, is a significant leap forward. The description of the verification procedure is clear and convincing.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core concepts and methodology. The use of examples and algorithms enhances understanding.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement)**

These are suggestions to further strengthen the paper, not criticisms affecting my decision.

* **Related Work:** While the paper cites relevant work, a more in-depth discussion of related research on program synthesis, automated theorem proving, and formal verification would be beneficial. Specifically, connecting the formal verification method to existing techniques in program analysis would strengthen the argument.
* **Scalability of Verification:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of their verification procedure for very large state spaces. Expanding on this limitation and discussing potential alternative verification techniques for more complex scenarios would be valuable. Could techniques like model checking or abstract interpretation be applied?
* **Training Data & Hyperparameters:** Providing more details about the training data generation process (e.g., distribution of input lengths, types of examples) and the specific hyperparameters used for training would improve reproducibility.
* **Ablation Study:** While the results clearly show the benefit of full recursion, an ablation study examining the impact of *partial* recursion (as briefly mentioned for bubble sort) in more detail across all tasks would provide further insight into the importance of each recursive call.
* **Generalization to Different Architectures:** The work focuses on NPI. Discussing the challenges and potential benefits of applying this recursive approach to other neural program learning architectures (e.g., differentiable neural computers) would broaden the impact of the research.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

* **Verification Complexity:** The verification process seems to require careful construction of the verification set. What are the practical limitations of scaling this verification procedure to more complex tasks with larger state spaces? Are there automatic ways to construct the verification set beyond the manual approach described?
* **Alternative Training Methods:** You mention exploring new training methods beyond providing explicit execution traces. Could you elaborate on potential avenues for exploring this? (e.g., reinforcement learning, meta-learning).
* **Spurious Correlations:** While you hypothesize that non-recursive models learn spurious correlations, do you have any insights into *what* those correlations are specifically for the tasks you’ve investigated? Could visualization techniques be used to understand the hidden state representations and identify these patterns?
* **Robustness:** How robust are the trained recursive models to noisy or slightly malformed inputs? Do they maintain perfect generalization under these conditions, or does noise introduce errors?



In conclusion, this is a well-executed and impactful paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neural program learning. The novel application of recursion, coupled with the formal verification of generalization, sets this work apart and makes a strong case for its acceptance. Addressing the minor feedback points would further enhance the paper's quality and impact.
