## Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of generalization in neural program synthesis. The authors argue that existing approaches struggle to generalize beyond a certain complexity due to spurious dependencies learned by the networks. They propose incorporating recursion into neural architectures as a key mechanism to improve generalization and, crucially, enable *provable* guarantees of correctness. They demonstrate this by applying their approach to the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort), achieving perfect generalization and providing formal proofs of correctness through verification.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept.** This paper presents a significant and compelling contribution to the field of neural program synthesis. The core idea of leveraging recursion for improved generalization and provable correctness is novel and well-supported by the empirical results and the formal verification framework.  The authors demonstrate a clear path towards building more reliable and trustworthy neural programs.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The problem of generalization in neural program synthesis is well-established, and the authors accurately identify the limitations of current approaches. The proposal to utilize recursion is a clever and insightful solution, grounded in the principles of programming language design and formal methods. The emphasis on *provable* generalization is particularly noteworthy.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments across four diverse tasks demonstrate consistent and impressive results. The reported 100% accuracy on all valid inputs after training on a small number of examples is a strong indicator of the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The comparison with previous results highlights the significant improvement achieved through recursion.
* **Formal Verification:**  The development of a verification procedure and the successful demonstration of provable generalization is a major strength of this paper. This sets it apart from many other works in the field, which often rely solely on empirical evaluations. The detailed explanation of how the verification set is constructed and the logic behind the proofs is convincing.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the concepts and methodology. The connection between the theoretical motivation (recursion) and the practical implementation (modifying NPI training traces) is clearly articulated.

**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **Scalability of Verification:** While the verification procedure is demonstrated effectively, the paper acknowledges that it might not scale to tasks with very large input domains. Expanding on the potential limitations and exploring alternative verification techniques for such scenarios would strengthen the work.  Perhaps mentioning connections to formal verification techniques in program analysis could be helpful.
* **Generalization of Recursion Implementation:** The paper focuses on a specific implementation of recursion within the NPI framework. Discussing the potential for adapting this approach to other neural programming architectures and exploring different ways of incorporating recursion would broaden the impact of the research.
* **Ablation Studies:** While the paper demonstrates the improvement with full recursion, a more thorough ablation study comparing the performance of different levels of recursion (e.g., partial recursion on more tasks) would provide further insights into the benefits of each component.
* **Impact of Training Data:** The authors mention creating new training traces with recursive elements. Further detail on *how* these traces were generated (beyond stating they contained recursive calls) would be useful.  Were they automatically generated or manually crafted?  How sensitive is the performance to the quality of the recursive training data?



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding Scalability:**  You mention that the verification procedure may not scale to tasks with very large input domains.  What specific bottlenecks are you anticipating, and are there any existing techniques from formal verification or program analysis that you believe might be applicable to address this issue?
* **Regarding Training Data Generation:**  Could you elaborate on the process used to generate the recursive training traces?  Specifically, how did you ensure that the traces contained sufficient coverage of the base cases and reduction rules?
* **Regarding Alternative Architectures:** Do you foresee any significant challenges in adapting this recursive approach to architectures fundamentally different from NPI (e.g., architectures without an explicit stack)?  What modifications might be necessary?
* **Regarding Memory Capacity:** The recursive calls rely on the LSTM’s memory.  Have you explored the impact of LSTM size/capacity on the performance and generalization ability of the recursive models?  Could there be a point where memory limitations hinder the benefits of recursion?



In conclusion, this is a well-executed and impactful paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of neural program synthesis. The combination of strong empirical results, formal verification, and a clear conceptual framework makes it a compelling and noteworthy piece of research. I strongly recommend its acceptance.
