## Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of generalization in neural program synthesis. The authors argue that existing approaches struggle to generalize to inputs beyond a certain complexity due to spurious correlations learned by the network. They propose incorporating recursion into neural architectures as a key mechanism to address this, believing it promotes learning true program semantics and enabling provable generalization guarantees. They demonstrate this by applying their approach to the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture on tasks like addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, showing improved generalization and the ability to *prove* generalization with a verification procedure. This is a significant potential contribution, as provable guarantees are rare in deep learning.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling argument for the benefits of recursion in neural program synthesis and provides strong empirical evidence supporting this claim. The introduction of a verification procedure to *prove* generalization is particularly novel and valuable.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of current neural program synthesis methods regarding generalization and provides a solid rationale for exploring recursion as a solution. The connection to traditional programming principles is well-established.
* **Novelty:** The idea of explicitly incorporating recursion into neural architectures for improved generalization, and more importantly, the introduction of a formal verification procedure for these programs, is novel and represents a significant contribution to the field.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results across multiple tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) demonstrate that the recursive NPI models outperform non-recursive counterparts and achieve perfect generalization.  The table showing results on varying array lengths for bubble sort is particularly convincing.
* **Provable Generalization:**  The detailed explanation of the verification procedure and the claim of provable generalization is a major strength. The authors acknowledge the limitations of this procedure for extremely large state spaces, which is a sensible caveat.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **Clarify Training Data Generation:** While the paper mentions modifying the training traces, a more detailed explanation of how these recursive traces are generated would be beneficial.  Including pseudo-code or concrete examples of the trace modification process would strengthen the understanding.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** Implementing recursion inherently involves stack management. A brief discussion of the computational overhead (memory and time) associated with the recursive implementations compared to non-recursive implementations would be useful.
* **Hyperparameter Details:** More details about the hyperparameters used in training (e.g., learning rate, batch size, LSTM hidden size) would improve reproducibility and provide further insights into the model's performance.
* **Ablation Study on Recursion Levels:** For tasks like bubble sort where partial recursion was explored, an ablation study more thoroughly comparing the performance of partial vs. full recursion could be informative.  It would further clarify the necessity of full recursion for perfect generalization.
* **Future Work – Scalability:** The authors mention the limitations of the verification procedure for tasks with very large state spaces. Expanding on potential future directions to address this scalability issue (e.g., approximate verification techniques) would be valuable.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Trace Generation Details:** Could you provide a more concrete example of how the training traces were modified to incorporate recursion for the topological sort task? Specifically, how did you ensure that the generated traces encouraged the model to learn recursive calls?
* **Verification Complexity:** What is the computational cost associated with performing the verification procedure for a relatively complex task like quicksort on larger datasets?
* **Generalization to Other Architectures:**  You focused on NPI. Do you anticipate that the benefits of recursion would generalize to other neural program synthesis architectures (e.g., those based on transformers)? If so, what modifications might be necessary?
* **Role of LSTM Hidden State:** The paper mentions the LSTM hidden state being reset at each recursive call. How crucial is this reset for enabling the benefits of recursion? Would removing this reset affect generalization performance?



Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neural program synthesis.  The combination of strong empirical results and a novel verification procedure makes a compelling case for the importance of recursion in building more robust and generalizable neural programs.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions as suggested above.
