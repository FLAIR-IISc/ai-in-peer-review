## Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of generalization in neural program synthesis. The authors argue that current approaches struggle to generalize beyond a certain complexity due to spurious dependencies learned by the network. They propose incorporating recursion into neural architectures as a key abstraction to improve generalization and enable provable guarantees of correctness. They demonstrate this by applying their approach to the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort), achieving perfect generalization and, crucially, verifying this generalization through a formal proof process.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and compelling approach to improving generalization in neural program synthesis. The empirical results are strong, and the introduction of provable generalization guarantees is a significant contribution.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty & Motivation:** The core idea of leveraging recursion for generalization in neural programming is novel and well-motivated. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches and convincingly argue why recursion addresses those limitations. Connecting recursion to provable guarantees is a particularly strong aspect of the work.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results across all four tasks are impressive. Achieving 100% accuracy and, importantly, *proving* generalization is a substantial achievement. The comparison to previous NPI results demonstrates clear improvement. The inclusion of topological sort and quicksort adds to the breadth of the evaluation.
* **Provable Generalization:** This is the most significant contribution of the paper. The proposed verification procedure, while complex, is clearly explained and convincingly demonstrates that the learned programs are indeed correct for all valid inputs within the defined scope. The argument that recursion simplifies the verification process is well-articulated.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the concepts and methodology. The inclusion of detailed descriptions of the tasks and the program sets is helpful.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Scalability of Verification:** While the verification procedure is a significant contribution, the authors acknowledge limitations in its scalability to very large input domains. Expanding on potential future directions to address this scalability issue would be beneficial. Specifically, what alternative verification methods might be applicable in scenarios where exhaustive testing is infeasible?
* **Generalization of Recursion Concept:**  The paper focuses on implementing recursion within the NPI framework. While this is a strong demonstration, discussing the potential for adapting the recursive approach to *other* neural programming architectures would strengthen the paper's impact. Could the insights gained from this work be applied more broadly?
* **Ablation Study:** While the paper contrasts recursive vs. non-recursive approaches, a more fine-grained ablation study exploring the impact of *different levels* of recursion (e.g., the partial recursion in bubble sort) could provide deeper insights into the benefits of the proposed method.  Why is full recursion necessary for bubble sort, while less may suffice for addition?
* **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of potential limitations. For example, are there types of programs or tasks where recursion might *not* be beneficial? Are there potential challenges in learning recursive programs with more complex base cases or reduction rules?

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Scalability of Verification:** Given the scalability limitations of the verification procedure, what are the most promising avenues for extending this approach to problems with extremely large or infinite state spaces (e.g., continuous control tasks)?  Could techniques like formal methods abstraction or symbolic execution be integrated?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  How sensitive are the results to hyperparameters, particularly the learning rate and the size of the training set? Did you perform any hyperparameter optimization, and if so, what were the key findings?
* **Generalization to Unseen Program Structures:** The experiments focus on tasks where the core program structure (e.g., addition, sorting) is known. How well would this approach generalize to tasks requiring the discovery of *novel* program structures?  Would a curriculum learning approach, combined with the recursive framework, be helpful in these scenarios?
* **Tail Recursion Optimization:** The paper mentions tail recursion optimization. Was this optimization implemented in the experiments, and if so, did it significantly impact performance or the learning process?  If not, why was it omitted?




Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a significant contribution to the field of neural program synthesis. The combination of strong empirical results and a formal verification procedure makes a compelling case for the benefits of incorporating recursion into neural architectures. The suggested improvements are minor and aim to further strengthen the paper's impact and address potential limitations.
