## ICLR 2024 Review: Making Neural Programming Architectures Generalize via Recursion

**1. Summary**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of generalization in neural program learning. The authors argue that current neural program learning models struggle to generalize beyond a certain complexity due to spurious dependencies learned from training data. They propose incorporating recursion into neural architectures as a key mechanism to improve generalization and, crucially, enable *provable* guarantees of correctness. The authors demonstrate this approach by integrating recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture on four tasks – addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort – achieving perfect generalization and verifying it via a novel verification procedure based on covering base cases and reduction rules. This work represents a significant step towards building more robust and trustworthy neural program learners.

**2. Decision & Key Reasons**

**Accept.** This is a strong paper that addresses a critical issue in neural program learning: generalization. The introduction of recursion as a means to achieve provable generalization is a compelling contribution, and the empirical results are convincing. The verification procedure, while complex, is a vital component, and successfully demonstrating it on multiple tasks elevates this work above typical neural program learning papers.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of generalization in neural program learning and convincingly argues that existing approaches are limited by their inability to learn true program semantics. The motivation for using recursion, drawing parallels to traditional programming principles, is well-founded.
* **Novelty & Significance:** The idea of leveraging recursion for provable generalization in neural programs is a significant contribution. While recursion itself isn't new, its application to this domain with the explicit goal of verification is innovative.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The empirical results across four diverse tasks consistently demonstrate the superiority of the recursive approach over non-recursive baselines (or prior work) in terms of generalization. The fact that the models achieve 100% accuracy on all valid inputs is particularly impressive.
* **Rigorous Verification:** The proposed verification procedure is a key strength. The authors don't just claim generalization; they *prove* it by systematically covering base cases and reduction rules. This is a substantial contribution to the field.
* **Well-Explained Methodology:** The authors provide a clear explanation of how recursion is implemented within the NPI architecture, and how training traces were constructed to encourage recursive behavior. The description of the verification process is also detailed and understandable.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement)**

* **Scalability of Verification:** While the verification procedure is a major strength, its scalability to more complex tasks with large state spaces is a concern. The authors acknowledge this, but a more detailed discussion of potential solutions or limitations in such scenarios would be valuable. Could approximate verification techniques be explored?
* **Alternative Training Methods:** The paper focuses on modifying the training traces to induce recursion. Exploring other training methods that directly encourage recursive behavior, such as regularization terms or architectural constraints, could be a fruitful area for future work.
* **Ablation Studies:** While the results demonstrate the benefit of full recursion (e.g., in bubble sort), more detailed ablation studies exploring the impact of different components of the recursive approach (e.g., recursion in specific subprograms) would be helpful for understanding the mechanism at play.
* **Comparison to Curriculum Learning:** The paper rightly points out limitations of curriculum learning. However, it would be beneficial to explicitly compare the recursive approach to curriculum learning methods that incorporate structural information about the task (e.g., curriculum learning guided by the recursion tree).
* **Code Release:** Releasing the code for the experiments would greatly enhance the reproducibility of the results and accelerate further research in this area.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

* **Regarding Scalability:**  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the verification procedure as the problem size increases? What are the practical limitations of this approach for very complex programs?
* **Regarding Training:**  What specific techniques (e.g., learning rate scheduling, regularization) were found to be most effective for training the recursive NPI models? Did you observe any sensitivity to hyperparameter settings?
* **Regarding the Choice of NPI:** Why was NPI chosen as the architecture for demonstrating this approach? Are there other neural programming architectures that would be particularly well-suited for incorporating recursion?
* **Regarding Generalization Beyond the Verification Set:** While the verification set confirms correctness within the defined domain, how confident are you in the model’s ability to generalize to inputs that are significantly different from those seen in the training and verification sets (e.g., inputs with unexpected edge cases)?



Overall, this is a well-written and impactful paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neural program learning. The combination of theoretical insights, strong empirical results, and a rigorous verification procedure makes it a standout submission. I recommend it for acceptance.
