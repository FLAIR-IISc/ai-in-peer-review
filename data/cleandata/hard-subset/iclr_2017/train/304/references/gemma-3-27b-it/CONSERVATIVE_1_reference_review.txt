This paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural program learning architectures to improve generalization, arguing that it allows for provable guarantees of correctness. The authors implement this within the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) framework and demonstrate empirically that recursively trained models outperform non-recursive ones on tasks like addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort, achieving perfect generalization and allowing for formal verification of correctness.

**Reject.** While the idea of leveraging recursion for neural program generalization is interesting, the paper relies heavily on carefully constructed training data and a limited set of tasks, and the claims of "provable guarantees" feel overstated given the specific conditions required for verification. The empirical results, while positive, don’t convincingly demonstrate a broadly applicable advantage over existing methods, and the novelty is somewhat diminished by the reliance on a pre-existing architecture (NPI).

The primary concern is the level of scaffolding required to achieve the reported results. The success hinges on *specifically* crafting training traces to *explicitly* demonstrate recursion. This is a significant departure from typical neural program learning paradigms where the model is expected to *discover* algorithmic structure from input/output examples.  The paper acknowledges this (“we provide as an example one way to train a recursive neural program, by providing a certain training execution trace”), but frames it as a general principle. It’s unclear if this approach would generalize to more complex tasks where manually constructing such traces is infeasible. The leap from "recursion *can* help if explicitly taught" to "recursion is *essential* for generalization" is not adequately supported.

Furthermore, the verification procedure, while a positive aspect, is highly specific to the chosen tasks and relies on defining a complete and accurate "V" (set of valid inputs). The authors admit this becomes intractable for tasks with large or continuous input spaces. Therefore, the claim of "provable guarantees" is limited in scope and doesn’t translate to real-world applicability in many scenarios. The simplification of the bubble sort environment, as described in the appendix, to facilitate verification further weakens this claim. It feels like the verification process was *designed* to work for this specific, simplified setup, rather than representing a generalizable methodology.

The experimental setup also lacks a strong comparison to established baselines. While the paper mentions previous work (Vinyals et al., Kaiser & Sutskever, etc.), it doesn't present a direct comparison against these methods using a standardized evaluation protocol.  Showing that the recursive NPI outperforms a non-recursive NPI trained on standard data is valuable, but doesn't necessarily establish superiority over more sophisticated approaches.  The limited training data sizes (e.g., 200 traces for addition, 100 for bubble sort) raise concerns about the statistical significance of the results.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Broader Evaluation:** Evaluate the approach on a wider range of tasks with varying complexity and input spaces. Consider tasks beyond sorting and basic arithmetic.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** Conduct a thorough comparison against state-of-the-art neural program learning methods using a standardized evaluation protocol.
*   **Generalization of Training Procedure:** Explore methods for learning recursive programs without relying on explicitly constructed training traces. Could curriculum learning or other techniques be used to encourage the emergence of recursion?
*   **Scalability of Verification:** Investigate techniques for extending the verification procedure to tasks with larger and more complex input spaces.
*   **Clarify "Provable Guarantees":**  Acknowledge the limitations of the verification procedure and temper the claim of "provable guarantees" to reflect the specific conditions under which it holds.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of training traces?  Did you explore different trace generation strategies, and if so, what was the impact on performance?
*   What is the computational cost of generating the recursive training traces compared to standard input/output training data?
*   Can you provide more detail on how the verification set "V" is constructed for each task, and how you ensure its completeness?
*   Given the limitations of the verification procedure, what alternative methods could be used to assess the generalization capabilities of the learned models?
*   Would the approach be applicable to other neural program learning architectures besides NPI? If so, what modifications would be necessary?
