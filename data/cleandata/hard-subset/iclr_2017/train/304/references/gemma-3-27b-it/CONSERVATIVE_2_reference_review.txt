This paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural program learning architectures to improve generalization, arguing that recursion aids in learning true program semantics and enables provable generalization guarantees. The authors demonstrate this on four tasks – addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort – using a modified Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) and claim to achieve perfect generalization with a small number of training examples, backed by formal verification.

**Reject.** While the idea of leveraging recursion in neural program synthesis is interesting, the paper lacks sufficient novelty and rigor to warrant acceptance. The empirical results, while positive, are not convincing enough given the limited baselines and the potential for overfitting on the specifically crafted training data designed for recursive learning. The "provable generalization" claim feels overstated given the potentially limited scope of the verification sets and the dependence on a specific NPI architecture.

The primary concern lies with the lack of a comprehensive comparison to existing non-recursive approaches. The authors mention the limitations of previous methods but don't demonstrate a significant *improvement* beyond what could be achieved with more extensive training or architectural refinements of established techniques. Simply showing that a recursive model works well doesn’t demonstrate that recursion is *necessary* for good generalization. It’s possible the improved performance stems from the way the training data was specifically constructed to encourage recursive solutions. A fairer comparison would involve training existing NPI variants (without explicitly encouraging recursion) on the same recursively-designed training data.

Furthermore, the verification process, while a positive step, relies heavily on a carefully constructed verification set. The claim of "provable generalization" is limited to the scenarios covered by this set, which, as the authors acknowledge, may not scale to more complex or real-world scenarios. The description of how the verification sets were constructed feels somewhat hand-wavy; it's unclear how the authors ensured complete coverage of relevant base cases and reduction rules, particularly for tasks like topological sort and quicksort. The reliance on a modified domain-specific encoder for bubble sort to facilitate verification further weakens the argument.

Finally, the presentation of the recursive trace generation is somewhat convoluted. A clearer explanation of *how* the recursive traces were designed, and *why* they are superior for learning generalizable programs would be beneficial. The authors claim the non-recursive models learned spurious dependencies, but don’t provide evidence beyond the performance differences on the test sets.



**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Stronger Baselines:** Include a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art non-recursive neural program learning methods. Train these methods on the *same* recursively designed training data and compare performance.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to isolate the impact of recursion. For example, train NPI with partial recursion (as done for bubble sort) and assess the effect on generalization performance.
*   **Robustness Testing:** Evaluate the models on a wider range of test cases, including edge cases and adversarial examples, to assess their robustness.
*   **Verification Set Justification:** Provide a more rigorous justification for the construction of the verification sets, demonstrating complete coverage of base cases and reduction rules. Quantify the size and complexity of the search space explored to ensure completeness.
*   **Generalizability of Verification:** Discuss the limitations of the verification approach and how it might be extended to more complex tasks and environments.
*   **Clarify Trace Generation:** Provide a clearer explanation of the design choices behind the recursive trace generation, and provide more concrete reasons why it enables better generalization than alternatives.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How do you ensure that the improvements observed are due to recursion, and not simply due to the specific design of the recursive training traces?  Did you attempt to train non-recursive NPI variants on these same traces? If so, what were the results?
2.  Can you provide more details on the process of constructing the verification sets, specifically for topological sort and quicksort? How do you ensure that they cover all relevant base cases and reduction rules?
3.  What are the computational costs associated with verifying a recursively learned program compared to non-recursively learned programs?
4.  The paper mentions challenges in scaling the verification procedure to problems with very large input domains. What are some potential solutions to address this limitation?
5.  Why was a modification to the domain-specific encoder necessary for the bubble sort verification process? Does this indicate a weakness in the initial NPI formulation or in the proposed recursive approach?
