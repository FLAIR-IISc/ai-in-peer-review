This paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural program learning architectures to improve generalization and enable provable guarantees of correctness. The authors argue that recursion helps neural networks learn program semantics more effectively, leading to better performance on tasks like addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. They demonstrate empirically that recursive programs achieve perfect generalization with limited training data and, crucially, provide a verification procedure to *prove* this generalization, a novel contribution.

**Reject.** While the idea of leveraging recursion for neural program synthesis is promising and the empirical results are encouraging, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor but significant flaws—primarily in presentation, detail, and rigor—that collectively undermine confidence in the work. The paper feels rushed and insufficiently polished for ICLR acceptance.

The primary concern revolves around the lack of clarity and precision in several key areas. The description of the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) adaptation is often vague, and the concrete details of how recursion is implemented and enabled through training trace modification are not always sufficiently explained. The transition between stating the general concept of recursion and applying it to specific tasks feels disjointed. For example, the explanation of how recursion is implemented in quicksort feels particularly hand-wavy. The details regarding the precise form of the training traces, beyond high-level descriptions, are lacking. What specific modifications were made to the existing NPI traces to introduce recursive behavior? Providing example traces (beyond the partial illustration for addition) would be incredibly helpful.

Furthermore, the verification procedure, while a key contribution, is presented without sufficient detail. The construction of the verification set *V* is described as relying on "careful reasoning" and "analytically determining" elements, but the specifics of this reasoning are largely absent. The claim that the verification set for addition contains "20,181 input problems" feels arbitrary without justification for why that specific number is sufficient. The assertion that certain modifications (e.g., to the topological sort encoder) "enable" verification, implying a dependence on these changes for the proof, is also concerning – why were these changes necessary?  The justification for the sizes of the verification sets for topological sort and quicksort (73 and 10 respectively) appears suspiciously small and requires greater scrutiny. 

The experimental setup is also problematic. Training with only a handful of examples (e.g., 4 traces for quicksort) raises concerns about overfitting and the robustness of the results. While the paper claims perfect generalization, the limited training data casts doubt on this claim. A more thorough ablation study, exploring the impact of training set size and trace diversity, would be beneficial.  The lack of comparison to more recent and advanced neural program synthesis techniques is also a weakness.

Finally, the writing quality is uneven, with several instances of awkward phrasing and unclear explanations. The numerous citations, while demonstrating awareness of related work, are often abrupt and lack contextualization. 

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Clarify NPI adaptation:** Provide a more detailed explanation of how recursion is integrated into the NPI architecture. Include diagrams or pseudocode illustrating the stack management and recursive call mechanism.
*   **Detailed Trace Examples:** Provide complete examples of training traces (not just snippets) for each task to demonstrate how recursion is encoded.
*   **Rigorous Verification Procedure:** Elaborate on the process of constructing the verification set *V*. Explain how the base cases and reduction rules are identified and covered. Provide a formal definition of *V* for each task.
*   **Ablation Study:** Conduct an ablation study to evaluate the impact of training set size, trace diversity, and other hyperparameters on generalization performance.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** Compare the performance of the proposed approach to more recent and advanced neural program synthesis techniques.
*   **Improve Writing Quality:** Proofread the manuscript carefully to improve clarity, conciseness, and grammatical correctness.
*   **Justification for Verification Set Sizes:** Explain why the chosen verification set sizes (73 and 10) are sufficient for topological sort and quicksort, respectively.
*   **Explain encoder modifications:** Explain the rationale behind modifying the encoder for topological sort.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide the complete training traces (or a link to them) used for each task?
2.  What is the precise algorithm used to construct the verification set *V* for each task? Please provide a more detailed explanation than what is currently presented in the paper.
3.  Why are the verification set sizes for topological sort and quicksort so small (73 and 10, respectively)? What guarantees do you have that these sets are sufficient to cover all possible cases?
4.  How sensitive are the results to the size and diversity of the training set? Have you conducted an ablation study to evaluate this?
5.  How does the performance of your approach compare to state-of-the-art neural program synthesis techniques that do *not* explicitly incorporate recursion?
