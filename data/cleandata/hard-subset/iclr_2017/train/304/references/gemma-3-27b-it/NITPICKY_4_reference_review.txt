This paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural program learning architectures to improve generalization and enable provable guarantees of correctness. The authors argue that existing approaches struggle with generalization due to spurious dependencies and propose that recursion’s inherent structure simplifies the learning problem and facilitates verification. They demonstrate their approach on four tasks – addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort – using a modified Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture, achieving perfect generalization and providing formal proofs of correctness.

**Reject.** While the idea of leveraging recursion for improved generalization in neural program learning is promising, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet pervasive, flaws in presentation, experimental detail, and justification. These issues, collectively, undermine confidence in the results and the validity of the claims. The paper feels rushed and lacks the meticulous attention to detail expected of a strong ICLR submission.

Here’s a breakdown of my concerns:

1. **Lack of Rigorous Comparison:** The paper consistently frames improvements against “previous generalization results” without clearly defining or quantifying these baselines. For example, claiming outperformance on addition requires a direct comparison to the original NPI results on equivalent datasets and problem sizes. Simply stating it "outperforms" is insufficient. Similarly, the impact of recursion on bubble sort is demonstrated with tables showing improvement, but without a corresponding non-recursive model trained with the same hyperparameter tuning, it’s hard to isolate the effect of recursion.

2. **Insufficient Experimental Detail:**  The experimental setup is vague. Hyperparameters, network architectures (beyond LSTM layer count), and training procedures are glossed over. For instance, the Adam optimizer is mentioned, but learning rate, weight decay, and other relevant settings are absent. Reproducibility is a significant concern. The claim of achieving "perfect generalization" is bold and demands more rigorous presentation of the training and testing procedures to support it.

3. **Verification Procedure Concerns:** While the proposed verification procedure is conceptually sound, its implementation, as described, feels hand-wavy. The construction of the "verification set" V relies on analytical derivation for addition, but reverts to “careful reasoning” and “randomly generated graphs” for other tasks. The justification for the size of these sets is weak. The assertion that a 10-element array suffices for quicksort verification is particularly suspect and requires far more justification.  The description of how the procedure "tests" correctness is also lacking – what specific metrics are used?

4. **Overstated Claims & Lack of Nuance:** The claim of "provable guarantees of perfect generalization" is overstated. The verification process only confirms correctness on a *finite* set of test cases.  Extrapolating this to "perfect generalization" requires a stronger argument about the completeness of the verification set and the absence of corner cases not covered. The discussion of recursion’s role in simplifying the problem is also somewhat simplistic; while recursion *can* reduce complexity, its effectiveness depends heavily on the specific problem structure and the network's ability to exploit it.

5. **Formatting and Presentation:** The paper contains numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of bolding, font sizes, and spacing).  Several sentences are awkwardly phrased or grammatically incorrect. The figure captions are sometimes unclear or incomplete. These issues detract from the paper's professionalism.  The appendices, while providing some details, also suffer from similar issues of presentation.

6. **The Value of Training Trace Manipulation:** While a functional approach, the method of enabling recursion solely via modifying training traces feels more like a clever data engineering trick than a fundamental architectural contribution. It raises the question: is the recursion *learned* or simply *imposed* through the training data?



**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Provide a thorough, quantitative comparison to relevant baselines.** This includes NPI without recursion, and other relevant neural program learning architectures.
*   **Include a detailed description of the experimental setup.** Specify all hyperparameters, network architectures, training procedures, and evaluation metrics.
*   **Provide a more rigorous justification for the size and construction of the verification sets.** Clearly articulate how the set covers all relevant base cases and reduction rules.
*   **Elaborate on the verification procedure.** Describe the specific tests performed and the criteria for determining correctness.
*   **Address the limitations of the verification procedure.** Acknowledge that it only provides guarantees on a finite set of test cases and discuss the potential for generalization errors.
*   **Improve the clarity and conciseness of the writing.** Proofread carefully for grammatical errors and awkward phrasing.
*   **Consider exploring alternative methods for incorporating recursion.**  Can the network *discover* recursive patterns from data, rather than being explicitly trained on recursive traces?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a quantitative comparison of the training time and the number of parameters between the recursive and non-recursive NPI models for each task?
*   What specific measures were taken to ensure that the verification sets are complete and cover all relevant base cases and reduction rules for topological sort and quicksort?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters?  Have you performed an ablation study to assess the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the recursive NPI model?
*   What would be the next step in extending the verification process to domains where an exhaustive enumeration of base cases and reduction rules is intractable, such as tasks involving continuous inputs?
*   How did you determine the number of training traces needed to achieve perfect generalization (or near-perfect generalization)?



In conclusion, while the core idea has merit, the paper’s numerous shortcomings prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. A major revision addressing the concerns outlined above is necessary for this work to be considered a strong contribution to the field.
