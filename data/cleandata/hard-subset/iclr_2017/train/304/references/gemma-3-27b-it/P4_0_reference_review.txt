This paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural programming architectures to improve generalization. The core idea is that recursion reduces program complexity and allows for provable guarantees of generalization, something currently lacking in neural program synthesis. The authors demonstrate this by applying recursion to the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) on four tasks – addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort – achieving 100% accuracy and provable generalization with small training sets.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper addresses a significant issue in neural program synthesis – generalization – with a novel and well-motivated approach. The empirical results are compelling, and the claim of provable generalization is a significant contribution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty:** The explicit incorporation of recursion into neural program learning is a novel contribution. While recursion is fundamental to programming, it hasn’t been systematically explored in the context of neural program synthesis. The connection to provable generalization is particularly insightful.
* **Technical Soundness:** The paper clearly explains how recursion is implemented within the NPI architecture using a stack. The explanation of the verification procedure and the conditions required for provable generalization are well-defined. The examples provided for each task demonstrate a clear understanding of the underlying algorithms and how recursion simplifies them.
* **Empirical Validation:** The results on all four tasks are strong. Achieving 100% accuracy on complex tasks with limited training data is impressive, and the contrast with the performance of non-recursive models highlights the benefits of the proposed approach. The verification results, although limited by the scope of the tasks, support the claim of provable generalization.
* **Well-Motivated:** The paper effectively articulates the limitations of existing neural program synthesis techniques regarding generalization and provides a compelling argument for why recursion offers a solution.  The discussion of spurious dependencies learned by non-recursive models is insightful.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Scalability of Verification:** The paper acknowledges the limitations of the verification procedure for very large input domains. Expanding on potential solutions for scaling verification would strengthen the work. Are there alternative verification techniques (e.g., runtime monitoring, abstract interpretation) that could be explored?
* **Training Data Generation:** The method for generating training traces, while effective, could be further elaborated. What strategies were used to ensure the diversity and quality of the traces? Exploring methods to automatically generate effective training traces for recursive programs would be valuable.
* **Ablation Studies:** While the results demonstrate that recursion improves generalization, conducting more detailed ablation studies (e.g., varying the amount of recursion, exploring different stack implementations) could provide further insights into the benefits of the proposed approach.
* **Comparison to Alternative Approaches:** The introduction briefly mentions curriculum learning as an alternative approach. A more thorough comparison, outlining the limitations of curriculum learning and the advantages of recursion in more detail, could be beneficial.
* **Code Release:** Releasing the code would facilitate reproducibility and encourage further research in this area.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. The paper mentions potential challenges in scaling the verification procedure to large input domains. Could you elaborate on the computational complexity of the current verification approach, and provide a more detailed discussion of potential alternative verification strategies for larger problems?
2. How sensitive is the performance of the recursive models to the quality and diversity of the training traces? Were any specific strategies employed to ensure that the generated traces effectively capture the core recursive logic of each task?
3. In the quicksort example, you mention that the recursion isn’t purely tail recursive. Did you observe any performance differences or challenges due to this non-tail-recursive nature, and how might this be addressed in future work?
4. The verification process hinges on constructing set V. Could you provide some examples of how to identify problematic input sequences that need to be incorporated into V, and why those sequences are important for ensuring generalization?
