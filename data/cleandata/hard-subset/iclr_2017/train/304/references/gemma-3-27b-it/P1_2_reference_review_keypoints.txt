- Summary: Proposes adding recursion to neural programming (NPI) to improve generalization and enable provable correctness, demonstrated on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Decision: Recommend accept with minor revisions due to novelty, strong results, and provable guarantees.  
- Motivation: Clearly identifies poor generalization and spurious dependencies in prior neural program synthesis and motivates recursion from programming practice.  
- Novelty: Leveraging recursion for generalization and verifiability is a novel and significant shift from capacity/training-focused approaches.  
- Empirical results: Recursive NPI achieves perfect generalization across four tasks, outperforming non-recursive baselines.  
- Provable generalization: Authors provide a clear verification procedure and succeed in formally proving correctness for learned recursive programs.  
- Writing and organization: The paper is well-written, logically structured, and connects theory to implementation effectively.  
- Limitation discussion: Needs a more thorough discussion of verification scalability and which problem types are hard to verify.  
- Ablation studies: Recommend more experiments on sensitivity to training data and minimal data needed for perfect generalization.  
- Alternative architectures: Suggest discussing applying recursion beyond NPI to other neural programming architectures to broaden impact.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Request brief analysis of result sensitivity to hyperparameter choices.  
- Verification scalability question: Ask for potential research directions or approximation strategies to handle large state spaces.  
- Training data dependence question: Ask how sensitive learning is to specific examples and whether a smaller, strategic dataset could suffice.  
- Recursion depth question: Ask about issues with recursion depth, tail recursion optimization, or architectural constraints.  
- Generalization question: Ask which types of programming problems might be less suitable for the recursive approach and task characteristics influencing applicability.
