## Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of generalization in neural program synthesis, a significant challenge where models often fail to extrapolate beyond the complexity of their training data. The authors argue that the lack of explicit recursion in current architectures hinders generalization and propose incorporating recursion as a key component to improve performance and, crucially, enable *provable* generalization guarantees. They demonstrate this by integrating recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort), achieving 100% accuracy and formally verifying generalization via a procedure based on checking base cases and reduction rules. This is a novel and potentially impactful contribution to the field.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept.** The paper presents a compelling argument for the importance of recursion in neural program synthesis, supported by strong empirical results and, most notably, a novel verification procedure leading to provable generalization guarantees. This is a significant advancement over existing work in the area.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Clear Problem Statement and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the generalization problem in neural program synthesis and effectively motivates the use of recursion as a solution. The critique of existing approaches – their tendency to learn spurious correlations and lack of formal guarantees – is well-articulated.
*   **Novelty:** Introducing recursion explicitly into neural program synthesis and demonstrating provable generalization is a genuinely novel contribution.  While recursion is a common concept in programming, its formal application and verification within a neural network context is a significant step forward.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The results across four diverse tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The 100% accuracy achieved after training on limited data is impressive. The comparison against the original NPI results (showing its limitations) strengthens the claims.
*   **Provable Generalization:**  The proposed verification procedure is a standout contribution. The ability to formally prove generalization provides a level of confidence absent in most neural program synthesis research. The detailed explanation of how recursion facilitates this verification is persuasive.
*   **Well-Structured and Presented:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The explanations of the NPI architecture and the recursive modifications are clear and concise.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Explain the connection to program induction:** The paper touches on program induction, but a more explicit connection to the broader field would strengthen the work. Discussing how this approach relates to inductive program synthesis, version space algebra, or other relevant concepts would be beneficial.
*   **Scalability of Verification:**  The authors acknowledge limitations of the verification process for problems with very large state spaces. Exploring potential approaches to overcome this limitation (e.g., abstraction, compositional verification) would be a valuable direction for future work.
*   **Training Data Size:** While impressive, the training data size is relatively small.  A more thorough investigation of the sensitivity of the results to the size and diversity of the training data would be beneficial.
*    **Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not discuss how sensitive the performance of the recursive NPI model is to various hyperparameters. A discussion of these sensitivities would be useful.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Regarding the Verification Set:**  You mention constructing the verification set by analytically determining the base cases and reductions for addition, and then randomly generating candidates for other tasks.  Could you elaborate on the method used to ensure *completeness* of the randomly generated sets? What criteria do you use to determine that a set sufficiently covers all necessary cases?
*   **Alternative Architectures:** You mention that recursion can be implemented in other Neural Programming Architectures besides NPI. Have you explored implementing recursion in any other architectures? If so, what were the results? If not, what are the main challenges preventing implementation in other architectures?
*   **Future Directions - Training:** You state that a future direction is to explore new ways to train recursive neural programs without explicit training execution traces. Could you elaborate on potential approaches you are considering?
*   **Limitations of the Verification Approach:** You mention that the verification procedure does not scale to tasks with very large state spaces. What alternative verification approaches might be applicable in those settings? Would techniques like model checking or abstract interpretation be viable?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of neural program synthesis. The introduction of recursion and the associated provable generalization guarantees are a substantial advance. The suggestions above are minor and aimed at further strengthening an already excellent work.
