- Paper proposes using recursion in neural program architectures to improve generalization and enable provable correctness.  
- Applied recursion to the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Empirical results show perfect generalization (100% accuracy) on valid inputs after training on few examples.  
- The recursion-based approach substantially outperforms prior methods and addresses spurious dependencies.  
- Authors develop a formal verification procedure and provide proofs of correctness for the tasks.  
- Verification set construction and proof logic are clearly explained and convincing.  
- Paper is well-written and effectively connects theoretical motivation to practical implementation.  
- Concern: verification procedure may not scale to tasks with very large input domains.  
- Suggest exploring links to formal verification/program analysis and alternative verification techniques for scalability.  
- Limited to a specific recursion implementation in NPI; generalization to other architectures is not fully explored.  
- Recommend more ablation studies to assess different levels or components of recursion.  
- Request more detail on how recursive training traces were generated and sensitivity to trace quality.  
- Questions posed: scalability bottlenecks and applicable verification techniques, trace generation and coverage of base/reduction cases, adapting recursion to non-NPI architectures, and impact of LSTM memory capacity.
