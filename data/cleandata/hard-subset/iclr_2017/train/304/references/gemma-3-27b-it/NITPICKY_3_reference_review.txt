This paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural program synthesis, arguing it improves generalization and enables provable guarantees of correctness. The authors modify the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture to support recursive calls and demonstrate empirically that the resulting models achieve perfect generalization on sorting, addition, and graph tasks with surprisingly small training sets. A key claim is the ability to *prove* generalization given verification sets covering base cases and reduction rules.

**Reject.** While the idea of leveraging recursion for improved generalization in neural program synthesis is promising, and the empirical results are initially intriguing, the paper is marred by a concerning level of methodological imprecision, lack of experimental detail, and insufficiently rigorous justification for the "provable generalization" claim. The review process was hindered by numerous small errors and inconsistencies that collectively undermine confidence in the work.

My primary concerns are threefold: (1) **Lack of Rigorous Verification Details:** The description of the verification process is vague. Simply stating a verification set “covers” base cases and reduction rules isn’t sufficient. The process needs explicit, quantifiable criteria and a transparent explanation of how the verification set was constructed and used. The reference to “matching traces” is not helpful without details on how the equivalence is assessed (e.g., exact match of all outputs, tolerance for minor numerical differences). (2) **Insufficient Experimental Detail:** The training setups are described too briefly. How were the initial training traces generated? What hyperparameters were used? What is the model size? The claim of achieving perfect generalization with *very small* training sets raises a red flag, and a more comprehensive experimental setup is needed to rule out overfitting or sensitivity to initialization. (3) **Overselling the "Provable Generalization" Claim:** The authors repeatedly emphasize “provable generalization.” While recursion *can* aid in formal verification, the verification process described is not a formal proof in the traditional sense. It's an empirical check on a limited set of inputs. A true proof would require more robust, mathematically grounded arguments.

Here's a more detailed breakdown of issues:

*   **Typographical Errors and Inconsistencies:** The paper is riddled with minor errors (e.g., inconsistent formatting of equations, ambiguous variable names, missing punctuation) that, while individually minor, collectively indicate a lack of careful attention to detail.
*   **Vague Methodology:** Phrases like “modified the training procedure” (Section 1) are unhelpful without specific details. What modifications were made? The description of generating the traces for the recursive version of NPI is also insufficient.
*   **Lack of Ablation Studies:** It's unclear how much of the improvement is due to the recursion itself versus other potential factors, such as changes in the training data or hyperparameter tuning. Ablation studies are needed to isolate the impact of recursion.
*   **Overstated Claims:** The statement "This is the first time one can provide provable guarantees of perfect generalization for neural programs" is a significant claim that isn't adequately supported by the presented evidence.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The authors should discuss relevant work in formal methods for neural networks and program verification. This would help contextualize their contribution and acknowledge potential limitations.
*   **Limited Scope of Tasks:** The tasks considered (addition, sorting, topological sort, quicksort) are relatively simple. It's unclear whether the approach would generalize to more complex programming tasks.
*   **Missing Discussion of Limitations:** There’s a lack of discussion regarding the limitations of the proposed approach, such as its scalability to larger programs or its applicability to tasks with infinite state spaces.

**Additional Feedback (to improve the paper, not affecting the rejection decision):**

*   Provide a detailed pseudocode algorithm for generating the recursive training traces.
*   Include a table summarizing the hyperparameters used for each experiment.
*   Clearly define the criteria for constructing the verification sets and provide a detailed explanation of how the verification process works.
*   Provide a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of the “provable generalization” claim.
*   Consider adding ablation studies to isolate the impact of recursion.
*   Expand the related work section to include relevant research in formal methods and program verification.
*   Address the concerns raised about the limited scope of tasks.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a detailed description of the verification procedure, including the specific criteria used to construct the verification sets and the method for comparing the outputs of the learned model with the outputs of the correct program?
2.  What are the specific modifications made to the NPI training procedure to enable learning recursive programs?
3.  What hyperparameters were used for each experiment, and how were they chosen?
4.  How does the performance of the recursive model compare to the non-recursive model when trained on the same data?
5.  What are the computational costs associated with the verification process?
6.  How does this approach scale to more complex programs with larger state spaces?
7.  Could you provide the code used to generate the traces?
