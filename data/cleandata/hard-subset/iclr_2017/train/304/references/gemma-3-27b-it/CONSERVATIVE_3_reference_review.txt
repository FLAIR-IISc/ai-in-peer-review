This paper proposes incorporating recursion into neural program learning architectures to improve generalization, arguing that it allows for provable guarantees of correctness. The authors demonstrate this on four tasks – addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort – using a modified Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture, achieving perfect generalization and formal verification of correctness on these tasks.

**Reject.** While the idea of leveraging recursion for improved generalization in neural program synthesis is intriguing, the paper suffers from a lack of convincing empirical evidence beyond the chosen tasks and a reliance on highly specific training procedures to *enable* recursion, rather than the architecture itself *discovering* it. The claims of “provable generalization” are also overstated, as the verification process is narrowly tailored to the specific recursive formulations and is not broadly applicable.

The primary issue is the lack of comparative analysis. The paper demonstrates that a recursively-trained NPI performs well, but provides limited comparison to non-recursive NPI implementations or other state-of-the-art neural program synthesis methods *without* explicit recursion. Simply stating that existing methods fail beyond a certain complexity threshold is insufficient; a direct comparison on the same tasks, using the same training data sizes, is crucial. The performance gains attributed to recursion could, at least partially, be due to the specifically crafted training traces required to elicit recursive behavior. The authors acknowledge this ("we show that by modifying the training procedure…"), effectively admitting that the architecture isn’t inherently learning recursion, but rather being guided towards it. This is a significant limitation. 

Furthermore, the verification process, while presented as a novel contribution, appears tightly coupled to the manually designed recursive formulations.  The description relies on a complete understanding of the base cases and reduction rules, which, while possible for these relatively simple tasks, seems impractical for more complex problems where such a precise characterization is difficult to obtain.  The authors themselves acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion.  The claim of “provable guarantees” feels misleading in this context. A more accurate description would be “verifiable correctness for a specific, hand-crafted recursive solution.”

Finally, the paper lacks sufficient detail about the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters and training data size. The experiments use a very small number of training examples, raising concerns about overfitting. A more thorough investigation of the model’s robustness would be necessary to establish the generalizability of the approach.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Stronger Baselines:** Include a comparison against non-recursive NPI implementations trained with standard curriculum learning. Also, compare performance against other recent neural program synthesis methods.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Investigate the impact of the training trace modifications on the learned model's behavior. Can similar performance be achieved with less explicit guidance?
*   **Scalability:** Explore the performance of the recursive NPI on more complex tasks with larger state spaces.
*   **Broader Verification:** Discuss the limitations of the verification process and potential approaches for scaling it to more complex scenarios.  Consider whether formal methods beyond simple trace verification could be applied.
*   **Clarify "Provable Generalization":** Reframe the claim of “provable generalization” to more accurately reflect the scope and limitations of the verification process.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Beyond the specifically crafted training traces, what mechanisms could be incorporated into the NPI architecture to encourage the *discovery* of recursive patterns, rather than being explicitly guided towards them?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the size of the training set? Have you performed experiments with larger training sets to assess the model’s robustness?
3.  Can you elaborate on the computational cost of the verification process and how it scales with the complexity of the task?
4.  The paper acknowledges the difficulty of applying the verification procedure to tasks with large state spaces. What alternative verification techniques might be applicable in such scenarios?
5.  Can you show the performance of the non-recursive NPI on these same tasks, trained with curriculum learning, to demonstrate the relative performance gain of the recursively-trained NPI?
