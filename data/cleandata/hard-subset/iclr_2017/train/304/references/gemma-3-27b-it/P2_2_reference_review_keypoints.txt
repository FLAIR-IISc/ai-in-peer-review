- Paper addresses poor generalization in neural program synthesis by introducing recursion into architectures to learn true program semantics and enable provable generalization.
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.
- Clear problem statement and motivation linking recursion to programming principles and generalization.
- Novel contribution: explicit incorporation of recursion into neural architectures plus a formal verification procedure for provable generalization.
- Strong empirical results: recursive NPI outperforms non-recursive models on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort with perfect generalization.
- Convincing task-specific evidence (e.g., bubble sort results across varying array lengths).
- Provable generalization is a major strength, with authors acknowledging limitations for very large state spaces.
- Paper is well-written and organized, making methods and claims easy to follow.
- Request to clarify how recursive training traces are generated, with pseudo-code or concrete examples suggested.
- Ask for discussion of computational overhead (memory/time) from stack management in recursive implementations.
- Request full hyperparameter details (learning rate, batch size, LSTM sizes) for reproducibility.
- Suggest an ablation study comparing partial vs. full recursion, especially for bubble sort.
- Recommend expanding future work on scalability of verification (e.g., approximate techniques) for large state spaces.
- Question: provide concrete example of modified training traces for topological sort and how they encourage recursive calls.
- Question: what is the computational cost of verification for complex tasks like quicksort on larger datasets?
- Question: would recursion benefits generalize to other architectures (e.g., transformers) and what modifications would be needed?
- Question: how crucial is resetting the LSTM hidden state at each recursive call for enabling recursion’s benefits?
