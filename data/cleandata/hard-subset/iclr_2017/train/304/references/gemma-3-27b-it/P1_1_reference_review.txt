## Review of "MAKING NEURAL PROGRAMMING ARCHITECTURES GENERALIZE VIA RECURSION"

**1. Summary**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of generalization in neural program synthesis. The authors observe that current neural program learning models struggle to generalize to inputs beyond a certain complexity, often learning spurious correlations rather than true program semantics. They propose incorporating recursion into neural architectures as a key abstraction to improve generalization and, crucially, enable provable guarantees about program behavior. They demonstrate this by integrating recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture and applying it to four tasks: addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort. The results show significant improvements in generalization, achieving 100% accuracy on test inputs and, remarkably, allowing for formal verification of perfect generalization given appropriate verification sets.

**2. Decision**

**Accept** with enthusiasm. This paper presents a novel and compelling approach to a critical problem in neural program synthesis. The idea of leveraging recursion to enforce semantic understanding and enable provable generalization is both insightful and well-executed. The empirical results are strong, and the demonstration of formal verification is particularly impressive.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The core idea of using recursion to improve generalization in neural program synthesis is novel. The existing literature focuses on scaling up architectures or curriculum learning, whereas this work targets a fundamental issue of semantic understanding. Achieving *provable* generalization is a significant step forward, offering a level of confidence absent in previous approaches.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results across the four tasks are compelling. The authors consistently demonstrate improved generalization performance with the recursive NPI compared to previous approaches. The 100% accuracy on test sets is noteworthy, and the scalability to more complex tasks like topological sort and quicksort is encouraging.
* **Formal Verification:** The formal verification framework is a major strength. The authors provide a clear and principled approach to proving generalization, and successfully apply it to their learned programs. This is a landmark achievement in the field, potentially opening the door to more reliable and trustworthy AI systems.
* **Clear Explanation and Implementation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the problem, the proposed approach, and the experimental setup. The integration of recursion into the NPI architecture seems relatively straightforward and well-motivated.

**4. Additional Feedback**

The paper is very strong, but a few points could be addressed to further enhance its impact:

* **Broader Architecture Exploration:** While the NPI architecture is a good starting point, it would be valuable to briefly discuss the applicability of this recursive approach to other neural programming architectures (e.g., Differentiable Neural Computers, Neural Turing Machines).  Are there specific challenges or adaptations needed for these different architectures?
* **Ablation Studies:**  More detailed ablation studies could help pinpoint the specific benefits of each aspect of the recursive formulation (e.g., tail recursion optimization, the specific training trace modifications).
* **Complexity of Verification:** The verification process, while impressive, sounds complex. A more detailed explanation of the challenges in constructing the verification set, especially for tasks with large input spaces (like topological sort) would be helpful. What approximations or simplifications were made during this process?
* **Limitations & Future Work:** The paper acknowledges limitations regarding verification for very large input spaces. Expanding on this discussion and outlining a research agenda to address these limitations would strengthen the work.  Exploring methods for *learning* recursive structures rather than relying on pre-defined training traces is another promising avenue.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** What is the impact of incorporating recursion on the computational cost of training and inference? Is there a trade-off between generalization performance and computational efficiency?

**5. Questions for the Authors**

* **Regarding the choice of NPI:** Why was NPI chosen as the architecture to implement the recursive approach? Were there specific characteristics of NPI that made it particularly suitable for this task, compared to other architectures?
* **Verification Set Construction:** Could you elaborate on the criteria used to determine the "completeness" of the verification set?  How confident are you that the verification sets cover all relevant base cases and reduction rules, and what steps were taken to ensure this?
* **Scalability of Verification:** What are the primary bottlenecks in scaling the verification process to larger and more complex programs? What potential strategies could be employed to address these bottlenecks?
* **Non-Recursive Performance:** The paper mentions that previous approaches struggle with generalization. Could you provide a more direct comparison of the non-recursive NPI (with the same training data) to your recursive NPI, highlighting the specific areas where the recursive version significantly outperforms the non-recursive version? This would strengthen the case for the benefits of recursion.
* **Training Data Synthesis:** How sensitive are the results to the specific methods used for generating the training data, especially the recursive traces? Would different strategies for generating these traces lead to similar performance improvements?



Overall, this is an excellent paper with a significant contribution to the field of neural program synthesis. The authors have presented a compelling argument for the benefits of incorporating recursion, and their demonstration of provable generalization is a major achievement. I strongly recommend acceptance.
