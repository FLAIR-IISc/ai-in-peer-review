- Tackles neural program synthesis generalization by addressing spurious correlations that limit extrapolation.  
- Proposes incorporating recursion into neural program architectures to capture true program semantics and enable provable generalization.  
- Demonstrates the approach on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) using the NPI architecture.  
- Achieves perfect generalization on these benchmarks and verifies this generalization via a formal procedure.  
- Recommendation: accept with enthusiasm due to the novelty and strength of results.  
- Clear problem statement and motivation: recursion reduces complexity and encourages semantic learning.  
- Novel contribution: explicit use of recursion in neural program learning combined with formal verification.  
- Strong empirical evidence: perfect generalization across all evaluated tasks and insightful comparison to non-recursive NPI.  
- Formal verification is a standout feature, allowing proofs of generalization rather than only empirical claims.  
- Paper is well-written, organized, and includes reproducibility materials (appendix with program sets and traces).  
- Concern: need discussion on scaling to more complex programs and larger state spaces.  
- Suggest exploring alternative training procedures (e.g., regularizers or curriculum learning) to induce recursion beyond trace modification.  
- Clarify how dependent the approach is on NPI’s stack-based design and whether it generalizes to other neural programming architectures.  
- Verification complexity is potentially high; analyze computational cost and scaling of the verification procedure.  
- Questions for authors: what are the main verification bottlenecks and possible optimizations?  
- Can the model autonomously discover recursive abstractions, or does it require guided training traces?  
- How robust is the learned recursive program to noisy inputs or environmental variations?  
- How does this work connect to traditional program synthesis approaches that use recursion, and can synthesis tools/concepts be integrated?
