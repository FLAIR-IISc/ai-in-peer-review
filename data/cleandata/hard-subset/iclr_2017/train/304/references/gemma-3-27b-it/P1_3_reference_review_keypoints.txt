- Tackles generalization in neural program synthesis by incorporating recursion into neural architectures (NPI).  
- Argues recursion reduces program complexity and enables provable generalization guarantees.  
- Demonstrates approach on four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, quicksort) with 100% accuracy on unseen inputs.  
- Introduces a formal verification procedure to prove generalization.  
- Decision: Accept with enthusiasm.  
- Novelty: Recursion as a key abstraction is well-motivated and conceptually insightful.  
- Empirical results are strong and convincing, especially improvement over prior NPI results.  
- Provable generalization is the standout, novel contribution despite limits on verification scale.  
- Paper is clearly written and explains recursive implementation in NPI well.  
- Concern: Scalability of the verification procedure to complex tasks and large state spaces needs discussion.  
- Concern: Sensitivity to training data size and specific example selection requires further analysis.  
- Suggestion: Broader comparison to other program synthesis methods (sketching, meta-learning) is needed.  
- Suggestion: More ablation studies (e.g., partially recursive functions) to isolate impact of components.  
- Suggestion: Release code to facilitate reproducibility.  
- Question: What are the main bottlenecks and mitigation strategies for scaling verification (abstraction, sampling)?  
- Question: How robust are results to different training sets; were sensitivity experiments run?  
- Question: How does the recursive approach compare to state-of-the-art methods in data efficiency and scalability?  
- Question: Does recursion improve human interpretability of learned programs; are recursive functions understandable?  
- Conclusion: Highly impactful work that merits acceptance for advancing reliable and interpretable neural programs.
