- Proposal: Incorporate recursion into neural program architectures (NPI) to improve generalization and enable provable correctness; demonstrated on addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort with formal proofs.  
- Decision: Accept — significant contribution toward verifiable program correctness.  
- Novelty & importance: Recursion as a novel, intuitively appealing abstraction that moves learning from empirical success to verifiable generalization.  
- Technical soundness: Clear implementation of recursion in NPI and a well-defined (though complex) verification procedure.  
- Empirical validation: Strong results — 100% accuracy on valid inputs after training on small datasets across four tasks.  
- Clarity: Well-written with helpful examples, diagrams, and reproducibility details in appendices.  
- Limitation discussion: Needs deeper exploration of other limitations, e.g., problem types unsuited to recursion and when recursion adds unnecessary complexity.  
- Ablation studies: More detailed comparisons (e.g., partial vs. full recursion) across tasks would clarify the contribution of each recursive component.  
- Computational cost: Request discussion of training and verification compute overhead relative to non-recursive approaches.  
- Generalizability to other architectures: Should discuss applying recursion beyond NPI (e.g., differentiable neural computers).  
- Verification procedure question: How were verification sets for topological sort and quicksort generated to ensure coverage of base cases and reduction rules?  
- Scalability question: How to scale verification to larger/complex problems and could approximate verification methods be used?  
- LSTM interaction question: How does recursion interact with the LSTM’s hidden state/memory, and are certain configurations/training strategies preferable?  
- Dataset sensitivity question: How sensitive are results to training set size/diversity, and would larger datasets improve generalization?  
- Overall verdict: Strong paper with important contribution; provable generalization could meaningfully advance reliable AI systems.
