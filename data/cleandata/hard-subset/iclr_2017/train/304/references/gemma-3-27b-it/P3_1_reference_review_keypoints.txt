- Summary: Proposes incorporating recursion into Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) to improve generalization and provide provable correctness on tasks like addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort.  
- Decision: Accept — strong paper with significant empirical and theoretical contributions.  
- Motivation: Clearly identifies poor generalization due to spurious dependencies and argues recursion reduces complexity and enables proofs.  
- Novelty: Introducing recursion as a core principle for neural program learning and proving generalization is a novel and important contribution.  
- Empirical results: Recursive NPI models achieve near-perfect generalization across four diverse tasks and outperform non-recursive counterparts.  
- Verification: Presents a sound verification procedure to formally prove generalization given a suitable verification set.  
- Architecture: Builds on the well-established NPI framework, facilitating comparison and leveraging prior work.  
- Limitation discussion: Paper mentions limits for very large input domains but could discuss scalability and other drawbacks more thoroughly.  
- Alternative implementations: Focuses on one NPI-based recursion; should consider and discuss other ways to incorporate recursion into different neural architectures.  
- Ablation studies: More targeted ablations (e.g., varying recursion depth or partial vs. full recursion) would clarify which aspects drive improvements.  
- Visualization: Visualizing learned recursive programs or stack traces would help interpret how recursion is represented and executed.  
- Question — verification scalability: How can the verification procedure scale to tasks with very large input domains (e.g., sampling or approximation methods)?  
- Question — transfer learning: Would recursion-trained models transfer better to related tasks, and which properties of recursion enable transfer?  
- Question — learning recursion implicitly: Can models discover recursive structure from non-recursive data via curriculum learning or reinforcement learning?  
- Question — relation to program synthesis: How does this approach relate to formal-specification-based program synthesis and could the methods be combined?
