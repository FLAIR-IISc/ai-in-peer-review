## ICLR 2024 Review: Making Neural Programming Architectures Generalize via Recursion

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the crucial problem of generalization in neural program learning. The authors argue that existing approaches struggle to generalize beyond a certain complexity threshold due to spurious correlations learned by the network. Their key contribution is the introduction of recursion as a core architectural principle to improve generalization and, crucially, enable *provable* generalization guarantees. They demonstrate this by integrating recursion into the Neural Programmer-Interpreter (NPI) architecture across four tasks (addition, bubble sort, topological sort, and quicksort), showing empirically perfect generalization from small training sets and providing a formal verification procedure to prove this generalization.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept.** This paper presents a compelling and significant contribution to the field of neural program learning. The authors identify a key limitation of existing methods, propose a well-motivated and elegant solution, and convincingly demonstrate its effectiveness both empirically and – uniquely – with formal verification. The ability to *prove* generalization is a particularly strong aspect of this work and represents a substantial advance.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the generalization problem in neural program learning and motivates the use of recursion as a natural and powerful solution, drawing parallels to its role in traditional programming. The analysis of why existing methods fail (spurious correlations) is insightful.
* **Novelty & Significance:**  While NPI itself isn’t novel, the explicit incorporation and exploitation of recursion *within* NPI, coupled with the provable generalization guarantees, is a significant contribution.  The concept of enabling formal verification for neural programs is a particularly strong aspect.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results across four different tasks consistently show perfect generalization after training on a remarkably small number of examples when using the recursive formulation. The comparison to non-recursive approaches highlights the benefit of the proposed method.
* **Rigorous Verification Procedure:**  The development and explanation of the verification procedure are excellent.  The authors clearly outline the process of constructing the verification set and demonstrating how it allows for provable generalization.  The acknowledgment of limitations with scalability of this approach to more complex domains is also appreciated.
* **Well-Written & Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the results are impressive, a brief discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity would be valuable. How robust are the results to changes in learning rate, LSTM hidden size, etc.?
* **Ablation Studies:**  The authors show the benefit of *full* recursion (e.g., for bubble sort), but more detailed ablation studies examining the effect of *partial* recursion (as in the bubble sort example) would strengthen the analysis.  What specifically contributes to the improvement?
* **Computational Cost:** Briefly discussing the computational cost of training and verification would be helpful, especially given the formal verification procedure.
* **Generalization Beyond the Task Set:** While the four tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach, discussing potential limitations and challenges when applying this method to other types of programming tasks (e.g., tasks requiring more complex data structures) would be beneficial.
* **Code Release:** Releasing the code would significantly enhance the reproducibility and impact of this work.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Verification Scalability:**  You acknowledge the difficulty of scaling the verification procedure to very large state spaces.  Are there potential approaches to approximate or scale the verification process for more complex problems?  Perhaps focusing verification on critical code paths or using abstraction techniques?
* **Trace Construction:**  The construction of recursive training traces seems crucial.  How much does the performance depend on the quality of these traces?  Could the model learn to introduce recursion itself, perhaps through reinforcement learning or other techniques, rather than relying on explicitly crafted training data?
* **Alternative Architectures:** You focus on NPI. Do you believe the benefits of recursion are specific to NPI, or would they generalize to other neural programming architectures (e.g., those with different memory mechanisms)?  Have you considered applying this approach to other architectures?
* **Impact of LSTM Size:**  You mention using a 2-layer LSTM.  Did you experiment with different LSTM sizes and, if so, how did that impact performance and the ability to achieve provable generalization?



In conclusion, this is a well-executed and insightful paper with a significant contribution to the field of neural program learning. The approach is novel, the results are compelling, and the addition of formal verification is a major step forward. I strongly recommend accepting this paper.
