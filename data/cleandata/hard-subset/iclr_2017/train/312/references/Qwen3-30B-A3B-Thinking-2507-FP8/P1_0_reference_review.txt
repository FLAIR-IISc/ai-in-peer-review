

# Peer Review: Neural Architecture Search

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a gradient-based method for automatically discovering high-performing neural network architectures. The authors make a compelling case that designing neural network architectures currently requires substantial expert knowledge and time, and propose a novel approach where a recurrent neural network (the "controller") generates architectural descriptions as variable-length strings. The controller is trained using policy gradient methods to maximize the validation accuracy of the "child" networks it designs. The key contributions are:

1. A flexible framework for searching variable-length architecture spaces, which is more general than existing hyperparameter optimization methods that work only in fixed-length spaces.
2. The ability to discover novel architectures for both convolutional networks (on CIFAR-10) and recurrent cells (on Penn Treebank) that outperform human-designed architectures.
3. Empirical demonstrations that NAS can find architectures with better accuracy and efficiency than previous state-of-the-art models, with the CIFAR-10 model achieving 3.65% test error (better than human-invented architectures) and being 1.05x faster, and the Penn Treebank recurrent cell achieving 62.4 perplexity (3.6 perplexity better than the previous state-of-the-art).

The paper is well-motivated, with a thorough discussion of related work in hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, program synthesis, and meta-learning. The method is clearly described, with detailed explanations of the controller RNN, the reinforcement learning training procedure, and extensions to handle skip connections and recurrent architectures.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper presents a novel, well-motivated approach to neural architecture search that addresses a significant problem in deep learning research.
2. The empirical results are strong, showing clear improvements over previous human-designed architectures on standard benchmarks with thorough ablation studies and comparisons.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning. The NAS approach is fundamentally different from prior work in that it can search variable-length architecture spaces, which is a critical limitation of existing hyperparameter optimization methods. The paper's approach is also more practical than neuro-evolution methods, which are typically too slow for large-scale applications.

The experimental results are scientifically rigorous:
- For CIFAR-10, the NAS-generated architecture achieves 3.65% test error, which is better than the best human-invented architectures (3.74% for DenseNet) while being 1.05x faster.
- For Penn Treebank, the NAS-generated recurrent cell achieves 62.4 perplexity, which is 3.6 perplexity better than the previous state-of-the-art.
- The paper provides thorough ablation studies (e.g., with and without stride prediction, with and without pooling layers) and demonstrates the approach's robustness through control experiments (e.g., adding more functions to the search space).
- The comparison against random search (Figure 6) convincingly shows that the policy gradient approach outperforms random search.

The paper also demonstrates transfer learning capabilities, showing that the discovered recurrent cell generalizes well to different language modeling tasks and even improves performance in a machine translation framework (GNMT) without additional tuning.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, these suggestions would further enhance its quality and impact:

1. **Computational Cost Discussion**: The paper doesn't adequately address the computational cost of the NAS approach. Training 12,800 architectures (each requiring 50 epochs) with distributed training is expensive. It would be valuable to include a discussion of the computational resources required and how this might scale to larger problems or more complex architectures.

2. **Search Space Definition**: The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of how the search space was defined. For example, why were specific filter sizes (1, 3, 5, 7) and number of filters (24, 36, 48) chosen? A brief discussion of how the search space might be expanded or refined for different tasks would be helpful.

3. **Novelty Analysis**: While the paper claims to find "novel" architectures, it would be valuable to provide a more detailed analysis of how these architectures differ from existing ones. A simple comparison of the NAS-generated architecture with ResNet or DenseNet would help readers understand the nature of the novelty.

4. **Limitations Section**: The paper would benefit from a dedicated limitations section that discusses potential drawbacks of the approach, such as:
   - How the method might be affected by different initialization schemes
   - Potential biases in the search space
   - How the approach might scale to tasks with different data characteristics

5. **Comparison with More Recent Work**: The paper cites Huang et al. (2016) for DenseNet, but more recent work on neural architecture search (e.g., the 2017 NASNet paper) has emerged. A brief comparison with these more recent approaches would help situate the paper in the current landscape.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help me better understand and assess the paper, I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1. The paper mentions that the NAS approach "can design good models from scratch, an achievement considered not possible with other methods." Could you clarify which specific methods you're referring to that cannot do this, and why they cannot design models from scratch?

2. For the CIFAR-10 experiments, you state that "the model with 40 filters added is 1.05x as fast as the DenseNet model that achieves 3.74%." Could you provide more details on the exact computational complexity comparison (e.g., FLOPs, number of parameters) to better understand this speed advantage?

3. The paper mentions that "the controller RNN has to select a filter height in [1, 3, 5, 7]" for convolutional layers. Why was a filter height of 1 included in the search space? In practice, 1×1 convolutions are typically used for channel transformations rather than spatial feature extraction, so it's unclear why the controller would find them beneficial for the main convolutional layers.

4. For the Penn Treebank experiments, you state that "the model that achieves 64 perplexity is also more than two times faster because the previous best network requires running a cell 10 times per time step." Could you clarify what you mean by "running a cell 10 times per time step" and how the NAS cell avoids this inefficiency?

5. The paper mentions that "the code for running the models found by the controller on CIFAR-10 and PTB will be released." Could you provide an estimated timeline for when this code will be available, as this would be valuable for the community to reproduce and build upon your work?
