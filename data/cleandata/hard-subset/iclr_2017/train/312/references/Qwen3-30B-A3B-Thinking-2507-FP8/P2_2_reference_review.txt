

# ICLR Review: Neural Architecture Search

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper introduces "Neural Architecture Search" (NAS), a reinforcement learning-based method for automatically designing neural network architectures. The authors propose using a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate architectural hyperparameters as a sequence of tokens. The controller is trained using policy gradients, where the reward signal is the validation accuracy of the generated architecture (the "child network"). The key contributions are:

- A novel approach to architecture search that can handle variable-length architectures, unlike previous methods that were limited to fixed-length spaces
- Demonstrated success on two major benchmarks: CIFAR-10 image classification (achieving 3.65% test error, better than DenseNet's 3.74% with 1.05x speed improvement) and Penn Treebank language modeling (achieving 62.4 perplexity, 3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art)
- A method that can incorporate skip connections and branching layers, enabling more complex architectures
- A framework for generating recurrent cell architectures, with a new cell architecture that outperforms LSTM

The paper provides thorough experimental validation across multiple settings, including ablation studies and control experiments comparing to random search. The authors also release code and integrate their RNN cell into TensorFlow, enhancing the paper's practical impact.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

My key reasons for acceptance are:
1. The problem addressed (automated neural architecture design) is highly relevant and important in deep learning, with clear practical significance.
2. The paper provides strong empirical evidence supporting its claims, with thorough experiments on two major benchmarks showing clear improvements over human-designed architectures.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper excels in several critical aspects:

**Strong Motivation and Positioning in Literature:**
The paper provides an excellent literature review that clearly positions NAS in relation to hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution algorithms, and program synthesis. It effectively identifies the limitations of existing methods (fixed-length search spaces, slow search-based methods) and demonstrates how their approach overcomes these limitations. The connection to related work like learning to learn and meta-learning is particularly well-executed.

**Rigorous Experimental Validation:**
The experimental section is exceptionally thorough. The authors:
- Conduct experiments on two major benchmarks (CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank) with appropriate data preprocessing and augmentation
- Include multiple ablation studies (e.g., with and without stride prediction, with and without pooling layers)
- Provide detailed comparisons to human-designed architectures (DenseNet, ResNet, etc.)
- Include control experiments (random search comparison) that clearly demonstrate the superiority of their method
- Report both validation and test performance with appropriate statistical rigor

**Novel Technical Contributions:**
The paper makes several significant technical contributions:
- The use of a controller RNN to generate variable-length architectural descriptions
- The incorporation of skip connections and branching layers through an attention mechanism
- The extension to recurrent cell architectures with a tree-based representation
- The distributed training approach that enables practical training of the controller

The results are impressive: on CIFAR-10, their method finds an architecture that outperforms DenseNet (3.65% vs 3.74% error rate) while being faster, and on Penn Treebank, their cell achieves 62.4 perplexity, a 3.6 improvement over previous state-of-the-art.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these suggestions would further improve its quality and impact:

1. **Computational Cost Analysis:** The paper should provide more explicit discussion of the computational cost. How many GPU hours did it take to find the architectures? How does this compare to other architecture search methods (e.g., evolutionary approaches, random search)? The paper mentions distributed training but doesn't quantify the total computational resources required.

2. **Comparison to Recent Architectures:** The comparison to human-designed architectures could be strengthened by including more recent state-of-the-art models (e.g., EfficientNet, MobileNet, or recent ResNet variants) rather than focusing only on DenseNet. This would better demonstrate the generality of their approach.

3. **Limitations Discussion:** The paper should more thoroughly discuss the limitations of their approach. For example, how well would this method scale to more complex tasks like object detection or segmentation? What are the specific challenges when applying this to different domains?

4. **Clarification on Learning Rate:** The paper mentions adding learning rate as a prediction in Section 3.3 but doesn't elaborate on how this was handled in the experiments. A brief clarification would be helpful.

5. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions a small grid search for hyperparameters after finding the best architecture, it would be valuable to understand how sensitive the controller's performance is to its own hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, controller architecture).

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding and ensure confidence in my assessment, I'd appreciate answers to these questions:

1. Could you provide more details about the computational cost of your method? Specifically, how many GPU hours were required to find the architectures on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank? How does this compare to other architecture search methods in the literature?

2. In your CIFAR-10 results, you mention "DenseNet model that achieves 3.46% error rate (Huang et al., 2016b)" but state it's not an exact comparison because it uses 1x1 convolutions. Could you clarify how the comparison would change if you applied the same 1x1 convolution technique to your architecture?

3. For the Penn Treebank experiments, you mention using "shared embeddings" in some results. Could you clarify whether this was part of the architecture search or an additional technique applied to all models, and how it affected the performance of the searched architecture?

4. In Figure 6, you compare policy gradient against random search. Could you provide the specific numbers for the "best model using policy gradient" and "best model using random search" at the end of training? The paper states the policy gradient approach is better, but the exact performance difference would be helpful.

5. The paper claims their method "can design good models from scratch, an achievement considered not possible with other methods." Could you elaborate on what specific previous methods you're referring to, and how your approach fundamentally differs in being able to achieve this?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neural architecture search. The method is novel, well-executed, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages over previous approaches. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the ICLR conference.
