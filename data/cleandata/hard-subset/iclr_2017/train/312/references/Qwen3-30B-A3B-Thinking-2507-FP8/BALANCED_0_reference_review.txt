

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method that uses a recurrent neural network controller to automatically generate neural network architectures. The controller is trained via policy gradients to maximize the validation accuracy of the architectures it generates. The authors apply this approach to two tasks: designing convolutional architectures for CIFAR-10 image classification and recurrent cell architectures for Penn Treebank language modeling. They claim their method discovers novel architectures that outperform human-designed ones, achieving 3.65% test error on CIFAR-10 (better than previous models) and 62.4 perplexity on Penn Treebank (3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art).

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes an interesting contribution to automated machine learning, but the empirical results are insufficiently rigorous to support the claims of superior performance over human-designed architectures. The lack of detailed search space definition, computational resource reporting, and statistical validation undermines the scientific claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core idea of using a controller RNN with policy gradients to search for neural architectures is novel and well-motivated, drawing appropriate connections to related work in hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis. However, the empirical validation suffers from several critical shortcomings:

First, the search space is not clearly defined or justified. The paper mentions "variable-length string" representation but doesn't specify the exact boundaries of the space or how constraints were chosen. For instance, the controller's ability to predict stride values and pooling layers is presented as an improvement, but the paper doesn't explain how these additions expanded the search space or whether they were necessary for the claimed improvements.

Second, the claimed improvements are marginal and not statistically validated. The CIFAR-10 result (3.65% vs. 3.74% for DenseNet) is only 0.09% better, and the paper doesn't provide multiple runs or statistical significance testing to confirm this difference is meaningful. The "1.05x faster" claim is also unsubstantiated without clear computational efficiency metrics.

Third, the comparison with human-designed architectures is superficial. The paper states their model is "better than most human-invented architectures" but only compares against a few top-performing human-designed models, not a comprehensive set. The DenseNet comparison is further complicated by the fact that the authors' model doesn't use 1x1 convolutions, which are a key component of DenseNet's efficiency.

Fourth, the computational resources required for the NAS process are not adequately reported, making it difficult to assess the practicality of the approach. The paper mentions "800 GPUs concurrently" but doesn't provide total GPU hours or cost, which is essential for evaluating the feasibility of the method.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper for future publication, the authors should:

1. Provide a detailed, well-justified description of the search space, including all architectural parameters and constraints. A table summarizing the search space would be helpful.

2. Report all computational resources used (GPU hours, cost) to assess practicality, and include multiple independent runs with standard deviations for all results.

3. Expand the comparison with human-designed architectures to include a broader set of models, not just the top-performing ones. A systematic comparison would better support the claim of outperforming "most human-invented architectures."

4. Clarify the "1.05x faster" claim with concrete metrics (inference time, training time, FLOPs) and explain the comparison methodology.

5. Include a more thorough analysis of the search process, such as learning curves showing how the controller's performance improves over time.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How many human-designed architectures were evaluated against the NAS-generated architectures, and what criteria were used to select the comparison set?

2. What is the exact meaning of "1.05x faster" - is this referring to inference speed, training speed, or both? What specific computational metric was used for this comparison?

3. How many independent runs were performed for each experiment, and what were the standard deviations of the reported results?

4. Why was the search space constrained to the specific parameter ranges used (e.g., filter sizes [1,3,5,7]), and how might this constraint affect the generality of the results?

5. What is the total computational cost (in GPU hours) of the NAS process, and how does this compare to other architecture search methods?
