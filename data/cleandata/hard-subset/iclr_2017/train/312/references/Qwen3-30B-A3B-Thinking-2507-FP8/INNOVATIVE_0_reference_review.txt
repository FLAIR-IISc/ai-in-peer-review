

# Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel gradient-based method for automatically discovering neural network architectures, which the authors call Neural Architecture Search (NAS). The key insight is that neural network structures can be represented as variable-length strings, allowing a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate these strings. The controller is trained using reinforcement learning, with the validation accuracy of the generated architecture serving as the reward signal. The authors demonstrate that NAS can discover convolutional architectures for image recognition (achieving 3.65% error on CIFAR-10, outperforming previous human-designed architectures) and recurrent cell architectures for language modeling (achieving 62.4 perplexity on Penn Treebank, 3.6 perplexity better than the previous state-of-the-art). The paper makes a compelling case for a fundamentally new approach to architecture design that moves beyond human expertise.

## Decision
Accept. The paper presents a genuinely novel and bold approach to neural architecture search that breaks from existing paradigms. The core idea of using a controller RNN to generate architectural hyperparameters as sequences and optimizing it with reinforcement learning is a significant conceptual advance that addresses a fundamental limitation in the field.

## Supporting Arguments
This paper excels in novelty, which I highly value as a reviewer. While prior work has explored hyperparameter optimization (limited to fixed-length spaces), neuro-evolution (too slow at scale), and program synthesis (not directly applicable to neural architectures), NAS presents a fundamentally different approach that can search variable-length architectural spaces. This is a substantial conceptual leap that opens up new research directions.

The empirical results are impressive and well-supported. On CIFAR-10, NAS discovers an architecture that achieves 3.65% error (better than the previous best human-designed architecture at 3.74%) while being 1.05x faster. On Penn Treebank, the NAS-discovered recurrent cell achieves 62.4 perplexity, a 3.6 perplexity improvement over the previous state-of-the-art, and is more than two times faster than the previous best network. The results are scientifically rigorous, with proper validation procedures, ablation studies, and comparisons to strong baselines.

The paper is well-motivated and properly situated in the literature, clearly articulating the limitations of existing methods and positioning NAS as a solution to the variable-length search space problem. The authors also provide strong evidence of generalization by transferring the discovered cell to character language modeling and machine translation tasks.

## Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, I would recommend:

1. Providing more concrete details about the computational cost of NAS. How many GPU hours does it take to discover a good architecture? This is crucial for assessing practicality.

2. Including a comparison of the parameter count and inference speed between the NAS-discovered architecture and the best human-designed architecture on CIFAR-10, rather than just reporting error rates.

3. Clarifying the relationship between the controller's architecture (2-layer LSTM with 35 hidden units) and the quality of the discovered architectures. How sensitive is the approach to the controller's architecture?

4. Adding a brief discussion of how this approach could be extended to other domains beyond image recognition and language modeling, such as reinforcement learning or graph neural networks.

## Questions for Authors
1. How does the computational cost of NAS compare to random search or Bayesian optimization methods for architecture search? What is the trade-off between search time and final model performance?

2. The paper states the NAS-discovered architecture on CIFAR-10 is "the shallowest and perhaps the most inexpensive architecture among the top performing networks." What specific architectural features (e.g., filter shapes, skip connections) contribute to this efficiency?

3. The NAS-discovered architecture achieves 3.65% error on CIFAR-10, while the DenseNet model achieves 3.74%. The paper notes that DenseNet uses 1x1 convolutions to reduce parameters, which the NAS method did not. How would the NAS architecture perform if it were allowed to use 1x1 convolutions?

4. The paper mentions that the NAS-discovered recurrent cell is "more than two times faster" than the previous best network. What specific architectural components of the cell make it faster, and how does this speedup translate to real-world applications?

This paper represents a significant conceptual advance in neural architecture search that opens up a promising new research direction. The empirical results are strong and the approach is novel and bold, making it a valuable contribution to the field.
