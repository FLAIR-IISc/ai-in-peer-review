

# Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces "Neural Architecture Search," a novel gradient-based method for automatically discovering neural network architectures without human expertise. The authors propose using a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate architectural hyperparameters as a sequence of tokens, with the validation accuracy of the resulting "child" network serving as the reward signal for policy gradient training. The method is applied to both convolutional architectures for CIFAR-10 image classification and recurrent cell architectures for Penn Treebank language modeling. The authors claim their method discovers novel architectures that outperform human-designed models, achieving 3.65% test error on CIFAR-10 (1.05x faster than the current best model) and 62.4 test perplexity on Penn Treebank (3.6 perplexity better than previous state-of-the-art).

## 2. Decision
Reject

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision
The paper presents an interesting and well-motivated approach with promising empirical results, but the claims are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. The core technical contribution is sound, but the presentation of results and comparisons to prior work creates significant concerns.

The most critical issue is the lack of a fair and comprehensive comparison to human-designed architectures. The paper states their CIFAR-10 model "achieves a 3.65 test set error, while being 1.05x faster than the current best model," but does not provide a clear comparison to a representative set of human-designed models. The authors claim their architecture is "better than most human-invented architectures," but the paper only compares to a single DenseNet model (3.74% error) rather than a broader set of human-designed architectures. The speed comparison is also unclear—without knowing the parameter counts or computational resources used for comparison, the "1.05x faster" claim is difficult to evaluate.

The Penn Treebank results similarly lack context. The paper claims a "3.6 perplexity better than the previous state-of-the-art," but doesn't clarify whether this comparison includes the same training procedures, hyperparameters, or regularization techniques. The paper also fails to provide ablation studies showing the contribution of key components like skip connections or the controller architecture.

The paper's technical presentation is generally strong, but the lack of methodological transparency regarding the controller training process (e.g., total number of architectures evaluated, training time) and the absence of proper baselines for comparison weaken the scientific rigor.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen the paper for future submission, the authors should:

1. Provide a comprehensive comparison to a broad set of human-designed architectures on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, including not just the best-performing models but also a representative sample of recent human-designed models.

2. Clarify all speed and efficiency comparisons by reporting parameter counts, FLOPs, and training times under identical hardware conditions.

3. Include ablation studies to isolate the contribution of specific components (e.g., skip connections, controller architecture, search space design).

4. Provide more details about the controller training process, including the total number of architectures evaluated, training time, and convergence behavior.

5. Clarify the exact comparison points for all claims of "better than state-of-the-art" results, including whether the baselines were trained with identical hyperparameters and regularization.

## 5. Clarification Questions
1. On CIFAR-10, the paper states "our model with 40 filters added is 1.05x as fast as the DenseNet model that achieves 3.74%." How were the parameter counts and computational resources standardized for this comparison? Is the DenseNet model the same as the one referenced in Huang et al. (2016b)?

2. The paper claims the CIFAR-10 model is "better than most human-invented architectures" but only compares to a single DenseNet model. Could the authors provide a comparison to a broader set of human-designed architectures (e.g., ResNet, Inception, VGG) to substantiate this claim?

3. For the Penn Treebank results, the paper states the new cell achieves "3.6 perplexity better than the previous state-of-the-art." What was the exact baseline model used for this comparison, and were all models trained with identical hyperparameters and regularization techniques?

4. How many total architectures were evaluated during the controller training process? What was the total training time required to discover the final architectures?
