

# Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

The paper introduces "Neural Architecture Search" (NAS), a gradient-based method for automatically designing neural network architectures. The authors propose using a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate architectural hyperparameters as a sequence of tokens, which are then evaluated on a validation set. The controller is updated using policy gradient methods to maximize the expected validation accuracy of the generated architectures. The paper claims that NAS can design novel architectures that outperform human-invented models on CIFAR-10 (3.65% test error, 1.05x faster than previous best) and Penn Treebank (62.4 test perplexity, 3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art). The authors further claim their method can generate architectures from scratch without human intervention, which they assert is "not possible with other methods."

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes bold claims without sufficient empirical validation against established baselines and lacks methodological rigor in its experimental design.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's claims are not adequately supported by the evidence provided. While the results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are impressive, the experimental design lacks critical elements that would make the claims scientifically rigorous.

First, the comparison to established baselines is insufficient. The paper claims their CIFAR-10 model achieves "3.65% test set error, while being 1.05x faster than the current best model," but it fails to provide a fair comparison. The paper compares to DenseNet (3.74% error), but DenseNet uses 1x1 convolutions to reduce parameters, which NAS does not use. The paper acknowledges this discrepancy but does not provide a proper comparison with a DenseNet variant that uses the same parameter reduction techniques. Similarly, the Penn Treebank results claim a 3.6 perplexity improvement but don't compare to the most recent state-of-the-art models that might have already incorporated similar innovations.

Second, the paper lacks proper statistical validation. The claimed improvements (e.g., 3.6 perplexity better) are presented without standard error measurements or statistical significance tests. The paper claims their method "can design good models from scratch, an achievement considered not possible with other methods," but fails to provide a comprehensive comparison with other architecture search methods (e.g., Bayesian optimization, evolutionary algorithms) that have been previously applied to these tasks.

Third, the paper's computational cost is not properly discussed. The authors mention using "800 GPUs concurrently" for training, but don't compare the computational resources required to find these architectures versus the cost of human-designed architectures or other search methods. Given that the paper's main contribution is a method for architecture search, the computational efficiency of the approach is critical to its practical value.

Fourth, the paper's experimental design lacks proper ablation studies. The authors claim their method can "design novel ConvNet models that are better than most human-invented architectures," but fail to show what happens when they remove key components (e.g., skip connections, specific layer types) from their search space. Without such ablation studies, it's impossible to determine which aspects of their approach are truly responsible for the performance gains.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct a more rigorous comparison with established architecture search methods, not just human-designed architectures. This should include a fair comparison on the same computational budget.

2. Provide statistical significance testing for all claimed improvements, including standard errors and p-values where appropriate.

3. Conduct ablation studies to identify which components of their method are essential for performance gains.

4. Clarify the computational cost of their approach compared to human-designed architectures and other search methods. This is critical for assessing the practical value of the proposed method.

5. Address the discrepancy in parameter usage between their model and DenseNet, and provide a proper comparison with a DenseNet variant that uses similar techniques.

6. Include a more detailed discussion of the limitations of their approach. For example, how does the method scale to larger datasets or more complex tasks?

7. Provide more details about the search space. The paper mentions a search space with "approximately 6 × 10^16 architectures" but doesn't explain how this compares to the search spaces of other methods.

## Key Questions for Authors

1. How do you account for the discrepancy in parameter usage between your model and DenseNet? Did you intentionally avoid using 1x1 convolutions to make a fair comparison, and if so, why?

2. Can you provide statistical significance tests for your claimed improvements (e.g., 3.6 perplexity better on Penn Treebank)? What is the standard error of your results?

3. How does the computational cost of your architecture search method compare to the cost of human-designed architectures and other search methods (e.g., Bayesian optimization, evolutionary algorithms) on the same tasks?

4. Can you provide ablation studies showing what happens when you remove key components from your search space (e.g., skip connections, specific layer types)?

5. What is the expected time and resources required to apply your method to a new task, and how does this compare to the cost of human-designed architectures?

The paper's claims are impressive, but without rigorous validation against established baselines and proper statistical analysis, they remain unsubstantiated. As a reviewer who values methodological soundness and rigorous empirical validation, I find the paper's evidence insufficient to support its bold claims. The authors would need to provide substantially more evidence to convince me that their method represents a significant advancement over existing approaches.
