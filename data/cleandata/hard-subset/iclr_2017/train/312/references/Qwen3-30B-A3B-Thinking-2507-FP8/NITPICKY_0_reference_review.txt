

# Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces "Neural Architecture Search," a gradient-based method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a controller recurrent neural network (RNN) that generates architectural hyperparameters as sequences. The authors claim their approach can discover novel architectures that outperform human-designed models on CIFAR-10 (achieving 3.65% test error) and Penn Treebank (achieving 62.4 test perplexity). The method uses policy gradient training of the controller based on validation accuracy of the generated architectures, with extensions for skip connections and recurrent cell architectures. The paper presents promising empirical results and positions this work as a significant step toward automating architecture design.

## 2. Decision
Reject

Key reasons for rejection:
1. The paper contains multiple contradictory claims and lacks sufficient methodological detail to verify the presented results.
2. The paper makes strong claims about novelty and performance without properly supporting them through rigorous comparisons or clear literature placement.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's strongest contribution is the concept of using a controller RNN with policy gradient to search architectural spaces. However, several critical issues undermine the paper's credibility and scientific rigor:

First, the paper contains multiple contradictory claims about the results. In the introduction, it states "Our CIFAR-10 model achieves a 3.65 test set error, while being 1.05x faster than the current best model." However, in section 4.1, it states "the DenseNet model that achieves 3.46% error rate (Huang et al., 2016b) uses 1x1 convolutions to reduce its total number of parameters, which we did not do, so it is not an exact comparison." This creates confusion about what "current best model" refers to - the 3.74% or 3.46% error rate model. The paper claims to outperform the 3.74% model, but the comparison is muddled by the mention of the 3.46% model.

Second, the paper makes a strong claim that "Neural Architecture Search can design good models from scratch, an achievement considered not possible with other methods" without properly contextualizing this in the literature. The related work section fails to clearly distinguish between hyperparameter optimization (which optimizes fixed hyperparameters) and architecture search (which optimizes network structure), creating confusion about the novelty of the approach.

Third, the paper contains multiple technical inconsistencies and typos that suggest lack of attention to detail. In section 3.2, the REINFORCE rule is presented with "5θc" instead of "∇θc" and "m m∑" instead of "1/m ∑". These are not trivial typographical errors but could indicate deeper issues with the mathematical formulation.

Fourth, the paper lacks critical details about the experimental setup that would allow for reproducibility. The paper mentions "a separate held-out validation dataset" but doesn't specify the size of this dataset or how it was selected. The controller RNN architecture (2-layer LSTM with 35 hidden units) is presented without justification for this specific choice.

Fifth, the paper's claims about the "new state-of-the-art" results are overstated without proper comparison to all relevant prior work. For example, in section 4.2, it claims "the model that achieves 64 perplexity is also more than two times faster" without clearly stating what the baseline model was that was slower.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Clarify all contradictory claims about results and provide clear comparisons to the correct baseline models.
2. Provide a more precise literature review that clearly distinguishes between hyperparameter optimization and architecture search, and properly positions their contribution in the context of existing work.
3. Correct all mathematical notations and typos, particularly in section 3.2.
4. Add details about the validation set size and selection process for all experiments.
5. Justify the choice of controller architecture (2-layer LSTM with 35 hidden units) and the schedule for increasing the number of layers.
6. Provide a clear explanation of the "base number" concept in section 3.4 and its impact on the search space.
7. Include the exact repository path for the code release rather than a general GitHub URL.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims to achieve "3.65 test set error" on CIFAR-10, but states "the DenseNet model that achieves 3.46% error rate (Huang et al., 2016b) uses 1x1 convolutions to reduce its total number of parameters, which we did not do." Can you clarify which model (3.74% or 3.46%) is the correct baseline for comparison, and why your model is better than the 3.74% model despite not using 1x1 convolutions?

2. In section 4.2, you claim "the model that achieves 64 perplexity is also more than two times faster because the previous best network requires running a cell 10 times per time step." What specific model are you comparing to, and what is the exact computational complexity of your cell versus the previous best model?

3. The paper states "the controller RNN is a two-layer LSTM with 35 hidden units on each layer" but doesn't explain why this architecture was chosen. How did you determine these specific hyperparameters for the controller?

4. In the control experiment 1, you add "max to the list of combination functions and sin to the list of activation functions." What motivated the choice of these specific functions, and why did you expect them to work well?

5. The paper claims "Neural Architecture Search can design good models from scratch, an achievement considered not possible with other methods." Can you cite specific papers that claim to have done this, and explain why their methods are insufficient compared to yours?

These questions are essential to understand the paper's claims and verify the results. Without clear answers, the paper's scientific rigor cannot be confirmed.
