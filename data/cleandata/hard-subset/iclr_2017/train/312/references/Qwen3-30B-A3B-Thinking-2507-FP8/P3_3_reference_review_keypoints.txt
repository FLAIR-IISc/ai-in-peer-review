- Introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS): a controller RNN that generates architectures trained with policy gradients to maximize validation accuracy; supports variable-length search and is applied to CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank.  
- Empirical highlights: CIFAR-10 test error 3.65% (better and 1.05× faster than DenseNet); PTB perplexity 62.4 (3.6 points better than prior SOTA).  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions — significant contribution with strong empirical results across two domains.  
- Method motivation: Fills gap between fixed-length hyperparameter search and computationally intensive neuro-evolution, enabling flexible architecture search.  
- Experimental rigor: Comprehensive comparisons with human-designed architectures and careful experimental detail.  
- Major concern: Extremely high computational cost (800 GPUs for CIFAR-10, 400 CPUs for PTB) questions practicality for most researchers.  
- Missing comparisons: Lacks detailed comparison to recent NAS methods (e.g., ENAS, DARTS) to contextualize advantages.  
- Theoretical weakness: Limited theoretical grounding for using controller RNN + policy gradients and no analysis why this RL approach outperforms alternatives.  
- Reward analysis gap: No detailed analysis of the validation-accuracy reward signal and its impact on the search dynamics.  
- Suggestion — cost analysis: Add a cost-benefit discussion comparing NAS resource requirements to human-designed architectures.  
- Suggestion — recent work: Update experiments/discussion to compare with newer NAS approaches (ENAS, DARTS).  
- Suggestion — architecture analysis: Provide ablation studies and insights into which architectural features drive performance gains.  
- Suggestion — theoretical justification: Offer more rigorous reasoning for why the controller RNN with policy gradients is suited for architecture search.  
- Q1 for authors: How was the controller’s schedule for increasing layers determined — empirical observation or separate optimization?  
- Q2 for authors: How does the discovered CIFAR architecture without the extra 40 filters compare in parameter count and inference speed?  
- Q3 for authors: Provide more evidence on generalization of the discovered recurrent cell to other datasets/tasks beyond reported results.  
- Q4 for authors: Given a search space of ~6×10^16 and only 12,800 evaluations, what is the probability of finding the optimal cell and how does the method avoid local optima?
