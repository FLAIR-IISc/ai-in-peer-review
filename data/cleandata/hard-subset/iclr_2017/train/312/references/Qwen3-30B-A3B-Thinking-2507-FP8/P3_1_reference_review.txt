

# ICLR Peer Review: "Neural Architecture Search"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel gradient-based method for automatically discovering high-performing neural network architectures. The authors propose using a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate architectural hyperparameters as a sequence of tokens, then using reinforcement learning (specifically REINFORCE) to train the controller based on the validation accuracy of the generated architectures. The method is designed to search variable-length architecture spaces, which differentiates it from existing hyperparameter optimization approaches that typically operate in fixed-length spaces.

Key contributions include:
- A controller RNN that can generate convolutional architectures with skip connections and other modern layer types
- A method for generating recurrent cell architectures using a tree-based representation
- Empirical demonstrations showing NAS outperforms human-designed architectures on CIFAR-10 (3.65% test error vs. 3.74% for DenseNet) and Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity vs. 66.0 for previous state-of-the-art)

The paper is well-structured, with clear sections on related work, methodology, experiments, and results. The authors provide sufficient detail on implementation and make their code publicly available, which is commendable.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

**Key reasons for acceptance:**
1. The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to neural architecture search that addresses a significant limitation of existing methods (fixed-length search spaces).
2. The empirical results are strong and demonstrate clear improvements over human-designed architectures on standard benchmarks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

### Strengths:
- **Novel Approach:** The paper successfully bridges the gap between hyperparameter optimization and architectural search by using a controller RNN to generate variable-length architecture descriptions. This is a significant contribution as it moves beyond the limitations of previous methods that could only search fixed-length spaces.
- **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on CIFAR-10 (3.65% test error) and Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity) are impressive and clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The ablation studies (e.g., with and without skip connections, with different search spaces) provide valuable insights into the method's behavior.
- **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors conducted thorough experiments across multiple settings (CIFAR-10 with different search space constraints, Penn Treebank with different cell configurations) and included transfer learning results to demonstrate the generality of the found architectures.
- **Practical Implementation:** The authors provide detailed implementation details (e.g., distributed training setup with parameter servers) and make code available, which enhances reproducibility.

### Weaknesses:
- **Computational Cost:** The paper doesn't adequately address the enormous computational cost of the method (800 GPUs for CIFAR-10, 400 CPUs for PTB). This is a critical limitation for practical adoption, especially given the current resource constraints in many research settings. The authors should discuss the computational cost more thoroughly and compare it to other NAS methods.
- **Lack of Statistical Significance:** The results don't include statistical significance tests (e.g., p-values, confidence intervals) for the reported improvements. Given the high variance in deep learning results, this is a significant omission.
- **Potential Overfitting to Validation Set:** The paper states that the test set performance is computed "only once for the network that achieves the best result on the held-out validation dataset." This risks overfitting to the validation set, and the authors should clarify how they've mitigated this risk (e.g., by using a separate test set for final evaluation).
- **Comparison to Other NAS Methods:** The paper compares to human-designed architectures but doesn't adequately compare to other NAS methods (e.g., the recent DARTS method). This makes it difficult to assess how much of an improvement NAS actually is over the state-of-the-art in automatic architecture search.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

### Key Improvements Needed:

1. **Computational Cost Analysis:** Please add a detailed discussion of the computational cost (e.g., total GPU-hours required) and compare it to other NAS methods. Also, discuss potential ways to reduce this cost (e.g., by using more efficient search strategies or approximations).

2. **Statistical Significance:** Add statistical significance tests for all reported results. Given the high variance in deep learning experiments, this is essential for scientific rigor.

3. **Comparison to Other NAS Methods:** Include a comparison to other recent NAS methods (e.g., DARTS, ENAS) to better position your work within the current literature. This would help readers understand how much of an improvement your method represents.

4. **Validation Set Overfitting:** Clarify how you've avoided overfitting to the validation set. If you used a single validation set, consider explaining how you've validated your results (e.g., by using multiple random splits).

5. **Limitations Section:** Add a dedicated limitations section discussing the practical constraints of your method (e.g., computational cost, search space constraints) and potential future work to address them.

6. **Ablation Study Detail:** The paper mentions a "Control Experiment 1" with additional functions in the search space but doesn't provide detailed results. Please include these results in the main text or appendix.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that "the controller can design good models from scratch, an achievement considered not possible with other methods." However, the paper doesn't provide a clear comparison to other NAS methods (e.g., DARTS, ENAS, or evolutionary methods). Could you clarify how your method compares to these approaches in terms of performance and computational cost?

2. In the CIFAR-10 experiments, the authors report a 3.65% test error rate with a model that's "1.05x faster than the current best model." However, the paper doesn't specify which model this is comparing to (DenseNet-121? DenseNet-161?). Could you clarify this comparison and provide the exact computational complexity (e.g., FLOPs, number of parameters) of your model versus the DenseNet model?

3. The paper mentions that "the model with 40 filters added is 1.05x as fast as the DenseNet model that achieves 3.74%." Could you explain why adding filters would make the model faster? Typically, more filters would increase computational cost, not decrease it.

4. In the Penn Treebank experiments, the authors report that "the new cell does indeed generalize" to character language modeling. However, the paper doesn't provide any results for the character-level task beyond the table. Could you provide more details about these results, including the exact perplexity improvements?

5. The paper states that the controller RNN "is trained with the ADAM optimizer with a learning rate of 0.0006" for CIFAR-10, but doesn't discuss how this learning rate was chosen or if it was tuned. Given that the learning rate can significantly impact the performance of reinforcement learning methods, could you discuss your hyperparameter tuning process for the controller?

## Conclusion

This paper presents a novel and effective approach to neural architecture search that demonstrates impressive empirical results. While the computational cost is high, the method is well-motivated, the implementation is thorough, and the results are scientifically rigorous (with the exceptions noted above). With the requested revisions addressing the computational cost analysis, statistical significance, and comparison to other NAS methods, this paper would be a strong contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
