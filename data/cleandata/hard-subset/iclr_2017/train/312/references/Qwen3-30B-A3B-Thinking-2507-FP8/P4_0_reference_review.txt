

1. Summary: The paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network controller trained via reinforcement learning. The controller generates architectures as variable-length strings, which are then evaluated on a validation set. The authors demonstrate that this approach can discover novel architectures that outperform human-designed networks on CIFAR-10 (achieving 3.65% test error) and Penn Treebank (achieving 62.4 perplexity). The method is well-motivated, with clear connections to related work in hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis.

2. Decision: Accept. The paper presents a novel and well-executed approach to neural architecture search that is technically sound and empirically validated. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) The method is well-motivated and addresses a significant problem in deep learning; (2) The empirical results demonstrate clear improvements over state-of-the-art human-designed architectures.

3. Supporting arguments: The paper makes a strong case for its method through comprehensive experiments on two challenging benchmarks. The CIFAR-10 results show that the proposed method finds architectures that outperform DenseNet (3.65% vs. 3.74% error) while being 1.05x faster, with careful attention to architectural details that affect speed. The Penn Treebank results show a 3.6 perplexity improvement over previous state-of-the-art. The authors provide strong control experiments (including random search comparisons) that validate their approach's superiority. The technical description is thorough, with clear explanations of how the controller works, how skip connections are incorporated, and how recurrent cell architectures are generated. The method is well-placed in the literature, with a comprehensive related work section that properly contextualizes the contribution.

4. Additional feedback: The paper would benefit from more detailed comparisons to other automated architecture search methods beyond random search, as this would strengthen the novelty claim. The claim that the method "designs good models from scratch, an achievement considered not possible with other methods" could be clarified by comparing to other NAS approaches. The paper should explicitly state the computational cost of their method (e.g., total GPU hours) to provide context for the resource requirements. The claim about being "1.05x faster" needs clarification regarding the specific comparison point (DenseNet uses 1x1 convolutions which were not used in the NAS architecture, making the speed comparison potentially misleading).

5. Questions for authors:
- Can you clarify the comparison of speed (1.05x faster) between your model and DenseNet, given that DenseNet uses 1x1 convolutions to reduce parameters, which your model does not use?
- How does your method compare to other neural architecture search approaches (e.g., Zoph & Le 2017, Real et al. 2017) in terms of search space coverage and computational efficiency?
- The paper claims to outperform "most human-invented architectures" on CIFAR-10. Can you clarify which specific human-designed architectures were compared against, and whether the comparison includes all major architectures in the literature?
- Did you perform statistical significance testing on the reported improvements (e.g., 3.65% vs 3.74% error on CIFAR-10)? If not, could you provide details on the number of random seeds used for training?
