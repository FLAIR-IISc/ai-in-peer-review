

# Peer Review: Neural Architecture Search

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel gradient-based method for automatically discovering high-performing neural network architectures. The core innovation is using a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate architectural hyperparameters as a sequence of tokens, with the controller trained via policy gradient reinforcement learning to maximize the validation accuracy of the generated architectures. The approach is well-motivated as a solution to the time-consuming and expertise-intensive process of manual architecture design. The authors demonstrate NAS on two challenging tasks: finding convolutional architectures for CIFAR-10 image classification (achieving 3.65% test error, outperforming previous human-designed architectures) and discovering recurrent cells for Penn Treebank language modeling (achieving 62.4 perplexity, a 3.6 improvement over previous state-of-the-art). The paper also provides thorough ablation studies, comparisons to random search, and evidence of transfer learning to character-level language modeling and machine translation.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper addresses an important problem in deep learning with a well-motivated and technically sound approach, and the empirical results are compelling and rigorously evaluated.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning. The NAS approach is well-placed in the literature, with a thoughtful comparison to hyperparameter optimization (limited to fixed-length spaces), neuro-evolution (less practical at scale), and program synthesis (more relevant but less directly applicable). The methodology is sound, using policy gradient reinforcement learning to train the controller with a baseline to reduce variance, and implementing distributed training to accelerate the search process.

The empirical results are strong and well-supported. The CIFAR-10 results demonstrate a 3.65% error rate, which is better than previous human-designed architectures while being 1.05x faster. The Penn Treebank results show a 3.6 perplexity improvement over previous state-of-the-art. The authors provide comprehensive ablation studies, including control experiments with expanded search spaces and comparisons to random search, which convincingly demonstrate the superiority of their approach.

The paper's main limitation is the computational cost of the search process (800 GPUs for CIFAR-10), but the authors acknowledge this and position their method as a research direction for automated architecture discovery rather than an immediate practical solution. The trade-off between search cost and performance is reasonable for a research contribution.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, I recommend the following improvements to enhance clarity and impact:

1. Clarify the computational cost of the NAS process. The paper mentions using 800 GPUs for CIFAR-10 experiments but doesn't provide the total GPU hours required or the time to discover the final architecture. This context would help readers understand the practical feasibility of the approach.

2. Provide more precise details about the "1.05x faster" claim for the CIFAR-10 model. Is this inference speed on specific hardware, training speed, or something else? The paper should specify the exact metric being compared.

3. Clarify which "best human-invented architecture" achieves 3.74% error rate on CIFAR-10. Naming the specific architecture (e.g., DenseNet) would provide better context for the comparison.

4. Expand the comparison to recent LSTM variants for the language modeling task. The paper claims the NAS cell is "better than previous RNN and LSTM architectures" but doesn't directly compare to recent state-of-the-art LSTMs like those from Jozefowicz et al. (2015) or more recent work.

5. Provide more detail on how the search space size (6×10^16 architectures) was calculated for the recurrent cell. This would help readers understand the scale of the search space relative to other NAS approaches.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. Could you provide the total GPU hours required to discover the CIFAR-10 architecture? How does this compare to the cost of training the final model?

2. What is the exact metric used for the "1.05x faster" claim (e.g., inference speed on specific hardware, training speed, or FLOPs)? Please provide more specific details.

3. Could you clarify which human-designed architecture achieves 3.74% error on CIFAR-10 (e.g., DenseNet, ResNet, etc.)?

4. How does the NAS-generated recurrent cell compare to the most recent state-of-the-art LSTMs (e.g., those from the 2015-2016 period) beyond the specific models mentioned in the paper?

5. Could you explain how you calculated the 6×10^16 approximate search space size for the recurrent cell? What assumptions were made in this calculation?

These questions would help readers better understand the scale of the search space and the practical implications of your approach. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of automated architecture search, and addressing these points would further strengthen the paper's impact.
