

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper presents a novel approach called Neural Architecture Search (NAS), which uses a reinforcement learning-based controller to automatically design neural network architectures. The key contribution is the formulation of neural architecture search as a sequence generation problem where a recurrent neural network (the controller) generates variable-length architectural descriptions. The controller is trained via policy gradients using the validation accuracy of the generated architectures as the reward signal. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach on two challenging benchmarks: CIFAR-10 image classification and Penn Treebank language modeling. NAS discovers novel architectures that outperform previous human-designed models (3.65% test error on CIFAR-10, 62.4 perplexity on Penn Treebank), and the paper provides detailed ablation studies and comparisons to random search. The authors also release code and integrate their discovered RNN cell into TensorFlow as NASCell. The approach represents a significant step toward automating neural architecture design and opens up new research directions in this area.

## 2. Decision

**Decision: Accept**

**Key reasons for acceptance:**
1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in deep learning (automating neural architecture design) with a well-motivated and novel approach that advances the state of the art.
2. The empirical results are compelling, showing consistent improvements over previous human-designed architectures on two major benchmarks with thorough ablation studies.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper makes a strong case for its contribution on multiple fronts:

**Problem significance:** The paper correctly identifies that neural architecture design is still largely manual and time-consuming, requiring expert knowledge. The shift from feature engineering to architecture engineering (as the paper notes) has been a key factor in deep learning's success, and automating this process would be highly valuable.

**Novelty and positioning:** The paper provides a thorough literature review that clearly positions NAS relative to hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis. The key insight that architectures can be represented as variable-length strings and searched with a controller RNN is novel and well-motivated. Previous approaches either have fixed-length search spaces (hyperparameter optimization) or are too slow (neuro-evolution), making NAS a significant advance.

**Rigorous empirical evaluation:** The paper provides comprehensive experiments on two major benchmarks with multiple ablation studies. The CIFAR-10 results show that NAS discovers architectures that are both more accurate and faster than previous human-designed models (3.65% error vs. 3.74% for DenseNet, with 1.05x speed improvement). The Penn Treebank results show a 3.6 perplexity improvement over previous state-of-the-art. The control experiments (adding more functions to the search space, comparison against random search) further validate the approach's robustness and superiority.

**Technical soundness:** The methodological description is thorough, with clear explanations of the controller RNN, policy gradient training, skip connections, and recurrent cell generation. The implementation details (distributed training, parameter server scheme) are well-described, allowing for reproducibility.

The paper's contribution is significant and well-supported, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at ICLR.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where it could be improved:

1. **Computational cost discussion:** The paper mentions distributed training with 800 GPUs for CIFAR-10 and 400 CPUs for Penn Treebank, but doesn't quantify the total computational cost (e.g., GPU-hours) of the NAS process. This would help readers understand the practical feasibility of the approach. The authors should add a sentence or two about the total compute required.

2. **Comparison to more recent methods:** The paper cites Huang et al. (2016) as the best human-designed architecture for CIFAR-10 (DenseNet with 3.74% error), but since then, several more recent human-designed architectures have been proposed (e.g., MobileNets, EfficientNets). The authors should compare their architecture to these more recent human-designed models to better establish the significance of their improvement.

3. **Ablation study on skip connections:** The paper mentions skip connections as a key component, but doesn't provide a detailed ablation study showing how much skip connections contribute to the performance gain. A comparison of architectures with and without skip connections would strengthen the argument for this component.

4. **Visualization of the discovered architectures:** While the paper mentions Figure 8 in the appendix showing the discovered RNN cell, it would be helpful to include a more detailed visualization of the CIFAR-10 architecture in the main paper to help readers understand what the discovered architecture looks like.

5. **Potential limitations:** The paper could briefly discuss limitations of the approach, such as the difficulty of scaling to larger datasets or the potential for overfitting to the validation set during the search process.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To clarify my understanding and ensure confidence in my assessment, I have the following questions:

1. The paper mentions that the NAS controller discovers architectures that are "better than most human-invented architectures" on CIFAR-10. Could the authors provide a more precise comparison to the most recent human-designed architectures (e.g., EfficientNets, MobileNets) that have been published after the DenseNet paper cited in the paper?

2. The paper states that the NAS model with 40 additional filters per layer achieves 3.65% error rate while being 1.05x faster than the DenseNet model that achieves 3.74% error. Could the authors clarify whether the DenseNet model they're comparing to is the one with 1x1 convolutions (which they mention as not being an exact comparison), and provide more details about the computational complexity comparison?

3. The paper mentions that the controller RNN is trained with a schedule of increasing the number of layers in the child networks. Could the authors elaborate on how they determine when to increase the layer count (e.g., based on validation accuracy improvement) and whether this schedule significantly impacts the final architecture found?

4. The paper states that NAS discovers a novel recurrent cell that "has many similarities to the LSTM cell in the first few steps." Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of the discovered cell to the LSTM cell, highlighting both the similarities and the key differences that lead to the performance improvement?

5. In the Penn Treebank experiments, the authors mention that the new cell is "more than two times faster" than the previous best network. Could they clarify what they mean by "faster" (e.g., inference speed, training speed) and under what specific conditions this speedup occurs?
