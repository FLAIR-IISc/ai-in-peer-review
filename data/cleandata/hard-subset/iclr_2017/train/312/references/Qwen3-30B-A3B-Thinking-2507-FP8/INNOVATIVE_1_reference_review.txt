

# Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel gradient-based method for automatically discovering high-performing neural network architectures. The core insight is that neural network architectures can be represented as variable-length strings, which can be generated by a recurrent neural network (the "controller"). The controller is trained using policy gradient reinforcement learning to maximize the validation accuracy of the architectures it generates. The approach enables the discovery of architectures without human intervention, which the authors claim is a significant advancement over previous methods that either required human design or were limited to fixed-length spaces.

The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of NAS on two major benchmarks: CIFAR-10 image classification (achieving 3.65% test error, outperforming previous human-invented architectures while being 1.05x faster) and Penn Treebank language modeling (achieving 62.4 test perplexity, a 3.6 improvement over previous state-of-the-art). The authors also show that the discovered recurrent cell generalizes to other tasks, including character language modeling and machine translation.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper presents a genuinely novel and bold approach to neural architecture search that fundamentally shifts the paradigm from human-designed architectures to automatically discovered ones. The method's conceptual novelty and strong empirical results on challenging benchmarks justify acceptance despite minor methodological limitations.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution is conceptually novel and represents a significant departure from existing approaches to neural architecture search. Unlike hyperparameter optimization methods that search fixed-length spaces, or neuro-evolution algorithms that are slow at scale, NAS uses a gradient-based approach that can search variable-length architectures. This is a bold conceptual leap that opens up an entirely new research direction.

The empirical results are compelling and well-supported. The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on two major benchmarks without human intervention, with the discovered architectures outperforming previously human-designed ones. The authors provide thorough ablation studies, control experiments (including comparisons against random search), and transfer learning results that validate the robustness of their approach. The 3.6 perplexity improvement on Penn Treebank and 3.65% test error on CIFAR-10 are significant, especially considering these are discovered from scratch without human guidance.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper's contribution to be highly valuable. It's not just an incremental improvement but a fundamentally new direction for how we design neural networks. The paper successfully demonstrates that architecture search can be done automatically with gradient-based methods, which is a paradigm shift in the field.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. More detailed discussion of the computational cost of the method. While the paper mentions using 800 GPUs for CIFAR-10 experiments, a more thorough analysis of the computational resources required compared to other NAS approaches would be valuable for the community.

2. A deeper analysis of the discovered architectures. While the paper shows the architectures work well, an analysis of *why* they work better (e.g., specific architectural features that contribute to performance) would provide deeper insights into the method's capabilities.

3. A clearer discussion of the limitations of the approach. For example, how does the method scale to larger datasets or more complex tasks? What are the fundamental constraints of the search space that might prevent certain architectures from being discovered?

4. More detailed implementation details about the controller architecture and training process. While the paper provides some details, a more complete description of the hyperparameters used for the controller and how they were selected would help others reproduce the work.

5. A more thorough comparison with other recent NAS approaches (e.g., the 2017 NAS paper from Google AI). While the paper acknowledges related work, a more detailed comparison with the most recent NAS methods would better position the contribution.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper mentions using a schedule to increase the number of layers as training progresses. Could you elaborate on how this schedule was determined, and whether it significantly impacted the quality of the discovered architectures?

2. The paper states that the controller predicts stride in some experiments, but the search space for stride is limited to [1, 2, 3]. Were there any specific challenges with predicting larger strides, and how did the authors address these challenges?

3. The discovered recurrent cell for Penn Treebank has a "base number" of 8. How did the authors determine this base number, and what would happen if they used a different base number (e.g., 4 or 16)?

4. The paper mentions the controller can generate skip connections, but doesn't discuss how the method handles architectures with many skip connections that might cause computational issues. How did the authors ensure that the discovered architectures were computationally feasible?
