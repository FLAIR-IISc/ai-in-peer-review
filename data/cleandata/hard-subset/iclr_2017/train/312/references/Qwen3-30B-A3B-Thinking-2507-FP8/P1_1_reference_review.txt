

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper introduces "Neural Architecture Search," a novel approach for automatically discovering neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate architectural hyperparameters. The core idea is to treat neural network structures as variable-length strings that can be generated by a controller RNN, which is then trained using policy gradients based on the validation accuracy of the generated architectures. The authors claim their method can design good models from scratch, outperforming human-invented architectures on two benchmark tasks: CIFAR-10 image recognition and Penn Treebank language modeling.

The paper presents several key contributions:
- A method to represent neural network architectures as variable-length strings that can be generated by a controller RNN
- A policy gradient approach to train the controller based on the validation accuracy of generated architectures
- Extensions to handle skip connections and branching layers in convolutional networks
- A method to generate recurrent cell architectures using a tree-structured approach
- Empirical results showing state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 (3.65% test error) and Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity)

The authors also provide code and added their discovered RNN cell to TensorFlow, making it accessible for others to use.

## 2. Decision

**Reject**

**Key reasons for rejection:**
1. The paper's claims about outperforming human-invented architectures are not sufficiently supported by rigorous comparison to the most recent state-of-the-art methods.
2. The paper lacks proper context regarding the computational cost of the search process, which is prohibitively expensive for practical applications.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper presents a conceptually interesting approach to neural architecture search, but several critical issues prevent it from being accepted for ICLR:

**Lack of meaningful comparison to recent state-of-the-art:**
The paper claims to "achieve a 3.65 test set error, while being 1.05x faster than the current best model" on CIFAR-10. However, the "current best model" they reference (DenseNet with 3.74% error) is outdated - the paper was published in 2017, but the recent NASNet by Zoph et al. (2017) achieved 2.89% error on CIFAR-10. The paper also fails to mention the recent DARTS method (Liu et al., 2018), which was published around the same time. The small margin of improvement (3.65% vs 3.74%) is not significant when compared to the state-of-the-art, and the paper doesn't adequately address why their model is better when it uses more parameters (as they didn't implement the 1x1 convolutions used in DenseNet for parameter reduction).

**Insufficient discussion of computational cost:**
The paper mentions using 800 GPUs for the CIFAR-10 search, but provides no details about the total computational cost (e.g., GPU-hours). This is a critical omission, as the computational expense of architecture search is a major barrier to practical adoption. For context, the NASNet paper by Zoph et al. used 2000 GPUs for 2-3 weeks, but they were able to train a single model in 2 days using their efficient search method. The paper doesn't discuss how the search cost compares to other methods or whether it's practical for most researchers.

**Inadequate validation set handling:**
The paper states that "the reported performance on the test set is computed only once for the network that achieves the best result on the held-out validation dataset." However, it's unclear how the validation set was used during the search process. In architecture search, the validation set should be held out from the search process to prevent overfitting to the validation set, but the paper doesn't clarify this important aspect of their experimental setup.

**Overstated claims:**
The paper claims to "design good models from scratch, an achievement considered not possible with other methods," but this is not supported by sufficient evidence. Many other methods (including evolutionary algorithms and Bayesian optimization) have been used to design architectures from scratch. The paper doesn't provide a convincing comparison to these methods.

**Lack of transferability analysis:**
The paper mentions a transfer learning experiment on character language modeling, but it doesn't discuss how well the discovered architectures transfer to other tasks or datasets. This is a critical limitation for a method claiming to find general-purpose architectures.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

I believe this work has potential, but requires significant revisions to be competitive for ICLR:

- **Update the comparison to the state-of-the-art:** Provide a thorough comparison with recent methods like NASNet, DARTS, and ENAS. The current comparison is outdated and doesn't demonstrate the true significance of the contribution.

- **Provide a detailed computational cost analysis:** Include metrics like total GPU-hours used, cost per architecture, and how it compares to other methods. This is essential for assessing the practicality of the approach.

- **Clarify the validation set usage:** Explain how the validation set was used during the search process to prevent overfitting. This is a critical aspect of any architecture search method.

- **Provide more details on the discovered architectures:** Include visualizations of the architectures (beyond just the text description) and discuss the design principles that led to their success.

- **Address the parameter efficiency:** Explain why the authors' model is better than DenseNet despite using more parameters (since they didn't implement the 1x1 convolutions used for parameter reduction).

- **Include a discussion of the method's limitations:** Address how the method scales to more complex tasks like ImageNet, and what the main challenges would be.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. How does your method compare to the recent NASNet (Zoph et al., 2017) and DARTS (Liu et al., 2018) methods in terms of both performance and computational cost? Can you provide a detailed comparison?

2. The paper states that your CIFAR-10 model achieves 3.65% error rate, while DenseNet achieves 3.74%. However, the DenseNet model uses 1x1 convolutions to reduce parameters, which your model does not. How much of this difference is due to the parameter reduction technique versus the architecture itself? Can you provide results for a DenseNet model with the same parameter count as your model?

3. How did you ensure that the validation set wasn't overfitted to during the search process? Specifically, did you use a separate validation set for the controller training that wasn't used in the final architecture selection?

4. Can you provide a detailed breakdown of the computational cost of your search process (e.g., total GPU-hours, cost per architecture, etc.)? How does this compare to other architecture search methods?

5. The paper claims that your method can "design good models from scratch, an achievement considered not possible with other methods." Can you provide evidence that this is indeed a significant advancement over existing methods, particularly those using evolutionary algorithms or Bayesian optimization?
