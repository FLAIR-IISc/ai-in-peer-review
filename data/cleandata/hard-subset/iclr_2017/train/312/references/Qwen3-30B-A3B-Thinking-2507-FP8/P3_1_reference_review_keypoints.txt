- Introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS): a controller RNN trained with REINFORCE to generate variable-length neural architectures.
- Key contributions: controller generates conv architectures with skip connections, tree-based recurrent cell representation, and strong CIFAR-10/PTB results.
- Empirical claims: outperforms human-designed architectures on CIFAR-10 (3.65% test error) and Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity).
- Paper is well-structured, provides implementation details, and releases code for reproducibility.
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.
- Main acceptance reasons: novel method addressing fixed-length search limitation and strong empirical improvements.
- Strength: Bridges hyperparameter optimization and architecture search using a sequence-generating controller RNN.
- Strength: Strong empirical results with ablation studies demonstrating method behavior.
- Strength: Comprehensive experiments across multiple settings and transfer learning evaluations.
- Strength: Practical implementation details (distributed training) and public code enhance reproducibility.
- Weakness: Extremely high computational cost (e.g., 800 GPUs for CIFAR-10, 400 CPUs for PTB) not adequately discussed.
- Weakness: No statistical significance testing (p-values/confidence intervals) reported for results.
- Weakness: Risk of overfitting to validation set due to selecting the test-evaluated network by best validation performance.
- Weakness: Inadequate comparison to other NAS methods (e.g., DARTS, ENAS, evolutionary approaches).
- Improvement needed: Add detailed computational cost analysis (GPU-hours) and comparisons to other NAS methods, plus strategies to reduce cost.
- Improvement needed: Provide statistical significance tests for reported results.
- Improvement needed: Clarify validation/test evaluation protocol and mitigation of validation-set overfitting (e.g., multiple splits).
- Improvement needed: Add a limitations section discussing practical constraints and future work.
- Improvement needed: Include detailed results for the mentioned control/ablation experiments (e.g., Control Experiment 1).
- Question: How does NAS compare to other NAS methods in performance and computational cost?
- Question: Which DenseNet model is used for the speed comparison and what are exact FLOPs/parameter counts?
- Question: Why does adding 40 filters reportedly make the model faster—please clarify this contradictory claim?
- Question: Provide more details and metrics for the claimed character-level PTB generalization.
- Question: Explain how the controller learning rate (ADAM 0.0006) was chosen and whether it was tuned.
- Conclusion: Paper is novel and effective but requires revisions addressing computational cost, statistical rigor, and comparisons to other NAS approaches.
