

# Review

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims
The paper proposes "Neural Architecture Search" (NAS), a method that uses a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate neural network architectures through a reinforcement learning approach. The controller generates architectural hyperparameters as a sequence of tokens, and the validation accuracy of the resulting "child network" serves as the reward signal to update the controller via policy gradients. The authors claim their method can design novel architectures from scratch that outperform human-invented architectures on CIFAR-10 (3.65% test error) and Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity).

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper lacks sufficient experimental rigor and thorough validation against established baselines. The claims of outperforming human-invented architectures are not adequately supported by the evidence provided.

## 3. Supporting arguments
The paper's claims are not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented. The most critical deficiencies include:

1. **Inadequate baseline comparisons**: The paper claims to outperform "most human-invented architectures" but only compares against a limited set of architectures (e.g., DenseNet 3.74% error rate). The paper fails to compare against a comprehensive set of established baselines, including other NAS methods, and doesn't provide sufficient context about how many human-invented architectures were considered.

2. **Lack of statistical validation**: The reported results (e.g., 3.65% error rate) are presented without standard deviations or confidence intervals, making it impossible to assess the statistical significance of the improvements claimed.

3. **Insufficient computational details**: The paper doesn't provide details about the computational resources required (number of GPUs, total search time), making it difficult to assess the practicality of the approach. The claim that their model is "1.05x faster" than DenseNet is misleading without context about the computational resources used for training.

4. **Validation set overfitting concern**: The paper uses validation accuracy as the reward signal for training the controller, which creates a risk of overfitting to the validation set. The paper doesn't adequately address this critical issue, which is particularly problematic in architecture search where the validation set is used repeatedly during the search process.

5. **Minimal comparison to random search**: The control experiment comparing to random search (Figure 6) is superficial, showing only a single run without statistical significance testing, which is insufficient to claim that the proposed method is significantly better than random search.

## 4. Additional feedback
To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide comprehensive comparison against a wide range of established baselines, including other NAS methods, not just a few human-invented architectures.

2. Include statistical significance testing for all reported performance improvements.

3. Provide detailed information about computational resources (number of GPUs, total search time, cost).

4. Address the validation set overfitting concern with a proper experimental design.

5. Include ablation studies to isolate the contribution of different components of the proposed method.

6. Provide details about the search space size and the number of architectures evaluated versus the total possible search space.

Questions for the authors:
1. How many GPUs were used in total for the CIFAR-10 search, and how long did the entire search take?
2. What is the standard deviation of the test error rate reported for the CIFAR-10 model?
3. How many different random seeds were used to ensure reproducibility of the results?
4. How did the authors prevent overfitting to the validation set during the search process?
5. What is the computational cost of the search process compared to the training cost of the final model?
6. How does the search time scale with the size of the search space, and what is the maximum practical search space size for this method?
7. How many architectures were evaluated in total during the search, and how does this compare to the size of the search space?
