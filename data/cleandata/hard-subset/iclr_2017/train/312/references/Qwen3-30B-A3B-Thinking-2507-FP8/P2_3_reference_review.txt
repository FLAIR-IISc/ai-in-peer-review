

# Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

## Summary of Contribution
The paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel method for automatically discovering effective neural network architectures using a gradient-based approach. The key insight is that neural network structures can be represented as variable-length strings, which can be generated by a recurrent neural network controller. The controller is trained via policy gradients using the validation accuracy of the generated architectures as a reward signal. The authors demonstrate that NAS can discover superior architectures for both image classification (on CIFAR-10) and language modeling (on Penn Treebank), outperforming human-designed architectures in both domains.

## Decision
I recommend acceptance of this paper.

## Key Reasons for Acceptance
1. The paper addresses a significant and timely problem in deep learning: the difficulty of designing high-performing neural network architectures, which traditionally requires substantial expert knowledge and time.
2. The method is well-motivated, clearly distinguished from related work (hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution), and provides a technically sound solution to the problem of searching variable-length architecture spaces.
3. The empirical results are impressive, scientifically rigorous, and supported by multiple experiments including ablation studies, control experiments, and transfer learning.

## Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a compelling case for the significance of the problem it addresses. The authors correctly identify that while deep learning has achieved remarkable success, the process of architecture design remains a bottleneck requiring expert knowledge. Their method offers a paradigm shift toward automatic architecture search, which could democratize deep learning development.

The technical approach is well-justified and novel. The use of a controller RNN to generate architectural hyperparameters as a sequence of tokens, followed by training the controller via policy gradients based on validation accuracy, is a clever and effective solution. The authors clearly explain how their method differs from related work, particularly highlighting the flexibility of searching variable-length architecture spaces compared to fixed-length hyperparameter optimization methods.

The empirical results are particularly strong. On CIFAR-10, their method discovers a ConvNet model achieving 3.65% test error (surpassing previous human-designed architectures) while being 1.05x faster. On Penn Treebank, their discovered recurrent cell achieves 62.4 test perplexity, a 3.6 perplexity improvement over the previous state-of-the-art. The control experiments (adding more functions to the search space, comparing against random search) and transfer learning experiments (applying the discovered cell to character language modeling and machine translation) provide robust validation of the approach.

The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides sufficient implementation details for reproducibility. The experimental setup follows standard practices (using held-out validation sets for reward signals, reporting test performance only once for the best validation model), which enhances the scientific rigor of the results.

## Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Computational cost discussion**: The paper mentions training 12,800 architectures on CIFAR-10 using 800 GPUs concurrently, which is a significant computational investment. Adding a discussion about the trade-offs between computational cost and architectural quality would strengthen the paper.

2. **Architecture analysis**: While the paper mentions that the discovered CIFAR-10 architecture has "many rectangular filters" and prefers "larger filters at the top layers," a more in-depth analysis of why these design choices are beneficial would add valuable insight.

3. **Comparison clarity**: The comparison with the DenseNet model that achieves 3.74% error rate is somewhat ambiguous since DenseNet uses 1x1 convolutions to reduce parameters, which the authors did not do. A clearer comparison would help readers understand the relative advantages.

4. **Limitations discussion**: The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the method's limitations, particularly regarding scalability to more complex tasks like object detection or semantic segmentation.

## Questions for Authors

1. How does the computational cost of your method compare to other architecture search approaches? What are the trade-offs between the quality of discovered architectures and the computational resources required?

2. The paper mentions that the CIFAR-10 architecture "prefers larger filters at the top layers." Could you provide more insight into why this design choice might lead to better performance?

3. How do you expect your method to scale to more complex tasks like object detection or semantic segmentation, where architecture search spaces are significantly larger and more complex?

4. For the Penn Treebank results, you state that your cell is "more than two times faster" than previous best networks. Could you provide more specific metrics about this speedup (e.g., operations per second, wall-clock time)?

5. The comparison with DenseNet is complicated by different architectural choices (1x1 convolutions). Could you clarify how your model might be modified to make a fairer comparison with DenseNet?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neural architecture search. The method is novel, well-executed, and empirically validated with impressive results. I believe this paper will be of significant interest to the ICLR community.
