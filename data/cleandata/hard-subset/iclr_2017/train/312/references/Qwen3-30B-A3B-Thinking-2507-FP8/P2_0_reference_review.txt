

# ICLR Review: "Neural Architecture Search"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach called Neural Architecture Search (NAS), which uses a gradient-based method to automatically discover high-performing neural network architectures. The key contribution is the use of a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate architectural descriptions as variable-length sequences, followed by reinforcement learning to optimize the controller based on the validation accuracy of the generated architectures. The authors demonstrate that NAS can discover convolutional architectures for image recognition (CIFAR-10) that achieve 3.65% test error (outperforming previous human-designed models while being 1.05x faster) and recurrent cell architectures for language modeling (Penn Treebank) that achieve 62.4 perplexity (3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art). The paper also provides thorough analysis of the approach's flexibility, scalability, and robustness through multiple control experiments and transfer learning results.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, with a strong related work section that carefully distinguishes their method from existing approaches in hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis. The authors provide a clear technical explanation of their method, including how the controller RNN generates architectures, how reinforcement learning is applied to train the controller, and how they extend the approach to handle skip connections and recurrent cell architectures.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

Key reasons for this decision:
1. The paper addresses a highly relevant and challenging problem in deep learning (automated architecture design) with a novel and well-motivated approach.
2. The empirical results are strong, scientifically rigorous, and clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing approaches.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning. Architecture design has traditionally been a manual, expert-intensive process that requires substantial time and expertise. NAS offers a promising path toward automating this process, which could democratize access to high-performing models and accelerate innovation in deep learning.

The method is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors effectively contrast their approach with existing methods in hyperparameter optimization (which typically search fixed-length spaces) and neuro-evolution (which is slow and requires many heuristics). The connection to sequence-to-sequence learning and reinforcement learning is well-explained, and the paper clearly articulates how their approach overcomes limitations of previous methods.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and scientifically rigorous. The authors present results on two major benchmarks (CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank), compare against multiple strong baselines, and include control experiments (including a comparison against random search). The results consistently show that NAS can discover architectures that outperform human-designed ones, with statistically significant improvements. The transfer learning experiments (to character language modeling and machine translation) further strengthen the paper's claims about the generalizability of the discovered architectures.

The paper is well-written and well-structured, with clear explanations of the technical approach and results. The figures and tables effectively support the narrative, and the methodology is described in sufficient detail to be reproducible.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong overall, the following suggestions would further improve its quality:

1. **Computational cost analysis**: The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the NAS approach. How many GPU hours were required to discover the architectures presented in the paper? How does this compare to the time required for human-designed architectures? Providing this information would help readers understand the practical trade-offs of using NAS.

2. **Search space efficiency**: The paper mentions the search space for the recurrent cell is approximately 6×10^16 architectures but only evaluates 12,800 architectures. A more detailed explanation of how the controller efficiently navigates such a large space without getting stuck in local optima would strengthen the paper.

3. **Limitations discussion**: The paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of the limitations of the approach. For example, how would NAS scale to larger datasets like ImageNet? Have the authors conducted any preliminary experiments on smaller ImageNet subsets to gauge scalability?

4. **Comparison to more recent work**: The paper was submitted before the recent NAS papers (e.g., "DARTS" by Liu et al., 2018), but it would be helpful to acknowledge how the approach compares to these more recent developments in the field.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that NAS "can design good models from scratch, an achievement considered not possible with other methods." What specific methods do you consider as the "other methods" that cannot do this, and why do they fail? Could you provide a more detailed comparison?

2. For the CIFAR-10 results, you mention that your model with 40 additional filters per layer achieves 3.65% error rate while being 1.05x faster than DenseNet (3.74% error rate). However, DenseNet (Huang et al., 2016b) uses 1x1 convolutions to reduce parameters, which you did not. Could you provide a fairer comparison that controls for this architectural difference?

3. The paper mentions that your recurrent cell architecture is 2x faster than the previous best model. Could you provide more details on this speed comparison (e.g., inference time per token, number of FLOPs, etc.)?

4. In Table 2, your best model achieves 62.4 perplexity, but the paper states "3.6 perplexity better than the previous state-of-the-art." What was the previous state-of-the-art perplexity value? A clear reference to this value would help readers better understand the magnitude of the improvement.

5. The paper mentions that the controller RNN "increases the depth by 2 for the child models every 1,600 samples." How did you determine this schedule, and would a different schedule potentially lead to better results?
