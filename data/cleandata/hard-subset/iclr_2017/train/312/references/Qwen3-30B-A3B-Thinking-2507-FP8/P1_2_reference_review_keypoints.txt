- Proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS): a controller RNN trained with policy gradients to generate variable-length network architectures using validation accuracy as reward.  
- Demonstrates NAS on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, finding architectures that outperform human-designed models (3.65% test error on CIFAR-10, 62.4 perplexity on Penn Treebank) and releasing code/NASCell.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key acceptance reason 1: Tackles an important problem (automating architecture design) with a novel, well-motivated approach.  
- Key acceptance reason 2: Empirical results are strong and supported by thorough ablation studies.  
- Problem significance: Automating architecture engineering addresses a major manual bottleneck in deep learning.  
- Novelty and positioning: Treats architectures as variable-length strings generated by an RNN; clearly positioned relative to hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis.  
- Empirical evaluation: Comprehensive experiments and controls show NAS finds architectures that are more accurate and faster than prior human-designed models and beat random search.  
- Technical soundness: Method and implementation (controller RNN, policy gradients, skip connections, distributed training) are well-described and reproducible.  
- Improvement suggestion: Quantify total computational cost (e.g., GPU-hours) of NAS to clarify practical feasibility.  
- Improvement suggestion: Compare discovered architectures to more recent human-designed models (e.g., MobileNets, EfficientNets).  
- Improvement suggestion: Provide an ablation study isolating the impact of skip connections.  
- Improvement suggestion: Include a clearer visualization of the discovered CIFAR-10 architecture in the main paper.  
- Improvement suggestion: Discuss limitations such as scaling to larger datasets and potential overfitting to the validation set.  
- Question 1: Please compare NAS results precisely to the most recent human-designed architectures (e.g., EfficientNets, MobileNets).  
- Question 2: Clarify which DenseNet variant is used for the 3.74% comparison and provide computational complexity details for the speed comparison.  
- Question 3: Explain the schedule used to increase child-network layer count, how it’s determined, and its impact on final architectures.  
- Question 4: Provide a detailed comparison between the discovered recurrent cell and LSTM, noting similarities and differences that drive performance gains.  
- Question 5: Clarify what “more than two times faster” means for the Penn Treebank model (inference vs. training) and the conditions under which this speedup was measured.
