

# Peer Review: "Neural Architecture Search"

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel approach to automatically design neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (the "controller") that generates architectural hyperparameters as a sequence of tokens. The controller is trained via policy gradient methods to maximize the expected validation accuracy of the architectures it proposes. The method is flexible, capable of searching variable-length architecture spaces, and does not require fixed-length configuration spaces like previous hyperparameter optimization approaches. The authors demonstrate NAS on two challenging benchmarks: CIFAR-10 image classification (achieving 3.65% test error, outperforming human-designed architectures while being 1.05x faster) and Penn Treebank language modeling (achieving 62.4 test perplexity, 3.6 points better than previous state-of-the-art). The paper provides a thorough methodological description, including extensions for skip connections and recurrent cell architectures, along with comprehensive experimental results.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning with strong empirical results and a novel methodology that addresses a meaningful problem. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the method is well-motivated and addresses a genuine limitation in current neural architecture design practices, and (2) the empirical results are compelling and well-documented across two distinct domains.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution—using a controller RNN with policy gradients to search architecture spaces—is well-motivated and addresses a critical gap in the literature. Previous approaches to architecture search either require fixed-length spaces (hyperparameter optimization) or are computationally intractable (neuro-evolution methods). NAS bridges this gap by enabling flexible search over variable-length architectures, a capability not previously demonstrated in the literature.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous, with careful attention to experimental details. The authors provide comprehensive comparisons against state-of-the-art human-designed architectures, not just the previous best neural network architectures. The CIFAR-10 results are particularly impressive: the discovered architecture achieves better accuracy than DenseNet (3.65% vs. 3.74%) while being 1.05x faster. The language modeling results showing a 3.6 perplexity improvement over previous work is also significant.

However, the paper would benefit from addressing a few limitations. The computational cost is extremely high—800 GPUs for CIFAR-10 and 400 CPUs for Penn Treebank—raising questions about practicality for researchers without massive computational resources. While the authors acknowledge this in the conclusion, they don't provide a thorough discussion of the cost-benefit tradeoff. Additionally, the paper lacks a detailed comparison with other recent NAS approaches (e.g., the recent ENAS work by Pham et al. 2018), which would help contextualize the method's relative advantages.

The theoretical grounding could be strengthened. While the use of policy gradients is justified as a way to handle non-differentiable rewards, the paper doesn't discuss why this specific approach works better than other reinforcement learning methods for architecture search. A more detailed analysis of the reward signal (validation accuracy) and its relationship to the search process would add depth.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Computational Cost Analysis**: The paper should include a more detailed discussion of the computational cost versus benefits. A cost-benefit analysis comparing the computational resources required to NAS versus human-designed architectures would be valuable for the community.

2. **Comparison with Recent NAS Work**: The paper should be updated to include a comparison with recent NAS approaches, particularly those that have emerged since the initial submission (e.g., ENAS, DARTS). This would help position the work more precisely in the current landscape.

3. **Architecture Analysis**: The paper should provide more insight into *why* the discovered architectures work better. For example, what specific architectural features (beyond skip connections) contribute most to the performance gains? A detailed ablation study would strengthen the findings.

4. **Theoretical Justification**: While the method is empirically successful, the paper would benefit from a more rigorous theoretical justification for why the controller RNN with policy gradients is well-suited for architecture search. Why does this approach work better than other RL methods or evolutionary algorithms?

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper mentions that the controller increases the number of layers as training progresses. Could you elaborate on how this schedule was determined? Was it based on empirical observations, or was it optimized through a separate search process?

2. In the CIFAR-10 experiments, you mention that the discovered architecture with 40 additional filters per layer achieves 3.65% error rate. How does this compare to the architecture without the additional filters in terms of parameter count and inference speed? The paper states it's 1.05x faster than DenseNet, but doesn't provide a direct comparison with the architecture without the additional filters.

3. The paper claims that the discovered recurrent cell generalizes to other tasks (character language modeling and machine translation). Could you provide more details about the generalization performance on different datasets beyond the specific results presented in the paper?

4. The paper states that the search space for the recurrent cell has approximately 6×10^16 architectures, but the controller only evaluates 12,800 architectures. What is the probability that the discovered cell is truly optimal within this vast search space, and how does the method avoid getting stuck in local optima?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of automated neural architecture design. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent addition to the ICLR proceedings.
