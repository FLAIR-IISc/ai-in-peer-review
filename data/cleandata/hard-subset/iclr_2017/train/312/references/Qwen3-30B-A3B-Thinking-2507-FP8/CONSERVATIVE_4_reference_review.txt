

# Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces "Neural Architecture Search" (NAS), a method that uses a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate neural network architectures. The controller is trained via policy gradient reinforcement learning to maximize the validation accuracy of the architectures it generates (the "child" networks). The authors claim their method can automatically design high-performing architectures from scratch, outperforming human-invented models on CIFAR-10 (3.65% test error) and Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity). They present extensions for generating both convolutional and recurrent architectures, along with ablation studies and comparisons to random search.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper lacks sufficient evidence to justify the claim that this new approach is a meaningful contribution to the field, particularly given the high computational cost and lack of thorough comparison with established methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

My decision is based on three key concerns that undermine the paper's scientific contribution:

First, the paper fails to provide adequate evidence that this approach is genuinely superior to existing methods. The authors claim their method "can design good models from scratch, an achievement considered not possible with other methods," but they don't sufficiently compare with prior automated architecture search approaches. Their related work section mentions Bayesian optimization methods but doesn't provide a meaningful comparison of results. The paper claims to outperform human-invented architectures, but doesn't demonstrate that previous automated methods couldn't achieve similar results. The comparison to random search (Control Experiment 2) is insufficient - they don't specify how many random architectures they tested, which is crucial for assessing the value of their method.

Second, the computational cost is prohibitively high without adequate justification. The paper mentions training "800 networks being trained on 800 GPUs concurrently" for CIFAR-10. This level of computational expense makes the method impractical for most researchers and raises questions about whether the marginal gains justify the resources. The paper doesn't provide a cost-benefit analysis or compare the computational requirements to other methods.

Third, the paper lacks sufficient validation against established baselines and doesn't address potential overfitting to the validation set. The authors use the validation set to guide the search, which creates a risk of overfitting to this particular validation set. The paper doesn't provide a proper train-validation-test separation or demonstrate that the final model would generalize to new data. Additionally, the paper doesn't include statistical significance testing for their performance gains (e.g., 3.65% vs. 3.74% on CIFAR-10), making it difficult to assess whether these differences are meaningful.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, I recommend the following:

1. Conduct a thorough comparison with established automated architecture search methods (e.g., Bayesian optimization, evolutionary algorithms) rather than just random search. Provide a clear table showing the number of architectures searched, computational cost, and performance for each method.

2. Include a detailed analysis of the computational cost, including GPU hours required to find a single architecture, and compare this to other methods. Address whether the marginal performance gains justify the computational expense.

3. Perform a proper statistical analysis of the results, including confidence intervals and significance testing for the performance improvements.

4. Address the risk of validation set overfitting by implementing a more robust experimental protocol, such as using a separate hold-out validation set for the search process and a final test set for evaluation.

5. Clarify the relationship between the search space size and the performance. The paper mentions a search space of "approximately 6 × 10^16 architectures" for the recurrent cell, but doesn't explain how this relates to the number of architectures actually searched (which appears to be around 12,800 for CIFAR-10).

6. Provide more details about the training process for the controller, including how many gradient updates were performed and how the controller's performance improved over time.

## Questions for Authors

1. How many architectures did you search with random search to establish the baseline in Control Experiment 2? How does this compare to the number of architectures searched with your method?

2. What is the total computational cost (in GPU hours) to find a single architecture using your method? How does this compare to the cost of other automated architecture search methods?

3. How do you address the risk of overfitting to the validation set in your search process? What evidence do you have that the final model would generalize to new data?

4. Why did you choose to use "the maximum validation accuracy of the last 5 epochs cubed" as the reward function? How did you determine this was the optimal choice?

5. How do your results compare to the recent AutoML methods that have been published since this paper's submission (e.g., the Google AutoML papers)?
