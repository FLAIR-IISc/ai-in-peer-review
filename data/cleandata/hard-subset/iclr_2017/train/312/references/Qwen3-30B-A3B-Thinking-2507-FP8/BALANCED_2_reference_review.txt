

# Review for "Neural Architecture Search"

## 1. Summary
The paper proposes "Neural Architecture Search," a method using a recurrent neural network (the controller) to generate neural network architectures, which are then evaluated on a validation set. The controller is trained via policy gradients using the validation accuracy as the reward signal. The authors demonstrate their approach on CIFAR-10 image classification and Penn Treebank language modeling, claiming state-of-the-art results with architectures that outperform human-designed networks. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and presents a novel application of reinforcement learning to architecture search.

## 2. Decision
Reject. While the paper presents an interesting approach with promising results, the methodological and experimental concerns undermine the claimed contributions. The key reasons for rejection are: (1) insufficient statistical evidence for claimed performance improvements, and (2) questionable experimental setup that risks validation set overfitting.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's core contribution is the use of a controller RNN trained via policy gradients to search for neural network architectures. This approach is positioned as more flexible than fixed-length search spaces used in prior hyperparameter optimization methods. The authors demonstrate their method on two challenging benchmarks, claiming significant improvements over previous human-designed architectures.

However, several critical issues prevent us from accepting this paper:

First, the paper lacks statistical evidence for its performance claims. For example, the paper states that their CIFAR-10 model achieves "3.65 test set error, while being 1.05x faster than the current best model." It provides no confidence intervals or statistical tests to support the claim of superiority. Similarly, for the Penn Treebank results, the paper claims a "3.6 perplexity better" than previous work without providing statistical significance. In machine learning research, such claims require proper statistical validation to be credible.

Second, the experimental setup contains a critical flaw: the same validation set used for reward signal during controller training is also used for model selection. This creates a risk of overfitting to the validation set, as the controller is directly optimized to maximize performance on this specific set. The paper does not address this issue, which significantly undermines the reported results. A proper validation strategy would use a separate validation set for model selection that is not used during controller training.

Third, the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from prior work. The concept of using reinforcement learning to search for architectures has been explored before (e.g., Zoph & Le, 2016), and this paper doesn't clearly articulate how its approach is novel or superior. The paper also doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why the discovered architectures work well, making it difficult to assess the true contribution.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To strengthen this paper, the authors should:

- Provide rigorous statistical analysis of all performance claims, including confidence intervals and significance tests.
- Implement a proper validation strategy where the model selection uses a separate validation set not used during controller training.
- Conduct a more thorough comparison with prior architecture search methods, not just human-designed architectures.
- Include computational complexity analysis (e.g., FLOPs, parameter count) to better quantify the "1.05x faster" claim.
- Provide more detailed analysis of the discovered architectures, explaining what specific features contribute to their success.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. How did you ensure the discovered architecture isn't overfitting to the validation set? Did you use a separate validation set for model selection that wasn't used during controller training?
2. Can you provide statistical significance tests for the performance improvements claimed (e.g., p-values for the 3.65% vs. 3.74% CIFAR-10 error rate, or 62.4 vs. 66.0 perplexity on Penn Treebank)?
3. How does your method differ from Zoph & Le (2016) in a meaningful way, beyond the specific architecture search space? What are the key technical innovations?
4. Can you provide a detailed comparison of computational complexity (e.g., FLOPs, parameter count) between your model and the DenseNet model you compare to, to better substantiate your "1.05x faster" claim?
5. The paper claims the discovered architecture is "novel," but it's not clear how this differs from existing architectures. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the architectural features that contribute to its success?

While the paper presents an interesting approach with promising results, these methodological and experimental concerns prevent us from accepting it for publication in ICLR. Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the contribution and make the paper suitable for publication.
