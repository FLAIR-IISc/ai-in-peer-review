

# Review

## Summary of Contribution
This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel approach that uses a recurrent neural network (the controller) to automatically generate neural network architectures. The key insight is treating neural network structures as variable-length strings, allowing the controller to generate architectures through a sequence of tokens. The controller is trained via policy gradients using the validation accuracy of the generated architectures as the reward signal. This approach enables the discovery of architectures that outperform human-designed ones on both image recognition (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank) tasks. The paper demonstrates significant empirical results, including a 3.65% test error on CIFAR-10 (better than previous human-invented architectures) and a 62.4 perplexity on Penn Treebank (3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art). The method is also extended to discover novel recurrent cell architectures that generalize well to different tasks.

## Decision
Accept. The paper presents a genuinely novel and bold approach to neural architecture search that has the potential to significantly impact the field. The core idea of using a controller RNN with policy gradient optimization to discover architectures is a major conceptual leap from previous methods.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's novelty is its most compelling aspect. While prior work has explored hyperparameter optimization and neuro-evolution, NAS is the first to use a gradient-based approach to search variable-length architecture spaces. This represents a fundamental shift from previous methods that either searched fixed-length spaces or used evolutionary algorithms that are computationally expensive. The authors position their work well in the literature, contrasting with both hyperparameter optimization (limited to fixed-length spaces) and neuro-evolution (flexible but slow).

The empirical results are impressive and well-documented. The discovered CIFAR-10 architecture achieves 3.65% error (better than human-designed models) while being 1.05x faster than the DenseNet model. The Penn Treebank results show a 3.6 perplexity improvement over previous state-of-the-art. The paper includes thorough ablation studies, control experiments (including comparison against random search), and transfer learning experiments that demonstrate the generality of the discovered architectures.

The paper's approach is conceptually elegant and addresses a fundamental bottleneck in deep learning: the time-consuming, expert-dependent process of architecture design. The fact that the method can discover architectures that outperform human-designed ones is a significant achievement that justifies the paper's bold claim.

## Additional Feedback
1. While the paper demonstrates impressive results, a more detailed discussion of computational cost would be valuable. The distributed training approach with 800 GPUs is resource-intensive - it would be helpful to understand how this scales to smaller research groups.

2. The explanation of the recurrent cell generation (Section 3.4) is somewhat complex. Consider adding a clearer visual or simpler example to help readers grasp how the tree structure works.

3. The paper mentions "3.6 perplexity better than the previous state-of-the-art" but doesn't explicitly state what the previous state-of-the-art was. Clarifying this would strengthen the claim.

4. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach. For example, how does NAS perform on extremely large-scale tasks beyond CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank?

5. The comparison to random search in Figure 6 is compelling, but it would be helpful to see how the performance improves with more training iterations (e.g., 25,000 vs. 12,800 samples).

## Clarifying Questions
1. The paper states that the discovered CIFAR-10 model is 1.05x faster than the DenseNet model. Could you clarify what metric is being used to measure "faster" (e.g., inference time on specific hardware)?

2. The paper mentions that the controller RNN has to predict "a filter height in [1, 3, 5, 7]" - why were these specific values chosen? Were there other values considered that were ultimately excluded?

3. The paper states that the discovered recurrent cell outperforms LSTM on Penn Treebank, but the performance gain is relatively modest (3.6 perplexity). How does this compare to the gains achieved by other major improvements to RNNs (e.g., LSTM vs. basic RNN)?
