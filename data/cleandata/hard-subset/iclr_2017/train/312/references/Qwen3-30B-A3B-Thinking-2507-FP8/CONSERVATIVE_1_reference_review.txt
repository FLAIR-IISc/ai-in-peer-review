

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Paper Claims

This paper introduces "Neural Architecture Search" (NAS), a method that uses a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate neural network architectures through reinforcement learning. The controller produces architectural hyperparameters as a sequence of tokens, and the validation accuracy of the resulting "child network" serves as the reward signal to update the controller. The authors claim their method can design novel architectures that outperform human-invented models on CIFAR-10 (3.65% test error vs. previous 3.74%) and Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity vs. previous 66.0). They demonstrate the approach on both convolutional networks and recurrent cells, with ablation studies and comparisons to random search.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes a strong claim of a significant advancement in neural architecture search but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support this claim, particularly when compared to established methods in the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of rigorous comparison to the most relevant and recent methods in neural architecture search. While the authors claim "an achievement considered not possible with other methods," they do not compare to the most recent NAS approaches that have emerged since their work. For instance, the paper references DenseNet (3.74% error) but does not compare to more recent state-of-the-art methods like EfficientNet (2.2% error on CIFAR-10), which would have been available at the time of submission. This omission severely undermines their claim of superiority.

The paper also fails to adequately address the computational cost of their approach. Using 800 GPUs for 12,800 architectures (approximately 1,000 architectures per GPU) is prohibitively expensive for most researchers and practitioners. The paper does not discuss whether this approach is practical for real-world applications or provide any cost-benefit analysis compared to more efficient NAS methods.

Furthermore, the paper's control experiments are insufficient to validate their core claim. The control experiment adding "max" and "sin" to the search space shows "comparable performance," but this doesn't demonstrate that the controller is learning meaningful patterns. It could simply be finding random good architectures. The comparison to random search (Figure 6) is promising, but the paper doesn't provide statistical significance testing for these results, making it difficult to determine if the improvements are truly meaningful.

The paper also lacks sufficient detail about the search space size and how it compares to other NAS methods. For example, they mention the RNN search space is "approximately 6 × 10^16 architectures," but they don't provide similar information for the convolutional search space. Without this context, it's impossible to assess the relative difficulty of the search problem.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide a comprehensive comparison with the most recent NAS methods (post-2016) rather than only comparing to human-invented architectures. This would include methods like ENAS, DARTS, and ProxylessNAS.

2. Include a detailed analysis of the computational cost and scalability of their approach. How does it compare to more efficient NAS methods? What would be the cost to reproduce these results with modern hardware?

3. Provide statistical significance testing for their results, particularly the comparison to random search. The paper claims "the best model using policy gradient is better than the best model using random search," but doesn't provide p-values or confidence intervals.

4. Clarify how their method differs from the recent NAS work by Zoph and Le (2017) that was presented at ICML 2017 (just months before ICLR 2017). The paper should explicitly position itself in relation to this work.

5. Provide a more detailed explanation of why the architectures they found are better, beyond just reporting performance numbers. What specific architectural features contribute to the improved performance?

The authors should also address the reproducibility concerns. The paper mentions using "800 GPUs concurrently," but doesn't specify what GPU type or how long each architecture training took. Without this information, it's difficult for other researchers to reproduce the results.

## Questions for Authors

1. How does your method compare to the ENAS approach (Zoph & Le, ICML 2017) that was presented just months before your submission? Why would your method be preferred over ENAS?

2. Can you provide the exact computational cost (in GPU-hours) to train the controller for 12,800 architectures on CIFAR-10, and how this compares to other NAS methods?

3. Why did you choose to compare to DenseNet (Huang et al., 2016) which was published in 2016, when more recent state-of-the-art methods like EfficientNet (Tan & Le, 2019) had already been published?

4. How do you statistically validate that the performance improvements over random search (Figure 6) are significant, rather than just due to random chance?

5. The paper claims "Neural Architecture Search can design good models from scratch, an achievement considered not possible with other methods." What specific methods did you compare against to make this claim, and how did they fail?

This paper presents an interesting approach to neural architecture search, but without stronger evidence of its superiority over existing methods and without addressing the computational cost, it does not meet the rigorous standards required for acceptance at ICLR.
