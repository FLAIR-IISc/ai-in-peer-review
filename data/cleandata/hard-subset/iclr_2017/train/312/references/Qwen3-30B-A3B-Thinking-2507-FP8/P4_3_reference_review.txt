

# Review

## Summary
The paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a gradient-based method for automatically discovering neural network architectures. The authors propose using a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate variable-length strings representing network architectures, then training the controller via policy gradients using the validation accuracy of the generated architectures ("child networks") as the reward signal. NAS is applied to both convolutional architectures for image classification (CIFAR-10) and recurrent cell architectures for language modeling (Penn Treebank). The results demonstrate that NAS discovers architectures that outperform human-invented ones, achieving 3.65% test error on CIFAR-10 (1.05x faster than the best human-invented model) and 62.4 perplexity on Penn Treebank (3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art).

## Decision
Accept. The paper presents a well-motivated approach with strong empirical validation that addresses an important problem in deep learning.

## Supporting Arguments
1. The paper clearly defines a specific and significant problem: automatically discovering neural network architectures without human intervention. The authors effectively motivate their approach by identifying limitations of existing methods (fixed-length hyperparameter optimization and slow neuro-evolution), positioning NAS as a more flexible and practical solution.

2. The technical approach is sound and well-executed. The authors provide a clear description of how the controller RNN generates architecture strings, how policy gradients are applied to train the controller, and how the method can be extended to handle skip connections and recurrent architectures. The distributed training implementation with parameter servers is well-described and addresses a practical challenge in applying the method.

3. The empirical results are compelling and scientifically rigorous. The paper includes thorough experiments on two challenging benchmarks with appropriate baselines. The control experiments (adding more functions in the search space, comparison against random search) provide strong evidence that the results are not due to chance. The transfer learning experiments demonstrate that the discovered cell generalizes to different tasks (character language modeling, machine translation), further supporting the validity of the approach.

## Additional Feedback
- The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of computational cost. While the authors note the speed advantage of the discovered CIFAR-10 model, they don't quantify the computational cost of the NAS process itself compared to human-designed architectures.

- The paper could provide more specific details about the discovered architectures. For example, the paper mentions the CIFAR-10 architecture has "many rectangular filters" and "prefers larger filters at the top layers," but more concrete details about the architecture's structure would help readers understand what makes it effective.

- The paper states the NAS approach is "the first to design good models from scratch," but could more clearly distinguish this claim from previous work on automated architecture search (e.g., the concurrent work by Real et al. on "Regularized Evolution" for neural architecture search).

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper mentions the discovered CIFAR-10 architecture is "1.05x faster" than the DenseNet model. Could the authors clarify whether this refers to inference speed or training speed, and provide more specific details about the speed comparison (e.g., number of FLOPs, inference time on specific hardware)?

2. The paper states that the NAS method can search "variable-length architecture space," but the specific search space dimensions (e.g., maximum number of layers, possible layer types) aren't clearly quantified. Could the authors provide a more precise description of the search space complexity?

3. The paper claims the NAS approach "can design good models from scratch, an achievement considered not possible with other methods." Could the authors clarify which previous methods they are referring to and why they consider those methods incapable of this achievement?
