

# Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a gradient-based method for automatically discovering neural network architectures using a recurrent controller network. The approach frames architecture search as a sequence generation problem, where a controller RNN generates architectural hyperparameters as tokens, and uses reinforcement learning (REINFORCE) to optimize for validation accuracy. The authors claim their method can design architectures that outperform human-invented models on CIFAR-10 (3.65% test error, 1.05x faster than DenseNet) and Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity, 3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art).

## 2. Decision
Reject. Key reasons: The paper contains multiple mathematical errors, inconsistent results reporting, and formatting inconsistencies that undermine the credibility of the claims and suggest insufficient attention to detail.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's credibility is significantly damaged by multiple technical errors that would be immediately apparent to reviewers familiar with reinforcement learning and neural architecture search:

1. **Critical mathematical errors**: In Section 3.2, the REINFORCE gradient update is written as "5θc" (should be "∇θc") and the empirical approximation contains "m m" instead of "1/m". These are not mere typos but fundamental errors in the mathematical formulation that would invalidate the method as described.

2. **Inconsistent results reporting**: The abstract claims "3.65 test set error" while results consistently use "3.65%". More critically, the paper states "a 39-layer network that achieves 4.47% which is very close to the best human-invented architecture that achieves 3.74%" but later claims to have improved this to "3.65%". This creates confusion about what constitutes the best human-invented architecture.

3. **Misleading comparisons**: The paper states "a 20-layer architecture that achieves 6.01% error rate... is not much worse than the first set of experiments" (which achieved 5.50%). This is mathematically incorrect - 6.01% is meaningfully worse than 5.50%.

4. **Formatting inconsistencies**: The paper uses inconsistent spacing in mathematical expressions ("1e − 4" vs "1e-4"), inconsistent citation styles ("Bergstra & Bengio" vs "Bergstra and Bengio"), and inconsistent terminology ("error" vs "error rate").

5. **Missing critical details**: The paper fails to explain how the tree structure for recurrent cells is chosen (is it fixed or part of the search?), doesn't specify the decay rate for the exponential moving average baseline, and provides no computational cost analysis of the search process.

These issues collectively indicate a lack of careful attention to detail that would be expected in a top-tier machine learning conference. While the paper's core idea is interesting, the technical shortcomings and inconsistencies are too severe to justify acceptance.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Correct all mathematical errors in Section 3.2 (REINFORCE formulation) and Section 3.3 (equation for skip connections).

2. Clarify the comparison between the controller's results and human-invented architectures - specifically, what is the "best human-invented architecture" being compared to (3.74% or 3.65%)?

3. Provide a clear explanation of the computational cost of the search process (how many GPU hours were required?).

4. Fix inconsistent terminology and formatting throughout the paper (e.g., "3.65 test set error" vs "3.65% error rate").

5. Clarify the speed comparison claims (what does "1.05x faster" mean? 5% faster or 1.05x the speed?).

6. Provide a clear explanation of the tree structure for recurrent cells - is it fixed or part of the search?

7. Address the duplicate citation for Huang et al. (2016) in the references.

8. Clarify the relationship between the two mentions of "Figure 8" (the cell visualization and the architecture with max/sin functions).

## Questions for Authors
1. What is the exact computational cost (GPU hours) of the search process for CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank?

2. How does the paper's claimed 3.65% error rate on CIFAR-10 compare to the best human-invented architectures published at the time of submission (2017)?

3. In Section 4.1, the paper states "the controller can design a 39-layer network that achieves 4.47%... while having better performance" than the DenseNet model that achieves 3.74%. This is confusing - which model achieves what error rate?

4. What is the exact meaning of "1.05x faster" in Section 4.1? Is this 5% faster (1.05x the speed) or 5% slower (0.95x the speed)?

5. In Section 3.4, is the tree structure for recurrent cells fixed or part of the search space? If it's fixed, what's the justification for choosing that particular structure?
