

# Review

## 1. Summary of Claims
The paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a gradient-based method for automatically discovering neural network architectures. The authors propose using a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate variable-length strings specifying network architectures, then training this controller via reinforcement learning using the validation accuracy of the generated architectures as the reward signal. The method is applied to both convolutional architectures for image recognition (CIFAR-10) and recurrent cell architectures for language modeling (Penn Treebank). The authors claim their method discovers novel architectures that outperform human-designed models, achieving 3.65% test error on CIFAR-10 (better than previous human-designed models) and 62.4 perplexity on Penn Treebank (3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art).

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper presents a technically sound approach that makes a significant contribution to the field of neural architecture search, with strong empirical results supporting the claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper clearly addresses a meaningful problem: automating neural architecture design, which has traditionally required significant expert knowledge. The approach is well-motivated, with a thorough literature review that properly situates NAS within hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their method can search variable-length architecture spaces, unlike previous fixed-length approaches.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous. The paper provides comprehensive experimental details across multiple tasks with appropriate baselines (including random search as a strong baseline). The CIFAR-10 results show a 3.65% test error, which is better than previous human-designed models while being 1.05x faster. The Penn Treebank results achieve 62.4 perplexity, a 3.6 improvement over the previous state-of-the-art. The control experiments (adding more functions to the search space and comparing against random search) further validate the approach's robustness and effectiveness.

The methodology is technically sound, with clear descriptions of the controller RNN, the reinforcement learning update rule, and the extension to skip connections and recurrent cells. The distributed training approach to accelerate controller training is well-described and practical.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, the authors might consider:
- Providing more detailed analysis of the discovered architectures (e.g., why skip connections are beneficial in the CIFAR-10 model)
- Including computational complexity analysis of the NAS method compared to human-designed architecture search
- Adding a discussion of limitations and potential failure cases of the approach
- Clarifying the exact metrics used for "faster" in the CIFAR-10 results (inference time on specific hardware?)

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper states the NAS CIFAR-10 model is "1.05x faster" than DenseNet. Could the authors clarify what metric they're using for "faster" (e.g., inference time on specific hardware, FLOPs, or another measure)?
2. The Penn Treebank results claim a 3.6 perplexity improvement over previous state-of-the-art, but the table shows the previous best model (Zilly et al.) has 66.0 perplexity. Could the authors clarify if they're comparing to Zilly et al. or another model not listed in the table?
3. The paper mentions "we did not do" 1x1 convolutions like DenseNet, which affected the comparison. Could the authors provide an ablation study showing the impact of adding 1x1 convolutions to their architecture?
4. The controller training required sampling 12,800 architectures for CIFAR-10. Could the authors provide the total computational cost (e.g., GPU-hours) for this training process?
