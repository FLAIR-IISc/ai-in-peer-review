1. Paper proposes a gradient-based Neural Architecture Search (NAS) using a controller RNN trained with REINFORCE to generate architectures, evaluated on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank.  
2. Strong empirical claims: 3.65% CIFAR-10 test error and 62.4 perplexity on PTB, outperforming human-designed models.  
3. Decision: Reject.  
4. Primary reason: Complete omission of Zoph & Le (2017) ICLR paper, which is nearly identical and undermines novelty.  
5. Omission is an ethical/academic lapse that prevents the paper from claiming originality.  
6. Comparison methodology is weak — only compares to random search and not to the most relevant prior work.  
7. No statistical significance testing presented for reported improvements.  
8. Massive computational cost (e.g., 800 GPUs for CIFAR-10) makes the method impractical for most researchers.  
9. Paper fails to analyze computational trade-offs (time and monetary costs) versus human-designed architectures.  
10. Claim of being "1.05x faster" than the best model is misleading due to invalid DenseNet comparison (missing 1×1 convs).  
11. Recurrent cell search (Section 3.4) is confusing and under-specified, hindering reproducibility.  
12. Paper does not address risk of overfitting the search to the validation set.  
13. Major required fixes: cite and compare with Zoph & Le (2017) and other NAS methods.  
14. Major required fixes: improve baselines, add statistical significance tests, and clarify recurrent cell search description.  
15. Major required fixes: provide detailed computational cost analysis (GPU-hours, monetary cost).  
16. Reviewer questions for authors: why omit Zoph & Le (2017)?  
17. Reviewer question: how was overfitting to validation prevented?  
18. Reviewer question: exact computational cost to find final architectures and comparison to training human-designed models?  
19. Reviewer question: how would DenseNet comparison change if 1×1 convolutions were used?  
20. Reviewer question: provide clearer analysis of the search-space structure and justification for its sufficiency.
