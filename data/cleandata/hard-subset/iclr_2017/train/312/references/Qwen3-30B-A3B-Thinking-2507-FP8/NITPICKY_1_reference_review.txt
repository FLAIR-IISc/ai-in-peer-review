

# Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

## Summary
This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network controller trained with policy gradients. The authors claim their approach can discover novel architectures that outperform human-designed models on CIFAR-10 (3.65% error rate) and Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity), while being faster than existing models. The paper presents a comprehensive experimental evaluation across two challenging benchmarks.

## Decision
Reject

## Key Reasons for Rejection
1. The paper contains a major methodological flaw in the CIFAR-10 validation procedure that invalidates the reported results: the validation set is sampled from the training set (5,000 examples randomly sampled from the training set), creating data leakage and invalidating the reported validation accuracy.

2. The paper makes inaccurate claims about the novelty of their approach, specifically claiming "Neural Architecture Search can design good models from scratch, an achievement considered not possible with other methods," when prior work (Zoph & Le, 2016) had already demonstrated this capability.

## Supporting Arguments
The validation procedure flaw is particularly severe. In Section 4.1, the paper states: "the validation set has 5,000 examples randomly sampled from the training set, the remaining 45,000 examples are used for training." This creates data leakage, as the validation set is not independent of the training set. This is a fundamental error in experimental design that makes the reported results scientifically invalid. In deep learning experiments, validation sets must be completely separate from training data to provide an unbiased estimate of generalization performance.

The claim about novelty is also problematic. The paper cites no prior work on neural architecture search that designs models from scratch, despite the existence of Zoph & Le's "Neural Architecture Search" (2016) which directly addresses this problem. The paper's claim that their method "can design good models from scratch, an achievement considered not possible with other methods" is demonstrably false based on the cited literature.

Additionally, the paper contains numerous minor technical inconsistencies:
- The CIFAR-10 results are confusingly presented: they claim to achieve 3.65% error, but then state this model is "1.05x faster than the DenseNet model that achieves 3.74%" while also comparing to a DenseNet model that achieves 3.46% (which is better than their 3.65%).
- The reward function is cubed (validation accuracy cubed), but the paper doesn't justify this arbitrary transformation.
- The controller RNN uses 35 hidden units, but the paper doesn't explain why this specific number was chosen or whether it was tuned.
- The baseline for the REINFORCE update uses an exponential moving average, but the decay rate isn't specified.
- The reference formatting is inconsistent (some entries have \Q2016\E, others have \Q2015\E).

These minor technical inconsistencies, while individually small, collectively indicate a lack of rigorous attention to detail that undermines confidence in the paper's overall methodology.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper should be completely rewritten to address the validation set issue. The authors must use a proper, independent validation set that is not sampled from the training set.

2. The authors should carefully review prior work on neural architecture search and revise their claims about novelty. Zoph & Le (2016) is directly relevant and should be properly cited and contrasted.

3. The paper should clarify the exact relationship between their model's performance and the DenseNet models they cite. The current presentation is confusing and potentially misleading.

4. The paper should provide more details about the controller architecture (e.g., why 35 hidden units, why two layers) and the hyperparameters for the REINFORCE baseline.

5. The paper should explain why they cube the validation accuracy for the reward function, as this is an unusual choice without justification.

6. The reference list contains formatting inconsistencies and some incomplete entries that should be cleaned up.

## Questions for Authors
1. How did you verify that your reported validation accuracy is not inflated by data leakage (since your validation set was sampled from the training set)?

2. Can you provide a proper comparison to Zoph & Le (2016), which also demonstrated neural architecture search for designing models from scratch?

3. Why did you cube the validation accuracy for the reward function, and how does this affect the controller's learning dynamics?

4. Can you clarify the exact relationship between your 3.65% error rate and the DenseNet models cited (3.74% and 3.46%)? Which DenseNet model are you actually comparing to?

5. What computational complexity metric did you use when claiming "same computational complexity" for the GNMT experiments?

These questions are essential for evaluating the scientific validity of your claims. Without clear answers, the paper's results cannot be trusted, and the novelty claims cannot be substantiated.
