- Proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS): a controller RNN that generates architecture tokens and is trained with policy gradients using validation accuracy as reward.
- Handles variable-length architectures, unlike prior fixed-length search methods.
- Incorporates skip connections, branching layers, and attention to enable complex architectures.
- Extends NAS to design recurrent cell architectures with a tree-based representation that outperforms LSTM.
- Demonstrates strong empirical results on CIFAR-10 (3.65% test error, slightly better and faster than DenseNet) and Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity, +3.6 improvement).
- Releases code and integrates the discovered RNN cell into TensorFlow, enhancing practical impact.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key acceptance reasons: problem importance (automated architecture design) and strong empirical evidence across benchmarks.
- Positions NAS well in literature, contrasting it against hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and meta-learning.
- Experimental validation is thorough: multiple benchmarks, ablation studies, random-search controls, and proper validation/test reporting.
- Technical contributions include the controller RNN for variable-length descriptions, attention-based skip/branch incorporation, recurrent-cell search, and a distributed training approach.
- Results outperform several human-designed architectures and establish new baselines in tested domains.
- Suggests adding explicit computational cost analysis (GPU hours, resource comparisons to other methods).
- Recommends comparisons to more recent architectures (e.g., EfficientNet, MobileNet, newer ResNets) to demonstrate generality.
- Requests a clearer discussion of limitations and scalability to tasks like detection or segmentation.
- Asks for clarification on how learning rate prediction was handled in experiments.
- Requests analysis of controller hyperparameter sensitivity and how controller performance depends on its own hyperparameters.
- Questions for authors: report GPU hours and resource comparisons for CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank searches.
- Questions for authors: clarify how using 1x1 convolutions (as in DenseNet) would affect CIFAR-10 comparisons.
- Questions for authors: clarify whether shared embeddings in PTB were part of search or an extra technique and its effect.
- Questions for authors: provide exact final-performance numbers for best models found by policy gradient vs. random search in Figure 6.
- Questions for authors: specify which prior methods they claim could not design good models from scratch and how NAS fundamentally differs.
