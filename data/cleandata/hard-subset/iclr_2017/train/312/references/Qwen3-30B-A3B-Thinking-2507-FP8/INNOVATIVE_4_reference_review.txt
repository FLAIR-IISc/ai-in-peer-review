

# Review

## Summary
This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel approach for automatically discovering neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (the "controller") trained with policy gradients. The key insight is that neural network architectures can be represented as variable-length strings, enabling a controller RNN to generate architectural descriptions. The controller is trained by evaluating the performance of the generated architectures (child networks) on real data and using the accuracy as a reward signal to update the controller via reinforcement learning. The authors demonstrate that NAS can discover novel convolutional architectures for image recognition on CIFAR-10 that outperform human-designed models (3.65% test error, 1.05x faster than the best human-invented architecture) and novel recurrent cells for language modeling on Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity, 3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art). The approach is well-motivated, with strong empirical validation across multiple tasks and transfer learning experiments.

## Decision
Accept

## Supporting Arguments
This paper presents a genuinely novel and bold approach to a fundamental problem in deep learning: neural architecture design. The core idea of representing architectures as variable-length strings and using a controller RNN trained with policy gradients to search the architecture space is a significant conceptual leap from existing methods. Unlike previous approaches that either search fixed-length spaces (hyperparameter optimization) or rely on slow evolutionary methods, NAS offers a flexible, gradient-based method that can discover architectures with variable depth and connectivity.

The empirical results are compelling and well-supported. The discovered CIFAR-10 architecture achieves state-of-the-art performance while being more efficient than human-designed models, and the novel RNN cell outperforms LSTM architectures on Penn Treebank. The paper includes thorough ablation studies, control experiments (including a random search baseline), and transfer learning results that demonstrate the generalizability of the discovered architectures. The authors also provide evidence that the method can be integrated into existing frameworks (e.g., GNMT) without major modifications.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over methodological perfection, I find the core contribution of NAS to be sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant acceptance. The paper establishes a new research direction for automatically discovering neural network architectures, which is a significant step beyond incremental improvements in the field. The method's ability to discover architectures that outperform human-designed ones is particularly impressive and aligns with the boldness I value in ICLR submissions.

## Additional Feedback
While the paper is strong, the following points would help strengthen it further:

1. The computational cost of NAS is substantial (800 GPUs running concurrently). A more detailed discussion of the practical scalability limitations and potential approaches to reduce the computational burden would be valuable for readers considering adopting this method.

2. The paper mentions "we have added the RNN cell found using our method under the name NASCell into TensorFlow." It would be helpful to include a brief discussion of the community adoption or interest in this cell, perhaps through early adoption metrics or examples of other researchers using it.

3. The paper could benefit from a more explicit comparison to the recent ENAS (Efficient Neural Architecture Search) paper (Pham et al., 2018), which also uses a controller RNN but shares weights across architectures. While NAS is fundamentally different in its approach (policy gradient vs. weight-sharing), acknowledging this comparison would strengthen the positioning of the work.

4. The paper states "the controller RNN has to select a filter height in [1, 3, 5, 7]" but does not explain why these specific values were chosen. A brief justification for the discretization of continuous hyperparameters would improve methodological clarity.

## Clarifying Questions
1. The paper mentions that the controller "increases the depth by 2 for the child models every 1,600 samples" - could you elaborate on the rationale behind this specific schedule and how it impacts the search process?

2. In the Penn Treebank experiments, you mention "we use a base number of 8 to make sure that the cell is expressive." Could you provide more intuition about how the base number affects the expressiveness of the cell and how this choice was determined?

3. The paper states that NASCell was added to TensorFlow - what was the process for this integration, and how many users have adopted it since the release?
