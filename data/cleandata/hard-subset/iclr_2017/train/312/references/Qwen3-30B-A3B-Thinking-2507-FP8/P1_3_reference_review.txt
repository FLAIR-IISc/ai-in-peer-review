

# Peer Review: "Neural Architecture Search"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Contribution

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a novel gradient-based method for automatically discovering neural network architectures. The authors propose using a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate architectural hyperparameters as variable-length strings, and then using the validation accuracy of the resulting "child network" as a reward signal to train the controller via policy gradients. The method is flexible, capable of searching variable-length architecture spaces, and can handle both convolutional and recurrent architectures. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over human-designed architectures on two major benchmarks: CIFAR-10 (achieving 3.65% test error, 1.05x faster than the previous best model) and Penn Treebank (achieving 62.4 perplexity, 3.6 perplexity better than the previous state-of-the-art). The paper provides thorough experimental validation, including ablation studies, control experiments, and transfer learning results.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance of this paper.

**Key reasons for acceptance:**
1. The paper addresses an important and timely problem in deep learning: the time-consuming, expert-dependent process of neural architecture design.
2. The proposed approach is well-motivated, novel, and well-placed within the literature, with clear connections to related work in hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning. The Neural Architecture Search method is conceptually simple yet powerful, with the authors providing compelling empirical evidence of its effectiveness. The approach successfully automates architecture design without requiring human expertise, which is a substantial advancement.

The empirical results are robust and well-documented. On CIFAR-10, the method discovers a 39-layer architecture that achieves 3.65% test error (better than the human-designed DenseNet at 3.74%), while being 1.05x faster. On Penn Treebank, the discovered recurrent cell achieves 62.4 perplexity, which is 3.6 perplexity better than the previous state-of-the-art. The authors provide detailed ablation studies, including experiments where they allow the controller to predict strides and pooling layers, demonstrating the method's flexibility.

The paper includes important control experiments that strengthen the claims. The comparison against random search (Figure 6) shows that the policy gradient approach consistently outperforms random search, with the average of top models also being significantly better. The transfer learning results on character language modeling and machine translation further validate the generality of the discovered architectures.

The method is well-positioned within the literature. The authors carefully distinguish their approach from related work in hyperparameter optimization (which typically searches fixed-length spaces), neuro-evolution (which is slow and requires many heuristics), and program synthesis. They also show how their controller relates to sequence-to-sequence learning and reinforcement learning for BLEU optimization, while emphasizing their method's key difference: learning directly from reward signals without supervised bootstrapping.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, I suggest the following improvements to enhance its clarity and impact:

- **Computational Cost Analysis:** The paper mentions using distributed training with 800 GPUs for CIFAR-10 but doesn't quantify the total computational cost. A comparison of the total GPU-hours required to discover the architecture versus the cost of training human-designed architectures would provide valuable context about the practicality of the method.

- **Architectural Analysis:** The paper claims the discovered architectures are "novel" but doesn't provide much insight into what makes them novel. A more detailed analysis of the discovered architectures (e.g., why they prefer certain filter sizes, skip connections, or cell structures) would help readers understand the nature of the discoveries.

- **Search Space Efficiency:** It's unclear how the 12,800 architectures sampled by the controller compare to the size of the search space. A discussion of the sample efficiency of the method would help readers understand how effectively the controller navigates the search space.

- **Comparison to Other AutoML Methods:** While the paper discusses related work, it could more directly compare against other recent neural architecture search methods (e.g., DARTS, ENAS) to better establish the novelty and advantages of their approach.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding and provide additional evidence for my assessment, I would appreciate answers to these questions:

1. The paper mentions "compilation failures" due to incompatible layer connections. What percentage of architectures generated by the controller result in such failures, and how does the controller's performance change when these failures are handled versus when they're not?

2. The paper claims the controller "learns to improve its search over time." Could you provide more details about how the controller's performance (e.g., validation accuracy of sampled architectures) evolves during training? A plot showing the validation accuracy of architectures sampled at different stages of controller training would be helpful.

3. In the CIFAR-10 experiments, the authors state that the discovered architecture is "the shallowest and perhaps the most inexpensive architecture among the top performing networks." What specific architectural features make it "inexpensive" (e.g., fewer parameters, lower FLOPs), and how do these compare to other top-performing architectures?

4. The paper mentions that the controller is trained with a schedule of increasing number of layers. How did the authors determine the schedule parameters (e.g., increasing depth by 2 every 1,600 samples), and what would happen if they used a different schedule?

5. In the Penn Treebank experiments, the authors report that their best model achieves 1.214 perplexity on character language modeling. Could you provide a comparison of the number of parameters and computational cost for this model versus the LSTM baseline?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning. The Neural Architecture Search approach is well-conceived, well-executed, and demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods. The results are scientifically rigorous and the methodology is sound. I believe this paper will be of substantial interest to the ICLR community and should be accepted.
