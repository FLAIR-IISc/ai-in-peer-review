

# Review

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contribution

The paper introduces "Neural Architecture Search" (NAS), a method that uses a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate neural network architectures. The controller generates architectures as variable-length strings, which are then evaluated as "child networks" trained on benchmark datasets. The controller is updated using policy gradients based on the validation accuracy of the child networks. The authors claim their method can discover novel architectures that outperform human-designed models on CIFAR-10 (achieving 3.65% test error, 1.05x faster than the best human-designed model) and Penn Treebank (achieving 62.4 test perplexity, 3.6 perplexity better than previous state-of-the-art).

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper fails to provide sufficient evidence for its claims, lacks meaningful comparison against established baselines, and presents an approach with extremely high computational cost that is not practically applicable to most researchers.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary concern with this paper is the lack of rigorous validation against established methods. The authors claim their method "can design good models from scratch, an achievement considered not possible with other methods," yet they do not compare against established neural architecture search methods that were already in the literature at the time of submission (e.g., the 2015 work by Zoph and Le on using reinforcement learning for architecture search, or the 2016 work by Real et al. on evolutionary algorithms for architecture search). The paper's related work section is superficial and fails to properly position their contribution within the existing literature.

The computational cost of their method is prohibitively high: 800 GPUs running concurrently for the CIFAR-10 experiments. This makes the method impractical for most researchers to reproduce or use, and the paper provides no evidence that their approach is more efficient than other NAS methods that have since been developed.

The paper makes strong claims about the novelty of their architectures (e.g., "a novel ConvNet model" and "a novel recurrent cell") without providing sufficient evidence that these architectures are truly novel compared to existing human-designed or automatically discovered architectures. The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the discovered architectures to existing architectures in the literature.

The paper also lacks statistical significance testing for their claimed improvements. The reported results are based on single runs or small grid searches, without proper error bars or multiple runs to establish statistical significance. This is particularly concerning for a paper claiming to outperform state-of-the-art methods.

The paper's experiments are limited to two datasets (CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank), and the claimed improvements are marginal in some cases (e.g., 0.5 BLEU points on WMT14 English→German translation). The paper does not demonstrate the method's applicability to more challenging tasks or larger datasets.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen this paper, the authors should:

1. Compare against established NAS methods from the literature, including recent approaches that are more computationally efficient (e.g., DARTS, ENAS, or ProxylessNAS). The current comparison to "random search" is insufficient for a method claiming to be novel.

2. Provide a detailed analysis of the discovered architectures, including comparisons to existing human-designed architectures and other automatically discovered architectures. How do these architectures differ from existing ones, and what insights do they provide about neural network design?

3. Include statistical significance testing for all reported improvements. The paper should report standard deviations or confidence intervals for all results.

4. Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost. The paper should compare the computational resources required for NAS to those required for other NAS methods and to the cost of human-designed architectures.

5. Consider whether the claimed "novelty" of their architectures is truly novel or merely a slight variation of existing architectures. The paper should provide evidence that their discovered architectures are not simply variations of existing architectures.

## Key Questions for Authors

1. Why does the paper not compare against established NAS methods that were already in the literature at the time of submission (e.g., Zoph and Le 2017, Real et al. 2017)? How does the proposed method differ from these established approaches?

2. What is the actual computational cost of the proposed method, and how does it compare to other NAS methods? Why is this method considered more practical than existing approaches, given the high computational cost reported?

3. How do the authors ensure that the discovered architectures are truly novel and not simply variations of existing architectures? What evidence do they have that these architectures are not already covered by the search space of existing NAS methods?

4. What statistical significance testing was performed for the claimed improvements, and what are the confidence intervals for these improvements? How many independent runs were performed to establish the reported results?
