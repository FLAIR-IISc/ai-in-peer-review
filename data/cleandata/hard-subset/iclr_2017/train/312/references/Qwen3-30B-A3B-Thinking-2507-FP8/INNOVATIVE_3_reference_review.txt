

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel approach for automatically designing neural network architectures using a controller recurrent neural network (RNN) that generates architectural descriptions as variable-length strings. The controller is trained via policy gradient reinforcement learning to maximize the validation accuracy of the generated architectures. The authors demonstrate that NAS can discover architectures that outperform human-designed models on CIFAR-10 (achieving 3.65% test error, 1.05x faster than the best human-designed architecture) and Penn Treebank (achieving 62.4 perplexity, 3.6 better than the previous state-of-the-art). The method is flexible, capable of designing both convolutional and recurrent architectures, and the authors show the discovered architectures generalize to other tasks including character language modeling and machine translation.

## 2. Decision
Accept.

Key reasons: The paper introduces a bold, novel approach to automating neural architecture design, a problem that has traditionally required expert knowledge and significant manual effort. The core idea of using a controller RNN with policy gradient optimization to search for architectures is conceptually simple yet powerful, and the empirical results demonstrate clear superiority over human-designed architectures on standard benchmarks. This represents a significant new direction in neural network research.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution is the conceptual innovation of applying reinforcement learning to search for network architectures, which is a substantial departure from existing hyperparameter optimization and neuro-evolution methods. The authors provide strong empirical validation across two major benchmarks (CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank), showing not just incremental improvements but clear superiority over human-designed architectures. The results are scientifically rigorous with thorough ablation studies, comparisons to random search (which the method significantly outperforms), and transfer learning experiments demonstrating generalization to different tasks.

The novelty of the approach is substantial - it's not merely another method for optimizing hyperparameters, but a fundamentally new way to search the space of network architectures. The paper effectively positions itself against the literature, explaining how it overcomes limitations of existing methods (fixed-length space for hyperparameter optimization, slow scale for neuro-evolution). The fact that the method discovers architectures that are both better and faster than human-designed ones is particularly impressive and demonstrates the potential impact of the approach.

While the method is computationally expensive (requiring training many architectures in parallel), this is a reasonable trade-off for the significant novelty and performance gains. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper's contribution sufficiently groundbreaking to warrant acceptance.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper:

- The paper could provide more detail about the specific architectural choices the controller makes (e.g., the distribution of filter sizes, layer types, etc.) to better understand what the method has learned about good architectures.
- A comparison of the computational cost (in terms of GPU hours) to other NAS approaches (like those based on evolutionary algorithms) would be valuable for readers to assess practical applicability.
- The paper mentions "a schedule for increasing the number of layers" but doesn't explain how this schedule was determined or optimized. A brief explanation would help readers understand the practical implementation details.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that the discovered CIFAR-10 architecture is "1.05x faster" than the DenseNet model. Could you clarify what this means in terms of actual inference time or FLOPs, rather than just speedup relative to a specific implementation?
2. The paper mentions that the controller RNN was trained with specific hyperparameters (ADAM with learning rate 0.0006), but doesn't discuss how sensitive the results are to these choices. How robust are the results to changes in the controller's hyperparameters?
3. In the Penn Treebank experiments, the paper states that the new cell is "more than two times faster" than previous models. Could you elaborate on what this speedup refers to (e.g., inference time, training time, or something else)?
