

# Peer Review: "Neural Architecture Search"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a gradient-based method for automatically discovering neural network architectures. The authors propose using a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate architectural hyperparameters as a sequence of tokens, with the controller trained via policy gradients using the validation accuracy of the generated architectures as the reward signal. The key contributions include:

1. A novel approach to represent neural network architectures as variable-length strings, enabling flexible search across architectural spaces
2. A reinforcement learning framework for training the controller to generate better architectures over time
3. Extensions to handle skip connections and branching layers in convolutional architectures, as well as recurrent cell architectures
4. Empirical demonstrations showing that NAS can discover architectures that outperform human-designed models on CIFAR-10 (3.65% test error, 1.05x faster than DenseNet) and Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity, 3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art)

The paper is well-written, with clear technical descriptions of the method, thorough experimental results on standard benchmarks, and compelling evidence of the method's effectiveness.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to neural architecture search that addresses a significant pain point in deep learning research. The empirical results are strong and demonstrate clear improvements over previous methods on standard benchmarks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

### Strengths

1. **Novel Approach to Architecture Search**: The core idea of representing architectures as variable-length strings and using a controller RNN to generate them is elegant and well-motivated. This approach successfully addresses a key limitation of previous methods (fixed-length search spaces) and provides a more flexible framework for architectural exploration.

2. **Strong Empirical Results**: The results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are impressive. The claimed 3.65% test error on CIFAR-10 represents a clear improvement over previous human-designed architectures, and the 62.4 perplexity on Penn Treebank is a 3.6 improvement over the previous state-of-the-art. The transfer learning results (character language modeling and machine translation) further validate the generality of the discovered architectures.

3. **Technical Soundness**: The method is technically sound, with clear explanations of the policy gradient training, the controller architecture, and the extensions for skip connections and recurrent cells. The distributed training approach with parameter servers is well-described and addresses a key practical challenge.

4. **Comprehensive Experiments**: The paper includes multiple experiments (with and without stride prediction, with and without pooling layers), control experiments (adding more functions to the search space), and comparisons to random search, all of which strengthen the claims.

### Concerns

1. **Novelty vs. Prior Work**: While the paper cites relevant literature, it doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from the closely related "Neural Architecture Search" paper by Zoph and Le (2017), which was published in the same year. The two approaches are very similar (both use a controller RNN trained with policy gradients to search for architectures), and this needs to be clarified.

2. **Computational Cost**: The paper states they used 800 GPUs concurrently for CIFAR-10 and 400 CPUs for Penn Treebank, but doesn't provide a clear analysis of the computational cost relative to other methods or to the cost of human-designed architectures. This is a significant practical consideration for the field.

3. **"From Scratch" Claim**: The paper claims their method can "design good models from scratch," but the controller is trained on previous architectures (implicitly using human-designed architectures as the training data), so it's not truly "from scratch." This claim needs to be clarified.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

1. **Clarify Novelty**: The authors should provide a detailed comparison with Zoph and Le (2017), explicitly stating how their approach differs (e.g., in the search space design, training procedure, or controller architecture). If the approaches are very similar, they should acknowledge this and focus on the specific contributions of their method.

2. **Provide Computational Cost Analysis**: Include a detailed breakdown of the computational resources required (GPU hours, etc.) for the NAS process, and compare this to the cost of human-designed architectures or other NAS methods. This would help assess the practicality of the approach.

3. **Refine the "From Scratch" Claim**: The paper should clarify what "from scratch" means in this context. The controller is trained on previous architectures, so the search isn't truly from scratch. A more accurate phrasing would be "designing architectures without manual intervention" or "automatically discovering architectures."

4. **Expand on Related Work**: Add a comparison to more recent NAS methods, such as DARTS (Differentiable Architecture Search), which was published around the same time and has become influential in the field.

5. **Clarify the "1.05x Faster" Claim**: The paper states that their model is "1.05x faster than the current best model," but this phrasing is ambiguous. Is it 5% faster (1.05x as fast) or 5% slower (0.95x as fast)? This needs to be clarified.

6. **Add More Ablation Studies**: Include more ablation studies to isolate the contributions of different components (e.g., the skip connection mechanism, the controller architecture, etc.) to the final performance.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. How does your approach differ from the "Neural Architecture Search" paper by Zoph and Le (2017), which was also published in 2017? If the methods are similar, what are the key differences in your approach that justify the novelty claim?

2. Can you provide a detailed breakdown of the computational cost (in GPU hours) of your NAS process for CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, and how this compares to the cost of human-designed architectures or other NAS methods?

3. The paper claims that the controller "learns to improve its search over time," but doesn't provide evidence that the controller's performance improves during training. Can you provide a plot showing the validation accuracy of architectures generated by the controller as training progresses?

4. The paper states that "our CIFAR-10 model achieves a 3.65 test set error, while being 1.05x faster than the current best model." Can you clarify whether "1.05x faster" means 5% faster (i.e., 1.05 times as fast) or 5% slower (i.e., 0.95 times as fast)?

5. The paper mentions that their DenseNet-like model uses "40 more filters" than the base architecture. Can you provide the exact number of filters used in the final architecture and how this compares to the DenseNet model that achieves 3.74% error rate?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural architecture search, and with the suggested clarifications and additional details, it would be an excellent contribution to ICLR. The reviewers should be confident in the empirical results, but the novelty claim needs to be strengthened through a more thorough comparison with prior work.
