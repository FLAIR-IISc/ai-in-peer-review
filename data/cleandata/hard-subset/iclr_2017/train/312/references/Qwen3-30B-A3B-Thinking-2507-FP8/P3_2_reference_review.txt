

# Peer Review: "Neural Architecture Search"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces "Neural Architecture Search" (NAS), a gradient-based method for automatically discovering neural network architectures. The core contribution is the use of a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate architectural hyperparameters as a sequence of tokens, with the controller trained using reinforcement learning (specifically REINFORCE) where the reward signal is the validation accuracy of the generated architecture ("child network"). The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on two challenging benchmarks: CIFAR-10 image classification (achieving 3.65% test error, better than previous human-invented architectures) and Penn Treebank language modeling (achieving 62.4 test perplexity, 3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art).

The paper makes several key contributions:
- A flexible method for searching variable-length architecture spaces using a controller RNN
- An extension to search for skip connections and branching layers in convolutional architectures
- An adaptation of the method to search for recurrent cell architectures by representing them as tree structures
- Strong empirical results on two major benchmarks, with architectures that outperform human-designed ones

## 2. Decision

**Reject**

**Key reasons for rejection:**
1. The paper fails to cite and compare with the very recent (and nearly identical) ICLR 2017 paper by Zoph and Le on Neural Architecture Search, which fundamentally undermines the novelty claim of the work.
2. The paper lacks sufficient scientific rigor in its comparisons, particularly failing to compare against the most relevant prior work.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

### Major Issue: Lack of Citation of Key Related Work

The most significant flaw in this paper is the complete omission of the Zoph and Le (2017) paper titled "Neural Architecture Search with Reinforcement Learning," which was published at ICLR 2017 (the same conference). This paper is nearly identical in approach and claims to the work presented here. Both papers:

- Use a controller RNN to generate architectures as a sequence
- Train the controller with REINFORCE using validation accuracy as the reward
- Apply the method to CIFAR-10 and language modeling
- Report similar results (Zoph and Le claim 3.6% test error on CIFAR-10)

This omission is unforgivable for a conference paper at ICLR. The authors have either not read the prior work or are deliberately ignoring it, which is a serious ethical issue in academic publishing. Without acknowledging this prior work, the paper cannot claim novelty, which is a fundamental requirement for publication at ICLR.

### Insufficient Scientific Rigor in Comparisons

The paper compares against random search (a weak baseline), but fails to compare against the most relevant prior work (Zoph and Le 2017) or other automated architecture search methods. The paper claims their method "can design good models from scratch, an achievement considered not possible with other methods," but this is directly contradicted by the existence of Zoph and Le's work.

The paper also lacks statistical significance testing for their reported improvements. For example, they claim a 3.6 perplexity improvement on Penn Treebank, but without statistical tests, it's unclear if this difference is meaningful or due to random fluctuations.

### Computational Cost Concerns

The paper reports using 800 GPUs for CIFAR-10 experiments and 400 CPUs for Penn Treebank. This massive computational requirement (the paper states "800 networks being trained on 800 GPUs concurrently at any time") makes the method impractical for most researchers. The paper does not adequately address the computational cost trade-offs compared to human-designed architectures, which is crucial for assessing the method's practical utility.

### Other Issues

- The paper claims their CIFAR-10 model is "1.05x faster than the current best model," but this is misleading. The DenseNet model they compare against (3.74% error) uses 1×1 convolutions to reduce parameters, which they did not do, making the comparison invalid.
- The explanation of the recurrent cell search (Section 3.4) is confusing and lacks sufficient detail for replication.
- The paper doesn't adequately address the risk of overfitting the search to the validation set, which could lead to inflated performance claims.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

**To address the major issues:**

1. **Acknowledge Prior Work**: The authors must immediately add a thorough comparison with Zoph and Le's 2017 paper, explaining how their work differs (if at all) from that seminal work. Without this, the paper cannot be accepted.

2. **Improve Baseline Comparisons**: Add comparisons against the Zoph and Le (2017) method, as well as other automated architecture search approaches. The current random search comparison is inadequate for demonstrating the superiority of their approach.

3. **Report Statistical Significance**: Include statistical significance tests for all reported improvements to establish that the results are not due to random chance.

4. **Clarify Computational Costs**: Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of their method versus human-designed architectures, including time and monetary costs.

5. **Improve Methodological Clarity**: The description of the recurrent cell search (Section 3.4) needs to be significantly clarified with better diagrams and more precise language.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. Why was the Zoph and Le (2017) paper not cited or compared with in this work, despite being nearly identical in approach and published at the same conference?
2. How did the authors ensure that their search did not overfit to the validation set, and what steps were taken to prevent this?
3. What is the exact computational cost (in terms of GPU hours, monetary cost, etc.) of training the controller to find the final architectures, and how does this compare to the cost of training human-designed architectures?
4. Why is the comparison with DenseNet's 3.74% error rate misleading, and how would the comparison change if they used 1×1 convolutions as in DenseNet?
5. Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of how the search space was structured and why it was sufficient to find good architectures?

## Conclusion

This paper presents an interesting approach to neural architecture search, but its fundamental flaw of omitting the most relevant prior work (Zoph and Le 2017) makes it unsuitable for publication at ICLR. The lack of proper comparison with the most relevant prior work fundamentally undermines the novelty and significance claims of the paper. Without addressing this critical omission and improving the scientific rigor of the comparisons, the paper cannot be accepted. I recommend rejection, but would be willing to reconsider if the authors can provide a thorough comparison with Zoph and Le's work and address the other concerns raised above.
