

# Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a gradient-based approach. The authors propose representing neural network architectures as variable-length strings and using a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate these strings. The controller is trained via policy gradient methods (REINFORCE) to maximize the validation accuracy of the architectures it generates (the "child network"). The key contribution is demonstrating that this approach can discover novel architectures that outperform human-designed models on CIFAR-10 (3.65% test error, 1.05x faster than previous best) and Penn Treebank language modeling (62.4 perplexity, 3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art). The paper also extends their method to generate recurrent cell architectures and demonstrates transfer learning to character language modeling and machine translation tasks.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of deep learning by addressing a fundamental challenge: the time-consuming and expertise-dependent process of neural architecture design. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and supported by strong empirical results that clearly demonstrate its superiority over previous approaches.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in several key aspects:

**Novelty and Problem Significance**: The problem of automating neural architecture design is highly relevant, as manual architecture design has become increasingly time-consuming and requires significant expertise. The authors' insight that architectures can be represented as variable-length strings and searched using a recurrent controller is elegant and addresses a critical limitation of previous approaches (which typically operate on fixed-length search spaces).

**Literature Context**: The related work section thoroughly positions the paper within the literature, clearly differentiating it from hyperparameter optimization (fixed-length search), neuro-evolution (slow search), and program synthesis (different application domain). The connections to sequence-to-sequence learning and reinforcement learning for non-differentiable objectives are well-articulated.

**Empirical Rigor**: The results are scientifically rigorous and well-supported:
- On CIFAR-10, the NAS-generated architecture achieves 3.65% error (beating the previous human-designed model at 3.74%) while being 1.05x faster
- On Penn Treebank, the NAS cell achieves 62.4 perplexity (3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art)
- Comprehensive ablation studies (e.g., with and without stride prediction, with/without pooling)
- Control experiments demonstrating robustness (adding max/sin functions) and comparison to random search
- Transfer learning to character language modeling and machine translation tasks

**Technical Soundness**: The method is technically sound, with clear explanations of the controller RNN, the REINFORCE training procedure, and extensions for skip connections and recurrent cell generation. The distributed training approach is well-justified for scalability.

The paper's contributions are substantial and well-supported, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at ICLR.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend the following improvements:

1. **Code Availability**: The paper mentions NASCell has been added to TensorFlow, but a clear link to the implementation would be helpful for reproducibility.

2. **Controller Architecture Details**: The controller RNN architecture (two-layer LSTM with 35 hidden units) appears to be a key hyperparameter. It would be helpful to know if this was tuned or fixed, and how sensitive the results are to controller architecture choices.

3. **Search Space Complexity**: The paper states the search space has "approximately 6 × 10^16 architectures" for the recurrent cell, but doesn't provide a clear explanation of how this number was calculated. A brief explanation would help readers understand the search space size.

4. **Computational Efficiency**: While the paper mentions the NAS-generated model is "1.05x as fast," it would be helpful to clarify whether this refers to inference speed, training speed, or both, and under what specific hardware conditions this speedup was measured.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper mentions a "schedule of increasing number of layers" for child networks during training. Could you provide more specific details about this schedule (starting point, increment size, etc.)?

2. On the Penn Treebank task, you state your model is "more than two times faster" because the previous best network "requires running a cell 10 times per time step." Could you clarify what exactly this "cell" is and how the NAS cell avoids this computational overhead?

3. The paper states that the NAS cell achieves 62.4 perplexity on Penn Treebank, but the table shows 62.4 as the best result. Was this the final result after the grid search, or was there a further validation step?

4. The paper mentions "we use a parameter-server scheme with S=20 shards, K=100 replicas, and m=8 child replicas" for CIFAR-10. How did you determine these specific values, and how sensitive are the results to these choices?

5. The paper states that "the model with 40 filters added is 1.05x as fast as the DenseNet model that achieves 3.74%." Could you provide more details about the computational comparison (e.g., number of parameters, FLOPs, inference time) to better understand this speedup?

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of deep learning. The method is novel, well-motivated, and supported by compelling empirical results. The authors have demonstrated that neural architecture search can produce models that not only outperform human-designed architectures but also do so more efficiently. I strongly recommend acceptance.
