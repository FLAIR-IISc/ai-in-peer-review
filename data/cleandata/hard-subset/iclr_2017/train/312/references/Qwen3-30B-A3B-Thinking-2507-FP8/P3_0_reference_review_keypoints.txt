- Paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS): a controller RNN generates variable-length architecture strings and is trained with policy gradients using validation accuracy as reward.
- Key contributions: variable-length architecture representation, reinforcement-learning controller, extensions for skip/branching and recurrent cells, and empirical improvements on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank.
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.
- Strength — novel flexible search: controller RNN over variable-length strings addresses fixed-length search limitations.
- Strength — strong empirical results: CIFAR-10 3.65% error, PTB 62.4 perplexity, and transfer learning validations.
- Strength — technical soundness: clear policy-gradient training, controller design, and distributed training implementation.
- Strength — comprehensive experiments: multiple ablations, control experiments, and random-search comparisons.
- Concern — novelty unclear: insufficient distinction from Zoph & Le (2017), which uses a similar controller-RNN + policy-gradient approach.
- Concern — computational cost: massive resources (800 GPUs for CIFAR-10, 400 CPUs for PTB) but no clear cost analysis or comparisons.
- Concern — “from scratch” claim misleading: controller is trained on previous architectures, so not truly from scratch.
- Feedback — clarify novelty: explicitly compare and contrast with Zoph & Le (2017) and highlight unique contributions.
- Feedback — provide computational cost analysis: GPU-hour breakdown and comparisons to human-designed models and other NAS methods.
- Feedback — refine “from scratch” wording: use phrasing like “without manual intervention” or “automatically discovering architectures.”
- Feedback — expand related work to include recent NAS methods (e.g., DARTS) and their differences.
- Feedback — clarify “1.05x faster” ambiguity: state whether this means 5% faster or slower.
- Feedback — add more ablation studies to isolate contributions of components like skip connections and controller design.
- Questions — how does this differ from Zoph & Le (2017) and what justifies the novelty claim?
- Questions — provide detailed GPU-hour breakdown for CIFAR-10 and PTB and comparisons to other methods.
- Questions — show evidence that the controller improves over training (e.g., plot validation accuracy of generated architectures over time).
- Questions — clarify the meaning of “1.05x faster.”
- Questions — report exact filter counts in the final DenseNet-like architecture and compare to the DenseNet baseline.
