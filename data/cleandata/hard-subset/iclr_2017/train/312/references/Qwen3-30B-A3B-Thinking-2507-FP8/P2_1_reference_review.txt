

# Review for "Neural Architecture Search"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel reinforcement learning-based approach for automatically designing neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate architectural hyperparameters as a sequence of tokens, then train the generated architecture (the "child network") and use its validation accuracy as a reward signal to update the controller via policy gradients. The authors demonstrate that NAS can discover architectures that outperform human-designed ones on two challenging benchmarks: CIFAR-10 image classification (achieving 3.65% test error, 0.09% better than the previous state-of-the-art) and Penn Treebank language modeling (achieving 62.4 perplexity, 3.6 perplexity better than the previous state-of-the-art). The paper also shows that the discovered architectures generalize well to other tasks and can be used in existing frameworks like GNMT without additional tuning. The authors make the code and the discovered RNN cell (named NASCell) publicly available.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural architecture search with a novel, well-motivated approach that demonstrates strong empirical results on standard benchmarks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is strong on all three key criteria:

1. **Problem statement**: The paper clearly identifies the challenge of manually designing neural network architectures and positions NAS as a solution to automate this process. It correctly identifies the limitations of existing approaches (fixed-length search spaces in hyperparameter optimization, slow search-based methods in neuro-evolution, etc.) and proposes a more flexible and scalable solution.

2. **Literature placement**: The paper thoroughly situates itself within the relevant literature, correctly citing and discussing related work on hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, program synthesis, and meta-learning. The connections to sequence-to-sequence learning and BLEU optimization are particularly well-motivated.

3. **Scientific rigor**: The paper provides strong empirical evidence to support its claims. The results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are impressive and clearly demonstrate that NAS can discover architectures that outperform human-designed ones. The authors include thorough ablation studies, control experiments (adding more functions to the search space, comparison against random search), and transfer learning results to validate their approach. The use of a held-out validation set to compute the reward signal and the careful reporting of test set performance (computed only once for the best validation model) demonstrate scientific rigor.

The paper's main contribution is a scalable, flexible approach to neural architecture search that avoids the limitations of previous methods. The results are convincing, and the methodology is sound.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, I recommend the following improvements to strengthen it further:

- **Search space details**: The paper should provide more detailed justification for the search space design. How did the authors determine the specific hyperparameters to include (filter sizes, number of filters, etc.)? Why were certain values chosen (e.g., filter sizes in [1,3,5,7])? A table summarizing the search space dimensions would be helpful.

- **Computational cost analysis**: The paper mentions using 800 GPUs for CIFAR-10 and 400 CPUs for Penn Treebank, but doesn't discuss the total computational cost (e.g., GPU hours) or how this compares to training human-designed architectures. A brief discussion of the practical limitations due to high computational requirements would be valuable for readers.

- **Comparison with random search**: The comparison with random search in Figure 6 is promising, but the paper should clarify how many random samples were evaluated. How does NAS compare to random search after the same number of evaluations (e.g., 12,800 samples)? This would strengthen the argument that the approach is not just lucky.

- **Computational complexity**: The paper claims the NAS architecture is "1.05x as fast as the DenseNet model," but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of computational complexity (FLOPs, parameters, inference time) with other state-of-the-art models. A table comparing these metrics would help readers better understand the trade-offs.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. How did you determine the specific search space for each task (CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank)? What were the key considerations in defining this space, and how did you ensure it was expressive enough to find good architectures?

2. The paper mentions that the NAS architecture for CIFAR-10 is "1.05x as fast as the DenseNet model." Can you provide a more detailed comparison of computational complexity (FLOPs, parameters, inference time) with other state-of-the-art models, including the DenseNet model that achieves 3.74% error?

3. In the comparison with random search (Figure 6), how many random samples were evaluated? How does the performance of NAS compare to random search after the same number of evaluations (e.g., 12,800 samples)?

4. The paper states that "the model can achieve somewhat comparable performance" when adding max to the combination functions and sin to the activation functions. Can you provide more details about how the performance changes with these additions, and whether this suggests the NAS approach is robust to changes in the search space?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neural architecture search. The results are impressive, the methodology is sound, and the paper is well-written and well-motivated. With the minor revisions suggested above, it would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
