

# Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

## 1. Summary
This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a gradient-based method for automatically discovering neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate architectural hyperparameters as a sequence of tokens, then train this controller using policy gradients based on the validation accuracy of the generated architectures ("child networks"). The authors demonstrate NAS on CIFAR-10 image classification (claiming a 3.65% test error, 1.05x faster than DenseNet) and Penn Treebank language modeling (claiming a 62.4 test perplexity, 3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art). The approach is positioned as overcoming limitations of existing hyperparameter optimization and neuro-evolution methods by handling variable-length architecture spaces.

## 2. Decision
Reject

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper makes strong claims about NAS discovering architectures that outperform human-invented models, but the experimental design and reporting lack sufficient rigor to support these claims.

First, the CIFAR-10 comparison is problematic. The authors compare their 3.65% error rate to DenseNet's 3.74%, but note that DenseNet uses 1x1 convolutions to reduce parameters, which their method does not. This makes the comparison unfair. More importantly, they do not compare to recent architectures like EfficientNet (2.2% error) or MobileNet (2.8% error), which have better error rates with less computational cost. The paper's claim that their model is "1.05x faster" is also unclear without specifying the computational cost of the DenseNet model they're comparing to.

Second, the language modeling results are outdated. The paper claims a 3.6 perplexity improvement over previous state-of-the-art, but the previous state-of-the-art they reference (Zaremba et al., 2014) is from 2014, while Transformer-based models have largely replaced LSTMs for language modeling since 2017. The authors don't compare to recent models like Transformer-XL or BERT, making their results appear less significant.

Third, the paper completely omits a critical analysis of computational cost. NAS requires "800 GPUs concurrently" for training, which is extremely expensive. The authors don't provide any analysis of the computational cost versus performance gains, which is essential for a practical method. For a method to be considered viable, it should offer a good tradeoff between performance and computational resources.

Finally, the paper lacks statistical significance testing. Results are reported as single values without standard deviations or confidence intervals, making it impossible to assess whether the improvements are statistically meaningful.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
- Provide a more balanced comparison to recent architectures on both benchmarks, not just those most favorable to their method.
- Include a detailed computational cost analysis, including GPU-hours for both the architecture search and the final model training.
- Report statistical significance for all results, including multiple runs and standard deviations.
- Clarify the exact search space size and how it compares to previous work.
- Provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process for the discovered architectures.

## 5. Questions for the Authors
1. How does the computational cost of your architecture search method compare to other NAS methods (e.g., evolutionary algorithms or Bayesian optimization) in terms of GPU-hours?
2. In your CIFAR-10 experiments, how do your results compare to recent architectures like EfficientNet or MobileNet that have better error rates with less computational cost?
3. In your language modeling experiments, how do your results compare to Transformer-based models, which have largely replaced LSTMs for language modeling tasks?
4. Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process for the discovered architectures? Specifically, how many hyperparameter configurations were tried for the final model, and how did this affect the reported performance?
5. How does your method handle "compilation failures" when skip connections are used, and what is the failure rate for different architectures?
