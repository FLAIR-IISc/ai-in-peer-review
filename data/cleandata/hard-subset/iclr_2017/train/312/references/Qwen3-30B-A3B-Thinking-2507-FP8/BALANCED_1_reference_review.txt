

# Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a gradient-based method for automatically discovering neural network architectures. The authors propose a controller RNN that generates architectural hyperparameters as a sequence of tokens, with the child network's validation accuracy serving as the reward signal for policy gradient updates. The method is applied to both convolutional architectures (on CIFAR-10) and recurrent cells (on Penn Treebank), with reported results showing state-of-the-art performance in both domains. The paper makes a compelling case for the flexibility of the approach, as it can search variable-length architectures that are difficult to handle with traditional hyperparameter optimization methods.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper presents a novel approach with impressive empirical results, but the evidence supporting the claims is insufficient due to lack of critical details about computational resources, reproducibility, and ablation studies.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main strengths lie in its clear problem formulation, well-motivated approach, and impressive empirical results. The NAS method successfully discovers architectures that outperform human-designed models on both CIFAR-10 (3.65% error rate, 1.05x faster than DenseNet) and Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity, 3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art). The approach is well-placed in the literature, with thoughtful comparisons to related work in hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis.

However, the paper has several critical weaknesses that undermine confidence in the results. Most notably, the authors fail to provide essential details about computational resources and time required for the experiments. The paper states that "each gradient update to the controller parameters corresponds to training one child network to convergence" and that "training a child network can take hours," but provides no specific numbers about the total computational cost or time required. This information is crucial for assessing the practicality of the approach.

Additionally, the paper lacks sufficient ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components. For example, it's unclear how much the skip connections (Section 3.3) or the specific controller architecture contribute to the final performance. The paper also doesn't address potential biases in the search space, which could explain why the discovered architectures are similar to those humans would design.

The paper claims to "design good models from scratch," but doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the discovered architectures are truly novel or that they represent a significant improvement over the search space. The comparison with human-invented architectures is somewhat vague, as the authors don't clearly explain why their discovered architectures are better or how they differ from existing ones.

Finally, the paper lacks information about reproducibility. How many times was the experiment run? What were the standard deviations in the reported results? Without this information, it's difficult to assess the statistical significance of the improvements claimed.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen the paper, I recommend the following improvements:

1. Provide detailed computational resources: Include the total number of GPU hours required, the time taken to train the controller, and the total number of child networks trained.

2. Add ablation studies: Quantify the contribution of different components (skip connections, controller architecture, search space design) to the final performance.

3. Clarify the novelty: Better explain how the discovered architectures differ from human-designed ones and why they achieve better performance.

4. Address potential search space biases: Discuss how the search space was designed and whether it might have biased the results toward architectures similar to human designs.

5. Include more statistical analysis: Report standard deviations across multiple runs and provide more information about the reproducibility of the results.

6. Provide more details about the controller architecture: Explain why the specific controller architecture was chosen and how it compares to alternatives.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. Could you provide the total computational cost (in GPU hours) required to find the architectures presented in Section 4.1 and 4.2? How does this compare to the cost of training human-designed architectures?

2. How many times was the experiment run to obtain the reported results? What were the standard deviations in the test accuracy/perplexity?

3. What specific architectural features make the discovered CIFAR-10 architecture better than DenseNet (3.74% error rate), beyond just the final performance metric? How do these features contribute to the improved performance?

4. The paper claims that "the controller will learn to improve its search over time." Can you provide evidence that the controller's performance actually improves during training, and not just that the final architecture is good?

5. How do you address the risk of overfitting to the validation set, which is a common issue in architecture search?
