

# Peer Review: "Neural Architecture Search"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a novel approach for automatically discovering high-performing neural network architectures. The core insight is to represent neural network architectures as variable-length strings that can be generated by a recurrent neural network (the "controller"). The controller is trained using policy gradient methods to maximize the validation accuracy of the child networks it generates. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by discovering convolutional architectures for CIFAR-10 that achieve 3.65% test error (outperforming previous human-designed architectures) and recurrent cells for Penn Treebank that achieve 62.4 test perplexity (3.6 perplexity better than the previous state-of-the-art). The paper makes a significant contribution by showing that an automated search can discover novel, high-performing architectures that outperform human-designed ones.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically effective approach to neural architecture search. The results are compelling, the methodology is novel, and the implementation details are thorough. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of automated machine learning.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution is well-positioned in the literature. The authors provide a thoughtful comparison with related work in hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis. They correctly identify the key limitation of previous methods: their inability to search variable-length architecture spaces. The controller-based approach elegantly addresses this limitation.

The experimental results are strong and well-designed. The CIFAR-10 results demonstrate that the method can discover architectures that outperform human-designed ones while being more efficient (1.05x faster than the DenseNet model). The Penn Treebank results show consistent improvements over previous state-of-the-art models. The ablation studies (e.g., with skip connections) provide good insight into the method's capabilities.

However, there are important concerns that need addressing before final acceptance:

1. The paper doesn't adequately distinguish itself from the concurrent work by Zoph & Le (2016), which also used a controller RNN for architecture search. The authors should explicitly compare their approach with this seminal work and clarify their methodological differences.

2. The computational cost of their method is not thoroughly discussed. The paper mentions "800 networks being trained on 800 GPUs concurrently" for CIFAR-10, but doesn't provide the total GPU-hours required to discover the final architecture. This is crucial for assessing practicality.

3. The comparison against "random search" (Figure 6) is not strong enough. Random search is known to be a weak baseline, and the paper should compare against more sophisticated baselines like Bayesian optimization or Hyperband.

4. The paper lacks an ablation study to quantify the contribution of specific components (e.g., skip connections, controller architecture).

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Clarify the relationship with Zoph & Le (2016)**: The paper claims to "design good models from scratch, an achievement considered not possible with other methods," but this appears to contradict the Zoph & Le work. The authors should explicitly acknowledge this prior work and clarify how their method differs and why it's more successful.

2. **Provide computational cost metrics**: The paper should include the total computational resources required to discover the architectures (e.g., "12,800 architectures required X GPU-days to discover"). This is essential for understanding the practical viability of the method.

3. **Strengthen the baseline comparison**: Replace the random search baseline with a more meaningful comparison against Bayesian optimization or Hyperband, which are standard baselines for hyperparameter optimization tasks.

4. **Add an ablation study**: Include a table showing how removing specific components (skip connections, controller architecture, etc.) affects performance. This would help readers understand which aspects of the method are most critical.

5. **Clarify the speed comparison**: The claim that "this model with 40 filters added is 1.05x as fast as the DenseNet model" is ambiguous. Does 1.05x mean 5% faster or 5% slower? Please clarify.

6. **Address the DenseNet comparison**: The paper states that "the DenseNet model that achieves 3.46% error rate (Huang et al., 2016b) uses 1x1 convolutions to reduce its total number of parameters, which we did not do, so it is not an exact comparison." However, the DenseNet model with 3.74% error rate (the one they're comparing against) also uses 1x1 convolutions. The comparison should be clarified.

## Questions for Authors

1. How does your approach differ from Zoph & Le (2016), and why does your method achieve better results? The paper should explicitly address this key difference.

2. The paper states "the model that achieves 64 perplexity is also more than two times faster because the previous best network requires running a cell 10 times per time step." What is the actual speedup in terms of FLOPs or inference time, and how does this compare to the LSTM cell?

3. The paper claims their CIFAR-10 model is "1.05x faster than the current best model." Is this 1.05x faster (5% faster) or 1.05x slower (5% slower)? Please clarify.

4. The paper mentions that "the search space is larger" when allowing the controller to predict strides. How much larger is the search space, and how does this affect the search efficiency (e.g., number of architectures evaluated)?

This paper presents a significant contribution to automated machine learning. With the suggested revisions addressing the key concerns about related work, computational cost, and baseline comparisons, it would be a strong candidate for acceptance at ICLR.
