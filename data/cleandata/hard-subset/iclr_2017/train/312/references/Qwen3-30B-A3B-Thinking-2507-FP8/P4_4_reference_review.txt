

# Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims
The paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a gradient-based method for automatically discovering neural network architectures. The authors propose using a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate architectural hyperparameters as a sequence of tokens, which can specify variable-length network structures. The controller is trained using policy gradient methods, where the reward signal is the validation accuracy of the generated architectures. The authors demonstrate that NAS can design novel architectures that outperform human-invented models on CIFAR-10 (achieving 3.65% test error, 1.05x faster than the best human-designed model) and on Penn Treebank language modeling (achieving 62.4 perplexity, 3.6 better than the previous state-of-the-art). The paper also introduces techniques for incorporating skip connections and generating recurrent cells, with thorough experimental validation.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper presents a technically sound approach with strong empirical validation that addresses a significant challenge in deep learning.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper effectively addresses the three key review questions:

1. **Problem Statement**: The paper clearly identifies the problem of architecture design requiring expert knowledge and time, and proposes a gradient-based method to automate this process. The problem is well-motivated by the shift from feature engineering to architecture engineering in deep learning.

2. **Approach Motivation**: The paper is well-placed in the literature, with comprehensive related work covering hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, program synthesis, and sequence-to-sequence learning. It clearly articulates how NAS differs from and improves upon these approaches (e.g., more flexible than fixed-length Bayesian optimization, more practical than neuro-evolution methods).

3. **Claim Support**: The paper provides strong empirical evidence supporting its claims. The results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are scientifically rigorous, with appropriate baselines, ablation studies, and control experiments. The comparison against random search (Figure 6) demonstrates the superiority of the policy gradient approach. The paper also includes transfer learning experiments to validate the generalizability of the discovered architectures.

The technical approach is sound, with proper application of policy gradient methods (REINFORCE with baseline), distributed training, and careful handling of architectural constraints (e.g., skip connections). The method is clearly described and reproducible.

## 4. Additional Feedback
The paper is well-written and technically sound, but could be improved by:

- Providing more explicit comparisons to other NAS methods (e.g., the Zoph and Le 2017 paper, which appears to be the same work, but is not cited as prior work).
- Clarifying the relationship between this work and previous NAS approaches to better position the novelty.
- Including a more detailed discussion of computational costs (e.g., how many GPU hours were required to find the architectures).
- Adding more details about the search space constraints (e.g., how the controller's layer count schedule was determined).

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper claims the NAS model achieves "3.65 test set error" on CIFAR-10, but also states this model is "1.05x faster than the current best model." How was the speed comparison measured, and how does it account for different parameter counts between the NAS model and the DenseNet model (which uses 1x1 convolutions to reduce parameters)?

2. The paper describes the controller as generating "a variable-length string" for network architecture. What specific mechanism determines when the controller stops generating tokens (e.g., is there a maximum length constraint, or does it use a termination probability)?

3. The paper reports that NAS achieved 3.6 perplexity better than the previous state-of-the-art on Penn Treebank. Could the authors provide the exact perplexity values for the previous best model and their NAS model to clarify the improvement?
