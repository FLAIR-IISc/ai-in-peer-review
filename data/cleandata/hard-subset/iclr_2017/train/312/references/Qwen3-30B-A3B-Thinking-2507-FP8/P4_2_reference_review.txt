

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Paper Claims

The paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a gradient-based method for automatically discovering neural network architectures. The authors propose using a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate architectural hyperparameters as a sequence of tokens, with the controller being optimized via policy gradient reinforcement learning based on the validation accuracy of the generated architectures (the "child network"). The method is designed to handle variable-length architecture spaces, addressing limitations of previous approaches that were restricted to fixed-length search spaces. The authors demonstrate their method's effectiveness on two benchmarks: CIFAR-10 image recognition (achieving 3.65% test error, 1.05x faster than previous best human-designed models) and Penn Treebank language modeling (achieving 62.4 test perplexity, 3.6 perplexity better than previous state-of-the-art). The paper also includes control experiments showing the robustness of the method and its transferability to other tasks like character language modeling and machine translation.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to neural architecture search that is well-supported by rigorous empirical results on standard benchmarks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a strong case for its approach. First, the problem addressed—automatically designing neural network architectures—is highly relevant and challenging, with significant practical implications. The authors clearly articulate how their method differs from and improves upon existing approaches (Section 2), particularly in its ability to search variable-length architecture spaces.

The technical approach is well-justified and sound. The use of a controller RNN to generate architectures as sequences and optimizing it with policy gradient reinforcement learning is a natural fit for the problem. The method is well-placed in the literature, with appropriate comparisons to hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and related reinforcement learning techniques.

The empirical results are compelling and scientifically rigorous. The paper demonstrates clear improvements over human-designed architectures on two major benchmarks with appropriate baselines. The control experiments (Section 4.2, Control Experiment 2) provide strong evidence that the method consistently outperforms random search. The transfer learning experiments (Section 4.2, Transfer Learning Results) further validate the quality of the discovered architectures by showing they generalize to other tasks and settings.

The paper provides sufficient detail about the experimental setup, search spaces, and training procedures to be reproducible. The results are presented with appropriate statistical significance, including grid search and validation set selection procedures.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, the authors might consider:

1. Providing a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of their method compared to human-designed architectures, including total training time and resources required to discover the architectures.

2. Including a more thorough discussion of the specific architectural features that contributed to the improved performance (e.g., why rectangular filters or skip connections were preferred).

3. Clarifying the exact computational complexity comparison between the discovered architecture and the DenseNet model mentioned in Section 4.1, especially regarding the additional filters.

4. Providing more insight into the relationship between search space complexity and performance, particularly regarding the trade-off between predicting strides and achieving better accuracy.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In Section 3.3, the authors mention using "a set-selection type attention" to enable skip connections. Could you clarify how the controller decides which previous layers to connect to the current layer, and whether there are specific patterns or conditions that lead to the discovery of beneficial skip connections?

2. The paper states that the CIFAR-10 model with 40 additional filters achieves 3.65% error rate, which is better than the DenseNet model (3.74%). However, the DenseNet model uses 1x1 convolutions to reduce parameters, which the authors did not do. Could you clarify how the computational complexity (e.g., FLOPs, parameter count) of the discovered architecture compares to the DenseNet model, especially considering the additional filters?

3. The paper mentions the search space for PTB has approximately 6×10^16 architectures, but the controller evaluates a limited number of architectures (how many?). Could you provide more detail about how many unique architectures were evaluated during training and how this compares to the number of architectures evaluated in the random search baseline?
