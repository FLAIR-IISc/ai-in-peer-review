

# Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

The paper introduces "Neural Architecture Search," a gradient-based method for automatically discovering neural network architectures. The authors propose using a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate architectural hyperparameters as sequences, then training these architectures (the "child networks") and using their validation accuracy as a reward signal to update the controller via policy gradient methods. The paper claims this approach can design novel architectures that outperform human-invented models on CIFAR-10 (achieving 3.65% test error) and Penn Treebank (achieving 62.4 test perplexity), while being faster than previous state-of-the-art models. The authors also present extensions for generating recurrent cell architectures and demonstrate transfer learning to other tasks.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper contains multiple serious methodological errors and inconsistencies that undermine its technical credibility, despite the potentially interesting core idea.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The most critical issue is a fundamental mathematical error in the REINFORCE formulation (Section 3.2). The paper states:

"θcJ(θc) = T∑ t=1 EP (a1:T ;θc) [ 5θc logP (at|a(t−1):1; θc)R ]"

This should read "∇θc" (the gradient symbol) instead of "5θc," which appears to be a typo but represents a serious mathematical error in a core technical contribution. Such a basic error raises concerns about the paper's technical rigor.

Beyond this, the paper contains multiple formatting inconsistencies and unclear descriptions:

- In Section 3.3, the equation for P(Layer j is an input to layer i) uses inconsistent formatting ("Wprev ∗ hj" vs. proper mathematical notation).
- The paper claims "3.65 test set error" without specifying whether this is percentage or absolute error, while earlier in the introduction it states "3.65 test set error" without the percentage sign, creating confusion.
- The paper claims to be "1.05x faster than the current best model" but doesn't clarify which model it's comparing to (DenseNet with 3.74% error?).
- The reference section contains inconsistent formatting ("Bergstra et al.,? \Q2012\E" instead of proper citation format).

The experimental section lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility. For example, the paper states it changes the filter options from "[24, 36, 48, 64]" to "[6, 12, 24, 36]" but doesn't explain why this change was made or how it affects the search space. The paper also doesn't provide the specific ranges searched for hyperparameters like learning rate and weight decay.

Additionally, the paper fails to adequately distinguish itself from recent NAS work. The introduction states it can "design good models from scratch, an achievement considered not possible with other methods," but this claim is not supported by a proper comparison to recent NAS methods like the one by Zoph & Le (2017), which was published around the same time.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Mathematical Corrections**: The most urgent issue is correcting the REINFORCE formula. The paper should be thoroughly reviewed for similar mathematical errors.

2. **Experimental Details**: Provide specific ranges for hyperparameters searched in the grid search, including learning rate, weight decay, batchnorm epsilon, and epoch for learning rate decay. Clarify the exact meaning of "1.05x faster" and specify which model is being compared.

3. **Terminology Consistency**: Use consistent formatting for mathematical notation (e.g., W_1 instead of W1, proper multiplication symbols) throughout the paper.

4. **Comparison to Recent Work**: Add a more thorough comparison to recent NAS work (Zoph & Le, 2017) to properly position the contribution.

5. **Reproducibility**: Provide a specific GitHub commit hash or version number for the released code, not just a URL.

6. **Clarify Claims**: The claim that the method can "design good models from scratch" should be qualified to acknowledge that it still requires a carefully designed search space.

7. **Citation Consistency**: Ensure all references are formatted consistently, and distinguish between different Huang et al. (2016) papers.

## Questions for Authors

1. Could you clarify the exact meaning of "1.05x faster" in the CIFAR-10 results? What is the baseline model's inference time and what is the inference time of your model?

2. Why did you change the filter options from [24, 36, 48, 64] to [6, 12, 24, 36]? How did this affect the search space and the final architecture?

3. Could you provide the specific hyperparameter ranges used in the grid search for the CIFAR-10 model?

4. How does your approach differ from Zoph & Le (2017), which was published around the same time? What advantages does your method have over theirs?

5. Could you clarify the exact meaning of "3.65 test set error" - is this a percentage (3.65%) or absolute error (0.0365)?

These issues collectively undermine confidence in the paper's technical contributions and make it difficult to assess the validity of the claims. While the core idea of neural architecture search is potentially interesting, the technical errors and lack of precision in the presentation make the paper unsuitable for publication in ICLR.
