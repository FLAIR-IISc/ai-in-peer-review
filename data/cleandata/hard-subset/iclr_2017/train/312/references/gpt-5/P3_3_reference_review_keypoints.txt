- Proposes Neural Architecture Search using an RNN controller trained with REINFORCE to generate variable-length, discrete neural architectures; child models trained to convergence provide rewards.
- Controller emits layer-wise hyperparameters and skip-connection decisions; applied to CNNs (CIFAR-10) and RNN cells (PTB), with transfer to char-level PTB and GNMT.
- Reports competitive CIFAR-10 error (down to 3.65% after width scaling) and improved PTB perplexities versus strong baselines.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason 1: Novel, impactful framing of architecture design as sequence prediction optimized by RL with convincing cross-domain results.
- Key reason 2: Flexibility to search variable-length topologies including branching and skip connections.
- Key reason 3: Evidence of transferability of discovered cells to other tasks/frameworks.
- Supporting: Novel attention-like set-selection for skip connections is elegant and general compared to fixed-length searches.
- Supporting: Empirical strength—results competitive with DenseNet/ResNet on CIFAR and outperform tuned LSTMs/RHNs on PTB; controller outperforms random search.
- Supporting: Paper is well written and includes sufficient training and distributed engineering details.
- Reservation: Extremely high compute and poor sample efficiency (12.8k CNNs on 800 GPUs; 15k RNNs on 400 CPUs), limiting accessibility.
- Reservation: CIFAR-10 comparisons mix recipes and use post-hoc width scaling; runtime claims lack standardized measurement; PTB baselines may not have identical tuning/regularization.
- Reservation: Methodological concerns—task-specific reward shaping (accuracy^3 vs c·perplexity^2) lacks rationale/analysis; asynchronous updates may cause gradient staleness/bias.
- Reservation: Skip-connection sampling uses independent sigmoids without sparsity regularization and relies on heuristics to handle compilation failures, potentially biasing search.
- Reservation: Statistical robustness is weak—results appear from single runs without variance across controller seeds or best-of-N dispersion.
- Feedback: Provide standardized compute-budget comparisons to random search, Bayesian/TPE, and evolutionary baselines with identical training setups.
- Feedback: Add ablations for skip-connections, controller size, baseline/entropy regularization, depth schedule, and reward shaping, plus controller learning curves with confidence intervals across seeds.
- Feedback: Clarify post-search tuning vs search-fixed hyperparameters and ensure baselines get equivalent tuning budgets.
- Feedback: Report multi-run statistics (mean/std) for best-found models and fine-tuning runs per architecture.
- Feedback: Discuss failure modes—frequency/impact of compilation failures, average skip fan-in, and effects of zero-padding.
- Feedback: Consider adding multi-fidelity acceleration (early stopping, successive halving/Hyperband, learning-curve extrapolation) to reduce resources.
- Feedback: Improve metric clarity (use bpc for character LM, provide BLEU confidence intervals or significance tests).
- Question 1: Why use squared/cubed reward shaping (c·perplexity^2, accuracy^3), do you negate/invert rewards, and how sensitive are results to these choices?
- Question 2: What variance-reduction/exploration methods were used (entropy regularization, advantage normalization), and how sensitive is the controller to these choices and depth schedule?
- Question 3: How large is gradient staleness in asynchronous updates, did you see bias/instability, and is synchronous aggregation beneficial?
- Question 4: How sensitive are results to search-space heuristics (padding/pooling constraints, skip fan-in limits, inclusion of 1×1 convs) and what fraction of the space is pruned by compilation heuristics?
- Question 5: What exact environment and settings yielded the “1.05x faster” claim, and did PTB/GNMT baselines receive comparable tuning budgets?
- Question 6: How variable are final metrics across independent controller runs, and what performance is expected if selecting top-k architectures instead of the single best?
- Question 7: Did you try low-fidelity proxies (fewer epochs, smaller datasets) or learning-curve extrapolation during search, and how well did they correlate with final performance?
- Overall: Strong, timely contribution showing RL can learn competitive architectures across modalities despite compute intensity and some missing controls.
