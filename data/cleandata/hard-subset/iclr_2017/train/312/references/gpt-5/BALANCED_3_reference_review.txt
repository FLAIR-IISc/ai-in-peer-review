1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a reinforcement-learning-based framework in which a controller RNN generates variable-length descriptions of neural network architectures. Using REINFORCE with a moving-average baseline, the controller is trained to maximize the expected validation performance of “child” networks that are instantiated and trained to convergence. The approach supports rich search spaces, including skip connections via a set-selection attention mechanism for CNNs and a tree-structured parameterization for discovering novel RNN cells. The method is scaled using asynchronous distributed training with many replicas.

Empirically, the paper reports:
- CIFAR-10: discovery of competitive convolutional architectures, including a 39-layer model achieving 4.47% error and a modified version achieving 3.65% error, close to the best human-designed counterparts, with favorable speed in one comparison.
- Penn Treebank: discovery of a new recurrent cell outperforming strong LSTM baselines, achieving 62.4 test perplexity, and showing transfer to character-level LM and modest BLEU gains in GNMT without retuning.
- Ablations/control: comparison to random search for RNN cells, and a robustness check with an enlarged search space (additional functions).

Overall, the paper claims a general and flexible method for automatic architecture design that yields state-of-the-art or near–state-of-the-art performance across modalities.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Conceptual significance and novelty: A general, gradient-free optimization of variable-length architectures via a learned controller, with mechanisms for skip connections and recurrent-cell discovery, constitutes a substantial step beyond fixed-length hyperparameter optimization.
- Strong empirical evidence across domains: Competitive or state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and PTB, plus transfer to other tasks, suggest practical impact and generality.

3) Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? Automating the design of neural architectures over a large, variable-length search space—moving beyond hand-crafted designs or fixed-length hyperparameter optimization.

Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The paper clearly positions itself relative to Bayesian optimization and neuroevolution (flexibility vs. practicality), program induction/meta-learning, and sequence-to-sequence/RL work. The use of an autoregressive controller and policy gradients to handle non-differentiable rewards is appropriate and well motivated.

Do the results support the claims?
- The method discovers nontrivial CNNs with skip connections and unconventional filter choices, and a novel RNN cell that outperforms strong LSTM baselines. Transfer experiments strengthen claims of generality.
- The random-search control for the RNN-cell setting shows a performance gap in favor of the learned controller, suggesting the controller does learn useful search heuristics.
- Training and search details are reported (budgets, controller settings, reward shaping), and the authors plan code release.

Caveats tempering the evidence:
- Computational budget is extremely large (e.g., 800 GPUs training 12,800 CNNs for 50 epochs each; 400 CPUs for RNNs). This raises questions about accessibility and reproducibility for most researchers, and whether the results primarily reflect brute-force search budget.
- Potential selection bias/overfitting to the validation set: the controller repeatedly optimizes against a 5k-example validation subset, and the final selection is the best among thousands. While the test set is evaluated once, additional safeguards (e.g., a separate hold-out for final model selection) would increase confidence.
- Comparisons: While state-of-the-art human-designed models are included, baselines from strong black-box optimizers (e.g., TPE/SMAC/multi-fidelity BO) on the same budget are not reported for CIFAR-10. The random-search comparison is only shown for the RNN cell.
- Some fairness concerns in CIFAR-10: after search, the best model is further modified (e.g., +40 filters) and hyperparameters are hand-tuned via grid search, which complicates an apples-to-apples comparison to the architectures as “found.”
- Statistical rigor: Limited reporting of variance across independent controller runs, confidence intervals, and the correlation between proxy training (50 epochs) and final convergence rankings.

Despite these, the method’s generality, novelty, and breadth of positive results warrant acceptance.

4) Additional feedback and questions for the authors (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
Suggestions for improvement:
- Practicality and budget: Discuss strategies to reduce cost, e.g., early-stopping, learning-curve extrapolation, multi-fidelity search, or parameter/weight sharing across child models. A small-budget ablation (e.g., 10× fewer architectures) would be informative.
- Validation protocol: Mitigate selection bias by using a second hold-out set for final selection, or nested validation. Report how performance changes when using a separate selection set.
- Baselines: Include strong black-box optimization baselines for CIFAR-10 under comparable training budgets. Report compute-aware metrics (FLOPs, latency) and consider multi-objective rewards.
- Statistical analysis: Report variance over multiple independent controller runs; show rank correlation between 50-epoch rewards and final convergence; include confidence intervals for test performance.
- Reward shaping: Justify the choice of cubing accuracy and squaring perplexity; show sensitivity to these transforms and to baseline/advantage normalization.
- Search-space ablations: Analyze the contribution of skip-connection prediction, layer-type choices, and search-space size to outcomes. For RNN cells, examine the role of the base=8 tree depth.
- Reproducibility: Release search logs, discovered architectures, and training scripts; clarify all post-search modifications (e.g., adding filters) and report performance of the discovered architecture without manual changes.
- Resource-aware objectives: Consider integrating model size or inference cost into the reward to discover Pareto-optimal architectures.

Questions for the authors:
- How sensitive are results to the reward transformation (accuracy^3, perplexity^2) and to the moving-average baseline? Did you try advantage normalization or alternative variance-reduction techniques?
- What is the variability across independent runs of the controller (different seeds)? How consistent are the discovered architectures/cells?
- How well does the 50-epoch proxy reward correlate with final performance rankings on CIFAR-10? Any analysis of misrankings or early stopping effects?
- For CIFAR-10, can you provide a random-search baseline and/or a TPE/SMAC baseline under matched compute budgets?
- What fraction of proposed skip connections are actually used in the best architectures, and does zero-padding for mismatched sizes materially affect performance?
- In the 39-layer CIFAR-10 experiment, you restrict the search to 13 block predictions and later add +40 filters. What is the performance without the manual filter increase, and how sensitive is performance to this post-hoc change?
- For PTB and GNMT, can you quantify parameter count and per-step FLOPs for the discovered cell vs. LSTM, and confirm that comparisons are compute-matched?
- How does controller capacity (2-layer LSTM with 35 hidden units) affect search quality? Did you consider larger/smaller controllers or entropy regularization to manage exploration?

Overall, this is an ambitious, well-motivated paper with compelling results and clear potential for impact. Addressing the above points would strengthen its scientific rigor and practical relevance.
