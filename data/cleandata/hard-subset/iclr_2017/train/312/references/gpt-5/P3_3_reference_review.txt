Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), using a recurrent controller trained with policy gradients (REINFORCE) to generate variable-length, discrete descriptions of neural architectures. The controller emits layer-wise hyperparameters and skip-connection decisions; sampled “child” models are trained to convergence and their validation metric serves as the reward to update the controller. The method is applied to two families: CNNs for CIFAR-10 and RNN cells for PTB language modeling. The authors report competitive-to-SOTA CIFAR-10 error (down to 3.65% after a modest post-search width scaling) and improved PTB perplexities versus strong LSTM baselines, with encouraging transfer to character-level PTB and to GNMT (+0.5 BLEU). The paper also details practical engineering to enable large-scale, asynchronous distributed training.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Clear, impactful idea of treating architecture design as a sequence prediction problem optimized via RL, with convincing empirical results on two domains; (2) Flexibility to search variable-length topologies with branching/skip connections; (3) Evidence of transfer to other tasks/frameworks.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and motivation: Framing architecture construction as an autoregressive program that can output variable-length, compositional decisions (including skip connections) is a meaningful advance over fixed-length hyperparameter search. The use of a learned attention-like set-selection mechanism for skip connections is elegant and general.
- Empirical strength: On CIFAR-10, NAS yields architectures competitive with DenseNet/ResNet families; on PTB, discovered cells outperform carefully tuned LSTMs and RHNs with sizable gains. Transfer to character-level LM and to GNMT, while not exhaustive, shows the cell is not overfit to PTB. The comparison against random search shows the controller learns a useful policy.
- Scope and clarity: The paper is well written and positions the work relative to BO and neuroevolution, and it provides sufficient training details for both controller and child models, including distributed setup.

Reservations and limitations (do not preclude acceptance)
- Computational burden and sample efficiency: The approach evaluates 12.8k CNNs on 800 GPUs and 15k RNN cells on 400 CPUs, each to tens of epochs. This scale is beyond the reach of most groups; the method’s sample efficiency is limited relative to multi-fidelity or weight-sharing alternatives. While engineering is part of the contribution, the paper would benefit from strategies to reduce compute (early stopping, learning-curve extrapolation, low-fidelity proxies, parameter sharing).
- Comparisons and fairness: CIFAR-10 comparisons mix architectures and training recipes; the 3.65% result involves post-hoc width scaling not found by the controller. Runtime comparisons (“1.05x as fast as DenseNet”) lack standardized measurement conditions. For PTB, the best settings include additional tuning and embedding tying; it is not fully clear whether all baselines received the same hyperparameter budget and regularization choices.
- Methodological details: Reward shaping differs between tasks (accuracy^3 vs c·perplexity^2), but the rationale and effect on variance/exploration are not analyzed. Asynchronous updates with many replicas raise concerns about gradient staleness/bias in the controller’s policy gradient. The skip-connection sampling uses independent sigmoids without explicit sparsity regularization; the method relies on heuristics to handle “compilation failures,” which may bias the search.
- Statistical robustness: Reported results appear to be single runs of the best-found models. Variance across controller seeds, best-of-N dispersion, and test-time uncertainty (e.g., for BLEU) are not reported.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide a clear, standardized compute budget comparison against strong, modern baselines (e.g., random search with the same number of trials, Bayesian/TPE, and an evolutionary baseline), and report wall-clock, FLOPs, and parameter counts under identical training setups.
- Add ablations: effect of (a) skip-connection attention vs no-skips, (b) controller depth/size, (c) baseline/entropy regularization, (d) depth schedule, and (e) reward shaping. Show learning curves for controller reward vs steps with confidence intervals across seeds.
- Clarify and systematize the post-search tuning: which hyperparameters are fixed during search vs tuned afterwards, and ensure baselines receive an equivalent post-tuning budget.
- Report multi-run statistics for the best-found models (mean/std over independent searches) and for fine-tuning runs per architecture.
- Discuss failure modes and constraints: frequency and impact of “compilation failures,” average fan-in from skip selection, and the role of zero-padding on efficiency/accuracy.
- Consider adding simple multi-fidelity acceleration (early stopping with conservative stopping rules, successive halving/Hyperband-like schedules) to reduce the required resources.
- Improve clarity on metrics: use bits-per-character (bpc) for character LM, and provide BLEU confidence intervals or paired significance when possible.

Questions for the authors
1) Reward shaping: For PTB, you define reward as c·(validation perplexity)^2. Since lower perplexity is better, do you negate the reward or invert it? Why square, and how sensitive are results to this choice vs, e.g., −log perplexity? Similarly, why cube accuracy on CIFAR-10?
2) Variance reduction and exploration: Did you use entropy regularization or advantage normalization beyond an EMA baseline? How sensitive is the controller to these choices and to the depth-increase schedule?
3) Asynchronous updates: How large is gradient staleness across replicas, and did you observe bias/instability from asynchronous REINFORCE? Any benefit from synchronous aggregation?
4) Search space sensitivity: How do results change if you remove the padding/pooling heuristics, constrain skip fan-in, or include 1×1 convs? Can you quantify the fraction of the search space pruned by compilation heuristics?
5) Compute and fairness: What exact environment was used for the “1.05x faster” claim (hardware, batch size, cuDNN version)? For PTB and GNMT, did all baselines receive comparable hyperparameter tuning budgets?
6) Robustness: How variable are the final test metrics across multiple independent controller runs? What is the expected performance if one selects the top-k architectures instead of the single best?
7) Early stopping: Did you try low-fidelity proxies (fewer epochs, smaller datasets) or learning-curve extrapolation during search, and how did they correlate with final performance?

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution that convincingly demonstrates that RL-based controllers can learn to design competitive architectures across modalities. Despite compute intensity and some missing controls, the paper merits acceptance.
