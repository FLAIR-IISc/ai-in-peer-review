Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), in which a controller RNN samples discrete architecture descriptions for child networks. Child models are trained, and their validation accuracy is used as a reward to update the controller via REINFORCE. The approach supports variable-length architectures, skip connections via a set-selection attention, and asynchronous distributed training to evaluate thousands of candidates in parallel.
- The method is applied to CIFAR-10 (conv nets) and PTB (language modeling cells). The authors report strong results: a CIFAR-10 model with 3.65% error and a PTB test perplexity of 62.4, plus some transfer experiments (character LM, GNMT) showing modest gains.
- The paper’s ambition and scope are notable: a unified search framework for both CNN and RNN architectures, with practical engineering to scale the search.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Insufficiently rigorous empirical validation against established, compute-matched baselines (e.g., Bayesian optimization, evolutionary search), with the main comparison being a weak random search baseline; and
2) Heavy reliance on massive compute and ad hoc search-space/shaping choices (reward cubing, depth schedules, fixed pooling positions, manual post-hoc widening), which undermines claims of generality and “automatic” design, and limits reproducibility.

Supporting arguments
- Baselines and fairness. The method is positioned against BO/evolutionary approaches, but no head-to-head empirical comparisons under a matched evaluation budget are provided. The only baseline with curves is random search, which is known to be surprisingly strong but is not representative of the best HPO/architecture search methods. Without compute-normalized comparisons (same number of child trainings, same epochs), it is hard to attribute gains to the controller rather than to an enormous budget (12.8k–15k evaluations, 800 GPUs/400 CPUs in parallel).
- Sensitivity to search-space engineering. Several choices appear to materially influence outcomes: reward shaping (cubing accuracy), scheduled increases in depth, selective activation/combination sets, fixed pooling layers for some runs, grouping layers into 3-layer “blocks,” and then manually adding 40 filters to the final CIFAR model. These interventions blur the line between genuinely automatic discovery and designer-guided tuning, complicating attribution of the reported SOTA to the search procedure itself.
- Risk of meta-overfitting and post-selection bias. The controller is trained directly on a 5k “validation” split from CIFAR-10, after which the best architecture is further tuned via grid search and then reported on the test set. With 12.8k architectures sampled and the controller optimizing on the same validation split, the risk of overfitting to this split is substantial. No safeguards (e.g., nested validation, multiple validation splits, or re-search on different splits) are reported.
- Limited reporting on variance and statistical rigor. Results are largely single-run point estimates on test sets after selection. No confidence intervals, repeated trials, or significance tests are given, despite the high-variance nature of REINFORCE and the stochastic training of child models. The random-search comparison lacks details on seeds, exact budgets, and whether it includes the same post-hoc refinements.
- Reproducibility and accessibility. The search relies on very large-scale distributed infrastructure. Only the final models (not the full search code) are planned for release. For the community, it will be difficult to reproduce the claimed SOTA or to fairly compare new techniques without access to comparable compute and code.
- Comparisons and confounds. CIFAR comparisons mix different choices (e.g., not using 1x1 bottlenecks, different filter counts), and the “1.05x faster” claim is not clearly defined (inference vs training, hardware setup). On PTB, it is unclear whether regularization tricks (dropout variants, weight tying, etc.) are consistently matched across baselines. The transfer experiments (GNMT +0.5 BLEU) are promising but small and reported without variance.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide compute-matched, budget-controlled comparisons to strong baselines: BO/SMBO tuned for variable-length spaces, evolutionary algorithms, and RL-based variants (e.g., different credit assignment). Include learning curves of best/median-of-top-k vs wall-clock and vs child-evaluations.
- Quantify sensitivity: ablations for reward shaping, depth schedule, skip-connection mechanism, search-space size (e.g., base=4 vs 8 for cells), and asynchronous update cadence.
- Address meta-overfitting: use multiple validation splits, nested validation or a separate meta-validation set, and report performance stability across splits. Report the number of architecture evaluations and early-stopping rules that influence selection pressure.
- Standardize and clarify comparisons: report parameter counts, FLOPs, and training/inference time with consistent hardware and augmentation. Align regularization and optimization tweaks with baselines; where differences exist, provide both matched and best-known comparisons.
- Release the controller/search code and a small-scale, compute-feasible setting to enable replication. If not possible, provide detailed logs of architectures sampled, rewards, and seeds.
- Expand evaluation to additional datasets (e.g., CIFAR-100, ImageNet, WikiText-2) to support claims of generality and reduce dataset-specific overfitting concerns.

Questions for the authors
- How does NAS compare to BO and evolutionary strategies under identical evaluation budgets (same number of child trainings and epochs), both in best-found performance and sample efficiency?
- What measures did you take to mitigate overfitting of the controller to the 5k validation split on CIFAR-10? Have you tried re-searching with different validation splits and reporting test performance variance?
- Why cube the validation accuracy in the reward? How sensitive are results to this shaping and to the depth-increase schedule?
- For the CIFAR best 3.65% model: precise parameter count, FLOPs, training/inference time, and the definition of “1.05x faster.” Are augmentations and optimization schedules matched to DenseNet?
- On PTB, which baselines use shared embeddings and the same dropout variants? Please provide a table clarifying which tricks are enabled per model and ensure parameter counts are strictly matched.
- How robust are the discovered architectures to modest changes in the search space (e.g., different filter-size sets, allowing 1x1 bottlenecks, different pooling placements) without manual post-hoc widening?
- Can you report variance across multiple NES runs (different seeds) and the distribution of top-k architectures’ performance?

Overall, the idea is interesting and the results are strong, but in its current form the paper does not meet the bar of methodological rigor and reproducibility required to endorse a new approach over established alternatives.
