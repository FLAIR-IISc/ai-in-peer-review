- Paper proposes an RL-based controller RNN to generate variable-length neural architectures, optimized via REINFORCE on child validation performance.  
- Controller supports flexible search spaces including convolutions with skip connections and novel recurrent cell trees.  
- Training uses child validation accuracy/perplexity (with reward shaping), EMA baseline, and large-scale parallel asynchronous training.  
- Empirically finds competitive CNNs on CIFAR-10 (down to 3.65% after scaling) with notable motifs (rectangular filters, many short skip connections).  
- Discovers a novel RNN cell on PTB that outperforms strong LSTM/RHN baselines and transfers to char-level LM and GNMT.  
- Includes ablations/controls (random search comparison, expanded ops like max and sin) and analyses of skip-connection density.  
- Overall claim: general, flexible architecture-search framework with strong cross-domain empirical results.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason: novel, well-motivated method that generalizes prior HPO/evolution methods to variable-length architectures via an RL controller.  
- Key reason: strong empirical support across vision and language with evidence of transferability.  
- Problem/motivation praised: manual design is costly; prior HPO/BO methods ill-suited to variable-length discrete spaces.  
- Technical soundness: REINFORCE + EMA baseline, content-based sigmoid skip prediction, and tree-parameterized recurrent-cell search are reasonable and well-integrated.  
- Empirical support: competitive CIFAR-10 results, superior PTB perplexity, transfer experiments, and random-search control demonstrating learning benefit.  
- Scope/significance: opens general approach to learning architectures with breadth across CNNs and RNNs.  
- Caveat: Extremely compute-intensive (e.g., hundreds of GPUs/CPUs, 10k–15k models), raising practicality concerns.  
- Caveat: Comparisons may be unfair—child models trained for short budgets, later tuning applied, and differing training recipes across baselines.  
- Caveat: Top CIFAR result requires post-hoc scaling/tuning; speed comparisons to DenseNet are not strictly apples-to-apples.  
- Caveat: Search-space engineering and heuristics (fixed pooling, padding/concatenation fallbacks) may bias results; space not fully neutral.  
- Caveat: Reward shaping (accuracy^3, perplexity^2) and other controller hyperparameters are ad hoc with no ablation on their effects.  
- Caveat: Asynchronous/stale gradients and controller training stability (entropy reg., clipping, schedules) not deeply analyzed.  
- Caveat: Reproducibility limited by lack of full search code and precise compute cost reporting.  
- Suggestion: Report exact compute budgets (GPU/CPU days, wall-clock) and efficiency metrics (best reward vs evaluations).  
- Suggestion: Add variance analyses—multiple controller runs, confidence intervals, sensitivity to seeds.  
- Suggestion: Ablate reward shaping, baseline choice, entropy regularization, controller size, and asynchronous update accumulation.  
- Suggestion: Ensure fairness—unify training protocols across baselines or provide adjusted comparisons.  
- Suggestion: Quantify how search-space heuristics affect validity rate and performance; report fraction of invalid architectures.  
- Suggestion: Release a smaller-scale, reproducible search setup and logging of sampled architectures/rewards.  
- Suggestion: Provide deeper architectural analysis—recurring motifs, skip-connection correlation with performance, learned stride/pooling distributions.  
- Question: What are the exact computational costs (GPU/CPU days) and wall-clock times for CIFAR and PTB searches?  
- Question: How sensitive are results to reward shaping and baseline choice; were entropy regularization or KL constraints tried?  
- Question: How stable are results across multiple independent controller runs (variance in best-found performance)?  
- Question: How many sampled architectures are invalid and how often do padding/concatenation fallbacks activate; do these heuristics bias topologies?  
- Question: Are data augmentation and training schedules identical for NAS models and compared baselines; if not, provide matched baselines.  
- Question: What is the quantified contribution of skip-connection prediction vs other search dimensions; what if skip connections are disallowed?  
- Question: For the PTB cell, how much gain is from cell structure vs other choices (shared embeddings, dropout); provide controlled comparison.  
- Question: How was “speed” measured versus RHN and DenseNet (hardware, batch sizes, exact conditions)?  
- Question: Did the controller collapse to short/long architectures; how does depth schedule affect search quality?  
- Question: Are there failure cases where NAS finds suboptimal or unintuitive motifs; please discuss counterexamples.
