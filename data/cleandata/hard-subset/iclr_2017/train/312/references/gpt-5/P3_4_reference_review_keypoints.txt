- Proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS): a recurrent controller trained with REINFORCE that generates variable-length neural architectures token-by-token and is updated using child model validation performance as reward.  
- Extends NAS to convolutional networks with skip/branch connections via set-selection attention and simple padding/concatenation rules for incompatible shapes.  
- Extends NAS to RNN cell design using a tree-structured search space that composes operations on xt and ht−1 and injects/updates ct−1 and ct.  
- Uses substantial distributed parallelism (parameter servers, many asynchronous replicas) to make the search feasible.  
- Achieves competitive results: CIFAR-10 models down to 3.65% error (after post-hoc filter increase) and a PTB RNN cell with state-of-the-art perplexity and transfer gains.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Novel formulation treating architecture generation as autoregressive sequence generation with RL over a flexible, variable-length space including RNN cell design.  
- Key reason 2: Strong empirical results across vision and language, with ablations and fair random-search baselines.  
- Key reason 3: Concrete, scalable systems recipe demonstrating practical feasibility at scale.  
- Novelty/scope: Handles variable-length structures and complex connectivity in one general framework, more expressive than fixed-length hyperparameter or many neuroevolution methods.  
- Methodological soundness: Uses standard REINFORCE with moving-average baseline and appropriate distributed asynchronous training for expensive rewards.  
- Reward shaping rationale: Uses transformations (e.g., cubic accuracy, scaled perplexity) and selecting max over last epochs to adjust gradient magnitudes.  
- CIFAR-10 empirical strength: Discovered a shallow 15-layer model (5.50%); allowing strides/pooling yields 4.47%; adding filters yields 3.65%; ablations show skip connections are exploited.  
- PTB empirical strength: Learned cell improves perplexity by ~3.6 points over strong baselines, is computationally efficient, and transfers to character PTB and GNMT.  
- Controls: Random search baseline handled fairly; policy improves best and average top models over time and is robust to search-space expansion.  
- Clarity: Paper details controller RNN size, training schedules, reward definitions, and data splits.  
- Concern: Very high computational cost (e.g., ~800 GPUs for CIFAR, 400 CPUs for PTB), raising reproducibility and accessibility concerns.  
- Concern: Risk of overfitting to a small validation set (5k examples) given tens of thousands of architectures evaluated; needs quantification.  
- Concern: Best CIFAR result (3.65%) uses a post-hoc +40 filters capacity increase outside the searched space; fairness of comparisons and matching training/augmentation unclear.  
- Concern: Controller lacks explicit entropy regularization or exploration control; potential premature convergence—report entropy and exploration studies.  
- Concern: Reward-shaping choices (max over last 5 epochs, cubing accuracy) are ad hoc and need justification and sensitivity analyses.  
- Concern: Handling of incompatible convolutional connections via padding/concatenation may bias search and inflate feature widths—report resulting channel sizes, params, and memory use.  
- Concern: RNN cell search-space grammar and dimensionality constraints are under-specified; clarify wiring, arity, dimensional consistency, and how gradient flow is preserved.  
- Suggestions: Report compute budgets (GPU/CPU days), wall-clock time, scaling efficiency, and smaller-budget experiments to show graceful degradation.  
- Suggestions: Provide variability across independent searches and retraining runs for top architectures to assess reliability.  
- Suggestions: Include parameter/FLOP counts and throughput for compared CIFAR-10 models to substantiate efficiency claims.  
- Suggestions: Ablate depth schedule, reward transformation, and add entropy/temperature exploration studies.  
- Suggestions: For PTB, document exact regularization settings and isolate gains from the cell vs. regularization.  
- Suggestions: Release the list of sampled architectures and their rewards for meta-analysis and reproducibility.  
- Questions 1: How sensitive are results to the reward transformation and to using max vs average of last epochs—does this reduce variance or change the search target?  
- Question 2: Was entropy regularization or temperature scaling used for the controller; report controller entropy over training and any diversity collapse.  
- Question 3: What is the total compute budget (GPU/CPU days) per search, and how do results degrade when reducing K, m, or child-training epochs?  
- Question 4: How many independent search runs were performed and what is the variance in best-found performance; do top architectures retrain reliably across seeds?  
- Question 5: For the 3.65% CIFAR model, how much gain comes from adding 40 filters, and can the controller search capacity directly?  
- Question 6: In the RNN search space, what constraints ensure tensor dimensionality compatibility and prevent ct/ct−1 causing degenerate shortcuts?  
- Question 7: Given the 5k CIFAR validation set, was alternative splitting or cross-validation tried to assess overfitting during search?  
- Question 8: Please provide parameter/FLOP counts and exact data augmentation for all compared models to validate speed/accuracy comparisons.
