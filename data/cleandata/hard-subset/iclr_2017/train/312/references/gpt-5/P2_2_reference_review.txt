1) Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a reinforcement-learning-based framework in which a recurrent controller generates variable-length descriptions of neural network architectures. The controller is trained with REINFORCE to maximize expected validation performance of “child” models, using large-scale distributed, asynchronous updates. The method flexibly handles convolutional networks (with skip connections, pooling, BN, etc.) and recurrent cells (defining combination operators and activations over a tree structure, including memory connections). Empirically, NAS discovers high-performing CNNs on CIFAR-10 and novel RNN cells on PTB, achieving state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art results; it also shows transfer to character-level PTB and a GNMT machine translation system. The work is ambitious, well-motivated, and offers a general recipe to search over architectural design spaces that are difficult for standard hyperparameter optimization.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Substantive and timely contribution: a general, flexible, gradient-based search mechanism over variable-length architectures that goes beyond fixed-dimensional hyperparameter tuning. (2) Strong empirical evidence across modalities and tasks, including transfer, with careful engineering to make large-scale search feasible.

2) Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles automated architecture design, reducing hand-crafted trial-and-error. It is well-situated relative to Bayesian optimization (typically fixed-length, less flexible for structure), neuroevolution (flexible but often sample-inefficient), and meta-learning/program synthesis. Casting architecture generation as a sequence of actions with a non-differentiable reward is natural, and the REINFORCE-based solution is appropriate and simple.

- Novelty/technical soundness: Key ingredients include (a) an auto-regressive controller that emits a variable-length architecture specification, (b) content-based “set-selection” attention to propose skip connections, (c) a tree-structured parameterization for RNN cells with explicit memory routing, and (d) distributed asynchronous policy gradient to amortize the enormous evaluation cost. These are coherent, technically plausible design choices.

- Empirical support:
  • CIFAR-10: The method discovers competitive CNNs (down to 3.65% error after post-hoc channel scaling), with interesting learned patterns (rectangular filters, top-layer larger filters, skip usage). Comparisons to strong baselines (ResNet/DenseNet variants) and ablations (stride/pooling choices, skip connection perturbations) support claims.  
  • PTB word-level LM: The discovered cell improves perplexity by ~3.6 over prior SOTA while being computationally simpler than some competing architectures.  
  • Generalization: The cell transfers to char-level PTB and GNMT with positive gains, suggesting the controller learns compositional biases that are not dataset-specific.  
  • Baselines: A random search control is included, showing consistent gains from policy gradient over time; a larger search space (with extra ops/activations) yields comparable quality, indicating robustness.

- Rigor: The authors hold out validation data for reward and report test accuracy once for the best model, limiting test leakage. Parameter budget matching on PTB is reasonable. The distributed setup, reward baseline, and reward shaping are documented.

3) Caveats and limitations (do not block acceptance but temper the claims)
- Compute/sample efficiency: The approach consumes extraordinary compute (e.g., 800 GPUs for CIFAR, 400 CPUs for PTB), which limits reproducibility and raises fairness questions versus human-designed baselines developed under smaller budgets. The paper would benefit from a clearer accounting of total wall-clock and budget-normalized comparisons.

- Post-hoc tuning and scaling: After selecting architectures, the authors run grid searches and increase channel counts to improve performance. This conflates architecture discovery with training/hyperparameter tuning and complicates attributing gains solely to the search. It would help to separate “as-found” performance from “tuned” performance across methods.

- Statistical reporting: Results are largely single runs; variance across independent NAS runs, sensitivity to reward shaping (e.g., cubing validation accuracy) and controller hyperparameters are not quantified.

- Search space and constraints: Heuristics to resolve incompatible shapes (e.g., zero-padding, defaulting to image input) may bias the search and are not analyzed. The proportion of invalid or degenerate proposals and their effect on learning is not reported.

Despite these, the overall methodological innovation and empirical impact warrant acceptance.

4) Feedback to improve the paper
- Provide detailed compute accounting: number of sampled architectures trained to completion, average training time per model, total FLOPs, and wall-clock per task. Include budget-normalized comparisons (e.g., best-found vs random under equal evaluation counts).

- Report distributions, not only best: show histograms/percentiles of validation performance over time; plot top-k averages with confidence intervals; report variance over multiple controller seeds.

- Separate architecture vs training effects: report “frozen training recipe” performance for the selected architecture before any grid search or channel scaling; optionally show matched post-hoc tuning for strong baselines.

- Sensitivity/ablation: analyze (i) reward shaping choices (e.g., cubic vs linear), (ii) baseline/EMA parameters, (iii) depth schedule, and (iv) the skip-connection attention mechanism versus simpler wiring schemes.

- Search space transparency: precisely specify all tokens/options, constraints, and failure-handling rules; report the fraction of invalid samples and how they are treated in the reward.

- Reproducibility: release not only the final architectures but also search code or a minimal, smaller-scale reference setup; consider early stopping schemes (e.g., successive halving/Hyperband) to reduce cost.

Questions for the authors
- Compute and budget:
  • How many architectures were fully trained on each task, and what was the total compute (GPU/CPU hours)?  
  • How sensitive are results to reducing the number of evaluated architectures by 10x?

- Reward and optimization:
  • Why cube the validation accuracy for CIFAR, and how does that affect the controller’s exploration–exploitation trade-off and variance?  
  • Did you try advantage normalization or other variance-reduction techniques beyond an EMA baseline?

- Search space and invalid samples:
  • What fraction of sampled CNNs required default inputs or zero-padding to resolve incompatibilities? Do such fixes bias the search?  
  • For the RNN cell search, how often do trees produce degenerate or unstable cells, and how are they handled?

- Comparisons:
  • Is the random-search baseline given the same post-hoc grid search and channel scaling as NAS-selected models?  
  • Can you provide budget-matched comparisons to more advanced HPO methods that can handle conditional spaces (e.g., SMAC/TPE variants)?

- Robustness and transfer:
  • What is the variance in test performance across independent NAS runs (different controller seeds) for both CIFAR-10 and PTB?  
  • Have you tested the discovered CNNs on other datasets (e.g., CIFAR-100, SVHN) without further search to assess transferability?

Overall, this is a compelling, well-motivated paper that opens a fruitful line of research and demonstrates impressive results, albeit with substantial compute. I recommend acceptance.
