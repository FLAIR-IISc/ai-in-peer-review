1. Summary of claims and contributions
- The paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method that trains a recurrent controller via policy gradients (REINFORCE) to generate variable-length strings that specify neural network architectures. The controller is optimized to maximize validation accuracy of “child” networks sampled from its policy.
- The approach extends to complex architectural motifs by (a) enabling skip connections through set-selection attention, and (b) generating recurrent cells via a tree-structured composition space that includes injection and routing of cell state.
- Large-scale asynchronous distributed training is used to make the search feasible: hundreds of controller replicas and hundreds of child models trained in parallel.
- Empirically, NAS finds competitive-to-state-of-the-art CNNs on CIFAR-10 (down to 3.65% error after modest post-search scaling) and discovers a novel RNN cell that achieves 62.4 perplexity on PTB, surpassing prior strong baselines. The cell transfers to character-level PTB and GNMT with gains, and policy-gradient search outperforms random search in a control experiment.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novelty and scope: A general, flexible RL-based formulation that searches variable-length architectures, including CNN topologies with skip connections and new RNN cells, is a significant advance over fixed-length hyperparameter optimization and prior neuro-evolution methods.
- Empirical support across domains: Strong results on CIFAR-10 and PTB, including transfer to other tasks, plus ablations against random search, substantiate the central claims.

3. Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled?
- Automatically discovering neural architectures (both CNNs and RNN cells) by learning a policy over architectural decisions, rather than relying on manual design or fixed-length hyperparameter spaces.

Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature?
- The paper positions NAS relative to Bayesian optimization, random search, and neuro-evolution, highlighting limitations of fixed-length spaces and scalability of search-based methods. Drawing connections to program synthesis and sequence modeling supports the autoregressive controller design. Using REINFORCE for non-differentiable architectural rewards is appropriate and consistent with related sequence-level optimization work.

Do the results support the claims, and are they rigorous?
- NAS achieves competitive CIFAR-10 error rates with discovered architectures, and a novel RNN cell that improves PTB perplexity and transfers positively to character-level LM and GNMT. The paper includes a random search baseline and control experiment expanding the search space, indicating robustness of the approach.
- Technical details are provided for controller training, reward shaping, parallelization, and search-space construction (e.g., skip connections via learned sigmoids, recurrent cell tree indexing). The REINFORCE estimator uses a moving-average baseline to reduce variance; the distributed scheme is reasonable.
- Caveats: (a) Extremely large compute budgets are used (e.g., up to 800 GPUs) and not normalized across baselines, which challenges reproducibility and fair comparison. (b) Final CIFAR-10 results include post-search scaling (adding filters) and a small hyperparameter grid search, which complicates purity of the “from scratch” claim. (c) Statistical variability is not quantified (no multiple seeds/confidence intervals). (d) Reward shaping choices (e.g., cubed accuracy, perplexity transformations) are heuristic with limited ablation. Despite these limitations, the evidence is substantial and consistent with the claimed improvements.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not necessarily part of the decision)
- Report compute budgets explicitly (e.g., GPU/CPU hours, FLOPs), and, where possible, equalize or normalize search budgets across random search and other baselines. Include wall-clock comparisons supporting “1.05x faster” claims with hardware/config details.
- Provide variance estimates: repeat architecture search runs and/or retrain the same discovered architecture with multiple seeds to report mean ± std on validation/test metrics.
- Clarify the post-search tuning protocol: which hyperparameters are tuned after architecture selection, how selection uses validation data, and whether test metrics are reported only once per architecture to avoid test leakage.
- Analyze sample efficiency: performance vs number of architectures evaluated; sensitivity to reward shaping (cubing accuracy; c·perplexity^2) and baseline choice.
- Expand ablations on search space design: effect of allowing strides/pooling; impact of skip-connection attention mechanism; handling of invalid graphs and padding choices; distribution of selected operations.
- Discuss generalization beyond CIFAR-10/PTB and provide guidance on adapting search spaces to new domains. Releasing full search spaces and controller code (not only final cells) would strengthen reproducibility.
- Improve reference formatting and fix minor typos.

Questions for the authors
- Compute and fairness:
  - What are the total GPU/CPU hours used for each search (CIFAR-10, PTB)? How does this compare to the budgets used for random search and the strongest baselines?
  - For the “1.05x faster than DenseNet” claim, what are the exact inference/training setups and timings?
- Methodology details:
  - How sensitive are results to the reward shaping (e.g., cubing accuracy; choice of constant c in PTB)? Please provide ablations.
  - How often do sampled CNNs produce invalid graphs, and how much performance is affected by the fallback rules (auto-connecting to image input, concatenation/padding)?
  - What is the effect of the depth schedule during search on the final discovered architecture?
  - How does the variance-reduction baseline (EMA of rewards) compare to learned baselines or advantage estimation?
- Evaluation protocol:
  - For CIFAR-10, are final test results obtained after retraining the discovered architecture from scratch with the selected hyperparameters once? How many trials were run to choose those hyperparameters, and was test performance consulted only at the end?
  - For PTB, do the compared baselines also use tied embeddings and the same dropout variants? If not, can you provide matched baselines under your training setup?
- Transfer and robustness:
  - How robust is the discovered RNN cell across model sizes and datasets beyond PTB and GNMT (e.g., WikiText, large-scale MT)?
  - Can the controller’s learned policy transfer across related search spaces (e.g., fine-tune a policy trained on CIFAR-10 for SVHN/ImageNet subsets), improving sample efficiency?
