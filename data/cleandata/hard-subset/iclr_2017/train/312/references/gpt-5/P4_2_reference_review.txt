1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a reinforcement-learning-based framework for automatically designing neural network architectures. A recurrent “controller” network generates sequences of discrete decisions that specify a child model’s architecture (e.g., convolutional hyperparameters, skip connections, or a recurrent-cell computation graph). The controller is trained with REINFORCE to maximize expected validation performance of sampled architectures. Key contributions include:
- A general, variable-length architecture search formulation using an autoregressive controller trained on non-differentiable rewards.
- Mechanisms to expand the search space: content-based selection of skip connections; inclusion of additional layer types; and a tree-structured parameterization for discovering novel recurrent cells.
- A scalable distributed evaluation scheme with asynchronous updates to amortize the cost of training many candidate models.
- Strong empirical results: CIFAR-10 test error as low as 3.65% with a discovered convnet, and state-of-the-art PTB word-level perplexity (as low as 62.4), with transfer to character PTB and improvements in GNMT without retuning, and superiority over random search under matched budgets.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novelty and scope: A general, flexible architecture search method that handles variable-length, structured design spaces and discovers both convnets and RNN cells.
- Empirical strength: Multiple competitive or state-of-the-art results across vision and language, supported by ablations (random search comparison, expanded search space) and transfer experiments.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: Automating neural architecture design over large, structured, variable-length spaces. Prior hyperparameter optimization methods typically assume fixed-length spaces; neuro-evolution is flexible but often less efficient at scale. NAS addresses this by learning an autoregressive generator of architectures with policy gradients, directly optimizing validation performance.
- Motivation and placement: The approach is well-motivated by limitations of prior Bayesian optimization and evolutionary methods for high-dimensional, combinatorial, variable-length design spaces. The framing as sequence prediction aligns with seq2seq decoders and program induction. Optimization of non-differentiable validation objectives via REINFORCE parallels sequence-level training in NMT.
- Technical soundness:
  - The objective and gradient estimator are standard (REINFORCE) with a variance-reduction baseline (EMA of rewards). The action space includes both architectural hyperparameters and set-valued skip-connection decisions; REINFORCE applies unchanged.
  - The tree-based parameterization for recurrent cells is coherent and can recover known cells as special cases while exploring a large space (~6 × 10^16).
  - The distributed, asynchronous training scheme is a reasonable engineering approach to reduce wall-clock time; it does not alter the estimator’s unbiasedness (given baseline independence from current actions).
  - The search protocol uses a separate validation set, and final test performance is reported only for the best validation model, reducing test-set overfitting risk.
- Evidence supporting claims:
  - CIFAR-10: Found convnets match or surpass many human-designed models; with pooling and increased width, test error reaches 3.65%. The method sensibly exploits skip connections; perturbation experiments (dense/remove skips) show degradation, suggesting a meaningful local optimum.
  - PTB: Discovered RNN cell outperforms strong LSTM baselines and other advanced recurrent architectures; speed advantage over RHN is noted due to fewer internal steps. Transfer to character PTB and GNMT yields improvements without retuning, supporting generality.
  - Control experiments: Random search baseline under identical evaluation budgets is inferior both in best and top-k averages, supporting the value of learned search. Expanding the search space with additional ops degrades minimally, indicating robustness.
- Clarity: The method is described in sufficient detail, including search spaces, controller architecture, training schedules, reward shaping, and distributed setup. Figures (referenced) and appendices reportedly illustrate discovered architectures.

Caveats (do not preclude acceptance but contextualize claims):
- Computational cost is very high (hundreds of GPUs/CPUs concurrently), which affects accessibility and reproducibility of the full search procedure.
- Some result improvements involve post-search width scaling and small grid searches on training hyperparameters; fair-compute comparisons to baselines should be carefully interpreted.
- Policy-gradient variance reduction is minimal (EMA baseline); discussion of stability, convergence diagnostics, or sensitivity to reward shaping could be expanded.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decisive)
- Report compute budgets clearly: total sampled architectures, average/total GPU/CPU hours, and energy estimates. Provide efficiency metrics (best-found performance vs. number of evaluated architectures).
- Provide variance over multiple search runs (different seeds) to quantify stability and expected performance vs. best-case outcomes.
- Expand ablations:
  - Effect of reward shaping (e.g., cubing accuracy, perplexity scaling) and entropy regularization on exploration.
  - Contribution of skip-connection selection vs. other space components; effect of zero-padding for concatenation.
  - Sensitivity to the depth-schedule curriculum for CIFAR-10.
- Comparisons under matched training budgets for child models vs. baselines (e.g., identical epochs, augmentations, and width) would sharpen fairness.
- Reproducibility: release search space definitions, controller code, and training scripts for the search, not just final architectures.
- Statistical rigor: report confidence intervals or multiple trials for final test results where feasible.

Questions for the authors
- Compute and efficiency:
  - What are the total GPU/CPU hours used for CIFAR-10 and PTB searches? How does validation performance improve as a function of evaluated architectures?
  - How many independent search runs were conducted, and what is the variance in best-found validation metrics?
- Reward shaping and optimization stability:
  - How sensitive are results to using accuracy^3 (CIFAR) or scaled perplexity^2 (PTB)? Did you try entropy bonuses, per-decision baselines, or advantage normalization?
  - Did asynchronous updates introduce noticeable non-stationarity or stale-gradient issues? Any mitigation beyond gradient accumulation?
- Search space and fairness:
  - For the best CIFAR-10 model, how much of the final gain comes from post-search width scaling and hyperparameter grid search vs. the discovered topology?
  - Are child-model training budgets (epochs/regularization) matched to those used by the strongest baselines (e.g., DenseNet variants)?
- Overfitting to validation:
  - Given repeated evaluation on the same validation split, did you monitor for overfitting of the controller to that split (e.g., through cross-validation or re-sampling)?
- Generality and transfer:
  - How often do discovered cells/topologies transfer well across datasets and scales without retuning? Any negative transfer cases observed?
- Alternatives:
  - Did you evaluate other credit assignment strategies (e.g., actor-critic, PPO) or differentiable relaxations (e.g., continuous relaxations of discrete ops) for improved sample efficiency?
