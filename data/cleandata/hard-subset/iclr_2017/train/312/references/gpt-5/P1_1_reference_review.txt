Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method that uses a controller RNN trained with policy gradients (REINFORCE) to generate variable-length strings that define neural network architectures. A sampled “child” architecture is instantiated, trained, and its validation accuracy is used as a reward to update the controller so it biases future samples toward better architectures. The approach supports rich search spaces, including convolutional architectures with skip connections and multiple layer types, and recurrent cell structures expressed as computation trees with learnable connection points for memory states. The paper also details a large-scale, asynchronous distributed training scheme that enables evaluating thousands of candidate architectures in parallel.

Empirically, NAS discovers:
- A competitive CIFAR-10 ConvNet (best test error 3.65% after post-search width scaling) comparable to or better than many human-designed architectures while being relatively fast.
- A novel RNN cell for PTB language modeling achieving state-of-the-art perplexity (as reported), and transferring favorably to character-level LM and to GNMT with modest BLEU gains.
- Control experiments show policy gradient outperforms random search and remains robust under an expanded search space.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Clear, impactful contribution: a flexible, end-to-end method that automatically discovers competitive architectures across modalities.
- Strong empirical results with careful baselines and controls, demonstrating both performance and generality (vision, word- and char-level LM, MT transfer).

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and scope: The formulation of architecture design as an autoregressive sequence decision problem optimized via RL is compelling and general. The set-selection attention for skip connections elegantly expands the search space.
- Empirical strength: Results on CIFAR-10 and PTB are competitive or SOTA at the time, with meaningful transfer to other tasks (char-LM, GNMT) without bespoke tuning.
- Methodological rigor: The paper uses an explicit baseline for variance reduction, a principled asynchronous training architecture, and includes a random search control and a search space expansion control.
- Practicality and scalability: The distributed parameter-server and replica setup shows the approach can exploit large compute effectively; the layer-depth schedule is a sensible heuristic for stabilizing search.
- Clarity of problem framing: The paper is well positioned relative to hyperparameter optimization, neuroevolution, and meta-learning, and motivates the RL approach for non-differentiable objectives.

Caveats (not decision-blocking):
- Compute intensity: The method relies on massive parallelism (hundreds of GPUs/CPUs; ~12.8k–15k architectures), limiting accessibility and reproducibility without substantial resources.
- Selection bias/overfitting to validation: Selecting a single best architecture after evaluating thousands on a small validation split risks overfitting; safeguards and analyses could be stronger.
- Post-search tuning and width scaling: The final CIFAR-10 number includes additional grid search and width scaling, complicating fairness relative to baselines; more standardized comparisons (params/FLOPs) would help.
- Ranking fidelity: Training each child for a fixed small number of epochs may imperfectly correlate with final performance; multi-fidelity or learning-curve-based corrections could further improve sample efficiency.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Report compute budget: Provide wall-clock time, total GPU/CPU hours, and cost-per-found-architecture. Include performance vs. compute curves to contextualize efficiency and facilitate fair comparison to alternative methods.
- Robustness to reward shaping: Justify cubing validation accuracy (and c·perplexity^2) and present sensitivity analyses to reward transformations and baseline smoothing. Consider adding entropy regularization details if used.
- Fairness of comparisons: For CIFAR-10, report parameter counts, FLOPs, and single-GPU inference throughput for your best model versus DenseNet/WRN under identical preprocessing/augmentation. Clarify the 1.05x speed claim.
- Validation protocol: Given 12.8k trials on a 5k validation split, quantify potential overfitting (e.g., by training the top N architectures on a fresh validation or via cross-validation), or use a larger validation set.
- Search space ablations: Provide ablations isolating contributions from: skip-connection mechanism, layer-type diversity, stride prediction, and padding heuristics for incompatible shapes.
- Ranking fidelity: Show correlation between early-stop performance and fully trained performance on a subset of architectures to validate the proxy; consider Hyperband-style multi-fidelity scheduling.
- Reproducibility: Release controller code and search-space specs (in addition to the found models/NASCell), including seeds and training scripts for child models.
- Analysis of discovered patterns: Offer qualitative/quantitative analyses of common motifs (e.g., prevalence of rectangular filters, skip-connection distributions, recurrent gate-like structures) to provide insight into what NAS learns.

Questions for the authors
1) Reward shaping: Why cube validation accuracy (and square scaled perplexity)? How sensitive is controller learning and final results to this choice and to the moving-average baseline hyperparameters?
2) Exploration control: Did you use entropy regularization or other exploration bonuses to prevent premature collapse of the controller’s distribution? If so, with what coefficients and schedules?
3) Ranking fidelity: What is the Spearman correlation between early validation performance (50 epochs CIFAR-10; 35 epochs PTB) and fully trained performance for a sampled subset?
4) Validation overfitting: Given 12.8k–15k trials on a 5k validation split, did you observe degradation when evaluating the top architectures on an unseen validation set? Any estimate of selection-induced bias?
5) Compute: What are the total GPU/CPU hours per experiment (including controller training and post-search tuning)? How does performance scale with fewer replicas or fewer evaluated architectures?
6) Comparisons: For the CIFAR-10 best model, can you report precise parameter counts, FLOPs, and standardized inference speed vs DenseNet/WRN on the same hardware?
7) Search space design: How often do “compilation failures” occur, and how much do the padding/concatenation heuristics affect search outcomes? Any bias toward architectures that avoid size-mismatch penalties?
8) PTB setup: For “shared embeddings,” dropout, and decay schedules, are these matched exactly to prior SOTA baselines? Can you provide a table aligning settings to each baseline?
9) Controller variants: Did you try alternative RL algorithms (e.g., advantage actor-critic) or variance-reduction techniques beyond an EMA baseline? Any observed benefits?
10) Transferability: Beyond PTB char-LM and GNMT, have you tested cross-dataset transfer for the CIFAR-10 cell/architecture (e.g., CIFAR-100, SVHN, small ImageNet) without re-searching?

Overall, this is a strong and influential paper that opens a new line of research. Addressing the above points would further strengthen its clarity, fairness, and reproducibility.
