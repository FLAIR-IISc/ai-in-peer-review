Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a reinforcement learning framework in which a controller RNN generates variable-length descriptions of neural network architectures. The controller is trained with REINFORCE to maximize the validation accuracy (or minimize perplexity) achieved by a “child” network built from the sampled architecture. The approach is flexible enough to express modern constructs such as skip connections via a set-selection attention mechanism and to generate recurrent cells using a compositional tree of operations with learned connections to cell states. The method is scaled using distributed, asynchronous parameter-server training with large parallelism.

Empirically, the paper reports:
- CIFAR-10: automatically discovered ConvNets with test error as low as 3.65%, competitive with strong human-designed models, and with favorable speed compared to DenseNet.
- Penn Treebank word-level LM: a novel cell achieving 62.4 test perplexity, improving prior SOTA by ~3.6 perplexity; the learned cell transfers to character-level PTB and GNMT (yielding +0.5 BLEU) without retuning the whole framework.
- Control studies: robustness to expanded search space (adding max, sin) and clear gains over random search under matched sampling budgets.

Overall, the paper presents a compelling and general framework with strong cross-domain results and novel technical ingredients.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Clear, well-motivated, and novel framework for variable-length architecture search that goes beyond fixed-length hyperparameter tuning; (2) Strong empirical results across vision and language with evidence of transfer, plus ablations vs. random search.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses the costly, expert-driven process of architecture design and frames it as sequence generation under non-differentiable rewards. The positioning relative to Bayesian optimization/hyperparameter tuning (fixed-length), neuroevolution (flexible but often inefficient), and program synthesis/meta-learning is appropriate and well argued.
- Method: Using an auto-regressive controller trained with REINFORCE is conceptually simple yet powerful. The set-selection attention for skip connections is a practical innovation that allows the search to recover residual/dense-like patterns. The recurrent cell search-space based on compositional trees with explicit ct, ct-1 injection is well designed and yields interpretable structures. The distributed asynchronous scheme is necessary and well described at a high level.
- Empirical support: Results are competitive to SOTA on CIFAR-10 and advance SOTA on PTB; transfer to character LM and GNMT further strengthens the case that the learned cell is broadly useful. The random search comparison is important and shows policy gradient learns better than naive sampling. The control experiment expanding the function set demonstrates some robustness to search space growth.
- Rigor and limitations: While the results are strong, several aspects temper the strength of the claims:
  - Compute cost/sample efficiency: The method relies on training up to 12,800 child models (CIFAR-10) to ~50 epochs, with up to 800 GPUs in parallel. This raises questions of practicality and reproducibility for typical labs. The paper would benefit from explicit reporting of GPU-days and wall-clock time, and analysis of sample efficiency.
  - Selection bias/statistical reporting: The test set result is reported for the single best validation model; there is limited reporting of variance across runs, correlation between validation during search and final test, or sensitivity to random seeds.
  - Fairness of comparisons: For CIFAR-10, after selecting an architecture the authors perform a grid search on training hyperparameters and even increase channel counts by +40 filters to reach the best 3.65% number. This post-hoc tuning complicates a clean comparison to baselines and to the “default” architecture discovered by NAS (without capacity upscaling).
  - Reward shaping and baselines: Cubing validation accuracy and using an EMA baseline are reasonable, but ablation on these choices (e.g., different exponents/normalizations, variance reduction methods) is limited.
  - Search space constraints: For ConvNets the search space is constrained (filter sizes, limited set of strides/pooling placements, padding rules when dimensions mismatch). These pragmatic choices are sensible, but the paper could better quantify how much of the final accuracy derives from the search space definition vs. the learning algorithm, e.g., via oracle/random search in the same space (partially addressed) and via alternative spaces.

Constructive feedback to improve the paper
- Report compute and efficiency: Provide detailed wall-clock time, GPU/CPU-days, and the number of architectures evaluated per task. Include an early-stopping/partial-training analysis showing rank correlation between early validation and full training to justify the 50-epoch choice.
- Statistical rigor: Report mean±std of test performance for the top-k architectures retrained with multiple seeds. Consider evaluating the top N architectures (not just the top-1) to reduce selection bias.
- Fair comparisons: Clearly separate the “as-searched” architecture’s performance from post-hoc capacity scaling and grid-searched training hyperparameters. When comparing speed vs. DenseNet, specify the metric (training time per epoch? inference FLOPs? hardware) and ensure equalized settings.
- Ablations: Evaluate the impact of (a) skip-connection attention (vs. simpler fixed patterns), (b) reward exponent and baseline choices, (c) search space breadth (e.g., adding 1x1 conv or bottlenecks), and (d) controller size and optimization hyperparameters on sample efficiency.
- Broader evaluation: Add results on CIFAR-100 or a subset of ImageNet to strengthen generality claims for ConvNets, even if with reduced budgets.
- Reproducibility: Release controller code and search spaces, or at least provide a detailed specification (schemas) for both ConvNet and RNN-cell search spaces and training pipelines to enable reproduction under smaller budgets.

Questions for the authors
- Compute budget: How many total GPU/CPU-days were used per task? What was the wall-clock search time for CIFAR-10 and PTB? How sensitive are results to reducing m, K, or training epochs per child?
- Early stopping: Did you assess the correlation between 50-epoch validation accuracy and final accuracy if trained longer? Could partial training significantly reduce the search cost without hurting rank ordering?
- Reward shaping: Why cube the validation accuracy (or square c/val perplexity)? Did you compare against linear or other normalizations? How much does this affect variance and sample efficiency?
- Baselines: For the random search baseline on PTB, did you apply the same post-selection grid search as for NAS? Did you evaluate Bayesian optimization in the same search space?
- Robustness: How stable are the best-found architectures across different random seeds of the controller and different validation splits?
- Search space details: For ConvNets with pooling/strides, how do you ensure spatial compatibility and what is the impact of your “compilation failure” fixes (e.g., defaulting to the input image, zero-padding) on final models?
- GNMT evaluation: Is the +0.5 BLEU improvement statistically significant across multiple runs? Are decoding and tokenization identical to the baseline?

This is an impactful and timely contribution that convincingly demonstrates the promise of learned architecture search. Addressing the above points would further strengthen its scientific rigor and practical accessibility.
