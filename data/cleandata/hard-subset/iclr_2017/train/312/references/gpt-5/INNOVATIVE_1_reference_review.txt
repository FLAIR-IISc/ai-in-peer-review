Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles the problem of automatically designing neural network architectures by learning a policy (a controller RNN) that emits architecture descriptions, trained with policy gradient (REINFORCE) using validation accuracy/perplexity as reward. The approach supports variable-length, structured architectures including skip connections and branching. It further extends to generate recurrent cells via a tree-structured parameterization that can incorporate memory (ct) akin to LSTMs.
- The method uses massive parallelism with asynchronous updates to make the expensive inner-loop (training child networks) tractable, and introduces practical engineering choices (reward shaping, moving-average baseline, set-selection attention for skip connections) to stabilize learning.
- Empirically, it discovers competitive-to-SOTA convolutional architectures on CIFAR-10 (down to 3.65% error after modest post-search tuning) and a novel recurrent cell that achieves state-of-the-art perplexity on PTB, with evidence of transfer to character-level PTB and GNMT (BLEU +0.5) without bespoke tuning. Control experiments show improvements over random search and robustness to expanded search spaces.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel and bold idea: learning to construct architectures end-to-end with RL over a rich, variable-length design space. This is a clear conceptual advance over fixed-length hyperparameter optimization and a pragmatic alternative to neuroevolution at scale.
- Strong empirical signal that the idea works across modalities (vision and language), with SOTA or near-SOTA performance and promising transfer, despite inevitable imperfections in methodology.

Supporting arguments
- Q1 (Problem): The paper focuses on automating architecture design, moving beyond fixed-size hyperparameter tuning to discover both topology and connectivity, and doing so in a way that can in principle generalize across tasks. This problem is important and widely felt in practice.
- Q2 (Motivation and placement): The work is well situated relative to Bayesian optimization, random search, neuroevolution, and meta-learning/program induction. The key differentiators are (i) variable-length, flexible search space, (ii) direct optimization of task metrics via policy gradient, and (iii) an explicit controller that improves over time rather than pure black-box search.
- Q3 (Support for claims): The empirical results are compelling: competitive CIFAR-10 performance with interpretable motifs (rectangular filters, skip connections), a novel RNN cell that beats strong PTB baselines, and transfer to new settings. The random-search comparison and ablations (stride/pooling, search-space variants) strengthen the case that the controller is learning a meaningful policy. While some details (e.g., fairness of tuning budgets across baselines, sensitivity to reward shaping) could be expanded, the breadth of results across tasks compensates.

Strengths
- High-impact conceptual contribution that launches a new direction (learning to design architectures).
- Technically coherent framework with practical components (REINFORCE with baseline, attention-based skip selection, distributed async training).
- Demonstrated generality to both CNNs and RNNs, plus cross-task transfer.
- Clear empirical gains over strong baselines; analysis of design choices (e.g., skip connections matter).
- Reproducibility intent (code release, NASCell inclusion) is commendable.

Weaknesses / concerns (not blocking)
- Extremely compute-intensive search (hundreds of GPUs/CPUs, thousands of trained models); sample efficiency is low, and reproducibility for typical labs is challenging.
- Some comparisons may not equalize post-search tuning budgets between discovered architectures and baselines; grid search on discovered models may confer an advantage.
- Reward shaping (e.g., accuracy^3; perplexity-based scaling) and validation protocol may influence learning dynamics and risk overfitting to a single validation split.
- The controller training details (e.g., staleness in async updates, sensitivity to baseline/exploration schedule) could use deeper analysis.
- Search space design still encodes many choices; the impact of those priors is not fully quantified.

Suggestions to improve the paper (for the camera-ready; helpful, not central to my decision)
- Report compute budgets transparently: total child model trainings, GPU/CPU hours, and cost per 1% improvement; include a smaller-budget variant to guide practitioners.
- Sensitivity analyses: effect of reward shaping (power/exponent), validation split seeds, controller learning rate and baseline, and the schedule for depth expansion.
- Fairness of baselines: confirm that comparable hyperparameter tuning budgets are used for non-NAS baselines; consider reporting untuned performance of the discovered architectures as well.
- Provide more systematic ablations: skip-connection selection vs. no-skips; stride/pooling choices; compilation-failure heuristics; effect of using different base numbers for RNN cells.
- Discuss scalability to larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) with a realistic plan; consider weight-sharing or early stopping proxies to reduce cost (even if left for future work).
- Expand the qualitative analysis of discovered motifs and their prevalence; are there consistent patterns the controller rediscovers across runs?

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the reward shaping (e.g., accuracy cubed on CIFAR-10; the choice of c on PTB)? Did you try linear reward or different exponents?
- What measures did you take to avoid overfitting to a particular validation split during search? Any results with multiple splits or cross-validation?
- Can you quantify total compute (GPU/CPU hours) and the marginal utility of additional sampled architectures (e.g., performance vs. number of evaluations)?
- In the random-search comparison, were the number of evaluations, training budgets, and post-search tuning identical?
- How robust is controller learning under different levels of asynchrony/staleness in gradient updates?
- Did you attempt controller transfer (pretraining on CIFAR-10, finetuning on another vision dataset; similarly for RNN cells)? Any evidence of search-policy generalization?
- How often do “compilation failures” occur, and how much do the heuristic fixes (zero-padding, auto-connections) bias the search?
- For the RNN cell search, how does performance vary with the base number (e.g., 4, 8, 16)? Is the discovered cell stable across random seeds?
- What would be required to extend the search to include attention modules or normalization choices in the RNN setting?

Overall, this work is bold, timely, and opens a path that is already showing strong promise. I recommend acceptance.
