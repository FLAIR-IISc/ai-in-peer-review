Summary
This paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a reinforcement-learning-based framework that uses a controller RNN to generate variable-length descriptions of neural network architectures. The controller samples hyperparameters (e.g., filter sizes, connections, layer types) to instantiate a “child” network, which is trained and evaluated; the validation metric serves as the reward to update the controller via REINFORCE. The method scales via distributed, asynchronous training and introduces mechanisms to (i) predict skip connections through a set-selection attention and (ii) search over recurrent cell structures (with cell/memory wiring). Empirically, NAS discovers competitive-to-state-of-the-art convolutional architectures on CIFAR-10 (down to 3.65% error with modest inference cost) and a novel RNN cell outperforming LSTMs on Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity), with transfer benefits to character LM and GNMT. The paper positions NAS relative to hyperparameter optimization, neuroevolution, program synthesis, and meta-learning, and includes comparisons to random search.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: The paper introduces a bold, general, and flexible framework that expands the scope of automated architecture design to variable-length, compositional spaces and demonstrates strong empirical results across vision and language. The conceptual advance and breadth of evidence outweigh concerns about computational cost and some experimental limitations.

Supporting arguments
Strengths
- Novelty and scope: Moving from fixed-length hyperparameter tuning to end-to-end, variable-length architecture generation (including skip connections and recurrent cell structures) is a substantial conceptual advance. The set-selection mechanism for skip connections is elegant and general.
- General framework: The approach is not tied to a specific domain; it handles both conv nets and recurrent cells, and shows transfer across tasks (character LM, MT).
- Empirical impact: Results are on-par or better than strong human-designed baselines in two canonical benchmarks, with additional transfer to GNMT without substantial retuning.
- Comparative evidence: Includes a random-search control, and some robustness checks (expanded function space).
- Practicality of outputs: Providing the discovered NAS cell in TensorFlow and releasing code for child models increases impact.

Weaknesses
- Computational budget/reproducibility: The method relies on massive parallelism (up to 800 GPUs for CIFAR and 400 CPUs for PTB) and evaluates tens of thousands of models. This raises questions about accessibility, environmental/monetary cost, and whether the improvement is proportional to the search cost. The paper would benefit from budget-vs-performance tradeoff analysis and smaller-budget regimes.
- Fairness and rigor of baselines: While random search is a good baseline, other competitive AutoML methods (e.g., modern BO/BOHB, SMAC, evolutionary strategies) are not directly compared under similar budgets. The CIFAR-10 SOTA comparisons vary in parameterization and training recipes (e.g., post-hoc adding 40 filters, grid-searching training hyperparameters); exact compute/parameter/FLOPs matching is limited.
- Reward shaping and training protocol: Using cubed validation accuracy and fixed short training budgets (50 epochs CIFAR, 35 epochs PTB) can bias selection toward fast-learning architectures rather than best final performance. It would help to quantify the correlation between early reward and final accuracy, or adopt multi-fidelity/promotion strategies.
- Potential overfitting to validation: Reusing a single 5k validation split across many trials can induce overfitting to the validation set. There is no discussion of cross-validation or repeated splits.
- Clarity: Calling the approach “gradient-based” could be misleading; the method uses policy gradients on the controller but optimizes a non-differentiable objective over discrete architectures.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Add a systematic analysis of search efficiency: performance as a function of architectures evaluated, controller updates, and compute budget. Include variance across runs.
- Strengthen baselines: Compare against a contemporary evolutionary algorithm and a strong BO method, matched for wall-clock and model evaluations.
- Report detailed compute: total GPU/CPU hours, energy budget, and per-architecture training cost; also report FLOPs/params and inference latency for discovered models when comparing to DenseNet/WRN.
- Validate robustness to validation overfitting: use multiple validation splits or nested evaluation, and report performance stability.
- Ablations: quantify the contribution of skip-connection selection, the depth-schedule curriculum, reward shaping (cubic), and controller architecture/optimizer choices.
- Discuss search-space design: how much of the performance comes from the breadth of the space (e.g., inclusion of pooling/stride) versus the search algorithm; provide statistics on sampled architectural features.

Questions for the authors
- Compute/budget: What were the total GPU/CPU hours for CIFAR-10 and PTB searches? How does test performance scale with 10%, 25%, 50% of the reported budget?
- Validation overfitting: Did you observe drift between validation improvements during search and final test accuracy? Any experiments with multiple validation splits?
- Early-stopping bias: How well does the cubed validation accuracy at 50 epochs correlate with final accuracy if models are trained to full convergence?
- Baselines: Can you provide results for a competitive evolutionary method and a BO baseline under matched evaluation budgets and wall-clock?
- Search-space sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the choice of candidate filter sizes, number of filters, and allowed operations? What happens if you enlarge/restrict the space?
- Skip connections: Can you ablate the set-selection mechanism and compare to fixed dense or residual connectivity at equal parameter counts?
- Controller stability: How many independent controller runs did you perform? What is the variance in best-found performance across runs?
- Generalization: Beyond PTB and CIFAR-10, did you attempt CIFAR-100 or a medium-scale ImageNet proxy to test transfer of discovered cells/blocks?

Overall, despite computational intensity and some missing ablations, this is a bold and important direction with compelling evidence across tasks, meriting acceptance.
