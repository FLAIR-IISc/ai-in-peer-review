Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a reinforcement-learning-based framework where a controller RNN (an LSTM) generates variable-length descriptions of neural network architectures. The controller is trained with REINFORCE using the validation accuracy (or a function of validation perplexity) of the corresponding “child network” as reward. The search space includes convolutional architectures with skip connections (via a set-selection attention) and recurrent cell structures represented as compositional trees, enabling discovery of novel ConvNet topologies for CIFAR-10 and novel RNN cells for PTB. The method scales via massive parallelism (asynchronous parameter servers; hundreds of replicas). Empirically, the approach finds competitive-to-SOTA CIFAR-10 models and a new recurrent cell that outperforms LSTM variants on PTB and transfers to character-level language modeling and NMT with modest improvements. The idea of using an autoregressive controller to synthesize architectures directly is compelling, flexible, and (potentially) broadly impactful.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Methodological clarity and correctness issues (reward definition for PTB appears sign-inverted; multiple ad-hoc fixes for invalid architectures; inconsistent search spaces across experiments) undermine confidence that the reported gains are attributable to the proposed method without confounds.
2) Reproducibility and rigor concerns (heavy reliance on extreme compute, insufficient ablations, ambiguous training/augmentation details, inconsistent and error-prone presentation and references).

Supporting arguments
- Reward formulation inconsistency: For PTB the reward is stated as c(validation perplexity)^2 while the controller maximizes expected reward; this would favor higher perplexity unless a negative sign or inverse is used. The paper does not specify a negative sign or any transformation ensuring minimization. This is a critical issue requiring correction/clarification.
- Handling invalid architectures: The controller’s skip-connection mechanism can generate incompatible graphs. The paper “rescues” invalid networks by (i) feeding the raw image when a layer has no inputs, (ii) concatenating any unconsumed outputs at the end, and (iii) zero-padding to force shape compatibility. These heuristics meaningfully change the semantics of candidate architectures, bias the search, and confound credit assignment (the reward no longer faithfully reflects the sampled program). No ablation quantifies their effect.
- Inconsistent and constrained search spaces: CIFAR experiments vary whether strides, pooling, and filter set are included; in one setting, pooling is allowed only at fixed layers 13 and 24. The final reported best CIFAR model further adds 40 channels post hoc (outside the original search space). This makes cross-condition comparisons hard and weakens the claim that the controller alone discovers the best-performing architecture.
- Comparison fairness and reporting: On CIFAR-10, the text alternates between benchmarks of 3.74% and 3.46% for DenseNet; the claim “1.05x faster while having better performance” contradicts later acknowledgement that 3.46% is lower than 3.65%. “1.05x faster” (5%) is a marginal speedup; measurement methodology is unspecified (throughput, latency, FLOPs?). PTB baselines: the paper grid-searches hyperparameters for the NAS cell but does not indicate comparable re-tuning of LSTM baselines under the identical regularization/data pipeline.
- Compute budget and reproducibility: The method evaluates 12,800 CIFAR architectures for 50 epochs each, with up to 800 concurrent GPUs, and 15,000 RNN cells on 400 CPUs. This scale is beyond most labs; there is no analysis of sample efficiency vs. simpler alternatives (e.g., evolution, Bayesian optimization) under equal compute, beyond a single random search curve. No evaluation of stability across seeds or number of trials is provided.
- Ambiguous or nonstandard training details: CIFAR preprocessing describes “upsample then choose a random 32×32 crop,” which is atypical compared to the standard pad-and-crop regime; exact upsampling factor is unspecified. Reward “maximum validation accuracy of last 5 epochs cubed” is ad hoc without justification or sensitivity analysis. The schedule “increase depth by 2 every 1,600 samples” is ambiguous (samples = architectures? minibatches?). Asynchronous update details omit how stale gradients are handled or whether entropy regularization is used.
- Notation and presentation quality: Numerous typos and inconsistencies: “one a time,” “on the on the,” “hiddenstate,” “server” vs “sever,” “an WMT14,” etc. Equations use “θcJ(θc)” and “5θc” instead of ∇θc; baselines notated loosely; probability notation P(a1:T; θc) lacks factorization clarity in places. The references section contains many malformed entries (duplicate DenseNet citations, missing venues/years, BibTeX artifacts such as “\Q2016\E”), which falls short of ICLR standards.
- Limited ablations: Important design choices (EMA baseline, skip-connection attention, zero-padding, concatenation policy, reward shaping, controller size) lack ablations. The “max” and “sin” control is interesting but insufficient.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all are decision-critical)
- Correct the PTB reward sign and report sensitivity to the reward transformation (squared vs. linear vs. inverse; with/without baselines; entropy regularization).
- Provide ablations isolating the effect of the “rescue” heuristics for invalid graphs. Consider rejecting invalid samples and learning feasibility constraints, or using graph-normalizing transformations that preserve semantics.
- Normalize comparisons under fixed compute budgets and include stronger baselines such as evolution strategies and modern BO for architectures (with variable-length encodings).
- Clarify and standardize CIFAR preprocessing and training schedules; report exact augmentation pipeline, learning rate decay epochs, and whether cutout or label smoothing are used. Report multiple runs with mean/std.
- Report search efficiency metrics: best/median reward vs. number of evaluated architectures; controller learning curves; entropy over actions; qualitative analysis of learned priors.
- Use FLOPs/params/throughput for speed comparisons, and ensure apples-to-apples training and inference settings.
- Improve notation, fix typos, and clean up references; use consistent math symbols and proper bibliographic formatting.
- Make code available for the controller and for reproducing both search and final training, or at least provide full configuration files.

Questions for the authors
1) PTB reward: Is the reward negative perplexity or its inverse? Please specify the exact transformation and constant c, and provide evidence that conclusions are unaffected by this choice.
2) Invalid architectures: What fraction of sampled models trigger each “rescue” rule? How do results change if invalid samples are penalized or rejected?
3) CIFAR preprocessing: What does “upsample then random 32×32 crop” mean exactly (factor, interpolation)? How does performance change with the standard pad-and-crop?
4) Post hoc channel scaling: Was the “+40 filters” model rediscovered if allowed in the search? If not, how much of the gain is attributable to this manual modification?
5) Compute accounting: What is the total wall-clock and GPU/CPU hours per dataset? How would performance degrade if restricted to, say, 8–16 GPUs?
6) Baseline tuning: Were LSTM/RHN/DenseNet baselines (re-)tuned under your pipeline and compute? Please report matched training recipes and multiple seeds.
7) Controller training: Did you use entropy regularization or any exploration bonus? How sensitive are results to controller size and EMA baseline momentum?
8) Shape handling: Zero-padding for concatenation can inflate compute and alter statistics. Did you evaluate alternatives (e.g., projection via 1×1 conv) and their impact on search quality?
