Summary
This paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), using a controller RNN trained with policy gradient to generate variable-length architectures for CNNs (CIFAR-10) and RNN cells (PTB). The controller samples architectural decisions; a “child” network is trained, its validation score serves as reward, and REINFORCE updates the controller. The search space includes convolutional hyperparameters and skip connections via a set-selection attention; for RNNs, the controller labels a computation tree reminiscent of LSTM gates. Empirically, NAS finds a 39-layer CNN reaching 3.65% error on CIFAR-10 and an RNN cell achieving 62.4 perplexity on PTB, with signs of transfer to character PTB and GNMT. The work aims to show that gradient-based meta-learning can discover competitive architectures from scratch.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Serious clarity and reproducibility issues: critical training/search details are ambiguous or missing, and results rely on massive compute with no viable path for replication. (2) Insufficient scientific rigor and controls: several ad-hoc heuristics in the search space, limited ablations, and weak statistical analysis undermine confidence in the claimed advantages over strong baselines.

Supporting arguments
- Ambiguity in reward definitions and optimization objectives:
  - CIFAR-10 uses “maximum validation accuracy of the last 5 epochs cubed” as reward; PTB uses “c(validation perplexity)2.” As written, squaring perplexity would increase the reward with worse models unless negated; the sign and exact transformation are not specified. This is central to the algorithm and must be unambiguous.
  - “Trained to convergence” is stated repeatedly, but child models are trained for a fixed number of epochs (50 on CIFAR-10, 35 on PTB); “convergence” is a mischaracterization that matters for the legitimacy of the reward signal.
- Reproducibility and compute:
  - The method depends on 800 GPUs for CIFAR-10 and 400 CPUs for PTB, with 12,800–15,000 architectures evaluated. There is no report of total GPU/CPU days, wall-clock time, or resource variability. Only final models are promised to be released, not the search code, making the central contribution (the search procedure) hard to reproduce.
  - Many hyperparameters are tuned via a post-hoc grid search on the selected architectures, conflating gains due to architecture vs. training. No repeated runs or confidence intervals are reported.
- Search space design and ad-hoc fixes:
  - Skip connections selected by independent sigmoids generate variable fan-in without constraint; “compilation failures” are handled by ad-hoc heuristics (injecting the image as input if none selected; concatenating all unused outputs at the end; zero-padding to match shapes). These alter the effective network topology in ways not attributed to the controller decisions and may inflate performance in uncontrolled ways.
  - When adding pooling, the search uses 13 “blocks” of 3 layers each and shrinks filter choices, changing the search space substantially. This complicates comparisons across settings.
  - The schedule that increases depth every 1,600 samples imposes a curriculum that could bias the controller; no ablation shows its effect.
- Comparisons and rigor:
  - Claims of speed-ups or superiority vs DenseNet/RHN are not controlled for identical training budgets, parameter counts, or data augmentation. The 3.65% result is contrasted with DenseNet 3.74% while acknowledging 3.46% is achievable with 1x1 convs; speed claims lack standardized hardware/time reporting.
  - Random search control is only referenced via a figure; quantitative outcomes and statistical tests are absent. No variance across seeds is provided.
  - For PTB, reward shaping, controller hyperparameters, and regularization schemes (dropouts, shared embeddings) are mixed; it’s unclear which components drive improvements. The “more than two times faster” claim vs RHN is not substantiated with matched configurations.
- Presentation and formatting:
  - Numerous typos and inconsistencies: repeated words (“on the on the”), “hiddenstate,” misuse of symbols (∗ for multiplication, 5θc for gradient), missing/incorrect math formatting, and unbalanced notation.
  - References are heavily malformed (duplicates, “?, \Q2016\E,” missing venues/authors ordering), which is below ICLR standards and impedes verification.
  - Some claims are imprecise or overstated (e.g., “gradient-based method for finding architectures” while optimizing a non-differentiable reward via REINFORCE; fair but potentially misleading phrasing).

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Precisely define reward functions for all tasks (including sign, normalization, and any baselines/advantages) and provide an algorithm box with full training details (controller optimizer, entropy regularization, gradient clipping, baseline EMA parameters, batch sizes, update rules under asynchrony).
- Report full compute budgets: number of models evaluated, epochs per model, average training time per child, total GPU/CPU hours, controller wall-clock convergence, and hardware specifics. Report sensitivity to m, K, S, and update staleness.
- Provide ablations:
  - Effect of reward shaping (cubic accuracy, squared perplexity), EMA baseline, entropy bonus.
  - Effect of the depth-increase schedule.
  - Impact of skip-connection heuristics (image injection, end concatenation, zero-padding) and constraints on fan-in/out.
  - Comparisons to strong non-RL baselines (e.g., TPE/SMAC/BOHB) under matched evaluation budgets.
  - Influence of post-hoc hyperparameter tuning vs. architecture selection.
- Improve comparisons: standardize training pipelines across baselines, report parameter counts, FLOPs, and throughput in a consistent environment. Include multiple runs with mean±std and statistical tests.
- Clarify search spaces, especially when pooling is allowed and when “blocks” are used, and motivate these choices.
- Fix all formatting and reference issues per ICLR style. Provide figures/tables with sufficient detail to assess the results without ambiguity.
- Release, at minimum, a minimal reproducible search implementation with a small-scale search space so the core method can be tested without industrial compute.

Questions for the authors
1) What exact reward formulas were used on PTB and CIFAR-10 (including sign, scaling constants, and any normalization)? Was any entropy regularization used?
2) How many total GPU/CPU hours were consumed for CIFAR-10 and PTB? What is the average training time per child model?
3) How sensitive are the results to the curriculum of increasing depth and to the “compilation failure” heuristics? Can you provide ablations?
4) Are validation sets augmented or not? Is early stopping allowed using validation? How do you prevent leakage during the post-hoc hyperparameter grid search?
5) What is the variance across independent searches (different seeds) in terms of best validation/test performance?
6) How do independent sigmoid samples for skip connections avoid degenerate fan-in/out? Did you cap the number of inputs per layer?
7) For the DenseNet and RHN comparisons, can you provide matched training budgets, parameter counts, FLOPs, and wall-clock times on the same hardware?
8) Will you release the controller training code and configurations necessary to reproduce the search, beyond final architectures?
