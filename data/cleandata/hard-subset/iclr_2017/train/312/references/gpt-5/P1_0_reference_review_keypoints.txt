- Proposal: Introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a controller RNN trained with REINFORCE to generate variable-length neural architectures.  
- Novelty: Supports modern constructs (skip connections via set-selection attention, compositional RNN cell trees) and scales with distributed asynchronous training.  
- CIFAR-10 result: Found ConvNets achieving test error as low as 3.65%, competitive with strong human-designed models and faster than DenseNet in reported settings.  
- PTB result: Discovered an RNN cell giving 62.4 test perplexity, ~3.6 perplexity better than prior SOTA, and transferable to character PTB and GNMT (+0.5 BLEU).  
- Robustness: Shows gains over random search under matched sampling budgets and some robustness to expanded search space (adding max, sin).  
- Overall judgment: Accept — clear, well-motivated, and novel framework with strong cross-domain empirical support and useful ablations.  
- Motivation strength: Frames architecture design as sequence generation under non-differentiable rewards and positions work well relative to existing methods.  
- Method strength: Auto-regressive controller with REINFORCE is simple yet effective; set-selection attention and compositional cell search-space are practical innovations.  
- Empirical strength: Competitive SOTA results, transferability of learned cells, and control experiments that support effectiveness.  
- Compute cost concern: Method required massive resources (up to ~12,800 child trainings, ~800 GPUs); practicality and reproducibility for typical labs are questionable.  
- Statistical/reporting concern: Results report only the best validation-selected test model with limited variance reporting, seed sensitivity, or validation-test correlation analysis.  
- Fairness concern: Post-hoc tuning (grid search, +40 filters) on selected architectures complicates fair comparison to baselines and the “as-searched” model.  
- Reward/baseline choices: Use of cubed validation accuracy and EMA baseline lacks ablation; impact on variance and sample efficiency is underexplored.  
- Search space constraints: ConvNet search space has pragmatic limits (filter sizes, strides, padding fixes); unclear how much gains come from search space design vs. controller learning.  
- Request compute details: Paper should report wall-clock time, GPU/CPU-days, architectures evaluated, and sensitivity to search hyperparameters.  
- Request statistical rigor: Recommend reporting mean±std for top-k retrains, multiple seeds, and evaluating top N architectures to reduce selection bias.  
- Request fair comparisons: Separate performance of the “as-searched” architecture from post-hoc capacity scaling and clearly specify speed/computation metrics when comparing to DenseNet.  
- Ablation suggestions: Evaluate impact of skip-attention, reward exponent/baseline choices, broader search spaces (1x1 conv, bottlenecks), and controller hyperparameters on sample efficiency.  
- Broader evaluation: Add CIFAR-100 or reduced-budget ImageNet experiments to strengthen generality claims for ConvNets.  
- Reproducibility: Release controller code and detailed search-space specifications or schemas to enable reproduction under smaller budgets.  
- Questions for authors — compute: How many total GPU/CPU-days per task and wall-clock search time for CIFAR-10 and PTB? Sensitivity to m, K, or training epochs?  
- Questions for authors — early stopping: Is there correlation between 50-epoch validation and final accuracy; can partial training reduce search cost?  
- Questions for authors — reward shaping: Why cube validation accuracy (square c/val perplexity); comparisons to linear/other normalizations?  
- Questions for authors — baselines: Did random-search baselines receive the same post-selection tuning; was Bayesian optimization considered?  
- Questions for authors — robustness and search-space mechanics: Stability across controller seeds/validation splits and handling of spatial compatibility/compilation failures?  
- Questions for authors — GNMT: Is the +0.5 BLEU improvement statistically significant and were decoding/tokenization settings identical to baseline?
