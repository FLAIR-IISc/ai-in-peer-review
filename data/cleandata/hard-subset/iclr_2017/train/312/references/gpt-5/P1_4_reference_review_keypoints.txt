- Proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS) using a controller RNN trained with policy gradient to generate variable-length neural architectures.  
- Controller outputs sequences specifying layer types and hyperparameters; sampled child networks are trained and their validation accuracy used as reward.  
- Extends search space with set-selection attention for skip connections and supports predicting other layer types and learning rates.  
- Generalizes to sequence models by generating recurrent cell computation trees and injection points for cell states.  
- Uses a large-scale asynchronous distributed setup to train many child models in parallel to speed controller learning.  
- Achieves competitive CIFAR-10 results (4.47% and 3.65% after capacity scaling) and strong PTB perplexities (62.4) with transfer to character LM and GNMT.  
- Control experiments show improvements over random search and robustness to larger search spaces.  
- Decision: Accept based on novelty, motivation, and strong empirical gains across tasks.  
- Methodology uses standard REINFORCE with moving-average baseline; includes thoughtful design choices like set-selection attention and cell tree parameterization.  
- Empirical validation is solid with ablations (e.g., skip connection effects) and speed/parameter trade-off discussions versus DenseNet.  
- Learned recurrent cell outperforms LSTM baselines and is computationally efficient per time step.  
- Random-search comparisons show policy gradient improves both best-of-run and top-k averages.  
- Paper provides many training and engineering details, and promises code release and TensorFlow integration.  
- Main weakness: massive compute used (e.g., 800 GPUs for CIFAR), requiring clearer total compute accounting and fairer budgeted comparisons.  
- Needs more sensitivity analyses: validation split choice, reward shaping, entropy regularization, and controller size effects.  
- Request correlation analysis between early-stopped child performance and final accuracy to justify early stopping.  
- Should report variance across multiple NAS runs and stability of best-found architectures.  
- Comparisons should control for parameter count and FLOPs and clarify impact of post-hoc capacity scaling on final numbers.  
- Search space design needs discussion of induced biases (e.g., set-selection, base-8 tree) and ablations on alternative encodings.  
- Clarify proportion and impact of compilation-failure cases and any padding/compatibility fixes on search efficiency.  
- Reproducibility asks for full hyperparameter summaries, augmentation/training schedules, controller checkpoints, and released code.  
- Specific questions asked: total GPU/CPU hours, sensitivity to K and m, details on random search baseline parity, and justification for reward-shaping choices.  
- Also asks for correlation stats for early stopping, validation split stability tests, GNMT complexity/tuning details, and fraction of architectures needing fixes.
