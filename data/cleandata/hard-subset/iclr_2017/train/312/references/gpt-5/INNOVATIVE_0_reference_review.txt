Summary and contributions
This paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a reinforcement-learning-based approach that uses a recurrent “controller” to generate variable-length descriptions of neural network architectures. The controller samples architectures, trains the corresponding “child” models, and uses their validation accuracy as a reward to update itself via REINFORCE. The search space includes convolutional nets with skip connections and other layer types, and an extended scheme generates recurrent cell structures as computation trees with memory injection. A large-scale distributed, asynchronous setup accelerates training, enabling evaluation of thousands of models.

Empirically, the method discovers:
- A CIFAR-10 convolutional architecture competitive with top human-designed models (down to 3.65% error after modest capacity scaling) and with favorable speed relative to DenseNet.
- A recurrent cell for PTB that improves perplexity over strong LSTM/RHN baselines and transfers to character-level LM and GNMT with gains, suggesting generality.
- Policy gradient outperforms random search under the authors’ setup; control experiments modestly expand the function set without collapsing performance.

Overall, the paper claims a general, gradient-based controller that learns to compose neural architectures end-to-end, achieving state-of-the-art or near-SOTA across modalities, and opening a new direction for automatic architecture design.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong novelty and bold scope: a unified, learnable generator for variable-length CNN and RNN architectures using RL, with skip-connection selection and cell construction.
- Convincing empirical wins across tasks and evidence of transfer; the approach clearly advances the field and catalyzes a new research direction.

Supporting arguments
- Conceptual advance: Moving beyond fixed-length hyperparameter optimization to a learned, autoregressive generator of architectures is a significant leap. The set-selection attention for skip connections and tree-based RNN cell construction are elegant, general mechanisms.
- Generality: The same controller framework spans CNNs and RNN cells; the discovered RNN cell transfers to character LM and NMT with minimal retuning.
- Empirical strength: Results on CIFAR-10 and PTB are highly competitive; the PTB gains are notable given the maturity of that benchmark. The small but positive GNMT BLEU improvement without retuning underscores practicality.
- Search dynamics: The controller improves over time, surpassing random search. Use of an EMA baseline, reward shaping, and asynchronous distributed updates show sound engineering to make REINFORCE workable at scale.
- Clarity and impact: The paper is clearly written, situates itself among hyperparameter optimization, neuroevolution, and meta-learning, and promises code release/NASCell availability.

Concerns and limitations (not blocking)
- Compute intensity and accessibility: The reported setup (up to 800 concurrent GPUs for CNNs and 400 CPUs for RNNs) is far beyond most academic labs. This raises questions about democratization and reproducibility.
- Fairness and reproducibility of comparisons: 
  - The best CIFAR-10 model involves post-search capacity scaling (+40 filters) and a small grid search; comparisons to DenseNet could better control for parameter count/FLOPs and training budgets.
  - For PTB, details on matched parameter counts and exact regularization settings versus baselines should be fully standardized.
- Search space handcrafting: Some choices (e.g., fixing pooling at specific layers in one setting, limiting combination/activation sets) introduce designer bias and complicate attribution of gains.
- Overfitting to validation through massive search: Evaluating 12.8k architectures on a 5k-example validation split could induce selection bias. A statistical analysis of performance inflation would strengthen claims.
- Baselines: While random search is included, stronger black-box optimizers (e.g., Bayesian optimization over a comparable, fixed-structure relaxation; evolutionary strategies with similar budgets) are not comprehensively compared.
- Methodological details: Reward shaping (accuracy^3), depth-scheduling of child models, and asynchronous update aggregation may materially affect sample efficiency; more ablations would help.

Suggestions to improve the paper (for the camera-ready; not central to the accept decision)
- Report compute budgets and efficiency: wall-clock time, total GPU/CPU days per benchmark, number of controller updates, and sample efficiency curves (best vs. top-k averages over samples).
- Fairness controls: Compare against baselines under matched parameter/FLOP constraints and identical training pipelines; report parameter counts and computational costs for all models.
- Robustness: Show sensitivity to the validation split (e.g., multiple random splits), multiple controller seeds, and the effect of reward shaping and baseline choices.
- Ablations: 
  - Effect of skip-connection selection, layer-type prediction, and depth scheduling on final performance.
  - Impact of search space choices (e.g., allowing strides/pooling positions vs. fixing them) on both best-found performance and search difficulty.
- Multi-fidelity and early-stopping: Consider partial-training proxies or adaptive resource allocation to reduce the massive computational burden; even preliminary analysis would guide future work.
- Broader baselines: Include Bayesian optimization and at least one evolutionary method with comparable compute, to strengthen the case that policy gradient is the right choice here.
- Analysis of discovered architectures: Provide insights into common motifs learned by the controller (e.g., filter shapes, skip patterns, cell substructures) to aid interpretability and reuse.

Questions for the authors
- Compute and sample efficiency: What are the total GPU/CPU days per benchmark? How many controller parameter updates were performed, and how does performance scale with the number of sampled architectures?
- Validation selection bias: Did you assess the “winner’s curse” from selecting the best of 12.8k models on a 5k validation set (e.g., via bootstrapping or repeated splits)?
- Baselines: How were compute budgets matched for random search? Can you provide results with a strong Bayesian optimization baseline over a comparable space?
- Reward shaping: Why cube the validation accuracy for CIFAR-10? How sensitive is the controller’s progress to this choice and to the EMA baseline’s timescale?
- Search space constraints: In the CIFAR-10 experiments with pooling at fixed layers, why those positions? How much does allowing pooling positions to be learned change outcomes and sample complexity?
- Reproducibility: How stable are the discovered architectures across seeds? Do multiple runs converge to similar motifs?
- GNMT transfer: Did you explore modest retuning around the found cell in GNMT? Are the gains consistent across training runs?

Given its originality, breadth, and strong empirical results despite some heavy compute and comparison limitations, I recommend acceptance.
