Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a controller RNN trained with policy gradients (REINFORCE) to generate variable-length neural network architectures. The controller samples an architecture, a child model is trained, and its validation performance serves as a reward to update the controller.
- The method supports rich search spaces: convolutional networks with skip connections and other layer types; and recurrent cells specified as computation trees with choices over combination and activation functions, including where to inject and read from cell states.
- Large-scale, asynchronous distributed training is used to make the search practical, with tens to hundreds of replicas training child models in parallel.
- Empirically, NAS finds competitive-to-SOTA CNNs on CIFAR-10 and a novel RNN cell that improves SOTA perplexity on PTB, with evidence of transfer to character-level PTB and GNMT with no bespoke tuning.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) Clear, well-motivated contribution: a flexible, general, and effective method for learning architectures, including variable-length and connectivity patterns, with strong empirical successes across vision and language.
2) Strong evidence and breadth: convincing results on two canonical benchmarks, a credible comparison to random search, and demonstrations of transferability.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Automating neural architecture design beyond fixed-length hyperparameters. This is timely and important, moving the field from hand-designed architectures to learned ones.
- Motivation and placement: The method is well positioned relative to hyperparameter optimization (TPE/BO), neuroevolution, and meta-learning. It addresses the fixed-length limitation of BO and the scalability/practicality limits of evolutionary approaches by using a trainable controller with policy gradients over variable-length action sequences. The use of attention-like set selection for skip connections is a neat design to enable complex topologies.
- Claims supported:
  • CIFAR-10: The controller explores a meaningful search space (filters, skip connections, strides/pooling), finds a 39-layer model achieving 4.47% test error, further improved to 3.65% by scaling filters, competitive with DenseNet. Ablations about skip connections (remove/densify) show the learned topology matters. Training protocol and comparisons are largely in line with contemporaneous work, and the “test once” protocol mitigates test overfitting.
  • PTB: The learned cell outperforms strong LSTM baselines by ~3.6 perplexity. The search space is extremely large (~6e16), yet policy gradient outperforms random search over 15k samples, with curves showing advantage in both best and average-of-top models. Transfer experiments to character PTB and GNMT indicate the learned cell generalizes, which strengthens the claim that NAS discovers reusable primitives.
  • Methodological correctness: The REINFORCE update with a moving-average baseline is standard; the distributed async training scheme is sensible and clearly described. Heuristics for handling invalid graphs are documented.

Caveats (not fatal, but important):
- Compute cost and accessibility: The approach relies on massive parallelism (up to 800 GPUs for CIFAR, 400 CPUs for PTB), evaluating 12.8k–15k models. The paper would benefit from reporting GPU/CPU-days and a discussion of sample efficiency vs. performance, as this affects practical impact for groups without such resources.
- Fairness and tuning: The best CIFAR model is further improved by scaling filters and small grid searches; DenseNet comparisons aren’t apples-to-apples (e.g., 1x1 convolutions, parameter counts). For PTB, final models include tuned regularization; it’s appropriate, but clarity on which hyperparameters were fixed during search vs. tuned afterward is important.
- Search space design: Despite flexibility, several choices remain hand-crafted (e.g., fixed positions for pooling in one experiment, specific activation/combination sets, base number 8 for RNN). Results may partially reflect search-space design quality. An ablation on search space definitions would clarify robustness.
- Variance and reproducibility: The controller’s stochastic optimization can be high variance; the paper uses a moving-average baseline but does not report multiple independent runs or error bars. This makes it hard to assess stability.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Report full compute budgets (GPU/CPU days, energy), wall-clock time, and cost-performance trade-offs. Provide learning curves for the controller (reward vs. steps) and an analysis of sample efficiency.
- Add variance-reduction details (entropy regularization/temperature, gradient clipping, baseline smoothing factor) and ablations (with/without reward shaping like cubing).
- Provide sensitivity analyses: number of epochs for child training, depth schedule, m/K/S settings, and the effect of training child models to partial vs. full convergence on the final ranking.
- Clarify fairness: list the exact training/augmentation protocols for top baselines and whether any extra tuning was applied to them vs. to NAS-found models.
- Characterize discovered architectures: distributions of skip connections, receptive fields, in-/out-degree, and, for RNN cells, gate-like behaviors; this would make the scientific insights more generalizable.
- Reproducibility: releasing search code/checkpoints or a smaller-scale recipe would increase impact beyond releasing the final cell.

Questions for the authors
1) What are the total compute budgets (GPU/CPU-days) for CIFAR-10 and PTB searches? How does performance scale with the number of evaluated architectures?
2) How sensitive are the final selections to training the children for only 50 (CIFAR) or 35 (PTB) epochs? Do rankings change when child models are trained to full convergence?
3) Did you use entropy regularization, temperature, or other variance-reduction techniques in the controller? How critical is the reward shaping (cubing/max-of-last-5) to stability?
4) How robust are results to different random seeds for the controller (multiple independent searches)? Please provide variance across runs.
5) How much does fixing pooling positions (CIFAR) or the base number for RNN cells influence outcomes? Any ablations with alternative choices?
6) Do the “compilation failure” heuristics (e.g., padding, defaulting to image input) bias the controller toward certain patterns? Any observed failure modes?
7) Beyond random search, did you compare against a strong Bayesian optimization baseline over a comparable discrete space?

Overall, this paper is ambitious, well-motivated, and empirically compelling, and it opens a promising direction for automated architecture design despite the heavy compute footprint.
