1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), an RL-based framework that uses a recurrent “controller” network to generate variable-length strings specifying neural network architectures. The controller is trained with REINFORCE on the non-differentiable reward of validation performance of “child” models, and leverages distributed, asynchronous training to explore large search spaces. The method is extended to:
- Convolutional architectures with learned skip connections (via set-selection attention), and optional pooling/stride choices.
- Recurrent cell design, where the controller labels operations in a computation tree to define a novel RNN cell; the approach generalizes LSTM-like structures and learns where to connect memory variables.
Empirically, the method yields strong results:
- On CIFAR-10, searched CNNs reach 3.65% test error (with post-hoc widening), competitive with human-designed models while being 1.05× faster than a DenseNet baseline at similar accuracy.
- On PTB, the learned RNN cell achieves test perplexity 62.4, surpassing prior state of the art, and transfers to character modeling and GNMT with gains.
The paper also includes ablations on skip connections, a random search comparison, search-space robustness (adding extra ops), and some transfer experiments.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel and well-motivated approach to variable-length architecture search that is simple yet flexible, with strong empirical results across both CNNs and RNNs.
- Careful engineering to handle skip connections and large-scale parallel training, plus evidence of transferability of the learned RNN cell.

3. Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? Automating neural architecture design over large, variable-length spaces with a scalable, learning-based strategy rather than hand design or black-box HPO. This is a timely and important problem.

Is the approach well-motivated and well-placed in the literature? Yes. The paper clearly contrasts with Bayesian optimization (typically fixed-length hyperparameters) and neuro-evolution (flexible but often slow), and relates to sequence modeling, BLEU-optimized RL, and meta-learning. The controller-as-decoder perspective and handling of non-differentiable rewards via REINFORCE are coherent. The skip-connection mechanism is well thought out.

Do the claims hold, with rigor? The empirical evidence is strong:
- The controller reliably discovers performant CNNs and RNN cells; random search baselines are outperformed both in best and top-k averages.
- Variants show reasonable robustness to changes in search space (adding sin, max).
- The discovered RNN cell transfers to character LM and GNMT with non-trivial improvements.
However, there are weaknesses:
- Compute demands are enormous (e.g., 800 GPUs concurrently for CIFAR-10; 400 CPUs for PTB), which raises concerns about accessibility and fairness in comparison. Search cost is not quantified (e.g., total GPU-hours).
- Some search space choices are hand-engineered (e.g., fixed positions for pooling in one setting; later, narrowing by using 13 “blocks”), limiting claims of fully automated design.
- The final CIFAR-10 number includes a post-hoc width increase not explored during search; this blurs the comparison and may advantage NAS over baselines not similarly tuned.
- Child models are trained for limited epochs during search; the paper mitigates this with grid search and final full training, but the correlation between early reward and final performance is not analyzed.
- Statistical variability and multiple runs are not reported; no confidence intervals; validation split stability is not assessed.
Overall, despite these issues, the empirical gains, breadth across domains, and the clear, reusable methodology justify acceptance.

4. Additional feedback and questions (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
Suggestions for strengthening the paper:
- Report total search cost (GPU/CPU hours), wall-clock time, energy estimates, and cost-performance tradeoffs versus strong manual baselines. Include a “compute normalized” comparison.
- Analyze rank correlation between validation reward (with early stopping and reward shaping) and final test accuracy/perplexity to justify search-time proxies.
- Include ablations on controller design: effect of controller size, entropy regularization, advantage normalization, baseline choice, and reward shaping (cube of accuracy; value of c in perplexity).
- Clarify and rationalize search space engineering. For CIFAR-10, explain the choice to fix pooling at specific layers and subsequently reduce to 13 blocks of 3 layers, and whether the controller could learn pooling placement. Consider a more unified space where pooling/stride/width are always learned.
- Fairness of comparisons: ensure equivalent hyperparameter tuning budgets for baselines; report parameter counts, FLOPs, and training time for final models; avoid post-hoc width changes unless applied equally to baselines.
- Multi-objective search: consider including model size/FLOPs/latency in the reward, or report Pareto fronts.
- Reproducibility: provide exact search spaces, controller hyperparameters, reward baselines, and seeds; release the architectures discovered during the search, not only the best.
- Robustness: evaluate sensitivity to the validation split (e.g., multiple splits), and perform multiple independent search runs to report variance.
- CNN transfer: demonstrate transfer of discovered CNN architectures to other datasets (e.g., SVHN, ImageNet subset) to mirror the RNN transfer.

Questions for the authors:
- What is the total compute budget (e.g., GPU-hours) for the CIFAR-10 and PTB searches, and how does this compare to the cost of training the final models and to baselines?
- Reward shaping: Why cube the CIFAR-10 validation accuracy and square c·perplexity on PTB? How sensitive are results to these choices? Did you use entropy regularization or advantage normalization?
- Baseline details: What is the EMA decay rate for the baseline b? Did you try learned baselines or critic networks?
- How often did “compilation failures” occur due to incompatible skip connections, and how do the padding/default-input heuristics affect representational bias?
- In the CIFAR-10 “39-layer” experiments, why fix pooling at layers 13 and 24 and switch to 13 blocks? Could the controller learn pooling placement directly without preset positions?
- On the 3.65% CIFAR-10 result, the architecture is widened by +40 filters post hoc. Did you apply similar width scaling to competing human-designed baselines? How does the searched architecture perform at the original width?
- Random search baseline: Was it given identical search space, number of samples, and training/evaluation protocol? Please include quantitative curves and final best-of-N statistics in the main text.
- For PTB baselines, were regularization and training schedules tuned to the same extent as your searched models? Please provide matched parameter counts and training-time comparisons for the RHN comparison.
- Correlation between early stopping reward and final accuracy/perplexity: can you report Spearman correlations across a sample of architectures?

Overall, this is an influential and promising direction; addressing compute transparency, comparison fairness, and ablations would make the paper even stronger.
