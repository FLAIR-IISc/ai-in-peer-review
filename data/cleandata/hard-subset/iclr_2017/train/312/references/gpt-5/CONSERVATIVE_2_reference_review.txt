Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), in which a controller RNN uses policy gradients (REINFORCE) to sequentially generate variable-length neural architectures. The child models are trained and their validation performance is used as the reward to update the controller. The method extends the search space with skip connections via a set-selection attention mechanism and is applied to both convolutional networks (CIFAR-10) and recurrent cells (PTB). A large-scale distributed/asynchronous setup enables training thousands of child models in parallel. Empirically, the authors report: (a) CIFAR-10 architectures attaining 3.65% test error (after post-hoc filter inflation) and competitive results without strides/pooling; (b) a novel recurrent cell achieving strong perplexity on PTB, with some transfer to character LM and GNMT.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Evidence is not sufficiently controlled to support the strong claims. Many evaluation choices (early stopping, reward shaping, depth scheduling, post-hoc tuning beyond the search space) can confound conclusions, and key baselines (especially random search and BO/evolution) are missing or insufficiently matched in compute.
2) The approach’s practicality hinges on extremely large computational budgets (hundreds of GPUs/CPUs over many hours). Without stronger evidence of search efficiency and fair comparisons under equal budgets, it is hard to endorse this over established alternatives.

Supporting arguments
- Missing and/or unfair baselines:
  • CIFAR-10 lacks a random search baseline and comparisons to strong HPO methods (e.g., SMAC, TPE, BO) or evolutionary strategies under the same search space and compute. The PTB random-search comparison helps but is insufficient to establish superiority generally.
  • Comparisons to human-designed models are not compute-matched: training protocols, parameter counts, augmentations, and schedules differ. The “1.05x faster than DenseNet” claim is underspecified (inference vs training, hardware, batch size).
- Post-hoc tuning outside the search space confounds claims:
  • The headline 3.65% CIFAR-10 result is obtained by adding 40 filters to each layer post hoc, which was not part of the controller’s search space. This undermines the claim that the controller found the final architecture.
  • After selection, additional grid searches (learning rate, weight decay, BN epsilon, decay epoch) further decouple the search objective from the reported final performance.
- Evaluation protocol misalignment and potential biases:
  • Child models are trained for only 50 epochs during search and selected using a reward that cubes the last-5-epoch max validation accuracy. This reward shaping and early stopping can bias toward architectures that learn quickly rather than those with best eventual performance.
  • The schedule that increases depth over time introduces a moving target: improvements could be partly due to deeper models rather than better controller policies. No ablation quantifies this.
  • The handling of skip-connection “compilation failures” (auto-connecting to inputs, concatenating orphan outputs, zero-padding) may inject nontrivial biases in favor of certain connectivity patterns; no ablation is provided.
- Search efficiency and compute:
  • Results rely on massive parallelism (e.g., 800 GPUs for CIFAR, 400 CPUs for PTB). The paper does not quantify total compute (GPU-days/CPU-days) nor compare sample efficiency to baselines. Without such, it is unclear whether performance gains arise from method or sheer compute.
- Scope and generality:
  • While the recurrent cell transfers to character LM and GNMT, gains are modest in translation and the protocol was tuned for LSTMs. More thorough cross-task transfer and controlled comparisons would strengthen the generality claim.
- Clarity and positioning:
  • The method is described as “gradient-based” architecture search; in practice it is REINFORCE over a non-differentiable objective. The distinction matters; comparisons to other RL-based or evolutionary NAS are lacking.
  • The literature positioning understates prior work on variable-length BO/evolution; claims of greater generality would benefit from concrete head-to-head comparisons and ablations.

Feedback to improve the paper
- Provide strong, compute-matched baselines on both CIFAR-10 and PTB: random search, BO (e.g., TPE/SMAC), and evolution, all under identical search spaces, child training budgets, and number of evaluations.
- Report total compute budgets (e.g., GPU/CPU days), wall-clock time, and sample efficiency curves. Include multiple independent runs to report variability.
- Remove post-hoc modifications outside the search space from headline numbers, or explicitly include such choices in the controller’s decisions.
- Align the search and evaluation protocols: train child models longer during search or justify/ablate early stopping and reward shaping; report correlation between search-time reward and final test performance.
- Ablations:
  • Impact of depth scheduling (fixed-depth vs scheduled).
  • Effect of skip-connection handling strategies and zero-padding.
  • Including/excluding learning-rate prediction and other layer types.
  • Alternative RL variants (baselines/advantage, entropy regularization) to reduce variance.
- Fairness and reproducibility: standardize augmentations and training schedules with baselines; detail the “1.05x faster” measurement; release code and exact seeds/configs.

Questions for the authors
1) Compute budgets: How many GPU/CPU days were used for each experiment (including controller and final training)? What is the sample efficiency relative to random search on CIFAR-10?
2) Reward shaping: Why cube validation accuracy? How sensitive are results to this choice and to training for only 50 epochs during search? What is the correlation between search reward and final converged accuracy?
3) Depth schedule: How much of the observed improvement is attributable to increasing depth vs learned policy? Please provide an ablation with fixed-depth search.
4) Post-hoc filter inflation on CIFAR-10: Why is the best-reported model outside the search space? What is the performance without adding filters? Can the controller be extended to include width scaling so that headline results are within-search?
5) Baselines: Can you provide compute-matched random search and BO/evolution baselines on CIFAR-10 with the same child training budget and number of evaluations?
6) Handling invalid graphs: How often do “compilation failures” occur, and how do your automatic fixes affect search dynamics? Any evidence these heuristics bias toward particular motifs?
7) Comparability: How exactly was “1.05x faster than DenseNet” measured (hardware, batch size, training/inference)? Are parameter counts and FLOPs matched?
