1. Summary and contributions
This paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a reinforcement learning framework where a recurrent controller generates variable-length descriptions of neural architectures, which are then trained and scored on a validation set to provide a reward signal. The controller is trained with REINFORCE and uses an attention-based set-selection mechanism to propose skip connections and heterogeneous layer types. The method is applied to two distinct domains:
- CIFAR-10: discovers convolutional architectures with skip connections achieving up to 3.65% test error (with post-hoc widening), competitive with top human-designed models while being claimed 1.05x faster than a comparable DenseNet.
- Penn Treebank: discovers a novel recurrent cell that outperforms LSTM-based state of the art by ~3.6 perplexity and demonstrates transfer to character-level PTB and GNMT with modest BLEU gains, without retuning the overall framework.
The work also demonstrates large-scale distributed, asynchronous training for architecture search and compares against random search, showing better best and top-k averages.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Strong conceptual novelty: a general, learnable, variable-length architecture generator trained end-to-end with RL; a bold step beyond fixed-length hyperparameter search.
- Compelling empirical impact across vision and language, with evidence of transfer and better-than-random search performance.

3. Supporting arguments
- Clear problem framing: manual architecture design is time-consuming; prior methods are either fixed-length HPO or less scalable neuroevolution. NAS offers a flexible, data-driven generator over a rich search space (including skip connections and layer types).
- Methodological soundness: controller trained via policy gradient with a moving-average baseline; careful engineering (distributed, asynchronous search; depth scheduling; handling of invalid graphs) enables feasibility. The set-selection attention to form skip connections is a neat mechanism to grow model complexity while keeping gradients tractable for the controller.
- Breadth and generality: the same framework discovers both convolutional topologies and recurrent cells. The RNN cell transfers to character-level modeling and GNMT with minimal changes, suggesting the controller actually learns reusable motifs rather than overfitting to a narrow proxy task.
- Empirical validation: strong PTB results (notably faster than recurrent highway networks while better); competitive CIFAR-10 results, with analyses of skip-connection necessity and ablation versus dense/no-skip variants. The random search comparison is helpful and supports the claim that learned search improves over naïve baselines.
- Novel direction: This paper opens a research trajectory (program synthesis of NN architectures guided by task reward), which is valuable even if some engineering choices are heuristic.

4. Feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not blocking)
- Compute budget and accessibility: The experiments rely on massive resources (e.g., 800 GPUs). Please quantify total wall-clock time, GPU/CPU hours, and controller convergence characteristics. Discuss how performance scales when resources are constrained (e.g., fewer replicas, fewer sampled architectures).
- Fairness of CIFAR-10 comparisons: The best 3.65% model is obtained by post-hoc widening (+40 filters). Clarify whether widening was part of the search or manual post-processing; report performance of the exact searched architecture for a stronger apples-to-apples comparison. Also detail augmentation and training schedules relative to the strongest baselines.
- Proxy training fidelity: You train child models for 50 (CIFAR) or 35 (PTB) epochs as a proxy. Provide correlation analyses between proxy validation performance and fully trained test performance to justify the reward and early stopping choices.
- Reward design and variance reduction: The cubed accuracy and c·perplexity^2 rewards are somewhat ad hoc. Justify these choices and report sensitivity. Consider reporting whether entropy regularization, advantage normalization, or actor-critic variants were tried and their impact on stability/sample efficiency.
- Search space priors: The space still encodes expert choices (e.g., allowed filter sizes, activation sets, base number 8, fixed pooling layer positions in one setting). A clearer discussion of how these priors bias the outcomes, and ablations (e.g., with/without pooling position constraints), would help.
- Robustness: Report variability across multiple controller runs with different random seeds: distribution of best/median architectures, and whether discovered motifs are consistent.
- Reproducibility: You promise to release code/models and note NASCell is in TensorFlow. Please also release the controller code, search spaces, and scripts for distributed search to lower the barrier for the community.

5. Questions for the authors
- Stability and repeatability: Over multiple independent controller runs, how variable are (a) the best found performance and (b) the discovered motifs? What is the variance of top-1 and top-5 architectures?
- Sample efficiency: What is the correlation between early-proxy reward and final performance when retrained longer or with larger widths? Any results with early stopping, learning-curve extrapolation, or weight sharing to reduce cost?
- Reward shaping: Why cube accuracy and square perplexity? Did you try alternative scalings or normalized/relative rewards, and how did they affect convergence and variance?
- Baselines: Besides random search, did you compare against Bayesian optimization or evolutionary strategies on the same search spaces/budgets?
- Search space sensitivity: How do results change with different activation/combination sets, base numbers, or allowing stride/pooling locations to be searched freely? Does the controller exploit larger spaces effectively?
- GNMT transfer: Could you provide more details on BLEU improvements across validation/test sets and whether further tuning of global hyperparameters benefits the discovered cell differentially versus LSTM?
- Post-hoc widening: For CIFAR-10, how much of the final gain (3.65%) comes from widening vs the searched topology? Would the controller learn to prefer wider layers if given that degree of freedom?
- Practicality: With 10–100x fewer resources, what performance should practitioners expect? Any guidance on configuring S, K, m for budget-constrained settings?

Overall, this paper is a bold and influential step towards automated architecture design, with convincing results and a framework that is likely to catalyze substantial follow-up work.
