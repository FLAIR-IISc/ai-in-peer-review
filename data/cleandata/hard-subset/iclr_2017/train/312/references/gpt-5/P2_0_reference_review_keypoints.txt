- Proposal: NAS — an RL-based recurrent controller that generates variable-length neural architectures, trains sampled children, and updates via REINFORCE with an EMA baseline.
- Supported scopes: CNN architecture search (including skip connections via set-selection attention, pooling, batch-norm), RNN cell search (computation tree with memory injection/extraction), and large-scale asynchronous distributed evaluation.
- Main results: Competitive CNNs on CIFAR-10 (down to 3.65% after capacity scaling) and improved RNN cells on PTB (test perplexity 62.4) with positive transfer to char-level PTB and GNMT.
- Robustness: Control experiments show resilience to enlarged search spaces and superiority over random search on PTB.
- Decision: Accept — novel, clear approach to learning variable-length architecture generators with strong multi-modal empirical results and scalable engineering.
- Motivation strength: Addresses expensive manual design and moves beyond fixed-length hyperparameter optimization using a principled autoregressive controller and policy gradients.
- Method novelty/clarity: Controller-as-sequence, set-selection attention for skips, and computation-tree RNN search space are well-designed and extend existing architecture search capabilities.
- Training mechanics: Use of EMA baseline, reward shaping, and asynchronous distributed training is sensible and necessary for scalability.
- CIFAR-10 empirical detail: Finds meaningful architectural motifs; shallow 15-layer model reaches 5.50% without pooling, expanded search + scaling reaches 3.65%, near hand-designed SOTA.
- PTB empirical detail: Discovered cell yields substantial perplexity improvement and is more computationally efficient than some strong baselines; transfer experiments support generality.
- Random search comparison: NAS outperforms random search on PTB, with both best-of-run and top-k averages improving during training.
- Rigor: Paper includes extensive training details, ablations, and a clear reward/baseline specification; final evaluation protocol is reasonable.
- Compute/reproducibility concern: Authors should quantify total compute (GPU/CPU hours), wall-clock time, sample efficiency, and variance across controller seeds.
- Validation overfitting concern: Controller trained on a fixed 5k validation set reused many times — provide evidence against overfitting (e.g., second split or k-fold).
- Early-stopping bias: Analyze correlation between early-validation accuracy during search and final fully trained test performance to justify child training budgets.
- Fairness/capacity controls: Compare models at matched parameter counts/FLOPs and clarify the “1.05x faster than DenseNet” claim (inference vs training, hardware/batch sizes).
- Search-space ablations: Provide CIFAR ablations isolating skip-connection contribution and depth schedule; mirror PTB random-search comparisons; analyze sensitivity to design choices (base number, operator sets, ct placement).
- Alternatives to REINFORCE: Discuss or test lower-variance methods (actor-critic, advantage-weighted) to improve convergence and sample efficiency.
- Handling invalid architectures: Quantify frequency and impact of padded/default-input fixes for disconnected layers and size mismatches.
- Questions for authors: total compute costs for CIFAR/PTB searches; performance vs number of evaluated architectures; validation leakage checks; sensitivity to reward shaping and EMA hyperparameters; random search performance on CIFAR; number of controller runs needed and variance; search-space sensitivity studies; clarify the speed claim specifics.
