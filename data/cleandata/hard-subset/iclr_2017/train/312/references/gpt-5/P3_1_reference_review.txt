Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a reinforcement learning-based framework in which a controller RNN autoregressively generates variable-length descriptions of neural network architectures. Child networks sampled from this policy are trained to convergence on a task; their validation performance is used as the reward to update the controller via REINFORCE with a moving-average baseline. The method supports complex topologies via a set-selection attention mechanism to form skip/branch connections and can predict layer types and hyperparameters. The approach is scaled with asynchronous distributed training, evaluating hundreds of child models in parallel.

Two instantiations are presented: (1) convolutional architectures for CIFAR-10, including skip connections and optional pooling/strides; and (2) novel recurrent cells for Penn Treebank (PTB), where a tree-structured search space composes operations over xt and ht−1 and optionally injects/updates cell states. NAS achieves competitive to state-of-the-art error on CIFAR-10 (down to 3.65% after post-hoc widening) and new SOTA on PTB word-level perplexity (62.4) with favorable runtime vs RHN, plus transfer gains on character PTB and modest BLEU improvements when dropping the cell into GNMT. Control experiments show superiority to random search under the provided budget and robustness to enlarged search spaces.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Novel, general, and flexible formulation for searching variable-length architectures with strong empirical results across disparate domains.
- Clear methodological innovations (attention-based skip selection, cell search space, large-scale asynchronous REINFORCE) and compelling evidence that the framework discovers high-performing, nontrivial designs.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The paper is well-motivated: manual architecture design is expensive; existing BO typically handles fixed/conditionally structured spaces, and neuro-evolution methods can be less scalable. The authors cite relevant BO, neuro-evolution, meta-learning, and program synthesis work. While BO can handle conditional/variable structures (e.g., Hyperopt/SMAC) more than the text implies, the proposed controller policy over sequences is strictly more expressive and more naturally accommodates complex graph decisions.
- Methodological soundness: Using an autoregressive policy trained with REINFORCE is standard for non-differentiable rewards; the moving-average baseline and batching reduce variance. The set-selection attention for skip connections is elegant and general. The recurrent-cell search space is thoughtfully constructed and includes memory injections. The scaling strategy via parameter servers and many replicas is pragmatic and essential to make RL-driven NAS viable.
- Empirical strength: Results are strong on both CIFAR-10 and PTB. The method discovers architectures with recognizable motifs (rectangular filters, shallow skip patterns akin to ResNets) and a cell structurally reminiscent of LSTM yet distinct, supporting the claim that NAS can rediscover and extend human priors. Transfer to character-level LM and GNMT further supports generality. The random search comparison suggests the controller learns a useful policy beyond black-box sampling.
- Clarity: The description of the controller, reward shaping, skip-connection mechanism, and the recurrent-cell tree are clear. Training details and hyperparameters are reported for both controller and child models.

Concerns and limitations (do not preclude acceptance)
- Computational cost and accessibility: The approach consumes massive resources (up to 800 GPUs for CIFAR, 400 CPUs for PTB). This limits reproducibility and raises fairness concerns in comparing to human-designed baselines developed under far smaller budgets. Sample efficiency is low; no weight sharing or early-stopping proxies are used.
- Fairness of comparisons and post-hoc modifications: The best CIFAR-10 number (3.65%) comes from widening the discovered architecture by adding 40 filters post-search. It is unclear whether comparable capacity tuning was applied to baselines. Runtime comparisons (“1.05x faster”) need standardized measurement (same hardware, batch size, precision) and matched FLOPs/params.
- Search space and reward design: The controller benefits from a curated search space (e.g., allowed filter sizes, limited ops for recurrent cells). The claim that the method “designs architectures from scratch” should be tempered, as performance depends on these priors. Reward shaping (cubed accuracy; c·perplexity^2) is heuristic; sensitivity analyses are limited.
- Statistical rigor and robustness: Results are mostly single-run best-of-many with grid search after selection. Variance across seeds, correlation between proxy training (50/35 epochs) and full training, and meta-overfitting to the validation split are not quantified. Asynchronous updates with stale policies may bias learning; no entropy regularization or KL control is reported.

Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Report end-to-end compute budgets (GPU/CPU days) for search and final training, and normalize comparisons by budget where possible.
- Provide runtime/throughput and FLOPs/parameter counts on identical hardware for claimed speedups; include test-time augmentation usage if any.
- Add ablations: effect of entropy regularization, reward scaling, controller capacity, depth schedule, and skip-selection mechanism. Report sensitivity to search space design (e.g., enabling more ops) beyond the “max/sin” control.
- Quantify robustness: variance over multiple controller seeds, and correlation between proxy training reward and final performance after full training.
- Clarify and justify the post-hoc widening on CIFAR; provide the performance of the unmodified searched model under matched capacity to human baselines.
- Analyze the proportion of “repaired” architectures (zero-padding, input fallback) and their impact on performance/compute fairness.
- Discuss ethical/practical implications and pathways to democratize NAS (e.g., proxy tasks, weight sharing, early stopping, low-fidelity evaluations).

Questions for the authors
- Compute and fairness: What are the total compute budgets (e.g., GPU/CPU-days) for the search on CIFAR/PTB and for training the final selected models? How are runtime comparisons normalized?
- Reward shaping and exploration: How sensitive are results to the cubed-accuracy/perplexity^2 reward? Did you use entropy regularization or other exploration mechanisms? Any issues with reward nonstationarity due to the increasing-depth schedule?
- Robustness: How many independent controller runs were attempted? What is the variance of the best-found performance across seeds? How well does proxy (50/35 epochs) validation correlate with fully trained test performance?
- Validation/meta-overfitting: The controller repeatedly observes the same validation split. Did you monitor overfitting of the controller to that split (e.g., via a second hold-out or cross-validation)?
- Search space reliance: How do results change when you broaden the op set (e.g., include 1×1 convolutions, bottlenecks, dilations) or modify the recurrent op set? Are there failure modes when skip connections are disallowed?
- Architecture repairs: What fraction of sampled architectures required padding or input fallbacks? Do these repairs systematically bias the search?
- Post-hoc tuning: For CIFAR, the final 3.65% involves adding 40 filters. Was a similar capacity scaling applied to baselines? What is the accuracy of the unmodified discovered model under matched parameter/FLOP budgets?
- PTB specifics: Are embedding tying and dropout settings identical across compared models? How does the discovered cell perform under stronger baselines (e.g., with pointer-sentinel or neural cache) under matched training setups?

Overall, despite the compute-heavy nature and some fairness/robustness concerns, the paper makes a substantial and well-executed contribution that is likely to influence future research. I recommend acceptance.
