- Proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS): controller RNN trained with REINFORCE to generate variable-length neural architectures, using child validation accuracy as reward.
- Supports rich search spaces including convolutional architectures with skip connections and novel recurrent cell topologies expressed as computation trees with ct−1/ct inject/read options.
- Develops scalable asynchronous distributed training to evaluate hundreds of child models in parallel and uses an EMA baseline to reduce gradient variance.
- Finds a CIFAR-10 ConvNet competitive with strong human designs (3.65% test error after post-search widening).
- Discovers a NASCell achieving 62.4 test perplexity on PTB (~3.6 perplexity improvement), with positive transfer to character-level PTB and GNMT (+0.5 BLEU).
- Includes ablations/controls: expanded search space experiments and a random search comparison on PTB showing learning over time.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason 1: Novel, general framework for learning discrete, variable-length architectures via reinforcement learning, expressing skip connections and complex RNN topologies.
- Key reason 2: Strong empirical results across vision and language with evidence of transfer and superiority to plausible baselines, enabled by large-scale engineering.
- Problem/significance: Addresses costly manual architecture design and topology search limitations of most HPO methods.
- Positioning: Contrasts NAS with hyperparameter optimization, Bayesian methods for non-fixed spaces, and neuroevolution, highlighting policy-gradient advantages.
- Method soundness: Controller action sequences + REINFORCE with moving-average baseline is a correct estimator for non-differentiable rewards.
- Method novelty: Set-selection attention for skip connections enables arbitrary connectivity compatible with policy gradients.
- Engineering: Asynchronous parameter server, reward shaping, and increasing-depth schedule are sensible and likely crucial at scale.
- CIFAR-10 empirical support: Discovered architectures perform competitively without hand-crafted templates; sensitivity to skip connections validated.
- PTB empirical support: NASCell improves over published LSTM variants at similar parameter budgets and transfers across tasks; runtime advantage vs RHN noted.
- Controls: Enlarged function set still yields strong models; random search under same budget performs worse and gap increases with time (shown for PTB).
- Broader impact: Conceptually simple, extensible to other modalities, and releasing NASCell lowers adoption barriers.
- Caveat 1: Extremely high computational cost (e.g., thousands of architectures, hundreds of GPUs) raises reproducibility and sample-efficiency concerns.
- Caveat 2: CIFAR-10 final 3.65% requires post-search widening and small grid search; fairness of comparisons to DenseNet needs clarification.
- Caveat 3: Reward shaping (cubing accuracy) and schedules likely influence outcomes; limited ablations on these choices.
- Caveat 4: Random search baseline is shown for PTB but not for convolutional search, weakening cross-domain claims.
- Suggestion 1: Provide compute budget details (GPU/CPU days) and learning curves (median/top-k reward) plus multiple controller seeds for variability.
- Suggestion 2: Ablate reward transformations, baseline formulation, entropy regularization, and the increasing-depth schedule to understand exploration-exploitation tradeoffs.
- Suggestion 3: Analyze skip-connection attention distributions and quantify their contribution to gains.
- Suggestion 4: For CIFAR-10, report random search baselines under same budget, normalize comparisons by parameter count and FLOPs, and clarify training schedules.
- Suggestion 5: For PTB, clarify regularizers used in baselines and report sensitivity of discovered cell to hidden size and depth.
- Suggestion 6: Discuss safeguards against overfitting to validation splits and how many re-trainings were used for final selection.
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to reward transformation and exploration techniques (variance norm/entropy bonuses)?
- Questions: number of controller runs/seeds and variability of final best performances?
- Questions: CIFAR-10 random search performance under same budget and time-based top-k averages?
- Questions: frequency and bias of compilation failures and auto-fixes?
- Questions: how many re-trainings of top candidates to reduce validation noise and stability of ranking across seeds?
- Questions: total compute (GPU/CPU days), wall-clock time per experiment, and budget-vs-quality curves?
- Questions: does widening preserve ranking across seeds and is widening generally beneficial for discovered topologies?
- Questions: in GNMT, were NASCell hyperparameters tuned or just scaled, and which layers benefit most?
