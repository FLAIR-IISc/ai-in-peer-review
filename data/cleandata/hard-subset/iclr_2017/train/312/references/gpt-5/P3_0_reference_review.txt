Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a reinforcement learning framework in which a controller RNN sequentially emits variable-length strings describing neural network architectures. A sampled “child” architecture is trained to convergence; its validation accuracy serves as a reward to update the controller via REINFORCE. The search space covers convolutional networks (including skip connections and other layer types) and recurrent cells (represented as computation trees with learned combination/activation choices and optional cell state injection). The paper further details a large-scale, asynchronous distributed training scheme to evaluate many child models in parallel. Empirically, the method discovers competitive-to-SOTA ConvNet architectures on CIFAR-10 and a recurrent cell that achieves SOTA perplexity on Penn Treebank (PTB); the cell also transfers to character-level PTB and yields a small BLEU improvement in GNMT without retuning. Control experiments compare against random search and increased search-space complexity.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel and general formulation of architecture search as sequence prediction with policy gradients over variable-length spaces, plus a practical distributed training recipe.
- Strong empirical results across vision and language, including transfer, with ablations and controls that support the core claims.

Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? The paper seeks to automate neural architecture design, enabling search over variable-length, structured spaces (including connectivity) rather than fixed-length hyperparameters.

Is the approach well motivated and placed? Yes. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from Bayesian optimization (typically fixed-length hyperparameters), neuroevolution (flexible but often slow/heuristic), and connects to sequence modeling, REINFORCE for non-differentiable objectives, and meta-learning. The literature review is largely appropriate for the time, and the method is meaningfully different in both formulation and scope.

Do the results support the claims?
- CIFAR-10: The controller finds conv architectures competitive with human-designed models; the best model reported (after modest capacity augmentation) achieves 3.65% test error, close to DenseNet, with a speed edge. The paper provides sanity checks (effect of skip connections, stride prediction). However, some comparisons differ in training recipe or parameterization, complicating strict fairness. Still, the evidence indicates the search finds high-quality models.
- PTB: The discovered RNN cell improves validation/test perplexity over strong baselines with comparable parameter budgets and standard regularization (dropout variants, embedding tying). The reported gains (~3.6 perplexity) are substantial and the cell is computationally efficient relative to RHN. Transfer to char-level PTB and GNMT provides additional support for generality.
- Controls: The random search baseline is appropriate and shows a consistent advantage for policy gradient over the course of training; expanding the search space modestly degrades results but remains competitive, suggesting robustness.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: controller RNN over variable-length architectural programs optimized via REINFORCE.
- Flexible search space, including learned skip-connection selection via content-based sigmoids and a tree-based parameterization for recurrent cells with memory injection.
- Scalable distributed implementation that makes the approach practical in wall-clock time.
- Empirical breadth with cross-domain results and transfer.
- Some ablations/controls and reasonable training details.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Computational cost and accessibility: The reported setup uses up to 800 GPUs (CIFAR) and 400 CPUs (PTB) with thousands of fully trained candidates. This limits reproducibility and practical adoption, and makes baseline parity challenging. While this is a systems contribution, more discussion of efficiency vs accuracy trade-offs would help.
- Risk of overfitting to the validation split: Selecting the best among 12.8k–15k architectures on a 5k-image validation set (CIFAR) can overfit. There is no explicit use of a second “nas-validation” vs final validation split, nor evaluation of variability across different splits or seeds.
- Fairness of comparisons: Some top-line numbers involve post-hoc capacity changes (adding 40 filters) and hyperparameter grid searches. Differences in training and parameter budgets versus baselines (e.g., DenseNet) complicate head-to-head conclusions.
- Limited analysis of learned policies: Beyond qualitative cell visualizations, there is little quantitative analysis of what the controller learns (e.g., distribution over operations/skip patterns, entropy over time).
- REINFORCE details: The method uses an EMA baseline but does not report sensitivity to reward shaping (e.g., cubed accuracy), entropy regularization, or gradient variance. No learning curves of controller reward variance are shown.
- Search space design choices: The “compilation failure” fixes (auto-inject inputs, concatenation with zero-padding, final concatenation) may bias the search and are heuristic; their effect on outcomes is not ablated.

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Report results averaged over multiple random validation splits and search seeds, or use a holdout “architecture selection” set distinct from the controller’s reward set to quantify and mitigate overfitting.
- Provide tighter, capacity-matched comparisons with baselines using identical training recipes, and clearly separate “as found” vs “post-hoc tuned/augmented” numbers.
- Add ablations on reward shaping (e.g., with/without cubing) and controller regularization (entropy bonus), and report controller learning curves (mean/top-k rewards, variance).
- Analyze the learned distributions over architectural choices and skip connectivity over time to provide insight into what the controller discovers.
- Discuss and evaluate compute-efficient variants (e.g., early stopping proxies, lower-fidelity evaluations, weight inheritance or network morphisms) to make the approach more accessible.
- Clarify the recurrent cell search space indexing and constraints, and quantify the effect of the automatic fixes for invalid graphs.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are the CIFAR-10 and PTB results to the reward shaping (cubed accuracy; c·perplexity^2), baseline choice, and entropy regularization? Did you try alternative baselines (e.g., learned critics)?
- Given the large number of architecture evaluations, how do you assess and mitigate overfitting to the validation split? Can you report performance when re-splitting validation or using a separate selection set?
- What is the variance across independent NAS runs (different controller initializations) in terms of best-found performance and search efficiency?
- Can you provide capacity-matched comparisons for the CIFAR-10 best model without the post-hoc addition of 40 filters, and with identical training hyperparameters to the strongest baselines?
- How often do the “compilation failure” fallbacks (auto-input, zero-padding, final concatenation) trigger, and what is their impact on final performance?
- How does controller performance scale with the number of parallel workers and the number of epochs per child? Is there evidence of diminishing returns?
- Will you release the controller code and exact search spaces, not only the final discovered models, to facilitate reproducibility?
