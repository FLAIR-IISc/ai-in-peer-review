1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a reinforcement learning approach that trains a recurrent controller to generate variable-length neural network architectures. Using REINFORCE with a moving-average baseline, the controller samples architectural decisions; each sampled “child” model is trained to convergence, and its validation performance is used as the reward to update the controller. The search space includes convolutional architectures with skip connections and other layer types, and a separate search space for recurrent cells represented as computation trees with learnable routing of cell states. The method is scaled via distributed, asynchronous training with hundreds of concurrent workers. Empirically, NAS discovers:
- A CIFAR-10 CNN competitive with top human-designed models (down to 3.65% error after modest capacity scaling) and with reasonable depth and speed.
- A novel RNN cell that achieves state-of-the-art perplexity on PTB and transfers to character-level PTB and GNMT with gains, while being computationally efficient relative to some prior SOTA.
The paper includes comparisons against random search, limited control experiments, and visualizations of discovered cells. Overall, it presents a general, conceptually simple, and practically effective framework for end-to-end automatic architecture design.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel and well-motivated formulation that extends architecture search to variable-length, compositional spaces using a learned controller, with clear advances over fixed-length hyperparameter optimization.
- Strong empirical results across two modalities (vision and language), including new SOTA on PTB and competitive CIFAR-10 performance, plus transfer to GNMT.

3) Supporting arguments
Addressing the three core questions:
1. What problem is tackled? Automating neural architecture design over large, variable-length search spaces for both CNNs and RNN cells, reducing human effort and potentially discovering superior designs.
2. Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? Yes. The paper clearly situates NAS relative to Bayesian optimization, neuro-evolution, program induction, and meta-learning. The choice of an autoregressive controller aligns with sequence modeling, and REINFORCE is appropriate for the non-differentiable reward. The extension to skip-connection selection and recurrent cell tree structures is sensible and increases expressivity.
3. Do the results support the claims? Largely yes. The PTB results are compelling, with clear improvements and reasonable training protocol details. CIFAR-10 results are near-SOTA; the method finds compact, high-performing architectures. The random-search comparison indicates the controller learns nontrivial structure. Transfer to char-level PTB and GNMT, while modest on BLEU, is a valuable demonstration of generality. That said, there are caveats: the compute budget is enormous; some post-search tuning (grid search, added filters) blurs the line between “discovered” vs “hand-improved”; and broader benchmarks and ablations would strengthen the case.

Strengths
- General framework applicable to diverse architecture families.
- Ability to discover non-obvious patterns (e.g., specific skip connectivity, recurrent compositions).
- Solid engineering to scale search; clear reward design and variance reduction.
- State-of-the-art or near-SOTA results and evidence of transfer.

Weaknesses/concerns
- Very large computational cost (e.g., 800 GPUs/CPUs in parallel; 12.8k–15k models), limiting accessibility and reproducibility.
- Reliance on post-search grid tuning and capacity scaling (e.g., +40 filters) for final numbers; fairness of comparisons could be clearer.
- Limited ablations: depth schedule, reward shaping (accuracy^3/perplexity^2), skip-connection attention, asynchronous updates.
- Risk of overfitting to a small validation split with many trials; no analysis of selection bias or variability across runs/seeds.
- Comparisons mostly on CIFAR-10 and PTB; broader datasets would bolster generality claims.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not necessarily part of the decision)
- Report full search cost quantitatively (e.g., GPU-day/CPU-day) and sample efficiency curves; discuss practical trade-offs.
- Provide ablations: effect of reward shaping exponent, entropy regularization, baseline choice, depth schedule, and the skip-connection selection mechanism. Include results without post-hoc grid tuning or capacity changes to isolate NAS’s contribution.
- Fairness of baselines: ensure techniques like weight tying, dropout settings, and training lengths are matched; clarify which baselines you re-ran under your pipeline.
- Analyze variance: multiple NAS runs with different seeds; dispersion of top-k architectures; stability of the discovered design.
- Overfitting to validation: explore larger or cross-validation schemes; report performance vs number of architectures evaluated; consider a fresh validation fold late in search.
- Failure handling: quantify the rate of invalid/”compilation failure” architectures and their impact on learning; consider constraints to avoid padding-induced artifacts.
- Multi-objective rewards: show results when jointly optimizing accuracy, latency, and parameter count to improve practical utility.
- Reproducibility: releasing search code or a minimal, lower-cost setting would greatly enhance impact.

Questions for the authors
- Compute budget: How many total GPU/CPU days were used for CIFAR-10 and PTB searches? What is the wall-clock time to reach near-best models?
- Random search comparison: Was the budget strictly matched (same number of architectures, same training epochs)? Please provide variance across seeds and statistical significance.
- Post-search tuning: For CIFAR-10, what are the exact grid-search ranges, and how much of the final gain derives from tuning vs the architecture itself? Please report results for the exact discovered architecture without capacity scaling.
- Validation overfitting: With ~12.8k–15k evaluations, how do you mitigate selection bias on a 5k validation split? Did you try a separate hold-out for architecture selection?
- Baseline parity: For PTB, did you apply shared embeddings and the same dropout/optimizer schedules to the LSTM baselines you compare against?
- Controller details: Did you use entropy regularization or other variance-reduction techniques beyond a moving-average baseline? How sensitive are results to controller size and learning rate?
- Invalid architectures: What fraction of sampled architectures require the fallback rules (e.g., zero-padding, using the image as input), and do they correlate with poorer rewards?
- Transferability: Beyond PTB and GNMT, did you test the discovered cell on other language tasks? How sensitive are results to the base number (e.g., 4 vs 8)?
- Practicality: Can you incorporate resource constraints (params/FLOPs/latency) directly in the reward, and do you have preliminary results?

Overall, this is a substantial and influential contribution that, despite heavy compute and some missing analyses, merits acceptance.
