- Introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS): a controller RNN generates architectures, child nets are trained, and performance is used as a reward to update the controller.  
- Demonstrates NAS on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results.  
- Extends approach to include skip connections and recurrent cell design, showing method flexibility.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions — novel, compelling, and empirically strong.  
- Novelty/significance: Automates architecture design beyond traditional hyperparameter search and explores a larger design space.  
- Strong empirical results: Comparable or superior performance on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, notably discovering improved recurrent cells.  
- Clear explanation and technical soundness: Methodology, use of policy gradients, and implementation details are well justified.  
- Computational cost addressed via parallel training and asynchronous updates, improving feasibility.  
- Request for more ablation studies to quantify the contribution and cost of each component (e.g., skip connections, stride options).  
- Need deeper discussion of search space choices and guidelines for defining effective search spaces.  
- Suggestion to visualize the search process (policy evolution, early vs. converged architectures) for insight.  
- Ask for discussion on generalization to other datasets/tasks and potential challenges.  
- Recommend ensuring released code is well-documented and reproducible for adoption.  
- Question on search space sensitivity: how results change with different search space configurations.  
- Question on controller capacity: impact of RNN size on NAS performance and risk of overfitting or diminishing returns.  
- Question on exploration vs. exploitation: strategies to balance and avoid local optima during policy gradient training.  
- Question on scalability: bottlenecks and behavior on larger datasets and more complex architectures.  
- Question on transfer learning details: extent of hyperparameter tuning for Penn Treebank and WMT14 experiments.
