This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller and reinforcement learning. The core idea is compelling: formulate architecture design as a sequential decision process, leveraging policy gradients to optimize the controller for high-performing child networks. The authors demonstrate promising results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance with automatically discovered architectures.

**Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful approach, I believe it falls short of the bar for ICLR acceptance primarily due to a lack of sufficient novelty and depth in analyzing the learned architectures, alongside some concerns regarding reproducibility and experimental rigor. The core concept builds heavily on established ideas (policy gradients, RNNs, neuroevolution), and the contribution feels more like an engineering application of these techniques rather than a significant theoretical advance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The application of reinforcement learning to architecture search is not entirely new. Previous work (e.g., neuroevolution) has explored similar ideas. While the use of an RNN controller is a sensible choice, the fundamental concept isn't groundbreaking. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from existing approaches beyond the specific implementation details. The improvements achieved, while respectable, don't seem large enough to justify the complexity of the proposed method, especially considering the substantial computational cost.

2. **Insufficient Analysis of Learned Architectures:** The paper presents the final architectures discovered but lacks a detailed analysis of *why* these architectures perform well. What specific design patterns emerge? Are there common motifs? A deeper understanding of the learned structures would provide more insight and contribute to the broader understanding of neural network design principles. Simply stating that the architectures are competitive is insufficient; the paper needs to explain *what* makes them successful. The qualitative discussion of skip connections and filter sizes in the CIFAR-10 result is a good start, but more thorough analysis is needed.

3. **Computational Cost & Reproducibility Concerns:** The method is computationally expensive, requiring training numerous child networks. This raises concerns about accessibility for researchers without significant computational resources. While the authors mention distributed training, the scale of the infrastructure used is substantial. A more detailed breakdown of computational resources (GPU hours, etc.) is needed. Furthermore, while the authors state intent to release code, reproducibility remains a concern without clear details regarding hyperparameter tuning, training procedures, and random seeds. The search space itself feels rather large and not fully explored, and a more careful ablation study of different components within the search space would improve confidence.

4. **Baseline Comparisons:** The comparison to random search is presented, but a more comprehensive comparison against other automated architecture search techniques (e.g., Bayesian optimization-based methods) is necessary. The related work section acknowledges these methods but doesn't provide a direct empirical comparison.



**Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of different components, such as skip connections, parallel training, and the choice of the reward function.
* **Visualization of the Search Process:** Include visualizations of the search process, showing how the controller's policy evolves over time. This would provide a better understanding of the learning dynamics.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters, such as the learning rate of the controller, the size of the controller RNN, and the exploration-exploitation trade-off in the reinforcement learning process.
* **Transfer Learning Analysis:** Expand the transfer learning experiments. Demonstrating the generalizability of the learned cell beyond the specific tasks tested would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Clarify the Search Space:** Provide a more precise definition of the search space. What are the constraints and limitations imposed on the architectures that can be generated?
* **Theoretical Justification:** While not essential, providing some theoretical justification for the effectiveness of the RNN controller could strengthen the paper.
* **Detailed Computational Budget:** Provide a clear breakdown of the computational resources required for the experiments.




**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost of NAS compared to other automated architecture search methods?
* What strategies were employed to prevent the controller from getting stuck in local optima?
* How sensitive are the results to the choice of the baseline function in the reinforcement learning process?
* Can you provide more insight into the learned architectures? Are there any recurring patterns or design principles that emerge?
* What steps are being taken to ensure the reproducibility of the results? Specifically, will you be releasing code and random seeds?
* Were any efforts made to simplify the search space or reduce the computational cost without significantly sacrificing performance?
