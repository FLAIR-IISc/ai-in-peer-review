This paper presents Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a gradient-based method for automatically designing neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (controller) to generate network architectures represented as variable-length strings. The controller is trained using a policy gradient method, with the accuracy of the generated "child" network on a validation set serving as the reward signal. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of NAS on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results.

**Reject**

The primary reason for rejection is a lack of clarity and detail regarding critical experimental setups, specifically concerning the search space and hyperparameter tuning beyond the architecture itself. While the results are promising, reproducing them would be challenging without a more comprehensive description of the experimental conditions. Furthermore, the rapid pace of development in NAS necessitates a more thorough comparison to more recent and established methods.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Insufficient Detail on Search Space:** The paper outlines the search space but lacks granularity. For example, on CIFAR-10, the ranges for filter heights, widths, and the number of filters are provided, but it's unclear if all combinations were explored or if any constraints were imposed (e.g., memory limits, computational costs). The inclusion of skip connections is mentioned, but the specific strategy for their implementation (e.g., all possible connections, a limited subset) is not detailed. This makes it difficult to assess the complexity and breadth of the search.

2. **Limited Comparison to Baseline Methods:** While the paper acknowledges related work, the comparisons to existing NAS methods are minimal. The field has rapidly evolved since the initial submission of this work, with numerous advances in search strategies, search space design, and computational efficiency. A comparison against more recent state-of-the-art NAS algorithms (e.g., DARTS, ENAS) is crucial to establish the true contribution of this work.

3. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions a "small grid search" for learning rate, weight decay, etc., after finding the best architecture, but doesn’t detail the grid itself or the search procedure. The performance of a neural network is highly sensitive to these hyperparameters, and a rigorous tuning process is essential. Without this detail, it's unclear whether the reported results are robust or specific to a particular hyperparameter configuration.

4. **Practical Concerns and Scalability:** While the distributed training approach is mentioned, the scalability of this method to larger and more complex datasets is not discussed. The computational cost of training multiple child networks during the search process is substantial. An analysis of the computational resources required and potential strategies for reducing this cost would strengthen the paper.



**Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

*   **Detailed Search Space Definition:** Provide a precise and complete specification of the search space, including any constraints or restrictions.
*   **Comprehensive Baseline Comparison:** Include a detailed comparison against a wider range of NAS algorithms, including more recent methods.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning Procedure:** Provide a complete description of the hyperparameter tuning process, including the ranges explored, the search strategy used, and the validation set used for evaluation.
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:** Analyze the computational cost of the NAS process, including the number of GPU hours required and the memory footprint. Discuss potential strategies for reducing the computational cost.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the NAS framework, such as skip connections and the recurrent controller.
*   **Clarify Reward Shaping:** The "cubing" of the validation accuracy in the reward function is somewhat arbitrary. A justification for this choice or a sensitivity analysis would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed description of the search space, including any constraints or restrictions imposed during the architecture search?
2.  How does the performance of NAS compare to more recent state-of-the-art NAS algorithms, such as DARTS or ENAS, on the CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank datasets?
3.  Can you provide the specific hyperparameter ranges and search strategy used for tuning the hyperparameters of the child networks?
4.  What is the total computational cost (e.g., GPU hours) required to run the NAS process for CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank?
5.  Were any specific techniques used to prevent the controller from generating invalid or unstable architectures? If so, could you please describe them?
