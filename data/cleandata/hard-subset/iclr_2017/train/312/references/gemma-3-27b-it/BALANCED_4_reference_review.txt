## Review of Neural Architecture Search

This paper presents Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a gradient-based method for automatically discovering neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (controller) to generate architecture descriptions, train the resulting “child” network, and update the controller’s parameters via policy gradient to favor architectures with higher validation accuracy. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of NAS on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results with newly designed architectures.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper presents a compelling idea with promising results, the presentation lacks crucial details hindering a full assessment of its scientific rigor and reproducibility. The experimental setup, despite achieving strong performance, feels somewhat of a black box, and the significance of certain design choices remains unclear.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Lack of Detail in Experimental Setup:** The paper mentions significant complexity in the training process (distributed training, asynchronous updates, parameter servers) but provides limited implementation details. For example, specific hyperparameters for the controller RNN (beyond layer size and learning rate) are missing.  Without these details, reproducing the results is challenging.  The precise data augmentation pipeline for CIFAR-10, beyond a brief mention of whitening and cropping, is also lacking. This level of detail is essential for a strong ICLR submission.

2. **Justification of Design Choices:** Several architectural and training decisions appear somewhat arbitrary without sufficient justification. Why was the base number set to 8 for recurrent cell search? What motivated the specific reward function (accuracy cubed)?  The impact of these choices on the search process isn’t explored. The rationale for the increasing layer schedule during training isn't fully convincing, and a more systematic exploration of schedule parameters could strengthen the results.

3. **Limited Ablation Studies:** While control experiments are mentioned (adding more functions to the search space, comparison against random search), they are insufficient to fully understand the contribution of each component of the NAS framework. A more thorough ablation study, dissecting the impact of skip connections, the parameter server architecture, and the controller architecture itself, would be valuable.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Clarify the Search Space:**  A more formal and detailed definition of the architecture search space would be beneficial.  A diagram illustrating the possible connections and layer types would improve clarity.
* **Controller Architecture:** The choice of an LSTM as the controller is reasonable, but a brief discussion of alternatives (e.g., Transformers) and their potential advantages/disadvantages would strengthen the paper.
* **Computational Cost:** A more thorough analysis of the computational cost of NAS is needed. Quantifying the total training time and resources required to find a good architecture would be useful for the community. It's crucial to understand the trade-off between automation and computational expense.
* **Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to random search is a good start, but a more comprehensive comparison to other NAS methods (even if they have limitations) would provide a better context for the results.
* **Figures & Appendix:** The reference to figures in Appendix A is helpful, but the figures themselves should be included within the main body of the paper to facilitate understanding.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  Investigate the sensitivity of the discovered architectures to different hyperparameters (learning rate, weight decay, dropout) during the final training phase. This could reveal how robust the NAS-discovered architectures are.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed description of the hyperparameters used for the controller RNN (e.g., regularization, initialization schemes)?
2.  What motivated the choice of cubing the validation accuracy as the reward signal? Did you explore alternative reward functions?
3.  How sensitive are the discovered architectures to different data augmentation strategies on CIFAR-10?
4.  What is the approximate total compute cost (GPU hours) required to run the NAS algorithm for the CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank experiments?
5.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for choosing a base number of 8 for the recurrent cell search? How did you explore different base numbers?
6.  The paper mentions “compilation failures” with skip connections. Can you provide more detail on the specific challenges encountered and the effectiveness of the techniques used to circumvent them?
