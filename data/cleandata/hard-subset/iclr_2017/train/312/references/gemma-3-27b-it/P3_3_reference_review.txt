## ICLR 2024 Review: Neural Architecture Search

**1. Summary**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel gradient-based approach to automating the design of deep neural network architectures. The core idea is to leverage a recurrent neural network (controller) to generate architectural descriptions, train the resulting “child” networks, and then use the validation accuracy as a reward signal to update the controller via policy gradients. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of NAS on both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank), achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results with architectures discovered automatically. They further present improvements like skip connections and parallel training strategies to enhance the search process and scalability.

**2. Decision & Key Reasons**

**Accept.** This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning. The approach is innovative, the results are compelling, and the paper is well-written and provides sufficient experimental detail. The demonstrated ability to discover novel architectures that outperform human-designed ones is a strong indicator of the method’s potential.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The idea of framing architecture search as a reinforcement learning problem with a differentiable controller is highly innovative. Prior work in neural architecture search (e.g., evolutionary algorithms) often struggled with scalability and/or required significant computational resources. This gradient-based approach offers a more efficient and scalable alternative.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are very impressive. Achieving competitive performance, and even surpassing state-of-the-art on language modeling, with architectures automatically discovered is a major accomplishment. The speed-up on CIFAR-10 is a practical benefit that further strengthens the impact of the work.
* **Thorough Methodology:** The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the NAS algorithm, including the controller RNN, the training procedure, and the reward function. The extensions addressing skip connections and parallelization demonstrate a thoughtful approach to tackling the challenges of scaling NAS.
* **Related Work Discussion:** The related work section effectively positions NAS within the context of hyperparameter optimization, neuroevolution, and program synthesis. The authors clearly articulate the advantages of their approach over existing methods.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors state their intention to release code, which will be crucial for reproducibility and further research.  The detailed description of the experimental setup aids in this regard.



**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement)**

These suggestions are intended to strengthen the paper further and are not critical to the acceptance decision.

* **Ablation Studies:** While the paper provides a comprehensive set of experiments, a more detailed ablation study investigating the contribution of different components (e.g., skip connections, the baseline function in the REINFORCE algorithm) would be valuable. This would provide deeper insights into the effectiveness of the individual components and their interactions.
* **Search Space Exploration:** The paper mentions the challenges of defining the search space. Discussing strategies for more systematically exploring and characterizing the search space (e.g., using prior knowledge or heuristics) could be beneficial.  What were the considerations that led to the choice of the specific search space used in the experiments?
* **Computational Cost Analysis:**  While the authors mention the computational cost, a more detailed analysis comparing the total computational cost of NAS to the cost of manually designing and tuning a comparable architecture would provide a more complete picture of the practical benefits.
* **Visualization of Architectures:** The mention of visualizing discovered architectures (Figure 7 in the Appendix) is good. More visual representations of the architectures learned on the Penn Treebank task would be helpful for understanding the characteristics of the discovered recurrent cells.
* **Generalization across datasets:** While results are strong on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, exploring performance on other datasets, especially those with different characteristics (e.g., larger images, different data modalities), would strengthen the claim of general applicability.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

* **Regarding the Controller:**  What impact did different controller architectures (e.g., different numbers of layers, hidden units) have on the performance of NAS? Was any hyperparameter optimization performed on the controller itself?
* **Regarding the Reward Function:** The cubic reward function is used in CIFAR-10.  Why was a cubic function chosen specifically, and how sensitive are the results to different reward shaping strategies?
* **Regarding Scalability:** While the paper addresses parallel training, what are the practical limitations of scaling NAS to even larger and more complex datasets and architectures?  Are there inherent bottlenecks in the approach?
* **Regarding Transfer Learning:**  The results on WMT14 are promising.  Did you observe any cases where the NAS-discovered cell *performed worse* than a standard LSTM, and if so, what were the characteristics of those cases?  What insights did you gain from the transfer learning experiments that can inform future NAS research?
* **Regarding Search Space:** You mention the complexity of search space. Could you expand on how the initial search space was defined? Were there any prior assumptions or expert knowledge incorporated into the design of the search space?



In conclusion, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of automated machine learning. I believe it is worthy of acceptance into ICLR.
