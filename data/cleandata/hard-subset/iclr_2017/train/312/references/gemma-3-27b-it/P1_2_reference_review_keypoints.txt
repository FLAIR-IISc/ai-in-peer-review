- Summary: Proposes using an RNN controller and policy gradients to generate and optimize neural network architectures, tested on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank.  
- Decision: Recommend accept with minor revisions.  
- Novelty: Introducing an RNN controller to frame architecture search as a sequential decision process is novel and meaningful.  
- Strong empirical results: Automatically discovered architectures achieve competitive or state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank.  
- Technical soundness: Method builds on policy gradients and RNNs, with practical extensions like skip connections and parallel/asynchronous training.  
- Clear presentation: Paper is generally well-written and diagrams aid understanding, though some figures are missing.  
- Diagram clarity (feedback): Missing figures (e.g., parallel training, skip connections, discovered cells, architectures) must be included for clarity.  
- Search space details (feedback): Need a more detailed description of the search space constraints (max depth, allowable layer types, etc.).  
- Baseline comparison (feedback): Include more detailed comparisons to other NAS methods beyond random search to strengthen claims.  
- Ablation studies (feedback): More thorough ablations needed to quantify component contributions (e.g., skip connections, baseline in policy gradient).  
- Computational cost (feedback): Provide concrete analysis of total compute required for search and comparison to manual design/tuning costs.  
- Controller RNN question: Why a two-layer LSTM controller, and were other controller architectures (e.g., transformers) evaluated?  
- Reward function question: Why use the maximum validation accuracy over the last 5 epochs as the reward, and how sensitive are results to the window size?  
- Scalability question: Clarify how well the method scales to larger datasets and more complex architectures and any inherent limitations.  
- Transfer learning question: Describe challenges or modifications needed when transferring the learned RNN cell to different tasks (e.g., GNMT).
