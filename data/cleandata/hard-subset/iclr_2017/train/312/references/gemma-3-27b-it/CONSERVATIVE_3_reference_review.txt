This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network controller trained with policy gradients. The core idea is to represent an architecture as a string generated by the controller, train the resulting "child" network, and use the child's performance as a reward signal to refine the controller's search strategy. The authors demonstrate promising results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance with architectures discovered by their method.

**Reject.** While the concept is intriguing, the paper presents a significant leap in complexity without sufficient justification or rigorous evaluation compared to existing, well-established methods in neural architecture design and hyperparameter optimization. The claims of surpassing human-designed architectures "from scratch" feel overstated given the inherent inductive biases and extensive prior knowledge embedded in the search space and training methodology.

The primary concern lies with the lack of thorough comparison to strong baselines. The paper mentions related work like Bayesian optimization and neuroevolution but does not provide a comprehensive empirical comparison demonstrating a clear advantage of NAS. Simply stating that NAS can search a variable-length space is insufficient; a quantitative demonstration of its efficiency *over* methods designed to handle such spaces is crucial. The CIFAR-10 results, while competitive, lack context. How does the search time and computational cost of NAS compare to manually designing a comparable architecture or using established techniques like random search or grid search combined with manual refinement? The 1.05x speedup is marginal and doesn't justify the considerable complexity introduced by the NAS framework. 

Furthermore, the reliance on policy gradients, known for their high variance, necessitates a very stable and well-tuned training procedure. The details provided on the distributed training setup are complex and raise concerns about reproducibility. It’s unclear how sensitive the results are to the choice of hyperparameters for the controller itself. 

The Penn Treebank results are more compelling, but again, the context is missing. Are the gains substantial enough to justify the additional computational overhead of NAS? A comparison to recently published, optimized LSTM variants would be valuable. The claim of improved speed is also suspect without details on the actual inference time.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Search Space Definition:** The paper doesn't provide a clear and concise description of the entire search space. The choices made in defining the search space significantly impact the results, and it's important to understand the limitations and potential biases introduced by these choices.
*   **Ablation Studies:** More ablation studies are needed to understand the contribution of specific components, such as skip connections and the asynchronous parameter server.
*   **Statistical Significance:** The performance differences reported should be accompanied by statistical significance tests to ensure they are not due to random chance.
*   **Reproducibility:** The complexity of the setup makes reproducibility a concern. Providing more detailed instructions and potentially releasing the code is crucial.
*   **Computational Cost:** A detailed analysis of the computational cost of NAS, including training the controller and evaluating the child networks, is necessary to assess its practicality.
*   **Transferability:** While the authors tested transfer learning on a character-level language model, the results don’t generalize to another downstream task that is a more complex learning scenario.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   How does the computational cost (time and resources) of NAS compare to manually designing a similar-performing architecture or using established hyperparameter optimization techniques (e.g., Bayesian optimization)?
*   Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the search space, including the number of possible architectures and the probability distribution of architectures explored during training?
*   What is the sensitivity of the results to the hyperparameters of the controller RNN?
*   What steps have been taken to ensure the reproducibility of the results?
*   Can you explain the rationale behind the specific choices made in defining the search space and how these choices might influence the results?
*   How do the results change with different baseline functions for the policy gradient update?
