## Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel approach to automating neural network architecture design. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (controller) to generate architecture descriptions, train the resulting "child" networks, and use the validation accuracy as a reward signal to update the controller via policy gradients. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of NAS on both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank), achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results, and even discovering novel architectures.  The method addresses a key bottleneck in deep learning – the need for expert knowledge and substantial time investment in architecture engineering.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept.** This paper presents a significant and well-executed contribution to the field of automated machine learning. The idea is novel, the implementation seems sound, and the results are compelling, demonstrating the potential to automate a previously very manual and expertise-driven process.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of using a recurrent network to generate architectures and learning through reinforcement learning is genuinely new and inventive. It moves beyond traditional hyperparameter optimization and offers a more flexible, generative approach.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The performance on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank is impressive.  Achieving performance comparable to, or exceeding, hand-designed architectures is a strong validation of the approach. The fact that the learned architectures exhibit interesting characteristics (e.g., rectangular filters, skip connections) suggests the NAS is discovering non-obvious design choices.
* **Technical Soundness:** The paper clearly outlines the method, including details on the controller network, the training process (REINFORCE with baseline), and the distributed training scheme. The explanations of techniques like skip connections and generating recurrent cell architectures are clear and well-reasoned.
* **Addressing Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the challenges of variable-length search spaces and high variance in policy gradient estimation, and propose practical solutions (baseline, distributed training) to mitigate them.
* **Reproducibility:**  The authors state they will release code and the discovered architectures, which significantly enhances the impact and reproducibility of the work.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

* **Search Space Exploration:** While the paper demonstrates success with the chosen search space, it would be valuable to discuss the limitations and potential biases introduced by it. A more detailed analysis of how the search space design impacts the discovered architectures would be helpful.
* **Computational Cost:**  The computational cost of NAS is substantial, as highlighted by the need for distributed training. A more detailed discussion of the resource requirements and potential strategies for reducing them would be beneficial.  Could transfer learning or meta-learning techniques be used to accelerate the search process for new tasks?
* **Generalization:** While the results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are encouraging, further evaluation on other datasets and tasks would strengthen the claims about the generalizability of NAS.
* **Ablation Studies:**  More comprehensive ablation studies exploring the impact of different components (e.g., skip connections, specific reward function terms) on performance would be valuable.
* **Visualization of Search Process:**  Visualizing the evolution of the controller's policy over training iterations could provide insights into the search process and help understand how the controller learns to discover effective architectures.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* The paper mentions a schedule for increasing the number of layers during training. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind this schedule and its impact on the search process? Were different schedules explored?
* You mention "compilation failures" when using skip connections. Could you provide more specific examples of these failures and the strategies employed to handle them?
*  Regarding the random search baseline: While you present a comparative curve, what was the total computational budget allocated to the random search experiment? A fair comparison would ideally involve comparable resources.
*  You achieve SOTA results on PTB.  Did you observe any significant differences in the types of architectures generated for image classification vs. language modeling, and if so, what might explain these differences?
* The search space seems quite limited in the initial CIFAR-10 experiments (strides fixed to 1). Was this intentional to simplify the problem, or were there practical constraints that led to this decision?



Overall, this is a well-written and impactful paper that addresses a critical challenge in deep learning. The proposed approach is innovative, the results are compelling, and the paper is likely to stimulate significant research in automated architecture design. I recommend accepting this paper for publication at ICLR.
