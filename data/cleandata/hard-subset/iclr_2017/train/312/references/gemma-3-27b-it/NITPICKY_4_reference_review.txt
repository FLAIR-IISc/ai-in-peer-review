This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network controller trained with policy gradients. The core idea is to represent architectures as strings generated by the controller, train the resulting "child" networks, and use the validation accuracy as a reward signal to improve the controller’s architecture generation process. The authors demonstrate promising results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving competitive performance with human-designed architectures.

**Reject.** While the idea of automating architecture search is compelling and the results are promising, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and inconsistencies in presentation, experimental detail, and justification, ultimately leading to a lack of confidence in the robustness and generalizability of the proposed approach.

The primary reason for rejection stems from a pervasive lack of rigor in the experimental setup and reporting. The paper frequently lacks crucial details necessary to reproduce the results. For example, the precise hyperparameters used for training the "child" networks (beyond learning rate, momentum, and weight decay) are not fully specified. The motivation for specific choices – such as the base number of 8 in the recurrent cell search – feels arbitrary. The claim of 1.05x speedup on CIFAR-10 is interesting but lacks sufficient detail regarding the hardware used and precise inference timings. The architecture shown in Appendix A, Figure 7, is referred to as having "many rectangular filters" but the actual filter sizes aren’t explicitly stated in the text. Similarly, while distributed training is mentioned, the rationale behind the specific choices of S, K, and m values isn’t provided. The impact of these parameters on the overall performance and stability of the training process is unclear.

Furthermore, the paper contains numerous stylistic and presentation issues. There is excessive use of citations for well-established concepts (e.g., Hinton et al., 2012 for the success of deep learning). The writing is sometimes imprecise, making it difficult to follow the technical details. For instance, the explanation of skip connections and the set-selection attention mechanism is convoluted and could benefit from clearer diagrams and more intuitive descriptions. The explanation of the controller predicting layer types lacks clarity. The term “compilation failures” is used without a precise definition. The repeated use of phrases like "this is more challenging" without further elaboration weakens the argumentation.

The control experiments, while included, are not thoroughly analyzed. The claim that adding more functions to the search space only leads to "somewhat comparable" performance is vague. The comparison to random search lacks statistical significance testing and a clear demonstration of the benefits of the policy gradient approach beyond a visual comparison of learning curves (Figure 6). The justification for choosing specific baseline models for comparison feels somewhat ad-hoc.

**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

*   **Reproducibility:** Provide a comprehensive list of all hyperparameters used for training both the controller and the child networks. Include details about the hardware setup (e.g., GPU type, number of cores).
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to analyze the impact of different components of the NAS framework (e.g., skip connections, parallel training, baseline function).
*   **Statistical Significance:** Perform statistical significance tests to validate the performance improvements reported in the experiments.
*   **Clarity and Precision:** Revise the writing to improve clarity and precision. Avoid vague statements and provide concrete details whenever possible.
*   **Detailed Explanation:** Elaborate on the rationale behind key design choices, such as the choice of the base number for the recurrent cell search.
*   **Error Analysis:** Provide an error analysis of the architectures generated by the NAS framework. What types of errors do they make?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the NAS framework to the choice of hyperparameters.
*   **Search Space Exploration:** Discuss how the size of the search space affects the performance and scalability of the NAS framework.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What is the computational cost of the NAS process compared to manually designing and tuning a comparable architecture?
*   How does the performance of NAS vary with the size of the training dataset?
*   Have you explored different reward functions beyond the maximum validation accuracy?
*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the “compilation failures” and the techniques used to circumvent them?
*   What steps are being taken to ensure that the released code is well-documented and easy to use?
