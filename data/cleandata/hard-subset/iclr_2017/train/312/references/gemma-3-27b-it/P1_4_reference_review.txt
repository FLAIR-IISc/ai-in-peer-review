## Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

**1. Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to Neural Architecture Search (NAS) using a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller to generate network architectures. The core idea is to treat architecture design as a sequential decision process, where the controller learns to generate strings representing network configurations. These configurations are then evaluated by training the corresponding "child" networks, and the controller's policy is updated via a reinforcement learning framework (REINFORCE). The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method by discovering novel architectures that outperform human-designed models on both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank) tasks.  This work represents a significant step towards automating the laborious process of neural network architecture design.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** - This paper presents a compelling and technically sound approach to a challenging problem. The results are strong, demonstrating the ability of the proposed method to discover competitive architectures automatically. The clear presentation and thorough experimentation contribute to the overall strength of the paper.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty & Significance:** Automating architecture search is a crucial step towards democratizing deep learning. While NAS isn't entirely new, the proposed gradient-based method utilizing a recurrent controller is a significant departure from previous approaches, offering greater flexibility in exploring the architecture space compared to methods with fixed-length search spaces.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported improvements on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are impressive. Achieving a state-of-the-art perplexity on the Penn Treebank dataset and competitive performance on CIFAR-10 validates the effectiveness of the approach. The fact that the found architectures surpass human-designed ones is particularly noteworthy.
* **Technical Soundness:** The use of policy gradients (REINFORCE) is a well-established technique for optimizing non-differentiable rewards, and its application to this problem appears justified. The inclusion of techniques to mitigate variance in the gradient estimates (baseline function) and accelerate training (distributed training, skip connections) demonstrate a thoughtful approach to practical implementation.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The method is described clearly, and the experimental setup is sufficiently detailed.



**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Search Space Exploration:**  The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the search space.  How was the chosen search space defined?  What are the potential limitations of this specific space? A visual representation of the search space’s structure could be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention distributed training, a more thorough analysis of the computational resources required for NAS would be useful. Providing numbers related to GPU hours, and the cost of running the experiments, will give readers a more realistic understanding of the practicality of the method.
* **Generalization:** While the transfer learning experiments are encouraging, exploring the generalizability of the found architectures to other datasets (beyond Penn Treebank character modeling) would further strengthen the claims.
* **Ablation Studies:** More detailed ablation studies could isolate the contribution of individual components of the method (e.g., skip connections, distributed training).  For example, what is the performance difference *without* skip connections?  
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to the hyperparameters of the controller RNN (e.g., hidden layer size, learning rate) would be helpful.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the controller:** What factors influenced the choice of a two-layer LSTM for the controller? Were other controller architectures considered (e.g., Transformers)?
* **Regarding the reward function:** The reward function uses the cubed validation accuracy. Why was this specific function chosen?  Were other reward shaping strategies explored?  Did the choice of reward function significantly impact the search process?
* **Regarding the search space:** How was the range of hyperparameters defined (e.g., filter sizes, number of filters)? Was this based on prior knowledge or a systematic exploration?
* **Regarding the baseline function:** The paper mentions using an exponential moving average of previous accuracies as a baseline.  How was the smoothing factor for the EMA determined?  Did different baseline functions significantly impact the results?
* **Regarding scalability:** What are the limitations of this method when scaling to even larger datasets and more complex architectures (e.g., ImageNet)? Are there potential strategies to address these limitations?



In conclusion, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of neural architecture search. The method is innovative, well-supported by experimental results, and clearly presented. Addressing the additional feedback and answering the questions posed above will further strengthen the paper and its impact.
