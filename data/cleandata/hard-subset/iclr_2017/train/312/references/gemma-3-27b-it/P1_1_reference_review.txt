## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper:** Neural Architecture Search

**Reviewer:** AI Assistant

**1. Summary of Claims:**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel method for automatically designing neural network architectures. The core idea is to employ a recurrent neural network (controller) to generate architecture descriptions, train the resulting “child” networks, and then use reinforcement learning to update the controller based on the child network’s performance.  The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by achieving competitive results on CIFAR-10 (image classification) and Penn Treebank (language modeling), surpassing existing hand-designed architectures and state-of-the-art models in some cases. They also showcase improvements in training speed and generalization capabilities.

**2. Decision & Key Reasons:**

**Reject.** While the paper presents a significant and influential idea (NAS is a foundational work in the field), the presentation lacks the depth and rigor expected for ICLR today. The experiments, while promising for their time, are limited in scope and lack detailed comparisons to more recent NAS techniques. More critically, the paper feels more like a proof-of-concept rather than a thoroughly evaluated and analyzed contribution.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Limited Experimental Detail:** The experimental setup descriptions are relatively high-level. While key parameters are mentioned (e.g., learning rates, epochs), the rationale behind choices and the sensitivity to different hyperparameter settings are not thoroughly discussed. A more comprehensive ablation study would be highly beneficial.
* **Lack of Comparison to Baseline NAS Methods:** The related work section acknowledges other NAS methods (neuroevolution, Bayesian optimization), but the experiments do not directly compare against them. Demonstrating superiority over alternative NAS algorithms is crucial for establishing the value of the proposed approach. The comparison is mostly with hand-designed architectures.
* **Scaling and Computational Cost:** The paper mentions distributed training to accelerate the search process. However, the actual computational cost remains a significant barrier for broader adoption. A more detailed analysis of the computational resources required for the NAS process (e.g., GPU hours) would be valuable.
* **Reproducibility Concerns:** While the authors mention releasing code, the lack of detailed experimental setup information and the complexity of the distributed training setup raise concerns about reproducibility.
* **Presentation of Results:** The presentation of results could be more systematic. Providing error bars or statistical significance tests for performance comparisons would increase confidence in the findings.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Expand Related Work:** The related work section could benefit from a more detailed discussion of subsequent NAS approaches that have built upon this work (e.g., DARTS, ENAS).  Positioning the paper within the broader NAS landscape would strengthen the contribution.
* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to understand the impact of individual components of the NAS framework (e.g., skip connections, controller architecture).
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** Investigate the sensitivity of the NAS process to different hyperparameters, such as the learning rate of the controller, the reward function, and the search space.
* **Computational Cost Analysis:** Provide a detailed breakdown of the computational resources required for the NAS process, including GPU hours, wall-clock time, and memory usage.
* **Clarify the Reward Function:** The reward function (validation accuracy cubed) could be further justified. Why was cubing chosen? What are the implications of using different reward functions?
* **Address Potential Instabilities:** NAS can be prone to instabilities during training. Discuss any strategies employed to mitigate these issues and provide insights into the robustness of the approach.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*  Given the explosion of NAS research since this paper was published, how do the results presented here compare to more recent and efficient NAS algorithms like DARTS or ENAS in terms of performance and computational cost?
*  What were the primary bottlenecks encountered during the distributed training process, and how were they addressed?
*  Can you provide more details on the search space used for the CIFAR-10 experiments, including the precise ranges of hyperparameters explored?
*  Could you elaborate on the choice of the exponential moving average baseline for the REINFORCE algorithm and how its performance compares to other baseline options?
*   What steps were taken to ensure the reproducibility of the results, and what resources would be needed to replicate the experiments?
* The paper focuses on a limited set of datasets. Have you explored the applicability of NAS to other domains, and if so, what were the key challenges encountered?



In conclusion, while this paper presents a groundbreaking idea, the limited experimental detail, lack of comparison to modern NAS methods, and concerns about reproducibility prevent me from recommending acceptance to ICLR at this time.  The work is historically important, but does not meet the current standards for publication in a top-tier conference.
