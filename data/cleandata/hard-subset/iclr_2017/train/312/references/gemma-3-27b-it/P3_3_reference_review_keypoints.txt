- Summary: Proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a gradient-based RL approach using an RNN controller to generate architectures, trained with policy gradients and evaluated on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, with skip connections and parallel training to improve scalability.

- Decision & Key Reasons: Recommendation to accept due to novelty, strong results, and clear presentation demonstrating automatically discovered architectures that outperform human-designed ones.

- Novelty & Significance: Frames architecture search as a reinforcement learning problem with a differentiable controller, offering a more efficient and scalable alternative to prior evolutionary methods.

- Strong Empirical Results: Achieves competitive/SOTA performance on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank and shows practical speed-ups, supporting the approach’s effectiveness.

- Thorough Methodology: Provides clear descriptions of the controller RNN, training procedure, reward function, and extensions for skip connections and parallelization.

- Related Work: Situates NAS well within hyperparameter optimization, neuroevolution, and program synthesis, articulating advantages over existing methods.

- Reproducibility: Authors plan to release code and give detailed experimental setup, aiding reproducibility.

- Ablation Studies (feedback): Recommend more detailed ablations to isolate contributions of components like skip connections and the REINFORCE baseline.

- Search Space Exploration (feedback): Suggest discussing strategies and rationale for defining and exploring the search space, and how prior knowledge influenced design choices.

- Computational Cost Analysis (feedback): Ask for a more detailed comparison of NAS’s total computational cost versus manual architecture design and tuning.

- Visualization of Architectures (feedback): Request more visualizations, especially of discovered recurrent cells for Penn Treebank, to clarify learned structures.

- Generalization Across Datasets (feedback): Encourage evaluation on more and diverse datasets to validate broader applicability beyond CIFAR-10 and PTB.

- Controller Questions: Ask how different controller architectures and hyperparameter choices affected NAS performance and whether controller tuning was performed.

- Reward Function Questions: Ask why a cubic reward was used for CIFAR-10 and how sensitive results are to reward shaping choices.

- Scalability Questions: Request discussion of practical limits and bottlenecks when scaling NAS to larger datasets and architectures despite parallel training.

- Transfer Learning Questions: Ask whether NAS-discovered cells ever underperformed standard LSTMs in transfer experiments and what insights arose from WMT14 results.

- Search Space Questions: Ask for clarification on how the initial search space was defined and whether expert knowledge or assumptions influenced it.

- Conclusion: Overall assessment is that the paper is a strong, valuable contribution to automated machine learning and is worthy of acceptance.
