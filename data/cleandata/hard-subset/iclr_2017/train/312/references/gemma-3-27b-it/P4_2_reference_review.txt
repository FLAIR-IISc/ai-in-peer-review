This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a gradient-based method for automatically designing neural network architectures. The core idea is to utilize a recurrent neural network (controller) to generate the specifications of a “child network,” which is then trained and evaluated. The validation accuracy of the child network serves as a reward signal to update the controller via policy gradients, iteratively improving the architecture search process. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of NAS on both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank) tasks, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results.

**Reject.** While the idea of using reinforcement learning for neural architecture search is promising and the results are strong, the paper lacks sufficient detail and clarity regarding crucial implementation aspects and experimental setup, hindering a thorough assessment of its scientific rigor.

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of clarity in the experimental details. For example, the precise search space for CIFAR-10, especially regarding the interplay between different hyperparameters (filter sizes, number of filters, strides) and the scheduling of layer depth, is not fully explained. The statement that adding 40 filters improves performance to 3.65% seems almost tacked on and lacks a corresponding table or experiment demonstrating this clearly. A more detailed ablation study on the effect of different search space constraints and controller hyperparameters would be highly valuable. Furthermore, the baseline comparisons, while including relevant state-of-the-art methods, lack a strong random search baseline with comparable computational resources. While a random search result *is* presented, the justification for the computational budget given to the policy gradient method versus random search isn’t articulated.

Secondly, the method's scalability and computational cost are significant concerns. The distributed training setup is briefly described, but lacks essential details like the hardware specifications (GPU type, memory) and communication overhead. The authors state that training a single child network takes hours, which raises questions about the feasibility of applying this approach to larger datasets or more complex architectures. The claim of being 1.05x faster than the current best model on CIFAR-10 is interesting, but a more thorough speed and parameter comparison with all models would be more persuasive. The Penn Treebank experiments also feel somewhat superficial, with a focus on cell design rather than a full language model, and the transfer learning results, while encouraging, lack detailed analysis.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Search Space Definition:** Provide a more formal and comprehensive definition of the search space. A clear diagram or table outlining all possible hyperparameters and their ranges would greatly improve clarity.
*   **Controller Architecture:** Describe the controller RNN architecture in more detail, including the number of layers, hidden unit size, and activation functions. Justify the chosen architecture.
*   **Reward Function:** Explain the rationale behind the choice of cubing the validation accuracy as the reward. Explore alternative reward functions and their potential impact.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** Conduct a more rigorous comparison with random search, allocating comparable computational resources. Report the results clearly and statistically.
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:** Provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of the NAS process, including training time, hardware requirements, and communication overhead.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the method, such as skip connections and the baseline function.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the NAS process to different controller hyperparameters, such as the learning rate and the number of replicas.
*   **Reproducibility:** Include sufficient details to enable reproducibility of the results.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific strategies used to manage the computational cost of training the child networks, especially given that each training can take hours? What is the approximate cost to search for *one* architecture?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of baseline function? Did you explore alternative baseline functions and their impact on convergence speed and final performance?
3.  What is the impact of the number of controller replicas (K) and child replicas (m) on the performance and convergence speed?
4.  The paper mentions "compilation failures". Can you provide more details about what these failures are, and how the employed fixes address them?
5.  Can you provide more specific details on the hardware used for both controller and child network training?
