This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method leveraging a recurrent neural network controller to generate neural network architectures and optimize them using a policy gradient approach. The core idea – framing architecture design as a sequential decision process – is intriguing and the reported results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are promising, demonstrating the ability to discover novel architectures competitive with, and in some cases surpassing, human-designed models.

**Reject.** While the idea is novel and the results are encouraging, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and inconsistencies, a lack of thoroughness in experimental details, and questionable reporting practices, ultimately undermining confidence in the claims. The sheer *number* of these issues, despite the potential of the work, leads to a recommendation for rejection.

The paper is riddled with inconsistencies in notation and description. For example, the definition of *R* as the reward (validation accuracy) is clear, yet the baseline *b* is described as an exponential moving average *of previous architecture accuracies*, but this is never explicitly formalized. The update rule itself uses *Rk* representing the accuracy of the k-th architecture – a subscript *k* is used, but its relation to the broader *R* isn’t made clear. Similarly, the meaning of *m* shifts throughout the text (number of architectures in a batch vs. number of controller replicas). Such ambiguities hinder comprehension.

The experimental setup lacks crucial details. While the authors mention distributed training, the specific hardware configuration (number of GPUs per node, interconnect speed) and software framework versions aren’t provided. The parameter server scheme is described, but lacks crucial details such as the update frequency and synchronization strategy. The impact of these architectural choices on performance and scalability isn't addressed. The description of the search space is also somewhat vague. For instance, the choice of filter heights and widths [1, 3, 5, 7] is arbitrary and lacks justification. Why these specific values?  What effect do they have on the search process?

Furthermore, the presentation of results raises concerns. The reported improvements on Penn Treebank are significant (3.6 perplexity). However, the details of the baseline models used for comparison are insufficiently detailed – for example, what exact LSTM hyperparameters were used?  The claim of a 2x speedup on CIFAR-10 is presented without a rigorous benchmarking methodology. What metrics were used (e.g., images/second, FLOPs)? What hardware was the performance measured on? The statement that the authors "adjusted the hyperparameters" after finding the best cell structure on PTB is vague; what hyperparameters were tuned, and how?

The “Control Experiments” are perfunctory. Adding `max` and `sin` to the search space doesn't constitute a robust investigation of the method's sensitivity to search space modifications. The comparison against random search feels like a post-hoc justification rather than a carefully planned experiment. The fact that policy gradient outperforms random search isn’t surprising, but the *magnitude* of the improvement and its statistical significance aren’t convincingly demonstrated.

Finally, several minor stylistic and formatting issues detract from the paper’s readability. Redundant phrasing (e.g., repeatedly stating the problem) and verbose sentences are prevalent. The figures lack sufficient detail and clear labeling. The citation style is inconsistent in places.



**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

*   **Formalize Notation:** Provide a clear glossary of all symbols and variables used in the equations and throughout the text.
*   **Detailed Experimental Setup:** Provide a comprehensive description of the experimental setup, including hardware, software, hyperparameters, and training procedures.  Include standard deviation/error bars for all reported results.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to analyze the impact of different components of the NAS framework (e.g., skip connections, parameter server, baseline function).
*   **Statistical Significance:** Perform statistical significance tests to validate the reported improvements.
*   **Error Analysis:** Analyze the types of architectures discovered by NAS and identify any patterns or limitations.
*   **Code Release:** Ensure the released code is well-documented and reproducible.
*   **Clarify the Controller's Search Space:** A visual representation of the search space (e.g., a directed acyclic graph) would greatly enhance understanding.
*   **Address the computational cost of NAS**: NAS is computationally expensive. A discussion about scaling and potential optimizations is crucial.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources required to run the NAS experiments (e.g., number of GPUs, training time)?
*   What is the sensitivity of the results to the choice of the baseline function? Have you experimented with different baseline strategies?
*   How does the performance of NAS compare to other automated machine learning (AutoML) techniques, such as Bayesian optimization with Gaussian processes?
*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how the controller RNN learns to generate effective architectures? Are there any interpretable patterns in the controller’s behavior?
*   The paper describes a "local optimum" architecture on CIFAR-10. What further search strategies were attempted to escape this local optimum?
