This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller trained with policy gradients. The controller generates network architectures represented as strings, which are then evaluated by training the resulting “child” network. The validation accuracy of the child network serves as the reward signal to update the controller. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on CIFAR-10 (image classification) and Penn Treebank (language modeling), achieving competitive results and even surpassing state-of-the-art performance in some cases.

**Reject**

The primary reason for rejection is a lack of clarity and detail regarding the experimental setup, making it difficult to assess the reproducibility and significance of the results. While the paper claims state-of-the-art performance, crucial implementation details are glossed over. Additionally, the novelty, while present, feels incremental given the existing landscape of neural architecture search methods.

The supporting arguments for this decision are outlined below:

1. **Insufficient Experimental Detail:** The paper lacks sufficient detail concerning the search space definition. While the authors mention ranges for hyperparameters (e.g., filter sizes, number of filters), the complete search space and its constraints are not clearly defined. This makes it difficult to understand the scope of the search and the complexity of the problem. For example, the specific types of layers allowed beyond convolutional layers are introduced in Section 3.3 but their full extent isn't obvious. The statement that "to limit the search space complexity we have our model predict 13 layers" feels ad-hoc and lacks justification.

2. **Reproducibility Concerns:** While the authors state intent to release code, several crucial aspects are lacking in detail, hindering reproducibility. The baseline function used for variance reduction in the policy gradient is described as “an exponential moving average of the previous architecture accuracies” without specifying the averaging coefficient. The training details are also somewhat vague (e.g., learning rate schedules, specific optimizer settings beyond ADAM).

3. **Incremental Novelty:** The core idea of using reinforcement learning to search for neural architectures is not entirely novel (see related work). The contribution lies in the specific implementation using an RNN controller and the successful application to both convolutional and recurrent architectures. However, the presented method doesn’t represent a significant paradigm shift and feels like an engineering refinement of existing approaches. It's unclear what unique advantages this RNN controller approach offers over other NAS techniques, such as those using evolutionary algorithms or differentiable architecture search. The improvements over existing methods, while reported, seem modest in some cases and warrant more detailed analysis.

4. **Limited Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies presented are limited. While the authors mention “Control Experiment 1 – Adding more functions in the search space”, the results are not sufficiently detailed to assess the impact of this change. A more thorough exploration of the impact of different search space choices and controller configurations would strengthen the paper.



**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the search space:** Provide a comprehensive description of the search space, including all possible hyperparameters and their ranges. A table or diagram would be helpful.
*   **Provide more details on training:** Elaborate on the training procedure, including learning rate schedules, optimizer settings, batch sizes, and regularization techniques.
*   **Justify design choices:** Provide a rationale for specific design choices, such as the controller architecture (LSTM layers, hidden unit size), the reward function, and the search space constraints.
*   **Compare to other NAS methods:** Include a more comprehensive comparison to other state-of-the-art NAS methods, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.  A table comparing performance on standard benchmarks would be beneficial.
*   **Discuss computational cost:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the NAS process. How does the cost scale with the size of the search space and the complexity of the child networks?
*   **Explain the choice of cubing the reward:** The authors cube the reward signal, stating it is used for training, but the reasoning behind this choice is not apparent.
*   **Address the sensitivity of results to random seeds:** Was the procedure repeated with different random seeds? How stable are the results?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed description of the complete search space used in the CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank experiments, including all possible hyperparameters and their constraints?
2.  What is the specific averaging coefficient used in the exponential moving average baseline function?
3.  What specific advantages does the RNN controller approach offer over other NAS techniques, such as evolutionary algorithms or differentiable architecture search?
4.  How does the computational cost of your NAS method scale with the size of the search space and the complexity of the child networks?
5.  Were the experiments repeated with different random seeds, and how stable are the results?
6. Why was the validation accuracy cubed when used as a reward signal? What is the justification for this transformation?
