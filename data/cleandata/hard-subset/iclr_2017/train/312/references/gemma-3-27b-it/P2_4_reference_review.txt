## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper:** Neural Architecture Search

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel method for automating the design of deep neural network architectures. The core idea is to formulate architecture design as a reinforcement learning problem. A recurrent neural network (controller) generates architectures (strings of hyperparameters), which are then evaluated by training the corresponding “child network” on a given dataset. The accuracy of the child network serves as a reward signal, used to update the controller via policy gradients, iteratively improving its architecture generation capabilities. The authors demonstrate successful application of NAS to both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank), achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results.

**Decision:** **Accept**

**Key Reasons:**

This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning (AutoML). The idea of using reinforcement learning to explore the vast space of neural architectures is innovative and demonstrably effective. The reported results on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are compelling, showcasing the potential of NAS to discover architectures that rival or surpass human-designed ones. The paper is well-written and clearly explains a complex methodology.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty:** The application of reinforcement learning to neural architecture search is a genuinely novel idea. Prior work relied on more constrained search spaces or less flexible methods.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are strong. Achieving competitive performance on standard benchmarks like CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank provides substantial evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Specifically, surpassing previous state-of-the-art on Penn Treebank is a significant achievement.
* **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the methodology, including the controller RNN, the policy gradient training process, and the techniques used to accelerate training (parallelism, skip connections). The inclusion of a baseline comparison with random search further strengthens the validity of the results.
* **Impact:** Automating the architecture design process has the potential to democratize deep learning, enabling researchers and practitioners without specialized expertise to build high-performing models. This work is a major step towards realizing that potential.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

* **Ablation Studies:** While the results are impressive, a more detailed ablation study would be beneficial.  For example, it would be valuable to analyze the contribution of different components of the method, such as skip connections or the specific choice of controller RNN architecture.
* **Computational Cost Discussion:** The paper mentions the significant computational cost associated with NAS.  A more thorough discussion of this cost, including a breakdown of the resources required for training, would be helpful for potential users. Comparisons to the cost of human expert design could also be valuable.
* **Generalization to other Datasets:**  Demonstrating the performance of NAS on a wider range of datasets (e.g., ImageNet) would further strengthen the generalizability of the findings.
* **Visualization of Discovered Architectures:** While an example architecture is provided in the appendix, more detailed visualizations of the discovered architectures, perhaps highlighting common patterns or unique features, would be insightful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. **Scalability:** The paper mentions using a parameter server to accelerate training.  What is the practical limit to the size of the search space that can be effectively explored with this approach, and what modifications might be necessary to scale NAS to even larger problems (e.g., ImageNet)?
2. **Controller Architecture Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to the choice of the controller RNN architecture (e.g., number of layers, hidden unit size)? Did you explore different controller architectures, and if so, what were the findings?
3. **Reward Shaping:** You use the validation accuracy cubed as a reward signal. Did you experiment with other reward shaping techniques, and if so, what impact did they have on the performance and stability of the training process?
4. **Transferability:** Beyond the GNMT example, have you investigated whether architectures discovered on one task (e.g., CIFAR-10) can be effectively transferred to other, related tasks?
