- Paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS): an RNN controller generates architectures, trains child models, and uses validation accuracy as a reward to update the controller via policy gradients.
- Decision: Accept — novel, well-executed contribution with compelling results and potential to automate architecture design.
- Novelty: Using a recurrent controller with reinforcement learning to generate architectures is a genuinely new and inventive approach.
- Strong empirical results: Achieves competitive or state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, discovering non-obvious architectural motifs.
- Technical soundness: Methodology (controller, REINFORCE with baseline, distributed training) is clearly explained and reasonable.
- Addresses limitations: Authors acknowledge variable-length search spaces and high variance, and propose baseline and distributed training to mitigate them.
- Reproducibility: Authors commit to releasing code and discovered architectures, enhancing reproducibility and impact.
- Suggestion — search space: Need discussion of how the chosen search space biases results and analysis of its limitations.
- Suggestion — computational cost: High resource requirements warrant detailed discussion and strategies to reduce cost (e.g., transfer/meta-learning).
- Suggestion — generalization: Additional evaluations on more datasets/tasks needed to support broad generalizability claims.
- Suggestion — ablation studies: More comprehensive ablations to quantify the impact of components like skip connections and reward design.
- Suggestion — visualization: Visualizing the controller’s policy evolution could shed light on the search dynamics.
- Questions for authors: Clarify layer-increase schedule rationale and alternatives, examples and handling of “compilation failures” with skip connections, computational budget for random search baseline, differences between image vs. language architectures, and rationale for fixed-stride search choices in CIFAR-10.
