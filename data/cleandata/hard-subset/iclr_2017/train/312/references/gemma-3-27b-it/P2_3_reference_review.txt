## ICLR Review

**Paper Title:** Neural Architecture Search

**Reviewer:** [Your Name/Identifier]

**1. Summary of Claims:**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel method for automating the design of neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (controller) to generate architectural descriptions (strings) which define the "child network." This child network is then trained, and its performance (accuracy/perplexity) is used as a reward signal to update the controller via policy gradient methods. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of NAS on both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank), achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results with automatically designed architectures. They further extend their method to include skip connections and recurrent cell design, showcasing the flexibility of their approach.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions.  The paper presents a compelling and innovative approach to a critical problem in deep learning – neural architecture design. The empirical results are strong, demonstrating the ability to find architectures that rival or surpass human-designed models.  The method is well-explained and the authors address potential issues such as training instability and computational cost.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** Automating neural architecture design is a significant undertaking. NAS provides a novel solution that moves beyond traditional hyperparameter optimization methods and explores a much larger, more flexible architecture space.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are impressive. Achieving performance comparable to, and in some cases exceeding, established architectures on these benchmarks is a strong indicator of the method's efficacy. The results on the Penn Treebank are particularly noteworthy, showcasing the potential for NAS to discover better recurrent cell structures.
* **Clear Explanation & Technical Soundness:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the approach. The use of policy gradients and reinforcement learning is justified and appropriately implemented. The improvements – skip connections, parameter server parallelism – are well-motivated and contribute to the practicality of the method.
* **Addressing Computational Cost:**  The authors acknowledge the computational expense of NAS and address it through parallel training and asynchronous updates, making the method more feasible for real-world applications.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Ablation Studies:** While the authors demonstrate improvements with skip connections, more detailed ablation studies could further clarify the contribution of each component. For example, how much does the search space expansion (e.g., allowing different strides) impact performance, and at what computational cost?
* **Discussion of Search Space:**  The choice of the search space (e.g., the allowed layer types, filter sizes) is crucial. The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of how these choices impact the search process and the resulting architectures.  Are there guidelines or heuristics for defining an effective search space?
* **Visualization of Search Process:**  A visualization of the search process itself – how the controller's policy evolves over time, what types of architectures are initially explored, and how the search converges – would provide valuable insight into the method’s behavior.
* **Generalization to Other Datasets/Tasks:** While the results are strong on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, it would be beneficial to briefly discuss the potential for applying NAS to other datasets and tasks, and any potential challenges that might arise.
* **Code Release:** The authors mention a code release, which is excellent.  Ensuring the code is well-documented and easy to reproduce will be critical for wider adoption.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Search Space Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to the specific choices made in defining the search space?  Did you experiment with different search space configurations, and if so, what were the trade-offs?
* **Controller Capacity:** How does the capacity of the controller RNN (e.g., number of layers, hidden units) impact the performance of NAS?  Is there a point of diminishing returns, or even overfitting, with larger controllers?
* **Exploration vs. Exploitation:**  How do you balance exploration (trying new architectures) and exploitation (refining promising architectures) during the policy gradient training process?  Are there any strategies to avoid getting stuck in local optima?
* **Scalability:** While you've addressed the computational cost, how does the scalability of NAS change with even larger datasets and more complex architectures?  What are the potential bottlenecks?
* **Transfer Learning Details:** Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process for the transfer learning experiments on Penn Treebank and WMT14?  How much tuning was required to achieve the reported improvements?



Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning. The strengths of the approach, coupled with the strong empirical results, make it a compelling candidate for acceptance at ICLR.  Addressing the feedback provided above will further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
