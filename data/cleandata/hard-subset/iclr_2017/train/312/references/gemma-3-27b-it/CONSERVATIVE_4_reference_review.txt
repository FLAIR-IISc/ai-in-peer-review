This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network controller trained with policy gradients. The core idea is to represent an architecture as a sequence of tokens generated by the controller, train the resulting "child" network, and use its validation accuracy as a reward signal to update the controller. The authors demonstrate promising results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance with novel architectures.

**Reject.** While the idea of automating architecture search is compelling, the paper presents a somewhat under-developed and insufficiently validated approach. The claims of significantly outperforming established architectures, particularly on the Penn Treebank dataset, require much stronger justification and a more comprehensive comparison to well-known baselines.

The primary concern lies with the limited scope of the experimental evaluation. The comparisons on CIFAR-10, while showing improvement, are against architectures of varying complexity and established approaches are not exhaustively compared. More importantly, the gains on Penn Treebank – a 3.6 perplexity improvement – are substantial and necessitate a detailed analysis, including comparisons to more recent and highly optimized LSTM variants. Simply stating that it outperforms "previous RNN and LSTM architectures" lacks the specificity needed to assess the true contribution. The experiments feel somewhat cherry-picked to highlight the method's success, rather than a rigorous evaluation of its limitations. The use of a moving average baseline, while standard, doesn’t sufficiently address the high variance inherent in policy gradient methods applied to such a complex search space.

Furthermore, the method’s computational cost is significant. The paper mentions distributed training, but the sheer number of architectures that need to be evaluated to find a good solution is immense. A clear discussion of the resources required and a comparison to the cost of manual architecture engineering is missing. The improvement in performance needs to be weighed against the substantial computational expense.

Finally, the paper lacks a thorough ablation study. What is the contribution of the skip connections? How sensitive is the method to the choice of controller architecture? What happens if a simpler reward function is used? Addressing these questions would strengthen the paper's conclusions and provide valuable insights into the method's behavior.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Baseline Clarity:** Please provide a table explicitly listing the architectures used for comparison on both datasets, including their exact configurations and reported performance (with standard deviations).
*   **Computational Cost:** Quantify the computational resources (GPU hours, etc.) required to run the search process.
*   **Ablation Study:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the impact of different components of the method (e.g., skip connections, controller size, reward function).
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests to demonstrate that the observed improvements are not due to random chance.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the results to key hyperparameters, such as the learning rate of the controller and the number of samples used for the policy gradient update.
*   **Visualization:** Provide more detailed visualizations of the architectures found, beyond the single example shown in the appendix.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   How does the performance of the discovered architectures degrade when the training data is reduced? Does the method overfit the search space?
*   What measures were taken to prevent the controller from exploiting spurious correlations in the search space?
*   Can you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process for the child networks?
*   Were any techniques used to accelerate the training of the child networks beyond the standard methods mentioned?
*   How does the method perform on datasets with different characteristics (e.g., larger image datasets, different language modeling tasks)?
