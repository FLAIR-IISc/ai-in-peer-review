This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a gradient-based approach to automatically designing neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (controller) to generate the architecture description, train the resulting “child” network, and update the controller’s parameters via policy gradient to favor architectures with higher validation accuracy. The authors demonstrate the method’s effectiveness on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results.

**Reject.** While the idea of using reinforcement learning for neural architecture search is intriguing and the experimental results are promising, the paper lacks sufficient detail and justification in several key areas to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The primary reason for rejection is the limited novelty in the overall approach and the insufficient discussion of its computational cost and scalability.

The NAS approach builds upon existing techniques from reinforcement learning, recurrent neural networks, and differentiable programming. While combining these is a reasonable strategy, the paper doesn’t adequately highlight what *specifically* distinguishes this work from prior attempts at similar problems. The related work section acknowledges prior attempts at neuro-evolution and Bayesian optimization, but doesn’t thoroughly discuss the advantages of this gradient-based approach *compared* to these alternatives. A clearer articulation of the innovation is needed. 

Furthermore, the computational cost is immense. The authors mention distributed training to mitigate this, but provide insufficient details about the scale of the computational resources required (e.g., number of GPUs, total training time). The practical implications of such high computational demands are not discussed. This is crucial, as widespread adoption of NAS hinges on making it more accessible. While the authors state the resulting architectures achieve speedups on inference, the overhead of the search process itself isn’t sufficiently addressed.

Finally, the paper lacks a more rigorous analysis of the search space. What is the effect of the choices made in defining the architecture search space (e.g., the set of available layer types, filter sizes)? How sensitive are the results to these choices?  A more systematic exploration of the search space, or at least a justification for the chosen space, would strengthen the paper.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Detail on the Controller:** Provide more details about the controller RNN's architecture (number of layers, hidden unit size, activation functions, regularization techniques).  How was this architecture chosen?
*   **Search Space Exploration:** A more detailed discussion of the search space is needed. Consider providing ablation studies to show the impact of including skip connections or different layer types.
*   **Computational Cost Breakdown:** A precise breakdown of the computational resources used for each experiment would be invaluable. This should include the number of GPUs, training time per child network, and the total wall-clock time for the entire search process.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** While the results are competitive, a more thorough comparison to strong, well-tuned baselines is desirable. Consider comparing to recent state-of-the-art architectures found through other automated methods (e.g., differentiable architecture search, evolutionary algorithms).
*   **Generalization Analysis:** While transfer learning results are shown, it would be useful to analyze *why* the discovered architectures generalize well. Are there common patterns or characteristics that contribute to their success?
*   **Reproducibility:** Providing code and experimental details that allow for reproducibility would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Clarify Reward Shaping:** The paper states the reward is the maximum validation accuracy cubed. Why was cubing chosen? What impact does reward shaping have on the search process?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What specific advantages does this gradient-based NAS approach have over existing neuro-evolution or Bayesian optimization methods for architecture search, particularly in terms of sample efficiency or scalability?
2.  What is the total computational cost (in GPU-hours or similar metric) to find the best architecture on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the initial search space? Did you explore different search spaces, and if so, how did they affect the performance of the discovered architectures?
4.  Could you elaborate on the choice of the exponential moving average baseline in the REINFORCE algorithm? How does this choice affect the stability and convergence of the training process?
5.  Are there any theoretical guarantees or insights regarding the convergence properties of this NAS approach?
