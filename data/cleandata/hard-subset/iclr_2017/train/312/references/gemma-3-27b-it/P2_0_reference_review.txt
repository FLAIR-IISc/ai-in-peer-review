## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper Title:** Neural Architecture Search

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to Neural Architecture Search (NAS) leveraging a recurrent neural network (RNN) as a controller to generate neural network architectures. The core idea is to frame architecture design as a sequential decision-making process, using policy gradients to optimize the controller based on the validation accuracy of the generated architectures. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on CIFAR-10 (image classification) and Penn Treebank (language modeling), achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results compared to human-designed architectures and existing methods. They also showcase improvements in training efficiency through distributed training and demonstrate the ability to discover novel architectures with advantageous properties (e.g., speed on CIFAR-10).

**Decision:** **Accept**

**Key Reasons:**

1. **Novelty & Impact:** The paper presents a fundamentally new approach to NAS, moving beyond traditional search spaces and optimization strategies. The use of a RNN controller trained via reinforcement learning is a significant contribution, opening up possibilities for automating architecture design in a more flexible and efficient manner.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are compelling. Achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance on these benchmarks demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed method. The performance gains, particularly on Penn Treebank, are substantial.



**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of manual neural architecture design and its limitations. It positions the work well within the broader landscape of hyperparameter optimization and neuroevolution, effectively highlighting the novelty of their approach.
* **Well-Motivated Approach:** The rationale behind using an RNN controller is sound. Framing architecture design as a sequence generation task allows the method to explore a wider and more complex search space than traditional methods. The use of policy gradients is a natural fit for this framework.
* **Rigorous Experimentation:** The experiments are well-designed and provide a thorough evaluation of the method. The authors carefully detail their experimental setup, including data preprocessing, training parameters, and baseline comparisons. The ablation studies (e.g., with/without skip connections, with/without stride prediction) provide valuable insights into the impact of different design choices.
* **Strong Results:** The reported results consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the method. The improvement of 3.6 perplexity on Penn Treebank is particularly impressive.  The fact that the found architectures not only perform well but also have desirable characteristics (like speed on CIFAR-10) further strengthens the claims.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors commit to releasing the code and the found architectures, which is crucial for reproducibility and future research.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Search Space Exploration:** While the authors demonstrate the method’s ability to find good architectures, a more detailed analysis of the search space exploration process would be valuable. Visualizing the distribution of explored architectures or quantifying the diversity of the discovered structures could provide further insights.
* **Computational Cost:**  The paper acknowledges the significant computational cost associated with training the controller and evaluating numerous child networks. A more detailed discussion of the computational resources required and potential strategies for further reducing the cost (e.g., more efficient child network training) would be helpful.
* **Generalization to Other Datasets:** While the results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are strong, it would be beneficial to briefly discuss the potential challenges and opportunities of applying the method to other datasets and tasks.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief analysis of the sensitivity of the method to the controller’s hyperparameters (e.g., LSTM hidden size, learning rate) could provide valuable practical guidance to other researchers.




**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Scalability:** Given the computational cost, how do you foresee scaling this approach to even larger and more complex datasets (e.g., ImageNet)?  Are there specific optimizations beyond distributed training that you are considering?
2.  **Search Space Definition:** How sensitive is the performance to the initial definition of the search space (e.g., the range of filter sizes, the types of layers allowed)?  Do you have any guidelines for effectively defining a good search space for a given task?
3.  **Controller Architecture:**  You mention using a two-layer LSTM for the controller. Have you experimented with other controller architectures (e.g., Transformers)?  Did you observe any significant differences in performance or training stability?
4.  **Reward Shaping:** Beyond simply cubing the validation accuracy, did you experiment with other reward shaping techniques to accelerate learning or improve the stability of the training process?




Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a significant contribution to the field of Neural Architecture Search. The novelty of the approach, coupled with the strong empirical results, justifies its acceptance to ICLR.  The authors have addressed a challenging problem in a creative and effective manner, and the potential impact of this work is substantial.
