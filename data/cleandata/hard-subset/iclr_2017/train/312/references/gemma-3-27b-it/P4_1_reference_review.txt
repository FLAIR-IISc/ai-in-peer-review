This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network controller trained with policy gradients. The core idea is to represent architectures as variable-length strings generated by the controller, train the resulting "child" networks, and use their performance as a reward signal to update the controller’s policy. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of NAS on CIFAR-10 (image classification) and Penn Treebank (language modeling), achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results.

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting approach, the description lacks sufficient detail regarding experimental setup and implementation, making it difficult to assess the reproducibility and significance of the results. Furthermore, the novelty, while present, feels incremental and the presentation could be more rigorous.

The primary reason for rejection stems from insufficient methodological detail and clarity. The paper describes the overall approach but glosses over crucial implementation details. For instance, the precise formulation of the reward function (beyond cubing the accuracy) is unclear, and the hyperparameter settings for both the controller and the child networks are not comprehensively provided. The search space definition, while mentioned, lacks specificity (e.g., what specific constraints were placed on layer configurations). The description of the distributed training setup is also high-level, leaving open questions about the synchronization mechanisms and potential bottlenecks. Without this information, it’s difficult to evaluate the engineering effort involved and the robustness of the results.

A secondary reason is the presentation of the results. Claims of surpassing “most human-invented architectures” on CIFAR-10 are broad and require more concrete comparison with specific, well-established architectures. A more thorough ablation study, demonstrating the impact of individual components (e.g., skip connections) would strengthen the contribution. The improvement on Penn Treebank is significant, but the relatively small scale of the experiments raises questions about generalizability. 

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Reproducibility:** The authors mention releasing code, which is excellent. However, ensuring that the code is well-documented and that all necessary hyperparameters and training procedures are clearly specified will be crucial for enabling reproducibility.
*   **Search Space Exploration:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the search space. What are the limitations of the current space? How was the space designed? Are there alternative space definitions that could be explored?
*   **Baseline Comparison:** Include comparisons against more recent and competitive baselines, especially for CIFAR-10. Consider comparing against architectures designed using other automated architecture search techniques (e.g., evolutionary algorithms).
*   **Ablation Study:** Conduct an ablation study to assess the contribution of different components of the NAS framework (e.g., skip connections, distributed training).
*   **Computational Cost:**  The paper mentions the high computational cost. A more detailed discussion of the resource requirements (e.g., number of GPUs, training time) would be valuable for assessing the practicality of the approach.
*   **Reward Shaping:** Elaborate on the choice of cubing the validation accuracy as the reward function. Why was this particular transformation chosen? Were other reward functions considered?
*   **Figure 3 Clarity:** The diagram in Figure 3 needs to be more clear in depicting the gradient flow and asynchronous updates.
*   **Clarify "local optimum" claim:** The claim about the architecture being a local optimum needs justification. What perturbations were attempted? What metrics were used to determine if performance worsened?



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a complete list of hyperparameters used for training both the controller RNN and the child networks (learning rate, optimizer settings, batch size, etc.)?
2.  What specific criteria were used to define the search space for the convolutional architectures on CIFAR-10? Were any constraints imposed on the number of filters or the depth of the network?
3.  Could you provide more details about the distributed training setup, including the synchronization mechanisms and the communication overhead?
4.  What was the rationale behind using the cubic function for the reward signal? Did you explore other reward functions, and if so, what were the results?
5.  Regarding the claim about the architecture being a “local optimum,” can you elaborate on the perturbations that were attempted and the metrics used to assess the impact of these perturbations?
