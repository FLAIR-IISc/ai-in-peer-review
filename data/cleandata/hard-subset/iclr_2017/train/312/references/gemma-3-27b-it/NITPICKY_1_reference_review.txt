This paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a gradient-based method for automating neural network design using a recurrent neural network (controller) to generate network architectures. The controller learns to generate architectures that maximize validation accuracy through a policy gradient approach. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of NAS on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results.

**Reject.** While the core idea of using reinforcement learning to explore network architectures is interesting, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor yet significant shortcomings in presentation, methodological rigor, and experimental detail, ultimately undermining confidence in the reported results. The sheer number of these issues suggests a lack of meticulousness that, despite the innovative concept, compels a rejection.

The paper is plagued with inconsistencies and a lack of precision. For example, the description of the search space is vague. What specific constraints are placed on the number of filters? Are there constraints on kernel size combinations? The section on skip connections introduces attention mechanisms without fully explaining how they integrate with the overall search process. The description of the baseline function is minimal and lacks justification for its choice. The process of increasing the number of layers is described as following a "schedule" without any details on the schedule itself. The phrase “convergence” is used repeatedly without a clear, quantifiable definition. What criterion determines convergence for a “child network”? Is it a fixed number of epochs, a plateau in validation loss, or something else?  The notation is sometimes unclear (e.g., Rk is introduced but not consistently used).

The experimental setup also raises concerns. The justification for the specific hyperparameters chosen for the controller RNN (e.g., number of hidden units, learning rate) is lacking. The rationale for setting S to 20, K to 100 (or 400), and m to 8 (or 1) is not provided. The description of the grid search for hyperparameter tuning after NAS is incomplete. What hyperparameters were actually tuned? The reported speedup on CIFAR-10 (1.05x faster) is marginal and lacks a detailed performance breakdown (e.g., inference time, FLOPs). The phrase “comparable performance” is used liberally without quantifiable metrics. Furthermore, the authors claim improved performance on the GNMT framework "without any tuning", but this is a bold statement that necessitates more detailed analysis – what metrics showed improvement, and were any potential negative impacts (e.g., training time) assessed?  The comparison to DenseNet on CIFAR-10 highlights a caveat about 1x1 convolutions, but this feels like post-hoc rationalization rather than a fair comparison. The presentation of Control Experiment 1 and 2 are underwhelming and do not provide enough information to assess their usefulness.

The writing quality is also subpar, containing several instances of awkward phrasing and unnecessary repetition. The literature review, while extensive, feels somewhat disjointed and doesn’t effectively position NAS within the broader landscape of neural architecture search techniques.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the Search Space:** Provide a precise and exhaustive definition of the search space, including all possible hyperparameters and constraints.
*   **Define Convergence:** Provide a rigorous, quantifiable definition of convergence for the child networks.
*   **Justify Hyperparameters:** Explain the rationale behind the choice of all hyperparameters, both for the controller RNN and the child networks.
*   **Provide Detailed Experimental Results:** Include more detailed experimental results, including variance, confidence intervals, and statistical significance tests.
*   **Address Computational Cost:** Provide a more thorough analysis of the computational cost of NAS, including training time, memory usage, and the number of GPU hours required.
*   **Improve Writing Quality:** Revise the manuscript for clarity, conciseness, and grammatical correctness.
*   **Expand Related Work:** Provide a more nuanced discussion of related work, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources (GPU hours, memory) required to run NAS on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank?
2.  What specific criteria were used to determine convergence for the child networks?
3.  Can you explain the "schedule" for increasing the number of layers in the child networks in more detail?
4.  Why was the exponential moving average chosen as the baseline function, and what impact did it have on the training process?
5.  Could you elaborate on the specific hyperparameters that were tuned during the grid search after NAS, and how the best settings were selected?
