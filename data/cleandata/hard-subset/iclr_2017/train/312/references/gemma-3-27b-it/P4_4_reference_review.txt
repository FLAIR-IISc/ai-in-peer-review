This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network controller trained with policy gradients. The core idea is to represent network architectures as strings and have the controller generate these strings, then evaluate the resulting networks and use the performance as a reward signal to update the controller. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of NAS on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results.

**Reject.** While the idea is interesting and the results are promising, the paper lacks sufficient detail and justification for several key design choices, raising concerns about reproducibility and the significance of the results. The experimental setup, particularly the vast search space and computational resources, feels more like a proof-of-concept than a fully rigorous study.

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of clarity regarding the search space and its impact on the results. The authors mention increasing the complexity of the search space (e.g., adding skip connections, different layer types) but do not provide a clear articulation of the final search space dimensionality. How many possible architectures are there? How does this scale with dataset complexity? Without this information, it's difficult to assess the difficulty of the search problem and the effectiveness of the proposed method. Furthermore, the paper doesn't adequately address the computational cost. Training thousands of child networks to convergence is extremely resource intensive, making the approach inaccessible to many researchers. This severely limits the practical impact of the work. The benefit achieved – a modest improvement in accuracy – may not justify the massive computational expenditure.

A second concern is the limited ablation study. The paper reports results with and without strides, but a more comprehensive analysis of the contribution of each component (skip connections, different layer types, controller architecture) would be valuable. This would help understand which aspects of the NAS framework are most critical for success. The control experiment comparing against random search is a good start, but a comparison against other automated architecture search methods (e.g., evolutionary algorithms) would further strengthen the paper. 

Finally, the description of the baseline for the reinforcement learning component feels somewhat vague. While the exponential moving average is mentioned, the precise parameters used for this baseline (e.g., decay rate) are not specified, potentially affecting the stability and convergence of the training process.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Search Space Definition:** Explicitly define the search space for each experiment, including the number of possible architectures. A table summarizing the different hyperparameters and their possible values would be helpful.
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:** Provide a detailed breakdown of the computational resources used (number of GPUs, training time per child network, total training time). Discuss strategies for reducing the computational cost, such as using smaller datasets or less accurate evaluation metrics during the initial stages of the search.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct a more thorough ablation study to evaluate the contribution of each component of the NAS framework.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** Compare NAS against other automated architecture search methods, such as evolutionary algorithms or Bayesian optimization.
*   **Baseline Details:**  Provide the exact parameters used for the baseline in the reinforcement learning process (decay rate of the exponential moving average).
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters for the controller (e.g., learning rate, LSTM hidden size).
*   **Visualization of Architectures:**  Provide more detailed visualizations of the architectures discovered by NAS, beyond the single example in Appendix A. This would help understand the characteristics of the architectures and potentially gain insights into the search process.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost associated with NAS, including the number of GPU hours required for each experiment?
2.  What strategies could be employed to reduce the computational cost of NAS without significantly compromising its performance?
3.  How does the performance of NAS compare to other automated architecture search methods, such as evolutionary algorithms or Bayesian optimization?
4.  What is the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters for the controller RNN?
5.  Can you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing the specific search space for each experiment? What considerations guided the selection of the hyperparameters and their possible values?
