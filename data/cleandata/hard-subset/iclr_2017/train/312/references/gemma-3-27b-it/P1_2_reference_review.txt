## Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

**1. Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to Neural Architecture Search (NAS) using a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller to generate network architectures. The core idea is to frame architecture design as a sequential decision process, where the controller predicts the hyperparameters of a "child network".  The child network is then trained, and its performance is used as a reward signal to update the controller via policy gradients.  The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on CIFAR-10 (image classification) and Penn Treebank (language modeling), achieving competitive results with architectures designed automatically, even surpassing human-designed networks in some cases. The paper also details improvements like skip connections and parallel training for scaling the search process.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. This paper introduces a compelling and innovative approach to NAS that significantly advances the field. The experimental results are strong and demonstrate the ability of the proposed method to discover high-performing architectures.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of using a recurrent network to generate network architectures and optimizing it via policy gradients is novel and insightful. It moves beyond traditional grid or random search methods and unlocks the potential for automating complex architecture design.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors present convincing experimental results on two diverse tasks, demonstrating that the learned architectures achieve state-of-the-art or competitive performance compared to manually designed networks. The reported gains on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are substantial and showcase the practical benefits of the approach.
* **Technical Soundness:** The method builds upon established concepts (policy gradients, RNNs) and integrates them in a clever way.  The discussion of techniques for accelerating training (parallelism, asynchronous updates) and increasing architecture complexity (skip connections) shows a good understanding of the practical challenges involved.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The diagrams (although some details are missing in the provided text – see feedback) are helpful in understanding the process.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Diagram Clarity:** The description mentions figures 1, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, and 8, but only Figure 1 is present in the provided text. Please include the other figures as they are essential for understanding key aspects of the method (e.g., the parallel training setup, skip connection mechanism, the discovered RNN cell).  Specifically, visualizing the architectures found for CIFAR-10 (Figure 7) and Penn Treebank (Figure 8) would be incredibly valuable.
* **Search Space Details:** While the paper provides some information about the search space (e.g., filter sizes, number of filters), a more detailed description would be helpful.  Specifically, what constraints were placed on the architecture (e.g., maximum depth, allowable layer types)?
* **Baseline Comparison:**  The comparison to random search (Control Experiment 2) is good.  However, it would strengthen the paper to include a more detailed comparison to other NAS methods, even if they are not directly comparable due to different search spaces.
* **Ablation Studies:** A more thorough ablation study analyzing the impact of different components of the approach (e.g., skip connections, the baseline function in the policy gradient) would be valuable.
* **Computational Cost:** The paper mentions the computational cost (training many child networks), but a more concrete analysis of the total compute required for the search process would be helpful. How does this compare to the cost of manually designing and tuning comparable architectures?

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the controller RNN:** What is the motivation behind the choice of a two-layer LSTM controller? Did you experiment with other controller architectures (e.g., transformers)?
* **Regarding the reward function:** The reward function uses the *maximum* validation accuracy of the last 5 epochs.  Why this specific choice, and how sensitive is the performance to the window size (5 epochs)?
* **Regarding scalability:**  While you describe techniques for accelerating training, how does the scalability of this method to even larger datasets and more complex architectures? Are there inherent limitations in the approach that might prevent it from scaling indefinitely?
* **Regarding transfer learning:** The experiments with GNMT are encouraging.  Did you observe any challenges in applying the learned RNN cell to a different task, and what modifications (if any) were needed?
