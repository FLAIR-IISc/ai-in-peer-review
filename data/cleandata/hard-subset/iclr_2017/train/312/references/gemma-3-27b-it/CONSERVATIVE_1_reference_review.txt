This paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network controller trained with policy gradients. The core idea is to formulate the architecture search space as a sequence generation problem and leverage reinforcement learning to optimize the controller for high-performing child networks. The authors demonstrate promising results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance with automatically designed architectures.

**Reject.** While the idea of automating neural architecture design is compelling, this paper presents a proof-of-concept rather than a thoroughly validated method. The claims of surpassing human-designed architectures are not convincingly supported by the presented evidence, and the experimental setup lacks sufficient comparison to established NAS techniques and a deeper analysis of the search space.

The primary concern lies with the limited comparative analysis. While the authors compare against hand-designed architectures, they fail to adequately benchmark against other NAS methods – even relatively simple ones like random search (which they briefly mention but dismiss without a comprehensive comparison) or hyperparameter optimization techniques designed for discrete search spaces. Simply stating that existing methods struggle with variable-length configurations is insufficient justification. A rigorous comparison demonstrating a clear advantage over existing approaches is crucial. The presented results on CIFAR-10, while promising, are incremental and do not definitively establish NAS as a superior approach. The improvement on Penn Treebank is more substantial, but warrants further investigation into the robustness and transferability of the discovered cell. 

Furthermore, the scale of the experiments is computationally expensive, hindering reproducibility and wider adoption. The reliance on a large cluster and asynchronous updates, while practical for Google’s infrastructure, doesn’t address the accessibility of this method for researchers with limited resources. The paper doesn’t discuss any strategies to reduce the computational cost, such as knowledge transfer or efficient search space pruning. 

The method also relies heavily on hyperparameter tuning within the discovered architectures (grid search post-NAS). This begs the question: how much of the performance gain is due to the architecture search itself, and how much is due to careful hyperparameter optimization *after* the search? This contribution is not disentangled.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Thorough Comparative Evaluation:** Conduct a comprehensive comparison against existing NAS methods (e.g., ENAS, DARTS, AmoebaNet) and relevant hyperparameter optimization techniques. Include a detailed analysis of the search space size and the computational cost of each method.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to assess the contribution of key components, such as skip connections and the recurrent controller architecture.
*   **Search Space Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the search space. What are the limitations of the defined search space? How does it affect the types of architectures that can be discovered?
*   **Computational Cost Reduction:** Investigate and discuss potential strategies to reduce the computational cost of NAS, making it more accessible to a wider range of researchers.
*   **Reproducibility:** Provide clear and detailed instructions for reproducing the results, including specific hardware and software configurations.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the NAS process to the hyperparameters of the controller itself (e.g., learning rate, LSTM hidden size).
*   **Transferability:** Evaluate the transferability of the discovered architectures and cells to other datasets and tasks.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   How does the performance of NAS compare to random search given the same computational budget? A plot showing learning curves would be helpful.
*   What is the impact of the baseline function on the variance of the policy gradient estimates? Did you experiment with different baseline functions?
*   Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources (e.g., GPU hours) required to run the experiments?
*   What is the sensitivity of the discovered architectures to the post-NAS hyperparameter tuning? Can you quantify the performance gain attributed solely to the architecture search?
*   How does the choice of search space (e.g., filter sizes, layer types) influence the performance of NAS? Did you explore different search space configurations?
