## Review of Neural Architecture Search

This paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network controller and reinforcement learning. The core idea is to represent an architecture as a string generated by the controller, train the resulting "child" network, and use its validation accuracy as a reward signal to update the controller’s policy. The authors demonstrate promising results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance with architectures discovered through NAS.

**Reject.** While the idea is interesting and the reported results are compelling, the paper feels premature and lacks the rigor expected for a significant contribution to the field. Specifically, the extensive computational resources required and the limited comparison to well-established, manually designed architectures raise serious concerns about the practical impact and true novelty of this approach.

The central argument – that NAS can discover architectures superior to human-designed ones – is not sufficiently supported. While the CIFAR-10 results show a competitive performance, the improvement (3.65% error vs. 3.74%) is marginal and doesn't convincingly justify the substantial computational cost of the search process. The claims of surpassing "most human-invented architectures" are overstated. The comparisons are not exhaustive, and don't include a thorough analysis of architectural features that contribute to the performance gain. The Penn Treebank results are more convincing, with a more substantial improvement, but further validation with more complex language modeling tasks is needed.

My primary concerns stem from the following:

1. **Computational Cost:** The paper glosses over the immense computational resources required for NAS. Training thousands of child networks to convergence, even with distributed training, is prohibitively expensive for most researchers. The authors mention a parameter server scheme, but lack details on the infrastructure used and the total training time. This severely limits the reproducibility and accessibility of the method. A discussion of strategies to reduce the computational burden (e.g., transfer learning, early stopping) is missing.
2. **Baseline Comparisons:** The comparison to existing architectures feels incomplete. While DenseNet is mentioned, a more detailed comparison to other state-of-the-art convolutional networks (e.g., ResNeXt, EfficientNets) is needed to truly assess the effectiveness of NAS. Similarly, for the language modeling task, comparisons to more recent and powerful LSTM variants are lacking.
3. **Search Space Limitations:**  The initial search space, even with the additions of skip connections, is still constrained. The paper acknowledges this, but the impact of these limitations on the discovered architectures is not sufficiently discussed. It's unclear if the optimal architectures are being found *within* the defined search space, or if a broader search space would yield even better results.
4. **Lack of Architectural Analysis:** The paper describes the discovered architectures but offers limited insights into *why* these architectures perform well. A more thorough analysis of the learned architectures – identifying recurring patterns or novel design elements – would strengthen the contribution.  What principles are being "discovered" by the search process?
5. **Reproducibility:** Despite stating that code will be released, the complexity of the system and the large-scale distributed training setup raise concerns about reproducibility.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:** Include ablation studies to assess the impact of different components of the NAS framework (e.g., the controller architecture, the reward function, the baseline).
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the NAS performance to different hyperparameter settings.
*   **Scalability:** Discuss the scalability of NAS to even larger and more complex datasets.
*   **Visualization:** Provide more visualizations of the discovered architectures to aid understanding.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are important, a more formal theoretical analysis of the search process could provide valuable insights.



**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What was the total computational cost (e.g., GPU hours) required to run NAS on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank?
*   How do the discovered architectures compare to other state-of-the-art architectures in terms of parameter count and FLOPs?
*   What strategies, if any, can be employed to reduce the computational cost of NAS?
*   What is the impact of the search space definition on the discovered architectures?
*   Can you provide more insights into *why* the discovered architectures perform well?  Are there specific architectural features that contribute to the performance gains?
*   What is the convergence behavior of the controller? Does the performance continue to improve after a certain point, or does it plateau?
