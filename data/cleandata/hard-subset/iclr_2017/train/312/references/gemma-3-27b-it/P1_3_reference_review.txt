## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper Title:** Neural Architecture Search

**Reviewer:** AI Assistant

**1. Summary of Claims**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel gradient-based approach to automating the design of neural network architectures. The core idea is to utilize a recurrent neural network (controller) to generate architecture descriptions, train the resulting “child” network, and then use the child network’s performance as a reward signal to update the controller via policy gradients. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method by successfully discovering novel architectures for both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank), achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results compared to human-designed architectures.  A key contribution is the ability to search a variable-length architecture space, overcoming limitations of existing hyperparameter optimization techniques.

**2. Decision & Key Reasons**

**Accept.** This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning and neural architecture search. The idea of using a recurrent network to generate architectures and training via reinforcement learning is elegant and effective, as demonstrated by the compelling empirical results. The paper tackles a challenging problem (architecture design) and offers a promising solution that moves beyond manually designed architectures.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

*   **Novelty & Significance:**  The approach is genuinely novel.  While related work exists in hyperparameter optimization and neuroevolution, NAS distinguishes itself by its ability to search a variable-length space and by leveraging the power of gradient-based optimization with a recurrent controller. This is a crucial step towards automating the design process.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are convincing. Achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance on these benchmarks demonstrates the method's practical effectiveness.  The performance gains (3.65% error rate on CIFAR-10, 62.4 perplexity on Penn Treebank) are substantial.
*   **Technical Soundness:**  The methodology is well-described and logically sound. The use of the REINFORCE algorithm with a baseline is a reasonable choice for policy gradient optimization, and the techniques for accelerating training (parallelism, asynchronous updates) are appropriate for the scale of the problem. The addition of skip connections and more complex recurrent cell architectures expands the search space and further enhances the method’s capabilities.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and technical details.  Figures are helpful in illustrating the process.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement)**

*   **Search Space Details:** While the paper describes the search space, a more detailed breakdown of the possible architectural choices at each layer and the constraints imposed would be helpful. Providing a visual representation of the search space's complexity could be beneficial.
*   **Computational Cost:**  The computational cost of NAS is significant (training thousands of child networks).  A more thorough discussion of the computational resources required and potential strategies for reducing this cost (e.g., more efficient training methods, transfer learning) would be valuable.
*   **Ablation Studies:** While results are shown with various complexities (skip connections etc), more dedicated ablation studies investigating the impact of individual components (e.g., the baseline in the REINFORCE algorithm, the specific RNN architecture of the controller) on the overall performance would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Generalizability:** While the transfer learning results are positive, exploring the performance of the learned architectures on other datasets could further demonstrate their generalizability.
*  **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to the controller's hyperparameters (e.g., LSTM hidden size, learning rate) would be beneficial.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

*   Regarding the distributed training setup: What were the practical challenges encountered when scaling the training process to hundreds of GPUs, and how were they addressed?
*   The paper mentions a schedule for increasing the number of layers in the child networks.  How was this schedule determined? Was it tuned, or based on a heuristic?
*   Could you provide more details on the selection of the baseline function for the REINFORCE algorithm? Was it explored extensively, or was the exponential moving average chosen primarily for its simplicity?
*   What is the memory footprint of the controller network during training, and how does this scale with the complexity of the search space?
*   The CIFAR-10 results show 1.05x speedup. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost (FLOPs, inference time) of the NAS-discovered architecture versus the best human-designed architecture?



In summary, this is a well-written and highly impactful paper. The proposed NAS method represents a significant step towards automating neural architecture design, and the strong empirical results demonstrate its potential. I recommend accepting this paper for publication at ICLR.
