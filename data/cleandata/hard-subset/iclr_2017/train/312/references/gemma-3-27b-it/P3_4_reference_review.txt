## ICLR 2024 Review: Neural Architecture Search

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel gradient-based method for automating the design of deep neural network architectures. The core idea is to employ a recurrent neural network (RNN) – the “controller” – to generate architectural descriptions (strings) which define the structure of a "child" network. This child network is then trained, and its validation accuracy is used as a reward signal to update the controller via policy gradients, encouraging it to generate better architectures over time. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of NAS on both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank), achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results compared to human-designed architectures.  Notably, they report a model for CIFAR-10 surpassing existing designs in both accuracy and speed and a novel recurrent cell outperforming LSTMs on Penn Treebank.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning, demonstrating a surprisingly effective approach to neural architecture search. The results are compelling, showing NAS can discover architectures that rival, and in some cases surpass, manually designed models.  The methodology is clearly presented and the empirical validation is thorough.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty & Significance:** The idea of using a recurrent network to *generate* architectures and then learning through policy gradients is a genuinely innovative approach.  This departs from earlier NAS techniques (Bayesian Optimization, Neuroevolution) and opens up a new avenue for exploration. The successful application to both CNNs and RNNs highlights the generality of the approach.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are highly encouraging.  Achieving state-of-the-art performance on these established benchmarks is a strong indicator of the method's efficacy. The reported speed improvement on CIFAR-10 adds practical value.
* **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The methodology is explained clearly, and the experimental setup is well-defined. The inclusion of a discussion of related work provides helpful context.  The architectural diagrams (while potentially expandable – see feedback) aid understanding.
* **Technical Soundness:** The use of REINFORCE with a baseline is a standard and appropriate technique for policy gradient methods.  The discussion of the parameter server and asynchronous updates is crucial for scaling the training process, and is well explained. The extensions to support skip connections and more complex cell structures demonstrate a thoughtful evolution of the core idea.



**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Search Space Exploration:** While the authors mention the search space, a more detailed characterization (e.g., total number of possible architectures) would be beneficial.  Understanding the size of the search space and how effectively NAS explores it is important.  What constraints were explicitly placed on the search to make it tractable?
* **Controller Architecture Details:** The controller architecture (two-layer LSTM with 35 hidden units) feels somewhat arbitrary. A brief ablation study exploring the impact of controller size or architecture on the search performance would be valuable.
* **Visualization of Architectures:** The architectural diagrams in Appendix A are helpful, but a more visually appealing and intuitive representation of the discovered architectures would enhance the paper.  Consider using a graph-based visualization to better illustrate the connections and layers.
* **Robustness and Reproducibility:**  Providing details on the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters of the *controller* (learning rate, baseline, etc.) would strengthen the analysis.  Releasing code is commendable, but including instructions on reproducing the results with specific random seeds would further enhance reproducibility.
* **Ablation Study for Skip Connections:** The sentence stating the performance drop when removing skip connections is intriguing. A more formal ablation study specifically examining the impact of skip connections and other architectural features (e.g., pooling) would strengthen the analysis.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:**  NAS is computationally expensive, requiring training many child networks. A more thorough discussion of the computational resources required and potential strategies for reducing the search cost would be beneficial.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Search Space & Exploration:**  Could you provide a more precise estimate of the total number of possible architectures within your search space, and discuss the strategies employed to make the search tractable?  Were any specific architectural priors (beyond those explicitly mentioned) incorporated into the controller to guide the search?
2.  **Controller Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to the hyperparameters of the controller (learning rate, baseline function, LSTM hidden size)? Did you perform any hyperparameter optimization for the controller itself?
3.  **Scaling:**  Beyond the parameter server approach, what other techniques did you consider or explore to accelerate the training process, particularly with larger search spaces?  What are the bottlenecks currently limiting scalability?
4. **Generalization:** You demonstrate transfer to a character-level language model. Have you explored applying NAS to other domains or tasks, and what were the results? Does the effectiveness of the controller vary significantly across different task types?
5. **Future Directions:**  What are the most promising avenues for future research building upon this work? Are you exploring methods for incorporating human knowledge or constraints into the search process?



In conclusion, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning.  The proposed method is novel, effective, and well-presented. Addressing the suggested feedback would further enhance the paper and solidify its impact.
