## ICLR 2024 Review: Neural Architecture Search

**1. Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to Neural Architecture Search (NAS) called "Neural Architecture Search," leveraging a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller to generate network architectures. The core idea is to treat architecture specification as a sequence generation task, allowing the controller to explore a variable-length search space. The controller is trained using a policy gradient method, with the validation accuracy of the generated architectures serving as the reward signal. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on CIFAR-10 (image classification) and Penn Treebank (language modeling), achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results, even discovering novel architectures that outperform human-designed counterparts. The paper also details techniques to improve scalability, such as distributed training and the inclusion of skip connections.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of NAS, introducing a conceptually simple yet powerful and effective method. The results are strong and demonstrate the potential of gradient-based NAS to discover high-performing architectures.  The clarity of the presentation and thoroughness of the experiments are also commendable.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty & Significance:** The idea of using an RNN controller trained via policy gradient to generate neural network architectures is a clever and impactful contribution. Prior NAS methods often rely on either random search or evolutionary algorithms.  This gradient-based approach allows for more efficient exploration of the search space.  The demonstrated success on two diverse tasks highlights the generalizability of the method.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling results on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank.  Achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance, *and* discovering architectures that surpass human-designed ones, is a strong testament to the effectiveness of the proposed method.  The comparison to existing NAS techniques (implicitly, through performance benchmarks) is sufficient to establish the value of this work.
* **Technical Soundness:** The core methodology – combining RNNs, policy gradients, and a variable-length search space – is well-motivated and logically sound. The authors clearly explain the key components and provide sufficient detail to understand the implementation. The inclusion of techniques like skip connections and parallel training significantly enhances the practicality and scalability of the approach.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The inclusion of a diagram illustrating the search process (Figure 1) is helpful. The ablation studies and control experiments are well-designed and contribute to understanding the contribution of different components.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Search Space Exploration:** While the results are impressive, it would be beneficial to provide a more detailed analysis of the search space explored by the controller.  How diverse were the architectures generated? Are there biases in the search process?  A visualization of the explored architecture space could be insightful.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper mentions some hyperparameter tuning, but a more systematic investigation of the sensitivity of the approach to the controller’s hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, LSTM hidden size) would strengthen the analysis.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors address scalability with distributed training, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of this approach compared to other NAS methods would be valuable.  Providing actual training times for the controller and the child networks would be helpful.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** While not strictly necessary, some theoretical justification or analysis of the policy gradient optimization process in this specific context could be interesting. Why does this approach work so well? 
* **Code Release:** The authors state that code will be released, which is excellent. Ensuring that the code is well-documented and reproducible would maximize the impact of this work.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Diversity of Architectures:** Could you provide some examples of architectures found that are significantly different from existing, hand-designed networks?  Are there any surprising or unexpected architectural features discovered by the controller?
* **Controller Training Stability:** Did you encounter any issues with the stability of the controller training process? Were any specific techniques employed to prevent the controller from getting stuck in local optima?
* **Transfer Learning Details:**  Regarding the transfer learning results, could you elaborate on the specific adaptations made to the GNMT framework and the hyperparameters used for the new cell?  How much effort was required to integrate the discovered cell into the existing system?
* **Scalability Limits:**  What are the practical limitations of scaling this approach to even larger and more complex search spaces? Are there specific bottlenecks that need to be addressed to handle more complex tasks or datasets?



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of NAS. The acceptance recommendation is based on the novelty, strength of the empirical results, and the clarity of the presentation. Addressing the additional feedback points would further enhance the paper and its impact.
