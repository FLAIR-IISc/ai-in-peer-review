## Review of Neural Architecture Search

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel approach to automating neural network architecture design. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (controller) to generate network architectures, train the resulting "child" networks, and use the validation accuracy as a reward signal to update the controller via policy gradients. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of NAS on both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank), achieving competitive and, in some cases, state-of-the-art results. They also present techniques to improve search efficiency and complexity, such as skip connections and parallel training.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling and novel approach to a challenging problem – automated neural architecture design. The empirical results are strong, demonstrating the capability of NAS to discover architectures that outperform human-designed ones. The approach has the potential to significantly impact the field.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Novelty:** The idea of framing architecture search as a reinforcement learning problem and using a recurrent network controller is a significant and innovative contribution. This moves beyond traditional hyperparameter optimization methods, allowing for the exploration of variable-length architecture spaces.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank demonstrate the practical effectiveness of NAS. The reported improvements over existing architectures are substantial and convincing. The fact that NAS can discover architectures competitive with, and in some cases exceeding, human-designed models is particularly noteworthy.
*   **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of manual architecture design and the need for automated methods. The connection to related work (hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, program synthesis) is well-established.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The use of policy gradients (REINFORCE) is a reasonable approach for this problem. The introduction of a baseline function and the parameter server scheme to accelerate training are sensible and practical additions. The explanation of the skip connection mechanism is clear and the extensions to recurrent architectures are well-described.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors state intent to release code, which is crucial for validating these results and fostering further research.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Search Space Exploration:** While the results are impressive, a more detailed analysis of the search space and the types of architectures explored would be valuable. It would be helpful to understand what kinds of architectures the controller tended to favor and whether there were any bottlenecks in the search process. Visualizations of the search process (e.g., showing the evolution of best architectures over time) could be insightful.
*   **Computational Cost:** The computational cost of NAS is significant. A more thorough discussion of the resources required (GPU hours, etc.) and potential strategies for reducing this cost would be beneficial.
* **Ablation Studies**: More ablation studies analyzing the impact of each addition would improve the analysis. For example, how much did skip connections affect the final result?
*   **Generalizability:** While the results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are promising, it would be valuable to explore the generalizability of NAS to other datasets and tasks.  Even a brief discussion of potential challenges and adaptations for different domains would be helpful.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide more details about the initial exploration phase of the controller? How did you seed the initial search, and how did this affect the final results?
*   What strategies did you employ to prevent the controller from getting stuck in local optima? Were there any techniques used to encourage exploration of diverse architectures?
*   What are the main limitations of the current NAS approach, and what directions for future research do you see as most promising?
*   Considering the high computational cost, do you envision NAS being used as a one-time architecture search followed by fine-tuning, or as a more continuous process of architecture adaptation and improvement?
*   Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources (GPU hours, etc.) required to run the experiments on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank?



Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and effective approach. The results are compelling, and the paper is well-written and well-motivated. I believe it has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning.
