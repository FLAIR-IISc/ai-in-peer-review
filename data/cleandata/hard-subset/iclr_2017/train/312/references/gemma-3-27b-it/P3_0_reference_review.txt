## ICLR 2024 Review: Neural Architecture Search

**1. Summary of Claims & Contributions:**

This paper presents "Neural Architecture Search" (NAS), a gradient-based method for automatically designing neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate architectural descriptions (strings), train the resulting "child network," and then use the child network’s validation accuracy as a reward signal to update the controller via policy gradients.  The authors demonstrate successful application of this method to both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank), achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results with architectures discovered automatically, surpassing human-designed models in some cases. Key contributions include a novel framework for NAS, the demonstration of finding effective architectures from scratch, and extensions to handle skip connections and recurrent cell design.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the paper presents a fascinating and potentially impactful idea, its presentation feels dated (given the current landscape of NAS research), lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the claims, while strong, are not adequately supported by ablation studies or comparisons to more recent NAS baselines.  The core idea is interesting, but the work appears to represent an early exploration of the concept, rather than a fully-fledged, rigorously evaluated contribution suitable for ICLR.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Dated Approach & Limited Baseline Comparison:** The paper was published in 2017 but reads as if it were done in 2015. The NAS field has *exploded* since then, with numerous advancements in search spaces, search strategies (e.g., differentiable architecture search - DARTS, ProxylessNAS, ENAS), and efficiency improvements.  The related work section acknowledges some relevant areas but doesn't adequately position the work within the broader, more recent NAS landscape. The lack of comparison against more modern NAS algorithms (even high-level acknowledgement of their performance) is a significant weakness. Simply stating that existing methods struggle with variable-length spaces isn’t sufficient justification; the paper needs to demonstrate how its approach overcomes these limitations *compared to* newer methods attempting the same.
* **Lack of Reproducibility & Detail:**  The paper provides a high-level overview, but crucial details are missing. For instance, the specific hyperparameters of the controller RNN (beyond hidden size) are not fully specified. The training details for the child networks, while described, could be much more precise.  The controller's search space is defined, but the implementation details of handling invalid architectures (e.g., incompatible layers) are glossed over. Without more detailed implementation information, reproducing these results would be extremely difficult.
* **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** The experiments demonstrate performance gains, but lack thorough ablation studies. For example, what is the impact of the baseline function on training stability and performance? How sensitive are the results to the choice of the reward function? What is the benefit of using distributed training?  A clear understanding of the contribution of each component is crucial for evaluating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* **Overstated Claims:**  Statements like "Neural Architecture Search can design good models from scratch, an achievement considered not possible with other methods" are overly strong and unsupported.  Neuroevolutionary approaches, for example, have been designing architectures from scratch for much longer, albeit often with different tradeoffs.  The claim of "better than most human-invented architectures" on CIFAR-10 needs further qualification.  Is this "most" a statistically significant sample?  How do these results compare to more recent state-of-the-art models trained with extensive hyperparameter tuning?



**4. Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

* **Expand Related Work:**  A more comprehensive review of the NAS literature, including recent advances in DARTS, ENAS, ProxylessNAS, and other gradient-based and evolutionary NAS methods, is essential. Discuss how this work differs and builds upon these advancements.
* **Provide Detailed Implementation Information:** Include a more detailed description of the controller RNN architecture, the search space, and the implementation of the skip connection mechanism.  Consider open-sourcing the code to facilitate reproducibility.
* **Conduct Thorough Ablation Studies:** Investigate the impact of different components of the method, such as the baseline function, reward function, and distributed training scheme.
* **Compare to Modern NAS Baselines:**  Compare the performance of NAS against state-of-the-art NAS algorithms on the CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank datasets.
* **Address the Computational Cost:** The paper mentions the computational cost of training child networks. Quantify this cost and discuss strategies for reducing it.
* **Clarify the Search Space:** The search space for the recurrent cells is complex. Visualizing examples of generated cells, beyond Figure 5, would be helpful.
* **Discuss Limitations:**  A discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach, such as its scalability to larger datasets and more complex architectures, would be valuable.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* Given the significant advancements in NAS since this work was first conducted, how does this approach compare to more modern methods like DARTS or ENAS in terms of performance, computational cost, and ease of implementation?
* Can you provide more details regarding the specific hyperparameters used for training the controller RNN?
* What specific strategies were employed to handle invalid architectures generated by the controller (e.g., layers with incompatible dimensions)?
* Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of the results to the choice of baseline function and reward function?  Were other options explored?
* The paper mentions that the controller RNN learns to predict larger filters at the top layers. Can you explain the intuition behind this behavior?




In conclusion, while the ideas presented in this paper are promising, the lack of context within the current NAS landscape, limited detail, and insufficient evaluation prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time.  A significantly revised version addressing the points outlined above would be necessary for reconsideration.
