This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method leveraging a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller to generate architectures for both convolutional and recurrent neural networks, trained via a policy gradient approach. The core idea is compelling – framing architecture design as a sequential decision-making process amenable to reinforcement learning. The paper demonstrates promising results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance.

**Reject.** While the core idea is novel and the results are encouraging, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and a lack of rigorous detail that collectively undermine confidence in the reported results. The presentation feels rushed and lacks the precision expected of a strong ICLR submission.

The most significant issue is the lack of detail regarding experimental setup. Specifically:

*   **Hyperparameter Search Space:** The description of the search space is vague. For example, “a number of filters in [24, 36, 48, ]” is incomplete and unclear. Is the last element intended to be 64? The same applies to the stride selection.
*   **Training Details:** While mentioning distributed training, the description lacks crucial details. How many GPUs were used? What was the batch size *per GPU*? The parameter server configuration (S, K, m) is provided, but without context of the hardware setup, its significance is reduced. The precise settings used for the child network training (learning rate schedule, optimizer parameters) aren't consistently reported. The fact that the authors experimented with learning rates only *after* finding the best architecture suggests a flawed experimental design—learning rate is architecture-dependent.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** The claim of surpassing existing methods is weakened by insufficient detail about the compared baselines. Were they retrained with the same hyperparameters (besides architecture) as the NAS-generated networks? The statement about the DenseNet comparison specifically mentions differing approaches to parameter count reduction, making a direct comparison difficult to interpret.
*   **Reward Function:** The use of cubing the validation accuracy as a reward is arbitrary and not justified. Why this specific transformation? How sensitive are the results to this choice?
*   **Ablation Studies:** Limited ablation studies are presented. The experiment with adding max and sin activation functions is insufficient to assess the robustness of the approach.
*   **Figure References:** Numerous references to figures (e.g., Figure 3, Figure 4, Figure 5) are made without sufficient explanation of what the reader should be observing. The figures themselves lack sufficient labels and clarity. Figure 7 and 8 are mentioned as being in the appendix but are not provided.
* **Inconsistent Notation:** The use of 'm' to denote both the number of architectures sampled *and* the number of child networks trained in parallel is confusing.

These inconsistencies and omissions lead to concerns about the reproducibility and reliability of the reported results. The paper feels like a preliminary report rather than a polished ICLR submission.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   The related work section is adequate but could be more comprehensive. Specifically, a discussion of differentiable architecture search (DARTS) methods would be relevant.
*   The notation could be improved. Define all variables and use consistent terminology throughout.
*   The discussion of the REINFORCE algorithm is overly brief. A more detailed explanation of the baseline function and its impact on variance reduction would be beneficial.
*   The motivation behind using a recurrent network as the controller could be strengthened. Why is an RNN a natural choice for this problem?
*   Explore the computational cost of NAS. The training process appears computationally expensive. A discussion of methods to reduce this cost would be valuable.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Please provide a complete and detailed description of the experimental setup, including hardware specifications, batch sizes, optimizer parameters, learning rate schedules, and baseline training details.
*   What is the justification for cubing the validation accuracy in the reward function?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of the baseline function in the REINFORCE algorithm?
*   Can you provide a more rigorous comparison against relevant baseline methods, ensuring fair hyperparameter tuning?
*   What is the computational cost of NAS compared to manual architecture design or other automated methods?
*  Please provide Figures 7 and 8.
* Please clarify the incomplete filter size definition in the CIFAR-10 search space [24, 36, 48, ].
