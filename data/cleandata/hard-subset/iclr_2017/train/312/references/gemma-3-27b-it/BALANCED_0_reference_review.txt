This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller trained with policy gradients. The core idea is elegant – framing architecture search as a reinforcement learning problem where the RNN generates architectures, which are then evaluated, and the RNN's parameters are updated based on the resulting performance. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of NAS on both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank), achieving competitive and, in some cases, state-of-the-art results.

**Reject** – While the paper presents a promising and novel approach, the experimental details are lacking in crucial areas, making it difficult to fully assess the validity of the claims and reproduce the results. The improvements over existing methods, while reported, aren’t convincingly demonstrated as being directly attributable to the NAS approach, given the limited ablation studies.

The primary weakness lies in the lack of sufficient detail regarding the experimental setup. While the paper mentions distributed training and parameter server schemes, the specifics of the hyperparameter tuning process for the *child networks* are not clearly outlined. Were the hyperparameters (learning rate, weight decay, etc.) optimized independently for each architecture found by the controller, or were fixed values used? The performance gains reported depend heavily on proper tuning of the generated architectures; without knowing the tuning procedures, it's hard to determine whether the improvements are due to NAS finding a better architecture or simply better tuning of a given architecture.

Furthermore, the claim of designing models “from scratch” is somewhat misleading. The search space, while variable length, is still constrained by predefined layer types (convolutional, pooling, etc.). This isn’t truly “from scratch” in the sense of discovering entirely novel building blocks. The paper acknowledges this later, but the initial framing is strong.  The CIFAR-10 results, achieving a 3.65% test error, are impressive but need to be contextualized within the broader landscape of existing architectures. A more direct comparison to established architectures, trained with equivalent hyperparameter tuning strategies, is needed.

The Penn Treebank results are compelling, particularly the 3.6 perplexity improvement. However, the control experiments comparing NAS to random search are only briefly discussed and lack detailed quantitative results. A more thorough comparison, including statistical significance testing, would strengthen this claim.  The transfer learning experiments are positive, but the description is relatively brief.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:**  The paper would benefit from ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components of NAS. For example, what is the impact of skip connections?  What is the effect of using a larger/smaller controller RNN?
*   **Search Space Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the search space. How many possible architectures are there? What are the limitations of the current search space?
*   **Computational Cost:** NAS is computationally expensive.  A more detailed breakdown of the computational resources required (GPU hours, etc.) would be helpful.  Explore potential strategies for reducing the computational cost, such as using a smaller validation set or transferring knowledge between architectures.
*   **Visualization of Architectures:** While some architectures are shown in the appendix, providing a more systematic visualization of the architectures found by NAS (e.g., a gallery of architectures with varying performance) would provide valuable insights.
* **Reproducibility:** Providing more details of the implementation and code release are crucial for reproducibility. Specific versions of libraries used and the exact configuration files would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What specific hyperparameter tuning strategies were used for the child networks? Was a grid search, random search, or other optimization method employed, and what were the ranges of the hyperparameters tuned?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed comparison of the architectures found by NAS to existing architectures, trained with equivalent hyperparameter tuning strategies?
3.  Can you elaborate on the computational cost of NAS, including the GPU hours required for the CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank experiments?
4.  How was the baseline for the policy gradient calculation determined? Was it a simple moving average, or a more sophisticated baseline?
5.  Could you provide more detail regarding the distribution of architectures explored during the search? Are there common patterns or trends observed in the architectures generated by the controller?
