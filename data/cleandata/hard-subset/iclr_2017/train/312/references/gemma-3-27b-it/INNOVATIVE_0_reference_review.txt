This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network controller trained with policy gradients. The core idea – framing architecture design as a sequential prediction problem and leveraging reinforcement learning – is remarkably clever and addresses a critical bottleneck in deep learning: the need for expert knowledge and time-consuming manual architecture engineering. The results are compelling, demonstrating the ability to discover architectures that outperform human-designed models on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, even achieving state-of-the-art performance on the latter.

**Reject.** While highly innovative, the paper feels somewhat incomplete and overly ambitious in its scope. The rapid pace and breadth of ideas presented – generating convolutional *and* recurrent architectures, skip connections, distributed training – leaves many aspects feeling underdeveloped. The claims are strong, but a more focused and thoroughly validated contribution would be more convincing.

The paper’s strength lies in its novelty. Framing architecture search as a reinforcement learning problem is a genuinely insightful approach. The successful application to both image classification and language modeling suggests broad applicability. The reported performance gains are significant, especially the 3.6 perplexity improvement on Penn Treebank. The inclusion of skip connections and the controller’s ability to generate these are notable advancements.

However, several concerns temper my enthusiasm. First, the experimental details, while provided, feel rushed. The hyperparameter tuning process isn’t fully transparent, and the impact of different choices (e.g., the baseline function in the REINFORCE algorithm) isn't thoroughly explored. More detailed ablation studies would be valuable. Second, while the distributed training approach is mentioned, the specifics regarding parameter server shards and replica numbers seem somewhat arbitrary. A more rigorous analysis of the impact of these choices on convergence speed and performance would strengthen the claims. Third, the generalization to the WMT14 translation task is promising but lacks detail – a quantitative comparison with a standard LSTM baseline would be very helpful. Finally, the CIFAR-10 result with 3.65% error, while impressive, is presented somewhat vaguely. The specific architecture is mentioned but lacks a clear visual representation alongside the others.

The paper’s breadth is also a double-edged sword. Tackling both convolutional and recurrent architectures, along with skip connections and variable layer types, within a single paper feels like too much. A more focused contribution—perhaps concentrating solely on convolutional architectures or a more in-depth exploration of skip connection generation—would likely yield a more robust and convincing result.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Visualization:** Include clear visualizations of the architectures discovered on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank. This will greatly aid understanding and facilitate comparison with existing architectures.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more thorough ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components, such as skip connections, different combination functions, and activation functions.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters in the policy gradient algorithm (learning rate, baseline function, etc.).
*   **Computational Cost:** A more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the NAS process would be helpful. How does it compare to the cost of manually designing and tuning architectures?
*   **Reproducibility:** Provide more detailed information about the experimental setup and code to facilitate reproducibility.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What is the sensitivity of the results to the choice of baseline function in the REINFORCE algorithm? Did you explore other baseline options?
*   Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources required for the NAS process, including training time for the controller and child networks?
*   What specific architectural patterns did the controller consistently discover across different runs? Were there any surprising or unexpected features in the generated architectures?
*   How well does the learned controller generalize to datasets other than CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank? Have you explored transfer learning scenarios?
*   The paper mentions “compilation failures.” Can you elaborate on the frequency of these failures and the techniques used to mitigate them? Is this a significant bottleneck in the search process?
