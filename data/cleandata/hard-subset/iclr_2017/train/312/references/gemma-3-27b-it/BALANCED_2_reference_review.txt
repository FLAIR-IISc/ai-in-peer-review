This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a gradient-based method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller. The controller generates architectural hyperparameters, a "child network" is trained on real data, and the accuracy of the child network serves as a reward signal to update the controller's policy via reinforcement learning. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of NAS on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving competitive results compared to human-designed architectures and, in some cases, surpassing state-of-the-art performance.

**Reject.** While the idea of using a gradient-based approach to neural architecture search is compelling, and the empirical results are promising, the paper lacks sufficient detail and analysis to confidently support its claims. The experiments, while demonstrating improvements, don’t offer enough comparative data to establish the robustness and generalizability of the proposed method. Furthermore, several aspects of the implementation and search space remain unclear.

The main reason for rejection is the lack of clarity regarding the search space and the computational cost. The paper mentions increasing the complexity of the search space (skip connections, layer types) but doesn’t quantify how this impacts the search process. Is the performance improvement worth the exponential increase in search space? The authors mention distributed training to accelerate the process, but providing concrete numbers on training time and resource usage (e.g., total GPU hours) is crucial for evaluating the practicality of NAS. The claim of designing good models "from scratch" feels overstated, given the reliance on established building blocks like convolutions and LSTMs within the defined search space. It’s more accurate to say NAS optimizes *within* a predefined architectural landscape.

A second critical weakness is the limited ablation studies. While control experiments are presented (adding more functions to the search space, comparison with random search), they are not extensive enough. For example, how sensitive is the performance to the choice of the baseline function in the policy gradient update? What is the effect of varying the number of controller replicas and child networks?  A more detailed analysis of the controller's learned behavior – what types of architectures does it consistently favor, and why? – would significantly strengthen the paper. The CIFAR-10 results show improvements, but the details on the exact architecture found are limited to an appendix figure.  The speedup claim (1.05x faster than the current best model) is significant and requires more detailed benchmarking.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Search Space Definition:** Provide a precise and complete description of the search space, including the range of possible values for each hyperparameter (e.g., filter sizes, number of filters, learning rates). A table summarizing the search space would be very helpful.
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:** Include a detailed analysis of the computational resources required for NAS, including training time, GPU hours, and memory usage.  Compare the computational cost of NAS to the cost of manual architecture search.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more comprehensive ablation studies to assess the impact of different components of the NAS framework, such as the baseline function, the number of replicas, and the search space size.
*   **Controller Analysis:** Analyze the learned behavior of the RNN controller. Visualize the probability distributions over different architectural choices at different stages of training.  Identify any patterns or trends in the controller’s decisions.
*   **Generalization:** Explore the transferability of the architectures found by NAS to other datasets and tasks.
*   **Implementation Details:** Provide more details about the implementation of the distributed training framework, including the communication protocol and synchronization strategy.
*   **Fairer Comparisons:** Present a more thorough comparison with other neural architecture search methods, including evolutionary algorithms and Bayesian optimization.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the total computational cost (GPU hours) to find the best architecture on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, respectively?
2.  How does the performance of NAS degrade as the size of the search space increases?  Is there a point where the search becomes intractable?
3.  Can you provide more detail on the architecture found on CIFAR-10? What specific layers and connections contribute most to the performance improvement?
4.  What is the impact of different baseline functions on the stability and convergence of the policy gradient update?
5.  How sensitive is the performance of NAS to the initialization of the RNN controller?
6.  Could you elaborate on the "compilation failures" mentioned in section 3.3 and explain how the implemented solutions handle them robustly?
7.  Regarding the claim of “designing models from scratch,” is the search space limited to only convolutional and recurrent layers, or are other layer types also considered? If so, what are they?
