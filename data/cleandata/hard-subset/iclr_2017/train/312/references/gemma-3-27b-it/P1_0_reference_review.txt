## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper:** Neural Architecture Search

**Reviewer:** AI Assistant

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel gradient-based approach for automating the design of deep neural network architectures. The core idea is to utilize a recurrent neural network (controller) to generate architectural descriptions (strings), train the resulting "child" network, and then use the child network's performance as a reward signal to update the controller via policy gradients. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank), achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results compared to human-designed architectures. The paper details improvements to the core method, including skip connections and parallel training, and successfully extends the approach to recurrent cell design.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning. The core idea is innovative, well-executed, and demonstrates strong empirical results on multiple challenging datasets. The paper is clearly written and provides sufficient detail to allow for reproduction of the results.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Significance:**  Automated architecture search is a crucial area of research, and this paper proposes a unique and effective solution using reinforcement learning and recurrent neural networks.  Moving beyond hyperparameter tuning to *discovering* architectures is a substantial step forward.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates competitive or state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank.  The performance gains, particularly on the language modeling task, are noteworthy.  The demonstration of finding architectures *better* than human-designed ones is compelling.
* **Technical Soundness:**  The method appears technically sound, with a clear explanation of the policy gradient approach and the handling of variable-length architectures.  The inclusion of techniques to reduce variance in the gradient estimates (baseline) and accelerate training (parallelism) adds to the robustness of the approach.
* **Comprehensive Approach:**  The paper goes beyond the core NAS method, tackling increased complexity with skip connections, and successfully extending the method to design recurrent cells – a non-trivial extension.  The detailed explanation of the controller’s generation process for recurrent cells is particularly well-done.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

* **Computational Cost:** While the paper highlights parallelization, a more detailed discussion of the *overall* computational cost of NAS would be beneficial. How does it compare to the cost of manually searching for architectures, or other NAS methods? Providing more concrete numbers (e.g., total GPU hours) would be helpful.
* **Search Space Limitations:**  The paper acknowledges some limitations in the search space (e.g., limited layer types).  It would be valuable to briefly discuss future directions for expanding the search space and the potential challenges associated with doing so.
* **Ablation Studies:** While some control experiments are presented, additional ablation studies (e.g., removing the skip connections, varying the controller architecture) could further strengthen the analysis and provide insights into the importance of different components of the NAS framework.
* **Code Availability:** The statement about code release is excellent. Ensure the code and trained models are readily accessible to the research community.
* **Clarify Baseline:** The control experiment comparing to random search would be stronger with a clearer definition of the random search process (e.g., sampling strategy, number of trials).



**5. Questions for Authors:**

* **Scalability:** Beyond CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, what are the limitations of this approach in terms of dataset size and model complexity? Have you explored applying NAS to larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet)?
* **Generalization:** Are the architectures discovered by NAS transferable to other tasks or datasets? Have you evaluated the performance of the discovered architectures on related, but different, problems?
* **Stability:**  Does the performance of NAS vary significantly across different random seeds?  Are there any steps taken to ensure the reproducibility of the discovered architectures?
* **Controller Architecture Sensitivity:** How sensitive is the performance of NAS to the architecture of the controller RNN itself (e.g., number of layers, hidden units)? Did you perform any experiments to optimize the controller architecture?
* **Skipping Connections:** You mention that if you densely connect all layers with skip connections the performance becomes slightly worse, do you have any intuition about why this is the case?



In conclusion, this paper represents a significant advance in the field of neural architecture search. The authors have presented a well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated approach that has the potential to automate and improve the process of deep learning model design.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
