## Review

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel approach to automating the design of neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network controller and reinforcement learning. The core idea – framing architecture search as a sequential decision process and utilizing policy gradients – is remarkably elegant and has the potential to significantly impact the field. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of NAS on both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank), achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results. The ability to discover novel architectures that outperform human-designed models is a particularly compelling contribution.

**Reject.** While the idea is truly novel and the results are promising, the paper feels somewhat rushed and lacks crucial details regarding experimental setup and robustness, particularly given the complexity of the approach. The substantial computational cost and implicit hyperparameter tuning within the NAS process itself aren’t fully addressed. I’m willing to overlook these shortcomings *because* the core idea is so strong, but significant revisions are needed for acceptance.

The core strength of this work lies in its novelty. Existing methods for architecture search are largely limited to pre-defined search spaces or rely on computationally expensive evolutionary algorithms. NAS cleverly circumvents these limitations by learning to *generate* architectures, effectively expanding the search space. The experimental results are also strong, exceeding existing methods on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank. The observation that the discovered architectures exhibit characteristics often found in successful, manually designed networks (e.g., skip connections, varying filter sizes) lends further credence to the approach.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Firstly, the description of the search space, while present, is not exhaustive. A clearer articulation of the architectural constraints and choices available to the controller would be beneficial. Secondly, the impact of the baseline function on the training stability and performance of the controller isn’t sufficiently analyzed. The chosen exponential moving average is rather ad-hoc, and exploring alternative baseline strategies (e.g., value functions) could improve results. Thirdly, the computational cost of NAS is considerable, requiring significant distributed computing resources. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't offer a detailed breakdown of the cost or explore strategies for reducing it. Finally, the sensitivity of the results to the initial random seed and controller hyperparameters is not investigated. Given the stochastic nature of reinforcement learning, establishing the robustness of the discovered architectures is crucial.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:** It would be helpful to include ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the NAS framework, such as skip connections and the controller architecture itself.
*   **Visualization of Search Process:** Providing visualizations of the architectures explored during the search process could offer valuable insights into the learning dynamics of the controller.
*   **Detailed Experimental Setup:** A more comprehensive description of the experimental setup, including the exact hyperparameters used for training the controller and child networks, is necessary for reproducibility.
*   **Comparison to Existing NAS Methods:** While the paper acknowledges related work, a more direct comparison to other NAS methods (even if they are less general) would strengthen the claims.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** A more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach, such as its computational cost and potential for overfitting, would enhance the credibility of the paper.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the sensitivity of the final architecture discovered to different random seeds? Did you observe significant variations in performance across different runs?
2.  How does the choice of baseline function affect the training stability and final performance of the controller? Have you experimented with alternative baseline strategies?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost associated with NAS, including the total GPU hours required for the experiments?
4.  What strategies could be employed to reduce the computational cost of NAS, making it more accessible to researchers with limited resources?
5.  How do you prevent the controller from exploiting peculiarities of the validation dataset and overfitting to the search process?
