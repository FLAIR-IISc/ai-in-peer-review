This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method leveraging a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller to generate neural network architectures and employing reinforcement learning to optimize the controller based on the validation accuracy of the generated architectures. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of their approach on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results.

**Reject.** While the idea of automating neural architecture design is compelling and the results are promising, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and inconsistencies in methodology, presentation, and experimental detail, ultimately undermining confidence in the reproducibility and robustness of the findings. The level of detail provided is insufficient to fully assess the validity of the claims.

The primary issue lies in a lack of rigorous description and justification for numerous experimental choices. For instance, the controller RNN architecture (two-layer LSTM with 35 hidden units) seems arbitrarily chosen without exploration of alternative configurations. The parameter server setup (S=20, K=100/400, m=8/1) lacks a clear rationale, and the impact of these parameters on performance isn't discussed. The motivation behind cubing the validation accuracy as the reward signal (c(validation perplexity)2) is unclear and could significantly affect the optimization process. The method for increasing the number of layers during training is presented as a “schedule” but lacks details about its form and sensitivity analysis.

Furthermore, the paper contains inconsistencies in reporting and analysis. The CIFAR-10 experiments report multiple architectures with varying error rates, yet the explanation for the improvement from 5.50% to 3.65% is simply "by adding 40 more filters." This lacks sufficient justification. The statement that the 3.65% model is 1.05x faster than the "current best model" needs clarification—which model is being compared against, and what metric defines “faster”? The performance comparison on Penn Treebank also lacks sufficient details, such as the exact LSTM configuration used for the baselines and the specific hyperparameters used for the GNMT experiment. 

The appendix is mentioned but is not provided with the paper. Crucially, the promised visualization of the best architectures in Figures 7 and 8 is missing, hindering the ability to understand the discovered network structures. The control experiments are minimally discussed and lack detailed analysis. The claim that random search was used as a baseline requires quantification and a clear comparison of the search space sizes and computational budgets. 

Finally, the writing style, while generally clear, contains minor errors and redundancies. The overuse of citations (e.g., citing multiple papers after every sentence) creates clutter and reduces readability. The phrasing is sometimes imprecise, for example, stating the method "learns directly from the reward signal without any supervised bootstrapping" is somewhat misleading, as the initial training of the child networks relies on labeled data.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Provide a detailed ablation study:** Systematically investigate the impact of different hyperparameters (controller size, learning rate, reward function, number of replicas, etc.) on the performance of NAS.
*   **Justify architectural choices:** Explain the rationale behind the chosen controller architecture, parameter server configuration, and reward function.
*   **Thoroughly compare to baselines:** Include a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods, including random search with the same computational budget.
*   **Provide detailed experimental setup:** Include all relevant hyperparameters and training details for both the controller and the child networks.
*   **Include the appendix:** Provide the promised figures visualizing the discovered architectures.
*   **Address inconsistencies:** Clarify any ambiguities in the results and analysis.
*   **Improve writing quality:** Eliminate redundancies and minor errors.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What sensitivity analysis was performed on the controller RNN architecture and hyperparameters?
*   How was the number of parameter server shards (S), replicas (K), and child networks per replica (m) determined?
*   Why was the validation accuracy cubed (or squared for PTB) used as the reward signal, and how does this choice affect the optimization process?
*   What specific model is the 1.05x speedup comparison based on for CIFAR-10?
*   Can you provide a detailed breakdown of the computational cost for NAS compared to manual architecture design and other automated methods?
*   What were the exact hyperparameters used for the baseline LSTM models on the Penn Treebank dataset?
*   Why was the controller trained for a fixed number of architectures (12,800) and not until convergence based on a validation set?
