This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network controller trained with policy gradients. The core idea – framing architecture search as a reinforcement learning problem where the controller generates architectures and receives a reward based on their performance – is genuinely novel and a potentially significant shift in how we approach neural network design. The reported results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are compelling, exceeding existing human-designed architectures and achieving state-of-the-art performance.

**Reject.** While the idea is highly promising and the results impressive, the paper feels like a very strong first step rather than a fully-fledged, polished contribution ready for ICLR. The rapid progress in the field necessitates a higher bar for acceptance, and this work, while novel, needs further development and validation.

The primary justification for rejection lies in the lack of thorough analysis and reproducibility details. While the performance gains are reported, the experimental setup feels somewhat opaque. The search space, while described, isn’t fully explored in terms of ablation studies. For example, how sensitive is the performance to the specific choice of hyperparameters within the controller itself? What is the impact of the increasing layer schedule? The authors mention control experiments, but these are brief and don’t provide a deep understanding of *why* NAS works.  Furthermore, the computational cost of this method appears substantial (training thousands of child networks), and the paper doesn't adequately address the scalability or accessibility of this approach for researchers with limited resources. The transfer learning results are encouraging but feel somewhat tacked on and would benefit from more detailed analysis. The claim of 1.05x speedup on CIFAR-10 is interesting, but needs to be quantified more precisely (e.g., training time, inference time, model size).

Supporting Arguments:

* **Limited Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks a systematic ablation study to understand the contribution of different components of NAS (e.g., skip connections, distributed training). It's unclear which aspects are most critical for achieving the observed performance.
* **Reproducibility Concerns:**  While the authors mention releasing code, the scale of the experiments and the distributed training setup make full reproduction challenging. Clearer details on computational resources and implementation specifics are needed.
* **Search Space Exploration:** The paper doesn’t fully justify the chosen search space. Why these specific layer types, filter sizes, etc.? A more rigorous exploration of different search space configurations would strengthen the findings.
* **Scalability and Cost:** The computational cost is a significant drawback. The paper needs to discuss strategies for reducing this cost, such as transfer learning from smaller datasets or more efficient search algorithms.
* **Superficial Control Experiments:** The control experiments described (adding more functions, random search) are not sufficiently detailed or insightful to provide a strong baseline for comparison.

Additional Feedback:

* **Visualizations:** More detailed visualizations of the discovered architectures would be helpful. Beyond Figure 7, showing the learned cell structure from the Penn Treebank experiments is crucial for understanding the benefits of NAS.
* **Error Analysis:**  An analysis of the types of errors made by the NAS-designed networks could provide insights into areas for improvement.
* **Theoretical Justification:**  While the empirical results are strong, a more theoretical understanding of why NAS works could further enhance the contribution. For example, can we provide any guarantees about the convergence or optimality of the search process?
* **Comparison to other NAS methods:** While the paper mentions related work, it would be beneficial to explicitly compare NAS to more recent NAS techniques (even if only briefly).

Questions for the Authors:

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources required to run the experiments (e.g., number of GPUs, training time)?
2.  What is the sensitivity of the results to the choice of baseline function in the REINFORCE algorithm? Did you experiment with different baseline strategies?
3.  How did you determine the schedule for increasing the number of layers during training? What is the impact of this schedule on the final performance?
4.  Can you provide a more comprehensive comparison of NAS to other architecture search methods, including a discussion of the trade-offs between different approaches?
5.  What is the estimated inference speed of the discovered architecture on CIFAR-10 compared to other state-of-the-art models, reported in terms of images per second?
