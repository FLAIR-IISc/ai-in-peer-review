- Summary: Proposes a controller RNN that generates architectures, trains child networks, and updates via policy gradients to perform NAS, showing competitive results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank.  
- Decision: Reject — interesting idea but presentation is dated, under-evaluated, and not ready for ICLR in current NAS context.  
- Dated approach & limited baselines: Paper lacks comparison to more recent NAS methods (DARTS, ENAS, ProxylessNAS) and doesn't justify its advantages over newer techniques.  
- Lack of reproducibility & detail: Crucial implementation and hyperparameter details (controller RNN specifics, child training, handling invalid architectures) are missing.  
- Insufficient ablation studies: No thorough analysis of contributions from components like the baseline function, reward choice, or distributed training.  
- Overstated claims: Claims about designing models from scratch and outperforming human architectures are too strong and not adequately supported or qualified.  
- Expand related work: Needs a more comprehensive literature review situating this work relative to modern NAS advances.  
- Provide implementation details: Should include full controller architecture, search space description, skip-connection handling, and ideally open-source code.  
- Conduct thorough ablations: Recommend experiments isolating the impact of baseline/reward choices and other components.  
- Compare to modern NAS baselines: Evaluate against state-of-the-art NAS algorithms on the same datasets to validate claims.  
- Address computational cost: Quantify child-training cost and propose strategies to reduce search compute.  
- Clarify search space: Simplify/visualize the recurrent cell search space and show more example architectures.  
- Discuss limitations: Explicitly state scalability limits to larger datasets and more complex architectures.  
- Questions for authors: How does this compare to DARTS/ENAS in performance and cost; what are the controller hyperparameters; how are invalid architectures handled; how sensitive are results to baseline/reward; why does the controller favor larger top-layer filters?
