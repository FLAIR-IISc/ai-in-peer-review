### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel method, termed Neural Architecture Search (NAS), for automatically designing neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (RNN) as a "controller" to generate a variable-length string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. This child network is then trained to convergence on a target task, and its performance on a validation set is used as a reward signal. The controller is trained using a policy gradient algorithm (REINFORCE) to maximize this reward, thereby learning to generate better-performing architectures over time. The authors apply this method to two distinct tasks: designing a convolutional network for CIFAR-10 image classification and designing a recurrent cell for Penn Treebank language modeling. In both cases, the paper claims to discover novel architectures that achieve state-of-the-art or competitive performance compared to manually designed models.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Astronomical Computational Cost:** The proposed method requires a quantity of computational resources that is prohibitive for all but a handful of industrial labs. The CIFAR-10 experiments utilized 800 GPUs concurrently. This reliance on massive-scale parallelism makes the results difficult to verify and the method itself impossible to reproduce or build upon for the vast majority of the research community. While the final discovered architectures can be shared, the search method—the core contribution of the paper—is essentially a black box that cannot be independently studied or validated without similar resources.
2.  **Insufficient Comparison to Established Baselines:** The paper positions its method against human design but provides an inadequate comparison against established, computationally-tractable hyperparameter optimization methods. The only automated baseline presented is random search. While it is shown to be inferior, this is a relatively low bar. A comparison against more sophisticated, albeit less flexible, methods like Bayesian Optimization or even evolutionary algorithms on a constrained search space would be necessary to properly contextualize the performance of the proposed reinforcement learning approach. The paper dismisses these methods too quickly without providing empirical evidence of their inferiority in a comparable setting.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The central premise of using RL to "learn" architectures is intriguing, but the execution and evaluation raise significant concerns regarding methodological soundness and practicality.

**On Computational Demands and Reproducibility:** The paper reports training 12,800 child networks for the CIFAR-10 search, each for 50 epochs. With 800 concurrent workers, this represents an enormous computational budget. This approach feels less like an intelligent search and more like a brute-force exploration guided by a noisy gradient signal. Science progresses through reproducible experiments, and a method that requires an 800-GPU cluster is fundamentally not reproducible for the academic community this conference serves. This raises the question of whether the discovered architectures are a product of a genuinely effective learning procedure or simply the result of an exhaustive, massively parallel search that was bound to find a decent solution eventually.

**On Methodological Rigor and Analysis:** The RL formulation relies on the REINFORCE algorithm, which is known for its high variance and sample inefficiency. The paper uses a simple exponential moving average as a baseline to reduce variance, but the stability and convergence properties of this training process are not discussed. How sensitive are the results to the controller's own hyperparameters? Was the controller architecture itself tuned? Without such analysis, it is difficult to assess the robustness of the search method itself.

Furthermore, the final reported results for the best CIFAR-10 model are obtained after "a small grid search over learning rate, weight decay, batchnorm epsilon and what epoch to decay the learning rate." This conflates the contribution of the architecture search with a subsequent hyperparameter search. A more rigorous evaluation would involve training all child architectures with a fixed training protocol, or at least applying the same final hyperparameter tuning step to established baselines like ResNet or DenseNet to ensure a fair comparison. It is unclear how much of the reported performance gain comes from the novel architecture versus this final tuning step.

The comparison with random search (Figure 6) is a necessary but insufficient baseline. While policy gradient does outperform it, the gap is perhaps not as large as one might expect given the complexity of the RL controller. This reinforces the concern that the method's success may be heavily reliant on the sheer volume of architectures evaluated.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

While I am skeptical of the practicality and rigor of the proposed method, the ambition of the paper is commendable, and the results, taken at face value, are impressive. The following points are intended to help strengthen the paper.

*   **Analysis of Discovered Architectures:** The paper provides some description of the discovered architectures (e.g., preference for rectangular filters), but lacks a deeper analysis. What principles, if any, has the controller learned? Could you analyze the distribution of architectural motifs (e.g., skip connections, layer types) generated by the controller over the course of training? This would provide valuable insight into whether the controller is actually learning meaningful design principles.
*   **Efficiency Concerns:** The most significant barrier to this work's impact is its computational cost. I would strongly encourage the authors to discuss potential avenues for improving the efficiency of the search. Could proxies be used, such as training child models for fewer epochs, on a subset of the data, or at a lower resolution? Exploring such possibilities would greatly increase the value and accessibility of this research direction.
*   **Reward Function:** The reward for CIFAR-10 is the "maximum validation accuracy of the last 5 epochs cubed." The motivation for cubing the accuracy is not explained. This is a form of reward shaping that will strongly penalize small differences in accuracy among high-performing models. How does this choice affect the search dynamics compared to using the raw accuracy? A small ablation study on the reward function would be insightful.

**Questions:**
1.  Could you provide an estimate of the total compute time in GPU-days for the CIFAR-10 search?
2.  How was the comparison to random search implemented? Was it given the same computational budget (i.e., fully training 12,800 randomly sampled architectures)?
3.  For the transfer learning experiment on GNMT, was the baseline LSTM model subjected to the same hyperparameter regime as the NAS cell? The paper states the GNMT framework was "previously tuned for LSTM cells," which suggests the NAS cell may have had an advantage if the baseline was not re-tuned for this specific experiment.
4.  Can you provide more justification for dismissing established hyperparameter optimization methods like Bayesian Optimization? While they may be less flexible for variable-length architectures, a comparison on a fixed-length but complex search space could provide a crucial data point for evaluating your RL-based approach.
