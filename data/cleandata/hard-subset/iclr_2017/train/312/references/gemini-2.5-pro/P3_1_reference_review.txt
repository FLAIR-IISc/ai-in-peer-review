### **ICLR Review**

**Paper Title:** Neural Architecture Search with Reinforcement Learning

---

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel method for automatically designing neural network architectures. The core idea is to frame architecture design as a reinforcement learning problem. A controller, implemented as a recurrent neural network (RNN), learns to generate a variable-length string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. This child network is then trained on a target task, and its performance on a validation set is used as a reward signal to update the controller's parameters via a policy gradient algorithm (REINFORCE). The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on two highly competitive benchmarks: image classification on CIFAR-10 and language modeling on Penn Treebank (PTB). On CIFAR-10, NAS discovers a convolutional network that achieves state-of-the-art accuracy, outperforming most manually designed architectures. On PTB, it discovers a novel recurrent cell that surpasses the performance of heavily-tuned LSTM variants and demonstrates strong transferability to other tasks. The paper's main contribution is a general, scalable, and effective framework for automating architecture engineering.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **High Novelty and Impact:** The paper presents a paradigm shift in how neural network architectures are designed. By moving from manual expert-driven design to a learning-based approach, it opens up a significant and promising new research direction in meta-learning. The formulation of architecture generation as a reinforcement learning problem is both elegant and powerful.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The claims are substantiated with state-of-the-art results on two very different and challenging tasks. Discovering architectures from scratch that outperform meticulously engineered models like DenseNet (on CIFAR-10) and advanced LSTMs (on PTB) is a remarkable achievement and provides compelling evidence for the method's efficacy.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The primary strength of this work lies in its conceptual leap. While hyperparameter optimization and neuro-evolution are related fields, this paper's approach is substantially more flexible and scalable. The controller RNN's ability to generate variable-length sequences allows it to explore a vast and complex search space of architectures, including modern design elements like skip connections, which are crucial for deep models. The authors cleverly adapt an attention-like mechanism for predicting these skip connections, further enhancing the expressiveness of the search space.

The experimental validation is thorough and impressive. On CIFAR-10, the discovered model achieves a 3.65% test error, which was at or near the state of the art at the time of submission, demonstrating that the method can compete with human experts in the well-established domain of convolutional network design. The results on PTB are arguably even more significant. Designing novel recurrent cells is a notoriously difficult task, yet NAS discovers a cell that improves perplexity by 3.6 over the previous best result. The successful transfer of this cell to character-level language modeling and machine translation (GNMT) without task-specific tuning underscores the generality and robustness of the discovered architecture.

Furthermore, the authors include a crucial comparison against random search (Figure 6). This experiment effectively demonstrates that the policy gradient method is genuinely learning to navigate the search space and is not merely benefiting from a well-designed space or massive computation. The clear outperformance of policy gradient over random search validates the core learning-based mechanism of the paper.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is excellent, the following points could be addressed to strengthen it further:

*   **Computational Cost:** The most significant limitation of the proposed method is its extreme computational expense (e.g., training 12,800 networks on 800 GPUs for the CIFAR-10 search). This is a major barrier to reproducibility and adoption for the vast majority of the research community. While the results justify the expense in this foundational paper, a more explicit discussion of this limitation is warranted. It would be beneficial for the authors to comment on potential future directions for improving the sample efficiency of NAS, perhaps by using surrogate models, weight sharing, or more efficient reward estimation.

*   **Influence of Search Space Design:** The performance of NAS is inherently tied to the design of the search space. For example, the set of possible filter sizes `[1, 3, 5, 7]` or the tree structure for the recurrent cell are expert choices. It would be valuable to include a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the results to these choices. How much of the final performance is attributable to the search algorithm versus a well-constrained and well-chosen search space?

*   **Analysis of the Search Process:** The paper focuses on the final discovered architectures. However, an analysis of the controller's behavior during training would be highly insightful. For instance, how does the probability distribution over certain architectural choices (e.g., filter sizes, layer types) evolve over time? Does the controller converge to a specific region of the search space, or does it continue to explore? Such an analysis would provide a deeper understanding of what the controller is learning.

*   **Reward Function Details:** The reward for CIFAR-10 is the "maximum validation accuracy of the last 5 epochs cubed," and for PTB it is based on `c/(validation perplexity)^2`. The motivation for these specific transformations (cubing, squaring the inverse) is not fully explained. A brief justification for why these specific reward shapings were chosen over, for instance, the direct accuracy/perplexity would be helpful for clarity.

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational requirements? Specifically, what was the approximate wall-clock time for the CIFAR-10 and PTB search experiments? Do you have any insights on how to dramatically reduce this computational burden to make NAS more accessible?

2.  In Section 3.3, you describe heuristics to handle "compilation failures" (e.g., padding, connecting unconnected layers). How frequently did these failures occur during the search? Is it possible that these heuristics introduce a bias in the search process, perhaps favoring certain types of connectivity patterns?

3.  The generation of recurrent cells is based on a fixed tree structure with a "base number" of 8. What was the motivation for this specific tree-based parameterization? Did you experiment with allowing the controller to define the computation graph more freely?

4.  How stable was the REINFORCE training procedure? Given its known high variance, did you experience any issues with training instability, and were techniques beyond the moving average baseline (e.g., entropy regularization) considered or found to be necessary?
