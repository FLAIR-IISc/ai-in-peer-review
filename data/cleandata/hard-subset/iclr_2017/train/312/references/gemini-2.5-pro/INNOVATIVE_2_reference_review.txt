### Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

**Summary of Contributions:**
This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel and ambitious method for automating the design of neural network architectures. The core idea is to frame architecture generation as a learning problem. A controller, implemented as a recurrent neural network, learns to generate strings that specify the structure of a "child" network. This child network is then trained on a target task (e.g., image classification), and its final validation performance is used as a reward signal to update the controller via a policy gradient algorithm (REINFORCE). Over time, the controller learns to generate architectures that yield higher rewards. The authors demonstrate the power of this framework in two highly competitive domains: designing a novel convolutional network for CIFAR-10 that rivals the best human-designed models, and discovering a novel recurrent cell for Penn Treebank that establishes a new state-of-the-art in language modeling.

**Decision: Clear Accept**

**Key Reasons for Decision:**
1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Boldness:** The paper proposes a genuine paradigm shift. Instead of manually engineering architectures through intuition and trial-and-error, it presents a principled meta-learning approach where the process of design itself is learned. Framing architecture discovery as a reinforcement learning problem, where an agent (the controller) explores a vast, variable-length design space, is a highly original and powerful concept. This work moves beyond incremental improvements to existing architectures and opens up an entirely new research direction in automated model design.
2.  **Compelling and General Empirical Validation:** The claims are not merely conceptual; they are backed by extremely strong results on two distinct and challenging benchmarks. The fact that the same method can discover both a top-tier convolutional network and a new SOTA recurrent cell that outperforms the heavily-researched LSTM is a testament to the generality and power of the approach. The successful transfer of the discovered RNN cell to other tasks (character language modeling and machine translation) further solidifies the claim that the method discovers genuinely useful and fundamental computational structures.

**Supporting Arguments:**
The primary strength of this paper lies in its audacity. While prior work in hyperparameter optimization and neuro-evolution exists, this method provides a much more flexible and scalable framework for composing complex, variable-length architectures. Using an RNN controller to sequentially generate design choices is an elegant way to explore a combinatorial search space that would be intractable for traditional methods like grid or random search (as demonstrated by the comparison in Figure 6). The integration of techniques like skip connections via an attention-like mechanism further showcases the flexibility of the framework.

The empirical results are impressive. Achieving 3.65% error on CIFAR-10 is a remarkable feat, placing the discovered model among the very best, human-invented architectures. Even more compelling is the discovery of a new recurrent cell that improves upon the ubiquitous LSTM by a significant margin (3.6 perplexity on PTB). For years, the community has struggled to find recurrent structures that consistently outperform the LSTM, with most attempts resulting in more complexity for little to no gain. The success of NAS here is a powerful proof of concept that machine-driven discovery can surpass human intuition in complex design spaces.

The authors are forthcoming about the primary weakness of their method: the staggering computational cost (requiring 800 GPUs in parallel). While this makes the method inaccessible for most researchers, I do not see this as a reason for rejection. For a paper introducing a fundamentally new and promising direction, demonstrating its potential on a massive scale is a valid and valuable contribution. This work should be viewed as a foundational proof of concept, which will inevitably inspire a new line of research focused on making architecture search more efficient. The conceptual contribution is far too significant to be held back by its current computational demands.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors:**
These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.
1.  **Search Space Design:** The performance of the method seems dependent on the design of the search space itself (e.g., the set of possible filter sizes, the tree-based structure for RNN cells). Could you elaborate on how sensitive the final results are to these initial human choices? For instance, what happens if you provide a "bad" set of primitive operations? Does the controller learn to ignore them, or does performance degrade significantly?
2.  **Analysis of Discovered Architectures:** The paper provides visualizations of the discovered architectures, which is great. It would be even more insightful to have a deeper analysis of *why* these architectures might be effective. For example, in the discovered CNN, you note the prevalence of rectangular filters. Is there an intuition for why this might be beneficial on CIFAR-10? Similarly, for the RNN cell, what computational roles do the various discovered connections play?
3.  **Training Stability and Reward Shaping:** Training with REINFORCE using a sparse, delayed reward like final validation accuracy can be notoriously unstable. Could you comment on the stability of the controller's training process? Was the baseline crucial for making it work? Also, the reward for CIFAR-10 was `accuracy^3` while for PTB it was `c/(perplexity)^2`. Could you provide some intuition behind these specific reward shaping choices?
4.  **Clarity on RNN Cell Generation:** The process for generating recurrent cells in Section 3.4 is quite complex. While the example is helpful, a more detailed diagram or algorithm pseudocode that walks through the generation of one complete cell might improve clarity for readers trying to fully grasp the mechanics.
