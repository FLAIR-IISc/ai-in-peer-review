### **Review of "Neural Architecture Search"**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

The paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method for automatically discovering neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (RNN) as a controller. The controller generates a string that specifies the architecture of a "child network". This child network is then trained and evaluated on a validation set, and its performance (e.g., accuracy) is used as a reward signal to update the controller via policy gradient (REINFORCE). This process iteratively improves the controller's ability to generate high-performing architectures. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on two benchmark tasks: image classification on CIFAR-10, where they discover a novel convolutional architecture that rivals state-of-the-art human-designed models, and language modeling on Penn Treebank (PTB), where they generate a recurrent cell that outperforms standard LSTMs and achieves a new state-of-the-art result.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  The paper is marred by critical errors in key mathematical formulations and significant notational ambiguities that call into question the correctness of the implementation and the clarity of the proposed method.
2.  The experimental methodology lacks rigor, with several crucial design choices (e.g., reward shaping, training schedules) being arbitrary and unjustified. Furthermore, important implementation details are omitted, hindering reproducibility.
3.  The manuscript suffers from a pervasive lack of polish, including numerous typographical errors, grammatical mistakes, and a catastrophically formatted bibliography. The sheer volume of these minor flaws undermines the credibility of the work and suggests a rushed and careless preparation.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

While the core idea of using reinforcement learning to search for architectures is compelling and the empirical results are undoubtedly impressive, the execution and presentation of the paper fall far short of the standards required for publication at ICLR.

**A. Critical Technical and Notational Flaws:**
The most alarming issues are found in the core methodology section.
*   In Section 3.2, the policy gradient update rule (REINFORCE) is presented with what appears to be a severe typo. The gradient operator, which should be `∇θc`, is written as `5θc` in two separate equations. This is not a minor mistake; it is a fundamental error in the mathematical representation of the algorithm.
*   Throughout the paper, the multiplication operator is ambiguous. For instance, in Section 3.3, the attention equation uses `Wprev ∗ hj`. It is unclear if `∗` denotes matrix multiplication or an element-wise product. This ambiguity persists in Section 3.4, where `W1 ∗ xt` is written.
*   A critical ambiguity exists in the example computation for the recurrent cell in Section 3.4. The equation `a1 = ReLU ( (W3 ∗ xt) (W4 ∗ ht−1) )` is missing an operator between the two parenthesized terms. This is a crucial step in the cell's computation, and its omission makes the description of the method unintelligible.

**B. Lack of Methodological Rigor and Missing Details:**
The experimental setup contains numerous ad-hoc choices that are presented without any justification or ablation studies.
*   **Reward Shaping:** In the CIFAR-10 experiments, the reward is defined as the "maximum validation accuracy of the last 5 epochs cubed." In the PTB experiments, the reward is `c(validation perplexity)2`. Why cube the accuracy? Why square the perplexity? These are non-standard transformations that significantly alter the reward landscape. The paper provides no rationale for these choices, leaving the reader to wonder if they are essential for performance or simply arbitrary constants found through trial and error.
*   **Misleading Terminology:** In Section 3.2, the authors state that "convergence of each child network is reached when its training exceeds a certain number of epochs." This is a mischaracterization. A fixed number of epochs does not guarantee convergence; it is simply a fixed training budget. This terminological imprecision is misleading.
*   **Omitted Details:** For CIFAR-10 training, the paper states that the child model settings are "the same with those used in Huang et al. (2016a)" instead of specifying them directly. This is poor practice and hinders reproducibility. Similarly, the data whitening procedure is not specified (e.g., ZCA, per-channel standardization).
*   **Arbitrary Heuristics:** The training process relies on a curriculum where the controller increases the depth of child models "by 2 for the child models every 1,600 samples." The basis for this specific schedule is not explained.

**C. Poor Presentation and Formatting:**
The paper is replete with errors that should have been caught during proofreading.
*   **Bibliography:** The reference section is completely broken. Many entries contain raw BibTeX artifacts (e.g., `Andreas et al\.,? \Q2016\E, Andreas et al\.,`). This is unacceptable for a formal submission. Furthermore, there are duplicate entries (e.g., Huang et al., 2016 for DenseNet is listed twice consecutively).
*   **Typos and Grammar:** Minor errors are scattered throughout the text: "one a time" instead of "one at a time" (Sec. 2); "on the on the current action" (Sec. 3.2); "Residual Net" instead of "Residual Networks" (Sec. 3.3); a dangling comma in a list of filter sizes `[24, 36, 48, ]` (Sec. 4.1).
*   **Formatting:** A footnote in the introduction is placed awkwardly mid-sentence. The repeated use of scare quotes for terms like "child network" and "compilation failures" is stylistically awkward and unprofessional.

**4. Additional Feedback**

Despite the recommendation for rejection, the underlying research direction is highly valuable. Should the authors choose to revise and resubmit, I would strongly recommend the following:
*   Perform a thorough review of all mathematical equations for correctness and clarity. Explicitly define all operators and ensure consistent notation.
*   Provide clear justifications for all major methodological choices. If a specific form of reward shaping is used, explain why it was chosen and ideally include an ablation study showing its impact compared to a simpler reward function.
*   Incorporate all critical hyperparameters and training details directly into the paper to ensure it is self-contained and reproducible.
*   The description of the recurrent cell generation (Sec. 3.4) is particularly dense and difficult to follow. This section would benefit immensely from a clearer, step-by-step walkthrough with a simplified diagram that is better integrated with the text.
*   A comprehensive proofreading pass is essential to correct the numerous typos, grammatical errors, and, most importantly, to properly format the entire bibliography.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please confirm whether the `5θc` notation in the REINFORCE update equations is a typo for the gradient operator `∇θc`?
2.  What is the mathematical justification for cubing the accuracy and squaring the perplexity as reward signals? How sensitive is the search process to these specific transformations?
3.  In the recurrent cell computation `a1 = ReLU ( (W3 ∗ xt) (W4 ∗ ht−1) )`, what is the missing operator between `(W3 ∗ xt)` and `(W4 ∗ ht−1)`? Is it addition or element-wise multiplication?
