### **Review of "Neural Architecture Search"**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel method for automatically discovering high-performing neural network architectures. The core idea is to frame architecture design as a reinforcement learning problem. A controller, implemented as a recurrent neural network (RNN), generates a variable-length string that encodes the architecture of a "child" network. This child network is then trained on a target task (e.g., image classification), and its final validation accuracy is used as a reward signal. This reward is used to update the controller's parameters via a policy gradient algorithm (REINFORCE), encouraging the controller to generate better architectures over time.

The authors demonstrate the power and generality of this approach on two highly competitive benchmarks:
1.  **Image Classification on CIFAR-10:** NAS discovers a novel convolutional neural network that achieves a test error of 3.65%, outperforming state-of-the-art human-designed models at the time while being slightly faster.
2.  **Language Modeling on Penn Treebank (PTB):** NAS is used to design a novel recurrent cell that achieves a test perplexity of 62.4, a significant improvement of 3.6 perplexity over the previous state-of-the-art. The authors further show this cell's generalizability by achieving SOTA results on a character language modeling task and improving performance on a machine translation task.

The key contributions are: (1) a general, gradient-based framework for architecture search in a variable-length, compositional search space; (2) a method for incorporating modern architectural features like skip connections; and (3) state-of-the-art empirical results on two distinct and challenging domains, demonstrating the discovery of genuinely novel and effective architectures.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a new paradigm for automatic model design that is more principled and flexible than existing hyperparameter optimization techniques (e.g., Bayesian optimization) and more scalable than traditional neuro-evolution. The formulation of architecture generation as a learning problem, solved with reinforcement learning, is a significant conceptual leap that opens up a new and promising research direction.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The claims are backed by extremely strong, SOTA-level results on two very different and highly competitive benchmarks. Discovering an RNN cell that substantially outperforms the well-established and highly-tuned LSTM is a particularly impressive achievement and provides compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The approach presented in this paper effectively bridges the gap between hyperparameter optimization and neuro-evolution. While methods like Bayesian optimization are effective, they are typically limited to fixed-length search spaces. Neuro-evolution can explore complex topologies but often lacks the scalability and gradient-based efficiency of modern deep learning methods. NAS provides an elegant solution by using a differentiable controller (trained with a non-differentiable reward) to navigate a complex, combinatorial search space.

The empirical validation is thorough and convincing. For CIFAR-10, the authors don't just present a final number; they analyze the discovered architecture, noting its preference for rectangular filters and ResNet-like skip connections. The ablation showing that removing these connections drastically degrades performance supports the claim that the controller is discovering meaningful architectural motifs.

The Penn Treebank results are arguably the most impactful part of the paper. The language modeling community has long relied on the LSTM cell, with improvements being incremental. Discovering a novel cell structure that provides a significant leap in performance is a major contribution. Furthermore, the authors validate this discovery by demonstrating its successful transfer to character-level modeling and machine translation (GNMT), confirming that the learned cell is a genuinely useful building block and not just overfitted to a single task. The control experiment comparing against random search clearly demonstrates that the policy gradient approach is learning effectively and is superior to an unguided search, which is a crucial baseline for a method of this nature.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the work is impressive, there are several points that could be clarified or strengthened:

*   **Computational Cost:** The elephant in the room is the immense computational requirement. The CIFAR-10 experiment uses 800 GPUs concurrently to train 12,800 child networks. This is an extraordinary amount of compute that is inaccessible to almost the entire research community. While this doesn't invalidate the scientific contribution, the paper should be more upfront about this limitation in the main text. A discussion on the practicality of the method and potential avenues for improving its efficiency (e.g., weight sharing, performance prediction) would significantly strengthen the paper and set the stage for future work.
*   **Search Space Design:** The claim of designing models "from scratch" is slightly overstated, as the fundamental building blocks (types of convolutions, activation functions, combination methods for RNN cells) are predefined by human experts. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion on the design of these search spaces. How were the specific choices made (e.g., filter sizes [1, 3, 5, 7], the 'base 8' tree for the RNN cell)? The sensitivity of the final results to the design of this space is a critical aspect that is not fully explored.
*   **Reward Signal and Training Time:** The reward is the validation accuracy after training each child network for a fixed, relatively low number of epochs (50 for CIFAR-10). This is a computationally expensive and potentially noisy proxy for the architecture's true potential. Could the authors comment on the correlation between performance at 50 epochs and final converged performance? The fact that a separate hyperparameter grid search is needed on the best-found architecture suggests the initial search is not fully optimizing all parameters.

**5. Questions for Authors**

1.  Could you provide an estimate of the total computational cost for the main CIFAR-10 experiment in terms of "GPU-days"? Do you have preliminary ideas or results on reducing this cost, for instance, by training child models for fewer epochs or using parameter sharing across child models?
2.  In the PTB experiment, the search for the recurrent cell is based on a fixed tree structure. Could you provide more intuition behind this choice? Have you considered allowing the controller to define the tree structure itself, and what challenges might that entail?
3.  The REINFORCE algorithm is notoriously high-variance. How stable was the controller's training process? Did it require careful tuning, and were there instances of catastrophic forgetting where the controller's performance collapsed during training?
4.  How was the final architecture for CIFAR-10 selected from the 12,800 candidates? Was it simply the one with the highest validation accuracy during the search phase, or was there a re-evaluation process for a subset of promising candidates?
