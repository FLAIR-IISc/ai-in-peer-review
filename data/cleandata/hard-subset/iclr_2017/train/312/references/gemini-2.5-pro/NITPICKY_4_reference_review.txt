### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (RNN) as a controller. The controller generates a string that specifies the architecture of a "child network," which is then trained to convergence. The child network's performance on a validation set is used as a reward signal to update the controller via a policy gradient algorithm (REINFORCE). The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on two challenging tasks: designing a convolutional network for CIFAR-10 image classification and a recurrent cell for Penn Treebank language modeling. The paper claims that the automatically discovered architectures achieve state-of-the-art or competitive performance, outperforming many human-designed models.

**Decision: Reject**

While the core idea of using a controller trained with reinforcement learning to automate architecture design is promising and potentially impactful, the manuscript in its current state is marred by a substantial number of technical errors, methodological ambiguities, and severe presentational flaws. The sheer volume of these issues, ranging from critical typos in fundamental equations to a completely malformed reference section, prevents a confident assessment of the work's validity and suggests a lack of diligence in its preparation.

**Supporting Arguments for Decision**

1.  **Critical Technical Errors and Lack of Clarity:** The paper contains several errors and ambiguities that undermine its technical credibility.
    *   In Section 3.2, the policy gradient update rule is given with a "5θc" term (e.g., `5θc logP(...)`). This appears to be a critical typo for the gradient operator, `∇θc`. This error is repeated three times, casting serious doubt on the technical description of the core training algorithm.
    *   Throughout the paper, the multiplication operator `*` is used ambiguously (e.g., `W1 * xt + W2 * ht-1`). It is unclear whether this denotes matrix multiplication, element-wise product, or convolution, which is a crucial detail for reproducibility.
    *   The first paragraph of Section 3.1, which is meant to introduce the generation of convolutional architectures, ends abruptly with a colon: "...generate their hyperparameters as a sequence of tokens:". The subsequent explanation, presumably containing an example of the token sequence, is entirely missing. This is a major omission that hinders the understanding of the method.

2.  **Unjustified Methodological Choices:** The paper employs several non-standard techniques without adequate justification.
    *   For CIFAR-10 experiments, the reward signal is defined as "the maximum validation accuracy of the last 5 epochs cubed."
    *   For the Penn Treebank task, the reward is `c(validation perplexity)^2`.
    *   These non-linear transformations of the performance metric drastically alter the reward landscape. Cubing accuracy, for instance, disproportionately rewards high-performing architectures while severely penalizing others. No explanation is provided for why these specific transformations were chosen over more conventional reward formulations (e.g., raw accuracy or negative perplexity). This is a significant methodological detail that requires rigorous justification.

3.  **Gross Presentational and Formatting Deficiencies:** The paper suffers from a pervasive lack of polish that extends beyond minor typos.
    *   All figures and tables referenced in the text (Figure 1, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8 and Table 1, 2, 3) are absent from the document. Without these, it is impossible to evaluate the discovered architectures, verify the quantitative results, or assess the crucial comparison against random search.
    *   The reference section is completely unusable. The formatting appears corrupted, with misplaced characters (`\.,? \Q \E`), repeated author names (`Bergstra and Bengio.,? \Q2012\E, Bergstra and Bengio.,`), and duplicate entries (e.g., Huang et al. 2016 for DenseNets). This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a scientific publication and prevents the verification of citations and proper placement of the work within the literature.
    *   Numerous typos and grammatical errors are present throughout the text, such as "one a time" (should be "one at a time"), "on the on the current action," and an incomplete list of filter sizes: `[24, 36, 48, ]`.

The accumulation of these flaws, from fundamental equations to basic formatting, makes it impossible to fairly evaluate the scientific contribution of the work.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   **Thorough Proofreading:** The manuscript requires a complete and meticulous proofreading pass to correct all technical errors, typos, grammatical mistakes, and formatting inconsistencies. The REINFORCE equation, in particular, must be corrected.
*   **Include all Figures and Tables:** All referenced visual aids must be included and properly captioned. The results are meaningless without the tables, and the method is difficult to understand without the diagrams.
*   **Justify All Design Choices:** Provide clear reasoning for all methodological decisions, especially the non-standard reward functions. An ablation study showing the effect of these choices would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Improve Clarity and Notation:** Define all mathematical notation explicitly. The RNN generation process, especially for recurrent cells (Sec 3.4), is complex and would benefit from a clearer, more step-by-step explanation accompanied by well-designed diagrams.
*   **Formal Language:** Replace informal jargon like "compilation failures" with more precise, standard terminology such as "infeasible architectures."
*   **Properly Format References:** The bibliography must be completely rebuilt using a standard format (e.g., BibTeX with a standard ICLR style file).

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the gradient update rule in Section 3.2? Is the "5θc" term a typo for the gradient operator `∇θc`?
2.  What is the rationale behind cubing the validation accuracy and squaring the validation perplexity for the reward signal? How sensitive are the results to these specific transformations?
3.  Could you provide the missing text that was intended to follow the colon in the first paragraph of Section 3.1?
4.  In Section 4.2, you state the search space for recurrent cells has approximately `6 x 10^16` architectures with a "base number" of 8. How was this value calculated?
