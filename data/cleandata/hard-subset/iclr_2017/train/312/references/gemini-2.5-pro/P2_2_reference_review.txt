### Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces "Neural Architecture Search" (NAS), a novel method for automatically discovering neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (RNN), termed the "controller," to generate a string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. This child network is then trained on a target task, and its performance on a validation set is used as a reward signal. This reward is used to update the controller's parameters via a policy gradient algorithm (REINFORCE), encouraging the controller to generate better-performing architectures over time.

The authors demonstrate this framework on two distinct and highly competitive tasks:
1.  **Image Classification on CIFAR-10:** The controller generates convolutional neural network (CNN) architectures, including hyperparameters like filter sizes, strides, and skip connections.
2.  **Language Modeling on Penn Treebank (PTB):** The controller generates novel recurrent cell architectures, searching over combinations of operations and activation functions in a tree-structured computation graph.

The paper claims to achieve state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on both benchmarks. The discovered CNN for CIFAR-10 achieves a test error of 3.65%, rivaling the best human-designed architectures of the time. The discovered RNN cell achieves a new state-of-the-art test perplexity of 62.4 on PTB and shows strong generalization when transferred to other tasks like character-level language modeling and machine translation.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This paper presents a groundbreaking and highly ambitious approach to a fundamental problem in deep learning: architecture design. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and supported by exceptionally strong empirical results on challenging benchmarks.

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Leap:** The paper reframes architecture design as a learning problem, using a meta-learning approach where one network (the controller) learns to design another (the child). This is a significant departure from previous hyperparameter optimization methods, which typically operate on fixed-length search spaces. The use of an RNN controller to generate variable-length, compositional architecture descriptions is a powerful and elegant idea.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance:** The claims are backed by extensive and convincing experiments. Achieving a new state-of-the-art result on the Penn Treebank language modeling task with a novel, learned RNN cell is a major achievement. Furthermore, the discovered CNN architecture for CIFAR-10 is shown to be competitive with top-tier, manually engineered models like DenseNet, demonstrating the method's effectiveness in a very different domain.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's strength lies in its successful execution of a conceptually powerful idea.

First, the motivation is excellent. The introduction correctly identifies the shift from feature engineering to architecture engineering as a key bottleneck in deep learning, requiring significant human expertise. Automating this process is a well-established goal, and the paper provides a compelling argument for why its approach is more general than prior work in Bayesian optimization or neuro-evolution. The connection to program synthesis and reinforcement learning is well-drawn and situates the work within a solid theoretical context.

Second, the methodology is thoughtfully designed to handle the complexity of the problem. The use of REINFORCE is a natural choice for optimizing a non-differentiable reward (validation accuracy). The authors also incorporate several crucial techniques to make the approach viable:
*   **Parallelism:** The use of a parameter server with hundreds of controller and child replicas is a critical engineering contribution that makes this computationally intensive search tractable.
*   **Increasing Complexity:** The methods for incorporating skip connections via an attention-like mechanism and for designing complex RNN cells are non-trivial extensions that significantly expand the richness of the search space.
*   **Appropriate Baselines:** The comparison against random search is a vital control experiment. Figure 6 clearly shows that the policy gradient approach learns to explore the space more effectively than random sampling, which is a crucial validation of the learning-based methodology.

Third, the results are not just strong; they are insightful. The transfer learning experiments for the discovered RNN cell (named `NASCell`) are particularly persuasive. Showing that the cell performs well on character-level language modeling and improves BLEU scores in the GNMT machine translation framework provides strong evidence that the controller has discovered a genuinely useful and general-purpose computational building block, not just one that is overfitted to PTB.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is an excellent paper, it could be strengthened by addressing the following points. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from my "Accept" recommendation.

*   **Computational Cost:** The most significant concern is the immense computational resource requirement (e.g., "800 GPUs concurrently"). This is a major barrier to reproducibility and adoption for the broader academic community. While the results justify the expense, the paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of this limitation. A section discussing the total computational budget (e.g., in GPU-hours) and potential future work on improving search efficiency would provide important context.
*   **Influence of Search Space Design:** The performance of NAS is fundamentally dependent on the design of the search space (e.g., the set of possible operations, filter sizes, activation functions). The paper could provide more intuition or analysis on how these initial human choices affect the final discovered architecture. For instance, for the best CIFAR-10 result, the search space was constrained (predicting blocks of 3 layers). A brief discussion of why this was done and its impact would be illuminating.
*   **Clarity of RNN Cell Generation:** Section 3.4, which describes the generation of recurrent cells, is quite dense. While Figure 5 is helpful, a more detailed algorithmic description or pseudo-code could significantly improve clarity and aid others in implementing this part of the method.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The paper would be even stronger with more ablation studies. For example, how sensitive is the performance to the controller's architecture (e.g., a single-layer LSTM vs. two layers)? How critical is the use of an exponential moving average baseline for REINFORCE in stabilizing training?

**5. Questions for Authors**

1.  Could you quantify the total computational cost of a full NAS run for both the CIFAR-10 and PTB experiments? Do you have preliminary ideas or results on how this method could be made more computationally efficient (e.g., via weight sharing or performance prediction models) to make it accessible to researchers with fewer resources?
2.  During the search process, did you observe if the controller converged to a single mode of good architectures, or did it maintain a high-entropy policy that sampled diverse but similarly high-performing architectures?
3.  How robust is the final performance to the random seed of the controller's initialization? That is, if you were to run the entire search process multiple times, how much variance is there in the performance of the best architecture found in each run?
