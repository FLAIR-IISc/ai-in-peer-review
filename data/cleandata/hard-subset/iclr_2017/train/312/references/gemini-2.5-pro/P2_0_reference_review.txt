### **Review of "Neural Architecture Search"**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel method for automatically designing neural network architectures. The core idea is to frame architecture generation as a learning problem. A recurrent neural network, termed the "controller," is trained to output a variable-length string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. This child network is then trained on a target task (e.g., image classification), and its final validation performance is used as a reward signal. The controller's parameters are updated using a policy gradient algorithm (REINFORCE) to maximize this expected reward, encouraging it to generate better-performing architectures over time.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on two highly competitive benchmarks. For image classification on CIFAR-10, NAS discovers a convolutional network that achieves a test error of 3.65%, which is competitive with the best human-designed architectures at the time while being slightly faster. For language modeling on the Penn Treebank (PTB) dataset, NAS designs a novel recurrent cell that surpasses previous state-of-the-art models, achieving a test perplexity of 62.4. The generalizability of this discovered cell is further validated through strong performance on character-level language modeling and a modest improvement in a large-scale neural machine translation task.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel and significant research direction. My decision is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Problem Formulation:** The paper proposes a genuinely new paradigm for architecture design. By framing it as a reinforcement learning problem where an agent learns to generate architecture descriptions, it moves beyond the limitations of fixed-length hyperparameter optimization (like Bayesian optimization) and provides a more principled, learning-based alternative to the often heuristic-heavy field of neuro-evolution. This approach has the potential to fundamentally change how deep learning models are developed.

2.  **Impressive and Rigorous Empirical Results:** The claims are backed by state-of-the-art results on two very different and challenging tasks. The discovery of a novel, high-performing recurrent cell on PTB is particularly compelling, as this is a domain where architectural innovation has been notoriously difficult. The inclusion of a direct comparison against random search and ablation studies (e.g., on skip connections) provides strong evidence that the policy gradient method is effectively learning to navigate the search space.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The core strength of this work lies in its elegant combination of several powerful ideas—RNNs for sequential generation, reinforcement learning for optimizing non-differentiable metrics, and large-scale distributed training for feasibility.

The method's flexibility is a key advantage. The controller's ability to generate variable-length strings allows it to explore a vast and complex search space that includes modern architectural motifs like skip connections, which are crucial for deep networks. The extension from designing convolutional architectures to designing the internal structure of an RNN cell is a powerful demonstration of the framework's generality.

The experimental validation is thorough. On CIFAR-10, the discovered architecture is not only accurate but is shown to be non-trivial; ablating its skip connections significantly degrades performance, confirming that the learned structure is meaningful. The PTB results are even more impressive. Achieving a 3.6 perplexity improvement over the previous state-of-the-art is a significant leap. The subsequent transfer learning experiments, showing that the discovered `NASCell` generalizes to character-level modeling and machine translation, are crucial for demonstrating that the learned cell is not merely overfitted to the specifics of the PTB word-level task but captures some fundamental and useful recurrent computation. The comparison against random search is a critical control experiment that effectively refutes the simple explanation that the gains come merely from exploring a good search space, showing instead that the controller actively learns to find better regions of that space.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is excellent, the following points could further strengthen it. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from my decision to accept.

*   **Discussion of Computational Cost:** The experimental setup is massive, using 800 GPUs concurrently for the CIFAR-10 search. This represents a significant barrier to entry for most researchers. A more explicit discussion of the total computational budget (e.g., in GPU-days) would be valuable context. Furthermore, a brief discussion on the potential for future work to improve the sample efficiency of the search (which became a major focus of subsequent NAS research) would be welcome.

*   **Clarity on Search Space Constraints:** For the best CIFAR-10 model, the paper states that pooling layers were included "at layer 13 and layer 24" and that the search was structured around blocks of 3 layers. This feels somewhat ad-hoc compared to the general framework described. It would be helpful to clarify if these structural constraints were pre-defined or part of the search, and to motivate why they were necessary. A clearer distinction between what is learned versus what is manually specified in the search space would improve reproducibility.

*   **Analysis of Discovered Architectures:** The paper presents visualizations of the discovered architectures, which is great. Further analysis would be even better. For instance, are there recurring motifs that the controller learns to favor? How does the distribution of hyperparameters (e.g., filter sizes) evolve during training? Such insights could provide valuable knowledge not just about the search process, but about what constitutes good architectural design principles.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

To clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  The reward for the CIFAR-10 experiments is stated as the "maximum validation accuracy of the last 5 epochs cubed." What was the motivation for cubing the accuracy? How sensitive is the performance of the controller to this specific shaping of the reward function?

2.  Regarding the handling of "compilation failures" for CNNs (e.g., incompatible layer dimensions), the paper mentions several simple techniques. How often did these failures occur in practice, and did you find that the controller learned to avoid proposing such invalid architectures over time?

3.  Could you provide more detail on the total search time? For example, how many wall-clock days did it take to run the 12,800 architecture evaluations for the CIFAR-10 search, even with the massive parallelism?
