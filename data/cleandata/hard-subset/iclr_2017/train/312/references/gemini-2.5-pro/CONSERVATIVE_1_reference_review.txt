### Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes "Neural Architecture Search" (NAS), a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (RNN) as a controller. The controller generates a string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. This child network is then trained and its performance on a validation set is used as a reward signal to update the controller's parameters via a policy gradient algorithm (REINFORCE). The authors apply this method to two distinct tasks: designing a convolutional network for CIFAR-10 image classification and designing a recurrent cell for Penn Treebank language modeling. The paper claims that the automatically discovered architectures achieve state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art performance, outperforming many manually designed architectures.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Prohibitive Computational Cost:** The proposed method relies on a computational budget that is orders of magnitude beyond the reach of most research institutions. The use of 800 GPUs training child networks in parallel for the CIFAR-10 experiments represents an extreme application of brute-force computation. While the results are notable, the approach is more of an engineering demonstration of what is possible with massive resources than a practical, reproducible scientific method. This makes it difficult to verify the results or build upon the work, hindering its potential impact on the broader community.

2.  **Insufficient Validation and Analysis:** The paper's claims of discovering superior architectures are not supported by a sufficiently rigorous comparison to established baselines and a thorough analysis of the method itself. The comparison to random search is a necessary but not sufficient baseline. Furthermore, the final reported results for the discovered architectures are obtained after significant post-search manual tuning (grid search, adding filters), which complicates the claim of a fully automated process and calls into question the fairness of the comparisons to prior work.

### Supporting Arguments

**On Computational Cost and Practicality:** The methodology section describes a distributed setup involving 100 controller replicas and 800 child models being trained concurrently. Over the course of the experiment, 12,800 architectures are evaluated. Training a single deep CNN on CIFAR-10 for 50 epochs is already a non-trivial task; repeating this 12,800 times is an immense undertaking. This heavy reliance on computational power makes the method an outlier, raising concerns about its generalizability and scientific value. A core goal of machine learning research should be to develop efficient and principled methods. This work, while ambitious, seems to push in the opposite direction by substituting scientific insight with computational force. It is unclear what is being learned about the principles of good architecture design, beyond what can be found by an extremely expensive, guided search.

**On Methodological Soundness and Experimental Rigor:**

*   **Comparison to Baselines:** The paper compares its policy gradient approach to random search. While it shows an improvement, this is an expected outcome given the guided nature of RL. However, a crucial baseline is missing: random search constrained to the same total *wall-clock time* or *computational budget*. Given the overhead of the controller and the policy gradient updates, it is plausible that a well-tuned random search could evaluate more architectures in the same amount of time, potentially finding a comparable or better solution. The provided Figure 6, which plots perplexity against the number of models evaluated, does not address this critical comparison.

*   **Confounding Factors in Results:** The process for obtaining the final CIFAR-10 result (3.65% error) involves several steps after the automated search concludes: 1) selecting the best architecture based on validation performance, 2) running a small grid search over hyperparameters, and 3) adding 40 more filters to each layer. This manual post-processing makes it difficult to attribute the final performance solely to the novel architecture found by NAS. The baseline DenseNet model that achieved 3.46% error is dismissed somewhat cursorily for using 1x1 convolutions, which are a standard architectural component. A more convincing approach would have been to include such components in the search space.

*   **Lack of Ablation and Insight:** The paper presents a very complex system but provides limited analysis of its components. For example, the reward signal is the cubed validation accuracy. What is the justification for this specific shaping? How sensitive is the performance to this choice? The paper also does not explore the dynamics of the controller's learning process. For example, it would be illuminating to see how the controller's distribution over filter sizes or layer types evolves over time. Without such analysis, the controller remains a black box, and few generalizable principles of architecture design are extracted.

### Additional Feedback

While I have significant reservations about the practicality and rigor of the current work, the core idea of formulating architecture design as a learning problem is interesting and worthy of exploration. To improve the paper, I suggest the following:

*   **Address Computational Efficiency:** The most significant barrier to this work's impact is its computational requirement. The paper would be substantially stronger if it included experiments on a more efficient version of the method, perhaps using proxy tasks (e.g., training child models for fewer epochs or on a subset of the data) to approximate the reward.
*   **Strengthen Baseline Comparisons:** A comparison against random search and other established hyperparameter optimization techniques (e.g., Bayesian Optimization) under a fixed, reasonable computational budget would be far more convincing.
*   **Provide Deeper Analysis:** More insight into *what* the controller learns is needed. Visualizing the probability distributions over architectural choices throughout training could reveal interesting patterns. An ablation study on the components of the search space (e.g., with and without skip connections) for a fixed architecture would also help disentangle the contributions of different elements.
*   **Clarify Claims:** The language should be more precise. The process is not entirely "from scratch" as it relies on a predefined set of human-designed primitives (convolutions, batch norm, etc.). The contribution is the composition of these primitives, which is still a significant step, but the framing should reflect this.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you provide an estimate of the total GPU-days required for the main CIFAR-10 experiment?
2.  How was the reward function (cubed validation accuracy) chosen? Have you experimented with other transformations, such as a linear or log-scaled accuracy, and how did they affect the search process?
3.  Regarding the final CIFAR-10 model: what was the performance of the best architecture discovered by the search *before* the manual addition of 40 filters and the post-search hyperparameter grid search? How does that unmodified model compare to the baselines?
