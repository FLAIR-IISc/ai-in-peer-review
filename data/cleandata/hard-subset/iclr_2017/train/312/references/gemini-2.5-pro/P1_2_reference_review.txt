### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel and ambitious method for automatically designing neural network architectures. The core idea is to frame architecture design as a reinforcement learning problem. A "controller" recurrent neural network generates a variable-length string that encodes the architecture of a "child" network. This child network is then trained on a target task, and its performance on a validation set is used as a reward signal to update the controller's parameters via a policy gradient method (REINFORCE). The paper demonstrates this method's power in two distinct and highly competitive domains: discovering a convolutional neural network for image classification on CIFAR-10 and a recurrent cell for language modeling on Penn Treebank. The key contributions are: 1) A general, gradient-based framework for exploring complex, variable-length architecture spaces. 2) The introduction of techniques to incorporate modern architectural features like skip connections. 3) State-of-the-art empirical results on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, where the automatically discovered architectures outperform most prominent human-invented models.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **High Novelty and Impact:** The paper presents a new paradigm for architecture design that is significantly more flexible and powerful than existing hyperparameter optimization techniques. By framing the problem in a reinforcement learning context and using an RNN controller, it opens up a new and promising research direction in meta-learning and automated machine learning.
2.  **Exceptional Empirical Results:** The claims are substantiated with state-of-the-art results on two very challenging and standard benchmarks. Discovering a novel CNN that achieves 3.65% error on CIFAR-10 and a recurrent cell that achieves a 62.4 perplexity on Penn Treebank are remarkable achievements that convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness. The transfer learning results for the discovered RNN cell further strengthen these claims.

### Supporting Arguments

The approach is well-motivated by the increasing complexity and manual effort involved in modern deep learning architecture design. The paper does an excellent job of positioning itself within the literature, drawing clear distinctions between its method and related work in Bayesian hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis.

The technical method is sound. The use of the REINFORCE algorithm to optimize a non-differentiable reward (validation accuracy) is a natural fit for this problem. The authors astutely identify the high variance of this estimator and mitigate it with a baseline, which is standard and good practice. The proposed mechanism for generating skip connections using an attention-like model is an elegant way to expand the search space to include more complex, modern topologies reminiscent of ResNets or DenseNets.

The experimental validation is extensive and rigorous.
*   **Scale:** The use of a large-scale distributed training setup (e.g., 800 GPUs for CIFAR-10) is a major engineering feat and provides confidence that the search space was explored meaningfully. While the resource requirement is immense, it serves as a powerful proof-of-concept for the method's potential.
*   **Baselines:** The comparison against a wide range of top-performing, human-designed architectures in Tables 1 and 2 is thorough. Crucially, the authors include a control experiment comparing their policy gradient approach to random search (Figure 6), demonstrating that the controller is indeed learning to propose better architectures over time and is not just a product of a well-designed search space.
*   **Generalization:** The experiments on generating both convolutional and recurrent architectures showcase the method's flexibility. The transfer learning results—applying the PTB-discovered cell to character-level modeling and the WMT14 machine translation task—are particularly compelling evidence that the learned architectural motifs are not merely overfit to a single task but capture generally useful computational principles.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is excellent, a few points could be clarified or expanded upon to further increase its impact. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from my decision to accept.

1.  **Computational Cost:** The elephant in the room is the astronomical computational cost (e.g., 12,800 models trained for 50 epochs each on CIFAR-10). This currently places the method out of reach for most of the research community. While the paper's goal is to demonstrate what is possible, a discussion of this limitation and potential future work to improve efficiency (e.g., parameter sharing, early stopping, or learning on proxy tasks) would be very valuable.
2.  **Search Space Design:** The search space, while large, is still manually designed (e.g., the set of possible filter sizes [1, 3, 5, 7], the "base number" for the RNN cell). A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the final results to these initial choices would be informative. For example, how was the "base number" of 8 chosen for the RNN cell search?
3.  **Clarity on RNN Cell Generation:** The description of generating recurrent cell architectures in Section 3.4 is quite dense. While Figure 5 is helpful, the process of indexing the tree and predicting combinations, activations, and cell variable connections could benefit from a more step-by-step textual walkthrough to improve readability.

### Questions for Authors

1.  How sensitive is the REINFORCE training procedure to its own hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate of the controller, choice of baseline)? Given the high variance inherent in policy gradient methods and the expensive reward calculation, was the training process stable?
2.  Could you provide more intuition about the search process itself? For instance, does the entropy of the controller's policy decrease over time, indicating a convergence towards a specific region of the architecture space?
3.  The paper notes that the discovered CNN has many rectangular filters and that the discovered RNN cell has similarities to the LSTM. Are there other interesting or non-intuitive architectural motifs that were consistently discovered by the controller? This could provide valuable insights for manual architecture design in the future.
