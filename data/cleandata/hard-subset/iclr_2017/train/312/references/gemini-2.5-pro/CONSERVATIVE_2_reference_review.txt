### **Summary**

This paper proposes "Neural Architecture Search" (NAS), a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (RNN) as a controller. The controller generates a string that specifies the architecture of a "child network." This child network is then trained and its validation accuracy is used as a reward signal to update the controller's parameters via reinforcement learning (specifically, the REINFORCE algorithm). The authors demonstrate this approach on two benchmark tasks: designing a convolutional network for CIFAR-10 image classification and designing a recurrent cell for Penn Treebank language modeling. In both cases, the paper claims that the automatically discovered architectures achieve state-of-the-art or competitive performance compared to manually designed models.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Prohibitive Computational Cost:** The proposed method relies on a brute-force search at an astronomical scale (e.g., training 800 networks concurrently on 800 GPUs for the CIFAR-10 experiments). This makes the approach impractical for all but a few research labs, raising serious questions about its utility and reproducibility. It positions the work less as an advancement in efficient architecture design and more as a demonstration of what is possible with massive computational resources.

2.  **Insufficiently Rigorous Experimental Protocol:** The evaluation process conflates the contribution of the automated search with subsequent manual hyperparameter tuning and architectural modification. The final reported state-of-the-art results are not from the direct output of the search algorithm but are achieved after a separate grid search and, in the CIFAR-10 case, a manual adjustment to the number of filters. This lack of a clean ablation makes it difficult to assess the true effectiveness of the proposed search method itself.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The core idea of framing architecture design as a sequential generation problem for an RNN controller is novel. However, the implementation and evaluation presented here lack the rigor required to endorse this as a viable or superior alternative to established methods.

**On Computational Cost and Practicality:** The paper states that for CIFAR-10, 12,800 architectures were evaluated using a setup of 100 controller replicas, each training 8 child models in parallel. This requires 800 GPUs running concurrently. For the Penn Treebank experiments, 400 CPUs were used. Such requirements are far beyond the reach of the vast majority of the academic and even industrial research community. While state-of-the-art results are valuable, methods that achieve them must be scrutinized for their efficiency. The paper does not provide a compelling argument that this reinforcement learning approach is fundamentally more sample-efficient than simpler, established search strategies like random search or Bayesian optimization when normalized for computational budget. The comparison to random search (Figure 6) is a good first step, but a more thorough quantitative analysis is needed. The massive parallelism may simply be accelerating a search that is not significantly more intelligent than its baselines.

**On Methodological Soundness and Baselines:** The experimental procedure for validating the final discovered architecture is concerning. After the search phase identifies a promising candidate, the authors "run a small grid search over learning rate, weight decay, batchnorm epsilon and what epoch to decay the learning rate." Furthermore, the best CIFAR-10 result (3.65% error) is achieved by "adding 40 more filters to each layer of our architecture." This manual, post-hoc modification obscures the true performance of the architecture as discovered by the controller. A more scientifically sound evaluation would compare the raw output of the search against baselines, with all models being subjected to the same subsequent hyperparameter tuning protocol. As it stands, it is unclear whether the performance gains come from a superior architecture or from more extensive tuning.

Furthermore, the reward signal itself is based on training child networks for only 50 epochs on CIFAR-10. This is a very short training run, and it is well-known that different architectures can have vastly different convergence dynamics. This protocol inherently favors architectures that learn quickly, not necessarily those that can achieve the best final performance. This introduces a significant bias into the search process that is not discussed or justified. The choice to cube the validation accuracy as a reward signal also seems arbitrary and lacks justification; the sensitivity of the search to this reward shaping is not explored.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions**

While my primary recommendation is to reject the paper in its current form, the underlying concept is interesting. The following points are intended to help strengthen a future submission.

*   A more detailed analysis of the architectures discovered would be welcome. The paper notes that the controller found architectures with rectangular filters and skip connections, but it would be more insightful to see how the controller's preferences evolve during training. Does it learn generalizable design principles?
*   The work would be significantly strengthened by demonstrating its effectiveness in a more computationally constrained setting. Can this method find competitive architectures using, for example, an order of magnitude less compute? This would make a much stronger case for its general applicability.
*   The comparison against random search should be expanded. The best model found by random search after the full 12,800 trials should be reported and subjected to the same post-hoc tuning as the model found by NAS.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide an estimate of the total computational cost in GPU-days for the CIFAR-10 and PTB experiments? How does this compare to the estimated cost of running established hyperparameter optimization methods like TPE or Gaussian Processes on a similar search space?
2.  What is the performance of the best CIFAR-10 architecture as found by the controller *before* the manual grid search and the addition of 40 filters? This is a crucial baseline for understanding the direct contribution of your search method.
3.  Have you investigated the effect of the child network training time on the quality of the final architecture? Is it possible that training for only 50 epochs biases the search away from more complex architectures that might require longer to converge but could ultimately be superior?
4.  Can you provide a justification for using the cube of the validation accuracy as the reward signal? How did you arrive at this formulation, and have you tested simpler reward functions, such as the accuracy itself?
