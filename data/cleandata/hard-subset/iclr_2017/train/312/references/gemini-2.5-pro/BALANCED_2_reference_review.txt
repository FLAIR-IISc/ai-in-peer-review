### 1. Summary

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel method for automatically discovering high-performing neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (RNN), termed the "controller," to generate a variable-length string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. This child network is then trained on the target task, and its performance on a validation set is used as a reward signal. This reward is used to update the controller's parameters via a policy gradient algorithm (REINFORCE), encouraging the controller to generate better-performing architectures over time. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this method on two challenging and distinct tasks: finding a state-of-the-art convolutional neural network (CNN) for image classification on CIFAR-10 and discovering a novel, high-performance recurrent cell for language modeling on the Penn Treebank (PTB) dataset. The discovered architectures are shown to be competitive with, or superior to, the best human-designed architectures.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a highly novel and impactful approach to a fundamental problem in deep learning: architecture design. Framing architecture generation as a learning problem for an RNN controller trained with reinforcement learning is an elegant and powerful paradigm that opens up a significant new direction for research in automated machine learning (AutoML).
2.  **Exceptional Empirical Results:** The claims are substantiated by state-of-the-art results on two standard, competitive benchmarks in different domains (vision and language). The method not only matches but surpasses top human-designed architectures, providing strong evidence of its effectiveness and generality.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

**Strengths:**
*   **Powerful and General Framework:** The proposed method is highly flexible. The use of an RNN controller to generate a sequence of tokens allows for a vast and variable-length search space, which can accommodate complex designs, including skip connections and different layer types. The successful application to both CNNs and RNNs demonstrates the framework's generality.
*   **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The results are extremely compelling. On CIFAR-10, the discovered architecture achieves a 3.65% test error, which is on par with or better than the best manually designed networks at the time. On PTB, the discovered recurrent cell achieves a perplexity of 62.4, a significant improvement of 3.6 perplexity over the previous state-of-the-art.
*   **Valuable Discovered Artifacts:** The paper produces not only a method but also novel, high-quality architectures. The fact that the PTB cell (NASCell) generalizes well to other tasks (character language modeling and machine translation) and has been integrated into TensorFlow is a testament to its practical value.
*   **Thorough Experimental Design:** The authors perform crucial control experiments, most notably a comparison against random search, which clearly shows the benefit of the learned policy. They also provide ablations, such as removing skip connections from the discovered CIFAR-10 model, to demonstrate that the learned architectural motifs are meaningful. The engineering effort, involving large-scale distributed training, is substantial and necessary to make this computationally intensive search feasible.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Extreme Computational Cost:** The most significant limitation of the proposed method is its formidable computational expense. Training 12,800 models on CIFAR-10 requires 800 GPUs running in parallel. This cost makes the method inaccessible to the vast majority of the research community, raising concerns about reproducibility and broader applicability. While an important proof of concept, its practical utility is limited to institutions with massive computational resources.
*   **Sensitivity to Search Space Design:** While the search is automated, the design of the search space itself is a critical, manual step. The choice of operations (e.g., filter sizes, activation functions, combination methods for RNN cells) constrains the search and likely has a large impact on the final result. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how this search space was designed and its potential impact.
*   **Proxy Metric for Reward:** The reward signal is the validation accuracy of a child model after a fixed, and relatively short, number of training epochs (e.g., 50 for CIFAR-10). This is a computationally necessary but potentially noisy proxy for the true potential of an architecture. This approach may bias the search towards architectures that converge quickly, rather than those that might achieve better performance if trained to full convergence.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended as constructive suggestions and do not detract from my positive assessment of the paper.
*   It would be beneficial to include a more explicit discussion of the limitations, particularly the computational cost, and speculate on future research directions to improve efficiency. For instance, exploring techniques like weight sharing or performance prediction could be mentioned as promising avenues for making NAS more accessible.
*   The reward function for CIFAR-10 is specified as the "maximum validation accuracy of the last 5 epochs cubed." The cubic relationship is non-standard. A brief justification for this choice would be helpful. Does it help to amplify the signal for high-performing models? An ablation study on the reward shaping would strengthen the paper.
*   The process for obtaining the final 3.65% error model on CIFAR-10 involved adding more filters post-search. It would be good to clarify if this was a manual heuristic or part of a final automated tuning step (like the described grid search). This detail is important for understanding the degree of automation in the final result.
*   An analysis of the controller's behavior over time could provide fascinating insights. For example, showing how the distribution of certain architectural choices (e.g., filter sizes, use of skip connections) evolves during training would help illuminate the learning process.

### 5. Questions for Authors

1.  How was the curriculum learning strategy for the number of layers in the CIFAR-10 search (increasing depth every 1,600 samples) determined? How crucial is this strategy to finding a good final architecture compared to searching over a fixed, large depth from the start?
2.  Could you provide more detail on the random search baseline? Was it performed over the exact same search space, and was each randomly sampled architecture trained for the same number of epochs to get its validation accuracy?
3.  The computational cost is a major barrier. Have you explored or considered any methods for reducing the cost of evaluating a single architecture, such as training for fewer epochs, on a subset of the data, or using other forms of proxy metrics? How do you think this might affect the quality of the final discovered architecture?
4.  The hyperparameters of the controller itself (e.g., 2-layer LSTM with 35 hidden units, ADAM with lr=0.0006) seem to be manually chosen. How sensitive is the architecture search process to these meta-parameters?
