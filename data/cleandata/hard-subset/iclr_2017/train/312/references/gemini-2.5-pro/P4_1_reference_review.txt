### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method for automatically discovering neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (RNN) as a controller. The controller generates a string that specifies the architecture of a "child network". This child network is then trained on a target task, and its performance on a validation set is used as a reward signal to update the controller's parameters via policy gradient reinforcement learning (REINFORCE). The paper demonstrates this method in two distinct domains: designing convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for image classification on CIFAR-10 and designing recurrent cells for language modeling on the Penn Treebank (PTB) dataset. The key contributions include: 1) A novel, gradient-based framework for searching a variable-length, complex architecture space; 2) A method for incorporating modern architectural motifs like skip connections into the search space; and 3) The discovery of novel architectures that achieve state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance on both CIFAR-10 and PTB, outperforming many manually designed architectures. The discovered recurrent cell is also shown to generalize well to other tasks, including character-level language modeling and machine translation.

### 2. Decision: Accept

This paper is a clear accept. The key reasons for this decision are:
1.  **High Novelty and Impact:** The paper presents a novel and powerful paradigm for automating architecture design by framing it as a learning problem solvable with reinforcement learning. This approach fundamentally expands the scope of automated machine learning beyond hyperparameter tuning to the generation of complex, variable-length network structures.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The claims are exceptionally well-supported by state-of-the-art results on two very different and highly competitive benchmarks. Discovering a novel recurrent cell that outperforms the heavily-engineered LSTM on the Penn Treebank dataset is a particularly significant achievement.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and structured. The core idea is introduced intuitively in the first section and then detailed methodically. The use of figures, particularly for explaining the generation of skip connections and recurrent cells, is effective. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly, allowing the reader to understand the context and significance of the achievements.

**Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is technically sound. The formulation of architecture generation as a sequential decision process is logical, making the choice of an RNN controller natural. The application of the REINFORCE algorithm with a baseline to optimize the controller for a non-differentiable reward (validation accuracy) is a standard and appropriate technique. The engineering solutions to make this computationally demanding process feasible—namely, large-scale distributed training with asynchronous updates—are critical to the method's success and are well-explained. The control experiment comparing against random search provides strong evidence that the policy gradient method is effectively navigating the search space and not just benefiting from a well-designed space or massive computation.

**Novelty:** The work is highly novel. While related to hyperparameter optimization and neuro-evolution, it presents a distinct and more flexible approach. Unlike most Bayesian optimization methods, NAS can handle a variable-length, compositional search space. Compared to many neuro-evolution methods, it leverages gradient-based learning for the controller, which proves to be a very effective search strategy. The idea of having a neural network design another neural network is a powerful meta-learning concept, and this paper provides one of the first large-scale, successful demonstrations of this principle. The specific techniques for generating skip connections via an attention-like mechanism and for constructing recurrent cells from a tree of primitive operations are also novel contributions.

**Significance of Results:** The empirical validation is the strongest aspect of the paper.
*   On CIFAR-10, the discovered CNN achieves a test error of 3.65%, which was competitive with or superior to the best human-invented architectures at the time of publication, such as DenseNet.
*   On Penn Treebank, the discovered recurrent cell achieves a perplexity of 62.4, a significant improvement of 3.6 perplexity over the previous state-of-the-art. This is remarkable given how heavily optimized LSTM-based models are for this task.
*   The successful transfer of the discovered cell to character language modeling and machine translation (improving BLEU on a GNMT system) demonstrates that the learned architecture is not merely overfitted to a single dataset but captures a generally useful computational structure.

### 4. Additional Feedback

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Computational Cost:** The primary limitation of the proposed method is its extreme computational expense (e.g., 800 GPUs for the CIFAR-10 search). While the results are impressive, this cost makes the method inaccessible to most of the research community. It would strengthen the paper to include a more direct discussion of this limitation and perhaps speculate on future research directions to improve search efficiency (e.g., weight sharing, surrogate models), which would be a valuable contribution to the discussion.
*   **Clarity of RNN Cell Generation:** Section 3.4, which describes the generation of recurrent cells, is quite dense. A more detailed, step-by-step walkthrough of the example in Figure 5, perhaps in an appendix, could further improve the clarity of this complex but important part of the method.
*   **Search Space Design:** The paper is clear about what the search space is, but provides less intuition on *why* the specific choices were made (e.g., the set of filter sizes for CNNs, or the combination/activation functions for RNNs). A brief discussion on the design principles of the search space itself would be insightful. For instance, was the RNN search space explicitly designed to contain known cells like LSTM as a subspace?

### 5. Questions for the Authors

1.  The reward signal is the validation accuracy after training a child network for a fixed, relatively small number of epochs (50 for CIFAR-10). This serves as a proxy for the final converged performance. Have you investigated the correlation between this proxy reward and the performance of a fully-trained model? Is there a risk that the controller might be biased towards architectures that learn quickly in early epochs, rather than those with the highest final potential?
2.  In the PTB experiments, the discovered cell is shown to be superior to LSTM. Could you comment on the stability of the search process? If you run the entire search process multiple times with different random seeds, does it tend to converge to similar architectural motifs, or are the discovered top-performing cells qualitatively different from each other?
3.  For the CNN search on CIFAR-10, you note that the DenseNet model which achieves 3.46% error uses 1x1 convolutions, which were not included in your search space. Have you considered the effect of including such operations? How sensitive is the final performance of NAS to the design and richness of the search space provided to the controller?
