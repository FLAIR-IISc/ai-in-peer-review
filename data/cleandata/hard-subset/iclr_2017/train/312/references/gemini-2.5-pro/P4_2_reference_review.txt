### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel method for automatically discovering neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (RNN), termed the "controller," to generate a variable-length string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. This child network is then trained on a target task, and its performance on a validation set is used as a reward signal. The controller's parameters are updated using a policy gradient method (REINFORCE) to maximize this expected reward, effectively learning to propose better architectures over time.

The paper demonstrates the versatility of this approach by applying it to two distinct and challenging problems:
1.  **Convolutional Architecture Search for Image Classification:** On the CIFAR-10 dataset, the method discovers a novel convolutional network that achieves a test error of 3.65%, which is competitive with or superior to the best human-invented architectures at the time, while also being computationally faster than a comparable state-of-the-art model. The method is shown to be capable of generating complex structures, including skip connections inspired by residual networks.
2.  **Recurrent Cell Search for Language Modeling:** On the Penn Treebank dataset, NAS is used to design a novel RNN cell from scratch. The discovered cell achieves a test set perplexity of 62.4, a significant improvement over existing LSTM-based architectures. The paper further validates the generality of this new cell by showing strong performance on a character-level language modeling task and by improving the performance of the Google Neural Machine Translation (GNMT) system when used as a drop-in replacement for the LSTM cell.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **High Novelty and Impact:** The paper presents a new and influential paradigm for automating architecture design by framing it as a learning problem solvable with reinforcement learning. This approach is highly flexible, capable of generating variable-length and complex architectures, representing a significant conceptual advance over previous hyperparameter optimization and neuro-evolution methods.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Support:** The claims are substantiated with state-of-the-art results on two highly competitive benchmarks (CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank). The inclusion of a direct comparison against random search, a strong baseline, effectively demonstrates the value of the learning-based search strategy. The transfer learning experiments further strengthen the claim that the discovered architectures are not just overfit to a specific task but are genuinely robust.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The core methodology is explained logically, starting with the basic controller-child network setup and progressively adding complexity with skip connections and the design of recurrent cells. The figures, particularly those illustrating the generation of skip connections (Fig. 4) and the RNN cell (Fig. 5), are effective aids for understanding the mechanics of the proposed method.

**Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is technically sound. It correctly identifies architecture search as a problem where the objective (validation accuracy) is non-differentiable with respect to the architectural choices, and it appropriately applies a policy gradient algorithm (REINFORCE) to navigate the discrete search space. The authors address the high variance inherent in this method by incorporating a baseline, a standard technique in policy gradient implementations. The engineering effort to make this computationally demanding process feasible is a critical and well-executed component of the work. The use of a distributed, asynchronous training scheme with a parameter server is a sound approach to scaling the experiments to the necessary level.

**Novelty and Significance:** The primary contribution is the conceptual framing of architecture design as a problem that a neural network can learn to solve. While related to hyperparameter optimization and neuro-evolution, NAS is more general and scalable. Unlike many Bayesian optimization methods that operate on fixed-length parameter vectors, the RNN controller naturally handles variable-length outputs needed to define complex, deep architectures. The successful application to two fundamentally different domains—convolutional vision models and recurrent language models—demonstrates the generality of the framework. The results themselves are significant, as they produce novel architectures that advance the state-of-the-art on established benchmarks, proving that an automated method can compete with, and even surpass, years of expert-driven human design.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Computational Cost:** The experimental setup requires a very large computational budget (e.g., 800 GPUs for the CIFAR-10 search). While the paper is transparent about the setup, it would benefit from a brief discussion of the computational cost and its implications for the method's accessibility. Acknowledging this limitation and suggesting potential avenues for improving sample efficiency would make the work more complete.
*   **Reward Function Formulation:** The reward functions—specifically, cubing the validation accuracy for CIFAR-10 and using `c(validation perplexity)^2` for Penn Treebank—seem somewhat arbitrary. A short justification for these specific transformations would be helpful. For example, do they help sharpen the reward signal and guide the controller more effectively than a linear reward?
*   **Search Space Definition:** The design of the search space itself (e.g., the range of filter sizes, the choice of activation and combination functions) is a form of manual feature engineering. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the final results to the definition of this search space could provide valuable context.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   Could you elaborate on the motivation for the specific reward function transformations (cubing accuracy, squaring perplexity)? Were other reward formulations explored, and how sensitive were the search results to this choice?
*   Figure 6 shows that the policy gradient approach surpasses the best random search result after exploring approximately 6,000 architectures. Given that the final models were selected after searching 12,800 architectures, have you compared this to a random search baseline given the same total number of evaluations? This would help to more precisely quantify the sample efficiency gain from using reinforcement learning.
*   In the control experiment where `max` and `sin` were added to the RNN cell search space, did the final best-performing cell discovered in that experiment actually utilize these new functions, or did the controller learn to avoid them in favor of the more traditional operations? This would provide interesting insight into the controller's ability to discern useful primitives.
