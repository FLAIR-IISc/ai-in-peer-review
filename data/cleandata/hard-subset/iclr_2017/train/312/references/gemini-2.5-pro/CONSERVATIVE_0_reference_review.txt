### **Review of "Neural Architecture Search"**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

The paper proposes a novel method, Neural Architecture Search (NAS), for automatically designing neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a controller, implemented as a recurrent neural network, to generate a variable-length string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. This child network is then trained, and its performance on a validation set is used as a reward signal to update the controller's parameters via reinforcement learning (specifically, the REINFORCE algorithm). The authors demonstrate this method on two distinct and challenging tasks: designing a convolutional network for CIFAR-10 image classification and designing a recurrent cell for Penn Treebank language modeling. The empirical results are strong, with the discovered architectures achieving state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art performance on these standard benchmarks.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Prohibitive Computational Cost and Lack of Efficiency Analysis:** The proposed method, while intriguing, relies on a computational budget that is orders of magnitude larger than what is accessible to the vast majority of the research community. The experimental setup requires training thousands of child networks (12,800 for CIFAR-10) in parallel on an extensive hardware array (800 GPUs). This raises serious concerns about the practicality and scientific utility of the approach. The reported performance gains, while notable, do not seem to justify the astronomical increase in computational cost compared to established, human-designed architectures.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison with Relevant Baselines:** The paper compares its discovered architectures against other published state-of-the-art models. However, it fails to provide a rigorous comparison of the *search method itself* against established, computationally cheaper search strategies. The primary baseline provided is random search, which, while important, is often considered a lower bound for intelligent search. A compelling case for this complex RL-based approach would require a direct comparison against more sophisticated, yet standard, hyperparameter optimization techniques like Bayesian optimization or modern evolutionary algorithms, constrained to a comparable (and ideally, more reasonable) computational budget.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The central premise of using RL to navigate the space of network architectures is ambitious. However, the methodology as presented is more of an engineering feat of scale than a demonstration of a scientifically sound and efficient search procedure.

The computational requirements are staggering. The CIFAR-10 experiments involved 800 GPUs running concurrently to train child networks, and the PTB experiments used 400 CPUs. The paper states that 12,800 architectures were evaluated for the CIFAR-10 task. Without a clear analysis of the search efficiency—i.e., how quickly the policy improves compared to simpler methods—it is difficult to assess whether the RL controller is learning a genuinely effective search strategy or if the success is simply a product of a brute-force evaluation of an enormous number of candidates. The method's value is diminished if its success is inextricably tied to a level of computational power that is unavailable outside a few large industrial labs.

Furthermore, the comparison with baselines is incomplete. In Section 2, the authors dismiss Bayesian optimization and neuro-evolution as being "less general" or "slow" without providing direct empirical evidence within the context of their defined search space. To be convincing, the paper should have included experiments that run, for instance, a state-of-the-art evolutionary algorithm or a Bayesian optimization framework on the exact same search space, under a similar (or even a fractional) computational budget. The comparison against random search (Figure 6) is a good start, but it is not sufficient. It demonstrates that policy gradients are better than sampling uniformly, but it does not demonstrate that this specific, complex RL framework is superior to other well-established, non-gradient-based search methods.

Finally, the reported gains on CIFAR-10, while commendable, are incremental. The discovered model achieves a 3.65% error rate, which is an improvement over some prior work but does not substantially outperform the 3.46% achieved by DenseNet, an established architecture based on clear design principles. The claim that this is not an exact comparison because of the use of 1x1 convolutions in DenseNet is noted, but this highlights how specific design choices, informed by human expertise, remain critical. The gains on Penn Treebank are more significant, but the question of computational cost-benefit remains.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   **Sensitivity to Search Space:** The paper's contribution seems highly dependent on the careful design of the search space itself. For example, the final CIFAR-10 result required modifying the search to predict "a fully connected block of 3 layers." This suggests a significant amount of meta-design and human intuition is still required, which somewhat undermines the claim of fully automatic design. A more thorough discussion of how the search space was defined and its impact on the final results would strengthen the paper.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The methodology has many moving parts: the controller architecture (2-layer LSTM), the use of a baseline in REINFORCE, the reward shaping (cubing the accuracy), and the parallelization scheme. The paper would benefit from ablation studies that investigate the importance of these components. For instance, how does performance change with a simpler reward function or a different controller architecture?
*   **Reproducibility:** While the authors promise to release code and the final RNN cell, the core results of the paper are fundamentally irreproducible for most researchers due to the hardware requirements. It would be valuable to include experiments on a smaller scale (e.g., a smaller search space or dataset) that could be reproduced with more modest resources, even if the results are not state-of-the-art.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you provide an estimate of the total computation (e.g., in GPU-days) required for a single NAS run on CIFAR-10? How does this compare to the computation needed to develop and train an established architecture like DenseNet from scratch?
2.  Have you considered comparing your RL-based search method directly against a well-tuned evolutionary algorithm or a Bayesian optimization method (e.g., using Gaussian Processes or TPE) on your defined search space, given an identical, fixed computational budget? This seems crucial for validating the choice of such a complex search algorithm.
3.  The reward for the CIFAR-10 search is the "maximum validation accuracy of the last 5 epochs cubed." Can you justify this specific choice of reward shaping? How sensitive are the results to this formulation versus, for example, simply using the final validation accuracy?
4.  How much manual tuning was involved in designing the search space itself? The modification to predict blocks of layers for the best CIFAR-10 model suggests this is a critical, non-trivial step. Does this imply that NAS is effectively shifting the burden from architecture design to search space design?
