### Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes "Neural Architecture Search," a method for automatically discovering neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (RNN) as a controller. The controller is trained via reinforcement learning to generate a string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. The performance of the child network on a validation set is used as the reward signal to update the controller's parameters with a policy gradient algorithm (REINFORCE). The authors apply this method to two distinct tasks: discovering a convolutional architecture for CIFAR-10 image classification and a recurrent cell for Penn Treebank language modeling. The paper claims to find novel architectures that are competitive with or exceed the performance of state-of-the-art, human-designed models on these standard benchmarks.

**2. Recommendation and Key Reasons**

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the paper presents a conceptually novel and potentially impactful approach to automating architecture design, the manuscript in its current form is plagued by a substantial number of errors, inconsistencies, and methodological ambiguities. The lack of rigor in the presentation, including critical errors in core mathematical equations and a poorly structured experimental description, undermines the credibility of the impressive results. The work is promising, but it does not yet meet the high standards of clarity, correctness, and reproducibility expected for publication at ICLR.

**3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

My decision is based on three primary concerns:

**a) Critical Errors and Lack of Precision in Methodology:**
The description of the core method contains several significant errors and omissions that make it difficult to trust or reproduce.
*   **Incorrect Gradient Equations:** The equations for the policy gradient update in Section 3.2 contain a glaring typo, using `5θc` instead of the standard gradient operator `∇θc`. Furthermore, the empirical approximation is missing the `1/m` scaling factor for the Monte Carlo estimate. These are fundamental errors in the definition of the algorithm.
*   **Vague Terminology:** The paper repeatedly uses the term "convergence" to describe the state of a child network after being trained for a fixed number of epochs (e.g., 50 epochs for CIFAR-10). This is a misuse of the term; the networks are not trained to convergence, but for a fixed schedule. This distinction is critical as the stability and validity of the reward signal depend heavily on the reliability of the performance estimate.
*   **Unjustified Reward Shaping:** The reward functions are arbitrarily transformed: cubing the validation accuracy for CIFAR-10 and squaring the perplexity for Penn Treebank. No justification is provided for these specific non-linear transformations. Such reward shaping can drastically alter the optimization landscape and may heavily favor architectures that are only marginally better, without any analysis of this effect.
*   **Ambiguous Notation:** The notation for mathematical operations is inconsistent and ambiguous. For instance, `Wprev ∗ hj` (Section 3.3) and `(W3 ∗ xt) (W4 ∗ ht−1)` (Section 3.4) are not clearly defined as matrix-vector products, element-wise products, or other operations.

**b) Deficiencies in Experimental Reporting:**
The experimental setup is not described with sufficient clarity, and key details are either omitted or buried in the results section, confounding the interpretation of the results.
*   **Conflation of Architecture Search and Hyperparameter Tuning:** For both CIFAR-10 and PTB, the final reported performance is obtained after taking the best architecture found by NAS and performing a separate, final grid search over hyperparameters like learning rate and weight decay. This makes it impossible to disentangle the contribution of the discovered architecture from the contribution of the subsequent tuning. A fair comparison requires baselines to have been subjected to an equally rigorous tuning protocol.
*   **Hidden Search Space Constraints:** A major constraint on the CIFAR-10 search—that the controller predicts "a fully connected block of 3 layers"—is mentioned for the first time deep within the results section (4.1). This is a critical detail about the search space that belongs in the Methods section and significantly changes the interpretation of what is being searched for.
*   **Incomplete Reporting:** The paper states that CIFAR-10 child model training settings are identical to those in Huang et al. (2016a) without restating the key parameters. For reproducibility, such crucial details should be included, at least in an appendix.

**c) Numerous Formatting and Typographical Errors:**
The manuscript is rife with minor errors that, taken together, suggest a lack of care and professionalism in its preparation.
*   **Reference Formatting:** The bibliography is unusable, with numerous BibTeX formatting errors (`et al\.,? \Q2016\E,`) throughout. Citations for Huang et al. (2016) are duplicated.
*   **Typos:** The text contains many grammatical errors and typos, such as "one a time" (Sec. 2), "on the on the" (Sec. 3.2), "is has" (Sec. 3.4), a missing value in a list of filters `[24, 36, 48, ]` (Sec. 4.1), and confusing "highest" with "lowest" perplexity (Sec. 4.2). These errors disrupt readability and further reduce confidence in the work.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   I strongly urge the authors to perform a thorough proofreading of the entire manuscript, correcting all mathematical, grammatical, and formatting errors. The reference list must be completely rebuilt.
*   Provide clear and unambiguous mathematical definitions for all parts of your method. Define the dimensions of all matrices and vectors, and use standard, consistent notation for all operations.
*   Justify all non-standard methodological choices. Explain precisely why you chose to cube/square the reward signals and analyze the sensitivity of the search to these transformations.
*   Restructure the paper to present all details of the search space and experimental setup in the Methods section. The current organization is confusing.
*   Acknowledge the immense computational cost of this method (e.g., 800 GPUs) in a "Limitations" or "Discussion" section. While not a disqualifying factor, it is an important aspect of the work that deserves comment.
*   Moderate the claims in the introduction and conclusion. The work is strong enough not to require overstatements like "an achievement considered not possible with other methods."

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you clarify the exact structure of the "fully connected block of 3 layers" used in the final CIFAR-10 experiments? What was the motivation for this structural constraint versus a layer-by-layer prediction?
2.  How did you ensure that the baseline models to which you compare (e.g., DenseNet) were tuned with a comparable amount of effort to the final grid search you applied to your discovered architecture?
3.  Have you performed an ablation study on the effect of the reward shaping? How does the performance of the search change if you use the raw, untransformed validation accuracy/perplexity as the reward?
