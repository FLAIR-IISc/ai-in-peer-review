### **Review of "Neural Architecture Search"**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel and ambitious approach to automating the design of neural network architectures, a task that has historically relied on significant human expertise and intuition. The core idea, which the authors term "Neural Architecture Search" (NAS), is to frame architecture generation as a learning problem. They propose using a controller, implemented as a recurrent neural network, to generate a variable-length string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. This child network is then trained on a target task, and its performance on a validation set is used as a reward signal to update the controller's parameters via reinforcement learning (specifically, the REINFORCE algorithm). This process creates a feedback loop where the controller learns to generate progressively better architectures over time. The authors demonstrate the power of this approach in two highly competitive domains: designing a convolutional network for CIFAR-10 and a recurrent cell for language modeling on the Penn Treebank. In both cases, the automatically discovered architectures are shown to be competitive with, or even superior to, state-of-the-art models designed by human experts.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This paper is a clear accept. My decision is based on two key factors:

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Boldness of the Core Idea:** The work represents a significant conceptual leap in how we approach neural network design. It shifts the paradigm from manual architecture engineering to a principled, meta-learning framework. This is a foundational idea with the potential to open up an entirely new subfield of research.
2.  **Strong Empirical Proof of Concept:** Despite the inherent difficulty of the problem, the authors provide compelling evidence that their method works. Discovering a novel CNN that is competitive with top human-designed models on CIFAR-10 and, even more impressively, a new recurrent cell that outperforms the highly-optimized LSTM on Penn Treebank, demonstrates that the approach is not just a theoretical curiosity but a powerful and effective tool.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

This paper is precisely the kind of work that ICLR should champion. It is bold, creative, and tackles a fundamental problem in deep learning. While previous work on hyperparameter optimization and neuro-evolution exists, this method provides a much more flexible and general framework for generating complex, variable-length architectural descriptions. The use of an RNN controller trained with policy gradients to navigate this vast, non-differentiable search space is an elegant and powerful formulation.

The empirical results are remarkable. Achieving SOTA-level performance on CIFAR-10 is a strong result, but the discovery of a new recurrent cell that surpasses the LSTM is truly significant. The LSTM has been a cornerstone of sequence modeling for two decades, and the community has spent countless hours optimizing it. The fact that an automated search procedure can discover a superior architecture is a powerful testament to the method's potential. The successful transfer of this cell to other tasks (character language modeling and machine translation) further strengthens the claim that the discovered architecture possesses generalizable and useful properties.

I am willing to overlook the potential weaknesses of the paper because the core contribution is so strong. The most glaring issue is the astronomical computational cost (e.g., 800 GPUs running in parallel for the CIFAR-10 search). This currently places the method out of reach for most of the research community. However, this should be viewed as a successful, albeit expensive, proof-of-concept. The first paper to demonstrate a fundamentally new capability is not required to also make it cheap and efficient; that can and will be the subject of extensive follow-up work. Similarly, the REINFORCE algorithm is known for its high variance, and the training process may be unstable, but the fact that the authors made it work at all to produce these results is the key achievement. The focus here should be on the new door this paper opens, not on the rough edges of the initial implementation.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Computational Cost:** It would be beneficial to be more upfront about the computational requirements in the main body of the paper (e.g., total GPU-days). While this doesn't diminish the novelty, it provides important context for the community and can motivate future research into more efficient NAS methods (e.g., parameter sharing, surrogate models).
*   **Analysis of Discovered Architectures:** The paper shows the discovered architectures, which is great. It would be even more insightful to include a brief analysis of *why* these architectures might be effective. For example, in the discovered CNN, are there recurring motifs or patterns that differ from human-designed networks? For the RNN cell, what computational paths seem most critical? This could provide valuable insights for human architects as well.
*   **Search Space Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the final results to the initial design of the search space? For example, what if different filter sizes or a wider range of activation functions were provided as primitives? A brief discussion on this could help clarify the boundaries of the method's autonomy.

**5. Questions for Authors**

*   Could you provide more detail on the training dynamics of the controller? How does the entropy of its policy evolve over time? Does it quickly converge to a small region of the architecture space, or does it continue to explore for a long time?
*   You compare against random search and show policy gradient is superior. Did you consider more sophisticated black-box optimization techniques like Bayesian Optimization or evolutionary algorithms as baselines, and if so, how do you see NAS comparing to them in terms of sample efficiency and the complexity of architectures they can discover?
*   Given the high variance of the REINFORCE algorithm, how crucial was the baseline function (the exponential moving average of previous rewards) to making the training process stable and successful? Did you experiment with other variance reduction techniques?
