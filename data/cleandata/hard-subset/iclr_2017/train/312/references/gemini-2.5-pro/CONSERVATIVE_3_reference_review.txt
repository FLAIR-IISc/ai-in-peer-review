### Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

**Summary of Contributions:**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel method for automatically discovering neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (RNN), termed the "controller," to generate a variable-length string that specifies the architecture of a "child network." This controller is trained using a policy gradient reinforcement learning algorithm (REINFORCE), where the reward signal is the performance (e.g., validation accuracy) of the trained child network. The authors demonstrate this approach on two benchmark tasks: designing a convolutional neural network for image classification on CIFAR-10 and designing a recurrent cell for language modeling on Penn Treebank. The paper claims that the discovered architectures achieve performance that is competitive with, or superior to, state-of-the-art models designed by human experts.

**Decision and Key Reasons:**

Decision: **Reject**

While the ambition of automating architecture design is commendable and the results presented appear strong, I have significant reservations about the soundness and practicality of the proposed method. My recommendation for rejection is based on two primary concerns:

1.  **Prohibitive Computational Requirements:** The method, as presented, relies on a massive scale of computation (e.g., 800 GPUs concurrently for the CIFAR-10 experiments) that is far beyond the reach of the vast majority of the academic research community. This makes the results difficult to reproduce, verify, or build upon, and it calls into question whether the method's success is due to an efficient search strategy or simply an exhaustive, brute-force evaluation enabled by extraordinary resources.
2.  **Insufficient Comparison to Established Baselines:** The paper positions itself as a superior alternative to existing hyperparameter optimization methods but fails to provide a rigorous comparison. The only alternative search strategy evaluated is a simple random search. Given the complexity of the proposed RL framework, a much more thorough comparison against established, non-gradient-based optimization techniques like Bayesian Optimization or evolutionary algorithms is required to justify its value.

**Supporting Arguments for Decision:**

The central claim is that a gradient-based method can intelligently search the space of architectures. However, the evidence provided is not sufficient to disentangle the contribution of the learning algorithm from the sheer scale of the search. The CIFAR-10 experiments involved training 12,800 child networks. With such a large number of samples, it is not clear that the sophisticated REINFORCE-based controller offers a significant advantage over a well-tuned random search or other simpler, established search methods. The comparison in Figure 6 shows policy gradient outperforming random search over time, but the details of the random search baseline are sparse. A strong baseline is often difficult to beat, and the provided comparison is not comprehensive enough to be conclusive.

Furthermore, the methodology introduces substantial complexity. The system involves an RNN controller, a policy gradient algorithm known for high variance, asynchronous distributed training, and a custom attention mechanism for skip connections. Yet, the paper lacks the necessary ablation studies to justify these components. For example, how does the performance degrade if a simpler controller is used? How critical is the specific baseline function (exponential moving average of rewards) for stabilizing training? The burden of proof for a complex new method is high, and this paper does not fully meet it. Instead, it relies on the final performance numbers, which may be confounded by the massive compute budget and manual interventions.

For instance, the best-reported result on CIFAR-10 (3.65% error) was achieved not directly by the search, but by taking a discovered architecture and manually adding 40 filters to each layer. This post-hoc manual tuning undermines the claim of a fully automated discovery process and suggests the method may be finding a reasonable template that still requires expert intervention for optimal performance.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

1.  **Transparency on Computational Cost:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by a clear and upfront discussion of the total computational cost (e.g., in GPU-days) required to run a full search. This context is essential for other researchers to assess the practicality and efficiency of the method.
2.  **Rigor in Baselines:** To validate the necessity of the RL controller, a direct comparison with more sophisticated, established black-box optimization methods is needed. Techniques like Bayesian Optimization (using Gaussian Processes or TPE) or simple evolution strategies have proven effective for hyperparameter tuning and could be adapted to this problem. A comparison on a smaller, more constrained search space where these methods are viable would provide more convincing evidence of the proposed method's superiority.
3.  **Reward Function Justification:** The choice to use the cube of the validation accuracy as the reward signal is unconventional. The paper should provide a justification for this choice. How sensitive is the training of the controller to this form of reward shaping? An analysis of alternatives would improve the methodological rigor.
4.  **Analysis of Discovered Architectures:** The paper provides visualizations of the discovered cells but offers limited insight into *why* they perform well. Are there recurring motifs or design principles that the controller learns? A deeper analysis could provide valuable knowledge for the field, independent of the search method itself.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a direct comparison of search efficiency (e.g., best architecture found after N evaluations) between your method and a well-implemented Bayesian optimization baseline on a constrained version of your search space?
*   What is the rationale behind training each child network for a relatively small number of epochs (50 for CIFAR-10)? Does this introduce significant noise into the reward signal, and could this potentially favor architectures that learn quickly over those that achieve better final performance?
*   How much does the performance of the discovered architectures depend on the manual grid search for hyperparameters like learning rate and weight decay performed *after* the main search is complete? Does this suggest that the controller should also be tasked with finding these parameters?
