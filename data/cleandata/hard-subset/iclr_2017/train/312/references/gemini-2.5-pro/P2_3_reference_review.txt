### **Summary of Contributions**
This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (RNN) as a "controller." The controller is trained with reinforcement learning (RL) to generate a string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. The child network is then trained on a target task, and its performance on a validation set serves as the reward signal to update the controller via policy gradients (REINFORCE). The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on two challenging and distinct tasks: image classification on CIFAR-10 and language modeling on Penn Treebank (PTB). For CIFAR-10, NAS discovers a novel convolutional architecture that achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) or near-SOTA performance (3.65% test error) while being faster than a comparable human-designed model. For PTB, NAS designs a novel recurrent cell that outperforms SOTA LSTMs, achieving a test perplexity of 62.4 (a 3.6 point improvement). The generalizability of this discovered cell is further demonstrated through strong performance on character-level language modeling and machine translation tasks.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**
**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a groundbreaking and highly significant new paradigm for architecture engineering. Framing architecture design as a learnable, generative process driven by a controller RNN and optimized with RL is a major conceptual contribution that opens up a new and promising research direction. It effectively moves beyond fixed-search-space hyperparameter optimization and provides a more flexible and general framework than prior neuro-evolution methods.
2.  **Exceptional Empirical Results:** The claims are substantiated with extremely strong and convincing results on highly competitive benchmarks. Achieving SOTA performance on both CIFAR-10 and PTB with automatically generated architectures is a remarkable achievement. The subsequent transfer learning experiments for the discovered RNN cell provide powerful evidence that the method finds genuinely novel and useful architectural motifs, not just solutions overfit to a single task.

### **Supporting Arguments**
The core strength of this paper lies in its elegant formulation of a notoriously difficult problem and its rigorous empirical validation.

**Methodology:** The proposed method is well-motivated and technically sound. Using an RNN controller to generate a variable-length sequence of architectural choices is a natural fit for the problem. The application of the REINFORCE algorithm to optimize the controller based on the non-differentiable reward of validation accuracy is appropriate. The authors also address key practical challenges effectively:
*   **Scalability:** The use of parallel, asynchronous training is critical for making this computationally intensive search feasible and is a well-reasoned engineering choice.
*   **Search Space Complexity:** The extension to include skip connections via an attention-like mechanism is a clever way to dramatically increase the expressiveness of the search space to encompass modern, complex designs like ResNets.
*   **Generalization:** The adaptation of the method from designing convolutional stacks to designing the internal structure of recurrent cells is a powerful demonstration of the framework's flexibility.

**Experiments:** The experimental design is thorough and compelling.
*   **CIFAR-10 Results:** The paper demonstrates that NAS can discover a competitive CNN from scratch. The analyses are insightful, noting that the discovered architecture features many one-step skip connections and that its performance degrades if these connections are removed, confirming that the search found a meaningful structure. The comparison with human-invented architectures clearly shows the power of the approach.
*   **Penn Treebank Results:** The results on language modeling are arguably even more impressive. Improving upon the highly optimized LSTM cell is a significant challenge, and the discovered "NASCell" not only achieves a large improvement in perplexity but is also computationally more efficient than prior SOTA models. The transfer learning experiments—showing the cell works well for character modeling and improves BLEU scores in the GNMT framework—are a crucial piece of evidence for the cell's general utility.
*   **Baselines and Controls:** The comparison against random search is a critical control experiment. The results in Figure 6 clearly show that the policy gradient approach learns to guide the search toward better regions of the architecture space, outperforming the strong random search baseline. This validates that the "learning" component of the framework is essential to its success.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**
While this is an excellent paper, the following points could further strengthen it.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1.  **Computational Cost:** The primary weakness of the proposed method is its immense computational expense (e.g., training 800 networks concurrently on 800 GPUs for the CIFAR-10 experiment). This is a major barrier to reproducibility and adoption for most researchers. While the results justify the cost in this foundational work, the paper would be more complete with an explicit discussion of this limitation in the main text and a rough estimate of the total compute (e.g., in GPU-days) required.
2.  **Search Space Design:** The performance of NAS is contingent on the design of the search space (e.g., the choices of filter sizes, activation functions, and the base-8 tree structure for RNN cells). While the presented search spaces are reasonable, a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the results to these design choices would be beneficial. How much expert knowledge is still required to define a "good" search space?
3.  **Clarity on Reward Function:** For the CIFAR-10 experiments, the reward is stated as the "maximum validation accuracy of the last 5 epochs cubed." The cubic transformation is non-standard. A short justification for this choice would improve clarity—presumably, it more heavily rewards high-performing architectures and amplifies the gradient signal for good candidates.

**Questions for the Authors:**
1.  Could you provide more detail on the stability of the controller's training? Given the high variance often associated with policy gradient methods, did you observe stable convergence, or did different runs of the controller training yield significantly different qualities of final architectures?
2.  Regarding the discovered RNN cell: The paper mentions it has similarities to the LSTM cell. Could you elaborate on the key structural differences that you believe lead to its superior performance?
3.  Have you explored techniques to make the search more efficient, for example, by using performance predictors, early stopping for poorly performing child networks, or weight sharing between child models to reduce the training time for each architecture?
