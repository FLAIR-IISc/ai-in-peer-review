### Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel method for automatically designing neural network architectures. The core contribution is to frame architecture design as a reinforcement learning problem. A recurrent neural network, termed the "controller," is trained to generate a variable-length string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. This child network is then trained on a target task (e.g., image classification), and its final validation accuracy is used as a reward signal. This reward is used to update the controller's parameters via a policy gradient algorithm (REINFORCE), encouraging it to generate better-performing architectures over time.

The method is demonstrated on two highly competitive benchmarks: CIFAR-10 image classification and Penn Treebank (PTB) language modeling. For CIFAR-10, the method discovers a novel convolutional architecture that achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance (3.65% error) compared to human-designed models. For PTB, it designs a novel recurrent cell that surpasses the performance of heavily-tuned LSTM cells, achieving a new state-of-the-art perplexity of 62.4. The paper further shows that this discovered cell generalizes well, achieving SOTA results on a character language modeling task and improving performance when integrated into the GNMT machine translation framework.

**2. Decision: Accept**

This is a clear accept. The paper presents a groundbreaking and highly ambitious approach to a fundamental problem in deep learning: architecture engineering. The key reasons for this decision are:

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Significance:** The work establishes a new paradigm for architecture search. By casting the problem as a learning task for a meta-model (the controller), it moves beyond previous hyperparameter optimization techniques (which struggle with variable-length, conditional search spaces) and makes neuro-evolutionary methods practical at the scale of modern deep learning. This meta-learning approach is a significant conceptual leap.
2.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The claims are substantiated by extremely strong, state-of-the-art results on two very different and challenging domains. Successfully designing both a top-tier ConvNet and a novel, superior RNN cell with the same general framework is a powerful demonstration of the method's potential and generality.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this paper is its elegant formulation of a notoriously difficult, combinatorial search problem into a tractable, gradient-based optimization problem. The use of an RNN controller is a natural fit for generating sequential, variable-length architectural descriptions. The application of policy gradients to optimize for a non-differentiable metric (validation accuracy) is well-motivated and draws clever parallels to work in sequence generation and machine translation.

The technical contributions to make this core idea work are also significant. The method for incorporating skip connections using an attention-like mechanism is clever, allowing the search space to include modern, complex topologies like those found in ResNets. Similarly, the tree-based representation for generating recurrent cells is a well-designed abstraction that enables a rich search space while maintaining structure.

The empirical validation is thorough and convincing. The comparison against random search (Figure 6) is crucial and clearly demonstrates that the policy gradient approach is learning effectively and not just benefiting from a massive search. The ablation-like experiments on the discovered CIFAR-10 model (e.g., removing skip connections) validate that the learned architectural motifs are indeed meaningful. Furthermore, the transfer learning experiments for the discovered RNN cell provide strong evidence that the method is not just overfitting to a specific task but is finding genuinely robust and effective computational structures.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the work is outstanding, there are several points that could be clarified or addressed to further strengthen the paper.

*   **Computational Cost:** The most significant weakness is the immense computational requirement. The CIFAR-10 experiments used 800 GPUs concurrently to train 12,800 child networks. This is an extraordinary amount of compute, placing the method far out of reach for most academic labs and many industrial ones. The paper should be more upfront about this limitation in the main body and discuss its implications. Is this cost a fundamental aspect of the approach, or are there clear avenues for improving its efficiency?
*   **Search Space Design:** The performance of NAS is heavily dependent on the design of the search space. For instance, the best CIFAR-10 result was obtained after modifying the search space to predict blocks of layers and adding pooling. This suggests that considerable human expertise is still required to define a "good" search space for the controller to explore. A discussion on the sensitivity of the results to the search space definition would be very insightful.
*   **Reward Signal and Training Stability:** The reward is very sparse and delayed, as it requires training a child network for many epochs. The paper mentions using the "maximum validation accuracy of the last 5 epochs cubed" for CIFAR-10 and `c/(validation perplexity)^2` for PTB. The motivation for these specific transformations (cubing, squaring) is not explained. Given that REINFORCE is known to have high variance, more details on the stability of the controller's training and the effect of the baseline `b` would be beneficial.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the decision to modify the CIFAR-10 search space for the final, best-performing model (predicting blocks of 3 layers and adding pooling)? Was this change motivated by preliminary results, or was it a pre-specified search space? This seems crucial, as it implies that the design of the search space itself is a critical factor for success.
2.  The REINFORCE algorithm is notoriously sample-inefficient and can suffer from high variance. Did you experiment with other policy gradient algorithms, such as A2C/A3C or TRPO, which might offer better stability or sample efficiency?
3.  Regarding the computational cost, have you considered methods to accelerate the evaluation of a child architecture? For instance, could a reliable performance estimate be obtained from training a child network for only a few epochs, or by using proxy tasks? This would be a critical step toward making this powerful technique more widely accessible.
