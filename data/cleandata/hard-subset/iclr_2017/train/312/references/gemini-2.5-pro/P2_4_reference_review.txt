### **Review of "Neural Architecture Search"**

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel method for automatically designing neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (RNN), termed the "controller," to generate a variable-length string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. This child network is then trained on a target task, and its performance on a validation set is used as a reward signal. The controller's parameters are updated using a policy gradient algorithm (REINFORCE) to maximize this reward, effectively learning a policy that generates high-performing architectures. The authors demonstrate this method on two highly competitive tasks: image classification on CIFAR-10 and language modeling on Penn Treebank (PTB). For CIFAR-10, their method discovers a convolutional architecture that achieves a test error of 3.65%, outperforming the state-of-the-art human-designed models at the time. For PTB, it designs a novel recurrent cell that achieves a test perplexity of 62.4, a significant improvement of 3.6 over the previous best result. The paper further shows that this discovered cell generalizes well to other tasks, including character-level language modeling and machine translation.

#### **2. Recommendation and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This paper represents a significant and highly impactful contribution to the field of machine learning. My recommendation for acceptance is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Method:** The paper proposes a fundamentally new and powerful paradigm for architecture design. By framing the problem as a reinforcement learning task where an agent (the controller) learns to generate architectures, it moves beyond traditional hyperparameter optimization techniques and manual architecture engineering. This is a foundational work that opens up an entirely new subfield of research.
2.  **Exceptional Empirical Results:** The claims are supported by state-of-the-art results on two very different and challenging benchmarks. Discovering architectures from scratch that outperform meticulously hand-tuned models like DenseNet and LSTM is a remarkable achievement and provides compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's approach is well-motivated and effectively situated within the literature. It correctly identifies the limitations of prior work in hyperparameter optimization (fixed-length search spaces) and neuro-evolution (scalability issues) and presents a more general and scalable solution.

The methodology is described with sufficient detail. The use of an RNN controller to autoregressively generate architecture tokens is an elegant solution to searching a complex, variable-length space. The application of the REINFORCE algorithm is appropriate for optimizing the non-differentiable reward (validation accuracy). The engineering solutions to make this computationally intensive process feasible—namely, large-scale distributed training with asynchronous updates—are crucial to the method's success and are clearly explained. The extensions to include modern architectural motifs like skip connections (via a clever attention-like mechanism) and to design complex recurrent cells demonstrate the flexibility and power of the framework.

The empirical validation is exceptionally strong and rigorous.
*   **CIFAR-10 Results:** The discovered architecture not only achieves a lower error rate (3.65%) than the best comparable DenseNet model (3.74%) but is also reported to be faster. The ablations, such as removing skip connections, confirm that the learned structural components are essential to its performance.
*   **Penn Treebank Results:** The discovered RNN cell surpasses highly optimized LSTM variants by a large margin (3.6 perplexity). This is a significant breakthrough on a benchmark where progress has often been incremental.
*   **Generalization and Controls:** The authors perform crucial experiments to validate their findings. The successful transfer of the learned cell to character language modeling and machine translation suggests the discovered architecture has fundamental advantages not just overfit to PTB. Furthermore, the direct comparison against random search (Figure 6) clearly shows that the policy gradient approach is learning a meaningful search strategy and is not simply a product of extensive computation.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is an excellent paper, the following points could be addressed to further improve its clarity and impact. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from my decision to accept.

*   **Computational Cost:** The primary limitation of this work is its immense computational requirement (e.g., training 800 networks concurrently on 800 GPUs for the CIFAR-10 search). This makes the method inaccessible to most of the research community. While the results are a powerful proof-of-concept, the paper would benefit from a more direct discussion of this limitation and perhaps a brief commentary on potential future work for improving search efficiency.
*   **Search Space Design:** The quality of the final architecture is highly dependent on the design of the search space (e.g., the set of possible filter sizes, layer types, or cell operations). The paper could provide more insight into the process of designing these search spaces. How much expert knowledge is still required at this stage? A discussion on the sensitivity of the results to the search space definition would be valuable.
*   **Reward Function Heuristics:** The reward functions used are somewhat heuristic (e.g., "maximum validation accuracy of the last 5 epochs cubed" for CIFAR-10, and `c(validation perplexity)^2` for PTB). A brief justification for these specific choices (e.g., why cubing the accuracy?) would strengthen the paper's methodological description. Did the authors explore other reward formulations?

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**

To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  Regarding the computational cost: Given the resources required, do you have any insights on how this method's efficiency could be improved to make it more practical for researchers with smaller computational budgets? For instance, could techniques like weight-sharing or performance prediction shorten the child network training loop?
2.  In Section 3.4, you describe the generation of recurrent cells as labeling nodes in a tree. How is the structure of this tree (e.g., the number of leaf nodes and their connectivity) determined? Is this structure fixed, or is it also part of the search space?
3.  The use of a baseline `b` (an exponential moving average of previous rewards) is critical for reducing the variance of the policy gradient. Could you comment on how sensitive the controller's training convergence is to the specific implementation of this baseline?
