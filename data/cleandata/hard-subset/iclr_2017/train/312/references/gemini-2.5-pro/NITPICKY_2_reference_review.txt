### Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces "Neural Architecture Search" (NAS), a novel method for automatically designing neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network, termed the "controller," to generate a variable-length string that specifies the architecture of a "child network." This controller is trained using a policy gradient approach, where the validation accuracy of the trained child network serves as the reward signal. The authors apply this method to two distinct and challenging tasks: image classification on CIFAR-10 and language modeling on the Penn Treebank (PTB). On CIFAR-10, their method discovers a convolutional architecture that achieves competitive performance against human-designed models. On PTB, the method generates a novel recurrent cell that surpasses the performance of established LSTM cells and sets a new state-of-the-art result. The work positions itself as a significant step towards automating a critical and time-consuming aspect of deep learning research.

**2. Recommendation: Reject**

While the paper presents a conceptually novel and potentially impactful direction, the manuscript is marred by a substantial number of errors, methodological ambiguities, and formatting inconsistencies. Key mathematical formulations appear to be incorrect, critical design choices lack justification, and the overall presentation falls short of the standards expected for publication at ICLR. These issues, in aggregate, undermine the reader's confidence in the technical correctness and scientific rigor of the reported results.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

My decision is based on the following major concerns:

**A. Critical Errors and Typos in Core Methodology:**
The paper contains several significant errors that raise serious questions about its technical soundness.

*   **Incorrect Gradient Formulation:** In Section 3.2, the REINFORCE rule is presented with the gradient operator written as `5θc`. This is almost certainly a typo for the nabla operator, `∇θc`. This error appears in two separate equations that are fundamental to the proposed training algorithm. Such a mistake in a core mathematical expression is deeply concerning.
*   **Incomplete Search Space Definition:** In Section 4.1, under "Training details," the search space for the number of filters is given as "[24, 36, 48, ]." The list is incomplete, leaving the reader to guess the full range of options available to the controller. This is a crucial detail of the experimental setup that has been omitted.
*   **Typographical Errors:** The paper contains numerous smaller typos, such as "hyperparameters one a time" (should be "at a time") in Section 2 and a repeated "on the" in the description of the baseline function in Section 3.2. While minor individually, their frequency suggests a lack of careful proofreading.

**B. Insufficient Justification for Methodological Choices:**
Several key design choices are presented without adequate motivation or analysis, making it difficult to assess their impact or generality.

*   **Arbitrary Reward Shaping:** The reward functions are arbitrarily shaped. For CIFAR-10, the reward is "the maximum validation accuracy of the last 5 epochs cubed." For PTB, it is `c(validation perplexity)^2`. Why were these specific transformations (cubing and squaring) chosen? No justification is provided. This form of reward shaping can drastically alter the gradient landscape and bias the search, yet its effect is not discussed or ablated.
*   **Vague Convergence Criteria:** The paper states that "convergence of each child network is reached when its training exceeds a certain number of epochs" (e.g., 50 for CIFAR-10). This is not convergence; it is a fixed training schedule. This setup may unfairly penalize architectures that require longer training to reach their full potential, thereby biasing the search towards architectures that learn quickly. This crucial methodological detail is not adequately addressed.
*   **Unsubstantiated Claims:** The authors claim a discovered CIFAR-10 architecture "is a local optimum in the sense that if we perturb it, its performance becomes worse." This is a strong empirical claim, but no evidence is provided. What perturbations were tested? How was this experiment conducted? Without supporting data, this statement is unsubstantiated.

**C. Poor Presentation and Formatting:**
The manuscript's presentation is unprofessional and contains significant formatting issues.

*   **Misplaced Footnote:** The footnote regarding the Google Brain Residency program is awkwardly attached to the word "connectivity" in the middle of a sentence in the introduction. This should be placed in the acknowledgments or author information section.
*   **Broken References:** The bibliography is severely malformed. Nearly every entry contains erroneous formatting, such as "Andreas et al\.,? \Q2016\E, Andreas et al\.," and "Bergstra and Bengio.,? \Q2012\E, Bergstra and Bengio.,". This is not a minor formatting quibble; it renders the bibliography nearly unreadable and indicates a profound lack of care in the manuscript's preparation.

**4. Additional Feedback**

Despite my recommendation, the core idea of the paper is compelling. Should the authors choose to revise and resubmit, I would suggest the following improvements:

*   **Rigorously Proofread:** The manuscript requires a thorough proofreading to correct all mathematical, grammatical, and formatting errors. The state of the bibliography is unacceptable.
*   **Justify Design Choices:** Provide clear justifications or ablation studies for key decisions, such as the reward shaping functions and the fixed-epoch training schedule for child models. This would significantly strengthen the scientific contribution.
*   **Improve Clarity and Reproducibility:** Important experimental details, such as the full search space for CIFAR-10 and the training settings for child models (which are currently deferred to a citation), should be explicitly stated in the paper or an appendix.
*   **Substantiate Claims:** All empirical claims, such as the "local optimum" assertion, should be backed by clear experimental evidence and methodology.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please confirm the correct form of the policy gradient update equations in Section 3.2? Is `5θc` indeed a typo for `∇θc`?
2.  What was the rationale behind cubing the accuracy for the CIFAR-10 reward and squaring the perplexity for the PTB reward? Have you experimented with other reward transformations or the raw reward signal?
3.  Could you provide the complete list of choices for the "number of filters" in the CIFAR-10 search space, which appears truncated in Section 4.1?
4.  How was the claim that the discovered CNN architecture is a "local optimum" validated? What specific perturbations were applied, and what were their results?
