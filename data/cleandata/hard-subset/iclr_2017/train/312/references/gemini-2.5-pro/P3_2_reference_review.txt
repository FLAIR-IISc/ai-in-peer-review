### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel method for automatically discovering neural network architectures. The core idea is to frame architecture design as a learning problem, where a recurrent neural network (RNN), termed the "controller," generates a variable-length string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. This controller is trained using reinforcement learning (RL), specifically the REINFORCE algorithm, with the validation accuracy of the trained child network serving as the reward signal. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on two highly competitive benchmarks: image classification on CIFAR-10 and language modeling on Penn Treebank (PTB). For CIFAR-10, NAS discovers a convolutional architecture that achieves a state-of-the-art error rate of 3.65%, outperforming most human-designed models. For PTB, NAS designs a novel recurrent cell that surpasses a well-tuned LSTM, achieving a new state-of-the-art perplexity of 62.4. The paper's main contributions are: 1) A general, gradient-based method for architecture search in a variable-length search space; 2) A powerful demonstration of this method's capability to design novel, state-of-the-art architectures from scratch in both vision and language domains; and 3) The release of a new, high-performing recurrent cell (NASCell) discovered by this process.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a groundbreaking paper that presents a significant conceptual leap in the field of automated machine learning and deep learning. My recommendation for acceptance is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Method:** The proposed method of using a controller RNN trained with policy gradients to search for architectures is a fundamentally new and powerful paradigm. It moves beyond traditional hyperparameter optimization techniques by learning to compose complex, variable-length structures. This work has the potential to shift the focus of deep learning research from manual architecture engineering to the design of search spaces and learning algorithms for discovering architectures, opening up a new and exciting research direction.

2.  **Exceptional Empirical Results:** The claims are substantiated by extremely strong results on two very different and challenging tasks. Discovering a CNN for CIFAR-10 that is competitive with top-tier, manually-designed architectures like DenseNet is a remarkable achievement. Even more impressively, the discovery of a recurrent cell that outperforms the highly optimized and ubiquitous LSTM cell on PTB by a large margin (3.6 perplexity) is a major breakthrough, demonstrating the method's ability to innovate beyond established human knowledge.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper is well-structured and clearly presented. The method is motivated by the limitations of existing hyperparameter optimization and neuro-evolution approaches, placing itself effectively within the literature. The core mechanism—an RNN controller generating architectural descriptions and being updated via RL—is elegant and powerful. The extensions to include modern architectural motifs like skip connections using an attention-like mechanism are well-conceived and crucial for finding high-performing models.

The experimental validation is extensive and convincing. The comparison against random search (Figure 6) is a critical control experiment that effectively demonstrates that the policy gradient method is genuinely learning to propose better architectures over time, rather than simply being an inefficient way to sample from a vast space. The transfer learning experiments for the discovered RNN cell are particularly compelling; showing its effectiveness on character-level modeling and its plug-and-play improvement within the complex GNMT framework validates the generality and robustness of the discovered cell.

The scale of the experiments is both a strength and a weakness. The use of 800 GPUs for the CIFAR-10 search is staggering and showcases a level of engineering and computational power that is impressive. While this demonstrates what is possible at the frontier, it also highlights the method's primary drawback, which is discussed below.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is an excellent paper, the following points could be addressed to further strengthen it:

1.  **Computational Cost:** The most significant limitation of this work is its immense computational expense (e.g., training 12,800 networks on 800 GPUs for the CIFAR-10 search). The authors should be more upfront about this in the main body of the paper. A discussion of this limitation and potential avenues for future work to improve efficiency (e.g., parameter sharing between child models, learning on a proxy task, or one-shot models) would significantly enhance the paper's perspective and impact. As it stands, the method is inaccessible to the vast majority of the research community.

2.  **Search Space Design:** The success of NAS is contingent on the design of the search space. For instance, the RNN cell search space is a clever construction based on a computation tree, but it is still a human-designed abstraction. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the final results to the choice of the search space (e.g., the set of available operations, filter sizes, or the 'base number' for the RNN cell) would be very insightful.

3.  **Details on Reward Function:** The reward for CIFAR-10 is the "maximum validation accuracy of the last 5 epochs cubed." The cubing of the accuracy is a non-standard choice. A brief justification for this form of reward shaping would be helpful. Did it provide a better gradient signal or accelerate convergence of the controller?

4.  **Analysis of Discovered Architectures:** The paper provides visualizations of the discovered architectures, which is great. Further analysis would be beneficial. For example, on CIFAR-10, the authors note the preference for rectangular filters and skip connections. Are there other discernible patterns or motifs that emerge consistently during the search process? This could provide valuable insights into what constitutes a good architecture.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  How stable is the REINFORCE training process for the controller? Given the known high variance of this algorithm, did you observe significant variability across different runs of the controller training? Do different controller runs tend to converge to topologically similar and equally performant child architectures?

2.  Could you elaborate on the schedule for increasing the number of layers during the search? What was the motivation for this curriculum-based approach, and how crucial was it for finding the final deep, high-performance models?

3.  The computational cost is a major barrier. Have you explored or can you speculate on more efficient variants of this method? For example, could child models be trained for only a few epochs to get a noisy but cheap reward signal, or could weight-sharing strategies be employed across the child networks?
