### Review

**Summary of Contributions:**

This paper presents "Neural Architecture Search" (NAS), a novel and ambitious method for automatically designing neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network, termed the "controller," to generate a variable-length string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. The process is framed as a reinforcement learning problem where the controller's policy is updated via policy gradients (REINFORCE). The reward signal is the performance (e.g., validation accuracy) of the child network after it has been trained. The authors demonstrate this approach on two highly competitive benchmarks: designing a convolutional neural network for CIFAR-10 image classification and designing a recurrent cell for Penn Treebank language modeling. Remarkably, the method discovers a CNN architecture that is competitive with the best human-designed models and a novel recurrent cell that surpasses the performance of well-established LSTM cells, setting a new state-of-the-art on Penn Treebank.

**Decision and Key Reasons:**

My recommendation is a clear **Accept**.

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Ambition:** The paper introduces a fundamentally new paradigm for architecture design. The concept of using a neural network to generate the description of another network, trained with reinforcement learning, is a bold and significant conceptual leap. It moves the field from manual architecture engineering towards a more automated, learning-based approach, tackling a notoriously difficult and high-dimensional search problem. This is precisely the kind of high-risk, high-reward research that pushes the boundaries of the field.

2.  **Compelling Empirical Validation:** The claims are supported by surprisingly strong results on very challenging benchmarks. While finding a competitive model on CIFAR-10 is impressive, the discovery of a novel recurrent cell that outperforms the highly-engineered and well-understood LSTM on Penn Treebank is a standout achievement. The fact that this discovered cell also generalizes and shows improvements on other tasks (character language modeling and machine translation) provides powerful evidence that the method is not just overfitting to a single task but is discovering genuinely useful architectural motifs.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary strength of this work lies in its conceptual novelty. While hyperparameter optimization and neuro-evolution are related fields, this paper’s formulation is more general and powerful. Unlike many Bayesian optimization methods that operate on fixed-length parameter spaces, the RNN controller can naturally handle the variable-length, compositional structures that define deep neural networks. The proposed method for generating skip connections is also an elegant way to explore complex, non-sequential topologies reminiscent of modern architectures like ResNets.

The empirical results are not merely incremental. Setting a new state-of-the-art on Penn Treebank by discovering a new recurrent cell is a major contribution in its own right. The comparison against random search is a crucial and well-executed control experiment, demonstrating that the policy gradient method is genuinely learning an effective search strategy and not just benefiting from a well-designed search space.

The paper does have limitations, the most glaring of which is the immense computational cost (e.g., 800 GPUs running in parallel for the CIFAR-10 search). This currently places the method out of reach for most researchers. Furthermore, the search space, while large, is still defined by human-specified primitives (e.g., a discrete set of filter sizes and activation functions). However, given the groundbreaking nature of the core idea, these are acceptable limitations for a pioneering paper. The immense compute demonstrates a proof-of-concept at scale, paving the way for future work to drastically improve the efficiency. The methodological details, such as training each child network for a fixed number of epochs or using an ad-hoc reward shaping (cubing the accuracy), are minor imperfections that are overshadowed by the significance of the overall contribution and the strength of the final results.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors:**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   **Computational Cost:** The elephant in the room is the computational expense. While I believe it is justified for a proof-of-concept, it would strengthen the paper to include a brief discussion on this limitation and potential avenues for future work to improve efficiency. For instance, could techniques like parameter sharing or performance prediction reduce the need to train every child network to convergence?
*   **Search Space Sensitivity:** The design of the search space seems critical to the success of the method. How sensitive are the results to the specific choices of primitives? For example, what happens if you provide a much larger or a "worse" set of filter sizes or activation functions? A short discussion on this would be insightful.
*   **Analysis of Discovered Architectures:** The analysis of the discovered recurrent cell is fascinating. Could you provide more intuition about *why* it works well? For example, does it learn gating mechanisms similar to LSTMs? A deeper dive into the information flow or the function of its components could provide valuable insights for the broader community.
*   **Reward Function:** The reward for CIFAR-10 is the maximum validation accuracy over the last 5 epochs, cubed. What was the motivation for cubing the accuracy? Did this shaping prove critical, or were similar results achieved with linear accuracy?
*   **Search Convergence:** Given that the search explores a tiny fraction of the total possible architectures, what indicators did you use to assess that the controller's policy was converging toward a good region of the space? Did the entropy of the controller's predictions decrease over time?
