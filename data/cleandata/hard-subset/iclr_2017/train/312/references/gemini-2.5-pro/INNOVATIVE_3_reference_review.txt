### Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a fundamentally new and ambitious approach to automating the design of neural network architectures, termed Neural Architecture Search (NAS). The core idea is to frame architecture generation as a learning problem. The authors employ a controller, implemented as a recurrent neural network, to generate a variable-length string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. This child network is then trained on a target task (e.g., image classification or language modeling), and its performance on a validation set is used as a reward signal. This reward is used to update the controller's parameters via a policy gradient algorithm (REINFORCE). Over time, the controller learns to generate architectures that yield higher rewards. The method is extended to handle complex designs, including skip connections (via an attention-like mechanism) and the discovery of novel recurrent cells from a set of primitive computations. The authors demonstrate the power of their approach on two highly competitive benchmarks: CIFAR-10 image classification and Penn Treebank language modeling. In both cases, the automatically discovered architectures achieve state-of-the-art or near-state-of-the-art performance, rivaling and even surpassing the best models designed by human experts.

**2. Recommendation: Strong Accept**

This paper is a clear accept and represents a significant conceptual leap in the field. My recommendation is based on two key factors:

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Boldness:** The paper proposes a radical departure from the traditional, human-centric process of architecture engineering. The idea of using a neural network to learn how to design other neural networks is a powerful meta-learning concept that opens up an entirely new direction for research.
2.  **Compelling Empirical Validation:** The authors provide strong proof-of-concept by demonstrating that their method can discover novel architectures from scratch that are competitive with, or superior to, meticulously hand-crafted models like DenseNet and LSTM on major benchmarks. Discovering a new recurrent cell that outperforms the highly-optimized LSTM is a particularly impressive achievement.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

This work's primary strength lies in its boldness and novelty. While hyperparameter optimization is a well-studied field, prior methods have largely been confined to searching over fixed-length, pre-defined search spaces. This paper shatters that limitation by using an RNN to navigate a compositional, variable-length space of architectures. This is not an incremental improvement; it is a paradigm shift. The formulation of architecture design as a reinforcement learning problem is elegant and allows the system to learn directly from the performance signal that matters.

The computational cost of the proposed method is staggering (e.g., training 800 networks concurrently on GPUs). In many contexts, this would be a severe limitation. However, in the context of this paper, I view it as a strength. It demonstrates a willingness to pursue a high-risk, high-reward idea at the scale necessary to validate it. The purpose of this work is not to present a computationally cheap tool for everyday use, but to prove that automated architecture discovery is possible at the highest level of performance. In this, it succeeds unequivocally.

The empirical results are extremely strong and directly support the paper's claims. On CIFAR-10, the discovered network achieves performance on par with the best in the field while being architecturally distinct from human designs (e.g., using many rectangular filters). The true standout result, however, is the discovery of a novel recurrent cell for Penn Treebank. The LSTM cell has been a cornerstone of sequence modeling for two decades; showing that a better-performing cell can be algorithmically discovered is a landmark result. The successful transfer of this cell to other tasks (character modeling and machine translation) further solidifies the generality of the discovery.

While the method is not perfect—for example, the final reported results require a small grid search on the best-found architecture—such minor imperfections are easily overlooked in light of the significance of the core contribution. The paper prioritizes a groundbreaking idea and demonstrates its viability, which is far more valuable to the community than a perfectly polished but incremental paper.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Analysis of the Search Process:** The paper focuses on the final discovered architectures. It would be fascinating to see more analysis of the controller's learning dynamics. For example, how does the policy's entropy evolve over training? Does the controller quickly converge to a small region of high-performing architectures, or does it continue to explore broadly? Visualizing the distribution of generated architectures over time could provide valuable insight.
*   **Search Space Design:** The design of the search space itself is a critical component of the method's success. A brief discussion on the rationale behind the chosen primitives (e.g., the specific filter sizes for CNNs, the operations for RNN cells) and the potential sensitivity of the results to these choices would be beneficial.
*   **Cost vs. Performance Trade-off:** While the immense compute is acceptable for a proof-of-concept, it would be helpful to include a brief discussion on future directions for making NAS more accessible. This could frame the work as a foundational step and motivate future research into more efficient search strategies.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

*   The comparison against random search is useful. However, given the massive parallelism, could you clarify the total computational budget for both REINFORCE and random search? Is it possible that a massively parallel random search, given the same total number of evaluated architectures (e.g., 12,800), could have found a similarly performing model?
*   The mechanism for adding skip connections is clever. Could you provide some insight into the types of skip patterns the controller typically learned? Did it rediscover well-known patterns like residual connections, or did it favor more exotic connectivity?
*   What were the most common "compilation failures" encountered during the search, and how often did the controller produce such invalid architectures? Understanding these failure modes could help in designing better search spaces in the future.
