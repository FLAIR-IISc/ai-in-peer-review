1. Summary
The paper tackles automatic neural architecture design by training a controller RNN with reinforcement learning to generate model descriptions that maximize validation performance. It claims contributions on both convolutional networks (CIFAR-10) and recurrent cells (PTB), including skip-connection-aware search spaces, distributed asynchronous training for efficiency, and empirical results that rival or surpass strong human-designed baselines. The generated architectures resemble contemporary human designs (e.g., use of skip connections and filter patterns), suggesting alignment with human expertise. The experiments are carefully executed, include control comparisons against random search, and demonstrate limited transfer to character PTB and GNMT.

2. Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons: (i) The RNN+RL approach to architecture generation is novel, well-motivated, and shows strong results on two representative modalities (vision and language). (ii) The discovered architectures are competitive with or better than human-designed models, indicating substantial potential impact despite heavy computational cost.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Clear and important—architecture design is labor-intensive; prior hyperparameter optimization and neuroevolution face limitations on variable-length structures or scalability. Positioning in literature is appropriate and acknowledges related program synthesis and meta-learning.
- Claims and evidence: Results on CIFAR-10 (3.65% with speed gains vs. comparable baselines) and PTB (62.4 perplexity, plus character PTB SOTA and modest GNMT gains) substantiate the core claim that the method can discover high-performing architectures. The random-search comparison and ablations (stride/pooling variants, search-space expansions) strengthen credibility.
- Limitations: The computational footprint is substantial (hundreds of concurrent trainings), and generality is only partially demonstrated (two datasets, limited diversity for sequence tasks). Fairness of some comparisons and sensitivity to reward shaping (cubed accuracy) could be further justified.

4. Feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not decisive)
- Broaden evaluation: add CIFAR-100, ImageNet (proxy), and larger language datasets (e.g., WikiText-103) to test generality; include more sequential tasks beyond PTB and char PTB.
- Report search efficiency: total FLOPs/GPU-days, learning curves of controller reward, and variance across independent runs; quantify sample efficiency over random search.
- Robustness: sensitivity to reward shaping, controller size, baseline choice, and search-space definitions; stability across different compute budgets.
- Reproducibility: release search spaces, controller hyperparameters, and code for the search (not only the final cells).

Questions for the authors
- What is the total compute budget (GPU/CPU days) per dataset, and how does performance scale with budget?
- How sensitive are results to the reward transformation (e.g., cubing accuracy) and baseline estimator?
- What is the variance across multiple NAS runs with different random seeds?
- How do results change under tighter resource constraints (e.g., 10–50x fewer child models)?
- Can the discovered PTB cell transfer to other sequence tasks (e.g., WikiText, speech) without retuning, and how does it compare under matched training budgets?
