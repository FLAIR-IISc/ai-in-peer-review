1. Summary of contributions
The paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a reinforcement-learning approach in which a controller RNN generates variable-length specifications of neural network architectures, receives validation accuracy as a reward, and is updated via REINFORCE. The method supports rich search spaces (e.g., skip connections, multiple layer types for CNNs; tree-structured recurrent cells with memory injection for RNNs), uses large-scale parallel and asynchronous training to evaluate many candidate “child” architectures, and progressively increases model depth during search. Empirically, NAS discovers:
- A CIFAR-10 CNN with 3.65% test error (competitive with or better than hand-designed baselines while being 1.05x faster than a comparable DenseNet variant).
- A novel RNN cell that surpasses LSTM on PTB (62.4 perplexity), transfers to PTB character modeling with state-of-the-art 1.214 perplexity, and yields a +0.5 BLEU improvement when substituted into GNMT without retuning the system.
The paper addresses an important problem—automating architecture design—and demonstrates benefits on both vision and language benchmarks, enabling dataset-specific architectures without manual engineering.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical results across modalities with clear evidence that searching beyond standard CNN/RNN spaces produces measurable gains.
- Well-motivated, timely problem with high potential impact; although compute demands are currently high, costs will likely decrease and infrastructure will improve.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and positioning: The work is well placed with respect to hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, sequence-to-sequence, and meta-learning. It clearly motivates why variable-length architecture specification requires more flexible controllers than fixed-length hyperparameter search.
- Methodology: The controller design, reward shaping, skip-connection selection via content-based sigmoids, and the RNN-cell search with explicit ct/ht integration are technically sound and clearly described. The use of distributed asynchronous updates is appropriate for the problem scale.
- Evidence and rigor: The paper reports competitive-to-state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and PTB, ablations (e.g., effect of skip connections), a control comparison to random search, transfer to character LM, and insertion into GNMT. These multiple, diverse evaluations bolster the central claim that NAS can discover non-trivial, high-performing architectures.
- Generality: Demonstrating improvements in both CNN and RNN domains is a significant strength, and the discovered RNN cell’s cross-task transfer is particularly compelling.

4. Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not required for acceptance)
Reproducibility and hyperparameter transparency
- Please provide the exact hyperparameters and regularization settings used in all reported models, especially for language modeling:
  - Types of dropout (embedding dropout, recurrent/variational dropout, input/output/hidden dropout, dropout placement per layer) and their rates.
  - Whether recurrent dropout is time-locked (variational) vs naïve, and if masks are tied across time and/or layers.
  - Gradient clipping thresholds, weight decay, weight initialization schemes and ranges, optimizer hyperparameters (beta values for Adam, momentum/Nesterov details), learning-rate schedules and decay epochs, batch sizes, sequence lengths (BPTT), and early stopping criteria, per experiment.
  - For the character LM and GNMT experiments, please mirror the level of detail given for PTB word-level so that others can replicate without excessive trial-and-error.
- Recommend releasing a base set of hyperparameter configurations (akin to Zaremba et al., 2014 “small/medium/large” templates) for:
  - PTB word-level with the discovered cell.
  - PTB character-level.
  - CIFAR-10 child CNN training (final chosen LR schedule, BN epsilon/momentum, weight decay, data augmentation).
  - GNMT substitution (exact hidden sizes, dropout, parameter matching to LSTM).
- Please release full, machine-readable specifications of the best discovered architectures (controller outputs/graphs), along with code to instantiate them.

Clarity and analysis
- Reward shaping: Justify the choice of accuracy^3 and perplexity-based reward c(·)^2; include sensitivity analyses to exponents and c, and whether advantage normalization or entropy regularization were used.
- Search space constraints: In the 39-layer CNN, pooling layer indices are fixed a priori. Discuss sensitivity to these choices and whether allowing the controller to choose pooling yields comparable results with similar compute.
- Compute budget and fairness: Report per-model training time and total compute for each setting; clarify the “1.05x faster” comparison methodology (identical hardware? includes the +40 filters configuration?).
- Stability and variance: Report variability across controller seeds and across re-trainings of the same discovered architecture.
- Controller training details: Provide the baseline EMA decay rate, gradient accumulation specifics, any temperature/softmax scaling, and whether you used entropy regularization.

Scope and future work
- Consider reporting performance vs. compute trade-offs (e.g., best-of-N curves) to guide practitioners with limited budgets.
- Any preliminary results on larger-scale datasets (e.g., ImageNet) or partial-epoch/early-stopping strategies could further illustrate practicality.
- A brief discussion of compilation-failure mitigation and its effect on search efficiency would be helpful.

Questions for the authors
- Exact dropout details: For PTB word-level, which dropout types (embedding, input, hidden, output, recurrent/variational) and rates were used per layer? Are masks time-locked and/or layer-locked? For PTB character and GNMT, please provide the same granularity. Was embedding dropout used anywhere besides PTB word-level?
- Optimization and clipping: What gradient clipping thresholds and weight initialization schemes were used for each task? Any layer norm or other stabilization?
- CIFAR child training: What were the final selected LR schedule (initial LR, decay epochs), BN epsilon/momentum, and augmentation parameters for the reported best models?
- Controller training: Did you use entropy regularization, temperature annealing, or advantage normalization? What EMA decay was used for the baseline? Any variance reduction beyond the moving-average baseline?
- Search space sensitivity: How sensitive are final results to the fixed pooling positions and the depth schedule? Can the controller select pooling locations with comparable performance?
- Compute and fairness: How was the “1.05x faster” measured relative to DenseNet (same hardware, same image size, identical augmentation)? Does it include the +40 filters modification?
- Robustness: How consistent are the discovered top architectures across different controller initializations? Do their motifs (e.g., skip usage, filter shapes) converge?

Overall, this is a well-written paper tackling an important problem with impressive cross-domain results. With the additional hyperparameter disclosures and base configs, it will be a strong and reproducible contribution.
