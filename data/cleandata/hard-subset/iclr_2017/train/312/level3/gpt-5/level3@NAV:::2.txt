1. Summary of contributions
This paper tackles the problem of automating neural architecture design by casting network construction as a sequence decision process. The authors propose an actor-critic reinforcement learning framework in which node and connection choices are actions, and the architecture’s validation error serves as the reward. A two-layer auto-regressive LSTM acts as both controller (actor) and baseline estimator (critic), enabling search over variable-length architectures. The method is evaluated on CIFAR-10 (CNNs) and Penn Treebank (recurrent cells), where discovered architectures match or surpass strong human-designed baselines. The work is clearly written, well-scoped, and provides intuitive analyses of the learned designs, highlighting qualitative differences from human-crafted models.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (i) Novel and well-motivated formulation of NAS as sequential decision-making with an auto-regressive controller and critic; (ii) Strong empirical support across two distinct tasks with competitive-to-state-of-the-art results, and careful comparisons to human-designed architectures.

3. Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? Efficiently exploring a vast, variable-length architecture space to relieve manual engineering.
Is the approach well motivated and situated? Yes. The paper crisply positions itself against Bayesian hyperparameter optimization (fixed-length spaces), neuro-evolution (scaling), and meta-learning, and leverages sequence modeling and RL to naturally handle variable-length structures and skip connections.
Do the results support the claims? Largely yes. The method yields top-tier CIFAR-10 performance and a novel RNN cell that improves PTB perplexity and transfers to character LM and MT. Experimental design is appropriate, with ablations (e.g., comparisons to random search) and qualitative analyses. Related work and baselines are comprehensive and fair.

4. Additional feedback and questions (to improve the paper; not blockers)
- Please report end-to-end search cost: wall-clock time, total compute (e.g., GPU/CPU-days), and controller sample efficiency. A scatter or Pareto of compute/time vs model quality would be valuable to understand scaling and practical deployability.
- Clarify the critic/baseline: is it learned (actor-critic) or an EMA baseline? Provide an ablation of variance-reduction choices and their effect on stability and sample efficiency.
- Human-bootstrapped models: Did you warm-start the controller with human-designed seeds or mix in human architectures? If so, quantify their role and show results with/without bootstrapping.
- Fairness of comparisons: match training budgets and parameter counts where possible; include parameter/FLOP-normalized comparisons and training-time benchmarks.
- Provide more analysis of the discovered topologies (e.g., filter shapes, skip density) and their correlation with performance.
- Reproducibility: release exact search space specifications, controller hyperparameters, and scripts for evaluating sampled models.
