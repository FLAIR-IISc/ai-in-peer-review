1. Summary of contributions
- Problem tackled: The paper aims to automate neural architecture design by casting architectures as variable-length sequences and learning a policy to generate them.
- Method: An auto-regressive two-layer LSTM controller samples architectural decisions (actions) one token at a time; the validation performance of the instantiated “child” network is used as reward. Learning uses policy gradient (REINFORCE) with a moving-average baseline (critic-like variance reduction), asynchronous distributed updates, and reward shaping.
- Search spaces: The controller predicts convolutional architectures (filters, widths, depths, strides, skip connections via set-selection attention, optional pooling) and recurrent cells (tree of combination and activation nodes with explicit ct/ct−1 injection).
- Results: On CIFAR-10, the learned CNN reaches 3.65% error and is reported as 1.05x faster than a strong human-designed baseline with similar macro-structure. On PTB, the learned recurrent cell outperforms LSTM and other SOTA cells (test perplexity 62.4), transfers well to character modeling (1.214 BPC) and yields modest gains in GNMT without retuning.
- Analysis: The method discovers qualitatively different motifs from human designs (e.g., rectangular filters, larger top-layer filters, many short skip connections). Related work is well covered (hyperparameter optimization, neuroevolution, program induction, RL for non-differentiable metrics). The paper is clear, and the experimental setup (datasets, splits, training protocols) is described in useful detail.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel and impactful formulation of NAS as sequence generation optimized with RL, capable of variable-length decisions and complex wiring (including skips) with strong empirical gains on two diverse tasks.
- Thorough and clear presentation with sensible ablations/baselines and compelling qualitative analyses of the discovered architectures.

3. Supporting arguments
- What is the question? Can we learn a policy that generates high-performing neural architectures—CNNs and RNN cells—by treating design as sequential decision making and optimizing non-differentiable validation performance?
- Is the approach well motivated and placed? Yes. The paper clearly articulates limitations of fixed-length hyperparameter search and practical scalability issues of neuroevolution, motivating a flexible, auto-regressive controller trained with policy gradients. It connects to program synthesis and sequence-to-sequence decoders, and to RL approaches that optimize task-level metrics (e.g., BLEU).
- Do the results support the claims?
  - CIFAR-10: The controller explores a rich design space (filters/strides/skips/pool), evaluates 12.8K architectures, and finds models competitive with top human designs. The reported best test error (3.65%) is strong given the similar architectural scheme and training recipe, and the speedup claim is plausible (though see requests below).
  - PTB: The learned cell convincingly outperforms LSTM/RHN and other strong baselines, with tests across sizes and regularization settings. Transfer to char-level PTB and GNMT demonstrates generality. A random-search comparison shows clear policy-gradient gains as the search proceeds. Robustness to expanded search spaces (adding max/sin) is encouraging.
  - Scientific rigor: The controller and child training details are explicit (optimizers, learning rates, depth schedules, reward shaping). The skip-connection mechanism and safeguards for invalid graphs are clearly specified. While compute demands are high, the asynchronous setup is clearly documented.
- Novelty/excitement: Treating architecture design as a variable-length action sequence and optimizing it end-to-end with RL (including skip wiring and RNN cell trees) is a significant step beyond fixed-template tuning. The qualitative differences between learned and human-designed structures are insightful.

4. Additional feedback and questions (to improve the paper; not all affect the accept decision)
- Compute budget, time, and sample efficiency
  - Please report wall-clock time, total GPU/CPU hours, and the number of child models trained to reach the reported CIFAR-10 and PTB results. Include learning curves of best/median reward vs. time.
  - Provide correlation plots between compute (number of sampled/evaluated models, or total training time) and final model quality to characterize sample efficiency and diminishing returns.
  - Clarify the “1.05x faster” claim against DenseNet: speed measured as training or inference, on what hardware and batch sizes, and for which model sizes?

- Actor-critic vs. REINFORCE
  - The method uses REINFORCE with an EMA baseline. Did you try a learned value function (critic) to further reduce variance and improve sample efficiency? Any empirical comparison?

- Human-bootstrapped models
  - You emphasize learning from scratch. Did you try seeding the controller with human-designed architectures (e.g., ResNet/DenseNet macro-blocks or LSTM variants) or constraining early search around them? Please report results to quantify the role (if any) of human bootstrapping or warm starts.

- Fidelity of proxy training and reward shaping
  - CIFAR-10 children are trained for 50 epochs and validation accuracy^3 is used as reward. How well does this proxy correlate with the final accuracy after full training? Please provide scatter plots and correlation coefficients.
  - Similarly for PTB, how sensitive are results to the choice of c(validation perplexity)^2 and to the validation split?

- Fairness and reproducibility
  - For each baseline compared, report parameter counts, FLOPs, training/inference throughput, and training recipes used (especially for models where you adapted hyperparameters).
  - Provide variance across controller runs (different seeds) for both tasks, including best/median of top-k results. How robust is the discovered architecture?
  - The paper mentions released code and the NASCell in TensorFlow; please ensure scripts reproduce the controller training (not just final models).

- Search space and inductive biases
  - Offer more detail on the search-space priors (e.g., allowed filter sizes, stride/pool locations for the 39-layer CNN composed of 3-layer blocks). How sensitive are outcomes to these choices?
  - For the learned CNNs: give aggregate statistics (histograms) of selected filter shapes, skip lengths, and layer widths over top-k architectures to substantiate observed differences from human designs.

- Extensions
  - Consider experimenting with a learned critic, performance predictors, or partial weight sharing across candidates to reduce compute (e.g., early stopping with confidence, supernet-style sharing).
  - Analyze stability of asynchronous updates (e.g., staleness effects) and whether synchronous alternatives change outcomes.

Overall assessment: An excellent and influential paper that convincingly demonstrates RL-driven neural architecture search across vision and NLP, with clear exposition, strong results, and a promising research direction. Pending the additional reporting on compute/time–quality tradeoffs and bootstrapping, I strongly recommend acceptance.
