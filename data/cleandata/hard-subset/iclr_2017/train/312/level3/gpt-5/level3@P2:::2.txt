Summary and contributions
The paper tackles the problem of automatically designing neural network architectures by casting architecture generation as a sequential decision process. A controller RNN (auto-regressive two-layer LSTM) emits variable-length descriptions of architectures; each description is trained as a “child” network and its validation performance is used as a reward signal. Using policy-gradient reinforcement learning (with a variance-reducing baseline), the controller improves its policy over time. The search space flexibly includes layer hyperparameters, skip connections, and recurrent-cell structure, and the method scales via large-scale parallel and asynchronous training.

Empirically, the approach is validated on CIFAR-10 (convolutional architectures) and Penn Treebank (RNN cell discovery), achieving state-of-the-art or near-SOTA results. Notably, the method discovers: (i) a CIFAR-10 model with 3.65% error, competitive with strong human-engineered architectures and with favorable speed; and (ii) a novel recurrent cell that outperforms LSTM baselines on PTB (e.g., 62.4 perplexity), transfers to character-level modeling (1.214 bpc), and yields a small BLEU gain when dropped into GNMT without tuning. The paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and provides thoughtful analyses (e.g., skip connections, random search comparison, expanded function sets). The observed structural patterns in discovered models (e.g., rectangular filters, one-step skip connections; RNN cell motifs reminiscent of LSTM) are particularly insightful.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Novel, impactful formulation of neural architecture search as RL over variable-length action sequences with strong empirical results on two challenging, diverse domains.
- Clear methodology and thorough experimental validation, including comparisons to strong human-designed baselines and useful ablations.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Architecture design is costly and expertise-heavy; a general approach that learns to design networks is of high interest to the community.
- Motivation and placement: The method is well-positioned relative to hyperparameter optimization, neuroevolution, program induction, and meta-learning. The auto-regressive controller with RL is an appropriate and elegant choice for variable-length spaces.
- Technical soundness: The use of policy gradient with a baseline, reward shaping, and a curriculum over depth are reasonable. The extension to skip connections and recurrent-cell search is carefully specified. Large-scale parallelism is necessary and well-executed.
- Empirical strength: Results on CIFAR-10 and PTB are competitive or SOTA; transfer experiments (character PTB, GNMT) support generality. Comparisons against random search show meaningful gains in both best and top-k averages, suggesting the controller learns a nontrivial policy.
- Clarity and completeness: The paper is readable, with detailed method sections, ablations, and visualizations. Related work coverage and comparisons are appropriate.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful but not central to my decision)
- Compute budget and efficiency: Provide comprehensive wall-clock time, number of child models trained, total GPU/CPU hours, and speedups from the asynchronous scheme. Include plots relating compute versus achieved validation accuracy (e.g., Pareto front).
- Actor-critic vs REINFORCE: The paper appears to use REINFORCE with a moving-average baseline. Please clarify whether you tried a learned critic and, if not, discuss trade-offs. If you did employ an explicit critic, detail its architecture, training, and effect on variance/sample efficiency.
- Fairness of comparisons: Where possible, match parameter counts and training budgets to human baselines, and report with/without auxiliary components (e.g., 1x1 bottlenecks, regularization) to isolate the contribution of the searched architecture.
- Stability and reproducibility: Report variance over multiple controller seeds and the distribution of top-k models’ performance. Provide more details on the search space priors and constraints that most affected outcomes, and release search code/configs if feasible.
- Reward shaping and early stopping: Discuss how cubing the accuracy and fixed-epoch training influence the learned policy; show sensitivity analyses (e.g., alternative reward scalings, early-stopping heuristics).
- Overfitting to the validation set: Describe safeguards (e.g., hold-out splits, re-evaluation protocol) and any observed signs of overfitting during the search.

Questions for the authors
- Compute/resources: How many child models were trained end-to-end per task, and what were the total wall-clock time and GPU/CPU hours? How does performance scale with budget (e.g., 10%, 25%, 50% of your compute)?
- Correlation analyses: Do better early-epoch validation accuracies reliably predict final performance? Could proxy training (fewer epochs, smaller models, lower resolution) accelerate search without degrading final quality?
- Baseline/critic: Is your baseline purely an exponential moving average, or did you experiment with a learned critic (actor-critic)? If tried, how did it affect variance reduction, sample efficiency, and final performance?
- Search space priors: How sensitive are results to the depth-growth schedule, allowed operations (e.g., activation/combination sets), and skip-connection parametrization? Which elements were most critical?
- Human bootstrapping: Did you seed the controller with any human-designed templates or warm starts? If not, how would adding such priors affect convergence and outcomes?
- Random search: How was the random search baseline matched for budget and sampling distribution? Did you try evolutionary strategies or Bayesian optimization over the same space?
- Generalization: Have you attempted searches on larger-scale datasets (e.g., ImageNet) or different modalities? How well do CIFAR-derived architectures transfer to ImageNet with minimal tuning?
- GNMT integration: Can you share more details on matching computational complexity and the effect of further tuning? Is the discovered cell consistently better across layers or tasks?

Overall, this is an excellent and exciting paper that advances the state of the art in automated architecture design with compelling results and a clear, general framework. I recommend acceptance.
