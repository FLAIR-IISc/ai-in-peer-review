Summary of contributions
- This paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS): a controller RNN that generates variable-length neural network architectures, trained via policy gradients (REINFORCE) to maximize child-model validation accuracy. The framework handles both convolutional architectures and recurrent cells.
- Key technical ingredients include: an autoregressive controller that emits architecture tokens; training with REINFORCE using validation accuracy as reward with an EMA baseline; distributed asynchronous training to evaluate many child models in parallel; a set-selection attention mechanism to propose skip connections and branching; and a tree-structured search space for RNN cells with explicit handling of cell state.
- Empirically, the method discovers:
  - CIFAR-10 ConvNets achieving 3.65% error, competitive with the best human-designed models of the time, while being reported as 1.05x faster than a comparable DenseNet variant.
  - A novel RNN cell that outperforms LSTM on Penn Treebank word-level language modeling (test perplexity 62.4), transfers to character-level PTB (1.214 bpc/perplexity metric as reported), and yields a modest BLEU gain when dropped into GNMT.
- The paper includes useful analyses: ablations on skip connections, a random-search control showing learning improves over naive search, and a robustness test with an expanded operation set.

Decision
Accept (weak accept)
Primary reasons: (1) The approach is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates strong empirical results across two distinct modalities (vision and language), indicating high potential impact. (2) The method opens a compelling direction—learning to design architectures—that resonates with community interest and shows clear advantages over fixed human-designed templates in the presented settings.

Supporting arguments
1) What problem is tackled?
- Automating neural architecture design by learning a policy that emits architectures optimized for task-specific validation performance, thereby reducing human effort and potentially discovering non-obvious designs.

2) Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature?
- Yes. The paper connects to hyperparameter optimization, neuroevolution, program induction, sequence-to-sequence decoders, and reinforcement learning for non-differentiable metrics. The motivation for a variable-length, flexible search space is strong, and the use of an RNN controller with policy gradients is a natural, well-argued choice. The attention-based mechanism for skip connections and the tree-structured RNN cell search space are thoughtfully designed.

3) Do the results support the claims with scientific rigor?
- Generally yes. On CIFAR-10 and PTB, the method achieves state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art performance. The experiments follow established training setups and isolate the controller’s role by fixing many child hyperparameters during search. The random-search control and ablations support the claim that the learned policy improves architecture quality over time.
- Caveats:
  - Compute cost is very high (hundreds of replicas, thousands of architectures, 800 GPUs in some experiments). This raises questions of accessibility and sample efficiency.
  - Generality is suggested (vision and language), but dataset breadth is limited (CIFAR-10, PTB, PTB-char, and a single GNMT configuration). More diverse evaluations would better substantiate general claims.
  - Some comparisons (e.g., DenseNet) depend on different parameterization choices; speed comparisons would benefit from standardized FLOPs and identical hardware details.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Practicality and compute efficiency:
  - Report total computational budget (GPU/CPU hours), number of architectures trained to reach best results, and wall-clock time. This transparency helps others assess feasibility.
  - Consider and discuss techniques for improving sample efficiency (e.g., early stopping with learning-curve extrapolation, partial training proxies, parameter sharing across architectures, smarter reward normalization/entropy regularization).
- Fair and comprehensive comparisons:
  - Provide standardized metrics (FLOPs, parameters, training/inference wall-clock) for the top NAS models vs. baselines under identical training schedules and hardware.
  - Expand image experiments (e.g., SVHN, CIFAR-100, or a subset of ImageNet) and language experiments (e.g., WikiText-2 or other corpora) to better support generality.
- Search space and controller design:
  - Clarify the inductive biases in the search space (operations allowed, constraints, padding rules for incompatible layers) and how these choices may steer solutions toward human-like designs.
  - Provide an ablation on reward shaping (e.g., cubing validation accuracy, perplexity scaling), baseline choice, and the layer-depth schedule to show robustness.
- Reproducibility:
  - Include more details on hyperparameter ranges for the post-search grid, exact data augmentation settings, and the random seeds/variance across runs.
  - Since code for running discovered models will be released, consider also releasing a minimal controller+search prototype on a smaller compute budget to facilitate community adoption.

Questions for the authors
1. Compute budget and efficiency:
   - What are the total GPU/CPU hours consumed to reach the reported CIFAR-10 and PTB results? How many architectures were trained to obtain the best models?
   - Did you explore early stopping or low-fidelity proxies (fewer epochs, smaller models, fewer filters) during search, and how did that affect final performance?

2. Reward shaping and optimization stability:
   - Why cube the validation accuracy (and square perplexity after scaling)? How sensitive are results to this choice, the EMA baseline parameters, and entropy regularization?
   - Did you observe mode collapse or premature convergence in the controller’s policy? If so, how was it mitigated?

3. Generality and transfer:
   - How well do the discovered CIFAR-10 architectures transfer to CIFAR-100 or SVHN without re-search? Conversely, can the PTB cell transfer to other language datasets beyond PTB and GNMT with similar gains?
   - For GNMT, can you provide more details on parity of compute (FLOPs, latency) and whether further tuning of the new cell narrows or expands the BLEU gap?

4. Search space influence:
   - To what extent do the skip-connection handling rules (e.g., zero-padding to concatenate) bias the controller’s preferences? Have you tried alternative compatibility rules, and do they change the discovered patterns?

5. Comparison baselines:
   - Beyond random search, did you compare against Bayesian optimization or evolutionary baselines over the same search space and compute budget? If not, can you comment on expected relative performance?

Overall, this is a strong and forward-looking paper. Addressing the questions above—especially around compute efficiency, fairness of comparisons, and generality—would further strengthen the contribution.
