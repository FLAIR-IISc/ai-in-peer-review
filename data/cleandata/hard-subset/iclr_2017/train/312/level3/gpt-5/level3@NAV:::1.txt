Summary of contributions
The paper tackles the important problem of automating neural network architecture design. It proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), where a controller RNN generates architectures and is trained with policy gradients to maximize validation accuracy. The method searches variable-length spaces with skip connections and multiple layer types, and extends to discovering recurrent cells. Strong empirical results are demonstrated across modalities: a CIFAR-10 model with 3.65% error and a PTB cell achieving 62.4 perplexity, with transfer to character PTB (1.214 ppl) and gains in GNMT. The paper is clearly written, well positioned in the literature, and includes informative ablations and a random-search comparison.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons: (1) Strong, cross-domain empirical gains with a flexible, well-motivated approach that expands beyond standard RNN/CNN search spaces. (2) Clear evidence that NAS enables dataset-specific architectures without manual engineering, with compelling ablations and baselines.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Automating architecture design is timely and impactful; computational cost is high today but likely to decline, making the approach increasingly practical.
- Motivation and related work: The positioning relative to Bayesian optimization, neuroevolution, and meta-learning is appropriate and accurate; the variable-length search and skip-connection mechanism are convincing advances.
- Evidence and rigor: Results on CIFAR-10 and PTB are competitive or state-of-the-art; transfer to character LM and GNMT adds credibility. The random-search control shows meaningful gains from policy gradients. Reporting that the test set is used once after validation selection is good practice.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not decision-critical)
- Reproducibility: Please provide exact hyperparameters and dropout types/rates (embedding dropout, recurrent/variational dropout, input/output/hidden masks, embedding tying details), to avoid excessive trial-and-error for replication. Release a “base set” of hyperparameters, in the spirit of Zaremba et al. (2014), for small/medium/large configurations.
- Extend the above to all experiments, including character PTB and GNMT (optimizer settings, LR schedules, clipping, batch size/BPTT, init, weight decay, augmentation, reward shaping constants, baseline details).
- Report full compute budgets (total GPU/CPU hours, number of architectures evaluated per setting), and sensitivity to reward shaping (accuracy^3, perplexity scaling).
- Consider releasing controller/search-space definitions and config files to facilitate future work.

Questions for authors
- What exact dropout variants and rates were used for PTB (embedding, recurrent, input/output masks) and for character PTB/GNMT?
- What gradient clipping, initialization schemes, and optimizer hyperparameters (betas, eps) were used per experiment?
- How sensitive are results to the reward transformation and baseline EMA timescale?
- Can you share canonical “base” hyperparameter sets and seeds to standardize comparisons?
