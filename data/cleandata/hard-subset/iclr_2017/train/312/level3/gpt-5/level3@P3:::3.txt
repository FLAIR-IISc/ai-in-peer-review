1. Summary of contributions and claims
- The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS): an RNN “controller” that emits variable-length descriptions of neural architectures and is trained with policy-gradient reinforcement learning (REINFORCE) to maximize validation accuracy of “child” networks. This enables task-specific, data-driven architecture design.
- The search space includes convolutional networks (with skip connections and other layer types) and recurrent cells; the method uses large-scale distributed/asynchronous training to evaluate many candidates in parallel.
- On CIFAR-10, the method discovers architectures competitive with state-of-the-art human-designed models, reporting 3.65% test error (with post-search widening) and reasonable speed. On Penn Treebank, the learned recurrent cell outperforms LSTM and strong baselines (best 62.4 perplexity), transfers to character-level PTB (1.214 perplexity), and yields modest gains in GNMT (+0.5 BLEU), suggesting compatibility with human-engineered systems.
- The generated architectures qualitatively resemble human designs (e.g., skip connections, filter patterns), indicating the approach can rediscover and extend expert priors.
- The idea is promising and the experiments on two datasets are solid.

2. Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Novelty and significance: A clear, general, and flexible framework (RNN+RL) for automatic architecture generation with variable-length search spaces; high potential impact.
- Strong empirical results: SOTA or near-SOTA on two distinct modalities (vision and language), plus initial transfer, demonstrating practical viability.

3. Supporting arguments
Addressing the three key questions:
- What problem is tackled? Automating neural network architecture design by learning to generate and evaluate architectures directly from task feedback, thereby reducing manual engineering and enabling task-specific topologies.
- Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? Yes. The paper positions NAS relative to Bayesian optimization/hyperparameter tuning (typically fixed-length and better with good seeds), neuroevolution (flexible but often slow/scalability issues), program induction, seq2seq-style autoregression, and RL for non-differentiable objectives (BLEU). The motivation for variable-length, structural search and for skip-connection prediction is compelling.
- Do the results support the claims with rigor? Largely yes. The method is instantiated for CNNs and RNN cells, with clear training details and distributed implementation. Results on CIFAR-10 and PTB are competitive or superior to strong baselines; ablations include a random search comparison for PTB, sensitivity to expanded RNN-cell search space (max/sin), and qualitative analyses of learned structures. However, there are caveats:
  - Computational cost is extreme (hundreds of GPUs/CPUs for days), limiting accessibility and reproducibility; sample efficiency of the controller remains a concern.
  - Some comparisons involve post-search modifications (e.g., adding 40 filters), complicating “pure” search-vs-human comparisons; DenseNet comparisons are noted as not exact.
  - Generality beyond CIFAR-10/PTB is only partially demonstrated (GNMT +0.5 BLEU) and lacks broader evaluation on other datasets and domains (especially larger-scale vision like ImageNet).

Pros
- The RNN+RL approach to architecture generation is interesting, promising, and clearly general across modalities.
- Generated architectures resemble human-designed ones, suggesting learned priors align with expert practices and are compatible with existing frameworks.
- Strong empirical performance with careful engineering (skip-connection prediction, tree-based RNN cell search, reward baselines, distributed async updates).

Cons
- Training time and compute requirements are very large, even with substantial resources; the approach is currently impractical for many labs.
- Limited evidence of generality: two primary benchmarks plus one transfer; more diverse datasets and tasks (especially other sequential domains) would strengthen the case.

4. Feedback to improve the paper (non-blocking suggestions)
- Fairness and clarity of comparisons:
  - Separate performance achieved by pure search from performance after post-search scaling/hyperparameter tuning; report both clearly.
  - Provide wall-clock time, total FLOPs/architecture evaluations, and controller sample efficiency metrics. Include confidence intervals or multiple runs where feasible.
- Strengthen baselines:
  - Add random search and Bayesian optimization baselines for CIFAR-10 under matched resource budgets; report top-k averages, not only best-of-run.
  - Compare against weight-sharing or low-fidelity baselines (if available) or discuss feasibility; consider early-stopping and learning-curve extrapolation to reduce cost.
- Generality and transfer:
  - Evaluate on at least one additional dataset in each modality (e.g., SVHN/Tiny-ImageNet for vision; WikiText/PTB-char or other LM datasets; another sequence task like speech or tagging).
  - More systematic transfer: train the controller on one task and reuse on related tasks to assess learned priors.
- Analysis:
  - Provide quantitative analyses of discovered architectures (distribution of operations, skip density, filter shapes) and correlations with performance.
  - Ablate reward shaping (e.g., cubed accuracy), controller size, baseline choice, and depth schedule to understand stability and variance.
- Practicality:
  - Discuss memory/compute trade-offs and any techniques to reduce cost (e.g., proxy tasks, partial training, parameter sharing among children).

Questions for the authors
- Compute and efficiency:
  - What is the total wall-clock time and compute budget (GPU/CPU hours) for CIFAR-10 and PTB searches? How many child models were fully trained to convergence vs. early-terminated?
  - How sensitive is performance to the number of evaluated architectures? What performance do you achieve with 10× fewer resources?
- Baselines and robustness:
  - Can you report CIFAR-10 comparisons against random search and Bayesian optimization under the same budget and training protocol?
  - How does reward shaping (cubing accuracy; choice of baseline) impact stability and sample efficiency?
- Search space and constraints:
  - How restrictive are the CNN and RNN search spaces (e.g., allowed ops, base=8)? Do results hold with richer ops (e.g., gating, depthwise separable convs)?
  - For the CIFAR model, to what extent do skip connections drive the gains versus other choices? Any ablation on skip attention?
- Generality and transfer:
  - Have you tried transferring the controller (or the learned cell) to other datasets without retraining? How does performance degrade or improve?
  - Any preliminary results on larger-scale vision (e.g., ImageNet) or other sequence tasks (speech, tagging)?
- Reproducibility:
  - Will you release not only final cells/architectures but also controller code, search spaces, and training scripts to enable reproducibility under smaller budgets?
