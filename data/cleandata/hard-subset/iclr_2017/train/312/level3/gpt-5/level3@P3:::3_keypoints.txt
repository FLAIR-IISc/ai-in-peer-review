- Proposes NAS: an RNN controller trained with REINFORCE to emit variable-length neural architectures optimized for validation accuracy.
- Search space covers CNNs (with skip connections and various layers) and RNN cells; uses large-scale distributed/asynchronous evaluation.
- Achieves competitive results: CIFAR-10 3.65% test error (with post-search widening) and PTB 62.4 perplexity; transfers to char PTB and yields +0.5 BLEU in GNMT.
- Discovered architectures qualitatively resemble human designs (skip connections, filter patterns), indicating learned priors align with expert practices.
- Method is novel, general, and flexible (RNN+RL for structural search) with high potential impact.
- Strong empirical performance across vision and language modalities, demonstrating practical viability.
- Paper provides clear implementation/training details, ablations (including random search for PTB), and qualitative analyses.
- Major caveat: extremely high computational cost (hundreds of GPUs/CPUs for days), limiting reproducibility and accessibility.
- Some comparisons mix pure search results with post-search modifications (e.g., adding 40 filters), complicating fair model comparisons.
- Limited evidence of generality beyond CIFAR-10 and PTB; larger-scale vision (ImageNet) and more sequence tasks are lacking.
- Pros: generalizable RNN+RL approach, architectures compatible with human-engineered systems, and careful engineering choices (skip prediction, tree RNN cells).
- Cons: impractical compute requirements for most labs and limited diversity of evaluation tasks.
- Suggestion: clearly separate pure search performance from post-search scaling/tuning and report both.
- Suggestion: report wall-clock time, total FLOPs, number of child evaluations, and controller sample-efficiency metrics.
- Suggestion: include confidence intervals or multiple runs and report top-k averages instead of only best-of-run.
- Suggestion: add matched-budget baselines (random search, Bayesian optimization) for CIFAR-10 and report comparisons.
- Suggestion: explore weight-sharing/low-fidelity baselines, early-stopping, or learning-curve extrapolation to reduce cost.
- Suggestion: evaluate on additional datasets per modality (e.g., SVHN/Tiny-ImageNet, WikiText, speech/tagging) and test controller transfer across tasks.
- Suggestion: provide quantitative analyses of discovered architectures (operation distributions, skip density, filter shapes) and correlate with performance.
- Suggestion: ablate reward shaping (e.g., cubed accuracy), controller size, baseline choice, and depth schedule to assess stability and variance.
- Suggestion: discuss memory/compute trade-offs and practical techniques to lower search cost (proxy tasks, partial training, parameter sharing).
- Question: what is the total wall-clock time and compute budget (GPU/CPU hours) for CIFAR-10 and PTB searches, and how many children were fully trained vs early-stopped?
- Question: how sensitive are results to the number of evaluated architectures and what performance is achievable with 10× fewer resources?
- Question: can you provide CIFAR-10 comparisons against random search and Bayesian optimization under the same budget and protocol?
- Question: how does reward shaping and baseline choice affect stability and sample efficiency of the controller?
- Question: how restrictive are the CNN/RNN search spaces (allowed ops, base channels) and do results hold with richer operations (e.g., depthwise separable convs, gating)?
- Question: to what extent do skip connections drive CIFAR gains versus other design choices; is there an ablation on skip attention?
- Question: can the controller or learned cell transfer to other datasets without retraining, and are there preliminary ImageNet or other sequence-task results?
- Question: will the authors release controller code, search spaces, and training scripts (not just final architectures) to enable reproducibility under smaller budgets?
