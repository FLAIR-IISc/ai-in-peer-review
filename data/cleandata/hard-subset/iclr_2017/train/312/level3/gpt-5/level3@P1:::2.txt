Summary of claims and contributions
- The paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), framing the design of neural network architectures as a sequential decision process. An auto-regressive controller RNN (a two-layer LSTM) emits variable-length “programs” that specify network hyperparameters and connectivity (including skip connections and branching). The controller is trained with policy gradients (REINFORCE) using validation performance of each sampled “child” network as a reward; a moving-average baseline reduces variance.
- The approach scales via large-scale parallel and asynchronous training of many child networks, with a parameter-server architecture. The search space includes convolutional models with optional pooling/striding and recurrent cells defined by compositional trees with learned combination and activation choices.
- On CIFAR-10, NAS discovers architectures competitive with or better than strong human-designed baselines. A 39-layer architecture achieves 3.65% error and is reported to be 1.05× faster than a DenseNet variant with similar architectural scheme. On PTB language modeling, NAS discovers a recurrent cell that outperforms LSTM and other strong baselines, achieving 62.4 perplexity; it also transfers to character-level PTB (1.214) and improves GNMT BLEU by 0.5 without retuning the full framework.
- The paper includes control experiments (random search vs policy gradient; enlargement of the search space), qualitative analysis of discovered motifs, and clear ablations (e.g., impact of skip connections). The method is well placed relative to hyperparameter optimization, neuroevolution, meta-learning, and sequence-level RL.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel, well-motivated formulation of architecture design as learnable sequence generation with strong empirical results across modalities (vision and language).
- Clear methodology and substantial evidence that the approach advances the state of the art and the practice of model design.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and motivation: Hand-crafting architectures is time-consuming and requires expertise. Casting architectures as variable-length strings lets a controller learn to explore a vast combinatorial space, addressing a key bottleneck in deep learning development. The literature positioning is accurate and fair; the paper explains why fixed-length hyperparameter optimization and classical neuroevolution are limited for this problem.
- Methodological soundness: The controller is trained with REINFORCE with a moving-average baseline; the reward designs (cubed validation accuracy for CIFAR-10; scaled squared perplexity for PTB) are reasonable to stabilize learning. The skip-connection prediction via set-selection attention is elegant and general. The recurrent-cell search space is clearly specified, including memory connections. While not actor-critic (no learned value network), the approach is principled and internally consistent.
- Empirical rigor: The CIFAR-10 and PTB experiments use standard preprocessing, regularization, and strong baselines. The paper reports competitive or SOTA numbers and shows transfer of the learned cell to character PTB and GNMT, bolstering claims of generality. The random-search control demonstrates that policy gradient improves both best-found and top-k average performance. The paper reports sensitivity to architectural perturbations (e.g., adding/removing skips), helping validate the search.
- Clarity and completeness: The search spaces, training schedules, distributed setup, and evaluation protocols are described in sufficient detail, and the authors commit to code release and provide the discovered RNN cell in TensorFlow, aiding reproducibility.

Additional feedback to help improve the paper (not all factors in the accept decision)
- Compute budget transparency: Please report wall-clock training time, total GPU/CPU hours, and the distribution of resources per experiment (e.g., for the 12,800 CIFAR-10 architectures, how many hours per child and total time to best model). A compute vs. quality plot would contextualize practicality.
- Sample efficiency and learning dynamics: Provide learning curves for the controller’s reward over time, and correlation between child training time/parameters and validation accuracy across the search. This helps quantify whether the controller learns nontrivial structure vs. favoring larger/more expensive models.
- Role of human priors: Some experiments constrain pooling locations or reduce per-layer filter options. Please discuss the extent of human guidance in defining the search space and any experiments seeding the controller with human-designed starting points vs. starting from scratch.
- Fair comparison details: Clarify the “1.05× faster than DenseNet” claim (measured as images/sec on identical hardware? batch size? with/without 1×1 bottlenecks). Also clarify whether the final 3.65% model includes the post-search “+40 filters” tweak and how much of the gain comes from this hand adjustment vs. the discovered topology.
- Robustness and variance: Report variance across multiple controller runs (different seeds) and across re-trainings of the same discovered architectures. Error bars or top-k dispersion would strengthen the statistical case.
- Overfitting to validation: Since the controller directly optimizes validation metrics and a small grid search follows, discuss safeguards (e.g., separate meta-validation, early stopping, or regularization of the controller) to avoid subtle meta-overfitting.

Questions for the authors
- Compute and time:
  - What are the total GPU-hours (CIFAR-10) and CPU-hours (PTB) to reach the reported best architectures? How long from initialization until the controller identifies a model within 1% of the final accuracy?
  - How does validation accuracy correlate with child model training time, depth, and parameter count across the search?
- Learning algorithm:
  - Did you try a learned baseline or actor-critic variant (value function) to reduce variance vs. the EMA baseline? Any observed benefits?
  - How sensitive is controller training to the reward shaping choices (e.g., cubing accuracy, squaring perplexity)?
- Search space and priors:
  - To what extent do constraints (fixed pooling positions, reduced filter sets) impact final performance and search efficiency? Any results with more open-ended pooling/stride choices?
  - Did you experiment with seeding the controller with human-designed architectures or partial templates? If so, how did that affect convergence and final quality?
- Results and fairness:
  - For the 3.65% CIFAR-10 model, what is the test error without the post hoc “+40 filters” modification? Please specify the exact throughput measurement protocol used for the 1.05× speedup.
  - On PTB, what is the variance over multiple trainings of the discovered cell? How do results change without the final grid search?
- Generalization:
  - Have you tested transfer of the discovered CNN topology to CIFAR-100 or SVHN? For the RNN cell, any results on WikiText or Billion Word benchmarks?

Overall, this is an excellent, impactful paper that meaningfully advances automated architecture design and is suitable for acceptance.
