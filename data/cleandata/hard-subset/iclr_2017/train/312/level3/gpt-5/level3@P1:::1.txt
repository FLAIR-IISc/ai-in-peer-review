This paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), which uses a controller RNN trained with REINFORCE to generate variable-length neural architectures and optimize them directly for validation accuracy. The approach supports complex design choices (e.g., skip connections and branching, layer types, and recurrent cell structures). The method is evaluated on two domains—image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (PTB)—and discovers architectures that match or outperform strong human-designed baselines. Notably, it yields a 3.65% CIFAR-10 error and a 62.4 perplexity on PTB, transfers successfully to character LM (1.214), and improves GNMT by +0.5 BLEU with a drop-in cell. The work is well written, clear, and makes a compelling case for automating architecture design.

Decision: Accept. Reasons: (1) Strong empirical advances on two canonical benchmarks with novel, general methodology; (2) Clear motivation and placement in the literature with evidence of superiority to random search and competitive human baselines.

Supporting arguments:
- Problem: NAS tackles automated discovery of high-performing architectures, reducing reliance on expert hand-design. This is timely and impactful, as architecture design has become the new bottleneck.
- Motivation and related work: The paper positions itself convincingly relative to Bayesian optimization (fixed-length limitations), neuroevolution (flexibility but lower practicality at scale), and meta-learning/program synthesis. The use of an auto-regressive controller with policy gradients to optimize a non-differentiable reward is well motivated and connected to sequence-level objectives in NMT.
- Method: The controller RNN samples architectural tokens; rewards are validation accuracy (or a monotonic transform), with variance reduction via a moving-average baseline. The search space is expressive (convolutions with skip connections, layer types; a tree-structured recurrent cell with base 8). Handling of skip connections and compilation constraints is practical. Distributed asynchronous training (parameter servers, hundreds of replicas) is clearly described.
- Empirical evidence: On CIFAR-10, the method finds a 39-layer model achieving 4.47% and, after increasing filters, 3.65% while being reported as 1.05x faster than a strong DenseNet variant. On PTB, the discovered cell outperforms tuned LSTMs and RHNs by 3.6 perplexity, and demonstrates transfer both to character LM (SOTA) and to GNMT (+0.5 BLEU without retuning the broader system). Control experiments indicate benefits over random search both in best and top-k averages. The results strongly support the claims that NAS can discover competitive or superior architectures from scratch in both CNNs and RNNs.
- Rigor: The selection protocol (choose the best on validation, then report test) is standard; the paper includes grid-search fine-tuning after search to fairly allocate capacity. Comparisons to random search are helpful. While the compute budget is very large, the methodology is sound and reproducible in principle.

Caveats and limitations (do not outweigh the contributions):
- Compute intensity is extreme (up to 800 GPUs/400 CPUs concurrently), which limits accessibility. However, this cost is likely to decrease and methods can be adapted with proxy tasks/early stopping.
- Reproducibility details are incomplete for some experiments (e.g., exact regularization and dropout configurations, reward transforms, selection of epochs/decay). This increases trial-and-error for replication.
- Potential overfitting to the validation set when evaluating thousands of architectures is a concern; safeguards and analyses could be elaborated (e.g., repeated trials or varying validation splits).
- Fairness of some comparisons (e.g., “1.05x faster than DenseNet”) would benefit from clearer hardware, batch size, and implementation parity statements.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (constructive; not central to the accept decision):
- Provide a complete set of hyperparameters and training details for all reported models, especially PTB (embedding dropout, recurrent/variational dropout settings, dropout placements, weight decay, initialization distributions, optimizer hyperparameters, gradient clipping, BPTT length, embedding tying specifics) and the character LM setup. A base “replication recipe” akin to Zaremba et al. (2014) would greatly aid the community.
- Detail the reward shaping: exact form (e.g., “max of last 5 epochs cubed”), constants (e.g., c for perplexity), and the rationale/ablation of these choices.
- Report the total number of architectures evaluated per experiment, wall-clock time, and compute budget in FLOPs or TPU/GPU-hours. Include early-stopping or proxy training strategies, if any, to reduce cost.
- Clarify the validation protocol and steps taken to mitigate overfitting to the validation set (e.g., cross-validation across multiple splits, repeating the final evaluation with different seeds).
- Expand ablations: effect of enabling skip connections; different base numbers for the recurrent cell; impact of controller size; influence of the schedule that increases depth.
- For claims of speedups vs DenseNet, specify hardware, software stack, image size, batch size, and whether both models use identical cudnn kernels and mixed precision, to ensure apples-to-apples.

Questions for the authors:
- Please list the exact hyperparameters for PTB (all dropout types and rates, embedding tying details, weight decay, learning rate schedules, initialization ranges, gradient clipping) and for character LM. Similarly, provide the precise CIFAR-10 training hyperparameters used in final fine-tuning (batchnorm epsilon, learning rate decay epochs, weight decay, data augmentation).
- How many architectures were evaluated per domain (CIFAR-10 with/without strides/pooling; PTB), and what was the total compute budget? Were any early-stopping heuristics used during search?
- How sensitive are results to the reward shaping (e.g., cubing accuracy; c(validation perplexity)^2)? Do linear rewards or different exponents materially change search outcomes?
- What measures were taken to reduce overfitting to the validation set given tens of thousands of trials? Did you observe performance gaps between validation and test for the selected architectures? Any results with alternative validation splits?
- Can you quantify variance across different random seeds for the search process and for training the final selected architectures?
- For the “1.05x faster than DenseNet” claim, what were the exact runtime measurement conditions? Is the speedup robust across batch sizes and frameworks?
- For the recurrent cell search, why choose base=8? How do base=4 or base=16 affect performance and search efficiency?
- In GNMT transfer, were any hyperparameters (e.g., dropout, layer widths) adjusted to match computational complexity? Please provide the exact modifications and the final BLEU with confidence intervals if available.

Overall, this is an important, well-executed paper that substantially advances automated architecture design and demonstrates benefits across modalities. I recommend acceptance.
