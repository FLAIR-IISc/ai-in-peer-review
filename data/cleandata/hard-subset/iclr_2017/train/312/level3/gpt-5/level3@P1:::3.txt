Summary of contributions
The paper tackles the problem of automating neural architecture design. It proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), which uses a controller RNN to emit variable-length architecture descriptions that are instantiated as “child” networks; the controller is trained with REINFORCE to maximize expected validation accuracy. The approach is well-motivated: manual architecture engineering is laborious; classical HPO largely targets fixed-length spaces; and neuro-evolution approaches are flexible but often slow and heuristic-heavy. Key technical contributions include: (a) a flexible autoregressive controller over architectural choices (including layer types, hyperparameters, and skip connections via set-selection attention); (b) a scalable distributed training setup with asynchronous updates; and (c) an extension to generate recurrent cells by labeling a computation tree over xt, ht−1, and ct. Empirically, the method discovers novel CNNs and RNN cells that rival or surpass strong human-engineered baselines: 3.65% error on CIFAR-10 with competitive speed, and 62.4 perplexity on PTB word-level LM, plus strong transfer to PTB character-level (1.214) and a modest BLEU gain when dropped into GNMT without tuning. Control experiments show robustness to expanded search spaces and superiority to random search over time. The discovered architectures share recognizable motifs (e.g., skip connections, repeated affine transforms), suggesting the search recovers human-like design principles.

Decision: Accept (weak)
- Novel and general formulation that automates architecture design with strong results on two distinct modalities (vision and language).
- The approach is impactful and promising; however, the empirical section would be stronger with broader generalization studies and a clearer accounting of compute and fairness in comparisons.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets automated discovery of high-performing architectures, a timely and important problem given the shift from feature engineering to architecture engineering. The controller RNN with policy gradient is a conceptually simple and extensible way to search variable-length spaces, addressing limitations of standard Bayesian optimization.
- Relation to literature: The work is well-situated relative to HPO, neuroevolution, meta-learning, and reinforcement learning for non-differentiable objectives. The set-selection attention for skip connections and tree-based parametrization for RNN cells are sensible extensions that connect to modern design patterns (e.g., ResNets, gated cells).
- Correctness and rigor: The learning setup (REINFORCE with baseline, reward shaping, asynchronous updates) is standard and sound. Controls against random search and search-space expansion are valuable. The PTB transfer to character-level LM and GNMT is a notable demonstration of external validity. The reported performance numbers on CIFAR-10 and PTB are competitive or SOTA for the contemporaneous baselines, and the method recovers interpretable motifs.
- Limitations and risks: The method is extremely compute-intensive (hundreds of GPUs/CPUs training thousands of models), which raises concerns about accessibility and reproducibility. The empirical scope is fairly narrow: CIFAR-10 (no CIFAR-100/ImageNet), PTB (a small corpus) with transfer to PTB-Char and one MT experiment. Some improvements involve additional manual tuning (e.g., add 40 filters to reach 3.65%), blurring the boundary between automated and manual design. Baseline fairness and speed comparisons would benefit from more careful normalization (e.g., DenseNet uses 1x1 compressions). The use of early proxy training (50 or 35 epochs) and reward shaping (cubed accuracy) are reasonable but their impact on sample efficiency and stability is not fully ablated.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Generalization and transfer:
  - Evaluate the discovered CNN on CIFAR-100 and/or SVHN; train at least one discovered CNN on ImageNet (even a small-epoch proxy) to assess scalability.
  - For RNNs, include another corpus (e.g., WikiText-2/103) or PTB subsets with domain shifts to test robustness.
- Compute and fairness:
  - Report total compute (GPU/CPU hours), energy, and cost. Provide anytime performance curves (best/median vs. architectures evaluated).
  - Normalize speed and parameter counts across baselines; clarify how “1.05x faster” was measured (wall-clock, FLOPs, batch size, hardware).
- Ablations and sensitivity:
  - Analyze the effect of the reward transformation (e.g., cubing accuracy), baseline choice, controller size, and depth schedule on stability and sample efficiency.
  - Quantify correlation between proxy training accuracy (after 35–50 epochs) and fully trained performance.
  - Separate the contribution of regularization and shared embeddings from the discovered cell in PTB; provide results with/without these components.
  - Clarify the degree of manual intervention (e.g., post-hoc filter scaling) and whether such knobs could be included in the search space.
- Reproducibility:
  - Provide seeds, variance over multiple controller runs, and performance distribution over the top-k architectures.
  - Release not only the final architectures but also the search code/scripts and a smaller-budget recipe for the community.

Questions for the authors
- Compute and budget:
  - What is the total compute used for the CIFAR-10 and PTB searches (GPU/CPU hours), and how does performance scale with 10x less compute?
  - How many architectures were trained to completion for the best results, and what is the variance across runs?
- Proxy objectives:
  - How strong is the correlation between early-stopping validation accuracy used for rewards and final test accuracy after full training? Please provide scatter plots or rank correlations.
  - How sensitive are results to the reward shaping (e.g., cubing accuracy) and the moving-average baseline?
- Search space and controller:
  - How does performance change if you include more layer types (e.g., 1x1 conv, bottlenecks) or limit skip connections? Any evidence of overfitting to specific motifs?
  - For the RNN cell, what is the effect of the base number (e.g., 4, 6, 8) on search efficiency and final quality?
- Fairness and transfer:
  - For CIFAR-10, can you compare fairly against DenseNet with matched use (or non-use) of 1x1 convolutions and parameter counts?
  - Beyond PTB-Char and GNMT, have you tried the discovered cell on a different dataset (e.g., WikiText or translation on a different language pair)?
- Practical use:
  - Can you provide guidance for practitioners with limited compute (e.g., a 1–8 GPU budget)? What reduced-cost settings still yield gains over strong human baselines?

Overall, this is a timely, well-motivated paper with strong empirical results and a generally applicable framework. Strengthening the generalization story and clarifying compute/fairness would make it more compelling.
