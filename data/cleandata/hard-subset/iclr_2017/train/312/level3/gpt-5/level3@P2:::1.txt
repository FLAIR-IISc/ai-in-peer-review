1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), where a controller RNN generates neural network architectures and is trained with REINFORCE to maximize the expected validation performance of the sampled “child” networks. The method flexibly searches variable-length spaces and can include skip connections, pooling, and other layer choices for CNNs, and a compositional tree of operations for RNN cells. The work demonstrates:
- On CIFAR-10: a controller discovers CNNs that are competitive with or exceed strong human-designed architectures, achieving 3.65% test error, with speed advantages relative to a comparable DenseNet configuration.
- On PTB word-level language modeling: a novel recurrent cell discovered by the controller outperforms LSTM and other state-of-the-art cells, reaching 62.4 perplexity.
- Transfer: the discovered cell improves character-level PTB (1.214 perplexity) and yields a +0.5 BLEU gain when dropped into GNMT with matched compute.
The paper also presents practical techniques (skip-connection prediction via set-selection attention, distributed asynchronous training of child models, reward shaping) and control experiments (larger search spaces and random search comparisons). Overall, it tackles an important problem—automating architecture design—and shows compelling results on both vision and NLP.

2. Decision
Accept.
Key reasons: (1) Strong empirical gains across two modalities, including transfer, demonstrating that reinforcement-learning-based architecture search can discover high-performing, nontrivial designs. (2) A well-motivated and clearly presented approach that extends the search space beyond fixed templates while remaining practical at scale.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Automating architecture design is impactful, reducing reliance on expert intuition and enabling dataset-specific architectures. Although computationally intensive today, the cost trend is likely to improve with hardware and algorithmic advances.
- Positioning and motivation: The paper situates NAS relative to Bayesian optimization, random search, and neuroevolution, highlighting the flexibility of variable-length, program-like search with an autoregressive controller. The framing as policy optimization over architectures is natural and well argued.
- Method correctness and clarity: The REINFORCE formulation, variance-reduction baseline, and distributed parameter-server scheme are standard and appropriate. The search space definitions for both CNN and RNN are explicit, and the skip-connection mechanism is sensible.
- Empirical rigor: Results are competitive or SOTA on CIFAR-10 and PTB. The transfer experiments strengthen the claim that the discovered cell is useful beyond the training setup. The random search comparison suggests the policy learns a meaningful search distribution. Ablations on search-space expansion (adding max/sin) show robustness.
- Caveats: Reproducibility depends on many training details; more complete hyperparameter reporting would substantially strengthen the paper. Compute cost is high; clearer accounting would contextualize the gains.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not central to the decision)
- Reproducibility: Please provide exact hyperparameters for all experiments, including:
  - PTB word-level: dropout types and rates (embedding dropout, recurrent/variational dropout, output dropout), gradient clipping, learning rate schedules, optimizer settings, initialization ranges, decay epochs, embedding tying specifics, BPTT length, batch size.
  - CIFAR-10: full augmentation pipeline, batchnorm details (epsilon, momentum), learning-rate schedule and decay epochs, weight decay, initialization, Nesterov settings, early stopping criteria, and grid-search ranges used after selection.
  - PTB char-level and GNMT transfer: all training schedules, dropout configurations, optimizer and LR schedules, any differences from referenced baselines.
  Publishing a “base configuration” (as in Zaremba et al., 2014) per task would greatly ease replication and fair comparison.
- Compute and fairness: Report search cost in GPU/CPU hours, number of child models trained to convergence, and the compute used for post-search tuning. Include parameter counts, FLOPs, and measured training/inference throughput to clarify the “1.05x faster” claim and to enable apples-to-apples comparisons (e.g., with DenseNet variants that use 1x1 bottlenecks).
- Sensitivity and variance: Provide variance across multiple NAS runs (different seeds) and sensitivity to reward shaping (e.g., cubing accuracy, perplexity scaling), baseline choice, and the schedule that increases model depth.
- Search space and priors: Discuss how hand-designed elements (e.g., base-8 tree, allowed ops) impact outcomes, and provide ablations on the base size/depth and op sets.
- Validation usage: Since the controller directly optimizes validation accuracy, consider reporting performance on a held-out test after selecting architectures using a separate validation split to reduce selection bias; briefly discuss overfitting risks and mitigation.

5. Questions for the authors
- PTB (word-level): Please list exact hyperparameters: embedding dropout rate; recurrent/variational dropout rate and whether locked per time step; output dropout; gradient clipping threshold; optimizer (Adam vs SGD) with betas/momentum; LR schedule and decay epochs; initialization scheme and scales; weight decay; BPTT length; batch size; embedding tying implementation details.
- CIFAR-10: Provide the precise BN epsilon/momentum, LR decay epochs, exact data augmentation parameters, weight initialization, and the full grid used post-search. How is “1.05x faster” measured (images/sec on which hardware, batch size)? Please include parameter/FLOP counts for the final architectures.
- Character-level PTB: Please specify all dropout types/rates, optimizer, LR schedule, gradient clipping, and any differences from Ha et al. (2016), along with the exact 80K-step training schedule and selection protocol.
- GNMT transfer: What hidden size did you use for the NAS cell to match compute with 1024-d LSTM? Were attention and layer norms unchanged? Any differences in gradient clipping, LR warmup, or regularization?
- Controller/training details: What are the controller RNN hyperparameters beyond hidden size (e.g., dropout, LR schedule, gradient clipping)? What was the moving-average baseline’s decay factor? How sensitive is performance to cubing the accuracy (or to the perplexity scaling constant)?
- Search cost and convergence: How many child models were trained per task in total? How does top-k validation accuracy improve over time? Can you share curves quantifying controller learning versus random search on CIFAR-10, analogous to the PTB plot?
- Skip-connection prediction: Could you clarify how you handle “compilation failures” quantitatively (how often, impact on training), and whether zero-padding for mismatched sizes affects performance?

Overall, this is a well-written and impactful paper with strong results. With fuller hyperparameter disclosure and clearer compute accounting, it will set an excellent standard for reproducibility in this emerging area.
