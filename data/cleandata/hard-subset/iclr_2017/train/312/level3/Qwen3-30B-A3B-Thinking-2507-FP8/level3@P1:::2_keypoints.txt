- Introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS): a reinforcement-learning method using a controller RNN to generate variable-length architectural descriptions trained via policy gradients with validation accuracy as reward.  
- Key innovation: controller RNN iteratively proposes architectures; child network validation performance provides the reward signal.  
- Strong empirical results: CIFAR-10 (3.65% test error, 1.05x faster than prior SOTA) and Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity, 3.6 better than prior SOTA).  
- Demonstrates transferability: discovered cells transfer well to character language modeling and machine translation tasks.  
- Provides insights into discovered architectures (e.g., preference for rectangular filters, skip connections) and differences from human-designed models.  
- Clear motivation and positioning against hyperparameter optimization and neuro-evolution; method handles variable-length architectural spaces elegantly.  
- Experimental design is thorough, with careful validation and fair baselines.  
- Technical details (controller RNN, policy gradients, recurrent-architecture extension) are presented rigorously and clearly.  
- Implementation and distributed training details (including use of 800 GPUs) aid reproducibility.  
- Recommendation: accept—makes a significant, novel contribution with compelling evidence.  
- Major suggestion: report total computational cost (GPU-days) and analyze correlation between computational resources (e.g., number of architectures evaluated, 12,800 for CIFAR-10) and model quality.  
- Clarify extent of human expertise in defining the search space (filter sizes, layer types) to support the “starting from scratch” claim.  
- Proposed experiment: compare NAS against a version seeded with human-designed architectures to assess true improvement over human design.  
- Request additional analysis explaining why the discovered cell outperforms LSTMs and quantifies computational efficiency gains (e.g., the reported “2x faster” for PTB).
