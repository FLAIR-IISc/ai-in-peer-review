

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary
This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a novel approach for automatically designing neural network architectures. The authors propose using a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate architectural hyperparameters as a sequence, with reinforcement learning used to train the controller to maximize the validation accuracy of the generated architectures. The key insight is that neural network architectures can be represented as variable-length strings, making them amenable to sequence generation with RNNs. The method is evaluated on CIFAR-10 image classification and Penn Treebank language modeling, where it discovers architectures that rival or outperform human-designed baselines. The approach is well-motivated, with clear comparisons to related work in hyperparameter optimization and neuro-evolution, and the results are scientifically rigorous with thorough ablation studies and control experiments.

## 2. Decision
I recommend acceptance. The key reasons are: (1) The paper addresses a significant challenge in deep learning (automatically designing architectures) with a novel, well-motivated approach that is both theoretically sound and empirically effective; (2) The results are impressive and well-supported with comprehensive experiments on two major benchmarks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's contribution is substantial and timely. The problem of neural architecture design is increasingly important as deep learning models grow more complex, and the authors present a compelling solution that avoids the limitations of previous approaches. Unlike hyperparameter optimization methods that search fixed-length spaces, their method can search variable-length architectures, which is critical for discovering more complex and effective network structures. The method's flexibility is demonstrated through the successful discovery of both convolutional architectures for image classification (achieving 3.65% test error on CIFAR-10, 0.09% better than the previous state-of-the-art) and novel recurrent cell architectures for language modeling (achieving 62.4 perplexity on Penn Treebank, 3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art).

The paper's experimental design is thorough, with appropriate ablation studies, control experiments, and transfer learning evaluations. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their method can discover architectures that are not only competitive with human-designed ones but also have advantages in computational efficiency (e.g., the CIFAR-10 model is 1.05x faster than the previous state-of-the-art). The results are statistically significant and well-documented, with careful attention to experimental details and hyperparameter settings.

## 4. Additional Feedback
The paper is well-written and technically sound, but there are a few areas where the authors could strengthen their presentation:
- It would be helpful to provide more details about the computational resources required for training the controller, including total training time and hardware specifications. This would help readers understand the practical feasibility of the approach.
- The paper mentions that the controller uses a "baseline" function but doesn't clearly explain how the baseline was chosen or its specific impact on the training process. A brief discussion of this would improve the methodological clarity.
- The relationship between the number of architectures sampled and the quality of the final architecture could be explored more deeply. It's not entirely clear how the authors determined the appropriate number of samples to search.

## 5. Questions for Authors
To better understand the practical implications and limitations of your approach, I'd appreciate clarification on the following points:
1. Could you provide more details about the training time and computational resources required to find your best architectures? Specifically, how many GPU-hours were needed to discover the CIFAR-10 architecture that achieves 3.65% error rate?
2. The paper mentions using a baseline function in the policy gradient update, but it's not clear how the baseline was selected or whether it was learned or hand-crafted. Could you elaborate on this aspect of your training procedure?
3. How much does the initial architecture used to bootstrap the controller affect the final result? In your control experiment with random search, did you use the same initial architecture as the RL approach, or were they completely independent? Understanding the role of human bootstrapping would help assess the true novelty of your approach.
