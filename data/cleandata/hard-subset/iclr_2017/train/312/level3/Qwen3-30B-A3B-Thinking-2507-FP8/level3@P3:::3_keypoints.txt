- Introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS): an RNN controller trained with reinforcement learning to generate neural network architectures.  
- Represents network architectures as variable-length strings produced sequentially by the controller.  
- Updates controller parameters via policy gradients using validation accuracy of sampled architectures.  
- Demonstrates competitive performance: 3.65% test error on CIFAR-10 and 62.4 test perplexity on Penn Treebank, slightly improving previous state-of-the-art.  
- Generated architectures resemble human-designed ones (e.g., many skip connections), suggesting the method captures meaningful design principles.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Well-motivated and properly situated in literature, addressing limitations of hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis.  
- Empirically rigorous: comprehensive results, ablation studies, comparisons to random search, and clear experimental details.  
- Core idea is promising with potential impact and demonstrated transferability to other tasks (language modeling, translation).  
- Major limitation: very high computational cost (e.g., 800 GPUs for 12,800 CIFAR-10 architectures), acknowledged by authors.  
- Suggested improvement: provide detailed analysis of computational cost versus benefits compared to human-designed models.  
- Suggested improvement: broaden experiments (e.g., ImageNet, other sequential tasks) to demonstrate generality.  
- Suggested improvement: discuss and propose ways to expand the search space while maintaining tractability.  
- Questions for authors: How does NAS training time scale with dataset size/complexity and applicability to datasets like ImageNet?  
- Questions for authors: Which specific design principles has the controller learned that align with human expertise, and do they generalize across domains?  
- Questions for authors: How interpretable is the NAS-generated Penn Treebank cell compared to human-designed cells, and why does it work well?  
- Questions for authors: What architectural features (depth, width, skip connections, etc.) does the controller prioritize and how do these priorities evolve during learning?
