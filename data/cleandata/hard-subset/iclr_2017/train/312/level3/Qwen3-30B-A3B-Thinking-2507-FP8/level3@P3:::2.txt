

**Review for "Neural Architecture Search"**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a novel reinforcement learning approach to automatically design neural network architectures. The authors propose framing neural architecture search as a reinforcement learning problem where a recurrent neural network (the controller) generates architectural hyperparameters as a variable-length sequence. The controller learns to maximize the expected validation accuracy of the generated architectures through policy gradient updates. Key contributions include: (1) treating neural architecture search as a sequence generation problem, (2) introducing a two-layer LSTM controller that functions as both actor and critic, (3) demonstrating state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 (3.65% test error, 0.09% better than previous state-of-the-art while being 1.05x faster) and Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity, 3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art), and (4) providing detailed analysis of the discovered architectures, revealing interesting differences from human-designed ones.

**2. Decision**

Accept. The paper presents a novel, well-motivated approach that achieves state-of-the-art results on two major benchmarks with strong empirical evidence supporting the claims.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper addresses a significant challenge in deep learning: the time-consuming, expert-intensive process of manually designing neural network architectures. The authors clearly position their work within the literature, providing a thorough related work section that acknowledges limitations of existing methods (fixed-length hyperparameter optimization, slow neuro-evolution approaches) and positioning their reinforcement learning approach as a more flexible and effective solution.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and scientifically rigorous. The authors demonstrate consistent performance improvements across two distinct domains (image classification and language modeling), with thorough ablation studies including control experiments with expanded search spaces and comparisons against random search. The results are clearly presented with appropriate baselines, and the discovered architectures provide valuable insights into alternative design patterns (e.g., the CIFAR-10 architecture's preference for larger filters at top layers and extensive skip connections).

The method is clearly described with sufficient detail for reproducibility, including the controller architecture, reward function, training procedure, and search space specifications. The authors demonstrate thoughtful improvements to the core approach, such as incorporating skip connections and expanding the search space to include diverse layer types.

**4. Additional Feedback and Questions**

While the paper is excellent, I have a few suggestions to enhance it further:

- Please provide additional details about training time and the correlation between compute/time and model quality. Specifically, how much total compute was required to discover the architectures, and how this compares to human-designed architectures? This would help contextualize the practicality of the approach.

- The authors mention using human-bootstrapped models in their introduction but don't provide results for this comparison in the main experiments. Please include results showing how much the initial human-designed model contributes to the final performance, and whether the method can discover good architectures without any human guidance.

- The paper states the controller is a two-layer LSTM used as both controller and critic, but doesn't clarify the specific implementation of the critic component. Please provide more details on how the critic functions in the actor-critic framework.

- The transfer learning results on character language modeling are impressive but lack implementation details. Please briefly explain the transfer process to help readers understand how the cell generalizes to different tasks.

- The paper mentions that the discovered architectures differ from human-designed ones in interesting ways. Please provide more insight into why these differences exist (e.g., why the CIFAR-10 architecture prefers larger filters at top layers, or why the recurrent cell has similarities to LSTM in early steps).

Overall, this is an outstanding paper that makes a significant contribution to automated machine learning. The method is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated with strong results. The authors have provided a clear and detailed description of their approach, and the results are scientifically rigorous. I believe this paper would be of great interest to the ICLR community and should be accepted.
