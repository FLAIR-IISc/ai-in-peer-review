

# Review for ICLR_2017

## 1. Summary

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a method that uses a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate neural network architectures, trained with reinforcement learning to maximize the expected validation accuracy of the generated architectures. The controller generates variable-length strings representing architectural hyperparameters, which are then evaluated on a validation set. The approach is applied to two key tasks: image classification on CIFAR-10 and language modeling on Penn Treebank. The method discovers novel architectures that outperform existing human-designed models, achieving a test error rate of 3.65% on CIFAR-10 (0.09% better than previous state-of-the-art) and a perplexity of 62.4 on Penn Treebank (3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art).

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning by introducing a promising method for architecture search that demonstrates strong empirical results on two important benchmarks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses a meaningful problem in machine learning: the difficulty of designing effective neural network architectures, which currently requires significant expert knowledge and time. The proposed Neural Architecture Search approach is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature, with a thorough review of related work on hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis.

The key strengths of the paper include:
- The RNN+RL approach to architecture generation is novel and well-executed, with a clear explanation of the controller's operation and training procedure.
- The results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are impressive, with the discovered architectures outperforming previous state-of-the-art human-designed models.
- The method is flexible enough to generate architectures with skip connections and other complex structures, which are common in modern neural networks.
- The paper provides a detailed experimental evaluation with sufficient ablation studies and control experiments.

The main concern is the substantial computational cost of training the controller (12,800 architectures trained), which may limit the practical applicability of the approach. However, the paper demonstrates that the discovered architectures are competitive with human-designed ones and even outperform them in some cases, suggesting the method has real value.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, the authors could:
- Include more detailed comparisons with recent neural architecture search methods (e.g., DARTS, ENAS) to better contextualize their contribution.
- Provide more thorough analysis of why the discovered architectures work well, including visualizations of the architecture structures and comparison with human-designed architectures.
- Discuss the computational limitations of the approach more explicitly, including the resources required and potential avenues for improvement.
- Include additional experiments on a different dataset or task to demonstrate the generalizability of the approach.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper demonstrates strong results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, but how would this approach scale to larger datasets like ImageNet or more complex tasks like object detection? Would the same architectural patterns discovered on smaller benchmarks generalize to these larger tasks?

2. The paper mentions that the discovered architectures resemble human-designed ones. Could the authors elaborate on this similarity and provide a more detailed comparison of the discovered architectures with human-designed ones (e.g., ResNet, LSTM) in terms of structure, complexity, and performance?

3. The training process requires significant computational resources (800 GPUs for 12,800 architectures). How does the computational cost compare to other neural architecture search methods, and what are the potential bottlenecks that could be addressed to make this approach more practical for broader adoption?

4. The paper mentions that the discovered recurrent cell can be transferred to character language modeling tasks. Could the authors provide more details on the transfer learning results, including how the architecture was adapted for the different task and whether the same performance gains were observed?
