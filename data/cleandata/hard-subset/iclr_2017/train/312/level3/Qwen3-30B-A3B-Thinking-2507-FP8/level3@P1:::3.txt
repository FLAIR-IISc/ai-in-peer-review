

This paper presents a novel approach to neural architecture design using reinforcement learning. The authors propose a controller RNN that generates neural network architectures as variable-length strings, with the controller trained using policy gradients to maximize validation accuracy. The method is applied to both convolutional architectures for image classification on CIFAR-10 and recurrent cell architectures for language modeling on Penn Treebank. The key contribution is the demonstration that a purely data-driven approach can design architectures that rival or surpass human-engineered models, achieving state-of-the-art results on both tasks.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The core idea is both elegant and promising, and the empirical results are strong and well-documented. The method successfully generates architectures that are not only competitive with human-designed models but also reveal interesting structural similarities to established approaches like ResNet and LSTM, suggesting the method can effectively leverage human design intuition.

The main strength of the paper lies in its well-motivated approach to the problem of architecture search. The authors correctly identify that most existing methods are limited to fixed-length hyperparameter spaces, while the proposed controller RNN can flexibly search variable-length architectures. The connection to related work in program synthesis and reinforcement learning for non-differentiable metrics is well-articulated, and the method is clearly positioned as an advancement over both hyperparameter optimization and neuro-evolution approaches.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous, with thorough ablation studies and careful attention to experimental details. The CIFAR-10 results (3.65% test error) demonstrate a clear improvement over previous state-of-the-art models, with the added benefit of being 1.05x faster. Similarly, the Penn Treebank results (62.4 perplexity) represent a significant improvement over previous models, with the added benefit of being more computationally efficient. The transfer learning results to character language modeling and machine translation (WMT14) further strengthen the case for the generality of the discovered architectures.

The main concerns I have are related to the computational demands and the limited scope of experiments. While the paper acknowledges the need for substantial computational resources (800 GPUs for CIFAR-10), the authors do not sufficiently address whether this is a practical limitation for wider adoption. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational cost versus the performance gains, as this is a critical consideration for real-world applications.

Additionally, while the two benchmark tasks are well-chosen, the paper would be stronger with experiments on additional datasets, particularly for the sequential model case. The authors should demonstrate that the method can generalize to other language modeling tasks or different types of sequential data to better establish the generality of their approach.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating that neural architectures can be effectively discovered through reinforcement learning, rather than relying solely on human expertise. The proposed method is both theoretically sound and empirically effective, and the results suggest it could become a valuable tool for neural network design.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend addressing the following points:

1. Include a more detailed analysis of the computational cost versus the performance gains. How much faster is the discovered architecture compared to human-designed models? What is the total computational cost of discovering the architecture versus designing it manually?

2. Expand the experimental evaluation to include at least one additional dataset for each task (e.g., another image classification dataset like ImageNet or another language modeling dataset like WikiText).

3. Provide more insight into why the discovered architectures resemble human-designed ones. Is this due to the structure of the search space, or does the RL approach naturally discover similar patterns?

4. Discuss the limitations of the method more thoroughly, particularly regarding the computational requirements and potential for overfitting to the specific validation set used for training the controller.

5. Consider adding a comparison with more recent architecture search methods that might have been published after the initial submission but before the conference.

The authors have provided a compelling demonstration of neural architecture search using reinforcement learning, and I believe the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent addition to ICLR 2017.
