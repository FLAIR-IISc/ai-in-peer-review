

# Review of "Neural Architecture Search" (ICLR 2017)

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel approach for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (RNN) as a controller trained with reinforcement learning. The authors observe that neural network structures can be represented as variable-length strings, making them suitable for generation by an RNN. The controller RNN generates architectural hyperparameters sequentially, and its parameters are updated using policy gradients based on the validation accuracy of the generated architectures. The paper demonstrates that NAS can design competitive architectures on two challenging benchmarks: CIFAR-10 (achieving 3.65% test error, 0.09% better than previous state-of-the-art) and Penn Treebank (achieving 62.4 test perplexity, 3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art). Crucially, the generated architectures resemble human-designed ones, suggesting compatibility with human expertise in neural network design.

## 2. Decision
Accept.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning by introducing a novel approach to architecture search that is flexible and capable of generating competitive architectures from scratch. The three key reasons for acceptance are:

First, the paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors clearly position their work relative to existing approaches in hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis, correctly identifying limitations of these methods that NAS addresses. The approach of using an RNN to generate variable-length architectural descriptions is both novel and logically sound.

Second, the empirical results are scientifically rigorous within the scope of the paper's experiments. The authors provide comprehensive results on two well-established benchmarks, with thorough ablation studies (including control experiments on search space expansion and comparison against random search). The results are presented with appropriate statistical rigor, and the paper provides clear details about the experimental setup.

Third, the paper's core idea is promising and has significant potential impact. The fact that the generated architectures resemble human-designed ones (e.g., the CIFAR-10 architecture has many skip connections like ResNet) suggests the approach is capturing meaningful design principles. The transfer learning results on character language modeling and machine translation further demonstrate the generality of the discovered architectures.

While the paper acknowledges the long training time (requiring 800 GPUs for 12,800 architectures on CIFAR-10), this limitation is well-documented and the authors provide a reasonable explanation for the computational demands given the approach. The paper's contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance despite this limitation.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper for future publication, the authors could:

1. Provide more detailed analysis of the computational cost versus benefits. A clear comparison of the total compute required for NAS versus human-designed models would help contextualize the trade-offs.

2. Include a broader set of experiments to demonstrate generality. While the transfer learning results are promising, additional experiments on other image recognition datasets (e.g., ImageNet) or different sequential tasks would better support claims of general applicability.

3. Consider addressing potential limitations of the search space. The paper notes that their search space is limited by the controller's ability to predict certain architectural features. A discussion of how to systematically expand the search space while maintaining tractability would be valuable.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. How does the training time of NAS scale with dataset size and complexity? Could the approach be reasonably applied to larger datasets like ImageNet with current computing resources?

2. The paper mentions that the generated architectures resemble human-designed ones. Could you elaborate on specific design principles that the NAS controller appears to have "learned" that align with human expertise, and how these might generalize to other domains?

3. In the Penn Treebank experiments, the authors achieved state-of-the-art results on character language modeling. How does the NAS-generated cell compare to human-designed cells in terms of interpretability and understanding of why it works well?

4. The paper states that the controller RNN generates architectures from scratch. What specific architectural features does the controller prioritize (e.g., depth, width, skip connections), and how do these priorities change as the controller learns?
