

**Review**

This paper presents Neural Architecture Search, a reinforcement learning approach for automatically designing neural network architectures. The authors propose using a controller RNN to generate architectural descriptions, which are then evaluated on validation sets and used to update the controller via policy gradients. The method achieves strong results on both vision (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank) benchmarks, discovering novel architectures that outperform human-designed ones.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The core contribution is well-motivated and addresses an important problem in deep learning. The approach is scientifically rigorous, with thorough experiments on multiple benchmarks demonstrating clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods. The paper's strength lies in its ability to explore beyond standard RNN/CNN search spaces and discover dataset-specific architectures without manual engineering.

I have one significant concern regarding reproducibility. The paper lacks specific details about the exact hyperparameters used, particularly regarding dropout types (recurrent, embedding, etc.) and other critical configuration details. Without these, replication requires excessive trial-and-error. The authors should provide a base set of hyperparameters as suggested in Zaremba et al. (2014) and include complete hyperparameter specifications for all experiments, including the character language modeling task. This is crucial for reproducibility and future research.

The paper is well-written with strong results and makes a valuable contribution to the field. The authors' acknowledgment that computational costs will likely decrease over time is also appropriately placed in the discussion of the method's practicality.

To improve the paper, I recommend:
1. Providing complete hyperparameter details for all experiments
2. Releasing a base configuration file with standard hyperparameters
3. Including more information about dropout implementation (types, rates, and application points)

These additions would significantly enhance the paper's reproducibility and impact without requiring major revisions to the core methodology. The authors have made a strong contribution that merits publication, and these minor additions would make it even more valuable to the research community.
