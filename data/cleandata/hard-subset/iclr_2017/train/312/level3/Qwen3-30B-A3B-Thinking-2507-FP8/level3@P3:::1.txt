

# Review of "Neural Architecture Search" (ICLR 2017)

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a novel approach that uses a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to automatically generate neural network architectures. The controller is trained via reinforcement learning to maximize the expected accuracy of the generated architectures on a validation set. The method is particularly noteworthy for its ability to search variable-length architecture spaces, which is a significant advancement over previous hyperparameter optimization methods that typically operate on fixed-length spaces. The authors demonstrate impressive results on both vision and language tasks: on CIFAR-10, their method designs a novel convolutional architecture achieving 3.65% test error rate (0.09% better than the previous state-of-the-art), and on Penn Treebank, it discovers a recurrent cell achieving 62.4 test perplexity (3.6 better than the previous state-of-the-art). The paper makes an important contribution to automating neural network design, a process that currently requires substantial expert knowledge and time.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to an important problem in deep learning, with compelling empirical results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully addresses a critical problem in deep learning: the high cost and expertise required to design effective neural network architectures. The authors provide a well-motivated approach that is clearly placed in the literature, with thoughtful discussion of related work in hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis. The method's flexibility in searching variable-length architectures represents a meaningful advancement over previous approaches.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous and well-supported. The authors evaluate on two major benchmark datasets (CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank), demonstrating the approach's versatility across different domains. The results on both tasks are strong, with the discovered architectures outperforming human-designed ones. The paper also provides valuable insights into the discovered architectures (e.g., the preference for rectangular filters and skip connections in the CIFAR-10 model, and the similarities to LSTM cells in the recurrent architecture), which helps understand the method's effectiveness.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to results. The authors have also made a commendable effort to provide implementation details and will release code to facilitate replication.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further improve the paper, I recommend the following:

1. The paper would benefit from releasing a base set of hyperparameters (as suggested by Zaremba et al., 2014) to aid future work and reproducibility. The current paper lacks sufficient detail on hyperparameters, making replication challenging without extensive trial-and-error.

2. Please provide the exact hyperparameters used for all experiments, including the specific types and configurations of dropout (recurrent, embedding, etc.) used in the language modeling experiments. This information is critical for reproducibility.

3. The paper mentions the character language modeling task but does not provide detailed hyperparameter information for this experiment. Please include these details to allow for proper replication of all results.

## 5. Questions for Authors

To clarify my understanding and ensure confidence in the assessment, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1. Could you please provide the exact hyperparameters used for the language modeling experiments, including the specific dropout types (recurrent, embedding, etc.) and their configurations?

2. Will you release a base set of hyperparameters (as in Zaremba et al., 2014) to facilitate future work and reproducibility?

3. Could you provide the hyperparameter details for the character language modeling experiment mentioned in Section 4.2?

4. The paper mentions that "the controller RNN has to select a filter height in [1, 3, 5, 7]" for CIFAR-10. Could you clarify whether these are the only filter sizes considered, or if there were additional filter sizes in the search space?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning and represents an important step toward reducing the expertise and time required to design effective neural networks. The results are compelling, the method is well-motivated, and the paper is well-written. With the addition of the requested hyperparameter details, this paper will be an excellent contribution to ICLR 2017.
