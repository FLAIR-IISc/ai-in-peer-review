

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a method that uses a recurrent neural network (controller) trained with reinforcement learning to automatically generate neural network architectures. The controller produces variable-length architectural descriptions, and the resulting networks are evaluated on a validation set with accuracy serving as the reward signal for improving the controller. The authors demonstrate strong results on CIFAR-10 (achieving 3.65% test error, 0.09% better than previous state-of-the-art) and Penn Treebank (achieving 62.4 perplexity, 3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art). Notably, the generated architectures resemble human-designed ones, suggesting compatibility with human expertise.

I recommend acceptance with major revision. The core idea is promising and the results on the two datasets are solid. However, the paper lacks evaluation on additional datasets to demonstrate generality, which is a significant limitation. The authors should evaluate their method on at least one more dataset to show broader applicability beyond image recognition and language modeling.

The paper also doesn't adequately address the long training time, which is a practical limitation of the approach. While this may be inherent to the method, the authors should discuss this limitation more thoroughly and compare computational costs with human-designed architectures.

Additional feedback: Please provide more detailed comparison with human-designed models to demonstrate the advantage of automatically generated architectures. Include ablation studies to show the impact of different components of your method. Consider adding a discussion of the computational cost versus the performance gain.

Questions for authors: How would your method perform on a different type of task (e.g., object detection or segmentation)? Could you provide more details on the computational resources required compared to human-designed architectures?
