- Proposes a novel controller RNN that generates variable-length neural architectures and is trained with policy gradients to maximize validation accuracy.  
- Applies the method to convolutional architectures on CIFAR-10 and recurrent cells on Penn Treebank, achieving state-of-the-art results.  
- Empirical highlights: CIFAR-10 test error 3.65% (1.05x faster) and Penn Treebank perplexity 62.4, plus successful transfer to character LM and WMT14 translation.  
- Key contribution: demonstrates that a purely data-driven RL approach can design architectures rivaling or surpassing human-engineered models.  
- Strength: flexibly searches variable-length architectures, advancing beyond fixed-length hyperparameter and neuro-evolution methods.  
- Strength: clear positioning relative to related work in program synthesis and non-differentiable RL metrics.  
- Strength: rigorous experiments with thorough ablation studies and careful experimental detail.  
- Observation: discovered architectures show structural similarities to ResNet and LSTM, suggesting alignment with human design intuition.  
- Major concern: extremely high computational demands (e.g., 800 GPUs for CIFAR-10) may limit practical adoption.  
- Concern: limited scope of experiments—more datasets needed, especially additional sequential tasks, to demonstrate generality.  
- Requested revision 1: provide detailed analysis of computational cost versus performance gains and total discovery cost.  
- Requested revision 2: expand experiments to at least one additional dataset per task (e.g., ImageNet, WikiText).  
- Requested revision 3: analyze why discovered architectures resemble human designs—search space vs RL bias.  
- Requested revision 4: discuss limitations more thoroughly, including compute requirements and potential overfitting to the validation set.  
- Requested revision 5: compare against more recent architecture search methods published since initial submission.
