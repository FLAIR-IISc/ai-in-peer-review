

# Review for ICLR_2017

## 1. Summary

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a novel approach for automatically designing neural network architectures using reinforcement learning. The authors propose a controller recurrent neural network that generates architectural hyperparameters as a sequence of tokens, which are then used to construct a "child" network. The controller is trained via policy gradient to maximize the validation accuracy of the child networks. The method is particularly significant for its ability to search variable-length architecture spaces, which previous hyperparameter optimization methods could not effectively handle. The authors demonstrate impressive results on two challenging benchmarks: achieving 3.65% test error on CIFAR-10 (outperforming previous state-of-the-art by 0.09% and being 1.05x faster), and achieving 62.4 perplexity on Penn Treebank (3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art). The method also successfully discovers novel recurrent cell architectures that outperform standard LSTM cells and transfer well to character language modeling tasks.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural architecture search with strong empirical results, well-motivated approach, and careful experimental design.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully addresses an important problem in machine learning: the manual design of neural network architectures remains time-consuming and requires significant expertise. The authors provide a well-motivated solution that is properly situated within the literature, with a thoughtful discussion of related work in hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis.

The paper's claims are strongly supported by rigorous empirical results. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements across two distinct domains (vision and language), with careful ablation studies showing the effectiveness of their approach. The CIFAR-10 results are particularly compelling, with the discovered architecture achieving better accuracy than human-designed architectures while being faster to compute. The PTB results further validate the approach with a 3.6 perplexity improvement over the previous state-of-the-art, and the transfer learning results to character language modeling provide additional evidence of the method's generalizability.

The method is scientifically rigorous with appropriate baselines and thorough validation. The control experiments (including random search comparisons and expanding the search space) provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the approach. The paper also makes a strong case for the practical utility of the method with the successful integration into the GNMT framework.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is well-written and the results are compelling, the authors could significantly improve reproducibility by providing more detailed hyperparameter information. The current description lacks specific details about dropout types (recurrent, embedding, etc.) and their exact rates used in the PTB experiments. This information would be valuable for future work to replicate and build upon the results. I recommend releasing a base set of hyperparameters (as done in Zaremba et al., 2014) to aid the community in reproducing the results.

Similarly, the hyperparameter details for the character language modeling experiments should be provided to ensure full reproducibility. The paper mentions these experiments but doesn't specify the exact settings used, which would be helpful for readers attempting to replicate or extend these results.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. Could the authors provide the exact hyperparameters used in the PTB experiments, particularly regarding the specific types and rates of dropout (embedding, recurrent, etc.)?

2. Could they share a base set of hyperparameters for replication, similar to what Zaremba et al. (2014) did for their LSTM experiments, to help the community reproduce these results more easily?

3. For the character language modeling experiments, could the authors provide the exact hyperparameters used, including dropout rates, learning rates, and other key settings?

These details would significantly enhance the reproducibility of the work and allow other researchers to more easily build upon this promising approach to neural architecture search. The paper's strong empirical results and novel methodology make it a valuable contribution to the field, and providing these additional details would further strengthen its impact.
