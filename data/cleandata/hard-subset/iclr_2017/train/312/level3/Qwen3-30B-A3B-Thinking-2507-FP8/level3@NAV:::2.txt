

# Review

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a novel reinforcement learning approach for automatically designing neural network architectures. The authors propose treating DNNs as variable-length sequences, framing node selection as RL actions, and using architecture evaluation error as reward. They employ an auto-regressive two-layer LSTM as both controller and critic, enabling the search of variable-length architecture spaces. The method is evaluated on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, demonstrating architectures that rival or surpass human-designed models in performance while offering computational advantages.

I recommend acceptance. This is an excellent and exciting paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neural architecture search. The approach is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental challenge in deep learning—hand-designing effective architectures. The empirical results are strong and demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods on two important benchmark tasks.

The paper's strengths include its clear technical exposition, comprehensive experimental evaluation, and compelling results showing that the automatically designed architectures can outperform human-designed ones. The authors provide a detailed description of their method and carefully address related work, making a strong case for their novel approach.

For further improvement, I suggest including more details about training time and computational efficiency, as well as clarifying the role of human-bootstrapped models in the search process. Specifically:

1. Please provide data on training time required for the controller and child networks, and the correlation between compute/time and model quality.
2. Please elaborate on the results and role of human-bootstrapped models in the search process—what would happen if they weren't used?

These additions would strengthen the paper's contribution by providing a more complete picture of the practical implications and limitations of the approach. The current work is already strong, but these details would help readers better understand the method's practical applicability and potential for broader adoption.
