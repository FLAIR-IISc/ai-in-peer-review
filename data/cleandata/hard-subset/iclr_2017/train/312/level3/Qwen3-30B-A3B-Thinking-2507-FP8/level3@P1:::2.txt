

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel reinforcement learning approach for automatically designing neural network architectures. The authors frame the problem as generating variable-length architectural descriptions using a recurrent controller network, trained via policy gradients to maximize validation accuracy. The key innovation lies in using a controller RNN to iteratively propose architectures, with the child network's validation performance serving as the reward signal. The method demonstrates impressive results on both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank) benchmarks, discovering architectures that rival or surpass human-designed models.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural architecture design with a well-motivated, novel approach that effectively addresses the challenges of manual architecture design. The method's strong empirical performance on two major benchmarks provides compelling evidence for its effectiveness.

The paper excels in several critical areas. First, the authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing hyperparameter optimization and neuro-evolution methods, positioning their work as a flexible solution that can search variable-length architectural spaces. The method's use of a controller RNN to generate architectural descriptions as sequences is both elegant and well-justified. Second, the experimental design is thorough and well-executed, with careful attention to proper validation procedures and fair comparisons against state-of-the-art baselines. The CIFAR-10 results (3.65% test error, 1.05x faster than previous SOTA) and Penn Treebank results (62.4 perplexity, 3.6 better than previous SOTA) are impressive and well-supported by the evidence. Third, the authors provide valuable insights into the characteristics of the discovered architectures, noting differences from human-designed models (e.g., preference for rectangular filters, use of skip connections) and demonstrating transfer learning capabilities.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with comprehensive coverage of related work that thoughtfully situates the contribution within the broader landscape of neural architecture search. The technical details of the controller RNN, the policy gradient training procedure, and the extension to recurrent architectures are presented with appropriate mathematical rigor and clarity. The implementation details regarding distributed training and parallelism are particularly valuable for reproducibility.

For additional improvements, I recommend the authors provide more explicit details about the computational requirements of their method. While they mention distributed training with 800 GPUs for CIFAR-10, it would be helpful to include the total training time (e.g., how many GPU-days) to give readers a better sense of the practical feasibility. Additionally, it would be valuable to include a more detailed analysis of the correlation between computational resources (e.g., number of architectures evaluated) and model quality, particularly for the CIFAR-10 experiments where the controller evaluated 12,800 architectures.

The authors should also clarify whether any human expertise was used to bootstrap the search space or guide the controller during training. While the paper states they start from scratch, the search space definition (e.g., filter sizes, layer types) might implicitly incorporate human knowledge. A brief discussion of the extent to which human input shaped the search space would strengthen the claim of "starting from scratch."

I would be interested to see results from a controlled experiment where the authors compare their method against a version that incorporates human-designed architectures as initial candidates. This would help clarify whether the discovered architectures represent a true improvement over human design or simply a more systematic exploration of the same space.

The paper's transfer learning results on character language modeling and machine translation are particularly compelling, demonstrating the generalizability of the discovered cell. I encourage the authors to include a brief analysis of why their cell outperforms LSTMs, especially regarding the computational efficiency gains (e.g., "2x faster" for the PTB task).

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a substantial contribution to the field. The method is novel, well-executed, and produces compelling results. The paper is clearly written, thoroughly evaluated, and provides valuable insights into the design of neural network architectures. The authors have made a strong case for the effectiveness of their approach through multiple benchmarks and careful experimental design.
