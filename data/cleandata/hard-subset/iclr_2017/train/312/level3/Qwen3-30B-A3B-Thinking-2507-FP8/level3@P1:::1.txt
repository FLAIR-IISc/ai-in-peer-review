

This paper presents Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel approach to automatically discovering neural network architectures using reinforcement learning. The authors propose a controller RNN that generates neural network architectures as variable-length strings, which are then evaluated on a validation set. The controller is trained via policy gradients to maximize the expected validation accuracy of the generated architectures. The method demonstrates impressive results on both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank) tasks, finding architectures that outperform human-designed models. On CIFAR-10, NAS discovers a convolutional network achieving 3.65% test error (0.09% better than previous state-of-the-art) while being 1.05x faster. On Penn Treebank, NAS discovers a novel recurrent cell achieving 62.4 test perplexity (3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art), which also transfers well to character language modeling and machine translation tasks.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The primary reasons for acceptance are: (1) NAS addresses a significant challenge in deep learning - automating the architecture design process - which has traditionally required substantial expert knowledge, and (2) the empirical results are compelling across multiple benchmarks with strong statistical significance.

The paper is exceptionally well-motivated and well-positioned within the literature. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches (hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, program synthesis) and demonstrate how their method overcomes these limitations by enabling flexible search in variable-length architecture spaces. The connection to sequence-to-sequence learning and BLEU optimization in machine translation is particularly insightful. The methodology is sound, with careful attention to practical implementation details like parallelism, asynchronous updates, and handling skip connections.

The empirical results are robust and well-validated. The authors provide comprehensive experiments across two fundamentally different domains (computer vision and natural language processing), demonstrating the versatility of their approach. The NAS-generated architectures consistently outperform human-designed baselines, and the improvements are statistically significant. The transfer learning experiments on character language modeling and machine translation further demonstrate the practical utility of the discovered architectures. The control experiments (adding more functions to the search space, comparison against random search) provide strong evidence that the approach is not merely finding a lucky architecture but is systematically discovering better ones.

For further improvement, I recommend the authors provide more detailed hyperparameter information to facilitate replication. While the paper mentions "the same settings used in Huang et al. (2016a)" for CIFAR-10 training, the exact hyperparameters (learning rate schedule, weight decay, momentum values, etc.) would be helpful for reproducibility. Similarly, for the Penn Treebank experiments, the authors should specify exactly which dropout techniques (recurrent, embedding, variational) were used in the final models, as these can significantly impact results. Providing a base set of hyperparameters (as Zaremba et al. did for LSTM) would be particularly valuable for the community.

Additionally, the authors should clarify the exact computational requirements of their approach. While the paper mentions training on 800 GPUs for CIFAR-10, it would be helpful to provide more specific details about the total computational cost (e.g., GPU-hours) to understand the practical feasibility of the method.

I have two specific questions for the authors to clarify:
1) For the CIFAR-10 experiments, could the authors provide the exact hyperparameters used for training the child networks (beyond "the same settings as Huang et al. 2016a")? This would help understand the reproducibility of their results.
2) For the Penn Treebank language modeling experiments, could the authors specify whether they used embedding dropout, recurrent dropout, or both in their final model, and how these were configured? This is particularly important given the sensitivity of language modeling results to these regularization techniques.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and results. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating that neural networks can effectively search for neural network architectures, moving beyond human-designed models. While the computational cost is currently high, this is likely to decrease as hardware and techniques improve, making this approach increasingly practical. The paper's impact on the field will be significant, as it opens up new directions for automated neural architecture design.
