### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel and ambitious method for automating the design of neural network architectures. The core contribution is to frame architecture generation as a learning problem. A controller, implemented as a recurrent neural network (RNN), is trained to generate variable-length strings that specify the architecture of a "child" network. This controller is optimized using reinforcement learning, with the validation performance of the trained child network serving as the reward signal. This policy gradient approach allows the controller to progressively discover better architectures over time.

The authors demonstrate the power of this method on two highly competitive and distinct benchmarks. For image classification on CIFAR-10, NAS discovers a novel convolutional architecture from scratch that achieves a new state-of-the-art test error of 3.65%, outperforming contemporaneous human-designed models while also being computationally faster. For language modeling on Penn Treebank, the method designs a novel recurrent cell that surpasses the performance of the widely-used and highly-tuned LSTM cell, achieving a state-of-the-art perplexity of 62.4. The paper further shows that this discovered cell generalizes well, achieving state-of-the-art results when transferred to character-level language modeling and showing promising improvements in a machine translation task.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The paper presents a highly original and elegant approach to automating architecture design. By using an RNN controller trained with reinforcement learning, it moves beyond traditional hyperparameter optimization methods, enabling a flexible and scalable search over a complex, variable-length space of architectures. This work opens up a significant new research direction in meta-learning.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The claims are strongly supported by impressive results on two challenging and well-established benchmarks. Discovering architectures from scratch that outperform meticulously human-engineered models like DenseNet (on CIFAR-10) and LSTM (on Penn Treebank) is a major achievement and provides compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's primary strength is its successful formulation of architecture design as a learning problem. The use of an RNN to autoregressively generate architecture specifications is a powerful concept, and training it with the REINFORCE algorithm is a natural fit for optimizing the non-differentiable validation accuracy metric. The technical details, such as the attention-based mechanism for generating skip connections and the tree-based representation for designing recurrent cells, are thoughtful solutions to the complexities of the search space. The control experiment comparing against random search effectively demonstrates that the policy gradient method is genuinely learning to navigate the search space and is not simply a product of extensive computation.

The empirical validation is thorough and convincing. On CIFAR-10, the discovered architecture achieves an error rate of 3.65%, which is a top-tier result. More impressively, on Penn Treebank, the method discovers a novel recurrent cell that improves upon the previous state-of-the-art by a significant margin of 3.6 perplexity. The successful transfer of this cell to character-level language modeling and its application in the GNMT framework suggest that the discovered architecture captures fundamental computational principles that are not merely overfitted to a single task. The fact that the generated CNN architecture exhibits features common in human designs, such as skip connections, lends further credence to the search process.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the work is excellent, the following points could be addressed to further strengthen the paper. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from the decision to accept.

*   **Computational Cost:** The most significant concern is the immense computational resources required (e.g., 800 GPUs concurrently for the CIFAR-10 search). This makes the method inaccessible to the vast majority of the research community. While the results justify the expense in this foundational work, a discussion of the search efficiency and potential avenues for reducing this computational barrier (e.g., via weight sharing or proxy tasks) would be highly valuable for future work in this area.
*   **Generality:** The experiments are convincing but limited to two datasets. To further solidify the claim of generality, it would be beneficial to see results on other domains or datasets. For example, evaluating the CIFAR-10 discovered architecture on CIFAR-100 or SVHN, or applying the RNN cell search to a different sequential task, would provide stronger evidence that the method is broadly applicable.
*   **Analysis of Discovered Architectures:** The paper provides visualizations of the discovered architectures, which is very helpful. The analysis could be deepened by providing more intuition or investigation into *why* these specific architectures perform so well. For instance, what specific motifs or patterns has the controller learned to favor, and how do they differ between the CNN and RNN search spaces?

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points in their response:

1.  Could you provide an estimate of the total computational cost (e.g., in GPU-days) for a complete search run on CIFAR-10?
2.  The search process trains child networks for a fixed number of epochs (50 on CIFAR-10) to obtain the reward signal. How strong is the correlation between this short-term validation accuracy and the final performance of a model trained to convergence? Is there a risk that the search might be biased towards architectures that converge quickly rather than those with the highest potential final accuracy?
3.  How sensitive is the search process to the design of the search space itself? For example, how crucial were the specific choices for the set of available operations and activation functions in the recurrent cell search?
4.  Regarding the REINFORCE algorithm, which is known for its high variance, how stable was the training of the controller? Did you need to perform multiple runs of the controller's training to achieve these results?
