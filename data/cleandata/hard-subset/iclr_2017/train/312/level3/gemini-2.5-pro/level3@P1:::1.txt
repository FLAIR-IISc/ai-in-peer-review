### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces "Neural Architecture Search," a novel method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller. The core idea is to frame architecture design as a problem where the controller generates a string representing the network's structure. This "child network" is then trained on a target dataset, and its performance on a validation set is used as a reward signal to update the controller via reinforcement learning (specifically, the REINFORCE algorithm). This elegant approach allows the controller to learn over time to generate better-performing architectures.

The authors demonstrate the power and generality of their method on two very different and highly competitive benchmarks. For image classification on CIFAR-10, the method discovers a convolutional neural network that achieves a new state-of-the-art test error of 3.65%, outperforming top human-designed architectures while being faster. For language modeling on the Penn Treebank dataset, the method designs a novel recurrent cell that outperforms the widely-used LSTM and other state-of-the-art models, achieving a test perplexity of 62.4. The learned cell also demonstrates strong transferability, setting a new state-of-the-art on character-level language modeling and showing improvements when integrated into the GNMT machine translation framework.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Recommendation: Accept**

This is a strong paper that I recommend for acceptance. The key reasons for this decision are:
1.  **High Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles the fundamental and challenging problem of automating architecture design, a major bottleneck in the application of deep learning. The proposed RL-based approach is novel, well-motivated, and provides a general framework for exploring vast and complex architectural search spaces that go beyond standard hyperparameter optimization.
2.  **Excellent Empirical Results:** The method achieves state-of-the-art results on two highly competitive and distinct benchmarks (CIFAR-10 for vision, Penn Treebank for language). This demonstrates the effectiveness and versatility of the approach, showing it can discover both novel convolutional architectures and fundamental recurrent cell structures.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's main strength lies in its ambitious goal and the compelling evidence provided to support its claims. Automating architecture engineering is a long-standing goal in machine learning, and this work presents a significant step forward. The method is well-placed in the literature, correctly identifying the limitations of prior work in hyperparameter optimization (fixed-length spaces) and neuro-evolution (less scalable, often heuristic-driven). By framing the problem in an RL context, the authors are able to use policy gradients to systematically explore a variable-length, compositional space of architectures.

The empirical validation is thorough and impressive. The ability to discover an architecture from scratch that surpasses meticulously hand-tuned models like DenseNet on CIFAR-10 is a remarkable achievement. The analysis of the discovered architecture, noting its preference for rectangular filters and its use of skip connections, provides valuable insight. The comparison against a random search baseline in the PTB experiments is crucial and effectively demonstrates that the policy gradient method is learning a meaningful search strategy, rather than just getting lucky in a large search space.

Perhaps most convincingly, the experiments on the Penn Treebank dataset show the method can design a new, fundamental building block—a recurrent cell—that not only outperforms the LSTM on the primary task but also generalizes successfully to character-level modeling and machine translation. This suggests the controller is not merely overfitting to a specific dataset configuration but is discovering genuinely useful computational structures.

While the computational cost of the method is extremely high (e.g., training 800 networks concurrently on GPUs), this is acknowledged as a current limitation. Given the rapid improvements in hardware and distributed systems, this cost is likely to become more manageable over time. The significance of the problem being solved justifies the large-scale computational investment, and the paper serves as a strong proof-of-concept for this research direction.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The paper is well-written and the results are very strong. The following points are intended to help improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility, and are not critical for my decision to accept.

My primary concern is the lack of specific hyperparameter details for the child networks, which makes replication of the final, state-of-the-art models very difficult.
*   For the Penn Treebank experiments, the paper mentions using techniques like embedding dropout and recurrent dropout from Zaremba et al. (2014) and Gal (2015). However, the exact dropout rates, initialization schemes, learning rate schedules, and other crucial hyperparameters for the best-performing model are not specified. Reproducing the results would require an extensive and costly grid search. To aid reproducibility and future work that builds upon these findings, I would strongly encourage the authors to provide a base set of hyperparameters for the final reported models, similar to the standard set provided in Zaremba et al. (2014).
*   This request for clarity extends to the other experiments as well. For example, what were the specific hyperparameters used for the best character-level language model? For the final CIFAR-10 model, the paper mentions a "small grid search" was performed; it would be very helpful to know the ranges and final values from this search.

**Questions for the authors:**
1.  Could you please provide the exact hyperparameter configurations (learning rates, specific dropout types and rates, weight initialization, decay schedules, etc.) used to train the best-performing child models for the CIFAR-10 and the word-level and character-level Penn Treebank tasks?
2.  In Section 4.1, the reward is defined as the "maximum validation accuracy of the last 5 epochs cubed." What was the motivation for cubing the accuracy? Did you experiment with other reward transformations, and how sensitive is the controller's performance to the shape of the reward function?
