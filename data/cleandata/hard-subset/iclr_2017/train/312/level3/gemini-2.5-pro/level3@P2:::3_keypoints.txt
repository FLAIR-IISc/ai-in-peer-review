- Proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS): an RNN controller trained with reinforcement learning to generate variable-length architecture descriptions using child validation accuracy as reward.
- Demonstrates NAS on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving state-of-the-art results (CIFAR-10 test error 3.65%; PTB perplexity 62.4).
- Discovers a novel recurrent cell that outperforms LSTM and transfers well to character-level language modeling and machine translation.
- Frames architecture design as a principled learning/meta-learning problem, enabling flexible, scalable search over complex spaces.
- Uses REINFORCE policy gradient to optimize the controller and an attention-based mechanism for skip connections plus a tree representation for RNN cell design.
- Control experiments against random search indicate the controller genuinely learns to navigate the search space.
- Generated CNNs show human-like motifs (e.g., skip connections), supporting the quality of the search process.
- Decision: Accept, based on originality and strong empirical evidence.
- Major concern: extremely high computational cost (e.g., 800 GPUs), limiting accessibility for most researchers.
- Limited scope of experiments (only two datasets); broader evaluations (e.g., CIFAR-100, SVHN, other sequential tasks) would strengthen generality claims.
- Suggest deeper analysis of discovered architectures to explain which motifs/patterns the controller favors and why they work.
- Requests clarification on total computational cost (GPU-days) for CIFAR-10 search.
- Asks about correlation between short-duration child training (50 epochs) and final converged performance, and potential bias towards fast-converging architectures.
- Seeks details on sensitivity to search-space design choices (operations/activations) for RNN cell search.
- Queries stability of REINFORCE training, variance issues, and whether multiple controller runs were needed to obtain results.
