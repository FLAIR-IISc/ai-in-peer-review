This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel and ambitious method for automatically designing neural network architectures. The core idea is to frame the problem of architecture generation as a reinforcement learning task. A controller, implemented as a recurrent neural network (a two-layer LSTM), learns to generate variable-length strings that specify the architecture of a "child" network. This child network is then trained on a target task, and its performance (e.g., accuracy on a validation set) is used as a reward signal to update the controller's policy via policy gradients (REINFORCE). The paper details several crucial improvements to make this approach feasible, including a parallelized, asynchronous training scheme to manage the high computational cost and a clever mechanism using an attention-like model to generate complex topologies with skip connections. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on two challenging benchmarks: designing a convolutional network for CIFAR-10 and a recurrent cell for language modeling on Penn Treebank. In both cases, the automatically discovered architectures are shown to be competitive with, and in some cases surpass, state-of-the-art, human-designed models.

**Decision: Accept**

My recommendation is to accept this paper. The primary reasons for this decision are:
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a highly novel and significant approach to a fundamental problem in deep learning: architecture design. By framing this as a learning problem, it opens up a new and promising research direction that could fundamentally change how practitioners develop models.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The claims are supported by strong and convincing empirical results on two distinct and highly competitive benchmarks (CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank), demonstrating that the method can discover architectures that rival or exceed the performance of meticulously hand-crafted models.

**Supporting Arguments**

The problem of architecture engineering is a major bottleneck in the application of deep learning, requiring significant expert knowledge, intuition, and trial-and-error. This work moves beyond traditional hyperparameter optimization (like random search or Bayesian optimization) by treating the architecture itself as a variable-length, compositional structure that can be generated by another model. The use of a controller RNN trained with reinforcement learning is an elegant and powerful formulation for this problem, connecting it to the broader research area of meta-learning or "learning to learn." The authors provide a clear and detailed description of their method, which is well-motivated and properly situated within the context of related work in hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis.

The experimental setup is rigorous and the results are impressive. On CIFAR-10, the discovered architecture achieves a test error of 3.65%, which is state-of-the-art for models with a similar architectural scheme, and it does so with competitive inference speed. The analysis revealing that the controller discovers architectures with features like rectangular filters and numerous skip connections is insightful. On Penn Treebank, the discovery of a novel recurrent cell that outperforms the widely-used LSTM is particularly compelling, achieving a new state-of-the-art perplexity. The successful transfer of this cell to character-level language modeling and machine translation further strengthens the claim of its general utility. Crucially, the comparison against random search (Figure 6) clearly demonstrates that the controller is learning a meaningful search strategy rather than simply succeeding through brute force in a large search space.

**Additional Feedback**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to further improve the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Search Space Constraints:** While the method is very general, the practical application seems to require some careful construction of the search space (e.g., the decision to predict blocks of 3 layers for the final CIFAR-10 experiment, or fixing the base number for RNN cells). It would be beneficial to expand on the rationale behind these choices and discuss their impact on the search process and overall performance.
*   **Analysis of Discovered Architectures:** The analysis of the discovered architectures is interesting. Further analysis could provide more insights into what constitutes a "good" architecture. For instance, are there common motifs or patterns that the controller learns to favor across different runs or at different stages of its training? A deeper dive into the structure of the novel RNN cell and a comparison of its components to the gates of an LSTM would be very illuminating.

**Questions for Authors**

To help clarify my understanding and strengthen the paper, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  The computational cost of this method is clearly very high (e.g., 800 GPUs for the CIFAR-10 experiment). Could you provide more detail on the total training time (in terms of wall-clock time and total GPU-hours) required for the controller to converge? This would be invaluable for understanding the practical applicability of the method.
2.  Is there a clear correlation between the number of architectures sampled (i.e., total compute budget) and the quality of the final discovered model? The comparison against random search is excellent, but it would be interesting to see a learning curve showing the best-found architecture's performance as a function of the number of training steps for the controller.
3.  The paper states the method starts "from scratch." Did you experiment with initializing or bootstrapping the controller with a policy that favors known good architectural patterns (e.g., ResNet-like skip connections)? While starting from scratch is a stronger result, understanding if prior knowledge can accelerate the search would also be valuable for future work.
