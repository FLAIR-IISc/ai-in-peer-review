### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a novel method for automating the design of neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (RNN), termed the "controller," to generate a variable-length string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. This controller is trained using a policy gradient reinforcement learning algorithm (REINFORCE) to maximize the expected validation accuracy of the child networks it generates. The validation accuracy serves as the reward signal for updating the controller's parameters, enabling it to progressively discover better architectures over time.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on two highly competitive benchmarks. For image classification on CIFAR-10, the method discovers a novel convolutional network that achieves state-of-the-art accuracy among similarly structured models, even outperforming its human-designed counterpart in both accuracy and speed. For language modeling on Penn Treebank, the method designs a new recurrent cell from scratch that surpasses the performance of the widely used LSTM and other state-of-the-art models. The authors further show that this novel cell generalizes well, achieving state-of-the-art results when transferred to character-level language modeling and improving performance when integrated into a large-scale neural machine translation system.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is an excellent and exciting paper that introduces a powerful new direction for neural network design. The two key reasons for my decision are:
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The formulation of architecture design as a reinforcement learning problem, where a controller network learns to generate optimal architectures, is highly innovative and addresses a major bottleneck in deep learning: the reliance on expert intuition and laborious trial-and-error.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The method achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on two very different and challenging tasks (image classification and language modeling), demonstrating its versatility and power. The fact that it can design a novel RNN cell that outperforms the LSTM is particularly impressive.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and technically sound. The core problem—the difficulty of designing high-performing neural network architectures—is a significant one for the field. The proposed solution is elegant and well-placed in the literature, drawing connections to hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and meta-learning, while clearly distinguishing its unique contributions.

The claims made in the paper are strongly supported by rigorous experiments.
*   **Effectiveness on Diverse Tasks:** The method is not tailored to a single domain. It successfully finds a top-tier convolutional architecture for CIFAR-10 (3.65% error) and a novel, state-of-the-art recurrent cell for Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity), highlighting its generality. The analysis of the discovered architectures, such as the preference for rectangular filters in the CNN and the similarities/differences to LSTMs in the RNN cell, provides valuable insights.
*   **Methodological Soundness:** The authors provide a detailed description of their method, including crucial implementation details that make the approach viable, such as asynchronous parallel training to manage the high computational cost and an attention-like mechanism to incorporate modern architectural features like skip connections.
*   **Rigorous Baselines:** The comparison against a random search baseline is essential and convincingly demonstrates that the policy gradient approach is genuinely learning to navigate the search space more effectively. The performance improvements shown in Figure 6 are a clear testament to the value of the learning-based approach.

Overall, this work presents a complete and compelling story, from a novel idea to a robust implementation that yields state-of-the-art results. It has the potential to significantly impact how researchers and practitioners develop neural network models in the future.

**Additional Feedback and Questions**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision. The work is already very strong.

The most significant aspect not fully detailed is the computational cost of the search process. While the parallelization scheme is described, the total resources required are substantial (e.g., "800 networks being trained on 800 GPUs concurrently"). Understanding this cost is crucial for assessing the practical applicability of the method.

I have a few questions for the authors that would clarify my understanding:
1.  **Computational Cost:** Could you provide an estimate of the total computational cost (e.g., in total GPU-hours) required to find the final architectures for the CIFAR-10 and PTB experiments? It would also be highly informative to see a plot showing the validation performance of the best-found architecture as a function of the number of architectures sampled (or total compute time), to better understand the trade-off between search cost and model quality.
2.  **Role of Bootstrapping:** A key strength of your method is its ability to find novel architectures "from scratch." As a point of comparison and to explore potential practical speed-ups, did you consider or experiment with initializing the controller by training it on a set of known, high-performing human-designed architectures? Do you believe such bootstrapping could help the search converge to good solutions faster, even if the final performance remains the same?
