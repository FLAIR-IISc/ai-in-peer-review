- Introduces Neural Architecture Search: an RNN "controller" trained with REINFORCE to generate child-network architectures using validation accuracy as reward.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, finding architectures that outperform human-designed counterparts.  
- Decision: Accept — paper is novel, significant, and exciting for automating architecture design.  
- Key reason 1: Novelty — formulates architecture design as a reinforcement learning problem, reducing reliance on human intuition.  
- Key reason 2: Strong empirical validation — achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results across diverse tasks.  
- CIFAR-10 result: discovered CNN achieves 3.65% error and outperforms comparable human-designed models in accuracy and speed.  
- Penn Treebank result: discovered recurrent cell attains 62.4 perplexity, surpassing LSTM and other state-of-the-art models and generalizes to other tasks.  
- Analysis provides insights into discovered architectures (e.g., preference for rectangular filters, RNN cell similarities/differences to LSTM).  
- Methodological soundness: clear description, important implementation details (asynchronous parallel training) and inclusion of modern features (attention-like skip connections).  
- Rigorous baselines: comparison against random search shows policy-gradient controller effectively learns the search space (performance gains shown in Figure 6).  
- Major concern: high computational cost of the search (e.g., training ~800 networks on 800 GPUs concurrently) — practical applicability unclear without total GPU-hour estimates.  
- Requested clarifications: provide total computational cost (GPU-hours) and a plot of best-validation performance vs. number of architectures sampled or compute time.  
- Suggested experiment/question: could bootstrapping the controller with known good human-designed architectures speed up convergence, and was this tested?
