This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a novel and promising method for automating the design of neural network architectures. The core contribution is the use of a recurrent neural network controller, trained with reinforcement learning, to generate model descriptions. By treating the validation accuracy of a generated "child" network as a reward signal, the controller learns to discover high-performing architectures. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach by achieving state-of-the-art results on both the CIFAR-10 image classification benchmark and the Penn Treebank language modeling task, discovering novel convolutional and recurrent cell architectures that outperform well-established, human-designed models.

I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance. The primary reasons are its ambitious goal of automating a critical and challenging aspect of deep learning research and the strength of its empirical results across different domains. The paper tackles an important, high-impact problem, and the proposed method is elegant and well-motivated.

The claims are well-supported by extensive experiments. The authors demonstrate that their method can discover competitive architectures from a vast search space, achieving results that rival or surpass the best human-invented architectures on both vision and text benchmarks. This shows the generalizability of the approach and its ability to explore beyond standard search spaces to create dataset-specific designs without manual engineering. The comparison against random search validates the effectiveness of the policy gradient approach. While the computational cost is currently very high, this is an inherent challenge in the field, and the presented framework is a significant step forward that will likely become more practical as hardware and algorithms improve.

To further strengthen the paper and aid reproducibility, I would offer the following suggestions. The paper would benefit greatly from a more detailed specification of the hyperparameters used to train the final child models, particularly for the Penn Treebank experiments. To replicate the results, it would be crucial to know the exact dropout rates and the specific types of dropout employed (e.g., embedding, recurrent, input/output). Releasing a base set of hyperparameters, as was done in Zaremba et al. (2014), would be an invaluable service to the community and establish a stronger baseline for future work. Could the authors please provide these details for all experiments, including the character language model, to ensure the work is fully reproducible?
