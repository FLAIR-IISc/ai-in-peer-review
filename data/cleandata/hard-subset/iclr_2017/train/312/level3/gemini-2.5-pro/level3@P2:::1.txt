### **1. Summary**

This paper introduces "Neural Architecture Search," a novel method for automatically designing neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (RNN), termed the "controller," to generate a string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. This controller is trained using a policy gradient reinforcement learning algorithm (REINFORCE), where the reward signal is the performance (e.g., accuracy) of the trained child network on a held-out validation set. This approach allows the controller to learn over time which architectural choices lead to better-performing models.

The authors demonstrate the power and generality of their method on two highly competitive benchmarks. For image classification on CIFAR-10, their method discovers a novel convolutional network architecture that achieves state-of-the-art accuracy, outperforming many prominent human-designed architectures. For language modeling on Penn Treebank, the method designs a new recurrent cell from scratch that surpasses the performance of the widely-used LSTM cell and other state-of-the-art models. The discovered cell also shows strong generalization, achieving state-of-the-art results on a character-level language modeling task and improving performance when integrated into the Google Neural Machine Translation system.

### **2. Decision**

**Accept**.

The paper addresses the highly important and challenging problem of automatic architecture engineering with a novel and effective method. The approach is validated by exceptionally strong empirical results on both vision and language benchmarks, demonstrating its ability to discover novel, state-of-the-art architectures.

### **3. Supporting Arguments**

The decision to accept is based on the following strengths:

*   **Importance and Novelty:** Automating architecture design is a critical problem in machine learning, as manual design is time-consuming and requires deep expertise. This paper presents a significant step forward by framing architecture generation as a learning problem for a controller network. The use of an RNN to generate a variable-length description of a network, trained with reinforcement learning, is an elegant and powerful formulation that moves beyond fixed-space hyperparameter optimization and provides a more principled approach than many neuro-evolution methods.

*   **Strong and General Empirical Results:** The authors validate their method on two very different and difficult tasks: CIFAR-10 image classification and Penn Treebank language modeling. In both cases, the discovered architectures achieve state-of-the-art or near-state-of-the-art results. Discovering a recurrent cell that outperforms the LSTM on its canonical benchmark is a particularly impressive achievement. The fact that this cell also transfers successfully to other tasks (character modeling and machine translation) provides strong evidence that the method is not simply overfitting to a single task but is finding genuinely useful architectural motifs.

*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The paper includes important control experiments, most notably a comparison against random search. The results clearly show that the policy gradient method learns to find better architectures over time and significantly outperforms this strong baseline, justifying the complexity of the proposed learning framework.

While the computational cost of the method is currently very high (requiring hundreds of GPUs in parallel), this is an engineering barrier that will likely lower over time with more efficient hardware and algorithmic improvements. The fundamental contribution of demonstrating that architectures can be *learned* effectively is significant and opens up a promising new research direction.

### **4. Additional Feedback**

The paper is well-written and the results are compelling. The following points are intended to help improve the final version and aid reproducibility for future work. They are not part of the decision assessment.

*   **Reproducibility of Child Network Training:** The paper provides good detail on the training of the controller RNN. However, it lacks sufficient detail on the hyperparameters used for training the child networks, especially for the Penn Treebank experiments. Reproducing results on this benchmark is notoriously sensitive to the exact configuration of learning rates, decay schedules, weight initialization, and, crucially, the specific types and rates of dropout used (e.g., embedding dropout, recurrent dropout, input/output dropout).

*   **Recommendation for Hyperparameter Reporting:** To facilitate future research and allow for fair comparisons, it would be extremely valuable if the authors could provide the full set of hyperparameters for their best-performing models. Following the example of Zaremba et al. (2014), who provided a clear set of hyperparameters for their LSTM baseline, would be a great service to the community. This should include details for the character-level language model as well.

### **5. Questions for Authors**

To help clarify my understanding and strengthen the paper, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

*   Could you please provide the full set of hyperparameters used to train the final child models, particularly for the Penn Treebank experiments? This would include the exact types of dropout used and their rates, the weight initialization scheme, optimizer details, and the learning rate schedule.

*   Similarly, for the character language model experiments, could you please provide the specific hyperparameters used for training both your discovered cell and the LSTM baseline to ensure a fair comparison and aid replication?
