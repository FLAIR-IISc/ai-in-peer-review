**Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a novel and promising method for automating the design of neural network architectures. The authors propose using a controller, implemented as a recurrent neural network (RNN), to generate architecture descriptions. This controller is trained using reinforcement learning (REINFORCE), with the validation accuracy of the generated "child" network serving as the reward signal. This approach effectively frames architecture design as a learning problem. The experiments demonstrate strong performance, with the method discovering a convolutional architecture for CIFAR-10 that rivals the best human-designed models and a novel recurrent cell for Penn Treebank that surpasses the state-of-the-art LSTM. The idea is compelling, and the results suggest that this automated approach can discover competitive, and in some cases superior, network structures.

**Decision: Weak Accept**

The core idea of using an RNN controller with reinforcement learning for architecture search is highly innovative and addresses a significant bottleneck in deep learning. The empirical results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are strong and demonstrate the method's potential. However, the paper's claims of generality are not yet fully substantiated due to the limited scope of the experiments and the significant computational cost.

**Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this work is its novel formulation of architecture design. By using a policy gradient method, the controller learns to generate better architectures over time, a more principled approach than random or grid search. The fact that the discovered architectures incorporate modern design patterns, such as skip connections, lends credibility to the search process, suggesting it learns meaningful structural priors.

The main weaknesses relate to the experimental validation and practical feasibility. First, the method is extremely computationally expensive, requiring hundreds of GPUs running in parallel for many days. This high cost is a major barrier to reproducibility and practical application for most of the research community. Second, while the results on two standard datasets are impressive, they are insufficient to fully support the claim of a general-purpose architecture search method. The performance of the discovered convolutional architecture on a more complex dataset like ImageNet, or the recurrent cell on other sequence modeling tasks like machine translation, remains unevaluated.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors**

To strengthen the paper, I would recommend the authors address the following points, which would help clarify the method's robustness and scalability.

*   **Scalability:** Given the substantial resources used for CIFAR-10, could you comment on the projected cost and feasibility of applying this method to larger-scale problems like ImageNet?
*   **Generality:** Have you considered experiments on datasets from different domains (e.g., speech recognition) to further test the generality of the discovered recurrent cell?
*   **Search Space:** How sensitive is the final performance to the design of the initial search space? For example, what happens if batch normalization or different activation functions are not included as options for the controller?
*   **Random Search Comparison:** The comparison against random search is valuable. Could you provide more detail on the distribution used for the random search baseline to ensure it was a strong and fair comparison?
