- Proposes Neural Architecture Search: a controller RNN that generates architectures and is trained with policy gradients using validation accuracy as reward.
- Demonstrates strong results: discovers a CNN achieving 3.65% test error on CIFAR-10 and a novel RNN cell that outperforms LSTM on Penn Treebank (~3.6 perplexity improvement).
- Controller RNN enables variable-length architecture generation and learns architectural patterns via reinforcement learning.
- Uses attention-like mechanism to generate skip connections and incorporates modern architectural features in the search space.
- Empirical validation is thorough: CIFAR-10 model is faster than some human-designed models; discovered RNN cell transfers well to character modeling and machine translation.
- Comparison to random search indicates the policy-gradient controller effectively learns to navigate the search space rather than relying on brute force.
- Major concern: extremely high computational cost (e.g., training 800 networks on 800 GPUs), threatening practicality and reproducibility.
- Limited evidence of generality for the discovered convolutional architecture — not evaluated on other image datasets (CIFAR-100, SVHN, ImageNet).
- Suggest deeper analysis of discovered architectures to identify recurring motifs and design principles learned by the controller.
- Need discussion of sensitivity to search-space meta-choices (e.g., filter sizes [1,3,5,7], RNN base number) and robustness of results.
- Recommend adding a dedicated discussion on efficiency improvements (weight sharing, network morphisms, surrogate performance models) for future work.
- Asks authors to quantify total computational cost (GPU-days) for a full CIFAR-10 search run.
- Requests comparison of NAS’s best architecture vs. the best found by random search after all 12,800 trials to assess the RL controller’s added value.
- Asks whether the CIFAR-10 architecture transfers to other image datasets like CIFAR-100.
- Asks rationale for the RNN search "base number" of 8 and whether other values were tested and how they affected results.
