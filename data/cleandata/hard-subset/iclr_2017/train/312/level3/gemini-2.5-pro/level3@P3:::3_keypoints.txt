- Proposes Neural Architecture Search: an RNN controller generates variable-length architecture descriptions, trained with REINFORCE using validation accuracy as reward.  
- Demonstrates NAS on CIFAR-10 (CNN) and PTB (recurrent cell), achieving competitive/SOTA results (3.65% error on CIFAR-10; 62.4 perplexity on PTB).  
- Decision: Accept — novel, influential framework that automates architecture design via an RL-trained controller.  
- Major conceptual strength: frames architecture search as sequential generation, enabling exploration of vast, variable-length configuration spaces beyond fixed-dimensional hyperparameter tuning.  
- Methodological strength: uses policy gradients to directly optimize non-differentiable performance metrics (validation accuracy).  
- Empirical strength: discovered architectures exhibit sensible motifs (skip connections, larger early filters) and a novel recurrent cell that outperforms LSTM and transfers well.  
- Major limitation: extremely high computational cost (hundreds of GPUs for days), hindering reproducibility and practical use.  
- Limitation: experiments limited to CIFAR-10 and PTB, leaving general applicability to larger-scale datasets, other modalities, or less-studied tasks unproven.  
- Suggestion: provide a more detailed analysis of search efficiency and potential ways to improve sample efficiency beyond random search baselines.  
- Suggestion: discuss sensitivity of final results to the human-specified search-space primitives (filter sizes, activations, etc.).  
- Suggestion: include ablation studies on RL training components (e.g., baseline subtraction, choice of policy-gradient algorithm).  
- Question: report concrete computational cost metrics (total GPU-days) for the CIFAR-10 and PTB searches.  
- Question: clarify stability of controller RNN training and sensitivity to controller hyperparameters (learning rate, network size).  
- Question: analyze architectures generated during training to see if common motifs emerge early, how refinement proceeds, and typical failure modes.
