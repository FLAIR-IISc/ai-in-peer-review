### Review

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces "Neural Architecture Search," a novel and ambitious method for automating the design of neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate a string representation of a network architecture. This controller is then trained using reinforcement learning, where the reward is the validation accuracy of the generated "child" network. This framing allows the controller to learn which architectural patterns lead to high performance, effectively exploring a vast and complex search space. The authors demonstrate the power of this approach on two challenging and standard benchmarks: image classification on CIFAR-10 and language modeling on Penn Treebank. For CIFAR-10, the method discovers a convolutional network that achieves state-of-the-art accuracy, outperforming the best human-designed architectures at the time while also being computationally faster. For Penn Treebank, the method designs a novel recurrent cell that surpasses the performance of the widely-used LSTM and other strong baselines. The fact that this discovered cell also shows strong transfer performance on character language modeling and machine translation tasks further validates its quality.

**Decision: Accept**

My decision is to accept this paper. The primary reasons are:
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The proposed method of using a controller RNN trained with policy gradients to search for architectures is a highly original and significant contribution. It formalizes architecture design as a learning problem and presents a general framework for tackling it.
2.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The method achieves state-of-the-art results on two very different, highly competitive benchmarks (CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank). This provides strong evidence that the approach is not only conceptually interesting but also practically effective.

**Supporting Arguments**

The main strength of this paper lies in its core idea. Automating architecture engineering is a long-standing goal in machine learning, and this work provides a powerful, learning-based paradigm to address it. Using an RNN controller allows for the generation of variable-length architectures, a clear advantage over traditional hyperparameter optimization methods that operate in fixed-dimensional spaces. The application of reinforcement learning (specifically REINFORCE with a baseline) is a natural and well-suited choice for optimizing the non-differentiable reward signal (validation accuracy). The technical details, such as using an attention-like mechanism to generate skip connections, demonstrate a thoughtful approach to incorporating modern architectural features into the search space.

The empirical validation is thorough and compelling. On CIFAR-10, achieving a 3.65% test error is a top-tier result that rivals meticulously hand-crafted models like DenseNet. The fact that the discovered architecture is also faster is an important practical advantage. The results on Penn Treebank are arguably even more impressive. Discovering a novel recurrent cell that outperforms the LSTM by a significant margin (3.6 perplexity) is a major achievement. The authors strengthen this claim by showing the cell's robustness through transfer learning experiments on character modeling and neural machine translation, where it provides benefits without task-specific tuning. The comparison against random search further reinforces that the policy gradient method is effectively learning to navigate the search space and is not just a product of extensive computation.

However, the work is not without its weaknesses. The most significant concern is the immense computational cost. The CIFAR-10 experiments required training 800 networks concurrently on 800 GPUs. This level of resources is inaccessible to the vast majority of the research community, which raises serious questions about the method's practicality and reproducibility. While the results are impressive, the cost to obtain them is staggering and this should be acknowledged more directly as a major limitation.

A second concern is the limited evidence for the generality of the discovered *convolutional* architectures. While the discovered RNN cell was shown to transfer well, the CIFAR-10 architecture was not evaluated on other image datasets (e.g., CIFAR-100, SVHN, or a scaled-up version for ImageNet). It is unclear if the method found fundamental, transferable design principles or an architecture that is highly over-specialized to CIFAR-10.

Despite these concerns, the novelty of the framework and the strength of the results on these benchmarks make this a landmark paper that opens up a new and exciting research direction in automated machine learning.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   **Analysis of Discovered Architectures:** The paper briefly notes that the discovered CNN has many rectangular filters and that the RNN cell has similarities to an LSTM. This analysis could be deepened. Are there any other recurring motifs or design principles that the controller learned? A more in-depth analysis could provide valuable insights for human architects and help understand what the controller is learning.
*   **Sensitivity to Search Space:** The design of the search space is a crucial, yet under-discussed, component of the method. For instance, the choice of filter sizes `[1, 3, 5, 7]` or the "base number" of 8 for the RNN cell seem somewhat arbitrary. A discussion on the sensitivity of the final results to these meta-choices would strengthen the paper by clarifying the robustness of the approach.
*   **Discussion on Efficiency:** Given the extreme computational cost, a dedicated section in the conclusion or future work discussing potential avenues for improving efficiency would be very valuable. This could include ideas like weight sharing, network morphisms, or surrogate models for performance prediction, which would foreshadow the next generation of NAS methods.

**Questions for Authors**

1.  Could you quantify the total computational cost for a full search run? For instance, how many total GPU-days were required to produce the final CIFAR-10 model?
2.  In the comparison against random search (Figure 6), the policy gradient method is clearly superior over time. However, how does the final best architecture found by NAS compare to the best one found by random search after the full 12,800 trials? Is the final performance gap substantial enough to justify the additional complexity of the RL controller?
3.  Have you performed any experiments to test the transferability of the best-found CIFAR-10 architecture to other, similar image classification datasets like CIFAR-100? This would provide stronger evidence that the controller is learning generalizable architectural principles.
4.  For the RNN cell search, what was the rationale for choosing a "base number" of 8? Have you experimented with other values, and how did that impact the search process and final cell performance?
