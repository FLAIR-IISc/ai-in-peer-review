- Proposed method: An RNN "controller" uses reinforcement learning and policy gradients to generate neural architectures trained for reward, evaluated on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank with state-of-the-art results.
- Decision: Accept with reservations due to novelty and promise but limited experimental scope.
- Strength: Well-motivated and innovative RNN+RL approach that produces architectures competitive with human designs.
- Concern: Experiments are limited to only two datasets, raising questions about generalization to other tasks.
- Concern: Controller training is very time-consuming even with substantial compute resources.
- Suggestion: Evaluate the method on a wider range of datasets and tasks to demonstrate generality.
- Suggestion: Provide detailed training protocol for the controller, including hyperparameters and convergence iterations.
- Suggestion: Compare performance against other architecture search methods (e.g., Bayesian optimization, evolutionary algorithms).
- Suggestion: Offer deeper analysis of the architectures generated and how they differ from human-designed models.
- Question: What are the exact hyperparameters and number of iterations required for controller training and convergence?
- Question: How do the authors plan to reduce or address the long training times for the controller?
- Question: Have the authors tested the approach on other datasets/tasks, and what were the outcomes?
- Question: Can the authors provide more insight into the structural characteristics of generated architectures versus human-designed ones?
