This paper presents Neural Architecture Search, a novel approach to automating the design of neural network architectures. The authors propose using a recurrent neural network as a controller to generate model descriptions of neural networks, which are then trained using reinforcement learning to maximize the expected accuracy on a validation set. The method is evaluated on both vision and text benchmarks, demonstrating impressive gains by exploring beyond standard RNN/CNN search spaces and enabling dataset-specific architectures without manual engineering.

I recommend accepting this paper. The key reasons for this decision are the paper's strong results and its well-motivated approach, which addresses an important problem in the field of deep learning. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their method on challenging benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness in designing good models from scratch.

One of the strengths of this paper is its ability to evaluate the proposed method on both vision and text benchmarks. The authors demonstrate that their approach can design novel convolutional architectures that rival the best human-invented architectures on the CIFAR-10 dataset, and can also design novel recurrent cells that outperform the widely-used LSTM cell on the Penn Treebank dataset. These results are impressive and demonstrate the potential of the proposed method.

Another strength of this paper is its well-motivated approach. The authors provide a clear explanation of the problem they are trying to solve and the approach they take to solve it. They also provide a thorough discussion of related work, demonstrating that their approach is well-placed in the literature.

To further improve the paper, I would like to see more details on the hyperparameters used in the experiments. Specifically, I would like to see the exact hyperparameters and types of dropout (recurrent, embedding, etc.) used, as well as the hyperparameter details for other experiments (e.g., the character language model). Providing this information would make it easier for other researchers to replicate the results and build upon the proposed method.

Additionally, I would like to see a base set of hyperparameters released, similar to Zaremba et al. (2014), to aid future work and reproducibility. This would make it easier for other researchers to compare their results to the proposed method and would facilitate further research in this area.

Overall, this is a well-written paper with strong results and a well-motivated approach. I believe it makes a significant contribution to the field of deep learning and has the potential to inspire further research in this area.
