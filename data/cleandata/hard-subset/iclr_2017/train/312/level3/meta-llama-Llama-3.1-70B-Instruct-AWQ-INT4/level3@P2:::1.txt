This paper proposes a novel method for neural architecture search, using a recurrent neural network as a controller to generate model descriptions of neural networks. The controller is trained with reinforcement learning to maximize the expected accuracy of the generated architectures on a validation set. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on two challenging benchmarks, CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving state-of-the-art results on both tasks.

I recommend accepting this paper. The key reasons for this decision are the paper's strong empirical performance and its well-motivated approach to addressing the important problem of automating architecture search. The authors' evaluation on both vision and text benchmarks demonstrates the flexibility and effectiveness of their method, and their exploration of dataset-specific architectures without manual engineering is a significant contribution.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their approach. The results are impressive, with the proposed method achieving state-of-the-art results on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of their results, including ablation studies and comparisons to other methods.

One area for improvement is in providing more details on the hyperparameters and types of dropout used in the experiments. The reviewer notes that replication of the results would require excessive trial-and-error without this information. The authors should consider releasing a base set of hyperparameters, as in Zaremba et al. (2014), to aid future work and reproducibility. Additionally, the authors should provide hyperparameter details for other experiments, such as the character language model.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural architecture search, and its strong empirical performance and well-motivated approach make it a strong candidate for acceptance.

Additional feedback:

* The authors may want to consider providing more details on the computational cost of their method, as this is an important consideration for many practitioners.
* The authors' use of a parameter server approach to speed up training is an interesting contribution, and they may want to consider providing more details on this aspect of their method.
* The authors' comparison to other methods, such as random search, is helpful in evaluating the effectiveness of their approach. However, they may want to consider providing more comparisons to other state-of-the-art methods in the field.
