**Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel approach to neural architecture search using reinforcement learning, treating deep neural networks (DNNs) as variable-length sequences. The authors employ an auto-regressive two-layer LSTM as both the controller and critic, using node selection as the RL action and architecture evaluation error as the reward. The method is evaluated on two tasks, CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, and compared against multiple human-designed architectures. The authors provide a detailed and clear description of their approach, including necessary improvements, and demonstrate its capability through datasets and experiments.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

Based on the review guidelines, I decide to **Accept** this paper. The two key reasons for this choice are:

1. **Well-motivated approach**: The paper proposes a novel and exciting approach to addressing the challenges of hand-designing neural network architectures. The use of reinforcement learning to search for optimal architectures is well-motivated and has the potential to make a significant impact in the field.
2. **Strong empirical performance**: The paper demonstrates strong empirical performance on two challenging benchmarks, CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, and compares favorably against multiple human-designed architectures.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the proposed approach, including the use of an auto-regressive two-layer LSTM as the controller and critic. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the results, including interesting observed differences between generated and human-designed architectures. The related work coverage and comparisons are handled well, providing a comprehensive understanding of the paper's contributions.

**Additional Feedback**

To further improve the paper, I would like to request additional information on the following:

* Training time: It would be useful to provide data on the training time required for the proposed approach, as well as the correlation between compute/time and model quality.
* Human-bootstrapped models: The authors mention the use of human-bootstrapped models in the related work section. It would be interesting to see the results of using these models as a starting point for the proposed approach.
* Ablation studies: To better understand the contributions of each component of the proposed approach, it would be useful to see ablation studies that evaluate the performance of the method with and without certain components.
