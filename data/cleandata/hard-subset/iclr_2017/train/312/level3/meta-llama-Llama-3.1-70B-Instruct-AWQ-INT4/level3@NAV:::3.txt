**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to neural architecture design using a recurrent neural network (RNN) and reinforcement learning. The RNN, referred to as the "controller," generates model descriptions of neural networks, which are then trained on a validation set to obtain a reward signal. The controller is trained using policy gradient methods to maximize the expected accuracy of the generated architectures. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on two benchmark datasets, CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving state-of-the-art results in both cases.

**Decision**

I decide to accept this paper, but with some reservations. The main reason for my decision is the novelty and promise of the proposed approach, which has the potential to revolutionize the field of neural architecture design. However, I also have some concerns regarding the limited scope of the experiments and the lack of generality of the results.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper presents a well-motivated approach to neural architecture design, which is a pressing problem in the field of deep learning. The use of an RNN and reinforcement learning is innovative and has shown promising results on two benchmark datasets. The authors have also demonstrated the ability of their approach to generate architectures that are competitive with human-designed ones.

However, I have some concerns regarding the limited scope of the experiments. The authors have only evaluated their approach on two datasets, and it is unclear whether the results will generalize to other datasets or tasks. Additionally, the training time of the controller is quite long, even with substantial computing resources.

**Additional Feedback**

To improve the paper, I would suggest the following:

* Evaluate the approach on a wider range of datasets and tasks to demonstrate its generality.
* Provide more details on the training process of the controller, including the hyperparameters used and the number of iterations required to converge.
* Compare the approach to other methods for neural architecture design, such as Bayesian optimization or evolutionary algorithms.
* Provide more insights into the types of architectures generated by the controller and how they differ from human-designed ones.

**Questions for the Authors**
