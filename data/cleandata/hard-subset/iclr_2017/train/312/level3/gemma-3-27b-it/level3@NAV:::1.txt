## Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

This paper presents a novel approach to automating neural architecture design, a traditionally labor-intensive process. The core idea – using a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller trained with reinforcement learning to generate network architectures – is compelling and demonstrably effective. The results are strong, achieving state-of-the-art or competitive performance on both CIFAR-10 (image classification) and Penn Treebank (language modeling) benchmarks. Crucially, the method appears capable of discovering architectures beyond those typically explored by human engineers, and of tailoring architectures to specific datasets without manual intervention. This is a significant contribution to the field.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper addresses an important and challenging problem, presents a well-motivated and innovative solution, and demonstrates compelling empirical results. The demonstrated success across both vision and language tasks further strengthens the validity of the approach.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper effectively frames the problem of neural architecture design as a search problem amenable to reinforcement learning. The authors clearly articulate the benefits of their approach – automated discovery of potentially superior architectures and reduced reliance on expert knowledge. The experimental results are robust, showing clear gains over existing methods. The demonstration of successful transfer learning to a character language modeling task is particularly impressive, illustrating the generalization capabilities of the learned cell structure. The improvements on both datasets are not marginal, and the accompanying analysis reveals interesting insights into the architectures discovered (e.g., preference for rectangular filters).

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is well-written and the results are strong, reproducibility is a concern. The authors mention the use of hyperparameters during training (e.g., learning rate, dropout) but lack specific details regarding the exact values and, more importantly, *types* of dropout used (recurrent, embedding, etc.). Providing these details is crucial for allowing others to replicate their results. Following the precedent set by Zaremba et al. (2014), releasing a base set of hyperparameters alongside the code would significantly aid future work and encourage further exploration of this promising area. 

I also request that hyperparameter details be provided for all experiments, including the character language model. Specifically, what dropout rates were used, what optimization algorithm and parameters were utilized, and any specific data augmentation strategies. 

I am curious about the computational resources required for this search. While the paper acknowledges the high cost, quantifying this (e.g., total GPU hours) would be valuable.  Finally, could the authors elaborate on how the depth of the controller RNN impacts performance, and if any experimentation was done with different controller architectures?
