## Review for ICLR 2017

This paper presents a novel and compelling approach to neural architecture search (NAS) using reinforcement learning. The authors frame the problem of designing neural network architectures as a sequential decision process, employing a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller to generate architectures and optimizing it via a policy gradient method based on the validation accuracy of the resulting networks. The results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are highly encouraging, demonstrating the ability to discover architectures that rival or surpass human-designed baselines.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper tackles a significant challenge in deep learning – automating architecture design – with a well-motivated and technically sound approach. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the method, achieving state-of-the-art or competitive performance on multiple tasks.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of representing neural network architectures as variable-length sequences and utilizing an RNN controller is insightful and well-executed. The authors clearly articulate the motivation for this approach, highlighting the limitations of manual architecture engineering. The detailed description of the method, including the training procedure with REINFORCE, the parallelization scheme, and the incorporation of skip connections, is commendable. 

The experimental validation is strong. The CIFAR-10 results, where the proposed method achieves a test error of 3.65% (outperforming existing architectures), are particularly impressive. Similarly, the superior performance on the Penn Treebank language modeling task, exceeding LSTM performance, showcases the generalizability of the approach. The inclusion of transfer learning experiments on character language modeling further strengthens these findings.

The paper demonstrates a good understanding of related work, appropriately positioning the contribution within the context of hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis. The ablation studies and control experiments (adding more functions to the search space, comparison against random search) provide valuable insights into the method’s behavior and robustness. The authors also acknowledge potential limitations and avenues for future work.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is excellent, the following points could further enhance it:

*   **Computational Cost:** The training process appears computationally demanding. Providing more detailed information on the total training time and resource usage (e.g., number of GPUs, wall-clock time) would be valuable. Investigating the correlation between computational resources allocated, training time, and the quality of the discovered architectures could also provide useful insights.
*   **Human-Bootstrapped Models:**  The paper focuses on starting the search from scratch. It would be interesting to explore whether initializing the controller with knowledge derived from existing architectures (e.g., pre-training on a dataset of known architectures) could accelerate the search process or improve the resulting architectures. Examining the role of any human-bootstrapped models in the initial stages of exploration could strengthen the analysis.
*   **Visualization of Search Space Exploration:** A visualization illustrating how the controller explores the architecture space over time could provide a better understanding of the learning process.  This could reveal whether the search converges quickly, gets stuck in local optima, or exhibits other interesting behaviors.
*   **Architectural Analysis:** The authors mention observing interesting differences between generated and human-designed architectures. Expanding on this observation with a more detailed architectural analysis could provide further insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the learned architectures.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detailed information regarding the computational resources required to run the experiments, specifically the total training time and the number of GPUs utilized?
2.  Have you investigated the effect of initializing the controller with information derived from existing architectures, and if so, what were the results?
3.  What strategies, if any, are employed to mitigate the risk of the controller getting stuck in local optima during the search process?
4.  Could you elaborate on the observed differences between the architectures generated by your method and those designed by humans, and what implications these differences have for understanding the design principles of effective neural networks?
