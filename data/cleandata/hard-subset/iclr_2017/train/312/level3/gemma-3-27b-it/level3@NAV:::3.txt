## Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

This paper proposes an innovative approach to neural architecture design using a recurrent neural network (RNN) as a controller, trained via reinforcement learning to generate high-performing network architectures. The core idea of formulating architecture search as a sequential decision process and leveraging RNNs for this purpose is promising and represents a significant departure from traditional, manual architecture engineering. The experimental results on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are compelling, demonstrating that the proposed method can discover architectures competitive with, and in some cases surpassing, human-designed state-of-the-art models. The fact that generated architectures share characteristics with human-designed ones is also noteworthy, suggesting a degree of intuitive design principles being captured.

**Decision: Accept**

I recommend acceptance, primarily because of the novelty and potential impact of the proposed approach. The results on both image classification and language modeling are strong and showcase the effectiveness of the RNN+RL framework. The paper successfully demonstrates that automated architecture search can yield competitive results, opening new avenues for deep learning research.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper clearly articulates the problem of manual architecture design and presents a well-motivated solution. The use of reinforcement learning to optimize the RNN controller is logical and effective. The experiments are well-conducted, with clear baselines and comparisons to existing methods. The reported performance gains on both datasets (3.65% error on CIFAR-10 and 3.6 perplexity improvement on Penn Treebank) are substantial and provide strong empirical evidence supporting the claims. The inclusion of transfer learning experiments on the character language modeling task further strengthens the validity of the findings.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, further experimentation is needed to assess the generality of the proposed method. Evaluating the approach on a wider range of datasets, particularly those involving sequential models beyond recurrent cells (e.g., Transformers), would be beneficial. The reported training time is a significant limitation; exploring techniques to accelerate the training process, such as more efficient exploration strategies or transfer learning from previous searches, would be valuable. 

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   **Scalability:** How does the performance of the method scale with the complexity of the search space? What are the practical limitations in terms of computational resources required for searching increasingly complex architectures?
*   **Robustness:** How sensitive is the performance to the choice of hyperparameters for the reinforcement learning algorithm (e.g., learning rate, baseline function)?
*   **Interpretability:** Can you provide further insights into the characteristics of the architectures generated by the RNN controller? Are there any recurring patterns or design principles that emerge from the learned architectures?
*   **Comparison to NAS with evolutionary algorithms:** Can you provide a direct comparison to other NAS methods that rely on evolutionary algorithms? What are the key benefits of your approach over evolutionary strategies?
