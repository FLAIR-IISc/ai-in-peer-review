## Review of Neural Architecture Search

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel approach to automating neural network architecture design. The core idea is to utilize a recurrent neural network (RNN) as a controller to generate architecture descriptions, and then train this controller using reinforcement learning to maximize the performance of the generated architectures on a given task. The results presented on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are compelling, demonstrating that NAS can discover architectures competitive with, and in some cases surpassing, human-designed models.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a technically sound and innovative approach to neural architecture search, achieving impressive results on benchmark datasets. While further investigation into generality is warranted, the current findings are strong enough to merit acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary strength of this work lies in its elegant and effective approach to a challenging problem. Automating neural architecture design has traditionally been a computationally expensive and expertise-dependent process. NAS offers a promising alternative, framing the problem as a reinforcement learning task. This allows the algorithm to explore a vast design space and discover novel architectures that might not be readily apparent to human designers. 

The experimental results are particularly noteworthy. Achieving a 3.65% error rate on CIFAR-10, surpassing previous state-of-the-art results, is a significant accomplishment. Similarly, the gains observed on the Penn Treebank dataset, particularly the development of a recurrent cell outperforming LSTMs, are highly encouraging. The fact that the discovered architectures share characteristics with human-designed networks (e.g., skip connections, filter sizes) suggests that NAS is not simply finding random, non-intuitive solutions, but is rather learning to leverage established design principles. 

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The inclusion of details regarding the distributed training setup is helpful, as it highlights the computational resources required for this approach. The authors also acknowledge the limitations of the current work and propose potential avenues for future research.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, several points could be addressed to further enhance its impact. 

*   **Generalization:** The experiments are currently limited to CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank. Exploring the performance of NAS on other datasets, particularly those with different characteristics (e.g., ImageNet, other language modeling tasks) would be crucial for demonstrating its generality. The sequential nature of the models generated, and the fact the experiments focus on sequential and image tasks warrants exploring the method’s efficacy across a wider range of model types (e.g., GANs, Transformers).
*   **Computational Cost:** The authors acknowledge the high computational cost of training the controller, even with a substantial distributed computing infrastructure. A more detailed analysis of the computational resources required (e.g., total GPU hours) would be valuable for readers considering applying this technique. Investigating methods to reduce the training time, such as more efficient exploration strategies or transfer learning from previously discovered architectures, would also be beneficial.
*   **Ablation Studies:** While the paper mentions the addition of skip connections and other layer types, further ablation studies could help isolate the impact of these features on performance. For example, comparing the performance of NAS with and without skip connections would provide insights into their importance.
*   **Search Space:** The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the chosen search space. What factors guided the selection of the available hyperparameters? Were any preliminary experiments conducted to evaluate the impact of different search space configurations?
*   **Comparison to Bayesian Optimization:** The paper mentions Bayesian optimization methods as a related approach. Providing a more direct comparison between NAS and state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization techniques on the same datasets would be informative.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources required to train the controller on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank (e.g., total GPU hours, cost)?
2.  Have you explored any techniques to reduce the training time of the controller, such as transfer learning or more efficient exploration strategies?
3.  What were the considerations that guided the selection of the search space (e.g., filter sizes, layer types)?
4.  Have you performed a more direct comparison between NAS and state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization techniques? If so, what were the results?
5.  The paper notes that generated architectures share characteristics with human-designed ones. Do you have any insights into *why* this is the case? Is the controller learning to rediscover established design principles, or is there some other mechanism at play?
