## Review for ICLR 2017 Submission: Neural Architecture Search

**1. Summary**

This paper presents a novel approach to automating neural architecture design, termed Neural Architecture Search (NAS). The core idea is to utilize a recurrent neural network (controller) to generate descriptions of neural network architectures, and then train this controller using reinforcement learning to maximize the validation accuracy of the generated architectures. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank) tasks. On CIFAR-10, the discovered architecture rivals state-of-the-art human-designed networks. On Penn Treebank, the generated recurrent cell outperforms existing LSTM-based models, and transfers well to character-level language modeling. This work offers a promising direction toward automating the challenging and time-consuming process of neural network architecture engineering.

**2. Decision**

**Accept.** The paper tackles an important problem with a novel and compelling approach, and demonstrates strong results on multiple benchmarks. While the computational cost is currently high, the potential impact of automating architecture search is significant.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper addresses a core bottleneck in deep learning: the expert knowledge and significant time required to design effective neural network architectures. The proposed NAS method offers a data-driven alternative to manual design, capable of discovering architectures competitive with, and in some cases surpassing, those created by human experts. 

The experiments are well-designed and convincingly demonstrate the method’s capabilities. Evaluating NAS on both vision (CIFAR-10) and text (Penn Treebank) datasets is a key strength. This shows the generality of the approach and its potential applicability beyond specific problem domains. The ability to discover a recurrent cell that outperforms LSTM on language modeling is particularly noteworthy, demonstrating the potential to move beyond established architectural paradigms.  The transfer learning result on character-level language modeling further supports the quality and adaptability of the generated architectures. 

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the complex methodology. The inclusion of baseline comparisons and ablation studies (control experiments) helps to establish the efficacy of the proposed approach. The parallelization strategy employed to speed up the computationally intensive training process is a practical consideration and demonstrates the authors' awareness of the method’s limitations.

**4. Additional Feedback**

The following points are offered to further strengthen the paper, and are not critical to the acceptance decision.

* **Hyperparameter Details:** The reviewer's comment regarding the exact hyperparameters used is critical. Providing a complete list of hyperparameters for both the controller RNN and the child networks (learning rate, optimizer settings, batch size, weight decay, dropout rates, embedding dimension, etc.) is crucial for reproducibility. Specifically, detailing the *type* of dropout employed (recurrent, embedding, etc.) is necessary. Releasing a base set of hyperparameters, similar to Zaremba et al. (2014), would be highly beneficial to the research community.
* **Hyperparameter Details for Character LM:** The same level of hyperparameter detail should be provided for the character language modeling experiments. This will allow other researchers to reproduce and build upon the results.
* **Search Space Exploration:** While the paper describes the search space, a more detailed discussion of how this search space was chosen would be valuable. What considerations guided the selection of potential layer types, filter sizes, and other hyperparameters?
* **Computational Cost Discussion:** The paper acknowledges the high computational cost of NAS. Providing a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources required (number of GPUs, training time) would be helpful for readers to assess the practical feasibility of applying this method.
* **Controller Architecture:** More details on the architecture of the controller RNN (number of layers, hidden unit size, activation functions) beyond what's currently provided would be beneficial.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you provide a complete table listing all the hyperparameters used for the CIFAR-10 experiments, including the controller RNN and the child networks? Please specify the type of dropout used.
2.  What criteria were used to define the search space for each task (CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank)? Were there any initial assumptions or heuristics that guided the selection of potential architectural components?
3.  The paper mentions a schedule for increasing the number of layers in the child networks during controller training. Could you elaborate on the specific schedule used?
4.  Could you clarify the baseline function used in the REINFORCE algorithm? Specifically, how is the exponential moving average calculated, and what is the decay rate?
5.  Regarding the transfer learning results, what specific modifications were made to the GNMT framework beyond replacing the LSTM cell with the NAS-discovered cell? Were any hyperparameters re-tuned for the GNMT framework after the cell replacement?
