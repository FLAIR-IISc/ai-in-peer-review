## Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

This paper presents a novel and exciting approach to Neural Architecture Search (NAS) by framing the problem as a reinforcement learning task. The core idea of using a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller to generate DNN architectures, and then using the validation accuracy as a reward signal, is well-motivated and addresses the significant challenges associated with hand-designing effective neural network architectures. The authors provide a remarkably detailed and clear description of their method, highlighting necessary improvements such as skip connections and parallel training. 

I **accept** this paper. The approach is innovative, and the results on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank demonstrate the method’s capability to discover architectures competitive with, and in some cases surpassing, human-designed models. The experimental setup appears robust, and the ablation studies and comparisons to related work are handled well. The provided code release is also a significant contribution.

My assessment is based on the following supporting arguments. First, the paper tackles a clearly defined and important problem: automating the laborious process of neural architecture design. Second, the proposed method is well-placed within the existing literature, building upon advances in both reinforcement learning and recurrent neural networks. The authors effectively contextualize their work within the fields of hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis. Third, the empirical results strongly support the claims. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on both image classification and language modeling tasks, without extensive manual tuning, is compelling evidence of the method's effectiveness. The fact that the generated architectures exhibit interesting differences from those designed by humans also suggests that the approach is uncovering genuinely novel solutions. 

However, to further strengthen the paper, I have a few suggestions. While the performance gains are impressive, it would be beneficial to have data on the **training time** required for both the controller and the child networks. Specifically, providing a correlation between compute resources (e.g., GPU hours) and the quality of the generated architectures would help assess the practical feasibility of the approach. Additionally, the paper mentions bootstrapping, but the role of **human-bootstrapped models** in the process is unclear.  Were any pre-trained models used to initialize the search, and how did this affect the final results? Finally, exploring the sensitivity of the method to different hyperparameters of the controller (e.g., LSTM hidden size, learning rate) could provide valuable insights into its robustness and generalization ability.
