## Review for ICLR 2017

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel approach to automating neural network design. The core idea is to utilize a recurrent neural network (RNN) as a “controller” to generate network architectures, and then train this controller using reinforcement learning, rewarding architectures that perform well on a validation set. The method achieves impressive results on both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank), rivaling and even surpassing state-of-the-art, human-designed architectures. Specifically, on CIFAR-10, the generated architecture achieves a 3.65% test error rate, outperforming previous models. On Penn Treebank, the designed recurrent cell outperforms LSTMs, achieving a perplexity of 62.4.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting and technically sound approach, the lack of broader experimentation and a clear demonstration of generalization capabilities prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. The results are compelling within the scope of the experiments, but the limited dataset variety raises concerns about the robustness and practical applicability of the proposed method.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary strength of this work lies in its innovative approach to neural architecture search. The framing of architecture design as a sequential generation problem, amenable to RNNs and reinforcement learning, is insightful and potentially groundbreaking. The fact that the generated architectures on CIFAR-10 are competitive with manually designed ones suggests the method is learning meaningful architectural patterns, rather than simply stumbling upon good configurations. Furthermore, the improvement over LSTMs on Penn Treebank is a significant achievement, particularly given the difficulty of advancing performance on this benchmark.

However, the experiments primarily focus on two datasets: CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank. While these are important benchmarks, they represent relatively specific types of data and network structures. It remains unclear whether this method would generalize well to other vision tasks (e.g., ImageNet), different sequential data types (e.g., time series, audio), or different network architectures (e.g., transformers). The authors acknowledge the computational expense of training the controller, requiring significant parallelization and numerous training iterations, which may limit its accessibility to researchers with limited resources. While the authors demonstrate transfer learning to a character language modeling task on PTB, the experiment feels somewhat tacked on and doesn’t fully address the broader generalization concern.  

Moreover, the paper does not adequately address the potential for hyperparameter sensitivity. The results are presented as achieving state-of-the-art performance, but without a more thorough comparison against a wide range of carefully tuned baseline architectures, it’s difficult to assess whether the improvements are truly attributable to the architecture search process itself, or simply to a particularly favorable hyperparameter setting discovered during the search.

**Additional Feedback:**

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology. However, several areas could be improved:

*   **Ablation Studies:** Providing more detailed ablation studies would help to understand the contribution of different components of the approach, such as the skip connections and the use of a parameter server.
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:** A more detailed analysis of the computational resources required for the search process would be beneficial, including wall-clock time, GPU hours, and memory usage.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** Elaborate on the hyperparameter search strategy used to find the best models after the controller training. How many trials were conducted, and what criteria were used to select the final models?
*   **Comparison to Bayesian Optimization:** While the paper briefly mentions Bayesian optimization, a more direct comparison, perhaps with a well-tuned Bayesian optimization baseline, would strengthen the argument for the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How does the performance of the NAS-generated architectures degrade when applied to larger and more complex datasets, such as ImageNet? Have you explored any strategies to address the increased computational cost associated with searching larger architectural spaces?
2.  The paper mentions "compilation failures" related to skip connections. Can you elaborate on the frequency of these failures and the impact of the mitigation strategies you employed?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of reward function (e.g., the cubic power applied to validation accuracy)? Did you explore alternative reward functions, and if so, what were the results?
4.  Can you provide more detail on the architectural search space? What constraints were placed on the architecture search process to ensure that the generated architectures were valid and computationally feasible?
5.  The cell found for Penn Treebank is said to be faster. What is the computational cost savings in terms of FLOPs or inference time?
