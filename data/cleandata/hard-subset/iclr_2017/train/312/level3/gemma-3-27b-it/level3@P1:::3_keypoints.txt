- Introduces Neural Architecture Search (RNN controller + reinforcement learning) to automate neural network design.  
- Achieves strong results: 3.65% test error on CIFAR-10 and 62.4 perplexity on Penn Treebank.  
- Decision: Reject — insufficient demonstration of generalization and broader experimentation.  
- Strength: Novel framing of architecture design as sequential generation and promising discovered architectural patterns.  
- Strength: Generated architectures rival or surpass human-designed models on evaluated benchmarks.  
- Main concern: Experiments are limited to CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, raising questions about robustness to other tasks and datasets.  
- Concern: Computational expense and need for heavy parallelization limit accessibility and practicality.  
- Transfer experiment to character-level PTB feels insufficient to address generalization concerns.  
- Potential hyperparameter sensitivity not adequately analyzed; unclear if gains stem from search or lucky hyperparameter settings.  
- Missing ablation studies to quantify contributions of components like skip connections and parameter server.  
- Missing detailed computational cost analysis (wall-clock time, GPU hours, memory).  
- Lack of detail on hyperparameter tuning strategy and selection criteria for final models.  
- Weak comparison to Bayesian optimization; a well-tuned baseline would strengthen claims of novelty/effectiveness.  
- Question: How do NAS architectures scale/degrade on larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) and can search be made tractable for larger spaces?  
- Question: Frequency and impact of "compilation failures" from skip connections and effectiveness of mitigation strategies?  
- Question: Sensitivity of results to reward function choice (e.g., cubic power on validation accuracy) and alternatives tried.  
- Question: More detail requested on architectural search space constraints to ensure validity and feasibility of generated architectures.  
- Question: Quantify computational cost savings (FLOPs or inference time) for the Penn Treebank cell claimed to be faster.
