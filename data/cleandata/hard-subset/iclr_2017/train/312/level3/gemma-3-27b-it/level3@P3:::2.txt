This paper presents a novel approach to Neural Architecture Search (NAS) by framing the problem as a reinforcement learning task. The core idea of using a recurrent network (controller) to generate DNN architectures, and then using the validation accuracy as a reward signal, is highly compelling and well-executed. The paper demonstrates strong results on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, surpassing human-designed architectures in certain scenarios. The authors provide a clear and detailed description of their methodology, including necessary improvements like skip connections and parallel training.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning, demonstrating a promising new direction for NAS. The experimental results are strong, the approach is well-motivated, and the paper is exceptionally well-written.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles a crucial problem: the time-consuming and expert-knowledge-dependent process of neural network architecture design. The proposed method directly addresses this by automating the search process. The approach is strongly motivated by the observation that architectures can be represented as variable-length sequences, making them suitable for generation by a recurrent network. This framing is clever and allows the application of powerful reinforcement learning techniques.

The results presented are compelling. Achieving competitive or superior performance on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, compared to established human-designed architectures, is a strong indicator of the method’s efficacy. The transfer learning results on the character language modeling task further demonstrate the generalizability of the learned recurrent cell. The comparison against random search highlights the benefits of using a policy gradient approach. The inclusion of control experiments further strengthens the validity of the findings.

The paper is meticulously detailed, covering all aspects of the methodology, from the controller architecture to the training procedure. The authors acknowledge limitations and discuss potential improvements, demonstrating a critical and thoughtful approach to their work. The code release and inclusion of the NASCell into TensorFlow are valuable contributions that will facilitate further research in this area.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is excellent, providing more detail on the computational cost would be beneficial. Specifically, a table showing the training time for different architectures, alongside their performance, would allow readers to better assess the trade-off between model quality and computational resources. Furthermore, correlating the compute/time spent on architecture search with the final model performance could reveal insights into the efficiency of the search process.

The role of human-bootstrapped models deserves further clarification. While the authors state the method starts “from scratch,” it would be helpful to understand if any initial architectures were pre-defined or inspired by existing knowledge. Providing results with and without any initial seeding or guidance could illuminate the method’s ability to discover truly novel architectures.

It would also be interesting to see a more in-depth analysis of the generated architectures. The authors mention differences between generated and human-designed architectures. Providing a visual comparison of these architectures, along with a discussion of the underlying principles that might explain these differences, would enrich the paper.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a table detailing the training time (in hours/days) for the different architectures evaluated on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, alongside their corresponding test accuracy/perplexity?
2.  Was any prior knowledge or human-designed architecture used to initialize or guide the search process? If so, could you provide results comparing the performance with and without this initial seeding?
3.  Can you elaborate on the specific differences observed between the architectures generated by your method and those typically designed by humans? Are there any recurring patterns or design principles that characterize the NAS-generated architectures?
4.  The paper mentions a grid search for hyperparameters after the controller training. What was the range of hyperparameters explored during this grid search and how did the final selection impact the reported performance?
