## Review of Neural Architecture Search

This paper presents Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel approach to automating the design of neural network architectures. The method utilizes a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller to generate architectural descriptions, which are then evaluated via reinforcement learning to maximize accuracy on a validation set. The authors demonstrate impressive results on both CIFAR-10 (image classification) and Penn Treebank (language modeling), achieving performance comparable to, and in some cases exceeding, human-designed state-of-the-art architectures. Specifically, their CIFAR-10 model achieves a 3.65% test error, outperforming previous models, and their novel recurrent cell surpasses LSTM on the Penn Treebank, achieving a new state-of-the-art perplexity of 62.4.

**Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper. The approach is innovative, the results are compelling, and the potential impact of automating neural architecture design is significant. The performance gains achieved on both image and text datasets are substantial and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. While computationally expensive, the authors acknowledge this and it is a limitation expected to diminish with increased computational resources.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles a critical problem in deep learning: the difficulty and expertise required for neural network architecture design. Current best practices largely rely on manual exploration and intuition. This work presents a promising alternative, automating this process and potentially unlocking architectures beyond what human experts can readily conceive. 

The approach is well-motivated, drawing connections to related work in hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing methods and position their work as a novel solution capable of exploring a variable-length architecture space.  The combination of a recurrent network controller with reinforcement learning is a clever and effective strategy, allowing the system to learn and refine its architecture generation process over time. 

The empirical evaluation is strong.  The results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are compelling, demonstrating that the NAS method can discover architectures that outperform existing models. The authors go beyond simply reporting performance metrics; they provide detailed analysis and comparison to other state-of-the-art architectures.  The transfer learning experiments on the character language modeling task further support the generalizability of the discovered recurrent cell.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is well-written and the results are impressive, providing more details regarding the hyperparameters used in the experiments would significantly enhance its reproducibility and usability for other researchers. 

Specifically, it would be helpful to provide:

*   **Detailed Dropout Configuration:** What type of dropout was used (recurrent, embedding, etc.) and what were the specific dropout rates? Knowing these values is crucial for replicating the results.
*   **Hyperparameter Settings for Penn Treebank:** Similar to the above, providing a complete list of hyperparameters used for the character language modeling experiments (learning rate, weight decay, batch size, etc.) is essential.
*   **Baseline Hyperparameters:** Releasing a base set of hyperparameters, similar to the approach taken in Zaremba et al. (2014), would allow others to quickly establish a baseline and build upon the presented work.
*   **Computational Resources:** While the authors acknowledge the computational cost, providing a more precise estimate of the resources required (e.g., total GPU hours) would be beneficial for assessing the practicality of the approach.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the choice of the reward function (e.g., cubing the validation accuracy)? What impact did different reward function formulations have on the performance of the search?
2.  The paper mentions skipping connections, but does not detail the specifics of how skip connections were implemented and controlled within the search space. Can you describe this in more detail?
3.  What strategies, if any, were employed to prevent the controller from getting stuck in local optima? Were any techniques used to encourage exploration of the architecture space?
4.   You mention a parameter server approach. What were the key bottlenecks in scaling up the training process, and how did the parameter server address these issues?
5.  The paper discusses the potential for further improvement with different layer types and learning rate prediction. Have you explored these avenues further since submitting the paper, and if so, what were the results?
