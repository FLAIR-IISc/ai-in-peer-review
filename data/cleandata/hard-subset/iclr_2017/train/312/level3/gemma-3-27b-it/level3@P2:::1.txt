This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel method for automating the design of neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (controller) to generate model descriptions, and train this controller with reinforcement learning to maximize the accuracy of the generated architectures on a validation set. The authors demonstrate impressive results on both CIFAR-10 (image classification) and Penn Treebank (language modeling), achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance with architectures discovered automatically.

**Decision:** Accept. This paper presents a compelling and well-executed approach to a challenging problem – neural architecture search. The results are strong, demonstrating significant performance gains on established benchmarks, and the method exhibits promising generality across different domains (vision and language).

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles a significant problem in deep learning: the time-consuming and expert-driven process of neural architecture design. While hyperparameter optimization techniques exist, they are often limited to fixed-length search spaces. NAS overcomes this limitation by leveraging a recurrent network to generate variable-length architectural descriptions. The method is well-motivated, building on recent advances in reinforcement learning and sequence generation. 

The empirical results are particularly convincing. On CIFAR-10, the NAS-designed architecture rivals the best human-invented architectures, achieving a 3.65% test error rate – an improvement of 0.09% and a 1.05x speedup compared to the previous state-of-the-art. The performance on Penn Treebank is even more remarkable, surpassing existing LSTM-based models and achieving a new state-of-the-art perplexity of 62.4. The transfer learning results to character-level language modeling further solidify the robustness and generality of the discovered architectures. 

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The experimental setup is described in detail, and the results are presented in a clear and concise manner. The inclusion of control experiments (against random search) adds further confidence in the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The authors also acknowledge the computational cost associated with NAS, recognizing that this is an area for future improvement.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is excellent, a few points could further enhance its clarity and reproducibility. 

1. **Hyperparameter Details:** The authors mention using standard training procedures for the child networks but lack specific details regarding hyperparameters (learning rate schedule, weight decay, optimizer, etc.) and dropout configurations (recurrent, embedding, etc.). Providing this information, even in an appendix, would significantly aid in replication. Replicating these results without extensive trial-and-error is currently very difficult. I recommend releasing a base set of hyperparameters, similar to those provided in Zaremba et al. (2014), to facilitate future research. This applies to both the CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank experiments, as well as the character language modeling task.

2. **Scaling and Computational Cost:** While the authors acknowledge the high computational cost, a more detailed discussion of the resource requirements would be beneficial. For example, how many GPU-hours were required for the entire search process? This information would help readers assess the feasibility of applying NAS to their own problems.

3. **Skip Connection Details:** The explanation of how skip connections are handled ("compilation failures") could be slightly more detailed. A simple example illustrating how padding is used to ensure compatibility between layers would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources required to run the NAS algorithm, including the number of GPUs and the total training time?
2.  What impact did different baseline functions have on the stability and performance of the reinforcement learning process? Were other baseline functions explored beyond the exponential moving average?
3.  Regarding the character language modeling experiment, can you clarify how the hyperparameters of the discovered RNN cell were tuned for this task? Were they kept fixed from the Penn Treebank experiments, or were they re-optimized?
4.  The paper mentions that the controller RNN predicts layer types.  Could you elaborate on the complete set of layer types that the controller is capable of generating?
