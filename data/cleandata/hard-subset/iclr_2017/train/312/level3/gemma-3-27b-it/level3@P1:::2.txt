## Review of Neural Architecture Search with Reinforcement Learning

This paper presents a novel and compelling approach to automating neural architecture design using reinforcement learning. The authors frame the problem of architecture search as a sequential decision process, where a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller generates architectures represented as variable-length sequences, and the resulting network’s performance on a validation set provides the reward signal. This allows the controller to learn, via policy gradients, to generate increasingly effective architectures. The results are impressive, achieving state-of-the-art or competitive results on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank datasets, even surpassing human-designed architectures.

**Decision:** **Accept** with enthusiasm. This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning and has the potential to greatly impact how neural networks are designed in the future. The problem is well-defined, the approach is sound and thoroughly explained, and the empirical results are compelling.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core strength of this paper lies in its innovative application of reinforcement learning to the challenging problem of neural architecture search.  Previous methods often rely on predefined search spaces or Bayesian optimization, which can be limiting. By formulating architecture design as a sequential decision process and utilizing an RNN controller, the authors introduce a much more flexible and powerful approach. The detailed description of the method, including the use of a policy gradient method (REINFORCE) with a baseline and parallel training via a parameter server, demonstrates a strong technical understanding and careful implementation. 

The experimental results are particularly convincing.  The reported performance on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank not only matches but often exceeds the performance of existing, hand-crafted architectures. The achievement of a 3.65% error rate on CIFAR-10, improving upon previous state-of-the-art models, is a significant result. The success on the Penn Treebank dataset, designing a recurrent cell that outperforms LSTM, further highlights the generality and effectiveness of the proposed method. The transfer learning results to character language modeling are also very strong. 

The paper also thoughtfully addresses related work, positioning their method within the broader landscape of hyperparameter optimization, neuroevolution, and program synthesis. The discussion of limitations and the inclusion of insightful observations about the differences between generated and human-designed architectures (e.g., the preference for rectangular filters) demonstrate a critical and nuanced understanding of the field.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is excellent, a few minor points could further strengthen it:

*   **Training Time:**  The paper mentions training is computationally expensive, especially for the child networks. Providing more detailed information about the total training time required for the controller, including the number of architectures evaluated, would be valuable. 
*   **Compute vs. Performance:** It would be interesting to analyze the relationship between the computational resources used during the search process (e.g., GPU hours) and the quality of the resulting architectures.  Is there a clear correlation between more compute and better performance?
*   **Human-Bootstrapped Models:** The paper notes the limitations of earlier methods requiring good initial models. It would be helpful to explore the impact of incorporating human-designed architectures into the training process as a form of pre-training or initialization for the controller. Did including such models accelerate learning or improve the final performance?
* **Ablation Studies:** Given the complexity of the proposed method, exploring the impact of individual components (e.g., skip connections, the specific choice of RNN architecture for the controller) through ablation studies could offer more insights into their contribution to the overall performance.
* **Visualizations:** Expanding the visualizations to include examples of architectures generated during the training process (not just the final best architecture) could offer valuable insight into how the controller learns and explores the architecture space.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the total training time (e.g., GPU hours) required to train the controller and evaluate the architectures on both the CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank datasets?
2.  Is there a discernible correlation between the amount of computational resources used during the search process and the performance of the discovered architectures?
3.  Have you experimented with incorporating human-designed architectures as starting points or during training to potentially accelerate learning? If so, what were the results?
4.  What are the most significant challenges encountered when scaling this approach to even larger datasets and more complex architecture search spaces?
