- Decision: Accept (with reservations).
- Paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS): an RNN controller trained with reinforcement learning to generate neural network architectures.
- Demonstrates strong results on CIFAR-10 (image) and Penn Treebank (language), including a better recurrent cell than LSTM.
- Novelty and significance: genuinely novel approach that automates architecture design, reducing reliance on expert knowledge.
- Strong empirical evidence: generated architectures are competitive and resemble human-designed models, suggesting learned useful principles.
- Technical soundness: well-described method using policy gradients with baseline and clear distributed training details.
- Major concern: very high computational cost and long training time, requiring substantial infrastructure to reproduce.
- Limited generalization: experiments limited to two datasets; unclear performance across other modalities or tasks.
- Unclear advantage over human-designed models when accounting for computational expense; more direct comparisons needed.
- Recommendation for more ablation studies to quantify contributions of components like skip connections and controller hyperparameters.
- Need for deeper search space analysis: justification of hyperparameter ranges and trade-offs between space size and cost.
- Suggest visualizing the controller's policy evolution to understand how effective architectures are learned.
- Request more analysis of scaling behavior: how training time grows with search space complexity and dataset size.
- Advise a rigorous cost-performance analysis quantifying resources required for given accuracy levels.
- Questions for authors: estimated computational cost (GPU-days) to reproduce CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank results.
- Questions for authors: have methods been explored to reduce training time (transfer learning, efficient RL)?
- Questions for authors: what design choices defined the search space and how sensitive are results to those choices?
- Questions for authors: can you detail specific similarities/motifs between generated architectures and human designs?
- Questions for authors: how would this method scale to very large/complex search spaces (e.g., video, RL tasks)?
