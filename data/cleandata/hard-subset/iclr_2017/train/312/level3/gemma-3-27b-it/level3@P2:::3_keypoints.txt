- Proposes NAS: an RNN controller trained with reinforcement learning to generate neural architectures automatically.
- Reports strong empirical results, including 3.65% error on CIFAR-10 and improved recurrent cells on Penn Treebank outperforming LSTMs.
- Decision: Accept — method is technically sound, innovative, and results merit publication.
- Strength: Automates a traditionally expert-driven, expensive process and can explore a vast architecture search space.
- Discovered architectures often mirror human design principles (e.g., skip connections, filter sizes), suggesting meaningful solutions.
- Paper is well-written and clearly explains methodology and distributed training setup.
- Authors acknowledge limitations and outline avenues for future work.
- Limitation: Experiments are limited to CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, leaving generality unproven.
- Recommendation: Evaluate NAS on more diverse datasets and model types (e.g., ImageNet, GANs, Transformers).
- Concern: High computational cost; more detailed analysis of required resources (total GPU hours, cost) is needed.
- Suggestion: Explore methods to reduce controller training time (efficient exploration, transfer learning).
- Suggestion: Conduct ablation studies to isolate the impact of features like skip connections.
- Suggestion: Provide a more detailed discussion and justification of the chosen search space and its design choices.
- Suggestion: Offer direct comparisons to state-of-the-art Bayesian optimization techniques on the same tasks.
- Questions for authors: provide detailed computational resource breakdown (GPU hours/cost), techniques tried to speed up training, rationale for search space choices, results of any direct Bayesian optimization comparisons, and insights into why the controller rediscovers human design principles.
