## Review of “Neural Architecture Search”

This paper presents a novel and exciting approach to Neural Architecture Search (NAS) by framing the problem as a reinforcement learning task. The core idea – using a recurrent neural network (RNN) as a controller to generate DNN architectures, and then using the validation accuracy as a reward signal – is well-motivated and addresses a significant challenge in deep learning: the laborious and expert-driven process of hand-designing neural network architectures. The authors provide a detailed and clear description of their method, including important improvements like skip connections and parallel training, making the work readily reproducible. 

I **accept** this paper. The results are compelling, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank. The fact that the generated architectures rival, and in some cases outperform, human-designed architectures is a strong indicator of the method’s effectiveness. Furthermore, the transfer learning results on the character language modeling task solidify the generalizability of the discovered recurrent cell. 

The paper successfully addresses the problem of automating architecture design. The approach is well-placed within the existing literature, acknowledging and building upon previous work in hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis. The empirical results convincingly support the claims made by the authors. The datasets used are appropriate for demonstrating the method’s capabilities, and the comparisons against established architectures are thorough. The observed differences between the generated architectures and those designed by humans are also an interesting and valuable observation, suggesting potentially novel design principles.

However, to further strengthen the paper, I would appreciate additional information. Specifically:

*   **Training Time:** What is the total training time for the controller RNN on each dataset? Understanding the computational cost associated with the search process is crucial for assessing the practical applicability of the method.
*   **Compute/Time vs. Quality:** Is there a correlation between the amount of compute (e.g., number of architectures evaluated) and the quality of the resulting architecture? A discussion of this relationship would be insightful.
*   **Human-Bootstrapped Models:** The paper mentions that previous methods benefit from a good initial model. Were any human-designed models used to bootstrap the search process in this work? If so, what was their impact on the final results?



These questions are intended to help the authors refine their presentation and provide a more complete picture of their work. Overall, this is an excellent and impactful paper that represents a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning.
