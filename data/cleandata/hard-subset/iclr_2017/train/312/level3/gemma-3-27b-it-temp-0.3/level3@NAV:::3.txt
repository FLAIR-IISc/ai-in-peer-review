## Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

This paper proposes a novel approach to neural architecture design, leveraging a recurrent neural network (RNN) as a controller to generate network architectures and employing reinforcement learning to optimize this controller for task-specific performance. The core idea of framing architecture search as a sequential decision-making problem is promising and represents a significant departure from traditional manual or grid-search based methods. The experiments on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank demonstrate solid results, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance.

**Decision: Accept with Minor Revisions**

I recommend accepting this paper, primarily due to the novelty and potential impact of the proposed method. The results presented are encouraging and suggest that automated architecture search is a viable and promising research direction. However, further experimentation is needed to solidify these claims and demonstrate broader applicability.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The strength of this work lies in its innovative approach. The use of an RNN to generate architectures allows for exploration of a vast and complex design space. The fact that the generated architectures on CIFAR-10 rival human-designed ones, and even outperform them in terms of speed, is a compelling result. Similarly, the success on the Penn Treebank dataset, surpassing LSTM performance, highlights the method’s versatility. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The inclusion of control experiments, such as adding more functions to the search space and comparing against random search, strengthens the validity of the findings.

However, the training time remains a significant concern, even with substantial computing resources. While the authors address this with distributed training, it still represents a barrier to wider adoption. More importantly, the experiments are currently limited to two datasets. The lack of evaluation on other datasets, particularly those involving different modalities or sequential data beyond language modeling, limits the generalizability of the findings. Demonstrating performance on datasets requiring different architectural patterns (e.g., time series analysis, audio processing) would significantly strengthen the paper.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:** It would be beneficial to include more detailed ablation studies to understand the contribution of specific components, such as skip connections and the choice of activation functions.
*   **Search Space Analysis:** A more thorough analysis of the search space explored by the RNN controller would be valuable. What types of architectures were frequently generated? Were there any surprising or unexpected patterns?
*   **Computational Cost Breakdown:** Provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost associated with training the controller and evaluating the generated architectures.
*   **Comparison to NAS with Evolutionary Algorithms:** A more detailed comparison to other NAS methods, specifically those based on evolutionary algorithms, would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What strategies could be employed to reduce the training time of the controller RNN without sacrificing performance?
2.  What modifications would be necessary to adapt this method to datasets with different characteristics, such as those involving time series data or audio processing?
3.  Can you provide more insight into the types of architectures generated by the controller? Were there any architectural patterns that emerged as particularly effective?
4.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of hyperparameters for the reinforcement learning algorithm (e.g., learning rate, baseline function)?
