## Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

This paper presents a compelling approach to automating neural architecture design using a recurrent neural network controller trained with reinforcement learning. The core idea – framing architecture search as a sequential decision-making problem – is well-motivated and addresses a significant challenge in deep learning: the expert knowledge and time required for manual architecture engineering. The authors demonstrate strong results on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance while exploring beyond traditional RNN/CNN search spaces. The ability to discover dataset-specific architectures without manual intervention is a particularly valuable contribution.

I recommend this paper for acceptance. The results are strong, the writing is clear, and the approach is novel and promising. The demonstrated gains on both vision and language tasks highlight the potential of this method to significantly impact the field.

My assessment is based on the following: the paper tackles a specific and important problem (automated architecture search), the approach is well-motivated within the context of reinforcement learning and program synthesis, and the empirical results convincingly support the claims made by the authors. The performance improvements on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, particularly surpassing existing state-of-the-art models, provide strong evidence of the method’s effectiveness.

However, to further enhance the reproducibility and impact of this work, I strongly encourage the authors to provide more detailed information regarding the hyperparameters used in their experiments. Specifically, the exact hyperparameters for the controller RNN (learning rate, optimizer settings, hidden layer sizes, etc.) and the types of dropout used (recurrent, embedding, etc.) are crucial for replication. Replicating these results currently requires substantial trial-and-error. Following the precedent set by Zaremba et al. (2014), releasing a base set of hyperparameters would be extremely valuable to the research community and facilitate future work building upon this approach. This request extends to the character language model experiment as well.

Furthermore, could the authors clarify the schedule used for increasing the number of layers during training? What criteria were used to determine when to increase the layer count? Also, regarding the baseline function used in the REINFORCE algorithm, was it simply an exponential moving average of the *rewards* or the *accuracies*? Finally, the paper mentions a grid search for hyperparameter tuning *after* the controller identifies the best architecture. Could the authors elaborate on the scope of this grid search (which hyperparameters were tuned, the range of values explored)?
