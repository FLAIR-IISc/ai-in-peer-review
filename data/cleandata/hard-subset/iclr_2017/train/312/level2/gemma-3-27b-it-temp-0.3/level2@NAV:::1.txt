## Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

This paper presents a novel approach to neural architecture design, termed Neural Architecture Search (NAS), which leverages a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller trained with reinforcement learning to generate network architectures. The core idea – framing architecture search as a sequential decision process – is compelling and well-motivated, addressing the significant expertise and time currently required for manual architecture engineering. The authors demonstrate strong results on both CIFAR-10 (image classification) and Penn Treebank (language modeling), achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance with architectures discovered automatically. Specifically, the 3.65% error rate on CIFAR-10 and 62.4 perplexity on Penn Treebank are impressive achievements.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is innovative and the results are strong, demonstrating a significant step towards automating neural network design. The performance gains on both datasets, particularly surpassing human-designed architectures on CIFAR-10, provide compelling evidence of the method’s effectiveness.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the methodology. The use of policy gradients and the parameter server approach for distributed training are appropriate and well-described. The inclusion of skip connections and the extension to recurrent cell design further demonstrate the flexibility of the proposed framework. The ablation studies and control experiments (comparing against random search) strengthen the validity of the findings. 

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification in a few areas. The computational cost of NAS is substantial, and a more detailed discussion of resource requirements (e.g., GPU hours) would be valuable. While the authors mention the search space, a more explicit characterization of its size and the impact of different search space choices would be helpful. Additionally, the choice of the reward function (validation accuracy cubed) seems somewhat arbitrary; a justification for this choice, or an exploration of alternative reward functions, would be beneficial. Finally, while the transfer learning results are promising, a more detailed analysis of the learned cell’s properties and why it generalizes well would be insightful.

Specifically, I would like to know: 1) What is the total computational cost (in GPU hours) for the CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank experiments? 2) How sensitive are the results to the choice of the baseline function in the REINFORCE algorithm? 3) Could the authors elaborate on the rationale behind using the cubed validation accuracy as the reward signal, and whether other reward functions were considered?
