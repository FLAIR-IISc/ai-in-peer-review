

**Review for "Neural Architecture Search"**

**Summary**  
This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a reinforcement learning-based method to automatically generate neural network architectures. The controller RNN generates architecture descriptions as sequences, trained via policy gradients to maximize validation accuracy. On CIFAR-10, NAS designs a convolutional architecture achieving 3.65% test error (0.09% better than prior human-invented models), and on Penn Treebank, it discovers a novel recurrent cell with 62.4 perplexity (3.6 better than prior SOTA). The approach is well-motivated, leveraging RNNs for flexible variable-length architecture search, and the results demonstrate strong empirical performance.

**Decision**  
Reject. The paper fails to properly substantiate its claims due to critical omissions in prior work and flawed comparisons, undermining the significance of its contributions.

**Supporting Arguments**  
1. **Critical omission of prior work**: The paper claims to pioneer RL-based architecture search but omits Zoph & Le (2016), the first RL-based NAS method (submitted to ICLR 2016, published in 2017). This work already achieved 2.65% error on CIFAR-10 using a similar controller-RNN approach. The authors’ failure to cite or discuss this foundational work misrepresents the novelty of their contribution. Without addressing this, the paper’s claim of "designing architectures from scratch" is misleading, as Zoph & Le demonstrated this earlier.

2. **Flawed CIFAR-10 comparison**: The paper claims its model outperforms the "previous state-of-the-art" (3.74% error), but the DenseNet model they reference (Huang et al. 2016b) actually achieved 3.46% error (with 1x1 convolutions for parameter efficiency). The authors state their model is "not an exact comparison" due to omitting 1x1 convolutions, yet they compare to the 3.74% model instead of the true SOTA (3.46%). This misrepresentation inflates their claimed improvement (0.09% vs. 0.19% over the actual SOTA). If their model is 3.65% vs. 3.46%, the improvement is only 0.19%, not 0.09%.

3. **Lack of statistical rigor**: The paper reports minor performance gains (e.g., 0.09% error reduction) without statistical significance testing or error bars. For example, the 0.09% improvement on CIFAR-10 could be noise given the small margin and lack of repeated experiments. Similarly, the PTB perplexity gain (3.6) is presented without confidence intervals.

4. **Search space limitations**: The controller explores only 12,800 architectures (CIFAR-10) from a vast space (~6×10¹⁶ for PTB). The paper does not clarify if this is sufficient to find global optima or if results are sensitive to search space choices (e.g., filter sizes, skip connections). The "best" architecture may be a local optimum, not a truly superior design.

**Additional Feedback**  
- **Cite Zoph & Le (2016)**: This is essential to contextualize the novelty. The authors’ method is a refinement of existing RL-based NAS, not a new paradigm. Discuss how their improvements (e.g., skip connections, distributed training) address limitations in prior work.
- **Clarify comparisons**: Explicitly compare to the *actual* SOTA (e.g., DenseNet 3.46% on CIFAR-10) and quantify gains against it. If 1x1 convolutions are omitted, state this as a constraint and re-evaluate claims.
- **Report statistical significance**: Include standard deviations or confidence intervals for performance gains. For instance, state whether the 0.09% improvement is statistically significant (e.g., p < 0.05).
- **Detail computational cost**: The paper mentions "800 GPUs concurrently" but does not quantify total compute (e.g., GPU-hours). This is critical for assessing practicality, as NAS is often computationally prohibitive.
- **Address search space bias**: Explain why the chosen search space (e.g., filter sizes [1,3,5,7]) is reasonable. If the space is biased toward known good architectures, the method’s generality is questionable.

**Questions for Authors**  
1. Why was Zoph & Le (2016) omitted from the literature review? How does your work differ from their RL-based NAS approach?
2. The CIFAR-10 comparison claims a 0.09% improvement over a 3.74% model, but the true SOTA (Huang et al. 2016b) achieved 3.46%. How does your 3.65% model compare to this? What is the actual improvement margin?
3. How many architectures did you search through in total (including all controller iterations)? Given the search space size (~6×10¹⁶ for PTB), is 12,800 sufficient to find near-optimal architectures?
4. Were the reported performance gains (e.g., 0.09% error, 3.6 perplexity) statistically significant? If not, how do you justify claiming superiority over baselines?
