

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a method that uses a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to automatically generate neural network architectures through reinforcement learning. The controller learns to produce model descriptions (as sequences of tokens) that maximize the validation accuracy of the corresponding child networks. The authors demonstrate their approach on CIFAR-10 image classification and Penn Treebank language modeling tasks, reporting state-of-the-art results in both domains without human-designed architectural prior knowledge.

Decision: Accept (with minor revisions)

The paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of automated machine learning. The core idea of using reinforcement learning to search for neural network architectures is novel and well-motivated, addressing a significant pain point in deep learning (the difficulty of architecture design). The method is well-placed in the literature, with thoughtful comparisons to related work in hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results that clearly support their claims, with the CIFAR-10 model achieving 3.65% test error (0.09% better than previous state-of-the-art) and the PTB model achieving 62.4 perplexity (3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art).

The methodology is well-explained and the experimental design is rigorous. The authors provide thorough ablation studies, including a control experiment comparing their method to random search (which shows clear advantages), and they demonstrate transfer learning capabilities to character language modeling and machine translation tasks. The distributed training approach with parameter servers effectively addresses computational challenges, and the paper provides sufficient technical details for reproducibility.

However, there are several areas where the paper could be strengthened:

1. The paper claims to "start from scratch" but doesn't sufficiently clarify the extent to which the search space is constrained by the authors' prior knowledge. The search space for CIFAR-10 includes specific constraints on filter sizes, numbers of filters, and layer types. A more detailed discussion of how these constraints were determined and their impact on the search would be helpful.

2. The computational cost is substantial (800 GPUs running concurrently), and the paper doesn't adequately address the practicality of this approach for researchers without access to massive computational resources. A brief discussion of computational requirements versus the gains would help readers assess the method's broader applicability.

3. While the results are impressive, the paper would benefit from more detailed analysis of *why* the discovered architectures perform well. For instance, what specific architectural features (e.g., the use of rectangular filters, skip connections, specific activation functions) contribute most to the performance gains? A more in-depth analysis would provide valuable insights for the community.

4. The comparison to random search is useful, but it's unclear whether the random search was conducted over the same search space as the controller. Clarifying the random search implementation would strengthen this comparison.

5. The paper mentions the controller uses an exponential moving average for the baseline, but doesn't discuss how the baseline was initialized or how this choice affected convergence. A brief explanation would improve the methodological clarity.

I would like to ask the authors to clarify the following points:

1. How were the specific constraints on the search space (e.g., filter sizes, number of filters) determined? Were these choices based on prior knowledge of effective architectures, or were they selected to make the search tractable?

2. The paper states that the CIFAR-10 architecture is "1.05x faster" than the DenseNet model. Could the authors clarify what exactly they mean by "faster" (e.g., inference speed, training speed, or computational complexity in FLOPs)? This would help readers better understand the practical advantages of their architecture.

3. In the PTB experiments, the authors mention "we use the ADAM optimizer with a learning rate of 0.001" for the character language modeling task. However, they don't report the learning rate used for the initial PTB language modeling experiments. Please clarify this for consistency.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of automated neural architecture search. The method is well-designed, the results are impressive, and the approach has clear potential to influence future work in deep learning. With the minor clarifications requested above, this paper would be an excellent fit for ICLR 2017.
