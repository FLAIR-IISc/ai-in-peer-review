

The paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method that uses a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to automatically generate neural network architectures. The controller is trained via reinforcement learning to maximize the expected validation accuracy of the generated architectures, enabling flexible search over variable-length architecture spaces. The approach is demonstrated on two standard benchmarks: CIFAR-10 image classification and Penn Treebank language modeling. On CIFAR-10, NAS designs a convolutional architecture achieving 3.65% test error (0.09% better than previous state-of-the-art) while being 1.05x faster. On Penn Treebank, NAS discovers a novel recurrent cell achieving 62.4 perplexity (3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art), with strong transfer learning results on character language modeling and neural machine translation tasks.

The paper is well-positioned within the literature, correctly identifying limitations of existing hyperparameter optimization (fixed-length search spaces) and neuro-evolution (slow search methods). The reinforcement learning approach is well-motivated and effectively addresses the problem of architecture design. The empirical results are scientifically rigorous, with thorough ablation studies including control experiments against random search and extensions to the search space.

The paper should be accepted because it presents a novel, well-motivated approach to a significant problem in deep learning, with strong empirical evidence demonstrating clear improvements over previous methods on standard benchmarks.

Additional feedback to improve the paper:
- Provide explicit computational cost metrics for the search process (e.g., GPU-hours required), which would help readers assess practical applicability.
- Clarify the meaning of "two times faster" in the Penn Treebank results (referring to cell computation or full model inference).
- Include more detailed structural comparison between the discovered cell and existing cells (beyond the visualization in Figure 8) to better highlight the novelty.

Questions for authors:
- How many GPU-hours does the search process require for the CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank experiments?
- Can the authors clarify whether the speed improvement mentioned refers to the cell itself or the full model?
- What specific architectural features of the discovered cell contribute to its performance gains over LSTMs, beyond what's shown in Figure 8?
