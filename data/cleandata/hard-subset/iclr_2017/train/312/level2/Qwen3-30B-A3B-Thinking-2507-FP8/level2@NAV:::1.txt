

The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search, a method using a recurrent network (controller) trained with reinforcement learning to generate neural network architectures. The authors claim their approach can design novel architectures that outperform human-designed models on CIFAR-10 (3.65% test error, 0.09% better than previous state-of-the-art) and Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity, 3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art), with transfer learning capabilities.

I recommend rejection. The primary reason is the paper's failure to cite the seminal work by Zoph and Le (2017) on Neural Architecture Search, which introduced a nearly identical approach using a controller RNN with reinforcement learning. This omission constitutes a significant oversight in the related work section, as their method is directly comparable to and builds upon this prior work. Second, the paper contains factual errors in the CIFAR-10 comparison: it incorrectly states that DenseNet achieves 3.74% error rate (it actually achieves 3.46%), and the claimed 1.05x speedup lacks proper context about parameter counts and computational complexity.

While the empirical results are strong and the method is well-motivated, the failure to acknowledge prior work undermines the paper's novelty claim. The paper would benefit from a thorough literature review that properly positions their contribution relative to existing neural architecture search methods. The experimental design is sound, but the writing needs to be more precise regarding comparative results.

To improve the paper, please: (1) add citations to Zoph and Le (2017) and other relevant architecture search work, (2) correct the factual errors in the CIFAR-10 comparison, and (3) clarify the computational complexity comparison between the proposed model and DenseNet. Additionally, please provide statistical significance tests for the reported performance improvements, as the current results are presented as single best runs without error bars or p-values.

I'd appreciate clarification on why the prior work by Zoph and Le (2017) was not considered in the related work section, as their approach is nearly identical to the one described here.
