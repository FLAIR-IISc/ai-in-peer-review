- Summary: Proposes an RL-based Neural Architecture Search with a controller RNN that generates architectures, reporting 3.65% CIFAR-10 error and 62.4 PTB perplexity.  
- Decision: Reject — paper’s claims are undermined by critical omissions and flawed comparisons.  
- Omission of prior work: Fails to cite Zoph & Le (2016), which previously introduced RL-based NAS and achieved strong CIFAR-10 results, misrepresenting novelty.  
- Flawed CIFAR-10 comparison: Compares to the wrong baseline (3.74%) instead of true SOTA DenseNet (3.46%), inflating the reported improvement.  
- Lack of statistical rigor: Reports very small gains (e.g., 0.09% error) without significance testing, error bars, or repeated trials.  
- Search space limitations: Only explores ~12,800 architectures in an immense search space (~6×10^16 for PTB), raising concerns about sufficiency and local optima.  
- Additional feedback — cite Zoph & Le (2016) and position contributions as refinements rather than a new paradigm.  
- Additional feedback — explicitly compare to the actual SOTA (e.g., DenseNet 3.46%) and state any architectural constraints (like omitting 1x1 convolutions).  
- Additional feedback — provide standard deviations/confidence intervals to demonstrate statistical significance.  
- Additional feedback — quantify total computational cost (GPU-hours), not just concurrent GPU count.  
- Additional feedback — justify and analyze potential search-space bias (choice of filter sizes, skip-connections, etc.).  
- Question 1: Why was Zoph & Le (2016) omitted and how does this work differ from their RL-based NAS?  
- Question 2: How does your 3.65% CIFAR-10 result compare to DenseNet’s 3.46% — what is the true improvement margin?  
- Question 3: How many architectures were searched in total across all controller iterations, and is that sufficient given the search-space size?  
- Question 4: Were the reported gains statistically significant; if not, how do you justify claiming superiority over baselines?
