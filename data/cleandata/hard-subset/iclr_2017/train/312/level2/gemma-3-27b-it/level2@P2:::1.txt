This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network controller trained with reinforcement learning. The core idea – using an RNN to generate network descriptions and optimizing via REINFORCE – is compelling and well-motivated by the increasing complexity of manual architecture engineering. The reported results are impressive, achieving state-of-the-art or competitive performance on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, including a novel recurrent cell that outperforms LSTMs.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning, demonstrating a novel and effective approach to neural architecture search. The results are strong and clearly demonstrate the potential of the method.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of using an RNN controller and reinforcement learning for architecture search is a creative and substantial departure from existing methods like grid search or Bayesian optimization, which typically operate within a pre-defined architecture space.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates strong performance on two diverse datasets. Achieving a better accuracy than human-designed architectures on CIFAR-10 and outperforming LSTMs on Penn Treebank is compelling evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The gains, while not enormous, are statistically significant and demonstrate a clear advantage.
* **Well-Motivated:** The introduction clearly articulates the problem – the difficulty of manual architecture design – and positions NAS as a solution. The explanation of the method is relatively clear and the connection to related work (hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, program synthesis) is well-established.
* **Practical Considerations:** The authors address practical challenges like accelerating training with parallelism and handling skip connections, showcasing attention to real-world implementation concerns. The release of the code and the NAS cell into TensorFlow further supports the practical impact of the work.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Search Space Details:** While the paper describes the search space, a more detailed discussion of the choices made and their potential impact on the results would be valuable. Why were specific filter sizes, numbers of filters, and combination methods chosen? Were any ablation studies performed to assess the sensitivity of the results to these choices?
* **Baseline Comparison:** While LSTM is used as a baseline, a comparison to other NAS methods would be beneficial. Showing how NAS performs relative to other recent automated architecture design approaches would further strengthen the claims of novelty and superiority.
* **Controller Architecture:** The controller is described as a two-layer LSTM. A more in-depth explanation of the controller's architecture, including the size of the hidden state and the embedding dimensions, would be helpful. Understanding the capacity of the controller is crucial for understanding its ability to explore the architecture space.
* **Reward Shaping:** The use of cubing the validation accuracy in the reward function seems somewhat arbitrary. A justification for this choice or an exploration of alternative reward shaping strategies could be included.
* **Generalization:** While transfer learning results are shown, further investigating the generalization capability of the discovered architectures to other datasets would strengthen the paper.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions "compilation failures" related to skip connections. Could you elaborate on the specific types of failures encountered and how the implemented solutions fully resolve them?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of baseline function in the REINFORCE algorithm? Were alternative baseline functions considered?
3.  The search space for the recurrent cell appears quite complex. Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of how the controller navigates this space and what types of structures it tends to favor?
4.  What are the computational costs associated with running the NAS algorithm, and how does this compare to the cost of manually designing a comparable architecture? A discussion of the scalability of the method would be helpful.
5.  Are there any indications of what architectural principles or patterns the NAS algorithm seems to discover? Any insights into why the discovered architectures perform well?
