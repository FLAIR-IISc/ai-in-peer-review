This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network controller trained with reinforcement learning. The core idea is compelling: formulate architecture design as a sequential decision process and leverage policy gradients to optimize a controller for generating high-performing architectures. The work demonstrates strong results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance with architectures discovered automatically. The release of the discovered cell into TensorFlow is also a notable contribution.

The decision is to **accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and effective approach to a challenging problem – automating neural architecture design. The results are strong, and the method appears to have the potential to significantly impact the field.

The primary strength of the work lies in its empirical performance. Achieving competitive results on both image classification and language modeling tasks is a significant accomplishment. The demonstration that the discovered recurrent cell outperforms established LSTM architectures is particularly noteworthy. The ablation studies and control experiments, while brief, provide some evidence for the effectiveness of the approach and the importance of the different components. The inclusion of details regarding parallel training is also valuable, as it speaks to the scalability of the method. 

However, several areas could be strengthened. First, the search space definition is somewhat opaque. While the paper outlines the range of hyperparameters the controller can predict, a more detailed discussion of the design choices behind this space would be beneficial. What considerations informed the selection of filter sizes, the number of filters, and other architectural parameters? A more thorough exploration of the search space, and potentially an analysis of the architectures generated that *didn't* perform well, could provide further insight. 

Second, the baseline comparisons, while including strong competitors, could be expanded. Including comparisons with more recent or specialized architectures, particularly on the Penn Treebank dataset, would further solidify the claims of superiority. 

Third, the practical limitations of the method are not fully addressed. The computational cost of training the controller, and subsequently evaluating many child networks, is substantial. A more detailed analysis of the computational resources required for NAS would be helpful for assessing its applicability in different settings. The paper mentions parallelization, but quantifying the speedup and the required infrastructure would strengthen this aspect.

Finally, the connection between the controller’s learned policy and human-designed architectures is not explored. Does the controller learn to reproduce architectural patterns commonly found in human-designed networks, or does it discover truly novel structures? Analyzing the learned representations of the controller might offer valuable insights.

To improve the paper, consider the following:

*   **Expand the discussion of the search space.** Provide a rationale for the design choices made and consider exploring alternative search spaces.
*   **Strengthen the baseline comparisons.** Include more recent and specialized architectures, especially on Penn Treebank.
*   **Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost.** Quantify the resources required for training the controller and evaluating child networks.
*   **Investigate the learned policy of the controller.** Analyze the representations learned by the controller to identify any connections with human-designed architectures.
*   Clarify the scaling behavior of the method with increasing dataset size. Is the computational cost manageable for larger datasets like ImageNet?
