## Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

This paper presents a novel approach to neural architecture design, dubbed Neural Architecture Search (NAS), leveraging a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller trained with reinforcement learning to generate network architectures. The core idea – framing architecture design as a sequential prediction problem – is compelling and elegantly implemented. The authors demonstrate impressive results on both CIFAR-10 (image classification) and Penn Treebank (language modeling), achieving competitive and in some cases state-of-the-art performance with automatically designed architectures. Specifically, the results on PTB, outperforming LSTM cells, are particularly noteworthy. 

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper, primarily due to its significant novelty and strong empirical validation. The results demonstrate a clear ability to automate a traditionally manual and expertise-driven process – neural architecture engineering – with promising outcomes. The combination of RNNs and reinforcement learning to address this complex search space is a strong contribution.

The paper provides thorough experimental detail, including clear descriptions of the datasets, search spaces, and training procedures. The ablation studies, such as adding skip connections, further strengthen the findings. The authors also acknowledge the computational cost of the approach, which is important for transparency.  The inclusion of the discovered cell into TensorFlow is a commendable effort toward reproducibility and accessibility.

However, some areas could benefit from further elaboration. While the paper demonstrates improved performance, a more detailed analysis of *why* these architectures perform well would be valuable. For example, what characteristics of the generated architectures contribute to their success? Additionally, the scalability of the method to even larger datasets and more complex tasks could be discussed further, as the training process is computationally expensive. It would also be helpful to explore the sensitivity of the results to different reward functions and hyperparameter settings for the controller RNN itself. 

Further, could the authors clarify the selection of the “base number” of 8 for recurrent cell generation? What impact does this have on the search space and resulting performance? Finally, how robust is the transfer learning result on character language modeling – are the improvements consistent across different initializations and training runs? Addressing these points would further strengthen the paper's impact.
