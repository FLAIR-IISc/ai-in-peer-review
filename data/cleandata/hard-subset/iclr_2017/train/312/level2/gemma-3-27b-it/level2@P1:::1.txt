This paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller trained with reinforcement learning. The controller generates the description of a neural network, which is then trained and evaluated, and the controller’s parameters are updated based on the performance of the generated architecture. The authors demonstrate competitive results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, even surpassing state-of-the-art manually designed architectures in some cases.

**Decision:** Reject. While the idea is interesting and the results are promising, the paper lacks sufficient detail and clarity regarding several crucial aspects of the methodology, hindering a proper assessment of the novelty and reproducibility of the work. The claims of surpassing state-of-the-art models require more rigorous justification, and several experimental details are glossed over.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Lack of Detail in Search Space:** The paper mentions a search space of convolutional layers, skip connections, and layer types. However, it provides insufficient detail on the precise constraints and configurations explored within this space. Specifically, how are the filters, strides, and numbers of filters combined? What are the precise rules for generating the architecture string? This vagueness makes it difficult to understand the scope of the search and the potential impact of the design choices on the results.

2. **Reward Function and Training Instability:** The use of the cubed validation accuracy as a reward signal is not well-justified. Why cube the accuracy? Could this lead to instability or reward hacking? A detailed ablation study showing the sensitivity of the results to different reward function designs would be beneficial. The impact of the baseline function needs to be further clarified.

3. **Distributed Training Implementation Details:** The distributed training setup with parameter servers is mentioned but lacks sufficient technical detail. How many GPUs were used in total? What was the communication overhead? What safeguards were in place to prevent stale gradients? The asynchronous updates can significantly affect convergence, and a more thorough discussion of these aspects is needed.

4. **Reproducibility Concerns:** While the authors mention releasing code, the paper lacks sufficient detail to enable independent reproduction of the results. The hyperparameters used for training both the controller RNN and the child networks (e.g., learning rates, batch sizes, weight decay) are not always clearly stated or consistent across experiments.

5. **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to state-of-the-art models is not entirely fair. For example, the DenseNet model used for comparison on CIFAR-10 utilizes 1x1 convolutions to reduce the number of parameters. The authors acknowledge this but downplay it. A more equitable comparison should control for the number of parameters or computational cost.



**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarity of Figure 1:** Figure 1 is crucial for understanding the overall approach, but it lacks sufficient labeling and detail. It would be helpful to explicitly show the RNN controller and how it generates the architecture string.
*   **Skip Connection Mechanism:** The description of the skip connection mechanism using attention is somewhat unclear. A more visual explanation, perhaps with a diagram, would be helpful.
*   **Cell Generation Details:** The process of generating recurrent cells (Section 3.4) is complex and requires further clarification. The example provided is helpful, but a more systematic explanation of the tree structure and the controller's predictions would be beneficial.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Consider including ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of different components of the NAS framework, such as skip connections, pooling layers, and the choice of activation functions.
*   **Error Analysis:** Provide an error analysis of the architectures generated by NAS. What types of errors do they make? Are there any common patterns? This could provide insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more detailed description of the search space, including the possible configurations for each architectural component?
*   What is the rationale behind using the cubed validation accuracy as the reward signal? Have you experimented with other reward functions?
*   Could you provide more details about the distributed training setup, including the number of GPUs used, the communication overhead, and the safeguards against stale gradients?
*   What steps have you taken to ensure the reproducibility of your results?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters for training the controller RNN and the child networks?
