### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces "Neural Architecture Search," a novel method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (RNN) as a controller. The controller generates a string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. This child network is then trained and its performance on a validation set is used as a reward signal to train the controller via reinforcement learning (specifically, the REINFORCE algorithm). This elegant approach allows the controller to learn over time to generate better-performing architectures. The authors demonstrate the power and generality of this method in two highly competitive domains: image classification on CIFAR-10 and language modeling on Penn Treebank (PTB). For CIFAR-10, the method discovers a novel convolutional architecture that achieves state-of-the-art results, outperforming top human-designed models. For PTB, it designs a novel recurrent cell that surpasses the widely-used LSTM and achieves a new state-of-the-art in perplexity, with the discovered cell also showing strong transferability to other tasks like character-level language modeling and machine translation.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a landmark paper that introduces a powerful and general new paradigm for neural network design. My recommendation is based on two key strengths:

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Method:** The paper formulates the problem of architecture design as a learning problem, solved with a clever application of reinforcement learning. Using an RNN controller to sequentially generate architecture descriptions is an elegant way to explore a vast, variable-length search space that includes modern design elements like complex skip connections. This represents a significant conceptual advance over prior work in hyperparameter optimization and neuro-evolution.
2.  **Exceptional Empirical Results:** The claims are substantiated by state-of-the-art results on two very different and challenging benchmarks. Discovering a CNN for CIFAR-10 that rivals the best human-invented architectures is impressive. Even more significant is the discovery of a novel RNN cell for PTB that outperforms the LSTM, a feat that has proven exceptionally difficult for the research community. The successful transfer of this cell to other tasks provides strong evidence that the method is not simply overfitting to a specific dataset but is finding genuinely useful architectural motifs.

### Supporting Arguments

The core idea of training a controller network to design other networks is highly innovative. The framework is general, capable of generating both convolutional and recurrent architectures by defining an appropriate search space and generation process. The extension to include skip connections via an attention-like mechanism is a critical component that allows the method to discover architectures with the complex connectivity patterns seen in SOTA models like ResNet and DenseNet.

The experimental validation is thorough and compelling.
*   On CIFAR-10, the discovered model achieves a 3.65% test error, which was better than the comparable state-of-the-art (DenseNet) at the time of submission, while also being faster. This demonstrates that the approach is competitive in a domain dominated by meticulous human engineering.
*   The results on Penn Treebank are arguably the paper's most significant contribution. The discovered cell achieves a test perplexity of 62.4, a substantial improvement of 3.6 over the previous best result. That this novel cell can be dropped into entirely different tasks (character modeling, NMT) and yield improvements without task-specific tuning is a powerful testament to its robustness and general utility.
*   The control experiment comparing the policy gradient method to random search is crucial. The results in Figure 6 clearly show that the RL-based learning process is far more effective than undirected search, validating the core mechanism of the proposed method.

The paper is well-written, the method is explained clearly, and the experimental setup is detailed. The authors successfully bridge ideas from sequence modeling, reinforcement learning, and computer vision/NLP to create a powerful new tool.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is excellent, the following points could be addressed to further strengthen it. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from my decision to accept.

*   **Computational Cost:** The primary limitation of this method is its immense computational expense (e.g., training 800 networks concurrently on 800 GPUs for the CIFAR-10 search). This effectively puts the method out of reach for most academic labs and individual researchers. It would be beneficial for the authors to explicitly acknowledge this limitation in the main text and perhaps briefly speculate on future research directions that could drastically improve the sample efficiency of the search (e.g., weight sharing between child models, learning on proxy tasks, or more efficient reward estimation).
*   **Analysis of Discovered Architectures:** The paper provides some visualizations and high-level descriptions of the discovered architectures. A deeper analysis could provide valuable insights for the field. For instance, are there recurring motifs or design principles that the controller learns? Why might the discovered RNN cell be superior to an LSTM? A more detailed ablation study on the components of the new cell could help answer this. Such insights could potentially guide human experts in designing better architectures in the future.
*   **Search Space Design:** The performance of the method is necessarily constrained by the design of the search space, which remains a human-driven process. For example, the choice of operations (add, elem_mult) and activations for the RNN cell search is critical. A brief discussion on the sensitivity to these choices, or the process used to arrive at the presented search spaces, would add valuable context.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you provide an estimate of the total computational cost for a full search run, for example, in terms of P100-GPU-days for both the CIFAR-10 and PTB experiments? This would be a very useful metric for the community to understand the scale of this work.
2.  The reward signal is based on the validation performance of child networks trained for a fixed number of epochs (e.g., 50 for CIFAR-10). How sensitive is the final result to this choice? Is it possible that this short training schedule biases the search towards architectures that learn quickly, rather than those that might achieve higher final accuracy with longer training?
3.  The reward functions used are non-linear transformations of the validation metric (e.g., `accuracy^3` for CIFAR-10, `c/(perplexity)^2` for PTB). What was the motivation for this specific reward shaping? How much impact does it have on the stability and speed of the controller's training compared to using the raw validation metric as the reward?
