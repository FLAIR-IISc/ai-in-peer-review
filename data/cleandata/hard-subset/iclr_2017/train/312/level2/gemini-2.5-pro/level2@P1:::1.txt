### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces "Neural Architecture Search," a novel and compelling method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller. The core idea is to have the controller generate a variable-length string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. This controller is then trained using reinforcement learning (REINFORCE) to maximize the expected performance (e.g., validation accuracy) of the child networks on a target task. This general framework is impressively applied to two distinct and challenging problems: designing a convolutional network for image classification on CIFAR-10 and designing a novel recurrent cell for language modeling on Penn Treebank. In both cases, the method successfully discovers architectures from scratch that achieve state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance, rivaling and even surpassing the best human-designed architectures. The work represents a significant step towards the automation of machine learning model design.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel, ambitious, and impactful approach to a fundamental problem in deep learning. My recommendation is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a new paradigm for architecture design by framing it as a learning problem solved with a meta-learning approach. The concept of using an RNN controller trained via reinforcement learning is both elegant and powerful, opening up a promising new research direction. Automating this critical part of the deep learning workflow has been a long-standing goal, and this work provides a general and effective framework to tackle it.
2.  **Excellent Empirical Results:** The claims are substantiated with state-of-the-art results on two difficult and widely-recognized benchmarks. The ability to automatically discover a convolutional architecture for CIFAR-10 and a recurrent cell for Penn Treebank that outperform highly-engineered, human-invented models is a powerful demonstration of the method's efficacy and potential.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper is well-motivated, positioning the work effectively within the context of hyperparameter optimization and meta-learning. The technical approach is sound and well-explained. The use of the REINFORCE algorithm is a natural fit for optimizing the non-differentiable reward signal, and the authors make sensible technical choices to make the method practical, such as employing a baseline to reduce gradient variance and using large-scale distributed training to manage the immense computational cost.

The flexibility of the framework is a major strength, highlighted by its successful application to two very different domains (convolutional and recurrent architectures). The extensions for generating complex structures like skip connections via an attention-like mechanism and for designing recurrent cells via a tree-based representation are clever and demonstrate the method's generality.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare their results to a comprehensive list of state-of-the-art models. Crucially, they also include a comparison against random search, a strong baseline for hyperparameter optimization, and show that their policy gradient approach learns to find better architectures over time. Furthermore, the transfer learning experiments, which show the discovered RNN cell generalizing to character-level modeling and machine translation, provide strong evidence that the learned cell is not just overfitted to PTB but captures a genuinely useful recurrent computation structure.

While the computational expense of the method is a significant drawback, the authors are transparent about the resources required. Given the novelty of the approach and the strength of the results, this is an acceptable trade-off for a foundational research paper in this area.

### **Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper**

The following points are suggestions aimed at further strengthening the paper and are not critical for my decision to accept.

*   **Discussion on Search Space Design:** The design of the search space appears to be a critical component of the method's success. For instance, in the final CIFAR-10 experiment, the controller predicts blocks of layers rather than individual layers. It would be beneficial to include a more detailed discussion on the principles and trade-offs involved in designing these search spaces. How much human prior knowledge is encoded in these choices, and how sensitive are the results to them?
*   **Analysis of Computational Cost:** While the paper mentions the number of concurrent workers, it would be helpful to quantify the total computational cost more directly (e.g., in total GPU-days). A learning curve showing the performance of the best architecture found over the course of the search (e.g., after every 1000 architectures are evaluated) would also provide valuable insight into the efficiency of the search process and whether there are diminishing returns.
*   **Reward Function Details:** The reward for the CIFAR-10 task is the validation accuracy cubed. This is an interesting choice. Could you briefly elaborate on the motivation for this transformation and its effect on the learning process? It seems designed to more heavily penalize small drops in accuracy and amplify the signal from top-performing models, but a short justification would be useful.
*   **Further Analysis of Discovered Architectures:** The analysis of the discovered architectures is very insightful. Expanding on this could provide valuable knowledge for the community. For example, are there recurring motifs or patterns that the controller learns to favor? A deeper qualitative analysis could potentially uncover new architectural principles that could inform manual architecture design as well.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points during the discussion period:

1.  The REINFORCE algorithm is known for its high variance. Was the training of the controller stable? Did you consider or experiment with more advanced policy gradient methods, such as actor-critic variants, and if so, how did they compare?
2.  For the final, best-performing CIFAR-10 model, the search space was constrained to have the controller predict blocks of 3 layers. What was the primary motivation for this change from predicting individual layers? Was it primarily a way to manage the complexity of searching for deeper networks?
3.  Regarding the comparison with random search, could you provide a few more details on its implementation? Specifically, was it implemented by uniformly sampling a sequence of actions from the same search space and vocabulary available to the controller?
4.  The successful transfer of the discovered `NASCell` to the GNMT framework is very encouraging. The paper states this was done without tuning the existing framework's hyperparameters, which were likely optimized for LSTMs. Do you believe that further hyperparameter tuning specifically for the `NASCell` could unlock even greater performance gains in the translation task?
