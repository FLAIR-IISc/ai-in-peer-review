### Review of "Neural Architecture Search with Reinforcement Learning"

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method for automatically discovering neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (RNN), termed the "controller," to generate a variable-length string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. The controller is trained using a policy gradient reinforcement learning algorithm (REINFORCE) to maximize the expected performance of the generated child architectures on a validation set. This framework is demonstrated to be highly effective in two distinct and challenging domains. First, on the CIFAR-10 image classification task, the method discovers a novel convolutional neural network architecture from scratch that achieves a new state-of-the-art test error of 3.65%, outperforming the best human-designed architectures at the time while also being computationally faster. Second, on the Penn Treebank language modeling task, the method designs a novel recurrent cell that surpasses the performance of the widely-used LSTM cell and other state-of-the-art models, achieving a test perplexity of 62.4. The discovered cell also demonstrates strong generalization capabilities when transferred to other tasks, including character-level language modeling and machine translation.

**Decision: Accept**

This paper is a clear accept. The primary reasons for this decision are:
1.  **High Novelty and Impact:** The proposed method of framing architecture design as a reinforcement learning problem, where an agent learns to generate optimal network descriptions, represents a significant conceptual leap in automating machine learning. It provides a general and powerful framework for exploring vast and complex architectural search spaces.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The work is supported by exceptionally strong and comprehensive experimental results on highly competitive benchmarks (CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank). The fact that the method can discover architectures from scratch that rival or exceed the best human-engineered models is a compelling demonstration of its effectiveness.

**Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's approach is well-motivated by the increasing complexity and difficulty of manual architecture design. It is well-positioned in the literature, clearly distinguishing itself from traditional hyperparameter optimization methods, which typically operate on fixed-length spaces, and from neuro-evolution, which has historically been difficult to scale. The use of an RNN controller trained with policy gradients is a novel combination that enables a flexible and expressive search over variable-length, compositional architectures.

The claims are rigorously supported by the empirical evaluation.
*   **Convolutional Architectures:** On CIFAR-10, the discovered model not only achieves a top-tier error rate but does so with a novel structure that includes features like rectangular filters and specific skip-connection patterns. The ablation studies, such as removing skip connections, validate the utility of the discovered architectural motifs.
*   **Recurrent Architectures:** The success in designing a novel recurrent cell is particularly impressive, as improving upon the highly optimized LSTM cell is a notoriously difficult task. The discovered cell yields significant improvements on Penn Treebank. Furthermore, the transfer learning experiments, where the cell is successfully applied to character modeling and machine translation, provide strong evidence that the discovered architecture has learned generalizable computational principles.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** Crucially, the paper includes a comparison against random search, demonstrating that the policy gradient approach systematically finds better architectures over time. This comparison validates that the controller is indeed learning an effective search strategy rather than just benefiting from a well-designed search space.

The methodology is explained clearly, with helpful figures illustrating the overall process, the inclusion of skip connections, and the generation of recurrent cells. The publication of the discovered cell as `NASCell` in TensorFlow is a valuable contribution to the community.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are offered to help improve the paper further and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Computational Cost:** The primary limitation of the proposed method is its immense computational expense (e.g., training 800 networks concurrently on 800 GPUs for the CIFAR-10 search). While the results justify this cost from a research perspective, it would be beneficial for the paper to include a more explicit discussion of this limitation. A paragraph addressing potential future directions to improve sample efficiency—such as weight sharing across child models, surrogate models, or early-stopping strategies—would strengthen the paper and provide context for future work in this area.
*   **Analysis of Discovered Architectures:** The paper presents visualizations of the discovered architectures but offers limited analysis of *why* these particular structures are effective. A more in-depth discussion could provide valuable insights for human architects. For example, are there recurring motifs or principles in the best-performing architectures that could inform manual design?
*   **Search Space Sensitivity:** The design of the search space itself is a critical component of the method's success. It would be helpful to include a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the results to the choices made in defining the search space (e.g., the range of filter sizes, the set of activation functions, the 'base number' for the RNN cell). This would help readers better understand the trade-offs involved in applying this method.
*   **Reward Function:** For the Penn Treebank experiments, the reward is defined as `c * (validation perplexity)^2`. The motivation for squaring the perplexity is not immediately obvious. A brief sentence clarifying why this functional form was chosen (e.g., to more heavily penalize high perplexity or to create a steeper reward landscape) would be a helpful addition.
