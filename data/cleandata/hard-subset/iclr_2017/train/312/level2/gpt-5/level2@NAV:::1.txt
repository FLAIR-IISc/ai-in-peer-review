Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), where a recurrent “controller” network generates neural architectures that are evaluated as “child” models; the controller is trained with policy-gradient (REINFORCE) to maximize validation accuracy. The contributions are: (1) a flexible, variable-length architecture search framework that includes skip connections and multiple layer types; (2) large-scale, asynchronous distributed training to evaluate thousands of candidates; (3) state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 (3.65% error) and PTB (62.4 perplexity), plus transfer of the discovered RNN cell to character PTB and GNMT with gains; and (4) control experiments vs. random search and expanded search spaces.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Clear and significant novelty in formulating architecture generation as an RNN-driven, RL-optimized search over variable-length designs; (2) Strong, carefully benchmarked empirical support with new SOTA results and evidence of transferability.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Automating architecture design is timely and impactful; positioning vs. Bayesian optimization and neuroevolution is appropriate. The auto-regressive controller and skip-connection selection are well motivated and technically sound.
- Evidence: Results on CIFAR-10 and PTB are competitive or superior to strong baselines. The paper uses held-out validation rewards, grid searches only post-selection, and provides control experiments (random search, expanded function sets). Claims are backed by reproducible training details and a plan to release code/cells.
- Usefulness: The framework is broadly applicable and demonstrates transfer to character modeling and GNMT with minimal retuning, indicating practical value.

Additional feedback to improve (helpful suggestions, not decisive)
- Report full compute budgets (GPU/CPU-days), wall-clock search time, and energy cost; normalize comparisons by search budget to contextualize practicality.
- Provide ablations: effect of reward shaping (cubing accuracy), baseline choice, layer-depth schedule, skip-connection attention, and the asynchronous scheme.
- Clarify fairness of CIFAR comparisons (e.g., dense vs. added filters, absence of 1x1 bottlenecks); include parameter/FLOP counts and training-time parity for all baselines.
- Analyze robustness: variance across seeds, stability of the discovered cells/architectures, and sensitivity to the validation split (risk of overfitting the search to validation).
- Extend transfer analysis (e.g., ImageNet) or provide partial results to support scalability.

Questions for the authors
- What are the exact search costs (GPU/CPU-days) for CIFAR and PTB, and the per-child training time? How sample-efficient is NAS vs. random/Bayesian optimization under equal budgets?
- How sensitive is performance to the reward shaping (accuracy^3) and the moving-average baseline?
- How often do “compilation failures” occur, and how do the padding/concatenation fixes impact accuracy?
- What is the variance across independent NAS runs in the best-found accuracy/perplexity?
- On CIFAR-10, are speedups measured at equal accuracy and matched FLOPs/params? Can you report calibrated inference/training throughput?
- Did you observe validation-set overfitting by the controller, and how was it mitigated?
