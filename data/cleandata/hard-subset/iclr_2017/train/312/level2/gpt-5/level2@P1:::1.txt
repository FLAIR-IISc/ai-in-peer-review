Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a reinforcement learning approach that uses a controller RNN to generate variable-length descriptions of neural architectures. The controller is trained with REINFORCE to maximize expected validation performance of the “child” networks it proposes. The method supports complex topologies through learned skip connections, accommodates different layer types, and extends to searching recurrent cell structures via a flexible computation tree. A large-scale, asynchronous parameter-server setup enables training hundreds of child models in parallel. Empirically, NAS discovers:
- A competitive convolutional architecture on CIFAR-10 achieving 3.65% test error (after widening), rivaling strong human-designed models while being slightly faster than a comparable DenseNet variant.
- A novel recurrent cell that achieves 62.4 test perplexity on Penn Treebank (PTB), outperforming LSTM and other baselines, and transfers effectively to character-level PTB (1.214 perplexity) and to GNMT (+0.5 BLEU without retuning the framework).
The paper includes ablations (e.g., skip connections) and a control comparison showing consistent gains over random search.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Clear, well-motivated approach that expands the scope of architecture search to variable-length, structured designs using RL, with a careful formulation of the search space.
- Strong, multi-domain empirical results that support the central claims, including transferability and controls against random search.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Designing neural architectures remains time-consuming and expertise-heavy. A general method that learns to propose architectures directly from performance feedback is impactful and timely.
- Method novelty and placement: The controller-as-program-generator idea, optimized via policy gradients on non-differentiable rewards, is well grounded and related to sequence models, program induction, meta-learning, and neuro-evolution. The work advances beyond fixed-length hyperparameter search and provides a flexible mechanism for composing skip connections and recurrent cell graphs.
- Technical soundness: The REINFORCE training with a moving-average baseline is standard; the use of set-selection sigmoids for skip connectivity is a neat design; practical issues (graph validity, size mismatches) are addressed with simple, workable heuristics. Distributed, asynchronous training is described with sufficient detail to understand throughput.
- Empirical rigor: Results are competitive on canonical benchmarks. On CIFAR-10, the discovered designs compare favorably to recent architectures; the best number reported (3.65%) involves post-hoc widening, which the paper clearly notes. On PTB, the discovered cell achieves notable perplexity gains and is more computationally efficient than prior SOTA (e.g., RHN running multiple inner steps). Transfer experiments to character modeling and GNMT add credibility that the learned cell captures reusable structure. Control experiments (larger search space, random search baseline) provide evidence of robustness and learning signal quality.
- Clarity: The search spaces, training regimes, and reward shaping are described clearly; the figures (not included here) reportedly help.

Areas for improvement (constructive feedback; not all are decision-critical)
- Compute budget and accessibility: The search uses substantial resources (e.g., up to 800 GPUs for CIFAR-10). Please quantify total compute (number of child models trained, epochs per model, wall-clock, GPU-hours/CPU-hours) and provide guidance on scaling down (e.g., fewer replicas, early stopping, proxy training epochs).
- Fairness of comparisons: For CIFAR-10, separate the performance of the raw discovered macro-architecture from that after widening and other post-hoc tuning. Consider reporting a Pareto curve (accuracy vs. inference time/params) for NAS vs. baselines, and clarify the DenseNet comparison when 1x1 convolutions are disallowed on one side.
- Reward shaping: Justify the choice of cubing accuracy (CIFAR) and squaring (perplexity) with constant scaling (PTB). Include a brief ablation on reward transformations and their effect on variance and convergence.
- Sample efficiency: Provide learning curves showing best/median validation performance vs. number of sampled architectures; quantify gains over random search over time and variance across controller seeds.
- Search space sensitivity: Expand ablations on search space design (e.g., effect of enabling strides, pooling, skip mechanisms; base number for RNN cell trees). Discuss how much of the final performance is attributable to the flexibility of the space vs. the efficacy of the search.
- Reproducibility: The promise to release code and the NASCell is excellent. Consider releasing the discovered architectures and controller checkpoints, and practical recipes for reproducing results with modest compute (e.g., reduced epochs, weight sharing, or proxy tasks).
- Multi-objective extensions: Since latency and parameter count are important in practice, consider incorporating a composite reward (e.g., accuracy minus a penalty for FLOPs/latency) and/or constraints, and report preliminary results or discussion.
- Variance reduction/optimization details: Consider reporting whether entropy regularization or actor-critic variants were tried; these may improve stability over plain REINFORCE.

Questions for the authors
- Compute accounting: How many child models were trained for CIFAR-10 and PTB in total? What was the aggregate GPU/CPU time and wall-clock? How many samples were needed to reach within, say, 95% of the final best performance?
- Reward shaping: How sensitive is the controller training to the specific cubic/squared transforms? Did you try baselines such as normalized accuracy/perplexity, or advantage normalization?
- Post-hoc tuning: For the CIFAR-10 3.65% result, how much of the gain came from widening vs. the discovered topology? Could the controller directly optimize number of filters to reach the same operating point?
- Random search baseline: How were random samples constrained to be valid (same sampling priors and graph fixes as NAS)? Did you compare against a tuned Bayesian optimization baseline that supports variable-length structures?
- Generalization of the RNN cell: Beyond PTB and GNMT, have you evaluated on larger word-level datasets (e.g., WikiText-103) or other sequence tasks to further validate transfer?
- Robustness to validation overfitting: Given repeated use of a fixed validation set as reward, did you observe signs of overfitting at the controller level? Any mitigation (e.g., resampling validation splits, early stopping of controller)?
- Practical guidance: If constrained to, say, 8–16 GPUs, what settings (training epochs per child, controller LR/baseline, search space pruning) would you recommend to achieve reasonable results?

Overall, this is a well-motivated and carefully executed paper with clear impact. The empirical gains across modalities and the demonstration that nontrivial, transferable RNN cells can be discovered automatically make it a strong contribution.
