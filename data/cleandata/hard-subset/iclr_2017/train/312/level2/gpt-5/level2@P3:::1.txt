Summary
The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a reinforcement learning framework in which a controller RNN generates variable-length descriptions of neural network architectures and receives as reward the validation accuracy of the corresponding “child” model. The controller is trained with REINFORCE and supported by large-scale distributed evaluation of sampled architectures. The approach includes mechanisms to express modern design elements (e.g., skip connections via set-selection attention; branching; layer types), and a tree-based parameterization to search recurrent cells, including where to inject and read from memory states c_{t-1}, c_t. On CIFAR-10, the method discovers convolutional architectures competitive with or surpassing human-designed models (down to 3.65% test error with favorable speed compared to a strong DenseNet baseline). On Penn Treebank (PTB), the method discovers a recurrent cell that improves over LSTM and prior state-of-the-art models in perplexity (down to 62.4), transfers to character-level modeling (1.214 bpc), and shows a modest improvement when dropped into GNMT without retuning. Control experiments compare against random search and expand the search space with additional operations.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Clear, novel formulation of architecture search as sequence generation optimized with policy gradients over a flexible, variable-length search space; (2) Strong empirical results with new state of the art on PTB and competitive CIFAR-10 performance, supported by controls and transfer experiments.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Automating architectural design is important as modern performance depends heavily on intricate connectivity patterns. The formulation as a controller that emits architecture tokens directly addresses the limitations of fixed-length hyperparameter search and provides a principled means to handle variable-depth, skip-connected topologies.
- Placement in literature: The paper situates the method relative to Bayesian optimization for hyperparameters (e.g., Bergstra & Bengio, 2012; Snoek et al., 2012, 2015), neuro-evolution (e.g., Stanley et al., 2009), program synthesis/induction, and meta-learning. The auto-regressive controller and reinforcement learning objective are well positioned alongside sequence-to-sequence and sequence-level RL work (e.g., Ranzato et al., 2015; Shen et al., 2016).
- Methodological soundness: The REINFORCE update with a moving-average baseline is standard and appropriate for the non-differentiable reward. The skip-connection prediction via content-based sigmoids, handling of invalid graphs, and the recurrent-cell tree parameterization are clearly explained. Distributed asynchronous training is described with sufficient detail to understand throughput and update timing.
- Empirical support: Results on CIFAR-10 are strong given the search constraints, with meaningful analysis (e.g., effects of skip connections; stride prediction; inclusion of pooling; scaling the discovered model’s width). On PTB, the discovered cell outperforms strong baselines (including Zaremba et al., Gal, and RHN; Zilly et al., 2016) and transfers across tasks (character-level PTB) and frameworks (GNMT). A random search control indicates both best-of-run and top-k averages improve more with NAS than with random sampling under comparable sampling budgets.
- Generality: The same controller framework is adapted to both convolutional and recurrent design spaces, demonstrating flexibility.

Areas for improvement (to strengthen clarity, fairness, and reproducibility; not all are decision-critical)
Essential (feasible pre-camera):
- Computational budget and efficiency: Report wall-clock compute (GPU/CPU days) for the CIFAR-10 and PTB searches, the fraction of invalid/failed architectures, and the effective sample efficiency (improvement vs. number of models trained). This will help readers assess practicality.
- Validation overfitting: Since the controller optimizes validation accuracy on a fixed 5k split, include an analysis of potential overfitting to this split (e.g., repeat with multiple validation splits or show stability of the final test performance across a few runs).
- Baselines: The random search baseline is helpful; adding a Bayesian optimization/SMAC/Hyperband baseline on the same search space (even if with fewer samples) would make the comparison stronger and more broadly informative.

Nice-to-have (optional):
- Reward shaping sensitivity: Briefly examine sensitivity to the cubed-accuracy reward and baseline choice; include a figure comparing learning curves under alternative reward scalings.
- Controller architecture ablations: Report the effect of controller depth/size and the layer-growth schedule on search outcomes.
- Hyperparameter tuning fairness: Clarify what post-search tuning was applied to baselines versus NAS-discovered models, and report parameter counts and inference throughput for the final CIFAR-10 models to contextualize the 1.05× speed claim.
- Search space diagnostics: Provide statistics on operations chosen by top models (e.g., distribution of filter shapes, skip densities; for RNN cells, frequency of activation/combination choices) to offer insights into what the controller learns.

Questions for clarification
- How many total child models were invalid or required fallback due to incompatible shapes/isolated nodes, and how was their reward handled?
- How consistent are the best-found architectures across independent controller training runs with different seeds? Provide variance of top-1/average-of-top-k performance.
- For PTB, to what extent do improvements arise from the discovered cell versus post-hoc hyperparameter tuning (dropout rates, decay epochs)? Can the cell be compared head-to-head with an LSTM under identical training hyperparameters?
- For the CIFAR-10 “pooling allowed” setting, the final 3.65% result required adding 40 filters per layer after search. How does the best architecture perform if scaled by width in a comparable manner during search, and what is the parameter/FLOP parity with DenseNet baselines?
- In the GNMT experiment, which specific layer sizes were adjusted to equalize computational complexity? Provide parameter counts and training/inference FLOPs for both cells.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a compelling and influential approach to automated architecture design, with solid technical grounding and strong empirical validation. Despite the heavy compute requirement and some missing efficiency/variance details, the contribution is significant and timely. The work merits acceptance.
