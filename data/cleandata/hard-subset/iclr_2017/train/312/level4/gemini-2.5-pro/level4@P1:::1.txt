### **Summary**

This paper tackles the important and challenging problem of neural architecture search. The authors propose a method where a controller RNN generates network architectures, which are then trained and evaluated. The controller's parameters are updated using reinforcement learning, with the validation accuracy of the child network serving as the reward signal. This approach is demonstrated on two highly competitive benchmarks: image classification on CIFAR-10 and language modeling on Penn Treebank. In both cases, the method discovers novel architectures that achieve state-of-the-art or near-state-of-the-art performance, outperforming many manually designed models. This work effectively shows that automated architecture search can be competitive with, and even surpass, human intuition, offering a promising path towards tailoring architectures to specific tasks without extensive hand-engineering.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

The key reasons for this recommendation are:
1.  The paper introduces a novel and effective method for automated architecture search that is both general and flexible.
2.  The empirical results are very strong, achieving state-of-the-art performance on competitive benchmarks (CIFAR-10, Penn Treebank), which convincingly validates the proposed approach.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The proposed method, Neural Architecture Search (NAS), is a significant contribution. Using an RNN controller trained with policy gradients to generate architectures is a creative and powerful idea. This framework is well-motivated and general enough to be applied to different domains, as demonstrated by its success in designing both convolutional and recurrent networks. The paper also thoughtfully addresses practical challenges, such as incorporating skip connections and using parallel training to manage the high computational cost. While the technique is currently computationally intensive, this represents a reasonable trade-off for the quality of the resulting architectures, and this trade-off is likely to become more favorable as hardware improves.

The empirical validation is thorough and compelling. The authors tackle widely-used and difficult benchmark datasets, demonstrating that NAS can find architectures that are not only competitive but superior to well-established, human-designed models. For instance, the discovered convolutional network for CIFAR-10 rivals the best-known architectures in accuracy while being faster, and the novel recurrent cell for Penn Treebank surpasses the highly-optimized LSTM, setting a new state-of-the-art. This success across different domains strongly supports the claim that the method can discover non-obvious, high-performing architectures by exploring a vast search space beyond typical human design patterns.

### **Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

The primary suggestion concerns reproducibility. The paper states that after finding the best architecture, "a grid search is performed over learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates and decay epoch." For the community to build upon this work and accurately replicate the results, it would be immensely helpful to include the final hyperparameters used for the best-performing models on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank. As the authors of "Recurrent Neural Network Regularization" (Zaremba et al., 2014) demonstrated, providing a reference set of hyperparameters is a great service to the field and facilitates future research.

### **Questions for the Authors**

Could you please clarify the specific hyperparameters and types of regularization (e.g., recurrent dropout, embedding dropout) used for the final, best-performing Penn Treebank model? The paper mentions that "The best cell found was then run with three different configurations and sizes to increase its capacity." Providing the details for these final configurations would be very valuable for reproducibility. Similarly, what were the final hyperparameters for the state-of-the-art character language model?
