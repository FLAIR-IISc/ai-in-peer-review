This paper presents a method for discovering optimal neural network architectures using an actor-critic reinforcement learning framework. The approach treats the design of a neural network as a variable-length sequence of actions. A controller network, acting as the actor, generates the target architecture specification. The selection of each component of the network is an action, and the validation accuracy of the resulting architecture serves as the reward signal for training the controller. The controller itself is an auto-regressive two-layer LSTM, which is trained alongside a critic to improve stability. The method is evaluated on two distinct problems, and the discovered architectures are compared against a number of strong, human-designed baselines.

This is a very exciting and well-executed paper. Manually designing network architectures is a difficult and intuition-driven process, and it is often hard to know how far these hand-crafted designs are from an optimal solution. The presented method is novel and offers a principled approach to automating this critical task. The authors do an excellent job of describing their method in detail, including the various improvements that were necessary to make the approach viable. The chosen datasets and experiments effectively demonstrate the method's capabilities. It is particularly interesting to see the differences between the architectures generated by this method and those designed by humans, highlighting the potential for discovering non-intuitive yet powerful network structures. The paper is very clearly written, accessible, and provides a thorough discussion of the related literature.

The paper could be further strengthened by including more data on the computational costs of the search process. An analysis of the correlation between the time/resources invested in the search and the quality of the final model would be particularly insightful for practitioners. Additionally, it would be interesting to see experiments that explore how the method performs when initialized from a strong, human-designed architecture. This could shed light on the ability of the search process to refine and improve upon existing models.

Overall, this is an excellent and impactful paper that opens up a promising new direction for research.
