This paper presents a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller trained with reinforcement learning (RL). The core idea is promising, and the experimental results on CIFAR-10 and the Penn Treebank dataset demonstrate the method's effectiveness in discovering high-performing architectures.

**Strengths:**

1.  The central idea of using an RNN to generate a string that describes a network architecture, and then training this RNN with reinforcement learning, is both novel and compelling. This formulation provides a principled and scalable way to navigate the vast and discrete search space of network topologies, directly optimizing for a performance metric like validation accuracy.

2.  It is encouraging that the architectures discovered by the method bear a resemblance to successful human-designed models. For instance, the discovered convolutional cell incorporates elements like skip connections, which are known to be critical for training deep networks. This suggests that the search process is capable of capturing meaningful architectural principles that align with human expertise.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  A significant drawback is the method's computational expense. The architecture search process requires training thousands of individual networks, which, as noted by the authors, demands considerable computational resources over many days. This high cost may limit the practicality and accessibility of the approach. A discussion of potential avenues for improving search efficiency would strengthen the paper.

2.  The experiments do not sufficiently explore the generalizability of the discovered architectures. An architecture found for a specific dataset (e.g., CIFAR-10) should ideally perform well on similar datasets (e.g., CIFAR-100 or SVHN). The paper would be more impactful if it included experiments demonstrating that the learned architectures transfer effectively to new tasks. This is especially pertinent for the learned sequential models, where evaluating the discovered recurrent cell on other language or sequence modeling tasks would provide stronger evidence of its utility.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-executed and innovative paper that introduces a powerful new paradigm for neural architecture search. The proposed method is well-motivated and the empirical results are strong. While the high computational cost and the limited evaluation of architecture generalizability are notable weaknesses, the paper's core contribution is significant. Further experiments to demonstrate the transferability of the learned architectures would solidify the claim that this method has an advantage over manual, human-driven design.
