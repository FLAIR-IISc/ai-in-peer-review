This paper presents an ambitious and well-executed approach to automating neural network architecture design. The authors propose using a recurrent neural network, termed the "controller," to generate variable-length strings that specify network architectures. This controller is trained using a policy gradient reinforcement learning algorithm, with the validation accuracy of the generated "child" network serving as the reward signal. The work demonstrates this method, named Neural Architecture Search, on two challenging tasks: designing a convolutional network for CIFAR-10 image classification and a recurrent cell for Penn Treebank language modeling. In both cases, the method discovers novel architectures that achieve state-of-the-art or competitive performance compared to meticulously human-engineered models.

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are: 1) the novelty and generality of the proposed framework for architecture search, which addresses a significant and practical problem in deep learning, and 2) the strength of the empirical results, which convincingly demonstrate that the method can discover high-performing, non-intuitive architectures from scratch on competitive benchmarks.

The core idea of framing architecture generation as a reinforcement learning problem, where an RNN controller sequentially makes design choices, is elegant and powerful. It naturally handles variable-length and complex architectures, including modern features like skip connections, which represents a significant step beyond traditional hyperparameter optimization techniques that operate in fixed-dimensional spaces. The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the methodology, including crucial implementation details like the use of a parameter server for parallel training and the attention-like mechanism for creating skip connections.

The empirical validation is thorough and compelling. On CIFAR-10, the discovered architecture achieves a new state-of-the-art result (3.65% error) while being faster than the comparable human-designed model. On the Penn Treebank task, the method designs a novel recurrent cell that surpasses the widely-used LSTM and other strong baselines, achieving a state-of-the-art perplexity of 62.4. The successful transfer of this discovered cell to other tasks (character language modeling and machine translation) provides further evidence of its robustness and general utility. The comparison against random search effectively illustrates the value of the learned policy gradient approach. The paper is also very well-written and easy to follow.

The following suggestions are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.
*   The computational cost of this method appears to be substantial (e.g., "800 networks being trained on 800 GPUs concurrently"). While the results are impressive, the practicality of the approach is a key consideration for other researchers. It would be highly beneficial to include an estimate of the total computational resources (e.g., in GPU-days or a similar metric) required to arrive at the final reported architectures. This would provide valuable context for the community.
*   The experiments demonstrate searching "from scratch." Have the authors considered the effect of bootstrapping the controller? For example, initializing the search with a known, decent architecture (like a simple ResNet or LSTM) could potentially accelerate convergence or lead to different, perhaps better, local optima. Exploring this could provide interesting insights into the search process.
*   Could you clarify the relationship between validation set size and the quality of the search? The validation set for CIFAR-10 (5,000 examples) is relatively small, which could lead to a noisy reward signal. Did you investigate if the stability or outcome of the search is sensitive to the size of this validation set?
