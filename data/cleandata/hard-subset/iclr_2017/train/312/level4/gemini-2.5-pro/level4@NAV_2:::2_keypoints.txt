- Introduces Neural Architecture Search: an RNN "controller" generating variable-length architecture strings trained with policy gradient using child validation accuracy as reward.
- Demonstrates the method on CIFAR-10 conv nets and Penn Treebank recurrent cells, discovering novel architectures that achieve state-of-the-art or competitive performance.
- Framing architecture generation as reinforcement learning is elegant and handles variable-length, complex designs (including skip connections) beyond fixed-dimensional hyperparameter search.
- Provides clear, detailed methodology and implementation (e.g., parameter server for parallel training, attention-like mechanism for skip connections).
- CIFAR-10 results: discovered architecture achieves 3.65% error and is faster than comparable human-designed models.
- Penn Treebank results: discovered recurrent cell outperforms LSTM and baselines, achieving perplexity 62.4 and successfully transfers to character LM and machine translation.
- Comparison to random search effectively demonstrates the advantage of the learned policy-gradient approach.
- Paper is well-written and easy to follow.
- Concern: method is computationally expensive (e.g., 800 networks on 800 GPUs); report total resources (GPU-days) to assess practicality.
- Suggestion: consider bootstrapping the controller from known architectures (e.g., ResNet/LSTM) to accelerate convergence or explore different optima.
- Suggestion: clarify how validation set size (CIFAR-10’s 5k) affects search quality and whether reward noise or stability depends on validation size.
