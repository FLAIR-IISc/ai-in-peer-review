This is a well-written paper on an interesting and important topic, with strong empirical results. I recommend it be accepted.

The paper explores the crucial area of automated architecture search. While the proposed technique is computationally intensive, this represents a reasonable trade-off for the performance gains achieved, and one that will likely become more favorable as hardware technology advances.

A key strength of the work is its extensive empirical validation. The authors apply their method to standard tasks in both vision and language, evaluating on multiple benchmark datasets. The results demonstrate that the discovered architectures are not only competitive with but can also surpass state-of-the-art models designed by human experts. This provides compelling evidence that automated search is a viable strategy for exploring architectures beyond the standard RNN and CNN search spaces. Furthermore, the approach suggests a promising path toward tailoring architectures to specific datasets without requiring manual, dataset-specific engineering. While one could always wish for more experiments on additional datasets, the current scope is more than sufficient to demonstrate the method's effectiveness.

My primary suggestion for improving the paper relates to reproducibility. The paper states that for the final evaluation, "[a]fter the controller RNN is done training, we take the best RNN cell according to the lowest validation perplexity and then run a grid search over learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates and decay epoch." However, the specific hyperparameters and the search ranges used in this grid search are not provided.

For a work of this nature, these details are critical. Without them, replicating the reported results would require a great deal of trial and error, which could hinder future work that aims to build upon this method. Therefore, I strongly encourage the authors to include these details in the final version of the paper. Specifically, this should include the type of dropout used (e.g., recurrent dropout, embedding dropout), the ranges explored in the grid search, and the final hyperparameter configurations used for the main experiments. This request also applies to the other experiments, such as the character language modeling task. As demonstrated by influential papers like Zaremba et al. (2014), releasing a complete set of hyperparameters is a significant service to the research community and greatly assists future progress.

In summary, this is an excellent paper with significant contributions. The core idea is innovative, the results are strong, and the work opens up exciting avenues for future research. The inclusion of detailed hyperparameters would further strengthen the paper and solidify its impact.
