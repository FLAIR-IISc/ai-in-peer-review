This paper presents Neural Architecture Search, a novel and compelling method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network controller trained with reinforcement learning. The core idea is to have a controller RNN generate a string representing the architecture of a "child" network. This child network is then trained and its performance on a validation set is used as a reward signal to update the controller's parameters via policy gradients. The work makes significant contributions by demonstrating that this approach can, from scratch, discover novel architectures that achieve state-of-the-art performance on highly competitive benchmarks. Specifically, it designs a convolutional network for CIFAR-10 that surpasses prominent human-invented architectures in both accuracy and speed, and a novel recurrent cell for Penn Treebank that outperforms the widely-used LSTM and sets a new state-of-the-art in language modeling.

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The two key reasons for this decision are:
1.  The proposed method is highly innovative and significant, providing a general and scalable framework that successfully reframes the complex problem of architecture engineering as a reinforcement learning task.
2.  The empirical results are exceptionally strong and rigorously validated on two distinct and challenging domains, demonstrating that the automatically discovered architectures rival or exceed the performance of the best human-designed models.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its novel formulation of architecture design. By using an RNN controller to sequentially generate architectural decisions, the method can explore a vast and variable-length design space, a significant step beyond traditional hyperparameter optimization methods that operate in fixed-dimensional spaces. Training this controller with REINFORCE, using the child network's validation accuracy as a reward, is an elegant solution to optimizing a non-differentiable objective. The empirical validation of this approach is thorough and convincing. On CIFAR-10, the discovered architecture achieves a test error of 3.65%, which is not only better than a comparable state-of-the-art DenseNet model but also faster. On the Penn Treebank language modeling task, the discovered recurrent cell achieves a perplexity of 62.4, a substantial improvement of 3.6 over the previous best. The authors further strengthen their claims by showing this cell generalizes well, achieving state-of-the-art results on a character-level language modeling task and improving performance when integrated into a large-scale neural machine translation system. The comparison against random search clearly shows that the controller is learning an effective search strategy, justifying the use of reinforcement learning.

The following suggestions are intended to further improve the paper and are not critical to my assessment for acceptance.
*   **Computational Cost:** The computational requirements for the search are immense (e.g., 800 GPUs for the CIFAR-10 experiment). While this is necessary to achieve the reported results, it is a significant practical limitation. It would be beneficial for the authors to explicitly discuss this limitation and perhaps comment on potential future directions for improving search efficiency, which would broaden the applicability of this method.
*   **Search Space Design:** The construction of the search space seems to be a critical, and still manual, part of the process. For instance, the best CIFAR-10 result came from a search space that included pooling layers and predicted blocks of layers. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the final results to these meta-choices about the search space would provide valuable insight.
*   **Architecture Analysis:** The paper notes that the discovered convolutional architecture has many rectangular filters and one-step skip connections. A deeper analysis or visualization of the discovered architectures (beyond the appendix) could provide more intuition about what patterns the controller learns to favor, potentially informing future manual architecture design.

I have a few questions for the authors to clarify my understanding:
*   The reward functions are specified as the "maximum validation accuracy of the last 5 epochs cubed" for CIFAR-10 and `c/(validation perplexity)^2` for Penn Treebank. Could you elaborate on the motivation for these specific non-linear transformations of the performance metric? How sensitive is the stability and outcome of the search to the shape of this reward signal?
*   While the paper details the distributed training setup, it would be helpful to understand the overall time cost. Could you provide a rough estimate of the total wall-clock time required to complete the architecture search for the CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank experiments?
