This paper presents a framework for automatically designing network architectures using a combination of reinforcement learning (RL) and a recurrent neural network (RNN). The core idea of using an RNN controller to generate network topologies for specific tasks is compelling, and the approach is validated by solid experimental results on two datasets.

The strengths of the paper are:
1. The central concept of employing an RNN to produce a variable-length string describing a network, and then using RL to optimize the RNN's parameters, is both innovative and promising.
2. The architectures discovered by the method are qualitatively similar to those crafted by human experts, which suggests that the search process converges on meaningful and intuitive design principles.

The weaknesses of the paper are:
1. The method suffers from a significant computational burden, requiring extensive training time even when leveraging considerable hardware resources.
2. The experiments do not sufficiently explore the generalizability of the discovered architectures. The work would be strengthened by evaluating how an architecture generated for one task performs on other similar, yet distinct, datasets, especially concerning the sequential models.

Overall, this is a strong contribution. However, more extensive experimentation is needed to fully demonstrate its potential advantages over state-of-the-art, human-designed models.
