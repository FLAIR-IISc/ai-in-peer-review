The authors report that after training the controller RNN, the best-performing cell is selected based on the lowest validation perplexity. This cell then undergoes a hyperparameter grid search that includes learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates, and decay epoch. The optimized cell is subsequently implemented in three different configurations and sizes to increase its capacity.

For the sake of reproducibility, it would be highly beneficial if the authors could provide the specific hyperparameters and the types of dropout used (e.g., recurrent dropout, embedding dropout). Without these details, replicating the work would necessitate considerable trial and error. As demonstrated by prior influential work like "Recurrent Neural Network Regularization" (Zaremba et al., 2014), publishing a baseline set of hyperparameters is a significant service to the research community that facilitates future advancements. This level of detail would also be valuable for the other experiments presented, such as the character-level language modeling.

This paper investigates the automation of architecture search, a vital area of research in our field. While the proposed technique is currently computationally intensive, the associated trade-offs are expected to become more favorable as technology progresses.

The work's experimental validation is thorough, covering both standard vision and text tasks across numerous benchmark datasets. The results effectively demonstrate that there are performance gains to be realized by searching beyond the confines of conventional RNN and CNN architectures. Although one could always ask for evaluation on more datasets, the presented evidence is more than sufficient to show that the proposed technique is not only competitive with but can potentially surpass human architectural design. Furthermore, this approach suggests a method for tailoring architectures to specific datasets without extensive hand-engineering at every step.

This is a well-written paper on a significant topic, supported by strong empirical results. I recommend that it be accepted.
