### Summary

This paper introduces a novel approach for automating the discovery of neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller trained with reinforcement learning. The controller generates architectural descriptions for RNN cells or convolutional networks, which are then trained to convergence on a target task. The performance of the trained network serves as a reward signal to update the controller, encouraging it to generate better architectures over time. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on prominent language modeling (Penn Treebank) and image classification (CIFAR-10) benchmarks, discovering architectures that are competitive with, and in some cases surpass, state-of-the-art models designed by human experts.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses the fundamental and challenging problem of architecture engineering, a critical bottleneck in deep learning research. The proposed method of using a controller RNN to automate this search is both novel and promising. While the computational cost is currently high, this work represents a significant step towards automating a key aspect of machine learning research, a direction that will likely become more feasible as computational resources improve.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The empirical validation is a key strength. The authors apply their method across different domains and achieve impressive results. The discovered RNN cell, "NASCell," achieves a new state-of-the-art test perplexity on the Penn Treebank, and the discovered convolutional architecture achieves a competitive error rate on CIFAR-10. This demonstrates that the approach is not only effective but also generalizable.

3.  **Surpassing Human-Designed Architectures:** The most compelling finding is that the method can discover architectures that outperform meticulously hand-tuned models. This provides strong evidence that exploring beyond the conventional search space of human intuition can yield significant performance gains. It also suggests a powerful paradigm for tailoring architectures to specific datasets without extensive, manual trial and error.

### Weaknesses and Request for Clarification

The primary weakness of the paper in its current form is the lack of detail regarding the hyperparameter settings used for training the final, discovered architectures. This hinders the reproducibility of the excellent results presented.

The paper states: "[A]fter the controller RNN is done training, we take the best RNN cell according to the lowest validation perplexity and then run a grid search over learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates and decay epoch. The best cell found was then run with three different configurations and sizes to increase its capacity."

To ensure that other researchers can validate and build upon this work, it is crucial that the authors provide the specific values or ranges used in this grid search. Without this information, replication would require a great deal of costly and time-consuming experimentation. Specifically, please provide details on:

*   The grid search ranges and final values for the learning rate, weight initialization, and decay epoch.
*   The dropout rates used and, importantly, the type of dropout applied (e.g., recurrent dropout on hidden states, embedding dropout, output dropout).

This information is vital not only for the language modeling experiments on Penn Treebank but also for the image classification and character modeling tasks. As demonstrated by prior influential work, such as "Recurrent Neural Network Regularization" (Zaremba et al., 2014), releasing a base set of hyperparameters greatly accelerates progress in the field and allows the community to more effectively build upon these strong results.

### Recommendation

Overall, this is a well-written and impactful paper that tackles an important problem with a novel approach and compelling results. While the work would be significantly strengthened by the inclusion of detailed hyperparameters to ensure reproducibility, the core contributions are substantial and represent a valuable addition to the field.

I recommend acceptance.
