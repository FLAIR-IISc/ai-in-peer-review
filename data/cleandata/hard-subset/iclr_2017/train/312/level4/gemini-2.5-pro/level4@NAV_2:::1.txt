This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a novel and compelling method for automating the design of neural network architectures. The authors frame this challenging task as a reinforcement learning problem, where a controller Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) learns to generate descriptions of high-performing network architectures by being rewarded based on the validation accuracy of the generated "child" networks. The method's strength and versatility are demonstrated by designing both convolutional networks and recurrent cells from scratch, achieving state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on canonical benchmarks like CIFAR-10 and the Penn Treebank.

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The work addresses the critical and difficult problem of architecture engineering with an elegant and principled method, and the claims are substantiated by extensive and impressive empirical results that rival or even surpass the best human-designed architectures.

The proposed approach is notable for its flexibility. Using an RNN controller to generate a variable-length string of hyperparameters allows the exploration of a vast and complex search space, including modern architectural features like skip connections. This moves significantly beyond the scope of traditional hyperparameter optimization techniques. The empirical validation is thorough and convincing; by successfully tackling both a standard vision task (CIFAR-10) and a language modeling task (Penn Treebank), the authors demonstrate the broad applicability of their technique. Achieving state-of-the-art performance, particularly in discovering a new recurrent cell for the well-established Penn Treebank benchmark, is a significant achievement. It demonstrates that the search method can uncover genuinely novel and effective architectural motifs. The inclusion of a comparison against random search further validates that the policy gradient approach is effectively learning and is superior to a strong baseline.

To further strengthen the paper and maximize its impact on the community, I offer the following feedback. While the search methodology is well-described, the paper's primary weakness is the lack of sufficient detail to allow for the full reproduction of the final, state-of-the-art models. The authors state that after identifying the best architecture via their search, they subsequently perform a grid search over hyperparameters like learning rate, weight decay, and dropout rates to achieve the final reported scores. Providing the specific configurations resulting from this secondary search is crucial for enabling other researchers to verify, build upon, and extend these important findings. The release of base hyperparameters in influential papers, such as Zaremba et al. (2014), has historically proven to be a significant service to the field.

Could the authors please provide the specific hyperparameters (e.g., final learning rate, weight initialization scheme, dropout rates and types) used to train the best-performing architectures for the CIFAR-10, Penn Treebank word-level, and Penn Treebank character-level experiments? This information would be invaluable for replication.
