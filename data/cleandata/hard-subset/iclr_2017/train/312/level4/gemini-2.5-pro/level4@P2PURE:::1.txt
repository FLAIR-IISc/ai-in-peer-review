The paper would be significantly strengthened by the inclusion of key hyperparameter details. The authors state that after training the controller, they "take the best RNN cell according to the lowest validation perplexity and then run a grid search over learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates and decay epoch." However, the specific values used in this grid search and the type of dropout employed (e.g., recurrent dropout, embedding dropout) are not provided. Without this information, reproducing the results would require extensive trial and error. As demonstrated by prior work such as "Recurrent Neural Network Regularization" (Zaremba et al., 2014), releasing a base set of hyperparameters is highly beneficial for future research in the field. This request for hyperparameter details also applies to the other experiments presented, such as the character-level language modeling task.

This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of automated architecture search. While the proposed method is currently computationally intensive, this is a reasonable trade-off that is likely to become more favorable as technology improves.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach across a wide range of vision and language tasks on several benchmark datasets. The results show that exploring architectures beyond the typical human-designed search space can yield significant performance gains. The experimental validation is comprehensive and sufficiently demonstrates that the technique is not only competitive with, but can even surpass, human-designed architectures. This work also suggests a promising direction for automatically tailoring architectures to specific datasets without extensive manual engineering.

Overall, this is a well-written paper on an important topic, supported by strong results. I recommend acceptance.
