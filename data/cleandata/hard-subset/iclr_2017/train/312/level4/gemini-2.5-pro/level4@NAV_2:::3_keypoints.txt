- Proposes Neural Architecture Search: an RNN controller trained with reinforcement learning to generate child-network architectures automatically.
- Controller samples variable-length architecture strings; child networks are trained and validation performance is used as a reward to update the controller.
- Innovative and significant reframing of architecture engineering as a scalable RL problem.
- Uses REINFORCE to optimize a non-differentiable objective (validation performance), enabling exploration beyond fixed-dimensional hyperparameter spaces.
- Achieves state-of-the-art on CIFAR-10 with a discovered convolutional network (test error 3.65%) that is more accurate and faster than comparable DenseNet models.
- Discovers a recurrent cell for Penn Treebank that yields perplexity 62.4, a 3.6 improvement over prior bests.
- Demonstrates generalization: the discovered cell improves character-level language modeling and helps in large-scale neural machine translation.
- Comparison to random search shows the controller learns an effective search strategy, justifying the RL approach.
- Strong empirical validation across two distinct domains supports high acceptance recommendation.
- Primary strength is the novel formulation allowing exploration of vast, variable-length design spaces not handled by traditional hyperparameter optimization.
- Major practical limitation: enormous computational cost (e.g., ~800 GPUs for CIFAR-10), which restricts applicability and merits discussion of efficiency improvements.
- Search space design remains manual and may critically affect outcomes; sensitivity to meta-choices (e.g., inclusion of pooling, block predictions) should be discussed.
- Calls for deeper analysis/visualization of discovered architectures (beyond appendix) to explain patterns like rectangular filters and one-step skip connections.
- Questions for authors: motivation and sensitivity of specific non-linear reward transformations used (e.g., cubing accuracy, inverse-squared perplexity).
- Questions for authors: provide rough wall-clock time estimates for completing the CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank architecture searches.
