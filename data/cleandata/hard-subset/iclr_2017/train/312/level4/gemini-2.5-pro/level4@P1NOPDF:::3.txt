This paper proposes a method for automatically designing neural network architectures for specific tasks using a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller trained with reinforcement learning (RL). The core idea is promising, and the experimental results on two datasets demonstrate that the method is solid.

**Strengths:**
1.  The central idea of using an RNN to generate a description of a network architecture and then training this controller with reinforcement learning is novel and compelling. This approach provides a systematic and learnable framework for exploring the vast space of possible network designs.
2.  It is notable that the architectures discovered by this method exhibit motifs and structures reminiscent of those created by human experts. This suggests that the approach learns sensible design principles and that its outputs are aligned with established architectural best practices.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The primary drawback is the significant computational cost of the architecture search process. The authors report that discovering a high-performing architecture requires extensive training time, even when leveraging substantial computational resources. This high cost could limit the practical applicability of the method for many researchers and practitioners.
2.  The experiments do not sufficiently explore the generalizability of the discovered architectures. It would strengthen the paper to evaluate the performance of a generated architecture (e.g., one discovered on CIFAR-10) on other similar, but distinct, datasets. This would help determine whether the method finds architectures that are overfit to a single task or if it discovers more fundamental and transferable design principles. This point is especially relevant for the generated sequential models.

**Overall Assessment:**
In summary, this is a strong paper that introduces a promising direction for automated machine learning. However, the work would be significantly bolstered by additional experiments that better contextualize its potential advantages over human-designed models. Specifically, addressing the limitations regarding computational cost and the generalizability of the learned architectures would be crucial for establishing the method's practical impact.
