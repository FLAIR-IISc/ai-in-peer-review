The paper states: “After the controller RNN is trained, we take the best RNN cell according to the lowest validation perplexity and then run a grid search over learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates, and decay epoch. The best cell found was then run with three different configurations and sizes to increase its capacity.” Could you include (or provide here) the exact hyperparameters and the types of dropout used (e.g., recurrent dropout, embedding dropout, etc.)? Without these details, reproducing the results would require substantial trial and error. Following the precedent of “Recurrent Neural Network Regularization” (Zaremba et al., 2014), releasing a base set of hyperparameters significantly improves reproducibility and accelerates progress. The same request applies to the other experiments, including the character-level language modeling setup.

This paper addresses an important area—automated architecture search. While the method is currently computationally demanding, that cost will likely become less prohibitive as hardware and systems improve.

The work spans both vision and language tasks and evaluates across multiple benchmarks, demonstrating that exploring architectures beyond standard RNN and CNN search spaces yields tangible gains. Although additional datasets would always strengthen the case, the presented evidence is more than sufficient to show that the approach is competitive with, and at times superior to, human-designed architectures. Moreover, the results suggest a practical pathway for tailoring architectures to specific datasets without manual design at each step.

Overall, this is a well-written paper on a timely and impactful topic, supported by strong empirical results. Conditional on providing full training details (particularly the exact hyperparameters, dropout variants, and the specifics of the three final configurations and sizes), I recommend acceptance.
