Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes using reinforcement learning to search over variable-length neural network architectures. Architectures are represented as sequences of tokens, and a controller RNN (an auto-regressive two-layer LSTM) sequentially generates these tokens.
- The search is framed as an RL problem: selecting nodes/hyperparameters is treated as a sequence of actions and the validation performance of the resulting architecture is used as a reward. The setup is described in an actor-critic style, with the controller acting as the actor.
- The approach is evaluated on two tasks—image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (PTB)—and compared against several human-designed architectures. The paper is clearly written, presents the method and necessary engineering improvements in detail, and provides a solid discussion of related work.

Decision: Accept
- Key reasons: (1) The approach is novel and well motivated, addressing a central and difficult problem—automated architecture design—using a principled RL framework. (2) The empirical results on two distinct domains are compelling and demonstrate that the method can rival or surpass strong human-designed baselines.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and motivation: Hand-crafting architectures is time-consuming and requires expertise; it is difficult to know how close such designs are to optimal. The paper directly tackles this with an automated method that treats architecture search as a sequence prediction problem.
- Methodology and clarity: The method is thoroughly described, including how actions correspond to architectural choices, how rewards are computed from validation accuracy, and how the controller is trained. The additional engineering components (e.g., skip connections, distributed/asynchronous training) are explained clearly and seem necessary to make the approach practical.
- Placement in the literature: The paper situates the work well with respect to hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, sequence-to-sequence modeling, and meta-learning. The contrast to prior search methods and the advantages of a flexible, variable-length representation are well articulated.
- Empirical support: Evaluations on CIFAR-10 and PTB are appropriate and demonstrate the method’s capability. The comparisons to human-designed models are informative, and the qualitative differences between learned and human architectures are interesting and insightful.
- Presentation: The paper is accessible and well written, making a complex procedure easy to understand.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not central to the decision)
- Report computational budget and efficiency: Please provide more detailed data on training time and resources (e.g., number of architectures sampled, total GPU/CPU hours, parallelism used) and how these relate to final model quality. A plot of reward/validation performance versus wall-clock time or total compute would be very helpful.
- Analyze search dynamics: It would be informative to show the correlation between training time/resources and the quality of the sampled architectures, as well as how quickly the controller improves over random search throughout training.
- Human-bootstrapped starting points: It would be interesting to see how the method performs when initialized from or constrained by a human-designed seed architecture. Do such bootstrapped models improve further, and how do they evolve under the controller’s policy?
- Robustness and variability: If possible, include variability across runs (e.g., multiple independent controller trainings) to characterize stability and sensitivity to random seeds.
- Ablations: While the improvements are described, concise ablations quantifying the impact of key components (e.g., skip-connection prediction, distributed/asynchronous updates, reward shaping) would strengthen the empirical case.

Questions for the authors
1. Compute and efficiency: What is the total computational cost (e.g., total GPU/CPU hours) for the reported CIFAR-10 and PTB searches? How does validation performance improve over time/compute?
2. Reward and stability: How sensitive is the controller’s learning to the choice of reward shaping (e.g., cubing validation accuracy) and baseline? Did you consider or try an explicit critic beyond a moving-average baseline?
3. Search space and constraints: How does expanding or constraining the search space (e.g., adding/removing stride/pooling choices) affect convergence speed and final performance?
4. Transfer and bootstrapping: Have you tried initializing the controller with a human-designed architecture (or restricting the policy near it) to see whether the method can further refine strong human baselines?
5. Variance and reproducibility: What is the variance across independent runs of the controller? How consistent are the best-found architectures and their test performance?

Overall assessment
An excellent and exciting paper that addresses a timely and important problem with a novel, well-motivated approach and strong empirical results. The writing is clear, the method is well placed in the literature, and the experiments substantiate the main claims. I recommend acceptance.
