1. Summary of contributions
The paper tackles the problem of automating neural network architecture design. It proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), where a controller RNN emits variable-length descriptions of child architectures and is trained with policy gradients to maximize expected validation accuracy. The method supports rich search spaces (e.g., convolutions with skip connections and branching, and novel recurrent cells), and uses large-scale distributed, asynchronous training to evaluate many candidate architectures efficiently. Empirically, the approach discovers convolutional models for CIFAR-10 that are competitive with or better than strong human-designed baselines, and identifies a recurrent cell for PTB that outperforms standard LSTM variants. The discovered RNN cell also transfers to character-level PTB and yields gains in a GNMT machine translation setup with minimal tuning. Overall, the paper presents a compelling, general approach to programmatically composing high-performing neural architectures.

2. Decision
Accept (weak). Key reasons:
- Novel and well-motivated approach that unifies architecture generation with reinforcement learning over variable-length design spaces.
- Strong results on two distinct modalities (vision and language), with evidence of transfer for the learned recurrent cell.

3. Supporting arguments
- Specific question/problem: Can we automatically learn task-specific neural architectures that match or surpass human-designed models, by directly optimizing validation performance over a large, variable-length architecture space?
- Motivation and placement in literature: The method is clearly positioned relative to hyperparameter optimization (which typically assumes fixed-length configuration spaces) and neuroevolution (more flexible but often less scalable). It also connects to meta-learning and sequence-to-sequence modeling, extending these ideas to non-differentiable rewards via REINFORCE. The motivation for a controller RNN is sound given the sequential, variable-length nature of architecture descriptions.
- Do the results support the claims? The paper reports competitive or state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 (3.65% error with improved speed over a comparable DenseNet variant) and on PTB (test perplexity 62.4), plus transfer to character PTB and a GNMT setting. Controls include a random-search comparison showing policy-gradient improves both best and top-average performance, and an expanded search space test (adding new ops) with comparable outcomes. The approach is technically sound, with variance reduction for policy gradients and a careful handling of skip connections. However, the evaluation relies on substantial compute and offers limited evidence on cross-task generality of the discovered CNNs, and reproducibility under smaller budgets is unclear.

Strengths:
- Conceptually elegant, flexible, and extensible framework for architecture synthesis.
- Solid empirical gains on two canonical benchmarks, plus initial transferability of the learned RNN cell.
- Clear description of the controller, reward shaping, and distributed training scheme.

Limitations:
- High computational cost for search; practical adoption may be challenging without significant resources.
- Limited evidence of generality for discovered CNNs across datasets; most transfer analyses focus on the RNN cell.
- Some fairness questions around search-space design and post-search fine-tuning (grid search) relative to baselines.

4. Suggestions for improvement (to help the authors; not all are decision-critical)
- Generalization: Evaluate the best discovered CNN on additional image datasets (e.g., CIFAR-100, SVHN) without architecture changes; similarly test cross-corpus transfer for the RNN cell beyond PTB (e.g., WT2).
- Compute and reproducibility: Report end-to-end search cost (GPU/CPU hours), controller convergence behavior, and variance across runs. Provide guidance for low-budget settings (e.g., fewer replicas, early stopping heuristics).
- Ablations: Analyze sensitivity to reward shaping (e.g., cubing accuracy), depth-schedule choices, and skip-connection compilation rules. Show how performance changes with reduced search space or fewer sampled architectures.
- Baselines: Include stronger automated baselines (e.g., recent Bayesian optimization for non-fixed spaces, evolutionary strategies) with matched budgets. Clarify parameter counts and training budgets for the final models to ensure comparability.
- Search space design: Discuss priors embedded in the search space (e.g., available ops, pooling constraints), and how these priors influence outcomes. Consider learning or adapting the space itself over time.
- Controller details: Provide more intuition on why a small 2-layer LSTM suffices and whether deeper/wider controllers help. Explore entropy regularization or KL constraints to stabilize exploration.

Questions for the authors
- Compute budget and stability: What is the total wall-clock and accelerator-time cost for the reported searches (CIFAR-10 and PTB)? How sensitive are results to random seeds and the number of evaluated architectures?
- Reward shaping: Why use the cube of validation accuracy (or squared inverse perplexity)? Did alternative scalings or baselines change sample efficiency or final performance?
- Transferability: How well do the best CIFAR-10 architectures transfer to other datasets without modification? Conversely, does the discovered RNN cell perform well on larger corpora (e.g., WT103) or non-English data?
- Search space dependence: How much do final results depend on including specific ops (e.g., batch norm, particular kernel sizes)? If you exclude some ops or add more (as in max/sin), how does search efficiency and quality change?
- Controller and optimization: Did you try alternative policy-gradient methods (e.g., actor-critic, PPO) or variance-reduction techniques? Any evidence these improve sample efficiency?
- Reproducibility under constraints: With 10× fewer resources, what performance drop should practitioners expect, and which heuristics best mitigate it (e.g., weight sharing, early stopping of child models)?
- Fair comparisons: For the final test numbers, were training schedules and data augmentation strictly matched to baselines? Please provide parameter counts and training FLOPs for the best models.
