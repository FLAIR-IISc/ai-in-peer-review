Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method that uses a controller RNN trained with policy gradient to generate neural network architectures. The controller outputs variable-length sequences describing either convolutional networks (for CIFAR-10) or recurrent cells (for PTB). The reward is the validation performance of a “child” network trained to convergence. The approach flexibly handles skip connections and branching via an attention-based set-selection mechanism, and scales through distributed, asynchronous training.

Empirically, NAS discovers:
- A CIFAR-10 model achieving 3.65% test error, competitive with or better than contemporary human-designed architectures, while being about 1.05x faster than a strong DenseNet baseline of comparable scheme.
- A novel recurrent cell for PTB that improves test perplexity to 62.4, a gain of ~3.6 perplexity over prior state of the art, and transfers to character-level PTB (1.214 bpc) and GNMT with a +0.5 BLEU improvement without bespoke tuning.

The paper is well written, well situated in related work (hyperparameter optimization, neuroevolution, meta-learning, sequence-level RL), and tackles two distinct modalities, demonstrating the generality of the approach.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
1) Strong, state-of-the-art empirical results across both vision and language, including transfer to new tasks.  
2) A clear, well-motivated approach that expands the architecture search space beyond fixed-length hyperparameter settings, with a principled RL training setup and distributed systems design.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Automating architecture design is a central challenge; the method directly addresses the need to search rich, variable-length spaces (including skip connections and cell structures) rather than tuning shallow hyperparameters.
- Motivation and placement: The work thoughtfully contrasts with Bayesian optimization and neuroevolution, explaining why an autoregressive controller paired with REINFORCE is more flexible and scalable for variable-length architectural decisions.
- Technical soundness: The policy gradient setup is standard and correct. Variance reduction with an EMA baseline and reward shaping are clearly described. The extension to skip connections via content-based sigmoids, and to RNN cell trees with cell-state injection, is well formalized.
- Evidence for claims: Results are strong on CIFAR-10 and PTB, and the inclusion of transfer to character LM and MT strengthens the generality claim. The paper includes controls (random search vs. policy gradient; expanded search space with additional functions) and discusses sensitivity to design choices (e.g., strides, pooling).
- Practical relevance: While computationally intensive, the distributed training strategy is transparent and will become more accessible over time; the released TensorFlow NASCell and planned model code further increase impact.

Additional feedback and questions (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
Reproducibility and hyperparameters
- Please provide full hyperparameter settings for the top PTB and character PTB results and for the best CIFAR-10 model(s), ideally in an appendix or repository:
  - Optimization: optimizer type, learning rate schedule (values and decay epochs), momentum/β values, weight decay, gradient clipping (if any), initialization schemes.
  - Dropout: types used (embedding dropout, recurrent/variational dropout, output dropout), exact rates per layer, whether tied across time, and where applied.
  - Batch sizes, BPTT lengths, training epochs/steps, early-stopping criteria.
  - For “shared embeddings,” the exact tying configuration and any regularization.
  - Reward shaping constants (e.g., c for PTB and the “accuracy cubed” choice on CIFAR-10) and their observed impact.
Providing a “base” configuration, as in Zaremba et al. (2014), would greatly improve replicability and accelerate follow-up work.

Compute budget and fairness of comparison
- Please report total search cost per dataset (e.g., GPU-days/CPU-days), average/median time to train a child model, and the number of architectures evaluated to produce the final result.
- For CIFAR-10, comparisons to DenseNet and others would be clearer with exact parameter counts, FLOPs, and wall-clock speed on a common hardware setup, as well as details about data augmentation equivalence.
- For PTB, consider reporting variance across multiple re-trainings of the best cell and confidence intervals for validation/test perplexity.

Search space and constraints
- On CIFAR-10, you restrict pooling layers to fixed positions for tractability. Could you quantify how this constraint affects the search space and performance? Is the controller able to learn competitive pooling placement when allowed to choose positions?
- For skip connections: please clarify how often “compilation fixes” (e.g., zero-padding to resolve shape mismatches, defaulting to image input) are invoked and whether these heuristics bias the search outcomes.

Ablations and sensitivity
- Reward shaping: How sensitive are outcomes to the “accuracy cubed” transformation and to the EMA baseline timescale?
- Controller capacity: Did you observe gains from larger controller models or different RNN types? Any signs of overfitting to the validation reward distribution?
- Depth schedule: Provide an ablation on the increasing-depth schedule for CIFAR-10 to show its necessity/effect.
- Random search baseline: The comparison is valuable. If feasible, add a Bayesian optimization baseline configured for the same budget to further contextualize gains.

Methodological clarifications
- For the RNN cell search, please specify the exact “base 8” tree topology, parameter sharing across nodes (if any), and whether internal-node operations have learnable parameters.
- Detail any gradient clipping, layer norm, or other stabilization used in training child RNNs.
- In GNMT experiments, confirm that computational complexity was matched (params/FLOPs per step) and report BLEU variance across checkpoints or runs.

Data usage and evaluation protocol
- Confirm that test sets were evaluated only once for final selected models and that no test feedback informed the controller.
- For CIFAR-10, specify exact preprocessing/augmentation and whether cutout or other augmentations were used (it appears not).

Overall, this is an excellent and timely paper. The main weaknesses are practical: computational cost and some missing training details that hinder replication. These are addressable by releasing full configs and code (which the authors indicate they will do). With that clarification, the contribution is likely to have substantial and lasting impact.
