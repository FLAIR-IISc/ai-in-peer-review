Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a reinforcement learning framework in which a controller RNN generates variable-length descriptions of neural network architectures. The controller is trained with REINFORCE to maximize validation performance of the sampled “child” networks. The search space includes modern components such as skip connections and multiple layer types, and the method scales via distributed, asynchronous updates. NAS discovers high-performing models from scratch: on CIFAR-10, a CNN achieving 3.65% test error while being 1.05x faster than a comparable DenseNet variant; on Penn Treebank, a recurrent cell that surpasses LSTM with 62.4 test perplexity, transfers to character PTB with a state-of-the-art 1.214, and improves GNMT by +0.5 BLEU without retuning. The work is well written, well positioned relative to hyperparameter optimization, neuroevolution, and meta-learning, and provides compelling empirical evidence that automated architecture search can match or exceed human designs.

Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) Strong, cross-domain empirical results that convincingly demonstrate the promise of automated architecture search. (2) A clear, well-motivated approach that broadens the practical search space (variable-length, skip connections) and shows superiority over a strong random-search baseline.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Automating architecture design addresses a central bottleneck in modern ML. The method targets the structure and connectivity of models rather than only fixed-length hyperparameters.
- Methodological soundness: The controller RNN with policy gradient is appropriate for a non-differentiable reward. The paper details reward shaping, variance reduction via a moving-average baseline, and a scalable distributed training setup.
- Positioning in the literature: The work is well placed among Bayesian optimization, neuroevolution, program induction, and meta-learning. The contrasts are accurate and fair.
- Empirical rigor: Results cover both vision and language, include transfer to character-level modeling and machine translation, and compare against strong baselines. The random-search control further supports the claim that learning the policy improves over naive exploration.
- Practical relevance: Although computationally intensive, the cost can plausibly be amortized across tasks or reduced as hardware improves; the demonstrated speed-accuracy tradeoffs (e.g., CIFAR-10) are encouraging.

Additional feedback and questions (to improve clarity and reproducibility; not central to my decision)
- Hyperparameters for final training:
  - Please provide the full hyperparameter settings for the best PTB models after the grid search: learning rates, initialization schemes, decay epochs, gradient clipping (if any), optimizer details, and exact dropout configurations (types and rates: embedding dropout, recurrent/variational dropout, input/output dropout).
  - For CIFAR-10, list the final training schedule for the selected architectures: base learning rate, decay epochs and factors, weight decay, batchnorm epsilon/momentum, momentum/Nesterov settings, and any cutout or additional augmentation (if not used, please state so).
- Dropout and regularization details:
  - Clarify precisely which dropout variants are used in each PTB configuration (with and without shared embeddings), and their rates per layer. For the character PTB and GNMT transfer, please specify whether embedding dropout, variational dropout, and/or weight decay were used, with exact values.
- Search and compute budget:
  - Report the total number of sampled/evaluated architectures per task, average training time per child model, total compute (GPU/CPU hours), and the number of architectures considered before the best model emerged.
  - Provide the schedule for increasing child-network depth (increments, frequency) and any early-stopping criteria for child training.
- Reward shaping and baseline:
  - Justify the choice of cubing validation accuracy for CIFAR-10 and squaring c/validation perplexity for PTB. Did alternative reward scalings change the outcome? Please provide sensitivity or an ablation if available.
  - Detail the moving-average baseline’s decay factor and any normalization across batches.
- Search space specification:
  - For CNNs, enumerate the exact options for each decision (filter sizes, counts, stride choices, layer types, pooling positions) and constraints that avoid invalid graphs. For RNN cells, list the full set of combination and activation functions and the tree indexing scheme, including how ct and ct−1 are injected.
  - Clarify padding/concatenation rules when skip connections produce mismatched shapes, and whether projection layers were ever allowed.
- Fairness and comparison:
  - For the CIFAR-10 comparison to DenseNet, please clarify differences in parameter counts and use of bottleneck/transition layers to contextualize the speedup claim.
  - Report variance across multiple runs (different seeds) for both the controller and the final trained child models.
- Reproducibility aids:
  - Including a configuration file or script that reproduces the best PTB and CIFAR-10 models would meaningfully reduce trial-and-error for the community, similar to the baselines released with Zaremba et al. (2014).

Overall, this is an impactful and timely paper that advances the state of the art and opens a promising avenue for data-driven architecture design. The additional details above would further strengthen its reproducibility and long-term utility.
