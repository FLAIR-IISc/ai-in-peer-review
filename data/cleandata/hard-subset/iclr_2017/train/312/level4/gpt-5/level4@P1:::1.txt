1. Summary of contributions
This paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a controller RNN trained with policy gradients (REINFORCE) to generate variable-length neural network architectures. The controller samples model descriptions (for CNNs and RNN cells), each sampled “child” network is trained, and its validation accuracy serves as the reward to update the controller. The approach supports skip connections, multiple layer types, and a flexible search space, and is scaled via distributed, asynchronous training. Empirically, the method discovers novel CNNs on CIFAR-10 that rival or surpass strong human-designed baselines, and a recurrent cell on PTB that outperforms LSTM-based models and transfers well to character-level PTB and GNMT, indicating both competitiveness and potential superiority to hand-crafted designs.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Strong empirical results across vision and language tasks, including state-of-the-art or near-SOTA performance, demonstrating the practical value of the approach.
- A clear, well-motivated method that meaningfully expands the search space beyond standard hyperparameter optimization, with evidence that learned architectures can match or surpass human designs.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Automating neural architecture design is an important and timely direction, reducing reliance on expert intuition and enabling task-specific architectures.
- Method motivation and placement: The paper situates NAS relative to Bayesian hyperparameter optimization and neuroevolution, highlighting its flexibility in variable-length spaces and practical scalability via distributed training.
- Empirical support: On CIFAR-10, the discovered architectures are competitive with top-performing models while being relatively efficient; on PTB, the learned cell improves perplexity over LSTM-based baselines and generalizes to character-level language modeling and GNMT with gains. Control experiments (e.g., larger search space; comparison to random search) support the robustness and necessity of policy gradient over naive baselines.
- Limitations: The method is computationally intensive, but this trade-off is reasonable given current trends in compute, and the results demonstrate the payoff.

4. Additional feedback and questions (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
Reproducibility. The paper states: “After the controller RNN is done training, we take the best RNN cell according to the lowest validation perplexity and then run a grid search over learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates and decay epoch. The best cell found was then run with three different configurations and sizes to increase its capacity.” To facilitate replication and downstream work (as in Zaremba et al., 2014), please provide a consolidated set of hyperparameters and training settings for the final runs, ideally in an appendix or repository. Without them, reproducing results would likely require substantial trial and error.

Requested details (PTB word-level; also for character LM and CIFAR-10 where applicable):
- Dropout: types used (e.g., recurrent/variational dropout, embedding dropout, input/output dropout), precise rates per layer, whether shared masks are used, and any differences across “small/medium/large” configurations.
- Optimization: optimizer (e.g., Adam vs. SGD with momentum), initial learning rate(s), schedule/decay epochs, decay factors, gradient clipping (threshold and norm type), weight decay, and any warmup.
- Initialization: scheme(s) used for recurrent and non-recurrent parameters.
- Model capacity: hidden sizes, number of layers, embedding sizes, whether input–output embeddings are tied, and vocabulary handling.
- Training regimen: batch size, BPTT length, number of epochs/steps, early stopping criteria, validation protocol, and exact definition of “convergence” for child models during search.
- Reward shaping: exact reward function constants (e.g., c in c(validation perplexity)^2; cubing validation accuracy for CIFAR-10), and rationale.
- Data processing: data splits, preprocessing, and augmentation details (e.g., whitening, crop strategy, flips).
- Search specifics: search space enumerations (e.g., allowed filter sizes, strides, pooling positions, base number for the RNN cell tree), schedules (e.g., increasing depth schedule), and any constraints or padding rules for skip connections.
- Final CIFAR-10 models: the exact architecture(s) selected post-search, the grid-search ranges (learning rate, weight decay, BN epsilon, decay epochs), and the configuration with the +40 filters. Indicate how speedup (1.05x) was measured.
- Seeds and infrastructure: random seeds, number of controller/child replicas used for final reported results, and any deviations between search-time and final training settings.

Providing a “base configuration” for each reported result (CIFAR-10, PTB word-level, PTB character-level, and GNMT) would greatly aid the community in reproducing and building upon this work.

Overall, this is a well-written paper on an important topic with compelling results. I recommend acceptance.
