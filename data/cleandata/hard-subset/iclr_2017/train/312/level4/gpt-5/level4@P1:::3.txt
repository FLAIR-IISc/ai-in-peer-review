1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method that uses a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller trained with reinforcement learning (REINFORCE) to generate neural network architectures as sequences of tokens. The controller samples architectural choices (e.g., filter sizes, number of filters, skip connections, and for RNNs, cell structure), builds and trains the corresponding “child” networks, and then updates its policy to maximize expected validation accuracy. The approach is flexible enough to explore variable-length architectures and is scaled using distributed, asynchronous training. Empirically, the method discovers convolutional architectures on CIFAR-10 that are competitive with human-designed models and constructs a recurrent cell for Penn Treebank (PTB) that outperforms standard LSTM baselines. Overall, the idea is promising, the setup is well-motivated and grounded in related work on hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, sequence-to-sequence, and meta-learning, and the results on two benchmarks are strong.

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Limited evidence of generality/transferability of the discovered architectures, especially for the sequential models, beyond the specific datasets considered.
- Very high computational cost and training time, which raises concerns about practicality and reproducibility.

3. Supporting arguments
- Generality: While the method shows solid gains on CIFAR-10 and PTB, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence that the discovered architectures or cells transfer to other datasets or tasks with minimal or no re-search. This is particularly important for the RNN cell to demonstrate that improvements are not dataset-specific. Stronger demonstrations on related datasets would better support the claim that the method can learn generally useful architectural motifs.
- Computational cost: The search requires training thousands of child models to convergence using large-scale distributed infrastructure (hundreds of GPUs/CPUs). Even with such resources, the end-to-end time is long. This makes the approach difficult for most practitioners to reproduce or apply. The heavy compute budget also raises the question of whether simpler, more accessible baselines (e.g., random search with a well-chosen search space) might achieve comparable results within a similar budget.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not central to my decision)
- Generalization studies:
  - For CNNs, evaluate transfer by training the best CIFAR-10 architecture on related datasets (e.g., CIFAR-100, SVHN) without changing the architecture, and report performance gaps and fine-tuning needs.
  - For RNNs, provide results on additional language modeling benchmarks (e.g., WikiText-2) or other sequence tasks to demonstrate broader usefulness of the learned cell. Clearly separate “search on source dataset” vs. “zero-shot transfer” vs. “light tuning.”
- Practicality and compute budget:
  - Report detailed wall-clock time, total compute (GPU/CPU hours), and the relationship between performance and the number of architectures evaluated. Curves showing diminishing returns would help readers understand the cost-benefit tradeoff.
  - Discuss avenues for improving sample efficiency (e.g., weight sharing across child models, early stopping, learning curve extrapolation, low-fidelity proxies), even if not implemented.
- Analysis of discovered architectures:
  - Provide more interpretation of the learned patterns (e.g., prevalence and role of skip connections, filter shapes across depth, recurring motifs in the RNN cell) and how they compare to human-designed designs. This would strengthen the scientific insight and help practitioners.
- Reproducibility:
  - Since the paper promises code release, ensure that both the controller and search spaces (for CNNs and RNNs) are included, with scripts to reproduce the main experiments and reasonable defaults for smaller-scale runs.

Questions for the authors
- Transferability:
  - How well does the best CIFAR-10 architecture perform on CIFAR-100 and SVHN without further search? What fine-tuning is required to recover strong performance?
  - For the discovered RNN cell, how does it perform on other language modeling datasets (e.g., WikiText-2) or larger-scale corpora when simply swapped in?
- Compute and efficiency:
  - What is the exact wall-clock time and total compute used to obtain the reported results on CIFAR-10 and PTB? How does validation performance improve as a function of the number of sampled architectures?
  - How sensitive is performance to the reward shaping (e.g., cubing validation accuracy) and the moving-average baseline? Did you try alternative baselines or variance reduction techniques?
- Search space and constraints:
  - How much do the results depend on the predefined search space (e.g., allowed filter sizes, presence of skip connections, base number for RNN cells)? Do smaller search spaces yield comparable results with fewer samples?
  - For the RNN cell, did you evaluate stability across multiple training runs of the same discovered architecture to assess variance?

In summary, this is a well-motivated and promising approach with strong results on two datasets. However, the current evidence for generality, especially for sequential models, and the very high computational cost prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. I encourage the authors to add broader transfer experiments and discuss practical strategies to reduce compute and improve accessibility.
