- Introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS): treats architecture design as a sequence-generation problem using a controller RNN trained with policy gradient.
- Controller is a two-layer LSTM that autoregressively emits architecture decisions and is trained with REINFORCE and a moving-average baseline.
- Supports variable-length architectures, skip connections, branching, and scales with distributed asynchronous training.
- Demonstrates strong results on CIFAR-10 (3.65% test error) and Penn Treebank (improved word-level perplexity), with transfer to char-PTB and GNMT.
- Decision: Accept.
- Novel formulation: frames architecture search as policy optimization over a flexible, variable-length search space, improving on fixed-length HPO and neuro-evolution baselines.
- Strong empirical evidence: state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art results in both vision and language, plus meaningful transfer experiments.
- Problem significance: automates time-consuming manual architecture design and discovers competitive architectures from scratch.
- Methodologically sound: uses standard policy gradient with reasonable baseline, reward shaping, and distributed updates; search-space design is well engineered.
- Empirical rigor: compares to strong baselines, includes ablations (e.g., random search), and analyzes discovered motifs.
- Clarity and positioning: well-written, thorough related work coverage, and explicates design choices.
- Ask to report compute/time budgets: number of architectures evaluated, GPU/CPU days, per-model training time, and a compute-performance plot.
- Request robustness/variance analysis: variability across seeds for controller training and final retraining, and whether controller overfits validation set.
- Ablation requests: effect of reward shaping and baseline choice; impact of search-space components (skip connections, layer types, pooling); sensitivity to max-depth schedule.
- Fairness/comparability: compare with top DenseNet variants, report FLOPs/latency/parameter counts, and consider resource-aware (multi-objective) rewards.
- Reproducibility asks: release search-space specs, controller hyperparameters, and details/justification of the post-NAS grid search.
- Analysis request: provide qualitative insights into discovered cells/architectures (operation distributions, recurring motifs, reasons for outperforming LSTMs).
- Questions about compute efficiency: how many child models trained, total GPU/CPU hours, and controller reward vs wall-clock learning curves.
- Reward design questions: sensitivity to reward transformations (e.g., cubing accuracy, perplexity constant) and use of additional variance reduction methods.
- Validation-overfitting concern: did they use multiple validation splits or cross-validation to avoid controller overfitting?
- Robustness of discovered models: stability of CIFAR-10 and PTB results when retrained with different seeds and modest hyperparameter changes.
- Search-space contribution question: which search-space components contributed most, and how controller handled downsampling when strides/pooling were allowed.
- Baseline/update details: what decay rate for moving-average baseline and any experiments with actor-critic baselines?
- Post-search tuning transparency: how much did final grid search affect reported results and performance without post-search tuning?
- Transfer/generalization: have discovered architectures been tested on other datasets/tasks to show reusable design principles?
- Resource-aware search inquiry: have they tried including FLOPs/latency/parameters in the reward and what changed in discovered architectures?
