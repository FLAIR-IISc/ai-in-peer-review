Summary:
This paper investigates automated neural architecture search using a controller RNN and demonstrates strong results across both vision and language tasks. While the method is computationally intensive, the trade-off is likely to improve as hardware and software advance. The work convincingly shows that searching beyond standard CNN and RNN design spaces can yield architectures that are competitive with, and in some cases surpass, human-designed counterparts.

Strengths:
- Important problem: Automating architecture design is a central goal for the field, with clear practical impact.
- Breadth: The method is applied to both text and vision tasks across multiple benchmark datasets, indicating generality rather than dataset-specific tuning.
- Empirical gains: The results suggest that the learned architectures can outperform common human-designed baselines.
- Clarity and presentation: The paper is well written and easy to follow.
- Potential for adaptability: The approach provides a path to tailoring architectures to specific datasets without extensive hand engineering.

Main concern: Reproducibility and hyperparameter transparency
The paper states: “After the controller RNN is done training, we take the best RNN cell according to the lowest validation perplexity and then run a grid search over learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates and decay epoch. The best cell found was then run with three different configurations and sizes to increase its capacity.” To enable faithful replication and reduce the need for extensive trial-and-error, please include (either in the paper or supplemental material) the exact hyperparameters and training details used after the architecture search phase. In particular:
- Specify the optimizer and its settings, the learning rate values and decay schedule (including decay epoch), batch size, gradient clipping (if any), weight initialization scheme and ranges, and any regularization beyond dropout.
- Clearly describe the types of dropout employed and where they are applied (e.g., recurrent/variational dropout, embedding dropout, input/output dropout, dropout on residual or skip connections).
- Provide the precise hyperparameter configurations for the three “different configurations and sizes” used to scale capacity, including how width/depth were changed.
- List the random seeds or the procedure for seed selection, if applicable.

These details would substantially improve reproducibility. As in Zaremba et al. (2014, “Recurrent Neural Network Regularization”), releasing a base set of well-performing hyperparameters (and, if possible, configuration files) greatly assists future work.

The same level of detail would be valuable for the other experiments (e.g., character-level language modeling and the vision tasks). For each setting, please provide the final selected architecture specification, the full training hyperparameters, and any task-specific regularization or preprocessing. If computationally feasible, releasing the final discovered cells/blocks and the scripts used to train them would further strengthen the paper.

Additional suggestions (optional but helpful):
- Report the search budget in a standardized way (number of architectures sampled, epochs per candidate, and compute resources used). This will help readers contextualize the cost–performance trade-off.
- Clarify whether any post-search manual tuning was performed beyond the stated grid search.

Overall assessment:
This is a strong paper addressing an important problem with convincing empirical results across multiple domains. The primary shortcoming is the lack of detailed hyperparameter and training specifications needed for reproducibility. Addressing the above requests—especially detailing the post-search training setups and dropout types—would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact and facilitate follow-up work.

Recommendation: Accept.
