Summary
This paper proposes an actor–critic approach to neural architecture search (NAS) that models a deep network as a variable-length sequence of design decisions. An auto-regressive two-layer LSTM serves as the controller (actor) and critic, sequentially selecting architectural components; the resulting candidate model is trained, and its evaluation performance provides the reward. The method is tested on two tasks and compared against a range of human-designed architectures.

Strengths
- Timely and important problem: manual architecture design is labor-intensive and can be far from optimal.
- Novel and well-motivated formulation using reinforcement learning over variable-length sequences of choices.
- Clear, accessible presentation with careful discussion of the necessary engineering improvements.
- Empirical results are compelling and illustrate the method’s capabilities, including interesting qualitative differences between machine-discovered and human-designed architectures.
- Strong coverage and positioning relative to related work.

Questions and suggestions for improvement
- Computational cost and efficiency: Please report wall-clock time, hardware, number of candidate models evaluated, and total compute (e.g., GPU-days) for each task. A budget–performance curve would help readers understand how accuracy improves as search resources increase.
- Reward and training protocol: Clarify whether the reward is accuracy, negative validation error, or another metric, and how variance in candidate training (e.g., early stopping, training epochs) affects reward reliability. An analysis of the correlation between training budget per candidate and final model quality would be valuable.
- Role of the critic: Since the framework uses an actor–critic design, an ablation comparing against a policy-gradient baseline without a critic (or with a simpler baseline) would help quantify the stability and sample-efficiency benefits of the critic.
- Variance and robustness: Report variability across independent runs of the controller (different random seeds) and provide confidence intervals for the final results. This helps assess the consistency of the search process.
- Human-bootstrapped models: It would be informative to explore seeding the controller with human-designed templates or fine-tuning from human architectures, and to compare how such hybrid initializations perform and evolve during search.
- Search space specification and sensitivity: Please detail the operations, depth/width ranges, and constraints in the search space. An analysis of sensitivity to these priors (and to controller hyperparameters such as LSTM depth and hidden size) would clarify how much the results depend on design choices.
- Generalization and transfer: Discuss whether the learned controller’s policy transfers across datasets or tasks, and whether knowledge from one domain accelerates search in another.
- Reproducibility: Include complete training details (preprocessing, optimization schedules, hyperparameters) and, if feasible, release code or search logs to facilitate verification.

Minor comments
- “A auto-regressive” should be “An auto-regressive.”
- Briefly explain how variable-length termination is handled (e.g., an end-of-sequence token or a maximum length).
- A short qualitative comparison of discovered motifs versus common human-designed patterns would further contextualize the results.

Overall evaluation
An excellent and compelling paper. The approach is novel, clearly presented, and empirically strong, and it addresses a central challenge in deep learning. With additional reporting on computational cost, ablations of the critic, sensitivity analyses, and run-to-run variance, the work would be even stronger. I recommend acceptance.
