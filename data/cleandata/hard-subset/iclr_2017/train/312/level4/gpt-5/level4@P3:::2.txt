Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method that uses a recurrent “controller” network to generate variable-length descriptions of neural network architectures, and trains this controller with policy gradient (REINFORCE) to maximize expected validation accuracy of the sampled architectures. The approach is flexible: it handles convolutional architectures (including skip connections, pooling, batch norm, and rectangular filters) and constructs novel recurrent cells via a tree-structured search space that generalizes LSTM. Large-scale asynchronous distributed training enables evaluating thousands of candidate architectures in parallel.

Empirically, NAS discovers:
- A CIFAR-10 convolutional architecture achieving 3.65% test error, competitive with state-of-the-art human-designed models and reported to be 1.05x faster than a comparable DenseNet.
- A new recurrent cell for Penn Treebank (PTB) that surpasses LSTM baselines, reaching 62.4 perplexity. The cell transfers to character-level PTB (1.214 bits-per-character) and yields a small BLEU improvement when dropped into GNMT without additional tuning.
The paper includes helpful design details (reward shaping, skip-connection parameterization, handling of invalid graphs) and control experiments (expanded search spaces, comparison to random search).

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel, well-motivated approach that automates architecture design across domains, with strong empirical results that advance or match state of the art on standard benchmarks.
- Methodologically sound and clearly presented, with careful placement in the literature and ablations that support the central claims.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The work directly targets the costly, expertise-intensive process of manual architecture design. Casting architecture generation as sequence prediction and optimizing non-differentiable validation accuracy via RL is well motivated. The paper contrasts effectively with hyperparameter optimization (fixed-length vectors), neuroevolution (flexibility but lower practicality at scale), and meta-learning/program induction.
- Methodological soundness: The use of REINFORCE with an EMA baseline is appropriate; the controller is auto-regressive, enabling variable-length search. The skip-connection selection via content-based sigmoids is elegant, and the recurrent-cell search space is expressive while still structured.
- Empirical support: Results on CIFAR-10 and PTB are competitive or superior to strong baselines. The control experiment against random search on PTB shows clear gains, and transfer experiments lend credibility to generality. Implementation details (training schedules, distributed setup) are provided at a useful level of detail, and comparisons are generally careful.

Suggestions to improve the paper (not required for acceptance)
- Compute budget and efficiency:
  - Report total wall-clock time and GPU/CPU hours for the searches on CIFAR-10 and PTB, and average time per architecture. This will help readers assess practicality and sample efficiency.
  - Provide learning curves of controller performance versus number of evaluated architectures, and a direct comparison to random search on CIFAR-10 as was done on PTB.
- Fairness and clarity of comparisons:
  - Clarify how “1.05x faster” was measured (training throughput? inference latency? FLOPs), and on what hardware.
  - On CIFAR-10, the final 3.65% model adds 40 filters per layer post-search. Discuss whether such post-hoc widening was allowed for baselines and how it affects fairness. Consider reporting the searched model’s performance without widening.
  - For PTB, ensure parameter counts and compute are matched to baselines when comparing perplexities; include exact parameter counts for your top models.
- Search space and reward shaping:
  - Provide ablations on key design choices: effect of skip-connection mechanism, inclusion of pooling, stride prediction, and reward shaping (e.g., cubing validation accuracy; constant c for perplexity).
  - Discuss robustness when expanding the search space (e.g., adding more combination/activation functions) beyond the one control experiment; how does this affect convergence speed and final quality?
- Overfitting and reproducibility:
  - Detail how the validation set used for rewards is partitioned relative to the final test set to mitigate overfitting of the controller to the validation distribution.
  - Report variance across multiple independent controller runs (best and median performance), and provide seeds.
  - Since code for training discovered architectures is to be released, consider also releasing the search spaces, logs of sampled architectures and rewards, and sufficient configuration to reproduce the search (even if full-scale compute is unavailable to most).
- Methodological refinements:
  - Consider entropy regularization for the controller policy to balance exploration/exploitation.
  - Discuss potential multi-fidelity or early-stopping strategies to improve sample efficiency (e.g., partial training, low-fidelity proxies) and how they might integrate with your asynchronous setup.

Questions for the authors
- Algorithmic details:
  - Please confirm: training uses REINFORCE with an EMA baseline (no learned critic). Did you try a learned value function or other variance-reduction techniques?
  - How sensitive is performance to the reward shaping choices (accuracy^3 on CIFAR-10; c·perplexity^2 on PTB) and to the depth schedule for child networks?
  - What fraction of sampled architectures encountered “compilation failures,” and how do such failures impact the controller’s learning signal?
- Compute and efficiency:
  - What were the total GPU/CPU hours and wall-clock times for the CIFAR-10 and PTB searches? What is the average time per evaluated architecture?
  - How does controller performance progress over time compared to random search on CIFAR-10?
- Comparisons and robustness:
  - How was the 1.05x speedup measured for the CIFAR-10 model relative to DenseNet? Is this training or inference speed, and is parameter/FLOP parity ensured?
  - For the widened CIFAR-10 model (adding 40 filters), what is the performance of the unmodified discovered architecture? Did you also explore narrowing or other simple post-search modifications?
  - On PTB, what is the variability across runs when retraining the discovered cell with different random seeds? Please report mean/std over several runs.
- Generalization:
  - In GNMT, could you provide the exact BLEU scores (case-sensitive, tokenization) and confidence intervals, and confirm compute parity between LSTM and your cell?
  - How does changing the recurrent-cell base number (e.g., 4 vs 8 vs 12) affect search tractability and final performance?

Overall, this is an excellent and impactful paper that advances automated neural architecture design with strong results and a clear presentation. The above clarifications and additions would further strengthen the work and aid reproducibility.
