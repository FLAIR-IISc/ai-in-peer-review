Summary of contributions
The paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method that treats the design of neural networks as a sequence-generation problem. A controller RNN (a two-layer LSTM) autoregressively emits architectural decisions (e.g., layer types and connections), and is trained with policy gradient (REINFORCE with a moving-average baseline) to maximize expected validation accuracy of the sampled “child” networks. The approach supports variable-length architectures, includes mechanisms to propose skip connections and branching, and scales via distributed, asynchronous training. The method is demonstrated on two benchmarks: (1) CIFAR-10, where NAS discovers a convolutional architecture achieving 3.65% test error while being faster than a comparable human-designed model; and (2) Penn Treebank, where NAS discovers a recurrent cell that outperforms LSTM-based baselines in word-level perplexity and transfers well to character-level PTB and a GNMT setup. The paper is clearly written, well positioned in the literature, and provides compelling empirical results.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel and well-motivated formulation of architecture design as policy optimization over a flexible, variable-length search space, with clear advances over fixed-length HPO and neuro-evolution baselines.
- Strong, state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art empirical results on both vision and language, with meaningful analysis and transfer experiments.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Manual architecture design is time-consuming and relies on expert intuition; NAS provides a principled, automated approach that discovers competitive or superior architectures from scratch.
- Methodological soundness: The controller is trained with a standard policy gradient estimator; the use of a baseline, reward shaping, and distributed asynchronous updates is reasonable and clearly described. The search space design (including skip connections and concatenation logic) is thoughtfully engineered.
- Empirical rigor: Results span two distinct domains (CNNs and RNN cells), include comparisons to strong baselines, ablations (e.g., random search control), and transfer tests (character PTB, GNMT). The discovered architectures/cells exhibit nontrivial motifs and achieve notable improvements (e.g., 3.65% CIFAR-10, 62.4 PTB perplexity, 1.214 char-PTB).
- Clarity and positioning: Related work is well covered (HPO, Bayesian optimization, neuro-evolution, meta-learning, sequence-to-sequence, RL for non-differentiable metrics). The paper is accessible and detailed enough to understand design choices.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Report compute and time budgets: number of architectures evaluated, total GPU/CPU days, and per-model training time. Provide a compute-performance plot to illustrate how controller performance improves with more samples.
- Robustness and variance: Include variability across runs (different seeds) for both controller training and final retraining of selected architectures. Clarify whether the controller overfits the validation set used for reward.
- Ablations:
  - Effect of reward shaping (e.g., cubing validation accuracy; the constant used for perplexity) and baseline choice (moving-average parameters).
  - Impact of search-space components (skip connections, layer types, stride/pooling options) on final performance and search efficiency.
  - Sensitivity to the schedule that increases maximum depth.
- Fairness and comparability: Discuss differences relative to top DenseNet variants (e.g., use of 1x1 bottlenecks, parameter counts) and provide FLOPs/latency where possible. Consider a resource-aware reward (multi-objective for accuracy vs speed/parameters).
- Reproducibility: Release search-space specifications, controller hyperparameters, and details of the small grid search applied after NAS (and justify its scope to avoid post-hoc overfitting).
- Analysis: Provide qualitative insights into the discovered cells/architectures (e.g., distribution of operations/activations, recurring motifs) and why they may outperform LSTMs.

Questions for the authors
- Compute budget and efficiency: How many child models were trained per experiment, and what were the total GPU/CPU hours? How does performance scale with the number of samples? Can you share a learning curve of controller reward vs. wall-clock time?
- Reward design: How sensitive are results to the reward transformation (e.g., cubing accuracy, the perplexity constant)? Did you try variance reduction beyond EMA (e.g., advantage normalization)?
- Validation overfitting: Since reward uses a fixed validation set, how do you mitigate overfitting of the controller to that set? Did you use multiple validation splits or cross-validation during search?
- Robustness of discovered models: When retrained from scratch with different random seeds and modest hyperparameter changes, how stable are the CIFAR-10 error and PTB perplexity?
- Search space and constraints: Which parts of the search space contributed most to gains (e.g., skip connections vs filter sizes)? When allowing strides/pooling, performance dropped in one setting—can you elaborate on why and how the controller handled downsampling?
- Baseline and update details: What decay rate was used for the moving-average baseline? Did you experiment with actor-critic style baselines, and if so, how did they compare?
- Post-search tuning: To what extent does the final grid search affect reported SOTA results? Could you report performance of the selected architectures without any post-search tuning for transparency?
- Transfer and generalization: Beyond PTB/char-PTB/GNMT, have you tested the discovered cells/architectures on other datasets or tasks? Any evidence that the controller learned reusable design principles?
- Resource-aware search: Have you tried adding FLOPs/latency/parameters into the reward to directly trade off accuracy vs efficiency during search? If so, what changed in the discovered architectures?
