The paper reports that “[a]fter the controller RNN is done training, we take the best RNN cell according to the lowest validation perplexity and then run a grid search over learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates, and decay epoch. The best cell found was then run with three different configurations and sizes to increase its capacity.” Could you include (or provide here) the exact hyperparameters and the types of dropout used (e.g., recurrent dropout, embedding dropout)? Without these details, replication would require substantial trial and error. As in “Recurrent Neural Network Regularization” (Zaremba et al., 2014), releasing a base set of hyperparameters would greatly support follow-up work. This level of detail would also be desirable for the other experiments, such as the character-level language model.

This paper explores an important area of our field—automating architecture search. While the technique is currently computationally intensive, this trade-off will likely improve as hardware and tooling advance.

The paper covers both vision and text tasks and tackles many benchmark datasets, demonstrating that there are gains to be made by exploring beyond the standard RNN and CNN search spaces. Although one would always like to see the technique applied to additional datasets, the current scope is more than sufficient to show that the approach is not only competitive with human architectural intuition but may even surpass it. This further suggests a practical path to tailoring architectures to specific datasets without extensive hand-engineering at each stage.

Overall, this is a well-written paper on an interesting and timely topic with strong results. I recommend acceptance.
