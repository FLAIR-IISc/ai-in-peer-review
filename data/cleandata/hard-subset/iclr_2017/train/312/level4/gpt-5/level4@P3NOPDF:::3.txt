This paper proposes using a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller trained with reinforcement learning (RL) to generate neural network architectures tailored to specific tasks. The idea is promising, and experiments on two datasets indicate that the approach is solid.

Strengths:
1. The use of an RNN to sequentially emit architecture descriptions, trained end-to-end with RL using validation performance as reward, is interesting and promising.
2. The discovered architectures resemble human-designed networks, suggesting that the method can recover effective design patterns and is compatible with human expertise.
3. The reported results are competitive on two benchmarks, demonstrating that the framework can produce high-quality models.

Weaknesses:
1. Training is computationally expensive and time-consuming, even with substantial resources, which limits accessibility, reproducibility, and practical deployment.
2. The paper offers limited evidence for generality or transfer. It would be valuable to assess whether architectures learned on one dataset perform well on related datasets, especially for sequential models.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide clearer accounting of compute: number of models evaluated, total GPU-hours, wall-clock time, and controller/sample efficiency. Compare to strong baselines (e.g., random search, Bayesian optimization, and established human-designed architectures) under matched compute budgets.
- Evaluate cross-dataset generalization and transfer: train the controller on one dataset and test the selected architecture on related but distinct datasets (for both image and sequence tasks). This would help establish whether the method discovers broadly useful design patterns rather than overfitting to a specific benchmark.
- Report variability across runs and sensitivity to design choices (reward function, search space, exploration schedule). Include ablations that isolate key components of the method.
- Offer qualitative analysis of the learned architectures (e.g., distributions of depth, filter sizes, connections) to illuminate what the controller learns and how it aligns with human priors.
- If possible, release code, search spaces, and trained controllers to facilitate replication and further research.

Overall assessment:
This is a well-motivated and timely paper with an appealing idea and solid initial results. However, the practical cost of the search and the limited evidence for generality make it difficult to judge the method’s advantage over expert-designed models. With additional experiments on transfer/generalization and a clearer discussion of compute efficiency, this work would be significantly strengthened. I lean positive but would like to see more comprehensive empirical validation.
