- Proposes using reinforcement learning with an auto-regressive two-layer LSTM controller to generate variable-length neural network architectures as sequences of tokens.  
- Frames architecture search as an RL problem where sequential choices are actions and validation performance is the reward, using an actor-critic style setup.  
- Evaluates the method on CIFAR-10 and PTB, comparing against strong human-designed baselines; paper is clearly written and details engineering improvements.  

- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reasons for acceptance: novel, well-motivated approach to automated architecture design using a principled RL framework; strong empirical results across two domains that rival or surpass human baselines.  

- Addresses a significant problem by automating time-consuming, expert-dependent architecture design via sequence prediction.  
- Methodology is thorough and clear, explaining action-to-architecture mapping, reward computation, controller training, and necessary engineering (skip connections, distributed/asynchronous training).  
- Situates the work well in the literature, contrasting with hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, seq2seq modeling, and meta-learning, and argues for the benefits of a flexible variable-length representation.  
- Empirical support is convincing: appropriate benchmarks, informative comparisons to human models, and interesting qualitative differences in learned architectures.  
- Presentation is accessible and well written, making the complex approach easy to follow.  

- Request to report computational budget and efficiency: include number of sampled architectures, total GPU/CPU hours, parallelism, and performance vs. wall-clock/compute plots.  
- Suggest analyzing search dynamics: show how sampled architecture quality correlates with training time and how controller improves relative to random search over training.  
- Recommend experiments with human-bootstrapped starting points to see if initializing or constraining by human designs accelerates or improves search.  
- Ask for robustness/variability analysis: include variability across independent controller runs and sensitivity to random seeds.  
- Call for concise ablations quantifying the impact of key components (skip-connection prediction, distributed/asynchronous updates, reward shaping).  

- Questions for authors: report total compute cost for CIFAR-10/PTB searches and validation performance vs. time/compute.  
- How sensitive is controller learning to reward shaping (e.g., cubing accuracy) and baseline choice; was an explicit critic tried?  
- How does expanding or constraining the search space affect convergence speed and final performance?  
- Have you tried initializing the controller from a human-designed architecture or constraining policy near it to refine human baselines?  
- What is the variance across independent controller runs and consistency of best-found architectures/test performance?  

- Overall assessment: excellent, timely paper with a novel, well-motivated method, strong empirical results, clear writing, and solid placement in the literature; recommend acceptance.
