- Proposal: Use an RNN controller trained with reinforcement learning to sequentially generate neural network architectures tailored to tasks; experiments on two datasets show promise.

- Strength: Training the RNN to emit architecture descriptions end-to-end with RL using validation reward is an interesting and promising approach.

- Strength: Discovered architectures resemble human-designed networks, indicating the method can recover effective design patterns.

- Strength: Results are competitive on two benchmarks, demonstrating the framework can produce high-quality models.

- Weakness: Training is computationally expensive and time-consuming, limiting accessibility, reproducibility, and practical deployment.

- Weakness: Limited evidence for generality or transfer; unclear if architectures learned on one dataset work well on related datasets, especially for sequential models.

- Suggestion: Provide clear compute accounting (# models evaluated, GPU-hours, wall-clock time, controller/sample efficiency) and comparisons to strong baselines under matched compute budgets.

- Suggestion: Evaluate cross-dataset generalization and transfer by training on one dataset and testing on related but distinct datasets for image and sequence tasks.

- Suggestion: Report variability across runs and sensitivity to design choices (reward, search space, exploration schedule) and include ablation studies isolating key components.

- Suggestion: Offer qualitative analysis of learned architectures (distributions of depth, filter sizes, connections) to clarify what the controller learns and alignment with human priors.

- Suggestion: Release code, search spaces, and trained controllers to facilitate replication and further research.

- Overall: Well-motivated and timely with solid initial results, but high search cost and limited generality evidence mean more comprehensive empirical validation is needed; reviewer leans positive.
