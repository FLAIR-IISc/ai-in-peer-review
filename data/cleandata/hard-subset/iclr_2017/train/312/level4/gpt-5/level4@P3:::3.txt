Summary and contributions
This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a reinforcement learning framework in which a controller RNN sequentially generates variable-length descriptions of neural network architectures. The controller is trained with REINFORCE to maximize expected validation performance of “child” networks. The search space supports modern design elements such as skip connections and multiple layer types via set-selection attention, and extends to recurrent cells by labeling nodes in a computation tree that transforms (xt, ht−1, ct−1) into (ht, ct). Large-scale, asynchronous distributed training is used to evaluate many candidate architectures in parallel.

Empirically, NAS discovers high-performing models from scratch across modalities:
- CIFAR-10: a 39-layer CNN reaches 3.65% test error, outperforming a comparable DenseNet variant and being 1.05x faster. Simpler settings (no learned strides/pooling) still achieve competitive error (5.50%) with relatively shallow architectures.
- Penn Treebank (PTB): a learned RNN cell attains 62.4 perplexity, ~3.6 better than prior state of the art. The cell transfers to character-level PTB (1.214 perplexity, SOTA) and improves GNMT by +0.5 BLEU without tuning.
- Controls show improvements over random search and robustness when expanding the search space.

Decision: Accept
Reasons:
1) Novel, general, and well-motivated approach to architecture design that moves beyond fixed-length hyperparameter search and hand-crafted templates.
2) Strong empirical results across vision and language, including state-of-the-art or near-SOTA performance, with appropriate controls (random search baseline) and clear methodological details.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles automatic architecture design, a central and timely problem as model performance hinges on structural choices. Prior Bayesian optimization typically targets fixed-length hyperparameters and often benefits from strong seeds; neuro-evolution methods are flexible but less scalable. NAS is well positioned in this literature by enabling variable-length, richly structured architectures via a learnable policy.
- Methodological soundness: The controller RNN, reward design, and policy-gradient training are clearly specified. Extensions to skip connections and recurrent cells are well described, including handling of invalid graphs. The distributed/asynchronous implementation is appropriate for the heavy compute demands and is explained sufficiently to reproduce the approach in principle.
- Empirical support: Results on CIFAR-10 and PTB are competitive to state of the art, sometimes surpassing it, with sensible ablations and controls (e.g., random search). The discovered RNN cell’s transfer to character modeling and GNMT adds credibility to the claim of generality beyond a single benchmark. Visualizations and qualitative analysis of the learned cell lend interpretability and align with known good practices (e.g., repeated affine transforms of ht−1 and xt).
- Rigor: Comparisons generally control for training protocols and use held-out validation for reward and an unseen test set for final reporting. The paper discloses key training settings, reward shaping, and search schedules.

That said, there are two notable limitations:
- Computational cost is extremely high (e.g., 800 GPUs for CIFAR-10, 400 CPUs for PTB), which may hinder adoption and complicate reproducibility.
- Demonstrations of transfer are stronger for the RNN cell than for CNN architectures. Additional cross-dataset tests would better evidence generality of the discovered CNNs.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all decision-critical)
- Compute budget and efficiency: Report total wall-clock time, GPU/CPU-hours, and energy estimates per experiment. Include the number of architectures evaluated to achieve the reported results (you cite 12,800 for CIFAR-10 and ~15,000 for PTB); plotting best/mean reward versus compute would contextualize cost-benefit.
- Variance and robustness: Provide results across multiple controller seeds to quantify variability in the discovered architectures and their final performance. Include confidence intervals for key numbers (e.g., CIFAR-10 error, PTB perplexity).
- Validation overfitting: Discuss safeguards against overfitting the controller to the validation set used as reward. For example, show correlation between validation reward during search and final test performance for top-k architectures, or use a “fresh” validation set for selection after search.
- Fairness of CIFAR-10 comparisons: Clarify parameter counts, FLOPs, and exact training/augmentation protocols versus the compared DenseNet variants (noting differences such as 1x1 bottlenecks). If feasible, include a matched-parameter/matched-FLOP comparison.
- Search space sensitivity: Analyze how results change with different action spaces (e.g., allowed filter sizes, stride choices, presence/absence of pooling, alternative normalization layers). This would help readers understand which search-space choices are most impactful.
- Reward shaping and baseline: Justify the choice of cubing the validation accuracy and the EMA baseline; include a brief sensitivity study (e.g., linear vs cubic rewards, advantage normalization), and whether this affected stability/sample efficiency.
- Invalid architectures: You currently default to using the image as input when no incoming edges exist and pad for size mismatches. Consider penalizing or excluding such cases to avoid biasing the search toward degenerate fixes.
- Transfer of CNNs: If resources permit, test the top CIFAR-10 architecture on CIFAR-100 or SVHN to strengthen claims of generality on vision tasks.
- Reproducibility: Since search is costly, releasing trained controller checkpoints, top-k discovered architectures, and scripts to train them would be highly valuable alongside the promised code.

Questions for the authors
- Compute and sample efficiency: What are the total GPU/CPU-hours for the CIFAR-10 and PTB searches? How many architectures did you train to obtain the reported best models, and what fraction were invalid or pruned?
- Variability: How consistent are results across independent controller runs? What is the variance in the best-found performance and in the distribution of top-k architectures?
- Reward and curriculum: How do reward normalization and the schedule of increasing child depth affect stability and final performance? Did you normalize rewards across depths to keep scales comparable?
- Baselines: Beyond random search, did you attempt Bayesian optimization or SMBO methods that can handle variable-length structures (e.g., TPE with a grammar)? Any results to report?
- CIFAR-10 comparison details: Can you provide matched parameter/FLOP counts versus the 3.74% DenseNet and clarify training-time differences (e.g., number of epochs, batch sizes)? Is 3.65% achieved without test-time ensembling?
- PTB regularization: For the learned cell, which regularization settings (embedding/recurrent dropout, shared embeddings) were used in the final results vs baselines? Were hyperparameters tuned equally for baselines and NAS cells?
- GNMT transfer: Besides replacing the cell, were any hyperparameters retuned (e.g., dropout, layer sizes)? How sensitive is the +0.5 BLEU gain to such choices?

Overall, this is an impactful and well-executed paper that advances automated architecture design and delivers strong empirical results. I recommend acceptance.
