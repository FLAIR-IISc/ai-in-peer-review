The paper reports that after the controller RNN is done training, the authors select the best RNN cell based on the lowest validation perplexity and then perform a grid search over various hyperparameters, including learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates, and decay epoch. The best cell found is then evaluated with three different configurations and sizes to increase its capacity.

For the sake of reproducibility, it would be beneficial to include the specific hyperparameters and type of dropout (e.g., recurrent dropout, embedding dropout) used in the experiments. Without this information, replication would require significant trial and error. As demonstrated in "Recurrent Neural Network Regularization" (Zaremba et al., 2014), releasing a base set of hyperparameters can greatly facilitate future work in the field. This information would also be valuable for the other experiments, such as the character language model.

This paper explores an important aspect of the field, namely automating architecture search. Although the technique is currently computationally intensive, this trade-off is likely to improve in the near future as technology advances.

The paper covers both standard vision and text tasks, tackling numerous benchmark datasets and demonstrating potential gains from exploring beyond the standard RNN and CNN search space. While it would be desirable to see the technique applied to more datasets, the current results are already sufficient to show that the method is not only competitive with human architectural intuition but may even surpass it. This approach also suggests a way to tailor architectures to specific datasets without resorting to hand engineering at each stage.

Overall, this is a well-written paper on an interesting topic with strong results. I recommend its acceptance.
