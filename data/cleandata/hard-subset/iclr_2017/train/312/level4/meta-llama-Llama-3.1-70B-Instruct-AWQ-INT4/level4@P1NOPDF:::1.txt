The paper reports that after the controller RNN is done training, the authors select the best RNN cell based on the lowest validation perplexity and then perform a grid search over learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates, and decay epoch. They then run the best cell found with three different configurations and sizes to increase its capacity.

To facilitate replication, it would be beneficial to include the hyperparameters and specify the type of dropout used (e.g., recurrent dropout, embedding dropout). Without this information, reproducing the results would require a significant amount of trial and error. As demonstrated in "Recurrent Neural Network Regularization" (Zaremba et al., 2014), releasing a base set of hyperparameters can greatly assist future work in the field. This information would also be valuable for the other experiments, such as the character language model.

This paper explores an important aspect of the field, namely automating architecture search. Although the technique is currently computationally intensive, this trade-off is likely to improve as technology advances. The paper covers both standard vision and text tasks, tackling many benchmark datasets, and demonstrates that exploring beyond the standard RNN and CNN search space can lead to gains. While it would be desirable to see the technique applied to more datasets, the current results are already sufficient to show that the technique is not only competitive with human architectural intuition but may even surpass it. This approach also suggests a way to tailor the architecture to specific datasets without requiring manual engineering at each stage.

Overall, this is a well-written paper on an interesting topic with strong results. I recommend its acceptance.
