The paper reports that "[a]fter the controller RNN is done training, we take the best RNN cell according to the lowest validation perplexity and then run a grid search over learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates and decay epoch. The best cell found was then run with three different configurations and sizes to increase its capacity." However, it would be beneficial to provide the specific hyperparameters and type of dropout used (e.g., recurrent dropout, embedding dropout) for replication purposes. As demonstrated in "Recurrent Neural Network Regularization" (Zaremba et al., 2014), releasing a base set of hyperparameters can greatly facilitate future work in the field. This level of detail would also be desirable for the other experiments, such as the character-level language model.

This paper explores an important aspect of the field, namely automating architecture search. Although the technique is currently computationally intensive, it is likely that this trade-off will become more favorable in the near future as technology advances.

The paper covers both standard vision and text tasks and tackles multiple benchmark datasets, demonstrating that there are gains to be made by exploring beyond the standard RNN and CNN search space. While it would be desirable to see the technique applied to more datasets, the current results are already sufficient to show that the technique is not only competitive with human architectural intuition but may even surpass it. This approach also suggests a way to tailor architectures to specific datasets without requiring hand engineering at each stage.

Overall, this is a well-written paper on an interesting topic with strong results. I recommend its acceptance.
