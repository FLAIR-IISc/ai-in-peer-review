This paper explores an important part of our field, that of automating architecture search. While the technique is currently computationally intensive, this trade-off will likely become better in the near future as technology continues to improve.

The authors present a novel method, Neural Architecture Search, which utilizes a recurrent neural network to generate model descriptions of neural networks and trains this RNN with reinforcement learning to maximize the expected accuracy of the generated architectures on a validation set. The method is applied to two challenging tasks: image classification on CIFAR-10 and language modeling on Penn Treebank.

The paper covers both standard vision and text tasks and tackles many benchmark datasets, showing there are gains to be made by exploring beyond the standard RNN and CNN search space. The results are impressive, with the method designing novel architectures that rival or even surpass the best human-invented architectures. Specifically, on CIFAR-10, the method achieves a test error rate of 3.65%, which is 0.09% better and 1.05x faster than the previous state-of-the-art model. On Penn Treebank, the method designs a novel recurrent cell that outperforms the widely-used LSTM cell and other state-of-the-art baselines, achieving a test set perplexity of 62.4.

The authors also provide a thorough analysis of their method, including a discussion of the search space, training details, and the use of skip connections and other layer types to increase architecture complexity. Additionally, they provide results on transfer learning, showing that the designed architectures can generalize to other tasks.

One minor suggestion for improvement is to include more details on the hyperparameters and type of dropout used in the experiments, particularly in the section on Penn Treebank. This would facilitate replication and further research in this area.

Overall, this is a well-written paper on an interesting topic with strong results. I recommend it be accepted.

Decision: Accept

Reasons for decision:

1. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to automating architecture search, which is an important problem in the field.
2. The method is thoroughly evaluated on multiple benchmark datasets, and the results show significant gains over state-of-the-art models.

Additional feedback:

* Consider including more details on the hyperparameters and type of dropout used in the experiments, particularly in the section on Penn Treebank.
* It would be interesting to see the method applied to more datasets and tasks to further demonstrate its generalizability.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational resources required to run the method, as well as potential ways to reduce the computational cost.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the hyperparameters and type of dropout used in the experiments, particularly in the section on Penn Treebank?
* How do you plan to address the computational intensity of the method, and are there any potential ways to reduce the computational cost?
* Have you considered applying the method to other tasks or datasets, and if so, what were the results?
