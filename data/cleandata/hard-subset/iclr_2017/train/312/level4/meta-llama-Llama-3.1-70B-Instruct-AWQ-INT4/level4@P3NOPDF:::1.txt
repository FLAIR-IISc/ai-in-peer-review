The paper presents a novel approach to automating architecture search, exploring an important aspect of the field. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their technique by applying it to standard vision and text tasks, tackling multiple benchmark datasets. The results show that there are indeed gains to be made by exploring beyond the standard RNN and CNN search space.

One of the key contributions of the paper is the use of a controller RNN to search for optimal architectures. The authors report that after training the controller RNN, they select the best RNN cell based on the lowest validation perplexity and then perform a grid search over hyperparameters such as learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates, and decay epoch. However, to facilitate replication and future work in the field, it would be beneficial to include the specific hyperparameters and type of dropout (e.g., recurrent dropout, embedding dropout) used in the experiments. Providing a base set of hyperparameters, as done in "Recurrent Neural Network Regularization" (Zaremba et al., 2014), would greatly assist in this regard.

This level of detail would also be desirable for the other experiments presented in the paper, such as the character language model. By providing a comprehensive description of the hyperparameters and experimental setup, the authors can ensure that their results are reproducible and can be built upon by future researchers.

The paper is well-written, and the topic is of great interest to the field. The authors' approach to automating architecture search has the potential to significantly impact the way we design neural networks. While the technique is currently computationally intensive, it is likely that the trade-off will become more favorable in the near future as technology continues to improve.

The results presented in the paper are strong, demonstrating that the proposed technique can not only compete with human architectural intuition but may even surpass it. This suggests that the approach can be used to tailor architectures to specific datasets without requiring hand engineering at each stage. While it would be desirable to see the technique applied to more datasets, the current results are already sufficient to demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.

Overall, I recommend accepting the paper. It presents a novel and impactful contribution to the field, and the results are well-supported by the experiments. With some minor revisions to include additional details on the hyperparameters and experimental setup, the paper has the potential to make a significant impact on the field.
