This paper proposes a novel approach to designing neural network architectures using reinforcement learning (RL) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs). The idea is intriguing and shows promise, with solid experimental results on two datasets.

The paper's main contribution is the use of an RNN as a controller to generate network architectures, which are then trained using RL to maximize their expected accuracy. This approach allows for the exploration of a vast and variable-length architecture space, making it a significant advancement in the field of neural architecture search.

The experimental results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The generated architectures are competitive with state-of-the-art models, and in some cases, even outperform them. The fact that the generated architectures resemble those designed by humans suggests that the method is capable of capturing domain-specific knowledge.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The training time of the network is substantial, even with significant computational resources. This could be a barrier to widespread adoption, especially for researchers or practitioners with limited access to resources.

Furthermore, the experiments do not provide a comprehensive evaluation of the generality of the generated architectures. It would be beneficial to see the performance of the generated architectures on other similar but distinct datasets, particularly for sequential models. This would help to establish the method's robustness and versatility.

In conclusion, this is a well-written and interesting paper that presents a novel approach to neural architecture search. While it has some limitations, the results are promising, and with further experimentation and evaluation, this method could become a valuable tool for the machine learning community.

Decision: Accept

Reasons for decision:

1. The paper presents a novel and intriguing approach to neural architecture search, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2. The experimental results on two datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

Additional feedback:

* To improve the paper, the authors could provide more details on the training process, such as the number of iterations, the reward function used, and the exploration strategy employed.
* The authors could also provide more analysis on the generated architectures, such as the number of parameters, the computational complexity, and the robustness to hyperparameter changes.
* To address the limitation of training time, the authors could explore ways to speed up the training process, such as using more efficient algorithms or parallelizing the training process.
* To evaluate the generality of the generated architectures, the authors could conduct experiments on other datasets, such as ImageNet, or on different tasks, such as natural language processing.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the reward function used in the RL algorithm? How did you choose the reward function, and what were the trade-offs involved?
* How did you select the hyperparameters for the RNN controller, and what was the impact of different hyperparameters on the generated architectures?
* Can you provide more analysis on the computational complexity of the generated architectures? How do they compare to state-of-the-art models in terms of computational resources required?
* Have you explored other applications of the proposed method, such as natural language processing or speech recognition? If so, what were the results, and what challenges did you encounter?
