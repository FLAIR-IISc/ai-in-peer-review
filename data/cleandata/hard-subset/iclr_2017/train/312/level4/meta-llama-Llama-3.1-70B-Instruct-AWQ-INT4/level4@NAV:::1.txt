The authors describe their post-training procedure for the controller RNN, where they select the optimal RNN cell based on the lowest validation perplexity. Subsequently, they perform a grid search over several hyperparameters, including learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates, and decay epoch. The top-performing cell is then evaluated under three distinct configurations and sizes to assess its capacity.

To facilitate replication and future research, it would be beneficial to provide specific details on the hyperparameters and dropout types (e.g., recurrent dropout, embedding dropout) used in the experiments. Following the example of "Recurrent Neural Network Regularization" (Zaremba et al., 2014), releasing a baseline set of hyperparameters would greatly aid in advancing the field.

This level of detail will likely be desirable for other experiments as well, such as the character language model.

The paper tackles a crucial aspect of our field, namely automating architecture search. Although the current technique is computationally intensive, this trade-off is expected to improve as technology advances.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a range of standard vision and text tasks, as well as multiple benchmark datasets, highlighting the potential gains of exploring beyond traditional RNN and CNN search spaces. While it would be desirable to see the technique applied to even more datasets, the current results are already sufficient to demonstrate the competitiveness and potential superiority of the proposed method over human intuition. This approach also suggests a promising direction for tailoring architectures to specific datasets without requiring manual engineering at each stage.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that presents strong results on an engaging topic. I strongly recommend its acceptance.
