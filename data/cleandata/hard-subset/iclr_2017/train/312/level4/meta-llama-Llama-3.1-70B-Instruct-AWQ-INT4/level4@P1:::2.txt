This paper presents a novel approach to searching for optimal neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network as a controller and reinforcement learning. The method treats deep neural networks (DNNs) as variable-length sequences and uses reinforcement learning to find the target architecture. The node selection process is framed as an action in the reinforcement learning context, and the evaluation error of the resulting architecture corresponds to the reward signal. An auto-regressive two-layer LSTM is employed as both the controller and critic.

The proposed method is evaluated on two distinct problems: image classification with CIFAR-10 and language modeling with Penn Treebank. In both cases, the method is compared to several human-created architectures, demonstrating its effectiveness in discovering competitive models.

This is a very exciting paper, as hand-selecting architectures can be challenging, and it is often difficult to determine how far from optimal the hand-designed networks are. The authors do an excellent job of describing the method in detail, including the necessary improvements. The tested data represents the capability of the method well, and it is interesting to see the differences between the generated architectures and those designed by humans. The paper is written clearly and is very accessible. The coverage and contrast with related literature are also well done.

To further strengthen the paper, it would be interesting to see data on the time required for training and the correlation between the time/resources needed to train and the quality of the model. Additionally, exploring how human-bootstrapped models perform and incorporating them into the framework could provide valuable insights.

Overall, this is an excellent and interesting paper that presents a promising approach to neural architecture search.
