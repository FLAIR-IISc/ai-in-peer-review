The paper reports that "[a]fter the controller RNN is done training, we take the best RNN cell according to the lowest validation perplexity and then run a grid search over learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates and decay epoch. The best cell found was then run with three different configurations and sizes to increase its capacity." However, to facilitate replication, it would be beneficial to provide the hyperparameters and specify the type of dropout used (e.g., recurrent dropout, embedding dropout, etc.). Without this information, replication would require a substantial amount of trial and error. As demonstrated in "Recurrent Neural Network Regularization" (Zaremba et al., 2014), releasing a base set of hyperparameters can greatly assist future work in the field. This level of detail would also be desirable for the other experiments, such as the character-level language model.

This paper explores an important aspect of our field, namely automating architecture search. Although the technique is currently computationally intensive, this trade-off is likely to improve as technology advances in the near future. The paper covers both standard vision and text tasks, tackling numerous benchmark datasets and demonstrating that there are gains to be made by exploring beyond the standard RNN and CNN search space. While it would be desirable to see the technique applied to more datasets, the current evaluation is already sufficient to show that the technique is not only competitive with human architectural intuition but may even surpass it. This also suggests an approach to tailor the architecture to specific datasets without resorting to hand engineering at each stage.

This is a well-written paper on an interesting topic with strong results. I recommend it be accepted.
