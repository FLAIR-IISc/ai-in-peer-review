This paper proposes a novel approach to designing network architectures for specific tasks using reinforcement learning (RL) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs). The core idea is promising, and the experimental results on two datasets demonstrate the method's efficacy.

The paper has several strengths. Firstly, the concept of leveraging RNNs to generate network descriptions and training them using RL is innovative and shows promise. Secondly, the generated architectures exhibit similarities to those designed by humans, indicating that the expertise of human designers and the generated architectures are compatible.

However, there are also some limitations. One major drawback is the prolonged training time required for the network, even with substantial computational resources. Furthermore, the experiments do not provide sufficient evidence of the generality of the generated architectures. It would be beneficial to evaluate the performance of the generated architectures on other similar yet distinct datasets, particularly for sequential models.

Overall, this is a well-written paper with a compelling idea. Nevertheless, additional experiments are necessary to fully demonstrate its potential advantages over human-designed models.
