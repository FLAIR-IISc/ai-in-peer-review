This paper introduces a novel approach to neural network architecture design, leveraging Reinforcement Learning (RL) and Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs). The core idea – using an RNN to generate network architecture descriptions and then training this RNN via RL – is both interesting and promising. The experimental results presented on two datasets demonstrate the viability of the proposed method and suggest a solid foundation for future work.

The primary strengths of this paper lie in its innovative concept and the encouraging initial results. The use of an RNN to parameterize the architecture search space is a compelling approach, offering a flexible and potentially powerful way to explore diverse network structures. Furthermore, the observation that the architectures generated by the system exhibit similarities to those designed by human experts is significant. This suggests that the learned representations capture elements of established architectural design principles, and indicates a potential for compatibility between automated design and human intuition.

However, the paper also has several areas that could be strengthened. A key limitation is the substantial computational cost associated with training the RL-driven RNN. The authors acknowledge this, but the long training times, even with significant computing resources, represent a practical barrier to wider adoption and further experimentation. A discussion of potential strategies for mitigating this cost – such as transfer learning, more efficient RL algorithms, or architectural constraints – would be valuable.

A more significant concern is the limited assessment of the generality of the generated architectures. While the results on the two chosen datasets are positive, they do not conclusively demonstrate the method’s ability to generalize to unseen, yet related, tasks. It would be considerably more impactful to see performance evaluations on additional datasets, particularly those with similar characteristics to the ones used in the paper, but with sufficient differences to truly test the adaptability of the generated sequential models. This would help establish whether the learned architectures capture fundamental principles applicable beyond the specific training scenarios. Demonstrating transferability would significantly bolster the claim of a generally useful architecture search method. 

Further analysis comparing the performance of the automatically designed architectures against strong, hand-crafted baselines *specifically tuned* for each dataset would also strengthen the evaluation. While the paper presents comparative results, clarifying the level of optimization applied to the baseline architectures would provide a clearer picture of the proposed method’s relative advantage.

In summary, this is a well-motivated and thoughtfully executed paper presenting a novel approach to neural architecture search. The idea is promising, and the initial results are encouraging. However, the high computational cost and limited evaluation of generalization currently limit the impact of the work. Addressing these concerns through further experimentation – particularly focusing on transfer learning to new datasets and more rigorous benchmarking against optimized baselines – would significantly strengthen the paper and highlight its potential to advance the field of automated machine learning. I believe this paper has merit and, with further development, could make a significant contribution.
