This paper explores an important and increasingly relevant area of machine learning: automated architecture search. The authors demonstrate a novel approach to neural architecture search, achieving competitive and, in some cases, superior results to hand-designed architectures across a variety of challenging benchmark datasets in both computer vision and natural language processing. While the technique is currently computationally intensive, the potential benefits – and the likely future improvements in computational efficiency – make this a valuable contribution.

The core of the method leverages a controller RNN to generate architectures, which are then evaluated. The paper clearly explains the overall process and presents compelling results on datasets including CIFAR-10, ImageNet, and several text benchmarks. The broad applicability demonstrated – moving beyond standard RNN and CNN search spaces – is particularly noteworthy. The results suggest that this approach can effectively tailor architectures to specific datasets, potentially reducing the need for extensive hand-engineering. 

One minor point requires clarification to facilitate reproducibility. The paper states, "[a]fter the controller RNN is done training, we take the best RNN cell according to the lowest validation perplexity and then run a grid search over learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates and decay epoch. The best cell found was then run with three different configurations and sizes to increase its capacity." Providing a detailed list of the hyperparameters explored during this grid search—including the specific range of learning rates, the types of weight initialization used (e.g., Xavier, He), the specific dropout rates tested *and the type of dropout applied* (e.g., recurrent dropout, embedding dropout, variational dropout)—would greatly aid future research and enable more straightforward replication of these results. Following the example set by influential work like Zaremba et al. (2014) (“Recurrent Neural Network Regularization”), releasing a base set of hyperparameters would be highly beneficial to the community. This request extends to the other experiments detailed in the paper, such as the character language model.

Despite this request for further detail, the paper is exceptionally well-written and presents a clear, logical argument. The experimental setup is thorough, and the results are convincingly presented. The authors acknowledge the computational cost, which is a valid limitation given the current state of the field. However, the potential for algorithmic improvements and advances in hardware suggest this limitation will become less significant over time. 

Overall, this is a strong paper addressing an important research area with promising results. I recommend acceptance to ICLR 2017.
