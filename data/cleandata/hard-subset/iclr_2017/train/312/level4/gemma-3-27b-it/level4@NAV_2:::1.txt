The paper reports that "[a]fter the controller RNN is done training, we take the best RNN cell according to the lowest validation perplexity and then run a grid search over learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates and decay epoch. The best cell found was then run with three different configurations and sizes to increase its capacity."
It would be valuable to include (or provide separately) the specific hyperparameters and dropout types (e.g., recurrent dropout, embedding dropout) used during this grid search and final training. Providing this information would greatly facilitate replication and benefit future research in the field, aligning with best practices exemplified by works like "Recurrent Neural Network Regularization" (Zaremba et al., 2014) which often release baseline hyperparameters. The same applies to the other experimental setups, such as the character language modeling task.

This paper explores a crucial area within our field – automating neural architecture search. Although currently computationally demanding, this trade-off is likely to improve with ongoing technological advancements. The research demonstrates promising results across standard vision and text tasks, tackling multiple benchmark datasets and revealing potential gains beyond conventional RNN and CNN design spaces. While application to a wider range of datasets would be beneficial, the current results sufficiently establish the technique’s competitiveness, even surpassing human-designed architectures, and suggest a pathway for dataset-specific architecture tailoring without extensive manual engineering.

This is a well-written paper addressing an interesting topic with strong empirical results. I recommend acceptance.
