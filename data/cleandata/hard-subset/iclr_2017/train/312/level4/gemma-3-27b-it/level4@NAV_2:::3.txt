This paper proposes a novel approach to neural architecture search, utilizing a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller trained with reinforcement learning (RL) to generate network architectures for specific tasks. The core idea is promising, and the experimental results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The results are strong, achieving state-of-the-art performance on multiple benchmarks, and the approach represents a significant step forward in automating neural network design. The demonstrated ability to discover architectures competitive with, and in some cases surpassing, human-designed networks is particularly noteworthy.

The paper’s strengths lie in its innovative application of RNNs and RL to the architecture search problem. The method successfully generates architectures that align with established principles in network design, suggesting the learned search process effectively captures relevant architectural patterns. The empirical validation on both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank) provides compelling evidence of the method’s versatility. Specifically, the reported 3.65% test error on CIFAR-10 and 62.4 perplexity on Penn Treebank represent substantial improvements over prior work. Furthermore, the transfer learning results to the character language modeling task demonstrate the generalization capability of the learned recurrent cell. 

To further strengthen the paper, I suggest the authors consider the following points: 
*   The computational cost of training is substantial, even with significant computational resources. A more detailed analysis of the resource requirements and potential strategies for reducing training time would be valuable.
*   Exploring the generality of the discovered architectures would be beneficial. Evaluating performance on related datasets beyond CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank could provide further insight into the robustness and transferability of the learned architectures, particularly for the novel recurrent cells.
*   Ablation studies exploring the contribution of specific components, such as skip connections or the choice of activation functions, would help to better understand the underlying mechanisms driving the performance gains.

I have several questions for the authors:
*   The paper mentions a schedule for increasing the number of layers during training. Could you provide more details on the specifics of this schedule and the rationale behind it?
*   How sensitive is the method to the choice of reward function? Have you experimented with different reward formulations, and if so, what were the results?
*   The baseline function used to reduce the variance of the policy gradient is described as an exponential moving average of previous accuracies. How was the smoothing factor for this average determined?
*   Regarding the transfer learning experiments, what specific modifications, if any, were made to the architecture or training procedure when applying the learned cell to the character language modeling task?
