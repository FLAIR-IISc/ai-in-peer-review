This paper proposes using reinforcement learning (RL) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to automatically design network architectures for specific tasks. The core idea is promising, and the experimental results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank demonstrate the method’s effectiveness.

The strengths of the paper are:
1. The approach of using an RNN to generate network descriptions and then employing RL to train the controller is innovative and holds significant potential.
2. The generated architectures are comparable to, and in some cases surpass, human-designed architectures, suggesting the learned architectures capture aspects of human expertise.

The weaknesses of the paper are:
1. The training process is computationally expensive and time-consuming, even with substantial computing resources.
2. The experiments lack a thorough assessment of the generalization ability of the generated architectures. It would be valuable to see performance on datasets similar but distinct from CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, particularly for the generated recurrent models.

Overall, I recommend **accepting** this paper. While the computational cost is a concern, the novelty of the approach and the strong empirical results on challenging benchmarks justify acceptance. The demonstrated ability to automatically design competitive architectures is a significant contribution to the field.

Here are some supporting arguments for my decision:

The paper tackles a fundamental challenge in deep learning: the laborious and often ad-hoc process of neural network architecture design. The proposed method offers an automated solution, potentially democratizing access to high-performing models. The use of an RNN as a controller is clever, allowing for the generation of variable-length architectural descriptions. The integration of RL provides a principled framework for optimizing the controller based on the performance of the generated networks.

The results on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are compelling. Achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance on these datasets demonstrates the efficacy of the proposed approach. The reported speedup on CIFAR-10 is also noteworthy, indicating that the generated architectures are not only accurate but also potentially more efficient. The successful transfer of the learned recurrent cell to the character language modeling task further strengthens the findings.

However, to improve the paper, I offer the following suggestions:

*   **Computational Cost:** Discuss potential strategies for reducing the computational cost of training. Could techniques like transfer learning, pre-training the controller, or more efficient exploration strategies be employed?
*   **Generalization:** Expanding the experimental evaluation to include additional datasets would strengthen the claims of generality. Specifically, testing the Penn Treebank-derived cell on other language modeling tasks would be insightful.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Provide more detailed ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components of the method, such as skip connections and the baseline function in the REINFORCE algorithm.
*   **Search Space:** Further clarification on the rationale for the chosen search space would be helpful. Were there any limitations or assumptions made in defining the search space?

I have a few questions for the authors that could help clarify my understanding:

1.  The paper mentions that "the validation accuracy of the last 5 epochs" is cubed for the reward signal. What is the reasoning behind cubing the accuracy? Does this have a significant impact on performance, and has it been tuned?
2.  Regarding the parallel training scheme, how sensitive is the performance to the number of controller replicas (K) and child replicas (m)? Are there diminishing returns or stability issues with increasing these values?
3.  The paper mentions "compilation failures" when using skip connections. Could you elaborate on the frequency of these failures and the effectiveness of the implemented workarounds?
4.  The results on the character-level language modeling task are impressive. What modifications, if any, were made to the generated recurrent cell before applying it to this task?
