The paper details a methodology for automated architecture search, focusing on refining a controller RNN post-training. Specifically, the authors select the best-performing RNN cell (based on lowest validation perplexity) and subsequently optimize hyperparameters via a grid search, varying learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates, and decay epoch. The resulting optimal cell is then evaluated across multiple configurations and sizes to explore capacity scaling. 

To facilitate reproducibility, a more detailed reporting of the hyperparameters employed during this grid search is crucial. Knowing the specific values tested for each parameter – and crucially, the *type* of dropout used (e.g., recurrent dropout, embedding dropout, variational dropout) – would significantly reduce the effort required for independent replication. Following the precedent set by impactful works like Zaremba et al. (2014), providing a baseline set of hyperparameters would be a valuable contribution to the community. This level of detail would also be beneficial when reviewing the other reported experiments, such as the character language model.

The work addresses an important problem – automating neural architecture search – and while currently computationally demanding, this is a reasonable trade-off given the potential for future improvements with advancements in hardware and algorithms. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on both standard vision and text tasks, achieving gains across numerous benchmark datasets by expanding beyond traditional RNN and CNN architectures. While application to an even broader range of datasets would be welcomed, the current experimental scope adequately demonstrates the technique's competitiveness, and even superiority, to manually designed architectures. This suggests a promising path towards dataset-specific architectural tailoring, reducing the need for extensive hand-engineering.

Overall, this is a well-written and compelling paper presenting strong results on a significant topic. I recommend acceptance.
