This paper explores an important area of our field: automating architecture search. While currently computationally intensive, this trade-off is likely to improve with continued technological advancements.

The paper demonstrates strong results across both standard vision and text tasks, tackling multiple benchmark datasets and showing gains beyond conventional RNN and CNN search spaces. While application to more datasets would always be valuable, the current work sufficiently demonstrates that this technique is not only competitive with human architectural intuition but may even surpass it. This suggests a path toward tailoring architectures to specific datasets without requiring extensive hand-engineering.

This is a well-written paper on an interesting topic with strong results. I recommend acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper successfully addresses the problem of neural architecture design by framing it as a reinforcement learning task. The core idea of using a recurrent network controller to generate architectures is novel and effective, as demonstrated by the competitive results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank. Achieving state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art performance on these benchmarks, *starting from scratch*, is a significant accomplishment. The reported improvements (e.g., 3.65% error on CIFAR-10, 62.4 perplexity on PTB) are noteworthy and suggest a promising direction for future research. The method’s ability to discover architectures competitive with, and in some cases exceeding, human-designed networks is particularly compelling. The inclusion of skip connections and the ability to generate recurrent cell architectures further demonstrate the flexibility of the approach.

**Additional Feedback:**

To facilitate reproducibility and further research, could the authors please include a more detailed description of the hyperparameters used for training in each experiment, particularly the specific type(s) of dropout employed (e.g., recurrent dropout, embedding dropout)? Releasing a base set of hyperparameters, similar to the practice in "Recurrent Neural Network Regularization" (Zaremba et al., 2014), would be invaluable to the research community. I’d also appreciate any further details regarding the grid search process – specifically, the ranges explored for each hyperparameter.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide the specific hyperparameters (learning rate, weight decay, dropout rates, etc.) used in each experiment, including the type of dropout applied?
2.  Can you elaborate on the range of values explored during the grid search performed after the controller training was completed?
3.  The paper mentions using a parameter server. What was the network bandwidth like between the controller replicas and the parameter server? Was bandwidth a limiting factor in training?
4.  In the character language modeling experiment, what were the precise settings (learning rate, dropout, batch size, etc.) used to achieve the 1.214 perplexity result?
