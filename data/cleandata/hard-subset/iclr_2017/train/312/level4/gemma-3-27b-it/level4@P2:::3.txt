This paper proposes a novel approach to neural architecture search using reinforcement learning (RL) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs). The core idea – employing an RNN to generate network architecture descriptions and then training this RNN with RL to maximize validation accuracy – is promising and supported by compelling experimental results on two benchmark datasets.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a significant advance in automating neural architecture design, achieving competitive results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank. The method demonstrates the potential to discover architectures comparable to, and in some cases exceeding, those designed by human experts.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The strength of this work lies in its innovative approach and strong empirical validation. The use of an RNN as a controller for architecture generation provides a flexible framework for exploring a vast design space. The successful application of policy gradients to optimize this controller is a key contribution. The reported results are particularly impressive: achieving a test error rate of 3.65% on CIFAR-10 (outperforming existing human-designed architectures) and surpassing state-of-the-art performance on the Penn Treebank language modeling task. The successful transfer of the discovered recurrent cell to character-level language modeling further highlights its generalizability. The authors also thoughtfully address practical challenges like training time by utilizing distributed training with asynchronous updates.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, several areas could be further explored to strengthen the work.  The significant training time required, even with substantial computational resources, is a limitation.  Investigating techniques to accelerate the search process (e.g., more efficient exploration strategies, transfer learning from related tasks) would be valuable.  While the transfer learning experiment to character-level language modeling is encouraging, further investigations into the generalizability of the designed architectures to different datasets and tasks (beyond variations of language modeling) would be beneficial. The paper focuses on convolutional and recurrent architectures; exploring the search space for transformers or other recent architectures could broaden the impact of the method. A more detailed analysis of the generated architectures – perhaps identifying common patterns or design principles – could offer insights into what makes them effective and inform future architectural design.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources required to reproduce the results (e.g., total GPU hours)?
2.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of reward function (e.g., using different powers of the validation accuracy)?
3.  The paper mentions “compilation failures” with skip connections. Could you elaborate on the frequency of these failures and the effectiveness of the implemented solutions?
4.  What steps were taken to ensure the stability and reproducibility of the RL training process? Is the performance consistent across multiple independent runs?
5.  How does the choice of the base number (8 in the recurrent cell experiments) affect the search space and resulting performance?
