## Review of Neural Architecture Search

This paper presents a novel approach to automating the design of neural network architectures, termed Neural Architecture Search (NAS). The method leverages a recurrent neural network (RNN) as a controller to generate model descriptions – effectively treating architecture design as a sequential decision-making process – and employs reinforcement learning to optimize the controller based on the performance of the generated architectures. The core idea of framing neural architecture design as an RL problem, where the controller acts as the agent and the child network’s accuracy serves as the reward, is particularly compelling. The authors demonstrate promising results on both CIFAR-10 image classification and Penn Treebank language modeling, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance with architectures discovered automatically. The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the methodology and experimental setup.

**Decision: Accept**

The strength of this paper lies in its innovative approach to a fundamental problem in deep learning – neural architecture design – and its impressive empirical results. The ability to discover architectures that rival or surpass human-designed models is a significant contribution. The thoroughness of the experiments, including comparisons to multiple baselines and exploration of architectural complexities like skip connections, further strengthens the paper's impact.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** Automating neural architecture design is a challenging and important problem. This paper offers a compelling solution by framing it within a reinforcement learning framework. The demonstrated success of the method on multiple tasks suggests a potentially paradigm-shifting approach.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are impressive. Achieving performance comparable to, or exceeding, state-of-the-art human-designed architectures is a strong indicator of the method’s effectiveness. The reported speedup on CIFAR-10 is a valuable practical benefit.
* **Clear and Thorough Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the methodology, including the key components (controller RNN, reward function, training procedure). The inclusion of detailed experimental settings and results enhances reproducibility.
* **Comprehensive Exploration:** The authors explore various aspects of the approach, including the addition of skip connections and the extension to recurrent cell design, demonstrating the flexibility and generality of the method. The transfer learning experiments further highlight the potential of the discovered architectures.

**Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **Computational Cost:** While the paper demonstrates impressive performance, the computational cost of NAS is a significant consideration. Providing a more detailed analysis of the training time required for the controller and child networks (e.g., total GPU hours) would be beneficial.  It would also be useful to discuss potential strategies for reducing the computational burden.
* **Search Space Sensitivity:** The performance of NAS is likely sensitive to the defined search space. A discussion of how the search space was chosen and how different search space configurations might affect the results would be valuable. A more formal ablation study on the impact of specific architectural elements included in the search space (e.g., skip connections) could also be insightful.
* **Controller Architecture:** The paper uses a two-layer LSTM as the controller. It would be interesting to explore the sensitivity of the results to the controller's architecture (e.g., number of layers, hidden unit size) and discuss the rationale behind the chosen controller configuration.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** While the paper mentions a grid search for hyperparameters after the controller finds a promising architecture, a more detailed description of this tuning process, including the range of hyperparameters explored, would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources (e.g., GPU hours) required to train the controller and evaluate the resulting architectures on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the definition of the search space? Did you perform any experiments with different search space configurations, and if so, what were the results?
3.  What is the impact of using a more complex controller architecture (e.g., deeper LSTM, transformer) on the performance of NAS?
4.  Can you elaborate on the exploration strategy used during the RL training process? Were any specific techniques employed to encourage exploration of the vast architecture space?
