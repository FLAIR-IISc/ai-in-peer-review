This paper introduces a novel method for automatically searching for optimal neural network architectures using a reinforcement learning framework. The approach represents a deep neural network as a variable-length sequence and employs an actor-critic mechanism to discover effective architectures. Specifically, the method uses a recurrent neural network (RNN), implemented as a two-layer LSTM, as a controller to generate architectures – essentially acting as the ‘actor’ – and evaluates their performance to provide a reward signal. This allows the controller to learn and improve its architecture generation process over time. The method is evaluated on both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank) tasks, demonstrating competitive results compared to hand-designed architectures.

**Decision: Accept.** This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning. The approach is innovative, well-motivated, and demonstrably effective, achieving state-of-the-art results on multiple benchmarks.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles a crucial and challenging problem – neural architecture design – which currently relies heavily on expert knowledge and time-consuming manual experimentation. The proposed approach offers a compelling alternative, automating the search process and potentially uncovering architectures that surpass human intuition. The methodology is clearly articulated and well-supported by experimental results. The performance gains on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are substantial and convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of related work, positioning their contribution within the broader landscape of hyperparameter optimization and neuro-evolution. The inclusion of ablation studies and comparisons to existing state-of-the-art methods strengthens the claims made. Furthermore, the successful transfer learning to character language modeling further highlights the generalizability of the learned recurrent cell. The detailed explanation of implementation choices, such as the use of skip connections and parallel training, shows careful engineering and a deep understanding of the challenges involved.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is excellent, providing some additional insights into the computational cost of the architecture search process would be beneficial. Specifically, detailing the total training time required, and exploring the relationship between training time/resources and the quality of the resulting architectures, would provide a more complete picture of the method’s practicality. It would also be valuable to investigate how incorporating human-defined priors or bootstrapping with existing architectures affects the search process. A discussion of the limitations of the search space (e.g., the types of layers considered) and potential avenues for expanding it would also be insightful. Analyzing the discovered architectures for common patterns or design principles could provide further understanding of what makes them effective.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources (e.g., GPU hours) required to perform the architecture search on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank?
2.  Have you experimented with incorporating human-defined priors or initializing the controller with knowledge from existing architectures? If so, how did this affect the search process and the resulting performance?
3.  What are the current limitations of the search space, and what are your plans for expanding it in future work? Specifically, could the method be extended to search over different optimization algorithms or activation functions?
4.  Can you elaborate on the architectural characteristics that distinguish the architectures discovered by your method from those designed by humans? Are there any recurring patterns or design principles that emerge?
