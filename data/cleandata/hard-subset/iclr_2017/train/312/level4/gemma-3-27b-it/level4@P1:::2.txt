This paper presents a novel approach to neural network architecture search based on a reinforcement learning framework. The method treats designing a deep neural network as a sequential decision process, where the architecture is generated as a variable-length sequence. A recurrent neural network (RNN) acts as a controller (the actor) proposing architectures, and the validation accuracy of the resulting network serves as the reward signal. An auto-regressive two-layer LSTM is used as the controller. The method is evaluated on image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank) tasks, achieving competitive results compared to human-designed architectures.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The problem it tackles – automating neural architecture design – is important and challenging, and the proposed approach demonstrates significant promise. The results are compelling, achieving state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art performance on both benchmark datasets.

The paper’s strength lies in its novel application of reinforcement learning to architecture search. The authors convincingly demonstrate that a recurrent network can effectively learn to generate high-performing network structures. The detailed description of the method, including improvements like skip connections and a parameter server approach for faster training, is well-presented and insightful. The results on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are strong, showcasing the generalizability of the approach. The comparisons to human-designed architectures and existing methods are comprehensive and clearly highlight the advantages of the proposed method. The paper also includes a thorough discussion of related work, demonstrating a strong understanding of the field.

Here are some suggestions that could further improve the paper (these points do not affect my acceptance decision):

*   **Computational Cost:** It would be valuable to include a more detailed analysis of the computational cost associated with the architecture search process. Specifically, how does the training time scale with the complexity of the search space or the size of the datasets? Providing information about the resources required (e.g., number of GPUs, training time) would help readers assess the practical feasibility of the method.
*   **Initialization and Bootstrapping:** Exploring the impact of different initialization strategies or incorporating human-designed architectures as a starting point for the search could provide further insights. Does starting with a pre-trained model accelerate the search process, and if so, how does it affect the final architecture discovered?
*   **Ablation Studies:** While the paper describes several improvements, more extensive ablation studies could further clarify the contribution of each component. For example, how much does skip connection search contribute relative to the base architecture search?
*   **Diversity of Architectures:** While the paper presents excellent results, discussing the diversity of architectures discovered during the search process could be interesting. Are there multiple, equally good architectures, or does the search tend to converge on a single solution?

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors:

*   In Section 3.3, you mention "compilation failures" and techniques to circumvent them. Could you provide more concrete examples of such failures and how the implemented solutions address them?
*   Regarding the controller RNN training in Section 4.1, what criteria were used to determine the optimal number of layers to increase during the training schedule? Was this a fixed schedule, or was it dynamically adjusted based on performance?
*   In the Penn Treebank experiments, the reward function includes a constant 'c'. How was the value of 'c' chosen, and what impact does it have on the search process?
