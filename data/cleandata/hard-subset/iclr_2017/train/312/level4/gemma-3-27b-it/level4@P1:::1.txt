The paper explores an important area of our field – automating architecture search. While the technique is currently computationally intensive, this trade-off will likely become more favorable in the near future as technology continues to improve.

The paper covers both standard vision and text tasks and tackles many benchmark datasets, demonstrating gains can be made by exploring beyond the standard RNN and CNN search space. While applying the technique to more datasets would be beneficial, the current results are sufficient to show it’s not only competitive with human architectural intuition but may even surpass it. This suggests an approach to tailoring architectures to specific datasets without extensive hand-engineering.

This is a well-written paper on an interesting topic with strong results. I recommend it be accepted.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper addresses a significant challenge in deep learning: the difficulty of neural network architecture design. It proposes a novel approach – Neural Architecture Search – that leverages reinforcement learning to automate this process. The core idea of using a recurrent network controller to generate network architectures is clever and well-motivated. 

The results presented are compelling. On CIFAR-10, the method achieves a competitive error rate, outperforming previous state-of-the-art models with similar architectural schemes and offering a speed improvement. More impressively, on the Penn Treebank dataset, the generated recurrent cell surpasses existing LSTM architectures, and exhibits strong transfer learning performance to character language modeling. The reported gains in perplexity are substantial.

The paper is well-structured, clearly explains the methodology, and provides sufficient experimental details. The inclusion of control experiments strengthens the validity of the findings. The authors acknowledge limitations and suggest future research directions.

**Additional Feedback:**

The paper would benefit from greater transparency regarding the hyperparameters used in the experiments, particularly the grid search performed after the controller training. Specifically, could the authors provide:

*   **For the CIFAR-10 experiments:** The range of learning rates, weight decays, batchnorm epsilons, and decay epochs explored during the grid search.
*   **For the Penn Treebank experiments:** Details of the dropout rates (recurrent dropout, embedding dropout, etc.) used, and whether embedding sharing was employed.
*   **For both tasks:** A more detailed description of the initialization scheme used for the weights.

Releasing a base set of hyperparameters, following the example of “Recurrent Neural Network Regularization” (Zaremba et al., 2014), would significantly aid reproducibility and facilitate future research in this area.

The parameter server setup described in Section 3.2 is relatively brief. A more detailed description of the synchronization strategy and the impact of asynchronous updates on training stability would be valuable.

Finally, the description of “skip connections” and how the controller predicts them could be clarified with a more illustrative example.
