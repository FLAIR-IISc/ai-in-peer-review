This paper investigates the compelling problem of automating neural network architecture search. The authors demonstrate a method capable of discovering architectures that achieve competitive, and in some cases superior, performance to manually designed networks on standard vision and text tasks, across a range of benchmark datasets. While the computational cost of the search process is currently substantial, the likely future improvements in hardware and optimization techniques suggest this trade-off will become more favorable.

The core of the method involves training a controller RNN, then selecting the best-performing cell based on validation perplexity. The paper states that a subsequent grid search is performed over learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates, and decay epoch to optimize the selected cell. To further refine the architecture, the best cell is then explored with varying configurations and sizes to adjust its capacity. 

To facilitate reproducibility – a critical aspect of advancing research in this field – I strongly encourage the authors to provide a detailed account of the hyperparameters used during this optimization process. Specifically, including the ranges explored in the grid search, as well as the *type* of dropout employed (e.g., recurrent dropout, embedding dropout, variational dropout) would be immensely valuable. As highlighted by Zaremba et al. (2014) in “Recurrent Neural Network Regularization”, sharing a robust base set of hyperparameters significantly aids future work and allows for more efficient replication of results. 

This request for detailed hyperparameters extends to the other experiments presented in the paper, such as the character language modeling task. Clear documentation of experimental settings is essential for others to build upon this promising work.

The strength of this work lies in its broad applicability and positive results. The authors successfully move beyond traditional RNN and CNN architectures, demonstrating gains on diverse datasets. While applying the technique to an even wider range of datasets would be beneficial, the current results provide strong evidence that the proposed method not only rivals human architectural intuition but can, in fact, surpass it. Importantly, this suggests a path toward dataset-specific architecture tailoring *without* requiring extensive manual feature engineering.

Overall, this is a well-written and compelling paper addressing an important topic with strong empirical results. I recommend acceptance.
