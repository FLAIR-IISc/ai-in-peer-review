## Review of “Neural Architecture Search”

This paper explores an important and challenging area of our field: automating neural architecture search. The authors propose a novel approach using a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller trained with reinforcement learning to generate network architectures, demonstrating strong results on both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank) tasks. While computationally intensive, the technique shows promising gains beyond standard, human-designed architectures, suggesting potential for dataset-specific tailoring without extensive hand-engineering. This is a well-written paper with compelling results, and I recommend it for acceptance.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a novel and effective approach to neural architecture search, achieving state-of-the-art results on challenging benchmark datasets. The results convincingly demonstrate the potential of automating architecture design and surpassing human intuition.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles a significant problem in deep learning: the difficulty and expertise required for neural network architecture design. The proposed approach, Neural Architecture Search, is well-motivated, building upon recent advances in reinforcement learning and sequence generation. The use of an RNN controller to generate variable-length architectural descriptions is a clever and effective idea. 

The experimental results are strong. On CIFAR-10, the discovered architecture achieves a test error rate competitive with, and in some cases exceeding, state-of-the-art human-designed networks, while also improving inference speed. The success on Penn Treebank, where the learned recurrent cell outperforms widely-used LSTMs and achieves state-of-the-art perplexity, is particularly impressive. The transfer learning results on character language modeling further demonstrate the generalizability of the discovered cell. The comprehensive experiments and ablations, including the control experiments against random search and exploring the addition of more functions into the search space, strengthen the validity of the findings. 

The paper clearly explains the methodology, including the training process, reward function, and the use of techniques to improve training stability and speed (parallelism, skip connections). The inclusion of detailed experimental settings, though lacking specifics on hyperparameters (see feedback below), facilitates a good understanding of the experimental setup.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not impacting the acceptance decision):**

While the paper provides a good overview of the experimental setup, it would significantly enhance reproducibility if the authors included a more complete specification of the hyperparameters used for training the child networks, especially for the Penn Treebank experiments. Specifically, details regarding the type of dropout employed (e.g., recurrent dropout, embedding dropout) would be highly valuable, mirroring the practice in related works like Zaremba et al. (2014) to aid future research. Providing this information for the CIFAR-10 experiments as well would be beneficial.

The discussion of the search space could be further expanded. While the paper outlines the basic components, detailing the rationale behind the specific ranges chosen for hyperparameters (e.g., filter heights, number of filters) could provide additional insight. 

The connection to program synthesis and inductive programming is briefly mentioned. Exploring this connection more deeply, and potentially framing the work within this broader context, might be fruitful for future research.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more complete list of the hyperparameters used to train the child networks on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, including details regarding the type(s) of dropout employed?
2. What considerations informed the selection of the ranges for hyperparameters within the search space (e.g., filter heights, number of filters)? Were any preliminary experiments conducted to refine these ranges?
3.  The paper mentions the controller learning to prefer larger filters at the top layers of the CIFAR-10 architecture. Do you have any intuition or analysis as to *why* this occurs? Is this a recurring pattern observed in other architectures learned by the controller?
