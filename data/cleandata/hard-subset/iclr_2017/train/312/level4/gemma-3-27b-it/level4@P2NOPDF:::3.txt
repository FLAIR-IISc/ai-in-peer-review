This paper presents a novel approach to neural network architecture design, leveraging reinforcement learning (RL) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs). The core idea – utilizing an RNN to generate network descriptions and then training this RNN with RL – is both interesting and holds considerable promise. The experimental results on two datasets demonstrate the method’s viability and suggest a robust performance.

The strengths of this work are significant. First, the proposed framework for automating architecture design is a compelling contribution. The combination of RNNs for architectural description and RL for optimization offers a flexible and potentially powerful alternative to manual design or more constrained search methods. Second, the generated architectures exhibit similarities to those designed by human experts. This observation is encouraging, suggesting that the learned architectures are not simply random structures, but rather capture elements of established design principles and are therefore potentially interpretable and effective. This compatibility with human-designed networks is a valuable characteristic.

However, the paper also has some limitations. A key practical concern is the substantial training time required, even with access to considerable computational resources. This lengthy training process could limit the scalability and accessibility of the approach. Further investigation into methods for accelerating training – perhaps through more efficient RL algorithms or architectural constraints – would be beneficial.

More importantly, the experiments conducted do not fully establish the generalizability of the generated architectures. While performance is demonstrated on two datasets, the extent to which these architectures transfer to other, similar but distinct tasks remains unclear. It would be significantly strengthened by evaluating the generated sequential models on a broader range of datasets. Assessing performance beyond the specific datasets used in the paper is crucial for demonstrating the method’s true potential. Specifically, testing on variations of the existing datasets, or datasets with similar characteristics (e.g., different image resolutions, or different sequence lengths), would provide valuable insight into the robustness and adaptability of the learned architectures.

Overall, this is a worthwhile contribution. The proposed approach is innovative and the preliminary results are positive. However, further experimentation is needed to convincingly demonstrate a clear advantage over manually designed models, and to better understand the breadth of its applicability. Addressing the computational cost and improving the generalizability of the generated architectures would substantially strengthen this work.
