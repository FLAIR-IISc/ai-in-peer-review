This paper introduces a novel approach to neural architecture search using an actor-critic reinforcement learning framework. The method frames deep neural networks as variable-length sequences and employs reinforcement learning to discover optimal architectures. Specifically, the architecture itself acts as the “actor,” with node selection constituting the action within the RL environment. The resulting network’s evaluation error serves as the reward signal. An autoregressive, two-layer LSTM functions as both the controller and critic within this framework. The proposed method is evaluated on two distinct problems, with performance compared to a range of manually designed architectures.

This is a highly promising paper addressing a significant challenge in deep learning: the laborious and often suboptimal process of hand-designing network architectures. It’s difficult to ascertain how close manually constructed networks are to true optimality, and this work offers a compelling alternative. The approach is genuinely novel, and the authors present it with commendable clarity and thoroughness, detailing the necessary improvements made throughout the development process. The experimental results effectively demonstrate the method’s capabilities. The observed differences between the discovered architectures and those designed by humans are particularly insightful. The paper is exceptionally well-written, accessible to a broad audience, and provides a comprehensive and well-contextualized review of related work. 

To further strengthen the paper, it would be beneficial to include data regarding the computational time required for training. Investigating the correlation between training time/resource consumption and the quality of the resulting models would also be valuable. Furthermore, exploring the performance of models initialized with human-designed architectures – effectively using human intuition as a “bootstrap” – and incorporating human feedback into the learning process could be an interesting avenue for future work.

Overall, this is an excellent and insightful contribution to the field.
