## Review of Neural Architecture Search

This paper proposes a novel approach to neural architecture design using reinforcement learning (RL) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs). The core idea—using an RNN controller to generate network architectures and training it with RL to maximize validation accuracy—is promising and well-executed. The experimental results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning, demonstrating a viable and effective approach to neural architecture search. The strong empirical results, particularly on the Penn Treebank dataset where the generated recurrent cell outperforms LSTM, provide compelling evidence for the method’s potential.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles a challenging problem – automating the design of neural network architectures – which has traditionally relied heavily on human expertise and laborious trial-and-error. The motivation is clearly articulated: designing effective architectures remains a significant bottleneck in deep learning. The approach is well-grounded in existing literature on hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis, clearly positioning the work within the broader research landscape. 

Critically, the paper provides strong empirical support for its claims. On CIFAR-10, the designed architecture matches or surpasses human-designed state-of-the-art models in both accuracy and speed. The results on Penn Treebank are even more compelling, with the learned recurrent cell demonstrably outperforming widely-used LSTM architectures. The authors also provide ablation studies and control experiments (e.g., adding more functions to the search space, comparison against random search) that strengthen the validity of their findings. The inclusion of transfer learning results to the character language modeling task further demonstrates the generality of the discovered cell.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, several points could be addressed to further enhance its clarity and impact.

*   **Computational Cost:** The long training time, even with substantial computational resources, is a notable limitation. A more detailed discussion of the scalability of the approach and potential strategies for reducing the training time (e.g., more efficient RL algorithms, transfer learning from simpler datasets) would be valuable.
*   **Generalization:** The authors acknowledge the limited investigation of generalization across datasets. Exploring the transferability of the generated architectures to other image classification datasets or language modeling tasks would further strengthen the paper’s conclusions.
*   **Search Space:** A more detailed analysis of the impact of the search space definition (e.g., the range of filter sizes, the choice of activation functions) on the resulting architectures would be insightful. Could a different search space lead to even better results?
*   **Visualization:** While Figure 5 and 8 are helpful, further visualizations of the architectures discovered by the system, particularly for the CIFAR-10 models, could provide a better understanding of the learned structures.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions using an exponential moving average for the baseline in the REINFORCE algorithm. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the baseline’s smoothing factor?
2.  The experiments primarily focus on convolutional and recurrent architectures. Are there inherent limitations in the proposed method that prevent it from effectively searching for other types of architectures (e.g., transformers)?
3.  Could you elaborate on the practical challenges encountered when scaling the distributed training setup, and how you addressed them?
4.  Given the long training time, have you explored techniques like warm-starting the controller with architectures pre-trained on related datasets to accelerate the search process?
