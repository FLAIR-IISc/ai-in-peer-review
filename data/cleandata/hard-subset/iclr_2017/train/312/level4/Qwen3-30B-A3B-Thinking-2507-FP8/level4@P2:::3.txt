

**Review for ICLR 2017**

**Summary of Contributions**  
This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a novel approach that uses a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to automatically generate neural network architectures through reinforcement learning. The controller produces a sequence of tokens describing a network's structure, and the controller is trained to maximize the validation accuracy of the corresponding "child" network. The authors demonstrate this method's effectiveness on two challenging tasks: image classification on CIFAR-10 and language modeling on Penn Treebank. Their approach discovers architectures that rival or exceed human-designed models, achieving state-of-the-art results with competitive efficiency. The method's flexibility in handling variable-length architectures and its ability to generate architectures with skip connections and novel recurrent cells represent significant contributions to the field.

**Decision**  
I recommend **acceptance** with the following key reasons:  
1. The paper presents a well-motivated and novel approach to neural architecture search that effectively bridges the gap between human expertise and automated design.  
2. The empirical results are scientifically rigorous and demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods, with thorough ablation studies and multiple validation metrics.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper's core contribution—using a controller RNN trained via policy gradients to search for optimal architectures—is well-justified and positioned within the relevant literature. Previous hyperparameter optimization methods operate in fixed-length spaces, while neuro-evolution approaches are often computationally prohibitive. The authors effectively address these limitations by enabling flexible, variable-length architecture search through a sequence generation framework. Their method's ability to discover architectures that mirror human-designed structures (e.g., skip connections in convolutional networks, LSTM-like components in recurrent cells) suggests a meaningful alignment with established design principles.

The empirical results are compelling and rigorously validated. On CIFAR-10, the discovered architecture achieves 3.65% test error (0.09% better than the prior state-of-the-art) while being 1.05x faster. On Penn Treebank, the generated recurrent cell achieves 62.4 perplexity (3.6 better than prior work) and demonstrates strong transferability to character language modeling and machine translation tasks. The ablation studies (e.g., adding skip connections, varying search space complexity) and comparison against random search provide strong evidence of the method's effectiveness. The authors also thoroughly document their implementation details, including distributed training protocols and hyperparameter schedules, enhancing reproducibility.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**  
To further strengthen the paper, I recommend addressing the following points:  
1. **Computational Efficiency**: While the paper acknowledges the long training time, it would be valuable to include a more detailed analysis of computational costs (e.g., total GPU-hours required) and how this compares to human-designed architecture development. A discussion of potential scalability improvements would also be helpful.  
2. **Generalization Analysis**: The paper demonstrates strong performance on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, but additional experiments on other datasets (e.g., ImageNet for image classification, other language modeling benchmarks) would better establish the method's broader applicability.  
3. **Architecture Interpretability**: While the paper notes that the discovered architectures resemble human-designed ones, providing visualizations or qualitative comparisons (e.g., layer types, connection patterns) would make this observation more concrete for readers.

**Questions for Authors**  
To clarify my understanding and strengthen my assessment, I would appreciate answers to the following:  
1. How does the computational cost of your method compare to the time required for human-designed architectures? Given that human experts often iterate through multiple designs, how does your method's total time compare to a human's process?  
2. The paper mentions that the discovered recurrent cell generalizes well to character language modeling and machine translation. Could you elaborate on whether this generalization is specific to the PTB dataset or if it extends to other domains?  
3. In the CIFAR-10 experiments, you report a 4.47% error rate for a 39-layer architecture that approaches the human-designed 3.74% result. What specific architectural features (beyond depth) contributed most to this performance, and how might these insights inform future human-designed architectures?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the growing field of automated machine learning. The Neural Architecture Search framework is well-conceived, the results are impressive, and the method's potential for broader application is clear. With minor improvements to the analysis of computational costs and generalization, this work would be an excellent contribution to ICLR 2017.
