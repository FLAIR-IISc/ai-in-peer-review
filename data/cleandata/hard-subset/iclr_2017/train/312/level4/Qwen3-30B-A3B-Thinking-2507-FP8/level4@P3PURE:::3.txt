

This paper proposes using reinforcement learning (RL) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to automatically design network architectures for specific tasks. The core idea is promising, and the experimental results on two datasets demonstrate robust performance.  

The primary strengths of the paper are:  
1. The approach of employing an RNN to generate network architectures and using RL to train the RNN is novel and well-motivated.  
2. The generated architectures exhibit structural similarities to human-designed counterparts, indicating compatibility between human expertise and the learned architectures.  

Key limitations include:  
1. The RL training process is computationally intensive, requiring substantial time even with significant computational resources.  
2. The experiments lack evidence of generalizability; the performance of the generated architectures on other similar but distinct datasets—particularly for sequential tasks—remains untested.  

Overall, this is a promising contribution. However, the paper requires additional experiments to substantiate its potential advantages over manually designed architectures.
