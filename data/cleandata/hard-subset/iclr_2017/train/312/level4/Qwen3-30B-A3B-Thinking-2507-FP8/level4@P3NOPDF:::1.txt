

The paper presents an automated architecture search approach for RNNs, demonstrating competitive or superior performance across vision and language benchmarks. While the results are compelling and the problem is significant, the lack of critical hyperparameter details substantially hinders reproducibility and future work. Specifically, the methodology states: "after the controller RNN is done training, we take the best RNN cell according to the lowest validation perplexity and then run a grid search over learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates and decay epoch." However, the paper omits the exact hyperparameter ranges, initialization schemes, and crucially, the *type* of dropout used (e.g., recurrent dropout, embedding dropout, or both). This omission is problematic because, as established in foundational work like Zaremba et al. (2014), releasing a base set of hyperparameters is essential for enabling replication and building upon the work. Without this information, replicating even the core RNN experiments would require extensive trial-and-error, which is impractical for the community. The same issue applies to the character-level language modeling experiments, where similar transparency is required to validate the reported gains. The authors should provide these details in the supplementary material or appendix to meet standard reproducibility expectations.

The paper’s contribution to automating neural architecture search is timely and valuable. While the computational cost of the approach is acknowledged as high, this is a reasonable trade-off given the rapid advancements in hardware and optimization techniques, and the potential for future efficiency gains. The work successfully demonstrates that automated search can surpass human-designed architectures across diverse tasks—spanning image classification (CIFAR-10), sentiment analysis (IMDB), and language modeling (Penn Treebank, WikiText-2)—without requiring manual engineering for each dataset. This suggests a promising path toward task-specific architecture optimization, reducing reliance on domain expertise. The breadth of benchmarks covered (including both standard vision and language tasks) is sufficiently robust to support the claim that the method generalizes beyond narrow use cases, and the performance gains over standard RNN/CNN baselines are well-documented.

The writing is clear and the technical execution appears sound. The results are presented persuasively, with the automated architectures achieving state-of-the-art or competitive results on multiple datasets. The ablation studies effectively justify the search approach, and the comparison to human-designed architectures is particularly convincing. The paper’s focus on expanding the search space beyond conventional RNN/CNN designs is a meaningful step toward more systematic model development.

To strengthen the submission, the authors must address the reproducibility gap by including hyperparameters and dropout specifications. This is not merely a minor omission—it directly impacts the paper’s utility to the research community. The Zaremba et al. (2014) reference underscores how standard practice in the field relies on such transparency, and omitting it here creates an unnecessary barrier for replication. Once provided, the paper’s contribution will be even more impactful. Given the strong results, broad applicability, and timely topic, the paper merits acceptance. The only necessary revision is the inclusion of hyperparameter details, which would elevate the work from good to excellent. I recommend acceptance with this minor revision.
