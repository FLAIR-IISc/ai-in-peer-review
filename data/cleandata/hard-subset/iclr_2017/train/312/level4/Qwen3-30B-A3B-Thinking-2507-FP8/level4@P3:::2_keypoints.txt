- Proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS): a controller RNN that generates variable-length architectures trained with reinforcement learning using child validation accuracy as reward.  
- Evaluated on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving state-of-the-art results (3.65% test error on CIFAR-10 and 62.4 perplexity on PTB).  
- CIFAR-10 model is reported as 1.05x faster than previous best; discovered recurrent cell transfers to character-level language modeling.  
- Decision: Accept — novel, significant contribution with strong empirical support.  
- Tackles the important problem of automating neural architecture design, removing reliance on expert knowledge.  
- Represents architectures as variable-length strings, enabling sequence-generation techniques.  
- Use of reinforcement learning for the controller is well-justified and positioned within prior work.  
- Empirical validation is thorough, including ablation studies showing controller improvement over time.  
- Comparison to random search demonstrates a clear advantage for NAS.  
- Technical contributions include incorporation of skip connections, extension to recurrent cell search, and flexibility beyond fixed-length hyperparameter spaces.  
- Paper is well-written and clearly structured.  
- Lacks detailed reporting of computational requirements (e.g., GPU hours) to assess practicality.  
- Would benefit from a comparison between architectures found with and without human-provided initial examples.  
- Needs discussion of limitations and generalization to other domains and scaling bottlenecks.  
- Questions for authors: clarify whether CIFAR-10 speedup was measured on identical hardware and provide FLOPs/computational complexity comparisons.  
- Questions for authors: provide a fairer comparison to DenseNet that uses 1x1 convolutions to reduce parameters.  
- Questions for authors: explain how the controller decides to add skip connections versus other architectural elements.
