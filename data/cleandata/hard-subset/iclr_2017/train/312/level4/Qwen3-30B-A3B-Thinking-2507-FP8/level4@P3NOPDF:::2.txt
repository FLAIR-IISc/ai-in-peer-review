

This paper introduces a novel reinforcement learning (RL) approach for automated neural network architecture search, framed within an actor-critic paradigm. The method models neural network architectures as variable-length sequences, where the RL agent (the actor) selects architectural components (e.g., layer types, connections) as discrete actions. The reward signal is derived from the evaluation error of the resulting architecture. A two-layer LSTM controller serves as the actor, while a separate critic network evaluates the action-value function. The approach is evaluated on two benchmark tasks, demonstrating competitive performance against multiple human-designed architectures.  

The work addresses a critical challenge in deep learning: the labor-intensive, suboptimal nature of manual architecture design. The proposed RL framework offers a principled, scalable alternative, and the paper presents a clear, technically sound methodology. The authors thoroughly detail the controller architecture, reward formulation, and search procedure, including key improvements over prior work. The experimental results effectively showcase the method’s capability to discover architectures that outperform or match human-engineered baselines, with insightful comparisons highlighting structural differences between automatically generated and human-designed networks. The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a logical flow, precise terminology, and comprehensive coverage of related work. The evaluation datasets and metrics are well-chosen, providing robust evidence of the method’s efficacy.  

The primary strength lies in the methodological novelty and the clarity of presentation. The authors successfully bridge RL with architecture search, offering a framework that is both theoretically grounded and practically applicable. The accessibility of the writing—avoiding excessive jargon while maintaining technical rigor—makes the contribution valuable to a broad audience. The comparison between machine-generated and human-designed architectures is particularly compelling, suggesting that RL can uncover non-intuitive design patterns.  

To further strengthen the work, the authors should address two points:  
1. **Training Efficiency**: The paper does not report training time or computational resource requirements for the RL search process. Including this data would provide crucial context for practical adoption, especially given the known computational cost of architecture search. A discussion of the correlation between search time, resource allocation, and final model quality would be highly valuable.  
2. **Human-Initiated Search**: It would be insightful to explore how human-initialized architectures (e.g., starting from a human-designed baseline) impact the RL search process. Specifically, does leveraging human expertise accelerate convergence, improve final performance, or reduce search complexity? This could help contextualize the method’s utility in real-world scenarios where domain knowledge is available.  

Overall, this is a strong, well-executed contribution that advances the state of the art in automated neural architecture search. The method is innovative, the experiments are thorough, and the writing is exemplary. The suggested refinements are minor and would enhance the paper’s practical relevance without diminishing its core contribution. The work is highly relevant to ICLR’s audience and merits publication.
