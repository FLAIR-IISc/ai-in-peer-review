- Summary: Proposes Neural Architecture Search using an RNN controller trained with reinforcement learning to auto-generate architectures, achieving state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 (3.65% error) and Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity).

- Decision: Accept.

- Key reason 1: Automates a time-consuming, expertise-heavy architecture design process and outperforms human-designed architectures on benchmarks.

- Key reason 2: Method is well-motivated and rigorously evaluated with strong empirical results, ablations, and baselines.

- Supporting argument — Problem: Clearly frames manual architecture design as costly and formulates an RL-based automation solution.

- Supporting argument — Approach: Justifies the RNN controller, variable-length search space, and positions work relative to hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis.

- Supporting argument — Evidence: Provides robust empirical support (CIFAR-10, PTB), proper validation, multiple runs, ablations, and transfer learning experiments.

- Additional feedback 1: Provide concrete computational-efficiency metrics (e.g., total GPU-hours) and compare to human design costs.

- Additional feedback 2: Expand ablations to compare discovered architectures directly with contemporary human designs (ResNet, DenseNet) to assess novelty.

- Additional feedback 3: Add deeper analysis of interpretability and architectural motifs (e.g., rectangular filters, larger top-layer filters) to explain effectiveness.

- Question 1: Clarify how computational-complexity comparison to DenseNet is measured given differing parameter-efficiency techniques like 1x1 convolutions.

- Question 2: Explain what makes the discovered Penn Treebank recurrent cell “more than two times faster” (fewer ops, memory patterns, or other design choices).

- Question 3: Clarify why skip connections helped in the complex architecture but not in the simpler CIFAR-10 variant, and how the controller chooses such complexity.
