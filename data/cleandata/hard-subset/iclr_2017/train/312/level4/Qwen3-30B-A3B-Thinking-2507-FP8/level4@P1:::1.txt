

The paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a method that uses a recurrent neural network controller to automatically generate neural network architectures. The controller learns to produce model descriptions by maximizing validation accuracy through reinforcement learning. The authors demonstrate that this approach can discover novel architectures that outperform human-designed models on both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank) benchmarks. On CIFAR-10, their method achieves a test error rate of 3.65% (0.09% better than the previous state-of-the-art) while being 1.05x faster. On Penn Treebank, their discovered recurrent cell achieves a perplexity of 62.4 (3.6 better than the previous best), with additional improvements on character language modeling and machine translation tasks.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to automating neural architecture design, demonstrating that reinforcement learning can discover architectures that rival or surpass human-designed models. The method's flexibility in exploring variable-length architectures represents an important advancement over existing hyperparameter optimization approaches.

The paper's key strengths include its clear technical contribution, strong empirical results across multiple challenging benchmarks, and careful comparison to relevant baselines. The method is well-motivated by the limitations of current architecture design approaches and effectively positioned within the literature. The authors convincingly show that their approach can generate novel architectures that outperform established human-designed models, including a convolutional architecture that achieves state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 and a recurrent cell that outperforms LSTMs on language modeling tasks.

I have two minor concerns that could be addressed to strengthen the paper further. First, the hyperparameters used for the final model configurations on Penn Treebank (including specific dropout types and values) are not fully detailed. While the authors mention "grid search over learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates and decay epoch," providing the specific hyperparameters used for the best model would significantly aid reproducibility. As with "Recurrent Neural Network Regularization" (Zaremba et al., 2014), releasing a base set of hyperparameters would be valuable for the community. This is particularly important for the character language modeling results, which rely on similar techniques.

Second, the paper states that the controller "can design a novel recurrent cell that outperforms the widely-used LSTM cell, and other state-of-the-art baselines" but doesn't explicitly clarify whether this was achieved through a single search or multiple independent runs. It would be helpful to know how many times the controller was trained and how consistent the results were across different runs to establish the robustness of the approach.

Regarding the CIFAR-10 results, the authors note that their model "is 1.05x as fast as the DenseNet model that achieves 3.74%." However, the comparison is not entirely fair since DenseNet uses 1x1 convolutions to reduce parameters, while the authors' architecture does not. A more direct comparison would be valuable, such as testing their architecture with 1x1 convolutions or reporting the parameter count for both models.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a compelling contribution to the field. The experimental results are thorough and well-documented, with appropriate comparisons to relevant baselines. The method's applicability to both vision and language tasks demonstrates its broad potential. While the computational cost of the approach is currently high, the authors appropriately acknowledge this as a limitation that may be mitigated as hardware and optimization techniques advance.

To clarify the results, I would appreciate it if the authors could provide the specific hyperparameters used for the final Penn Treebank models (including the exact dropout techniques and values) and address the consistency of their results across multiple controller training runs. This information would greatly aid reproducibility and help the community better understand the method's capabilities and limitations.
