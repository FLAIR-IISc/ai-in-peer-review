

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a novel approach that uses a recurrent neural network controller trained via reinforcement learning to automatically generate high-performing neural network architectures. The authors demonstrate their method's effectiveness by discovering convolutional architectures for CIFAR-10 that achieve 3.65% test error (0.09% better than the previous state-of-the-art) and a novel recurrent cell for Penn Treebank language modeling that achieves 62.4 perplexity (3.6 perplexity better than the previous state-of-the-art). The work is well-positioned within the literature on hyperparameter optimization and neural architecture search, and the authors provide thorough methodological details about their controller implementation, training procedure, and search space design.

I recommend accepting this paper. The key reasons for this decision are: (1) the paper presents a genuinely novel approach to neural architecture search that successfully discovers architectures competitive with human-designed ones, and (2) the experimental results are scientifically rigorous with clear comparisons to appropriate baselines across two distinct domains.

The authors' method effectively addresses the problem of architecture design by treating neural network structures as variable-length sequences that can be generated by a controller RNN. The reinforcement learning framework is well-motivated, with the validation accuracy serving as a meaningful reward signal. The paper's technical contributions are significant, particularly the controller architecture that can generate skip connections and branch layers, and the adaptation of this framework to generate recurrent cell architectures. The empirical results are compelling, with the CIFAR-10 model achieving better accuracy while being 1.05x faster than the previous state-of-the-art, and the Penn Treebank model outperforming established LSTM baselines. The thorough experimental evaluation, including ablation studies and transfer learning results, provides strong evidence for the approach's validity.

For further improvement, I suggest including more detailed information about the computational resources required to train the controller, including the total wall-clock time and GPU hours needed to discover the architectures. Additionally, the authors might consider including a comparison between their approach and human-designed architectures that have been optimized through extensive manual tuning, rather than just comparing to the final published human designs. This would provide a more complete picture of the method's relative efficiency in terms of human effort versus computational resources. The paper would also benefit from a brief discussion of potential limitations in the search space design and how these might be addressed in future work.
