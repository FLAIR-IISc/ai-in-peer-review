

The paper states: "[a]fter the controller RNN is done training, we take the best RNN cell according to the lowest validation perplexity and then run a grid search over learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates and decay epoch. The best cell found was then run with three different configurations and sizes to increase its capacity." The authors should include the specific hyperparameters (learning rates, weight initialization schemes, dropout types, and decay epochs) used in this process, as well as clarify the dropout type (e.g., recurrent dropout, embedding dropout, or both). Without these details, replication would require extensive trial-and-error. As demonstrated in "Recurrent Neural Network Regularization" (Zaremba et al., 2014), releasing a base hyperparameter configuration significantly aids reproducibility and future work in the field. This clarity is also essential for the character-level language modeling experiments.  

The paper addresses a critical direction in our field: automating neural architecture search. While the current computational cost is high, this trade-off is expected to diminish with advancing hardware. The work demonstrates strong empirical results across standard vision and text tasks, leveraging multiple benchmark datasets to show that exploring beyond conventional RNN and CNN search spaces yields tangible gains. The technique proves competitive with—potentially surpassing—human architectural intuition, suggesting a viable path to tailor architectures to specific datasets without manual hand-engineering. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and presents compelling evidence. I recommend acceptance.
