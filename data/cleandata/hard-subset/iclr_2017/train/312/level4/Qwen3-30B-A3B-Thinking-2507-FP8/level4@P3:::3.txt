

# Review for ICLR_2017

## Summary
This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a novel approach that uses a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller trained with reinforcement learning to automatically generate neural network architectures. The controller generates variable-length strings representing architectural hyperparameters, which are then used to construct and evaluate child networks. The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness on two challenging benchmarks: CIFAR-10 image classification and Penn Treebank language modeling. On CIFAR-10, the method discovers a novel architecture achieving 3.65% test error (0.09% better than the previous state-of-the-art) while being 1.05x faster. On Penn Treebank, it designs a new recurrent cell achieving 62.4 test perplexity (3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art), which also transfers effectively to character language modeling and machine translation tasks. The approach represents a significant step toward automating the architecture design process, with results that rival or exceed human-designed architectures.

## Decision
Accept.

## Key Reasons for Acceptance
1. The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning: the time-consuming, expertise-intensive process of neural network architecture design. The proposed method offers a principled, automated approach that outperforms human-designed architectures on benchmark tasks.
2. The method is well-motivated and rigorously evaluated. The authors provide compelling empirical evidence across multiple datasets and tasks, with thorough ablation studies and comparisons against strong baselines.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper successfully answers the three key questions for review:

1. **Problem Tackled**: The paper clearly identifies the problem of manual neural network architecture design as time-intensive and requiring significant expertise, and proposes a reinforcement learning-based approach to automate this process.

2. **Well-Motivated Approach**: The authors provide strong justification for their approach, positioning it within the context of hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis. The method's flexibility in searching variable-length architectural spaces represents a significant advancement over existing approaches that are limited to fixed-length configurations or require human-designed search spaces.

3. **Support for Claims**: The paper provides robust empirical evidence supporting its claims. The results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are statistically significant, with careful attention to experimental design (e.g., proper validation sets, multiple runs, and ablation studies). The authors also demonstrate the generality of their approach through transfer learning to character language modeling and machine translation tasks. The reported improvements (0.09% better error rate on CIFAR-10, 3.6 perplexity better on Penn Treebank) are substantial in the context of these benchmarks.

## Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. Including a more detailed discussion of computational efficiency. While the paper mentions the method's scalability through distributed training, it would be valuable to provide concrete metrics on the total computational cost (e.g., number of GPU-hours) required to discover the architectures, and how this compares to human design efforts.

2. Expanding the ablation studies to include a comparison of the discovered architectures with human-designed architectures from the same era (e.g., ResNet, DenseNet). This would help clarify whether the method discovers genuinely novel architectures or simply optimizes existing human-designed templates.

3. Providing more insight into the interpretability of the discovered architectures. For instance, the paper notes that the CIFAR-10 architecture has "many rectangular filters" and "prefers larger filters at the top layers." A more detailed analysis of these architectural properties would help establish why the discovered architecture is effective.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that the CIFAR-10 architecture with 40 additional filters achieves 3.65% error rate, which is 1.05x faster than DenseNet (which achieves 3.74% error rate). However, DenseNet achieves 3.46% error rate with 1x1 convolutions for parameter reduction. Could you clarify how the computational complexity comparison is made between these two models, given that they use different architectural techniques for parameter efficiency?

2. In the Penn Treebank experiments, the authors state that their cell is "more than two times faster" than the previous best network. Could you elaborate on what exactly makes the cell faster (e.g., fewer operations, more efficient memory access), and how this relates to the architectural design choices made by the controller?

3. The paper mentions that the controller can generate architectures with skip connections, but the CIFAR-10 results show that architectures without skip connections achieved better performance (5.50% error rate vs. 4.47% with skip connections). Could you provide more insight into why skip connections were beneficial in the more complex architecture but not in the simpler one? This would help understand the method's decision-making process regarding architectural complexity.
