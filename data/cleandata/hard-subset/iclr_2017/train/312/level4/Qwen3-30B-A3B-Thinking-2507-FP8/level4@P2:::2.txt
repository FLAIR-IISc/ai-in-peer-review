

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a novel approach to automatically generate neural network architectures using a recurrent network (the "controller") trained with reinforcement learning. The controller generates variable-length strings representing network architectures, which are evaluated on a validation set to provide reward signals for training. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on two challenging benchmarks: CIFAR-10 image classification and Penn Treebank language modeling. On CIFAR-10, their method discovers an architecture achieving 3.65% test error (0.09% better than the previous state-of-the-art) while being 1.05x faster. On Penn Treebank, their discovered recurrent cell achieves 62.4 perplexity (3.6 better than the previous state-of-the-art) and transfers successfully to character language modeling, achieving 1.214 perplexity.

Decision: Accept.

Key reasons: The paper presents a novel, well-motivated approach to a significant problem in deep learning (automated architecture design), and provides strong empirical validation through rigorous experiments on two well-established benchmarks with thorough ablation studies.

Supporting arguments: The paper clearly articulates the problem of manual architecture design being time-consuming and expert-dependent, and positions its approach within the literature on hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis. The method's novelty lies in using reinforcement learning to search variable-length architecture spaces, which addresses a key limitation of existing approaches that only search fixed-length spaces. The experiments are scientifically rigorous, with careful attention to experimental design (e.g., using separate validation sets for reward signals, running grid searches for hyperparameters before final evaluation). The results are impressive: on CIFAR-10, the method discovers architectures that outperform human-invented ones while being more efficient, and on Penn Treebank, it discovers a recurrent cell that surpasses the widely used LSTM architecture.

Additional feedback to improve the paper:
1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of computational efficiency. While the authors mention the use of distributed training, they don't provide concrete numbers about training time or resource requirements compared to human-designed architectures. It would be valuable to know how much longer the search process takes compared to traditional architecture design.

2. The paper mentions that the controller can predict strides and pooling layers, but the results for the 39-layer architecture with pooling (4.47% error) are not as impressive as the 3.65% result. It would be helpful to have more discussion about why including pooling layers didn't yield better results than the 15-layer architecture without pooling.

3. The paper claims the discovered architecture is "the shallowest and perhaps the most inexpensive architecture among the top performing networks" on CIFAR-10. This claim would be stronger with a clear comparison to the computational complexity (e.g., number of parameters, FLOPs) of other top-performing architectures.

4. The paper mentions that the controller can be trained to predict layer types beyond convolutional layers, but this isn't demonstrated in the experiments. Including a brief example of how the method could extend to other layer types would strengthen the claim of general applicability.

Clarifying questions for authors:
1. The paper states that the 3.65% error rate model is "1.05x faster than the previous state-of-the-art model" (DenseNet). Can the authors clarify what exactly "faster" means in this context (e.g., inference time, training time, or both)? Also, how does the computational complexity (number of parameters, FLOPs) compare between the two models?

2. The paper mentions that the controller's search space for the 39-layer architecture was limited by "predicting 13 layers where each layer prediction is a fully connected block of 3 layers." How was this limitation chosen, and how does it affect the search space's representational capacity?

3. In Table 2, the paper shows that the new cell achieves 62.4 perplexity on Penn Treebank, but the previous state-of-the-art was 66.0. The paper claims this is "3.6 perplexity better," but the difference between 66.0 and 62.4 is 3.6, which suggests the previous state-of-the-art was 66.0. However, the paper cites Zaremba et al. (2014) as achieving 66.7, which would make the difference 4.3. Could the authors clarify the exact previous state-of-the-art number and the difference?

4. The paper states that "the cell can also be transferred to the character language modeling task on PTB and achieves a state-of-the-art perplexity of 1.214." However, the previous state-of-the-art for character language modeling on PTB was reported by Kim et al. (2015) as 1.215. How significant is this improvement, and how does it compare to other recent character language modeling approaches?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of deep learning by presenting a novel method for automatically discovering neural network architectures. The results are impressive, the methodology is well-motivated and rigorously validated, and the paper is clearly written. The suggested improvements would further strengthen the paper's impact and clarity.
