

# Review for ICLR_2017

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel method for automatically discovering high-performing neural network architectures through reinforcement learning. The authors propose using a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate architectural descriptions as variable-length strings, which are then evaluated on a validation set. The controller is trained via policy gradients to maximize the expected validation accuracy of the generated architectures. The method achieves state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 (3.65% test error, 1.05x faster than previous SOTA) and Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity, 3.6 better than previous SOTA), demonstrating that neural architectures can be effectively discovered without human intervention. The paper also presents a novel recurrent cell architecture that outperforms LSTM and generalizes to character language modeling tasks. The work is well-motivated, addresses an important challenge in deep learning, and provides strong empirical evidence for the proposed approach.

**2. Decision**

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The key reason is that the authors successfully demonstrate the viability of using reinforcement learning to discover neural architectures that rival or exceed human-designed architectures on standard benchmarks, with results that are scientifically rigorous and well-supported by thorough experiments.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of neural architecture search. The core idea of using a controller RNN to generate variable-length architectural descriptions is well-motivated and logically presented. The authors provide strong empirical evidence across multiple challenging benchmarks (CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank), with results that consistently outperform previous state-of-the-art methods. The thorough ablation studies, including comparisons with random search, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The method's ability to discover architectures that surpass human-designed models on CIFAR-10 and outperform LSTM on PTB is particularly impressive.

The technical approach is sound, with careful attention to implementation details such as skip connections, parallel training, and the use of a baseline function to reduce variance in policy gradient estimates. The authors also provide a clear explanation of how their method can be adapted to discover both convolutional and recurrent architectures, showcasing the method's flexibility.

**4. Additional Feedback**

While the paper is strong overall, there are a few areas where additional detail would further strengthen the contribution:

- The paper mentions conducting a grid search for hyperparameters after identifying the best architecture, but does not provide the specific hyperparameters used (learning rates, dropout rates, etc.). Including these details would greatly aid reproducibility. For example, the paper states they "run a grid search over learning rate, weight initialization, dropout rates and decay epoch" for the PTB experiments, but without the specific values, readers would need to conduct extensive trial-and-error to replicate the results. As noted by Zaremba et al. (2014), providing a base set of hyperparameters is crucial for the field's progress.

- The paper mentions transferring the discovered recurrent cell to character language modeling, but doesn't provide the specific hyperparameters used for this task. Including these details would strengthen the transfer learning claim.

- The paper states that their CIFAR-10 model is "1.05x faster" than the DenseNet model, but doesn't clarify what metric this speedup is measured against (e.g., inference time per sample, training time per epoch). Clarifying this would make the comparison more meaningful.

**5. Clarifying Questions**

To help me better understand the paper and its results, I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1. For the PTB experiments, could you provide the specific hyperparameters used in the grid search (learning rates, dropout rates, weight initialization methods, etc.)? This would greatly facilitate replication of your results.

2. In the CIFAR-10 results, you state that your model is "1.05x faster" than the DenseNet model. Could you clarify what specific metric this speedup refers to (e.g., inference time per image, training time per epoch, etc.)?

3. The paper mentions that your method can discover architectures with skip connections, but doesn't elaborate on how these connections were incorporated into the controller's architecture. Could you provide more details about the attention mechanism used to select skip connections and how it differs from the skip connections in residual networks?

The authors have made a significant contribution to the field of neural architecture search with this work. The method is well-conceived, the results are impressive, and the paper is well-written. With the minor clarifications requested above, this work would be an excellent contribution to ICLR 2017.
