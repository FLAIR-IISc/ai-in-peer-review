- Introduces NAS: an RNN controller trained with reinforcement learning to generate variable-length neural architectures.  
- Demonstrates NAS finds architectures rivaling or surpassing human-designed models on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank.  
- CIFAR-10 result: a 39-layer architecture with 3.65% test error (0.09% improvement) and reported 1.05× speedup.  
- Penn Treebank result: a discovered recurrent cell achieving 62.4 perplexity (better than prior 66.0) and transferring to character-level LM (1.214 perplexity).  
- Paper provides detailed exposition of controller architecture, training method, and search space design with strong empirical validation.  
- Recommendation: accept — compelling contribution to automated machine learning and neural architecture design.  
- Two main reasons for acceptance: clear empirical superiority over human designs and a well-motivated, rigorously evaluated technical approach.  
- Core insight: representing architectures as variable-length strings makes them amenable to sequence-generation with an RNN controller, advancing over fixed-length hyperparameter searches and less-scalable neuro-evolution.  
- Empirical validation includes ablation studies (e.g., skip connections), random-search controls, and transfer experiments (e.g., GNMT).  
- Feedback 1: paper should report total training time and resource consumption (beyond noting 800 GPUs) to clarify computational efficiency.  
- Feedback 2: include deeper analysis of why NAS architectures perform better (e.g., how discovered skip connections differ from ResNet).  
- Feedback 3: explore how patterns like rectangular filters and larger top-layer filters emerge during search to provide behavioral insight.  
- Clarifying Q1: clarify which model (e.g., DenseNet) the 3.65% vs 3.74% comparison refers to, given inconsistent DenseNet numbers.  
- Clarifying Q2: clarify whether the 62.4 perplexity is comparable in computational cost to prior state-of-the-art (e.g., number of cell runs per time step).  
- Clarifying Q3: specify whether the reported 1.05× speedup is measured for inference, training, or both, and how many GPUs were used for that comparison.
