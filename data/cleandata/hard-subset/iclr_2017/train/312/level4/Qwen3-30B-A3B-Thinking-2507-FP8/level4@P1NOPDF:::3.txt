

This paper proposes an innovative approach using reinforcement learning (RL) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to automatically design network architectures for specific tasks. The core idea is compelling, and the experimental results on two benchmark datasets demonstrate the method's technical soundness.  

The paper's key strengths include:  
1. The novel use of an RNN to generate network architecture descriptions, coupled with RL for optimizing the RNN’s output, presents a promising direction for automated architecture search.  
2. The generated architectures exhibit structural similarities to human-designed networks, suggesting effective alignment between human expertise and the learned solutions.  

However, two significant limitations warrant attention:  
1. The training process is computationally intensive, requiring substantial time even with significant computational resources, which may hinder practical adoption.  
2. The experiments lack sufficient evidence of generalizability. It would be valuable to evaluate the generated architectures on additional datasets—particularly those involving sequential tasks—to confirm their robustness beyond the tested domains.  

Overall, this is a strong contribution with clear potential. To fully establish its advantages over human-designed models, the authors should conduct additional experiments comparing performance across diverse datasets and tasks. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact.
