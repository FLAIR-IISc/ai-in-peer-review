

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a novel approach for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network controller trained with reinforcement learning. The authors demonstrate that their method can discover architectures that rival or surpass human-designed networks on both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank) tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results in both domains.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The key reasons for this decision are: (1) The paper addresses a significant challenge in deep learning—automatically designing effective neural network architectures—and presents a well-motivated solution that effectively searches variable-length architecture spaces; (2) The empirical results are compelling, with the proposed method achieving 3.65% test error on CIFAR-10 (0.09% better than the previous state-of-the-art) and 62.4 test set perplexity on Penn Treebank (3.6 perplexity better than the previous state-of-the-art).

The paper's technical contributions are well-supported by thorough experiments across two distinct domains. The methodology is clearly explained, with the controller RNN generating architectural descriptions as sequences of tokens, trained via policy gradient to maximize validation accuracy. The results on CIFAR-10 demonstrate the method's ability to discover efficient architectures with skip connections, while the Penn Treebank experiments show the approach can design novel recurrent cells that outperform LSTMs. The additional transfer learning results to character language modeling and machine translation tasks further strengthen the paper's claims about the generality of the discovered architectures.

To enhance the paper, I recommend providing more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to human-designed architectures, including a breakdown of the total training time versus the time required to evaluate the discovered architecture. Additionally, including a more comprehensive comparison of the discovered architectures with recent human-designed networks (beyond just the state-of-the-art results) would help readers better understand the nature of the improvements.

I would appreciate clarification on the following points to strengthen my confidence in the assessment:
1. Could you provide more details about the computational resources required for the architecture search process, particularly how the distributed training setup affected the search efficiency?
2. The paper mentions that the discovered CIFAR-10 architecture is "1.05x faster" than the DenseNet model. Could you clarify what specific metric this speed refers to (e.g., inference time, training time, or throughput) and under what hardware conditions this measurement was taken?
3. The transfer learning results on character language modeling show promising results, but the paper doesn't provide a comparison to other state-of-the-art character language models. Could you include a brief comparison to recent human-designed character-level models to better contextualize the significance of these results?
