

**Summary**  
This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel framework for automatically discovering high-performing neural network architectures. The authors propose using a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller to generate variable-length architectural descriptions, trained via reinforcement learning to maximize validation set accuracy. The method successfully designs architectures that rival or surpass human-engineered models on both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank) benchmarks. On CIFAR-10, NAS discovers a 39-layer architecture achieving 3.65% test error (0.09% better than the previous state-of-the-art), while being 1.05× faster. On Penn Treebank, it designs a novel recurrent cell achieving 62.4 test perplexity (3.6 better than prior work), which also transfers effectively to character-level language modeling (1.214 perplexity). The paper provides a thorough technical exposition of the controller architecture, training methodology, and search space design, with strong empirical validation across multiple experiments.

**Decision**  
I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a compelling contribution to automated machine learning, addressing a significant challenge in neural network design. Two key reasons support this decision: (1) The proposed method demonstrates clear empirical superiority over human-designed architectures on standard benchmarks, and (2) The technical approach is well-motivated, rigorously evaluated, and properly situated within the literature on hyperparameter optimization and neural architecture search.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper's core contribution—using reinforcement learning to search for neural architectures—addresses a critical bottleneck in deep learning: the manual, time-intensive process of designing effective network structures. The authors effectively justify their approach by noting that neural network architectures can be represented as variable-length strings, making them amenable to sequence generation with an RNN controller. This insight is well-placed within the literature, with careful contrast to prior work on hyperparameter optimization (which typically searches fixed-length spaces) and neuro-evolution (which is less scalable). The method's flexibility in handling variable-length architectures represents a meaningful advance over existing approaches.

The empirical validation is thorough and convincing. The CIFAR-10 results demonstrate that NAS can discover architectures that outperform human-designed models while being more efficient (3.65% error vs. 3.74%, with 1.05× speedup). The Penn Treebank results show similar gains in language modeling, with the new cell achieving state-of-the-art performance (62.4 perplexity) and generalizing effectively to character-level modeling (1.214 perplexity). The ablation studies (e.g., comparing architectures with and without skip connections, and control experiments with random search) provide strong evidence for the method's robustness. The authors also demonstrate transfer learning to a neural machine translation task (GNMT), showing that their cell can improve performance without architectural changes.

**Additional Feedback**  
While the paper is strong, these suggestions would further enhance it:

1. **Computational Efficiency**: The review mentions distributed training with 800 GPUs, but the paper lacks details on total training time and resource consumption. Including a comparison of training time versus model quality would help readers understand the practical trade-offs of NAS.

2. **Architecture Analysis**: The paper identifies architectural differences between human-designed and NAS-generated models (e.g., preference for rectangular filters and skip connections), but a more detailed analysis of *why* these architectures perform better would strengthen the contribution. For instance, how do the discovered skip connections differ from those in ResNet?

3. **Search Space Exploration**: The paper notes that NAS discovers architectures with "many rectangular filters" and "larger filters at top layers." A brief exploration of how these patterns emerge during training would provide deeper insights into the method's behavior.

**Clarifying Questions**  
To help me fully assess the work, please address these points:

1. The paper states that the NAS model "achieves a test error rate of 3.65, which is 0.09 percent better and 1.05x faster than the previous state-of-the-art." Could you clarify whether this comparison is against the DenseNet model (which achieves 3.74% error) or another model? The paper mentions DenseNet as achieving 3.46%, which appears inconsistent with the 3.74% figure.

2. In the Penn Treebank experiments, the NAS cell achieves 62.4 perplexity, while the previous state-of-the-art (Zaremba et al., 2014) reports 66.0. However, the paper notes that "the previous best network requires running a cell 10 times per time step." Could you clarify whether the 62.4 result is comparable in computational cost to the previous state-of-the-art?

3. The paper mentions "a 1.05x faster" model on CIFAR-10. Is this speedup measured in terms of inference time, training time, or both? If training time, how many GPUs were used to make this comparison?
