

# Review for ICLR 2017

## Summary
This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel approach for automatically designing neural network architectures using reinforcement learning. The authors propose a controller RNN that generates variable-length architectural descriptions, with the validation accuracy of the resulting "child" network serving as the reward signal for training the controller. The method is evaluated on two challenging benchmarks: CIFAR-10 image classification and Penn Treebank language modeling. On CIFAR-10, NAS discovers an architecture achieving 3.65% test error (0.09% better than the previous state-of-the-art) while being 1.05x faster. On Penn Treebank, NAS discovers a recurrent cell achieving 62.4 perplexity (3.6 better than the previous state-of-the-art), which also transfers effectively to character-level language modeling.

## Decision
Accept. This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of deep learning by providing a novel, well-motivated method for automatically discovering high-performing neural network architectures without human design. The approach is scientifically rigorous, with strong empirical results on two major benchmarks.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses a critical problem in deep learning: the difficulty of designing effective neural network architectures, which has previously required significant expert knowledge. The authors provide a compelling motivation by recognizing that neural network structures can be represented as variable-length strings, making them amenable to sequence generation techniques. The use of reinforcement learning to optimize the controller is well-justified and properly positioned within the literature.

The method is scientifically rigorous and well-supported by thorough empirical validation. The authors provide detailed ablation studies showing how the controller learns to generate better architectures over time. Their comparison against random search (Figure 6) demonstrates a clear advantage of their approach. The results on CIFAR-10 (3.65% test error) and Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity) are state-of-the-art, with careful attention to the computational complexity of the discovered architectures (e.g., the CIFAR-10 model is 1.05x faster than the previous best model).

The paper's technical contributions are substantial, particularly the incorporation of skip connections and the extension to recurrent cell architectures. The method's flexibility in searching variable-length architectures represents a significant advance over previous hyperparameter optimization approaches, which typically operate within fixed-length spaces.

## Additional Feedback
While the paper is well-written and clearly structured, the following points would further strengthen it:

1. The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of the computational requirements. How many GPU hours were required to train the controller and discover the final architecture? This information would help assess the practicality of the approach for researchers with limited computational resources.

2. The paper mentions that the method can generate architectures without human bootstrapping, but it would be valuable to see a direct comparison between architectures generated with and without human-provided initial examples. This would clarify the true value of the method versus human-informed search.

3. The discussion of limitations would be strengthened by addressing potential constraints of the approach. For instance, how well would the method generalize to other domains beyond image classification and language modeling? What are the computational bottlenecks in scaling to larger datasets or more complex architectures?

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that the CIFAR-10 model is 1.05x faster than the previous state-of-the-art. Could the authors clarify whether this speedup is measured on the same hardware, and provide more details about the computational complexity (e.g., FLOPs) of the discovered architecture compared to the previous best?

2. The paper mentions that the method discovered an architecture with 3.65% test error, but it also notes that the DenseNet model achieving 3.46% error rate (Huang et al., 2016b) used 1x1 convolutions to reduce parameters. Could the authors provide a fairer comparison that accounts for this architectural difference?

3. The paper discusses the use of skip connections in the controller's architecture generation, but it's not entirely clear how the controller decides when to add skip connections versus other architectural elements. Could the authors elaborate on the decision-making process for skip connections in the main text?

This paper presents a compelling and novel approach to an important problem in deep learning. The methodology is well-motivated, the results are scientifically rigorous, and the paper is clearly written. I believe this work represents a significant contribution to the field and deserves acceptance at ICLR 2017.
