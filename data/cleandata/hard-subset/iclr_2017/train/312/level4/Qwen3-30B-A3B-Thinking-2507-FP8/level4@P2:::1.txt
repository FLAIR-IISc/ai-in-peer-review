

The paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a reinforcement learning-based controller. The approach uses a recurrent neural network to generate variable-length architectural descriptions, which are then evaluated on a validation set. The controller is trained to maximize the expected accuracy of the generated architectures, enabling it to discover novel network designs that rival or surpass human-engineered architectures. On CIFAR-10, NAS achieves a test error rate of 3.65% (0.09% better than previous state-of-the-art), while on Penn Treebank, it discovers a novel recurrent cell achieving 62.4 perplexity (3.6 better than previous best), with additional results showing strong transferability to character language modeling.

I recommend acceptance with the following key reasons: (1) The paper addresses a significant challenge in deep learning—automating architecture design—which has traditionally required substantial human expertise and time; (2) The method demonstrates strong empirical results across multiple challenging benchmarks, showing clear advantages over existing approaches.

The paper provides compelling evidence for its claims. For CIFAR-10, NAS discovers an architecture that achieves state-of-the-art performance (3.65% test error) while being 1.05x faster than the previous best model. The authors demonstrate that their controller can learn to generate architectures with skip connections and variable layer structures, which significantly improves performance compared to architectures without these features. For Penn Treebank, the discovered recurrent cell outperforms standard LSTM cells and other state-of-the-art approaches, with the best model achieving 62.4 perplexity. The transfer learning results on character language modeling further validate the generality of the discovered architectures. The ablation studies (including control experiments with expanded search spaces and comparisons against random search) provide strong evidence that the approach is robust and effective.

The paper is well-motivated and properly situated within the literature. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from related work in hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis. The method's ability to search variable-length architecture spaces represents a meaningful advancement over previous fixed-length search approaches. The technical description of the controller's operation, the policy gradient training procedure, and the extensions for skip connections and recurrent architectures are presented clearly and coherently.

To further improve the paper, I recommend the following:
1. Provide more detailed hyperparameter specifications for both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank experiments, including exact values for dropout types (recurrent, embedding), weight initialization methods, learning rate schedules, and weight decay values. While the paper mentions "grid search" over these parameters, the specific configurations used for the final reported results are not provided. This information is crucial for reproducibility and would align with standard practices in the field (as demonstrated in works like Zaremba et al., 2014).

2. Clarify the computational cost of the approach. While the paper acknowledges that the method is computationally intensive, it would be helpful to quantify this more precisely (e.g., how many GPU-hours were required to discover the final architectures) and discuss potential paths toward reducing this cost.

3. Include a more detailed analysis of why the discovered architectures perform well. For example, what specific architectural features (beyond skip connections) contribute most significantly to the performance gains? A brief qualitative analysis of the discovered architectures would strengthen the paper's contribution.

4. Consider adding a brief comparison to the recent work by Zoph and Le (2017) on neural architecture search, which was published in the same year and addresses a similar problem with a different approach. While this is not strictly necessary for acceptance, it would help situate the paper within the broader context of current research.

To clarify my understanding of the methodology, I would like to ask the authors the following questions:

1. In the Penn Treebank experiments, you mention using "embedding dropout and recurrent dropout techniques" but don't specify which exact dropout variants were used. Could you clarify whether you used the exact techniques described in Zaremba et al. (2014) or modified versions, and provide the specific dropout rates used?

2. In the CIFAR-10 experiments, you mention "a grid search over learning rate, weight decay, batchnorm epsilon and what epoch to decay the learning rate." Could you provide the specific values tested in this grid search for the final reported model?

3. The paper states that the controller RNN was trained with a schedule increasing the number of layers in the child networks. Could you clarify how this schedule was determined and whether it was optimized or simply set to a fixed progression?

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating that neural networks can effectively automate the architecture design process, with results that surpass human-engineered architectures on standard benchmarks. The technical approach is well-justified, the experiments are thorough, and the results are impressive. With the minor clarifications requested above, the paper would make an excellent contribution to ICLR 2017.
