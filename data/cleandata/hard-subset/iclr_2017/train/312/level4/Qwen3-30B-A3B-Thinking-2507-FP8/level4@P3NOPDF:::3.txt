

This paper introduces a novel approach leveraging reinforcement learning (RL) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to automatically design network architectures for specific tasks. The core idea—using an RNN to generate architectural descriptions and RL to optimize the RNN’s policy—is conceptually compelling. Experimental results on two benchmark datasets demonstrate robust performance, suggesting the method’s viability for generating effective architectures.  

**Strengths:**  
1. **Innovative Methodology:** The integration of RNNs to model architecture generation and RL to optimize this process is a significant conceptual advance. It effectively bridges automated neural architecture search (NAS) with sequence-based generation, offering a systematic alternative to manual design.  
2. **Human-Comparable Architectures:** The generated architectures exhibit structural similarities to human-designed networks (e.g., in layer composition and connectivity patterns), indicating strong alignment with established domain expertise. This compatibility is a notable validation of the method’s biological and practical relevance.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Computational Cost:** The training process is highly resource-intensive, requiring extensive computational resources even for modest-scale experiments. The paper lacks a detailed analysis of scalability or comparisons to other NAS methods in terms of time/energy efficiency, which is critical for real-world adoption.  
2. **Limited Generalizability:** The experiments focus narrowly on two datasets, leaving the generality of the approach unverified. Crucially, the authors do not evaluate the generated architectures on *additional* datasets—particularly those with distinct sequential characteristics (e.g., time-series or text data)—to assess transferability. Without such validation, the claim of "human-compatible" design remains partially unproven, as architectures optimized for one task may not generalize to related domains.  

**Recommendation:**  
This work presents a promising direction for automated architecture design, with clear potential to reduce human effort in network engineering. However, the current submission requires substantial strengthening to justify its significance. The authors must address the computational bottleneck through efficiency analyses or comparisons. More critically, they should extend experiments to multiple datasets (ideally including sequential data beyond the two tested) to demonstrate robust generalizability. Without this, the advantage over human-designed models remains speculative.  

**Final Assessment:**  
While the core idea is strong and the results on the tested datasets are credible, the paper’s contribution is currently limited by insufficient validation of scalability and generalizability. With additional experiments addressing these gaps, this work could make a meaningful impact. As presented, it is **borderline for acceptance** but requires major revisions to meet ICLR’s standards for novelty and thoroughness. I recommend *weak acceptance* contingent on the authors providing the requested generalization experiments and computational efficiency analysis.
