- Introduces Neural Architecture Search: a recurrent-controller trained with reinforcement learning to generate variable-length network architectures.  
- Controller outputs architecture strings evaluated on validation sets to provide reward signals for training.  
- Demonstrates effectiveness on CIFAR-10 (3.65% test error, 0.09% better than prior SOTA, 1.05x faster) and Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity), with successful transfer to character modeling (1.214 perplexity).  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason: novel, well-motivated solution to automated architecture design with strong empirical validation.  
- Positions work within hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis literature.  
- Main novelty: using reinforcement learning to search variable-length architecture spaces versus prior fixed-length searches.  
- Experiments are rigorous with separate validation rewards, hyperparameter grid searches, and thorough ablation studies.  
- Results indicate discovered architectures can outperform human-designed models while being more efficient.  
- Claim that the discovered model is “shallowest and most inexpensive” among top performers needs clearer computational comparisons (params, FLOPs).  
- Request more detailed discussion of computational efficiency and concrete training/resource numbers versus human-designed architectures.  
- Ask for explanation why adding pooling in the 39-layer architecture yielded worse results than the 15-layer no-pooling model.  
- Suggest demonstrating controller predictions for non-convolutional layer types to support claims of general applicability.  
- Clarify what “1.05x faster” refers to (inference, training, or both) and compare computational complexity to DenseNet.  
- Clarify the search-space limitation choice for the 39-layer model (predicting 13 blocks of 3 layers) and its effect on representational capacity.  
- Request clarification on reported baseline perplexities (66.0 vs. 66.7) and significance of the 1.214 character-model improvement versus prior work.
