The paper details a methodology for automated architecture search, specifically focusing on refining a controller RNN post-training. The authors report selecting the best RNN cell based on validation perplexity and subsequently optimizing hyperparameters via grid search, followed by evaluating the resulting cell across varying configurations and sizes to enhance capacity. 

To facilitate reproducibility, the inclusion of specific hyperparameters employed during this optimization process – including learning rate, weight initialization schemes, and dropout rates (specifying the type of dropout used, such as recurrent or embedding dropout) – would be highly valuable. As demonstrated by prior work like Zaremba et al. (2014), providing a foundational set of hyperparameters significantly aids future research efforts in the field. This level of detail would also be beneficial for the other experiments presented, such as the character language model.

This work addresses a crucial area within our field: the automation of architecture search. While the current computational demands are substantial, the anticipated advancements in computing technology suggest this trade-off will improve. 

The paper demonstrates the technique’s efficacy across both vision and text tasks, achieving gains on numerous benchmark datasets by exploring architectures beyond conventional RNN and CNN designs. While application to a broader range of datasets would be welcome, the current results convincingly demonstrate that this approach is not only competitive with, but potentially superior to, human-designed architectures. Furthermore, the methodology suggests a path towards dataset-specific architectural tailoring without extensive manual engineering.

Overall, this is a well-written paper presenting compelling results on an important topic. I recommend acceptance.
