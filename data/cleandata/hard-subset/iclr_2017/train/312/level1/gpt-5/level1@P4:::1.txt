Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a framework that uses a controller RNN trained with policy gradient (REINFORCE) to generate variable-length model descriptions (architectures) for neural networks. The controller receives as reward the validation accuracy (or transformed perplexity) of a “child” network trained to convergence, and improves its architecture proposals over time.
- The method supports a rich search space including convolutional architectures with skip connections and branching, and recurrent cell structures parameterized as computation trees that include memory cell connections (ct−1, ct).
- On CIFAR-10, NAS discovers convolutional architectures achieving 3.65% test error, competitive with strong human-designed models and with favorable inference speed relative to a comparable DenseNet baseline.
- On Penn Treebank, NAS finds a recurrent cell that improves perplexity over LSTM-based baselines (down to 62.4) and transfers effectively to character-level PTB (1.214) and to GNMT with a modest BLEU improvement, suggesting generality.
- The method scales via asynchronous distributed training with parameter servers and many replicas, enabling simultaneous training of hundreds of child models.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Clear, general, and well-motivated method for variable-length neural architecture search using reinforcement learning with strong empirical results across image and language tasks; (2) Substantive novelty relative to prior hyperparameter optimization and neuroevolution by enabling end-to-end learning over an expressive architecture space with policy gradient.

Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: Automatically designing neural network architectures is important and challenging. The paper directly targets this by mapping architecture design to sequence generation and optimizing expected validation performance.
- Motivation and placement in literature: The work is well situated against Bayesian optimization, random search, and neuroevolution, identifying their limitations on variable-length, compositional structures. The connection to sequence-to-sequence, BLEU optimization, and meta-learning is appropriate. While closely related concurrent work using RL for architecture search exists, this paper’s formulation with a policy-gradient controller over a large variable-length space and explicit handling of skip connections and recurrent cells is a meaningful advance.
- Technical soundness: The controller is trained with REINFORCE plus an EMA baseline, a standard, correct approach for non-differentiable rewards. The skip-connection selection via content-based sigmoids and the measures to avoid “compilation failures” are clearly specified. Asynchronous distributed updates with many parallel child trainings are consistent with prior large-scale ML systems. The recurrent-cell search formalization is well defined (tree labeling of combination and activation, and placement of ct, ct−1).
- Empirical support: 
  - CIFAR-10: The method explores a substantial search space, selects architectures based on validation, and reports test performance once, avoiding test leakage. The discovered models are competitive (down to 3.65%) and include analysis of architectural properties (e.g., skip connections, filter shapes). Ablations on skip connections (removal hurts; dense hurts slightly) support the learned design. 
  - PTB: Multiple model sizes/configurations are explored with consistent improvements over strong baselines; a control comparison to random search shows clear advantage. Transfer to character PTB and GNMT further supports generality. 
  - The paper reports inference speed comparisons and details substantial parallelization, demonstrating practical feasibility at scale.
- Rigor: The overall methodology is reproducible in principle, and many training details are provided (controller optimizer, rewards, validation split, schedule for depth, distributed setup). The comparisons are mostly fair; final models are tuned via grid search on validation, then tested once.

Areas for improvement (not necessarily decisive for acceptance)
- Compute cost and practicality: The approach relies on very large-scale parallel search (e.g., 800 GPUs), which limits accessibility and raises questions about efficiency. A more explicit accounting of total compute (GPU/CPU days) for each experiment and for the final best model would contextualize the cost-benefit trade-off.
- Comparisons: For CIFAR-10, the SOTA comparison to DenseNet is nuanced (3.74% vs 3.65%, while 3.46% exists with bottlenecks). A clearer, apples-to-apples comparison (e.g., with/without 1x1 bottlenecks; matched parameter counts/FLOPs) would strengthen claims. Discussion of concurrent RL-based architecture search (e.g., Q-learning approaches) would improve positioning.
- Statistical reporting: The paper reports single-run results for final models. Reporting variability across multiple independent searches or seeds, or confidence intervals for top-k models, would improve robustness.
- Search-space and controller design: The method’s performance is dependent on the engineered search space (e.g., allowed kernel sizes, pooling positions, base number in RNN cells). Additional ablations on search-space choices and reward shaping (e.g., cubing accuracy) would clarify sensitivity.
- Recurrent cell specification: While the tree-based formulation is clear, a more formal mapping between the discovered cell and standard gated architectures (e.g., gate-like behavior, gradient flow) and a FLOPs/latency analysis versus LSTM would aid interpretability and fairness.

Questions for the authors
1. Compute budget: What are the total GPU/CPU days used for the CIFAR-10 and PTB searches (including controller training and final model training)? How many controller updates were performed?
2. Sensitivity to reward shaping: How sensitive are results to cubing the validation accuracy (or the specific perplexity transform)? Did linear rewards or alternative baselines change outcomes?
3. Variance: Across multiple independent controller runs with different seeds, how consistent are the best-found architectures and final accuracies/perplexities?
4. Search-space dependence: How do results change if you expand/restrict the CIFAR-10 search space (e.g., include 1x1 bottlenecks, group/depthwise convolutions, different stride/pool placements) or vary the base number in the RNN cell beyond 8?
5. Efficiency: Can you quantify the number of evaluated architectures needed to exceed strong baselines (e.g., first time the controller’s best surpasses a reference model)? Any early stopping heuristics that significantly reduce cost?
6. Fairness of comparisons: For the “1.05x faster” claim vs DenseNet, how is speed measured (FLOPs, wall-clock on same hardware, batch size)? Are parameter counts/FLOPs matched for PTB comparisons to prior SOTA?
7. Regularization and tuning: For PTB, are all baselines reproduced under the same regularization (embedding dropout, variational dropout, tied embeddings) and training pipeline? How much of the final gain is attributable to the discovered cell versus the training regimen?
8. Reproducibility: Will you release the exact search spaces, controller hyperparameters, and code to reconstruct the controller training, not only the final models?

Overall, the paper introduces a general and impactful methodology, demonstrates strong cross-domain results, and is technically sound. The primary limitation is the substantial compute requirement and some missing comparative details; addressing the questions above would further strengthen the work.
