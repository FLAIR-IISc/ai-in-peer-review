1. Summary and contributions: The paper tackles the challenge of automatically designing neural network architectures by framing it as a sequence prediction problem optimized via reinforcement learning. A controller RNN samples variable-length architectural descriptions for “child” networks; their validation accuracy serves as a reward to update the controller with REINFORCE. The method flexibly expresses modern design motifs (skip connections, pooling, batch norm) and scales through a distributed, asynchronous setup. Empirically, on CIFAR-10 it discovers convolutional architectures that rival strong human-designed baselines (down to 3.65% error) with competitive speed, and on Penn Treebank it discovers a recurrent cell outperforming LSTM baselines (test perplexity 62.4) with further gains on character PTB and modest improvements in GNMT without retuning. The paper positions the work relative to hyperparameter optimization, neuroevolution, program induction, and sequence-level RL, and includes control experiments (random search, enlarged search space).

2. Decision: Accept. Key reasons: (i) Clear, well-motivated contribution that expands the architecture search space to variable-length designs with a principled, general controller; (ii) Strong, multi-domain empirical results that substantively support the claims of efficacy and generality.

3. Supporting arguments: The problem is important and timely, and the approach is well grounded in the literature (HPO, meta-learning, seq2seq, and RL for non-differentiable metrics). The empirical validation is substantial: competitive CIFAR-10 results with sensible analyses (effects of skip connections), a PTB cell that beats tuned LSTMs and transfers across tasks, and a random-search comparison showing consistent advantages. The methodology is described with enough detail (search space, reward shaping, distributed training) to assess rigor. Main caveat: the computational budget (hundreds of concurrent workers, 12.8K–15K architectures) is very large, raising concerns about accessibility and fairness versus baselines that may have used fewer resources.

4. Suggestions to improve (helpful, not decisive): 
- Report compute budgets (GPU/CPU hours, energy) alongside accuracy, and normalize speed claims with standardized hardware details.
- Provide variance across seeds for the controller and child retraining; include learning curves of the controller’s reward to show convergence.
- Ablate reward shaping (accuracy^3, EMA baseline), skip-connection attention, and schedule for maximum depth.
- Clarify fairness of post-search hyperparameter tuning relative to baselines.
- Analyze overfitting to the validation set (e.g., re-evaluate top-K architectures on a fresh validation split).
- Discuss sample efficiency and opportunities for early-stopping/partial training during search.

Questions for authors:
- How sensitive are final results to reward shaping (cubing), baseline choice, and controller LR? Any instability modes?
- What are the exact compute budgets for search on CIFAR-10 and PTB, and how do they compare to the strongest baselines?
- How many distinct architectures were retrained to select the final model, and with how many seeds?
- Did you observe transfer of the trained controller to a new dataset/search space (e.g., from CIFAR-10 to SVHN/ImageNet) without retraining?
- How do results change if you restrict post-search hyperparameter tuning, to isolate the contribution of the discovered topology?
