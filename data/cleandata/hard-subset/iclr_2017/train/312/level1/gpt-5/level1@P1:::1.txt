Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a reinforcement learning framework in which a controller RNN generates variable-length descriptions of neural network architectures; the sampled “child” networks are trained and their validation accuracy serves as a reward to update the controller via REINFORCE. This unifies architecture search for both CNNs and RNN cells.
- Technical contributions include: (i) an auto-regressive controller for architecture tokens; (ii) policy gradient training with moving-average baseline; (iii) a set-selection attention mechanism to choose skip connections; (iv) practical mechanisms to handle invalid graphs; (v) large-scale distributed, asynchronous training of many child models in parallel; and (vi) a search space for recurrent cells represented as trees that include gates and memory injections.
- Empirically, NAS discovers: (a) CIFAR-10 CNNs achieving 3.65% test error, competitive with or surpassing strong human-designed models while being 1.05× faster than a comparable DenseNet; and (b) a novel RNN cell that outperforms LSTM on PTB (test perplexity 62.4), transfers to character-level PTB (1.214 bpc), and yields modest gains in GNMT without retuning.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel, general, and well-motivated approach to architecture design that produces state-of-the-art or near-SOTA results on two distinct domains.
- Strong empirical evidence that the method learns nontrivial design patterns (skip connections for CNNs; gating/combination structure for RNNs), with additional transfer results.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem tackled: Automatically discover effective neural architectures (including connectivity and cell structures) from a vast, variable-length search space, reducing expert design effort and potentially improving performance.

2) Motivation and placement: The paper clearly situates itself relative to Bayesian hyperparameter optimization (typically fixed-length, less flexible for structure), neuro-evolution (flexible but often sample-inefficient), and meta-learning/program synthesis. Using an RNN controller with policy gradient is a natural extension of sequence modeling to program-like action spaces, and the work is timely and relevant.

3) Are claims supported?
- Methodology: The REINFORCE formulation is standard and correct; the variance reduction baseline and distributed asynchronous setup are well described. The skip-connection prediction via set-selection attention is reasonable and integrates seamlessly with policy gradient.
- Empirical results: The CIFAR-10 search trained 12,800 models and selected the best on validation; the final model after a small grid search achieves 3.65%—credible and competitive. The PTB search trained 15k models, with significant improvements over strong LSTM baselines and RHN; transfer to character PTB and GNMT strengthens the generality claim. A random search comparison on PTB shows sustained gains over time. The paper provides qualitative insights (e.g., rectangular filters, one-step skips; RNN cell reusing W1*h_{t−1}+W2*x_t paths), supporting that the controller learns meaningful patterns.
- Caveats: Some comparisons involve modest post-hoc changes (adding 40 filters, fixed pooling layers), and fairness vs. DenseNet (use of 1×1 bottlenecks) is noted by the authors. The method requires very large compute (up to 800 GPUs for CIFAR, 400 CPUs for PTB), which may limit accessibility. Nonetheless, the core claims are supported.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Computational budget and efficiency: Please report total compute in GPU-days/CPU-days, wall-clock time, and the controller’s sample efficiency (accuracy vs. number of evaluated architectures) for CIFAR analogous to the PTB random-search plot. A cost-benefit analysis will help readers gauge practicality.
- Fairness and controls on CIFAR: Clarify speed measurement methodology (hardware, batch size, inference vs. training), parameter counts, and whether the 40-filter increase was also allowed for competing baselines. Consider presenting results with matched parameter/FLOP budgets.
- Search space engineering: For the 39-layer CIFAR search, pooling is allowed only at fixed indices (13, 24) and filters are restricted; please discuss how such hand constraints influence outcomes. For RNN cells, the “base=8” tree is a substantial design choice; ablate base sizes and show sensitivity.
- Reward shaping and early stopping: You use max validation accuracy over the last 5 epochs cubed as reward, training children for 50 (CNN) or 35 (RNN) epochs. Provide evidence that this surrogate preserves final ranking (e.g., rank correlation between reward and fully trained accuracy), since misranking could bias learning.
- Variance and reproducibility: Report results over multiple controller runs (mean±std of best-seen architectures). Include confidence intervals for test metrics of selected models. The promise to release code and the NASCell is appreciated; consider also releasing search spaces and logs to enable replication.
- Handling invalid graphs: The padding and input-defaulting strategies are practical; please quantify how often they are triggered and whether they bias the controller (e.g., toward concatenation-heavy topologies).
- Broader generalization: The GNMT improvement (+0.5 BLEU) without tuning is encouraging. If feasible, add results or discussion on other datasets (e.g., CIFAR-100, ImageNet proxy) to demonstrate robustness.

Questions for the authors
1) Compute budget: What are the total GPU/CPU days for the CIFAR and PTB searches, respectively? How does controller performance scale with fewer workers or fewer sampled architectures?
2) CIFAR ranking fidelity: What is the Kendall/Spearman correlation between the reward (short training, cubed accuracy) and the final test accuracy after full training? Did you try alternative reward transformations?
3) Comparison to random search on CIFAR: Do you observe the same performance gap as on PTB? Please provide learning curves comparing NAS vs. random for the CIFAR search.
4) Sensitivity to search space: How do results change if pooling positions are not fixed, or if you allow 1×1 bottlenecks? For RNN cells, how sensitive are results to the set of activation/combination functions?
5) Controller stability: How many independent controller runs were conducted? What is the variance in the best-found architecture’s performance across runs?
6) Invalid-graph handling: What fraction of sampled architectures require padding or default-image inputs? Does removing these heuristics materially change outcomes?
7) GNMT setup: Besides matching computational complexity, were parameter counts and sequence lengths matched? Any qualitative analysis of the learned attention dynamics with the new cell?
8) Practical guidance: For researchers with 10–50 GPUs, what truncated setup (e.g., weight sharing, multi-fidelity evaluation) would you recommend to get useful results?

Overall, this is a compelling and influential paper with solid methodology and strong empirical results that merits acceptance.
