- Proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS): a controller RNN trained with policy gradient to generate variable-length neural architectures, rewarded by child model validation performance.
- Supports a rich, expressive search space including convolutional architectures with skip connections/branching and recurrent cell structures as computation trees including ct−1/ct connections.
- Achieves competitive CIFAR-10 results (3.65% test error) with favorable inference speed versus a DenseNet baseline.
- Finds improved recurrent cells on Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity) that transfer to character PTB and GNMT with modest BLEU gains, indicating generality.
- Scales via asynchronous distributed training with many parallel child model trainings (hundreds of models).
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason 1: Clear, general, well-motivated method for variable-length architecture search using RL with strong cross-domain empirical results.
- Key reason 2: Substantive novelty over prior hyperparameter optimization and neuroevolution by enabling end-to-end policy-gradient learning over an expressive architecture space.
- Problem addressed: Automates the challenging task of neural architecture design by mapping it to sequence generation optimized for validation performance.
- Literature placement: Well situated against Bayesian optimization, random search, neuroevolution, and related RL/seq2seq approaches; highlights meaningful advances over concurrent work.
- Technical soundness: Uses REINFORCE with EMA baseline, clear skip-connection mechanism, handling of compilation failures, and a defined recurrent-cell search formulation.
- Empirical rigor — CIFAR-10: Explores a large search space, avoids test leakage, reports competitive test error and architectural analyses, with ablations on skip connections.
- Empirical rigor — PTB: Explores multiple configurations with consistent gains over strong baselines, includes random-search control and transfers to other tasks.
- Practicality: Reports inference-speed comparisons and large-scale parallelization demonstrating feasibility at scale.
- Reproducibility: Many training and implementation details provided (controller optimizer, rewards, validation split, schedules, distributed setup).
- Area for improvement — Compute cost: Relies on very large-scale compute (e.g., ~800 GPUs); needs explicit GPU/CPU-day accounting for experiments and final training.
- Area for improvement — Comparisons: Requires clearer apples-to-apples comparisons with DenseNet/SOTA variants (bottlenecks, matched params/FLOPs) and discussion of concurrent RL methods.
- Area for improvement — Statistical reporting: Only single-run final results reported; should include variance across runs or confidence intervals/top-k statistics.
- Area for improvement — Search-space sensitivity: Performance depends on engineered search space; additional ablations on search-space choices and reward shaping are needed.
- Area for improvement — Recurrent-cell interpretability: More formal mapping of discovered cells to standard gated architectures and FLOPs/latency comparisons versus LSTM would help.
- Question 1 (compute budget): What are total GPU/CPU days for CIFAR-10 and PTB searches and how many controller updates were performed?
- Question 2 (reward sensitivity): How sensitive are results to reward shaping (e.g., cubing accuracy) versus linear rewards or alternative baselines?
- Question 3 (variance): How consistent are best-found architectures and final metrics across independent controller runs/seeds?
- Question 4 (search-space dependence): How do results change when expanding/restricting CIFAR-10 search space (1x1 bottlenecks, depthwise convs, different pool/stride placements) or varying RNN base number?
- Question 5 (efficiency): How many evaluated architectures are needed to surpass strong baselines, and are there early-stopping heuristics to reduce cost?
- Question 6 (fairness of speed claims): How is the “1.05x faster” claim vs DenseNet measured (FLOPs, wall-clock, hardware, batch size), and are params/FLOPs matched for PTB comparisons?
- Question 7 (regularization/tuning): Were baselines reproduced with the same regularization/training pipeline for PTB, and how much gain comes from the cell vs training regimen?
- Question 8 (reproducibility): Will the exact search spaces, controller hyperparameters, and controller-training code be released to reconstruct the search?
