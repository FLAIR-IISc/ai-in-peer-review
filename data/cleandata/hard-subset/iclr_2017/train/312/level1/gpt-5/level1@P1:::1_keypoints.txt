- Proposed Neural Architecture Search (NAS): an RNN controller samples variable-length architecture descriptions; child networks are trained and their validation accuracy used as REINFORCE rewards to update the controller, covering CNNs and RNN cells.

- Technical contributions: auto-regressive controller, policy-gradient training with moving-average baseline, set-selection attention for skip connections, practical invalid-graph handling, large-scale distributed asynchronous child training, and a tree-based search space for recurrent cells with gates and memory injections.

- Empirical highlights: discovered CIFAR-10 CNN with 3.65% test error (competitive with human designs and faster than a comparable DenseNet) and an RNN cell with PTB test perplexity 62.4 that transfers to character PTB and improves GNMT.

- Decision: Accept.

- Key reasons for acceptance: novel, general, well-motivated approach producing (near-)state-of-the-art results across distinct domains and learning nontrivial design patterns.

- Problem tackled: automatic discovery of effective neural architectures from a vast, variable-length search space to reduce expert effort and potentially improve performance.

- Motivation and placement: positioned relative to Bayesian optimization, neuro-evolution, and meta-learning; using an RNN controller with policy gradient is a natural, timely extension.

- Methodology assessment: REINFORCE formulation, variance-reduction baseline, and skip-connection attention are sound and well-described; distributed setup scalable.

- Empirical rigor: CIFAR search evaluated ~12.8k models and PTB ~15k models; final models and transfer experiments are credible and supported by qualitative design-pattern evidence.

- Caveats: some post-hoc tweaks (e.g., adding 40 filters, fixed pooling) and fairness questions vs. baselines; very large compute requirements (hundreds of GPUs/CPUs) limit accessibility.

- Request to report compute: report total GPU-/CPU-days, wall-clock times, and controller sample-efficiency curves (CIFAR analogous to PTB).

- Fairness and baseline controls: clarify speed measurement methodology, parameter counts, and whether filter increases were allowed for baselines; consider matched-parameter/FLOP comparisons.

- Search-space engineering concern: fixed pooling positions and filter restrictions may bias outcomes; ablate these constraints and RNN base sizes to show sensitivity.

- Reward shaping and early stopping concern: reward is max validation accuracy over last 5 epochs cubed after short training—provide evidence that this surrogate preserves final ranking (rank correlation).

- Variance and reproducibility: report multiple controller runs (mean±std), confidence intervals for selected models, and release search spaces and logs along with code/NASCell.

- Invalid-graph handling: padding and default-input heuristics are practical but should report how often they trigger and whether they bias architectures (e.g., toward concatenation).

- Broader generalization: GNMT gains are encouraging; adding results on CIFAR-100 or ImageNet proxies would strengthen robustness claims.

- Question 1 (compute): report total GPU/CPU days for CIFAR and PTB searches and how controller performance scales with fewer workers/samples.

- Question 2 (ranking fidelity): provide Kendall/Spearman correlation between short-training reward and final test accuracy and any alternative reward transforms tried.

- Question 3 (random search baseline): show NAS vs. random-search learning curves for CIFAR as done for PTB.

- Question 4 (search-space sensitivity): report effects of allowing arbitrary pooling positions, 1×1 bottlenecks, and varying activation/combination options for RNN cells.

- Question 5 (controller stability): how many independent controller runs were performed and what is the variance in best-found architectures across runs.

- Question 6 (invalid-graph stats): what fraction of sampled architectures required padding/default inputs and does removing heuristics change outcomes.

- Question 7 (GNMT details): confirm whether parameter counts and sequence lengths matched, and provide qualitative analysis of learned attention dynamics with the new cell.

- Question 8 (practical guidance): recommend truncated setups (e.g., weight sharing, multi-fidelity evaluation) for researchers with limited resources (10–50 GPUs).
