1. Summary of contributions
The paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method that uses a recurrent “controller” network trained with policy gradients (REINFORCE) to generate variable-length descriptions of neural network architectures. Child models sampled from the controller are trained on the task; their validation accuracy (or perplexity) is used as reward to update the controller, progressively biasing the search toward better designs. The search space covers CNNs with skip connections and various layer choices for CIFAR-10, and a rich, tree-structured recurrent cell space for PTB. The approach yields:
- CIFAR-10: a novel architecture achieving 3.65% test error (with post-hoc filter scaling), competitive with state-of-the-art while being 1.05× faster than a comparable DenseNet.
- PTB: a learned recurrent cell achieving 62.4 test perplexity, surpassing prior SOTA, and transferring to character PTB (1.214 bpc) and GNMT with a modest BLEU gain.
The paper also presents distributed asynchronous training for the controller, an attention-based mechanism to predict skip connections, comparisons to random search, and control experiments enlarging the search space.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) Significant and timely contribution—demonstrates that end-to-end learning can discover competitive architectures across vision and language from scratch, addressing a central challenge in deep learning; (2) Strong empirical results and a generally compelling experimental methodology that supports the core claims.

3. Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled?
The paper targets automatic discovery of neural architectures, overcoming the limitations of hand-designed models and fixed-length hyperparameter searches by enabling generation of variable-length, structured architectures (including skip connections and recurrent cell topologies).

- Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature?
Yes. The paper clearly contrasts NAS with Bayesian optimization/hyperparameter tuning (fixed-length, often needing good seeds) and with neuro-evolution (flexible but typically less scalable). It situates the controller as an autoregressive sequence model akin to seq2seq decoders, justified by the non-differentiable reward setting that aligns with RL work optimizing BLEU. The meta-learning framing is apt. Related work coverage is broad and accurate.

- Do the results support the claims with rigor?
Strengths:
  • CIFAR-10: The controller searches over meaningful architectural choices (filter sizes, channels, strides/pooling, skip connections) and finds models competitive with strong human designs. Ablations illustrate that discovered skip-connection patterns matter (removing them hurts).
  • PTB: The learned cell consistently outperforms LSTM baselines at similar parameter budgets, with careful regularization. Transfer to character modeling and GNMT indicates some generality.
  • Random search baseline: policy gradient outperforms random search both in best-found and top-k averages, supporting the utility of learning the search policy.
  • Engineering: the asynchronous distributed setup is a substantive contribution enabling large-scale search.

Caveats (do not outweigh strengths, but temper the claims):
  • Computational budget: The approach requires massive resources (e.g., 800 concurrent GPUs for CIFAR search; 400 CPUs for PTB). This raises concerns about reproducibility and accessibility for most groups.
  • Validation protocol: The controller trains against a 5k validation split from CIFAR-10 training. While the final test evaluation is performed once on the best validation model, the small validation set could facilitate overfitting by the controller; further safeguards or larger/rotating validation sets would strengthen rigor.
  • Fairness of comparisons: The top CIFAR result (3.65%) arises after post-hoc scaling (“adding 40 more filters”), and DenseNet comparisons differ in architectural constraints (e.g., 1×1 reduction not used here). Clarity on parameter counts/compute for final models would improve fairness.
  • Reward shaping and early stopping: Using the cube of validation accuracy and training child models for a fixed, relatively small number of epochs introduces biases in the surrogate objective; more analysis of sensitivity to these choices would increase confidence.
  • Random search details: The random search baseline is encouraging, but stronger evidence requires matched compute and explicit variance across seeds.

Overall, the empirical evidence is strong enough to support the main claim: learned architecture search can produce state-of-the-art or competitive models in multiple modalities.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Report exact compute budgets: wall-clock, GPU/CPU days, and energy estimates for both CIFAR-10 and PTB searches; include time-to-best-architecture curves.
- Robustness: Provide results across multiple controller seeds to quantify variance in best-found architectures and convergence stability.
- Validation leakage: Consider using a larger or rotating validation split, or cross-validation, and report test performance for multiple top candidates to assess selection bias.
- Fair comparisons: For CIFAR, report parameter counts and FLOPs of the final models and baselines; clarify which improvements are within the searched space versus post-hoc manual tweaks (e.g., adding filters).
- Ablations:
  • Impact of reward shaping (cubing accuracy), baseline choice, and entropy regularization on exploration/exploitation.
  • Effect of curriculum on depth and of enabling strides/pooling on search difficulty and outcomes.
  • In RNN cell search, sensitivity to base number, operator set, and connection rules for c_t and h_t.
- Failure analysis: Quantify the rate of invalid graphs and how padding/auto-connections affect sample efficiency and final quality.
- Broader generalization: Even preliminary experiments on ImageNet (proxy tasks, smaller budgets) would strengthen the claim of generality.

Questions for the authors
- Compute/resources: How many GPU/CPU days were used for each search? How long until the best CIFAR/PTB architecture was first sampled?
- Stability: Over multiple runs with different controller seeds, how variable are the best validation scores and final test metrics?
- Validation: Did you observe overfitting of the controller to the 5k validation split? Have you tried larger/rotating validation sets, and how did that affect outcomes?
- Reward shaping: Why cube the accuracy (or square c(perplexity))? How sensitive are results to this transformation and to the baseline/variance-reduction strategy?
- Random search: Was the random search baseline given exactly the same number of child evaluations and training budget? Can you report distributions of top-k results across seeds?
- CIFAR details: For the 3.65% model, is “adding 40 filters” within the original search space or a post-hoc adjustment? Please report parameter counts/FLOPs and training time versus the compared DenseNets and WRNs.
- RNN cell search: How are connections to c_{t-1} and c_t constrained to avoid pathological graphs? Are parameter counts exactly matched to LSTM baselines, given multiple affine ops inside the cell?
- Transfer: Beyond PTB and GNMT, have you tested the discovered cell on other datasets (e.g., WikiText-2) or tasks? How much tuning was required?

Overall, this is an impactful paper that opens a promising line of work. Despite the heavy compute requirements and some comparison caveats, the novelty and empirical strength merit acceptance.
