

# Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

The paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method that uses a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate neural network architectures and trains this controller using reinforcement learning to maximize the expected validation accuracy of the generated architectures. The authors claim their approach can automatically design novel, high-performing architectures for both image classification (CIFAR-10) and language modeling (Penn Treebank) tasks. Specifically, they report a CIFAR-10 test error rate of 3.65% (0.09% better than the previous state-of-the-art) and a Penn Treebank test set perplexity of 62.4 (3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art). The paper demonstrates that their method can discover architectures that outperform human-designed models and can be transferred to other tasks.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper presents a novel, well-motivated approach with strong empirical results that are scientifically rigorous and properly supported by the experiments.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses a significant problem in deep learning: the manual design of neural network architectures is time-consuming and requires expert knowledge. The authors correctly position their work in relation to existing methods for hyperparameter optimization and neuro-evolution, noting the limitations of fixed-length search spaces and slow search-based methods.

The technical approach is sound and well-motivated. The use of a controller RNN to generate architectural descriptions as sequences is well-justified, and the application of policy gradient methods to train the controller is appropriate for this problem. The paper provides sufficient detail about the controller architecture, search space, and training procedure. The authors demonstrate that their method can discover architectures that outperform human-designed models on two challenging benchmarks, with the CIFAR-10 model achieving a 3.65% error rate (0.09% better than the previous state-of-the-art) and the Penn Treebank model achieving a perplexity of 62.4 (3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art).

The experimental design is rigorous. The authors include control experiments (comparing against random search and expanding the search space) and demonstrate transfer learning to other tasks (character language modeling and machine translation). The results are presented clearly with appropriate comparisons to previous work, and the paper provides sufficient detail about the experimental setup to allow for reproducibility.

The claims are well-supported by the evidence. The paper provides both quantitative results (error rates, perplexity) and qualitative insights (visualizations of the discovered architectures) that demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach. The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their method (e.g., the search space constraints) and provide a clear explanation of their experimental setup.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To further strengthen the paper, the authors might consider:

1. Providing more detailed information about the computational resources required to train the controller, including the total number of GPU hours or days needed to discover the architectures. This would help readers understand the practical feasibility of the approach.

2. Including a more thorough analysis of the discovered architectures (beyond the visualizations), such as comparing the parameter counts and computational complexity of the discovered models with the previous state-of-the-art models.

3. Providing more context about the search space constraints. For example, how many different architectures were evaluated in total, and how does this compare to the size of the search space?

4. Clarifying the exact relationship between the controller's validation set and the final test set, to ensure there's no potential for data leakage.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. In the CIFAR-10 experiments, the paper claims that their model is 1.05x faster than the DenseNet model that achieves 3.74% error rate. However, the DenseNet model uses 1x1 convolutions to reduce parameters, which the authors' model does not. Could the authors provide a fairer comparison by modifying their model to include 1x1 convolutions, or explain why this comparison is still valid despite the architectural differences?

2. For the Penn Treebank experiments, the paper states that the new cell is "more than two times faster" than the previous best network. Could the authors clarify what aspect of the network is being measured (e.g., inference time, training time) and provide more details about the hardware used for the speed comparison?

3. The paper mentions that they use a "held-out validation set" to compute the reward signal. Could the authors clarify whether this validation set was completely independent of the training set and whether they used the same validation set for all controller iterations (to avoid overfitting to the validation set)?
