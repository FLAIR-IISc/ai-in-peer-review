

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method that uses a recurrent neural network (the "controller") trained with reinforcement learning to automatically generate neural network architectures. The controller learns to produce architectures that maximize validation set accuracy, enabling the discovery of novel architectures without human intervention. The authors demonstrate NAS's effectiveness on CIFAR-10 image classification, where it discovers an architecture achieving 3.65% test error (0.09% better than previous state-of-the-art) and 1.05x faster than the best human-designed model. On Penn Treebank language modeling, NAS discovers a novel recurrent cell achieving 62.4 perplexity (3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art), which also transfers well to character language modeling.

Decision: Accept

Key reasons: The paper presents a novel, well-motivated approach to automatic neural architecture design that addresses a significant challenge in deep learning. The empirical results are impressive and well-supported with thorough experiments across two major benchmarks.

Supporting arguments: The method is a natural extension of reinforcement learning to architecture search, with a clear and well-justified approach. The results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are strong and competitive with state-of-the-art methods. The paper provides sufficient detail about the experimental setup, includes important control experiments (e.g., comparing with random search), and demonstrates the practical utility of the discovered architectures through transfer learning. The approach is well-placed in the literature, distinguishing itself from both fixed-space hyperparameter optimization methods and slower neuro-evolution approaches.

Additional feedback: To further strengthen the paper, the authors should provide more details about the computational resources required for the search process (e.g., total GPU hours). A more comprehensive comparison with recent NAS methods would also be valuable. Additionally, a deeper analysis of why the discovered architectures perform well would enhance the paper's contribution.

Questions for authors: 1) How much computational time was required to discover the architectures (e.g., total GPU hours)? 2) How does your method compare to the recently published NAS method by Zoph and Le (2017), which also uses reinforcement learning for architecture search? 3) Can you provide more details about the discovered architectures, particularly what specific architectural features contribute to their success?
