- Introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS): an RNN controller trained with policy gradients to automatically generate neural network architectures evaluated by validation accuracy.
- Demonstrates NAS on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving state-of-the-art results (CIFAR-10 3.65% error; PTB 62.4 perplexity) with transferability to other tasks.
- Decision: Accept.
- Motivation: Addresses limitations of fixed-length hyperparameter search and slow neuro-evolution by offering a flexible, scalable RL-based controller.
- Methodology: Uses REINFORCE with a baseline and a distributed training setup to efficiently optimize the controller.
- Empirical support: Strong results, ablation studies, and comparisons showing NAS outperforms random search and that skip connections and other elements matter.
- Experimental design: Thorough search spaces, training procedures, and control experiments increase credibility of claims.
- Improvement suggestion 1: Provide detailed comparison with existing architectures, clarifying parameter-count and speed differences with DenseNet (1x1 convs).
- Improvement suggestion 2: Add more visualization of discovered architectures beyond the appendix to show novelty and structure.
- Improvement suggestion 3: Move controller architectural details (hidden size, layers) into the main text for clarity.
- Question 1: How does the authors' model speed compare to the better-performing DenseNet (3.46% error) that uses 1x1 convolutions?
- Question 2: Can the authors specify how the discovered architectures differ from ResNet/DenseNet in connectivity patterns and layer types?
- Question 3: How were hidden-unit counts chosen for the two-layer PTB experiments and how do parameter counts compare to similar baselines?
- Overall conclusion: Scientifically rigorous, empirically compelling work that meaningfully advances automated neural architecture design and warrants acceptance.
