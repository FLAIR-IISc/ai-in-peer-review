- Introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS): an RNN controller trained with reinforcement learning to generate neural network architectures.  
- Claims NAS can produce architectures rivaling or exceeding human-designed ones on CIFAR-10 (3.65% error) and Penn Treebank (62.4 perplexity).  
- Argues method is flexible for variable-length architecture spaces and can generate convolutional architectures and recurrent cells.  
- Demonstrates transferability of discovered recurrent cell to character language modeling and machine translation.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Primary reason for rejection: insufficient comparison with other emerging NAS methods (e.g., Zoph & Le 2016), obscuring novelty and relative performance.  
- Secondary reason for rejection: lack of adequate discussion and breakdown of computational cost, undermining practicality claims.  
- Experimental evaluation is interesting but falls short of top-tier expectations due to missing baselines and comparisons.  
- Fails to compare against concurrent NAS work (Zoph & Le 2016), making it impossible to judge advancement over similar approaches.  
- Reports using 800 GPUs in parallel but omits total GPU hours required to find architectures, a critical practicality metric.  
- Claim of “starts from scratch” is misleading because human expertise is still needed to design the search space and training procedures.  
- The “1.05x faster” claim is ambiguous (unspecified whether inference, training, or parameter count) and the DenseNet comparison is not apples-to-apples.  
- Related work section mentions Bayesian optimization and neuro-evolution but does not adequately position the approach relative to these alternatives.  
- Despite claiming flexible search spaces, the controller is constrained to predict only certain operations, limiting the asserted generality.  
- Recommended: add detailed comparisons with other NAS methods (especially 2016 work) to establish novelty and performance.  
- Recommended: provide a clear breakdown of computational costs (total GPU hours) to assess practicality.  
- Recommended: clarify the “1.05x faster” metric and provide fair comparisons (e.g., same parameter counts, FLOPs, or measured inference time).  
- Recommended: include detailed descriptions of search space definitions and controller adaptations for each task, and discuss limitations like scalability and domain transferability.  
- Questions for authors: comparison to Zoph & Le (2016) and whether that work was available during research.  
- Questions for authors: provide total GPU hours to find architectures.  
- Questions for authors: specify metric behind the “1.05x faster” claim and comparisons on parameters/FLOPs/hardware.  
- Questions for authors: detail how the recurrent cell search space and tree-structure constraints were defined.  
- Questions for authors: clarify whether the same search space was used for CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank or if it was customized and how that affected results.
