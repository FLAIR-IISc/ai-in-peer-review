

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel approach for automatically discovering neural network architectures through reinforcement learning. The authors propose using a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate model descriptions of neural networks, which are then evaluated on a validation set. The controller is trained with policy gradient methods to maximize the expected validation accuracy of the generated architectures. The paper demonstrates NAS's effectiveness on two benchmark tasks: image classification on CIFAR-10 and language modeling on Penn Treebank. On CIFAR-10, NAS discovers an architecture achieving 3.65% test error (0.09% better than previous state-of-the-art), while being 1.05x faster than the DenseNet model that achieves 3.74% error. On Penn Treebank, NAS discovers a recurrent cell achieving 62.4 test perplexity (3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art), and this cell transfers to character language modeling and machine translation tasks with state-of-the-art results. The paper provides thorough experiments, ablation studies, and comparisons against random search, demonstrating the method's efficacy and robustness.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural architecture search with a well-motivated approach that is scientifically rigorous and empirically compelling.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution is well-motivated and properly positioned in the literature. The authors clearly identify the limitations of existing approaches (fixed-length hyperparameter optimization and slow neuro-evolution methods) and position their reinforcement learning-based controller as a more flexible and scalable solution. The problem statement is clear: designing neural network architectures remains difficult despite neural networks' success, and NAS offers an automated approach to this problem.

The paper strongly supports its claims through multiple lines of evidence. First, the method is scientifically rigorous: the authors use a standard policy gradient approach (REINFORCE) with a baseline to reduce variance, and they provide a detailed explanation of their distributed training approach for efficiency. Second, the empirical results are compelling: NAS consistently outperforms random search (as shown in Figure 6), and the ablation studies demonstrate the importance of skip connections and other architectural elements. Third, the transfer learning results on character language modeling and machine translation demonstrate the generalizability of the discovered architectures, which is particularly impressive.

The paper's experimental design is thorough. The authors provide detailed search spaces, training procedures, and careful validation. The CIFAR-10 results include multiple ablation studies (with and without stride prediction, with and without pooling layers), and the Penn Treebank results include control experiments adding new functions to the search space. The comparison against random search is particularly convincing, as it shows that NAS's approach is not merely finding good architectures by chance.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To further strengthen the paper, the authors could:

1. Provide a more detailed comparison of their CIFAR-10 architecture with existing architectures, particularly addressing the parameter count difference with DenseNet (which uses 1x1 convolutions). This would clarify whether their speed advantage is due to architecture or parameter count differences.

2. Include more visualization of the discovered architectures (beyond what's in the appendix) to better demonstrate their novelty and structure.

3. Clarify the controller's architectural details (e.g., hidden size, layers) in the main text rather than relegating it to the appendix.

## Questions for the Authors

1. In the CIFAR-10 results, the authors claim their model is 1.05x faster than the DenseNet model that achieves 3.74% error rate. However, the DenseNet model that achieves 3.46% error rate (Huang et al., 2016b) uses 1x1 convolutions to reduce parameters. How does the speed of the authors' model compare to this better-performing DenseNet model?

2. The paper claims the discovered architectures are "novel." Can the authors provide more specific details on how their architectures differ from existing ones (e.g., ResNet, DenseNet), particularly in terms of connectivity patterns and layer types?

3. In the Penn Treebank experiments, the authors mention they use "two layers" with hidden units adjusted to match "medium" baselines. How did they determine the appropriate number of hidden units, and how does this compare to other models with similar parameter counts?

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural architecture search with a well-motivated approach that is scientifically rigorous and empirically compelling. The results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods, and the approach is well-positioned to influence future work in automated machine learning. The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at ICLR 2017.
