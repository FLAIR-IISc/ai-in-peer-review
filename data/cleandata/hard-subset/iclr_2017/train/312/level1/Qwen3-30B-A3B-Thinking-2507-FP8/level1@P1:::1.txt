

# Peer Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces Neural Architecture Search, a novel method for automatically designing neural network architectures using reinforcement learning. The authors propose a controller recurrent neural network that generates architectural descriptions as variable-length strings, which are then evaluated by training the corresponding "child" networks on a validation set. The controller is updated using policy gradients to maximize the expected validation accuracy of the generated architectures. The approach is applied to two domains: convolutional architectures for image recognition (CIFAR-10) and recurrent cell architectures for language modeling (Penn Treebank). The authors demonstrate that their method can discover novel architectures that outperform human-designed ones, achieving a test error rate of 3.65% on CIFAR-10 (0.09% better than the previous best) and a perplexity of 62.4 on Penn Treebank (3.6 better than previous state-of-the-art). The paper also shows transfer learning capabilities, applying the discovered recurrent cell to character language modeling and machine translation tasks.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning by demonstrating that reinforcement learning can effectively search for neural network architectures without human intervention. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the problem is highly relevant and well-motivated, addressing a critical bottleneck in deep learning; (2) the approach is well-placed in the literature with clear differentiation from related work; and (3) the empirical results are scientifically rigorous, with thorough validation across multiple benchmarks and ablation studies.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses a genuinely important problem: neural network architecture design remains a manual, expert-intensive process despite the success of deep learning. The authors correctly identify limitations in existing hyperparameter optimization methods (which search fixed-length spaces) and neuro-evolution approaches (which are slow at scale), positioning their method as a novel solution that can search variable-length architecture spaces efficiently.

The method is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors thoroughly survey related work in hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, program synthesis, and meta-learning, clearly articulating how their approach differs and improves upon these methods. The controller RNN's use of policy gradients to maximize validation accuracy is a natural extension of sequence-to-sequence learning and BLEU optimization in machine translation.

The paper's claims are strongly supported by scientifically rigorous empirical results. The authors provide extensive experimental validation across two challenging benchmarks with multiple ablation studies, control experiments (including comparison with random search), and transfer learning results. The results are presented with appropriate statistical detail (e.g., 12,800 architectures searched for CIFAR-10), and the claimed improvements (0.09% better error rate, 3.6 perplexity better) are consistent across multiple experiments and validation metrics.

The paper's method is not only effective but also generalizable. The authors successfully apply it to both convolutional and recurrent architectures, and demonstrate that the discovered architectures can be transferred to different tasks (character language modeling and machine translation) without extensive re-tuning.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, I suggest the following improvements to further enhance its contribution:

1. The comparison with DenseNet on CIFAR-10 is confusing because the paper states their model is 1.05x faster than "the previous best model that used a similar architectural scheme" (3.74% error rate), but then notes that DenseNet actually achieves 3.46% error rate. Clarify this comparison to avoid confusion.

2. Provide more detailed analysis of what makes the discovered architectures novel. For instance, what specific architectural features differ from existing human-designed architectures (beyond just the performance metrics)?

3. Include more information about the computational cost of the search process (e.g., how many GPU hours were required, how many GPUs were used in parallel), as this is an important consideration for practical adoption.

4. The paper mentions the controller "can design a novel network architecture that rivals the best human-invented architecture," but doesn't explicitly state how the discovered architecture differs from existing ones. A brief architectural analysis would strengthen the claim.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper states the CIFAR-10 model is "1.05x faster than the previous state-of-the-art model that used a similar architectural scheme." Could you clarify which specific model this refers to, given that the DenseNet model achieving 3.74% error rate is different from the one achieving 3.46%?

2. In Figure 8 (Appendix A), the discovered architecture appears to have similarities to LSTM cells. Could you elaborate on what specific architectural features make this cell novel compared to existing recurrent cell designs?

3. The paper mentions that the controller searches a space of approximately 6×10¹⁶ architectures but only evaluates 15,000. How did the authors ensure the controller was not biased toward certain architectures due to the limited search space?

4. For the Penn Treebank results, the paper reports a perplexity of 62.4, but the previous state-of-the-art was 66.0 (as stated in the paper). Could you confirm the exact value of the previous state-of-the-art that your model improves upon?
