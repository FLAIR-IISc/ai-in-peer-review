

# Peer Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method that uses a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate neural network architectures, trained with reinforcement learning to maximize validation set accuracy. The authors claim their approach can design novel architectures that rival or exceed human-invented ones on CIFAR-10 (achieving 3.65% test error) and Penn Treebank (achieving 62.4 perplexity). The method is flexible enough to search variable-length architecture spaces and can generate both convolutional architectures and recurrent cells. The authors also demonstrate transferability of the discovered recurrent cell to character language modeling and machine translation tasks.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The key reasons are: (1) The paper lacks sufficient comparison with other Neural Architecture Search methods that were emerging in 2016, making it difficult to assess the novelty and performance gains; (2) The computational cost of the approach is not adequately discussed, which is crucial for evaluating the practicality of the method.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution is an interesting application of reinforcement learning to architecture search, but the experimental evaluation falls short of what's expected for a top-tier conference. The authors claim their CIFAR-10 model achieves "0.09 percent better" test error than the previous state-of-the-art, but fail to compare with other NAS approaches that were published around the same time. For instance, the concurrent work by Zoph and Le (2016) on NAS using reinforcement learning was also published in 2016 and achieved similar results on CIFAR-10. Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine if the authors' method represents a significant advance.

The paper also fails to adequately address the computational cost of their approach. While they mention using 800 GPUs in parallel, they don't provide the total GPU hours required to find the architectures, which is critical for assessing the practicality of the method. NAS methods are often prohibitively expensive, and without this information, the claim that the method "starts from scratch" without human expertise is misleading—human expertise is still required to design the search space and training procedures.

The paper's claim of being "1.05x faster" than the DenseNet model is also problematic. The authors don't specify whether this refers to inference speed, training speed, or parameter count, making it difficult to interpret. The comparison to DenseNet is further complicated by the fact that DenseNet uses 1x1 convolutions to reduce parameters, which the authors explicitly state they did not do.

The related work section mentions Bayesian optimization and neuro-evolution methods but doesn't position the current approach relative to these alternatives. The authors cite Bergstra & Bengio (2012) and Snoek et al. (2015) as using fixed-length spaces, but don't acknowledge that their own approach also has constraints on the architecture space (e.g., the controller can only predict certain operations).

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, I recommend:

1. Adding a detailed comparison with other NAS methods, especially those published in 2016 (e.g., Zoph and Le, 2016), to establish the novelty and performance gains.

2. Providing a clear breakdown of computational costs (total GPU hours) to help readers assess the practicality of the method.

3. Clarifying the "1.05x faster" claim by specifying the metric used (inference time, training time, FLOPs, etc.) and providing a fair comparison (e.g., same number of parameters).

4. Including more details about how the search space was defined for each task and how the controller was adapted to handle different architecture types.

5. Discussing limitations of the approach, such as scalability to larger datasets or transferability to other domains.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How does your method compare to the concurrent work by Zoph and Le (2016) on NAS using reinforcement learning, which was also published in 2016? Did you have access to this work during your research?

2. Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost of your approach (e.g., total GPU hours required to find the architectures)?

3. What specific metrics were used to claim your model is "1.05x faster" than DenseNet? How does this compare to the number of parameters, FLOPs, or inference time on specific hardware?

4. How did you define the search space for the recurrent cell? What constraints did you place on the tree structure to make the search tractable?

5. Did you consider using the same search space for both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, or did you customize it for each task? How did this customization affect the results?
