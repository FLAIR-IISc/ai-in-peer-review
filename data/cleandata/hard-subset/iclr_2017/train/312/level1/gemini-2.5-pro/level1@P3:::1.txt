### Review of "Neural Architecture Search with Reinforcement Learning"

**1. Summary of Claims/Contributions**

This paper introduces "Neural Architecture Search," a novel method for automatically designing neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (the "controller") to generate a variable-length string that specifies the architecture of a "child" network. This controller is trained using reinforcement learning, specifically the REINFORCE algorithm, where the reward signal is the validation accuracy of the trained child network. This approach effectively frames architecture design as a learning problem, where the controller learns a policy to generate high-performing architectures.

The authors demonstrate the power of this method on two highly competitive benchmarks. For image classification on CIFAR-10, their method discovers a convolutional neural network that achieves a new state-of-the-art test error of 3.65%, outperforming meticulously human-designed architectures like DenseNet while being computationally faster. For language modeling on the Penn Treebank (PTB), the method designs a novel recurrent cell that surpasses the widely-used LSTM cell, achieving a state-of-the-art test perplexity of 62.4. Furthermore, the authors show that this discovered cell generalizes well, achieving state-of-the-art results on character-level language modeling and improving performance when dropped into the Google Neural Machine Translation (GNMT) system.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a landmark paper that introduces a new and powerful paradigm for automating a critical aspect of deep learning research. My decision is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles the highly challenging problem of architecture engineering with a novel and principled approach. By combining a recurrent policy network with reinforcement learning, it moves beyond traditional hyperparameter optimization and neuroevolution, creating a flexible and scalable framework for exploring vast, complex, and variable-length architecture spaces. This work has the potential to fundamentally change how researchers and practitioners develop neural networks.
2.  **Extremely Strong Empirical Validation:** The claims are supported by state-of-the-art results on two very different and difficult tasks. Designing a CNN from scratch that outperforms the best human-engineered models on CIFAR-10 is a remarkable achievement. Even more impressively, discovering a recurrent cell that improves upon the LSTM—a cornerstone of sequence modeling for two decades—is a major contribution and provides compelling evidence for the method's efficacy.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper is well-motivated by the increasing complexity and manual effort involved in modern network design. It correctly positions itself against related work, clearly delineating how it differs from and improves upon hyperparameter optimization methods (which typically handle fixed-length search spaces) and traditional neuroevolution (which has struggled to scale to state-of-the-art deep learning models).

The methodology is sound and well-explained. The use of an RNN controller to auto-regressively generate the architecture description is an elegant formulation. The application of REINFORCE with a moving average baseline is a standard and appropriate choice for optimizing the non-differentiable accuracy reward. The introduction of skip connections via an attention-like mechanism is a clever extension that significantly expands the search space to include modern architectural motifs like those in ResNets. The approach for generating recurrent cells is also innovative, generalizing the problem to that of constructing a computation tree.

The empirical results are outstanding and rigorously presented. The comparison against random search (Figure 6) is a crucial control experiment that clearly demonstrates that the policy gradient approach is learning effectively and is superior to simple uninformed search. The ablation studies on the discovered CIFAR-10 architecture (e.g., removing skip connections) validate that the learned structure is meaningful. The transfer learning experiments for the discovered RNN cell provide strong evidence of its generality, which is a critical attribute for any new fundamental building block. While the computational requirements are enormous (800 GPUs in parallel), this reflects the inherent difficulty of the problem and the ambition of the experiments, rather than a flaw in the method itself. The results justify the expense and demonstrate what is possible at scale.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Computational Cost:** The primary limitation of this method is its immense computational cost. It would be beneficial for the authors to quantify this cost more explicitly (e.g., total GPU-hours required for a search) and to discuss potential avenues for improving efficiency. This would help manage expectations for other researchers looking to adopt this method and could frame an important direction for future work (e.g., via weight sharing, surrogates, or more efficient search strategies).
*   **Analysis of Discovered Architectures:** The paper provides some visualizations and initial analysis of the discovered architectures. This analysis could be deepened. For instance, on CIFAR-10, are there recurring motifs or principles that the controller learns? Does it learn to stack layers of a certain type or size in a predictable way? For the recurrent cell, a more detailed functional comparison to the LSTM and GRU, beyond visualization, could yield valuable insights into *why* the new cell performs better.
*   **Sensitivity to Search Space Design:** The definition of the search space is a crucial, human-specified component of the system. How sensitive are the results to the choices made here (e.g., the set of possible filter sizes, the choice of a "base 8" tree for the RNN cell)? A brief discussion on the importance of designing a good search space would add valuable context.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you provide an estimate of the total compute (e.g., in P100-days) required for the CIFAR-10 and PTB architecture searches?
2.  The reward function for CIFAR-10 is the "maximum validation accuracy of the last 5 epochs cubed," while for PTB it is `c/(validation perplexity)^2`. How sensitive was the REINFORCE training to the specific form of these reward functions? Did you experiment with simpler rewards, like the final validation accuracy itself?
3.  The controller training uses asynchronous updates with `m=8` child replicas for CIFAR-10 but `m=1` for PTB, and parameter updates are accumulated differently. Could you elaborate on the reasons for these different choices? Do they reflect fundamental differences between the search problems?
4.  In designing the RNN cell search space, how did you arrive at the "base 8" tree structure? Did you experiment with simpler structures (e.g., base 2 or 4), and if so, how did that affect the performance of the best-found cells?
