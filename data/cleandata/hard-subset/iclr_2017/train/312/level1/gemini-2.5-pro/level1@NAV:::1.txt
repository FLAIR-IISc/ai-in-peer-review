This paper introduces Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a novel and ambitious approach to automate the design of neural network architectures. The authors propose using a recurrent neural network controller, trained with reinforcement learning, to generate model descriptions as variable-length strings. The reward signal for the controller is the validation accuracy of the generated "child" network, effectively guiding the search towards better-performing architectures over time. The primary contribution is a method that can discover state-of-the-art architectures from scratch on multiple, highly competitive benchmarks. Specifically, the paper demonstrates the ability to generate a novel convolutional network for CIFAR-10 that surpasses human-designed counterparts in accuracy, and a recurrent cell for Penn Treebank that achieves a new state-of-the-art in language modeling.

I strongly recommend **Accept** for this paper. The primary reason is the significance of the results; the method successfully generates novel, state-of-the-art architectures on two distinct and challenging tasks. A second key reason is the novelty of the approach, which elegantly frames architecture design as a reinforcement learning problem, effectively navigating a vast and complex search space.

The empirical evidence presented is compelling. Achieving a 3.65 test error on CIFAR-10 and a 62.4 perplexity on Penn Treebank are top-tier results that place this work at the forefront of the field. The authors support their claims with thorough experimentation, including a crucial comparison against random search, which demonstrates the efficacy of the policy gradient approach. The transfer learning experiments, where the discovered cell improves performance on character-level language modeling and machine translation, further strengthen the paper’s conclusions about the generalizability of the learned architectures. The large-scale experimental setup also lends credibility to the findings.

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical for my decision. While the results are impressive, the paper could benefit from a more direct discussion of the computational cost. A brief analysis of the total GPU hours required for a search would provide valuable context for reproducibility and practical adoption. Additionally, the design of the search space itself seems crucial. Could the authors elaborate on how sensitive the final results are to the choice of operations (e.g., filter sizes, activation functions) included in the search space?

I have a few questions for the authors:
1.  In the REINFORCE algorithm, an exponential moving average of previous rewards is used as a baseline. Have you experimented with other baseline functions, such as a learned value function, and did this have a significant impact on search efficiency?
2.  How stable is the search process? If you run the entire search multiple times with different random seeds, do you consistently find architectures of similar high quality, or is there high variance in the outcome?
