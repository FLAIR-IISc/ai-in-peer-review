### Review of "Neural Architecture Search with Reinforcement Learning"

**1. Summary of Claims/Contributions**

This paper introduces "Neural Architecture Search" (NAS), a novel method for automatically designing neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network, termed the "controller," to generate a string that describes the architecture of a "child" network. This controller is trained using a policy gradient-based reinforcement learning algorithm (REINFORCE), where the reward is the performance (e.g., accuracy) of the trained child network on a validation set. This approach allows the controller to learn over time to generate better-performing architectures.

The authors demonstrate the power and generality of their method on two distinct and highly competitive tasks:
1.  **Image Classification (CIFAR-10):** NAS is used to discover a novel convolutional neural network architecture from scratch. The resulting model achieves a new state-of-the-art test error of 3.65%, surpassing previous human-designed architectures like DenseNet, while also being slightly faster.
2.  **Language Modeling (Penn Treebank):** NAS is used to design a novel recurrent cell. The discovered cell outperforms the widely-used LSTM and other state-of-the-art models, achieving a test perplexity of 62.4. The paper further shows that this new cell generalizes well, achieving state-of-the-art results on a character-level language modeling task and improving performance when integrated into the GNMT machine translation system.

The key contributions are the formulation of architecture design as a learning problem solvable with RL, the demonstration of a flexible framework capable of generating complex, variable-length architectures with features like skip connections, and the achievement of state-of-the-art results on major benchmarks.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is an excellent paper presenting a highly novel and significant contribution to the field. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Novelty and Impact:** The paper proposes a new and powerful paradigm for automating a critical aspect of deep learning research: architecture engineering. By framing architecture generation as a learning problem for a controller network, it moves beyond traditional hyperparameter optimization and opens up a promising new research direction in meta-learning and automated machine learning (AutoML).
2.  **Exceptional Empirical Validation:** The claims are supported by remarkably strong and comprehensive experimental results. Achieving state-of-the-art on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank with a single, general method is a major achievement and provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper is well-motivated, clearly explained, and rigorously evaluated. The problem of architecture design is a well-known bottleneck that relies heavily on expert intuition and trial-and-error. The proposed solution is elegant and powerful.

The method's ability to handle variable-length architectures and complex connectivity patterns (like skip connections) is a significant advantage over prior hyperparameter optimization techniques, which are often limited to fixed-dimensional search spaces. The placement of the work within the literature is thorough, correctly connecting it to neuro-evolution, program synthesis, and RL for non-differentiable objectives.

The empirical results are the standout feature. Not only does the method match human-level performance, but it surpasses it on very challenging benchmarks. The CIFAR-10 result is impressive, as it discovers a novel ConvNet that outperforms a highly-engineered SOTA model. The Penn Treebank results are perhaps even more compelling; designing a novel recurrent cell that beats the venerable LSTM is a non-trivial feat. The subsequent transfer learning experiments, showing the cell's utility in character modeling and machine translation, add significant weight to the claim that the discovery is a genuinely useful and general architectural component.

Finally, the comparison against random search is a crucial control experiment. The results in Figure 6 convincingly demonstrate that the policy gradient method is learning to explore the search space more effectively than random sampling, validating the core learning-based mechanism of the approach.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Computational Cost:** The primary concern with this method is its immense computational expense. The CIFAR-10 experiments required training 800 networks concurrently on 800 GPUs. While the results justify this cost for a proof-of-concept, the paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of this limitation. A section on the total compute budget (e.g., in GPU-days) would provide valuable context for the community and could motivate future work on improving the efficiency of NAS, which will be critical for its wider adoption.
*   **Analysis of Discovered Architectures:** The paper provides some visualizations and high-level descriptions of the discovered architectures. However, a deeper analysis could yield valuable insights. For example, in the CIFAR-10 architecture, are there specific motifs or patterns (beyond skip connections and rectangular filters) that are repeatedly discovered? For the RNN cell, what are the key differences in data flow compared to an LSTM that might explain its superior performance? Such analysis could help human designers learn new architectural principles.
*   **Sensitivity to Search Space:** The design of the search space itself seems to be a crucial, human-driven step. How sensitive is the final performance to the choice of primitives (e.g., filter sizes, activation functions) available to the controller? A brief discussion on this aspect would be helpful for others looking to apply this method.
*   **Reward Function:** The paper uses `accuracy^3` for CIFAR-10 and `c/(perplexity)^2` for PTB as reward signals. While these likely encourage the controller to focus more on high-performing models, some justification for these specific reward shaping choices would strengthen the methodological description.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1.  Could you quantify the total computational cost for the CIFAR-10 and PTB experiments? For instance, how many total GPU-days were required to run the full search process that yielded the final reported models?
2.  The curriculum strategy of incrementally increasing the number of layers during the search is an interesting detail. How critical is this to finding good architectures? Did you experiment with searching directly in the full-depth space, and if so, how did it compare?
3.  For the random search baseline, was it executed for the same number of total architecture evaluations (i.e., 12,800 for CIFAR-10) and over the exact same search space as the RL agent?
4.  How was the number of layers/nodes for the child networks decided? For the CIFAR-10 experiment with pooling, the paper mentions predicting 13 layers where each is a block of 3. Was this structure a fixed constraint, or part of the search space?
