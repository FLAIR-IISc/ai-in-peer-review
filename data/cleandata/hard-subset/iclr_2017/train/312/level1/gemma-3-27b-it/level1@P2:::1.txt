This paper presents a novel approach to neural architecture search (NAS) using a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller trained with reinforcement learning. The core idea – representing architectures as sequences and using policy gradients to optimize the controller – is compelling and addresses a significant challenge in deep learning: the manual and often laborious process of network design. The results are impressive, achieving state-of-the-art or competitive performance on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank.

**Decision: Accept**

The primary reason for acceptance is the compelling novelty and strong empirical results. The paper demonstrates a functional NAS system that discovers architectures competitive with, and in some cases surpassing, human-designed models. The performance gains, particularly on the Penn Treebank dataset, are substantial. A second key reason is the clearly articulated problem and well-motivated approach. The shift from feature engineering to architecture engineering is a critical trend, and automating this process is highly valuable.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Performance:** The reported 3.65% error rate on CIFAR-10 and 62.4 perplexity on Penn Treebank represent significant achievements. The claim of surpassing previous state-of-the-art results is supported by quantitative comparison. The transfer learning results further highlight the generalization capability of the discovered architectures.
* **Novelty of Approach:**  The use of an RNN controller and reinforcement learning to generate network architectures is a unique and promising direction. While related work exists in hyperparameter optimization and neuro-evolution, this approach differs in its ability to search variable-length architecture spaces.
* **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper effectively frames the problem of architecture design as a sequential decision-making process, making it amenable to reinforcement learning. The motivation for automating architecture search is clearly presented.
* **Thorough Experimentation:** The paper includes control experiments (adding more functions to the search space, comparison against random search) that strengthen the validity of the results. The ablation studies demonstrate the impact of different design choices.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors state their intention to release code and models, which will significantly enhance reproducibility and facilitate further research.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Search Space Details:** While the paper describes the search space, providing a more detailed visualization of the possible architectures and constraints would be helpful. For example, a diagram illustrating the skip connection mechanism would aid understanding.
* **Computational Cost:** The paper mentions the use of distributed training, but a more detailed discussion of the computational resources required (e.g., number of GPUs, training time) would be beneficial. A comparison of the computational cost of NAS versus manual architecture design would be valuable.
* **Controller Architecture:**  The controller RNN is described as a two-layer LSTM. Justification for this specific choice, and perhaps exploration of other controller architectures, could be included.
* **Baseline Comparison:** A more comprehensive comparison to existing NAS methods would strengthen the paper. While the related work section mentions some alternatives, a direct comparison on the same datasets would be compelling.
* **Sensitivity Analysis:** A sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameters of the reinforcement learning algorithm (e.g., learning rate, discount factor) would be helpful to assess the robustness of the approach.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost of the NAS process, including the time required to train the controller and evaluate candidate architectures?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of baseline function in the REINFORCE algorithm? Did you experiment with different baseline functions?
3.  Could you elaborate on the criteria used to determine when a child network has “converged,” and how this affects the reward signal?
4.  In the Penn Treebank experiments, how does the discovered cell compare to LSTMs of similar size (number of parameters) when trained *without* the regularization techniques used in your experiments? This would isolate the benefit of the architecture itself.
5.  Are there any observed limitations or failure cases of the NAS system? For example, are there specific types of architectures that the controller struggles to discover?
