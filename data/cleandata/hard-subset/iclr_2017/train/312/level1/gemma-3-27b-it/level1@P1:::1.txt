## Review of Neural Architecture Search

**1. Summary of Claim:**

This paper presents “Neural Architecture Search” (NAS), a method for automatically designing neural network architectures. The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (RNN) as a “controller” to generate architectural descriptions (strings), train the resulting "child" network, and then use reinforcement learning to update the controller based on the child network's validation accuracy. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by achieving state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-10 (image classification) and Penn Treebank (language modeling), including designing a novel recurrent cell that outperforms LSTMs.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept**. This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of automated machine learning. The results are compelling, demonstrating the ability to discover architectures that rival or surpass human-designed ones on challenging datasets.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of NAS by achieving competitive results on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank. Specifically, surpassing previous state-of-the-art performance on both tasks is a strong indicator of the method's potential. The performance gain on PTB (3.6 perplexity improvement) is particularly impressive.
* **Novelty of Approach:** The combination of using an RNN to generate architectures and training it with reinforcement learning is a novel and insightful approach.  It moves beyond the limitations of traditional hyperparameter optimization, allowing for the exploration of variable-length architectural configurations.
* **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the method. The inclusion of diagrams (although referenced, not included in the submitted paper) relating to the controller network, skip connections, and recurrent cell generation is helpful for understanding.
* **Practical Implementation & Release:** The authors have made their code available, which facilitates reproducibility and further research. The inclusion of the discovered RNN cell in TensorFlow is a valuable contribution to the community.
* **Ablation Studies & Control Experiments:** The inclusion of control experiments (adding more functions to the search space, comparison against random search) strengthens the validity of the claims.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **Computational Cost:**  The paper acknowledges the significant computational cost of NAS. While distributed training is used, quantifying the resources needed to achieve these results (e.g., total GPU hours) would be useful for readers considering applying this method.
* **Search Space Exploration:** The paper doesn't provide much detail on how the search space was chosen or justified.  A discussion of potential biases introduced by the chosen search space would be beneficial.  Were any prior architectural insights incorporated into the search space definition?
* **Reward Shaping:** The use of a cubed reward (validation accuracy cubed) feels somewhat arbitrary. A justification for this choice and potentially an analysis of the impact of different reward functions would be interesting.
* **Generalization:** While transfer learning experiments are conducted, further investigation into the generalizability of the learned architectures to other datasets beyond PTB would be valuable.
* **Diagrams:** The references to Figures 3, 4, 5, 7 & 8, are problematic without the figures themselves being available.  These are essential for understanding the intricacies of the method.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Computational Resources:** Could you provide a more precise estimate of the total computational resources (GPU hours, etc.) required to train the controller and discover the architectures presented in the paper?
2.  **Search Space Design:** What considerations went into defining the search space for the convolutional and recurrent architectures?  Were any prior architectural insights or expert knowledge incorporated, and if so, how?
3.  **Reward Function Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to the choice of reward function? Did you experiment with other reward functions (e.g., linear scaling of validation accuracy), and if so, what were the results?
4.  **Controller Architecture Sensitivity:** How does the performance change with different controller RNN architectures (e.g. different number of layers or hidden units)?
5. **Missing Figures:** Could you please provide the referenced figures (3, 4, 5, 7, and 8) to fully understand the method?
