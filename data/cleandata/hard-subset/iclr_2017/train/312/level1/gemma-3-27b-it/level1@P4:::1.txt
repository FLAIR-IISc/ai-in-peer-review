This paper proposes Neural Architecture Search (NAS), a method for automatically designing neural network architectures using a recurrent neural network controller trained with reinforcement learning. The controller generates architecture descriptions, which are then used to build “child” networks that are trained and evaluated. The validation accuracy of the child network serves as the reward signal to update the controller’s policy. The authors demonstrate successful application of NAS to both CIFAR-10 (image classification) and Penn Treebank (language modeling), achieving competitive results compared to human-designed and existing state-of-the-art architectures.

**Reject.** While the results are promising, the paper lacks sufficient detail regarding experimental setup and reproducibility, and the novelty, while present, feels incremental given the existing body of work in neural architecture search and hyperparameter optimization.

The core idea of using an RNN to parameterize and search over architectures is reasonable and well-motivated. The connection to reinforcement learning and policy gradients is clearly explained. The empirical results demonstrate that the proposed method can discover architectures that perform competitively on two distinct tasks. Specifically, the improvement of 0.09% on CIFAR-10, while small, combined with the speed advantage over the previous state-of-the-art, is noteworthy. The success on the Penn Treebank task, surpassing LSTM, is also a significant accomplishment.

However, several aspects raise concerns. First, the experimental details are somewhat vague. While the authors mention the use of distributed training and asynchronous updates, the exact configuration (number of GPUs, parameter server shards, etc.) isn't always clear. This makes it difficult to replicate the results. Second, the paper doesn't adequately address the computational cost of NAS. Training the controller requires training numerous child networks, which can be prohibitively expensive. A more thorough discussion of the computational resources required would be beneficial. Third, the relationship to existing work, particularly in neuroevolution and Bayesian optimization, could be strengthened. While acknowledged, the differences and advantages of NAS over these approaches aren't fully elaborated. Finally, the appendix is referred to but not included in the submitted version, hindering a complete assessment of the architectural details. The claim of state-of-the-art results on character language modeling requires more explicit comparison with recent literature.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Reproducibility:** The authors should provide a more detailed description of the experimental setup, including the exact hardware configuration, software versions, and training hyperparameters. Providing the code would greatly improve reproducibility.
*   **Computational Cost:** A more thorough analysis of the computational cost of NAS is needed. How does the cost scale with the complexity of the search space?
*   **Comparison to Related Work:** A more detailed comparison to neuroevolutionary methods (e.g., NEAT) and Bayesian optimization techniques for architecture search would be helpful. Specifically, discuss the advantages and disadvantages of NAS in terms of scalability, sample efficiency, and expressiveness.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the NAS framework, such as the skip connections and the parameter server architecture.
*   **Appendix Inclusion**: The appendix needs to be included in the submitted version for proper review.
*   **Clarify reward function**: The description of the reward function (accuracy cubed) is a bit abrupt. Explain the rationale behind cubing the accuracy.
*   **Generalization**: Given that the method seems to benefit from the specific details of the datasets, a demonstration on a third dataset might be useful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the total computational cost (in terms of GPU hours) required to run the NAS algorithm for CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank?
2.  How does the performance of NAS change as the size of the search space increases? Are there any strategies to mitigate the curse of dimensionality?
3.  Can you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process for the child networks?
4.  How sensitive is the performance of NAS to the choice of the baseline function in the REINFORCE algorithm?
5.  What is the effect of the learning rate schedule on the training of the controller RNN?
6. How do the discovered architectures generalize to other datasets or tasks? Are there any observed transferability properties?
