## Review of Neural Architecture Search

This paper presents a novel approach to neural architecture search (NAS) leveraging a recurrent neural network (RNN) controller trained with reinforcement learning to generate network descriptions. The method demonstrates compelling results, achieving state-of-the-art performance on both CIFAR-10 image classification and Penn Treebank language modeling tasks. The core idea of framing architecture design as a sequential prediction problem and utilizing policy gradients for optimization is innovative and well-executed.

**Decision: Accept**

The key reasons for acceptance are: 1) The problem addressed – automating neural architecture design – is a significant challenge in deep learning, and this paper offers a promising solution. 2) The empirical results are strong, demonstrating competitive or state-of-the-art performance on benchmark datasets and showcasing the method’s ability to discover novel and effective architectures.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the difficulty of manual neural network architecture engineering. It situates the work appropriately within the context of hyperparameter optimization, neuroevolution, and program synthesis. The choice of using an RNN as the controller is logical given the variable-length nature of architecture specifications. The use of the REINFORCE algorithm is a standard and justifiable approach for optimizing a non-differentiable reward signal. 

The empirical evaluation is thorough. The CIFAR-10 experiments demonstrate a competitive test error rate (3.65%) and a speedup compared to previous state-of-the-art models. The results on Penn Treebank are particularly impressive, with the designed recurrent cell surpassing LSTM and achieving a new state-of-the-art perplexity. The transfer learning experiment on character language modeling further validates the generalizability of the learned cell. The inclusion of control experiments (adding more functions to the search space and comparison to random search) strengthens the claim that the observed performance is not simply due to chance. The ablation studies and detailed results, including architectural visualizations, build confidence in the findings.

The implementation details, while extensive, are clearly presented, and the authors have committed to releasing code and the discovered architectures, fostering reproducibility. The use of distributed training and asynchronous parameter updates demonstrates a practical consideration for scaling the method. The inclusion of skip connections and variable layer types expands the search space, making the approach more flexible and capable of discovering complex architectures.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where clarification or further investigation could enhance it:

* **Reward Shaping:** The use of the maximum validation accuracy cubed as a reward function seems somewhat arbitrary. A justification for this choice, or an exploration of alternative reward functions, would be beneficial. Does the cubic function substantially affect the search process, and how does it compare to, for example, simply using the validation accuracy?
* **Search Space Limitations:** While the authors acknowledge limitations in the search space (e.g., fixed filter sizes), it would be helpful to discuss the potential impact of these limitations on the discovered architectures. Are there known architectural motifs that are currently excluded from the search space that could potentially lead to further improvements?
* **Computational Cost:**  The paper notes the substantial computational resources required for training (800 GPUs concurrently). A more detailed breakdown of the computational cost – in terms of GPU hours – would provide a clearer understanding of the practical feasibility of applying this method to larger and more complex datasets.
* **Visualization of Search Process:** Showing a few examples of how the controller's policy evolves during training—perhaps visualizing the probability distribution over actions at different stages—could provide insights into the learning process.
* **Robustness to Initialization:** How sensitive are the results to the initialization of the controller RNN? Running multiple trials with different random seeds and reporting the variance in performance would strengthen the robustness of the findings.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the reward shaping – how was the cubic function chosen, and what impact did varying this exponent have on the search process and final performance?
2.  What are the primary constraints that currently limit the size and complexity of the search space? What steps would be needed to expand the search space without incurring prohibitive computational costs?
3.  Could you provide a more precise estimate of the total GPU hours required to complete the CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank experiments?
4.  Did you observe any consistent patterns or trends in the architectures discovered by the controller? Were there any surprising architectural choices that the controller made?
5.  How does the performance of the discovered architectures compare to architectures found by other NAS methods (e.g., those based on evolutionary algorithms) when trained using the same resources and hyperparameters?
