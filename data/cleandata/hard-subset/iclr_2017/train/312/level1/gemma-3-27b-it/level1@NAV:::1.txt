## Review of “Neural Architecture Search”

This paper presents a novel approach to neural architecture design, dubbed Neural Architecture Search (NAS). The core idea is to employ a recurrent neural network (RNN) as a controller to generate neural network architectures, then use reinforcement learning to optimize the controller based on the performance of the generated architectures on a validation set. The authors demonstrate impressive results on both CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, achieving state-of-the-art performance in some cases, and demonstrating the ability to discover architectures competitive with or exceeding human-designed counterparts. This is a compelling contribution with potentially significant impact on the field.

**Decision: Accept**

The strength of this paper lies in its innovative approach and strong empirical validation. It successfully addresses the challenge of automated neural architecture design, a traditionally labor-intensive and expert-driven process. The results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank, particularly outperforming established architectures like LSTM, strongly support the claims made in the paper.

The approach is well-motivated, building upon the growing body of work on hyperparameter optimization, neuro-evolution, and program synthesis. The paper clearly situates itself within the existing literature, highlighting the limitations of prior methods and articulating how NAS offers a more flexible and scalable solution. The detailed description of the method, including the training process and the use of techniques like skip connections and distributed training, allows for a clear understanding of the implementation. The experimental setup is rigorous, with careful attention to detail and clear reporting of results. The ablation studies and control experiments further strengthen the validity of the findings. 

However, the paper would benefit from greater clarity regarding the computational cost of this approach. While the distributed training helps, the resource requirements are still likely substantial. Also, the specifics of the search space could be clarified further - what constraints are in place, and how does the choice of search space affect the results? Finally, more detail on the sensitivity of the method to hyperparameter settings of the *controller* RNN itself would be valuable.  

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What is the approximate total compute time (e.g., GPU hours) required to run the NAS algorithm for a given dataset like CIFAR-10?
*   How does the performance of the NAS-generated architectures vary with different constraints placed on the search space (e.g., limiting the maximum number of layers or filter sizes)?
*   Did you perform any sensitivity analysis on the hyperparameters of the controller RNN (learning rate, hidden unit size, etc.) and how did variations in these parameters impact the search process?
*   Could you provide more insights into *why* the NAS-discovered architectures perform well? Are there any common patterns or characteristics that emerge from the search process?
