## Review of "Neural Architecture Search"

This paper presents a novel approach to neural architecture design, termed Neural Architecture Search (NAS). The core idea is to use a recurrent neural network (RNN) as a “controller” to generate descriptions of neural network architectures, and then train this controller using reinforcement learning to maximize the accuracy of the generated architectures on a validation set. The authors demonstrate impressive results on both CIFAR-10 (image classification) and Penn Treebank (language modeling), achieving state-of-the-art or competitive performance with architectures designed automatically, surpassing even human-designed models in some cases. The work is well-motivated, addressing the significant challenge of manual neural network architecture engineering.

**Decision: Accept**

The primary reason for acceptance is the strong empirical results and the novelty of the approach. The paper demonstrates a significant advance in automating neural architecture design, achieving compelling performance on established benchmarks. The second key reason is the clear and well-structured presentation of the method, making it relatively easy to understand the core concepts and experimental setup.

The paper convincingly supports its claims with thorough experimentation. The results on CIFAR-10 and Penn Treebank are statistically significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the NAS approach. The ablation studies and control experiments (comparing against random search) further strengthen the validity of the findings. The authors provide detailed descriptions of the experimental setup, including hyperparameters and training procedures, allowing for reproducibility. The inclusion of the discovered architectures in TensorFlow is a valuable contribution to the community.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, further clarification on several aspects would be beneficial. Specifically, the computational cost of NAS is substantial. A more detailed discussion of the resources required (e.g., GPU hours) for the experiments would be helpful for assessing the practical applicability of the method. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the generated architectures. What common patterns or principles emerge from the architectures discovered by the controller? Are there any surprising or counterintuitive design choices? 

I also wonder about the sensitivity of the results to the initial conditions of the controller RNN. How robust is the method to different random seeds? Finally, the paper mentions the potential for extending the search space to include more layer types and hyperparameters. A brief discussion of the challenges associated with scaling the search space would be valuable.
