

This paper introduces a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments that leverages the temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams to enable supervised learning without extraneous supervision. The authors present a framework where the agent learns to predict future measurements based on current sensory input, measurements, and goal specifications. This approach allows training without fixed goals and enables effective pursuit of dynamically changing goals at test time. The method demonstrates strong empirical performance in the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL approaches, particularly on complex tasks. The model trained with this approach won the Full Deathmatch track in previously unseen environments, outperforming the second-best submission by over 50%.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The key reasons for this decision are: (1) the paper presents a well-motivated and clearly articulated approach that effectively addresses important challenges in sensorimotor control, and (2) the experimental results are robust, scientifically rigorous, and demonstrate significant empirical advantages over state-of-the-art methods in challenging 3D environments.

The paper makes a compelling case for the value of vectorial measurements over scalar rewards in reinforcement learning. The authors effectively position their work within the literature, acknowledging connections to universal value function approximators (Schaul et al., 2015) while presenting a specific formulation that handles realistic sensory and measurement streams. The experimental design is thorough, with multiple scenarios of increasing complexity, generalization across environments, and ablation studies that clearly demonstrate the benefits of vectorial measurements and multi-temporal prediction. The competition win provides particularly strong validation of the approach's effectiveness in real-world conditions.

The paper's contribution is not merely an incremental improvement but a principled shift in how we conceptualize sensorimotor learning. By recognizing the rich supervisory signal in the temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams, the authors have created a framework that enables learning from raw experience without the need for reward engineering. This has significant implications for building more generalizable and adaptable agents.

For further improvement, I suggest the authors clarify the relationship between their approach and previous work on universal value functions. While they cite Schaul et al. (2015), it would be helpful to more explicitly state how their specific formulation differs from and builds upon this prior work. Additionally, the abstract and introduction could more precisely articulate the specific technical contribution of distilling these ideas into a practical framework for 3D navigation tasks.

The authors should also address the potential limitations of their approach. Specifically, they should discuss under what circumstances the model might struggle with goal generalization, and how they might mitigate catastrophic forgetting given the on-policy nature of their training method. The current paper doesn't sufficiently explore failure modes or the conditions under which the approach might not generalize well.

I would also be interested to learn whether the authors have considered applying their framework to other domains beyond Doom, such as Atari games. The paper mentions that their approach could be applied to other environments, but the experiments are limited to the Doom platform. Results on Atari would strengthen the generalizability claim of the approach.

To further validate the empirical claims, I recommend including more detailed analysis of the training dynamics, particularly regarding how the model's performance evolves during training on the more complex Battle scenarios. The paper presents final performance metrics but doesn't provide sufficient detail on the learning curves or convergence behavior.

Finally, I would appreciate clarification on the relationship between the measurement stream and sensory input. The paper distinguishes between these two components, but the boundary between them seems somewhat arbitrary. What criteria determine which sensory inputs become measurements? How might this distinction change in more complex environments?

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of sensorimotor learning, demonstrating that supervised learning techniques can be effectively applied to complex 3D environments with raw sensory input. The clear motivation, rigorous experiments, and compelling results make this an important contribution that advances the state of the art in immersive environment control.
