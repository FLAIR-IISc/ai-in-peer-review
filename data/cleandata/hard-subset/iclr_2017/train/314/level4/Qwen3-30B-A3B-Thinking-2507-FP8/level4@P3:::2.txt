

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments by leveraging the cotemporal structure of high-dimensional sensory streams and low-dimensional measurement streams. The authors frame sensorimotor learning as a supervised prediction problem: given current sensory input, measurements, and a goal (expressed as a vector of desired future measurement changes), the model predicts how different actions affect future measurements. This eliminates the need for extrinsic rewards or fixed goals during training, enabling goal-agnostic learning that generalizes to dynamically specified goals at test time. The method achieves state-of-the-art results in the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming sophisticated baselines (including a complex model with additional supervision) by over 50% in the Full Deathmatch track, while operating solely on raw visual input and intrinsic measurements like health/ammo.

**Decision: Accept**  
The paper makes a compelling empirical contribution to the problem of sensorimotor control in complex environments. Two key reasons support this decision: (1) the approach demonstrates significant, robust performance gains over strong baselines in challenging 3D navigation tasks, validated through extensive experiments and a real-world competition win; (2) the core insight—that dense vectorial measurements provide superior supervision to sparse scalar rewards— is well-motivated, rigorously tested, and directly enables the claimed benefits of goal-agnostic training.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper’s motivation is strong: it correctly identifies the limitations of traditional RL (sparse rewards, goal rigidity) and positions its approach within the literature, citing key works on supervised learning for control (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992) and goal-conditioned RL (Schaul et al., 2015). The proposed formulation is novel in its application to raw sensory input in complex 3D environments and its use of *vectorial* measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) as the supervisory signal. Empirically, the results are convincing: in the challenging "Battle" scenarios (D3/D4), the method outperforms DQN, A3C, and DSR by factors of 6x and 2.5x, respectively, and achieves strong generalization across 100 textured environments (Table 2). Crucially, the Visual Doom Competition win—without additional supervision (unlike the runner-up, which used game-engine knowledge)—provides unambiguous, real-world validation of the approach’s effectiveness. The ablation study (Table 4) further supports the hypothesis that vectorial feedback and multi-step prediction are key to the method’s success.

While the empirical case is strong, the paper could clarify two aspects to strengthen its theoretical grounding: (1) the exact relationship between the measurement stream and the underlying environment dynamics (e.g., are measurements sufficient to fully specify the state?); (2) a more detailed discussion of why vectorial feedback outperforms scalar rewards beyond the empirical results (e.g., does it reduce the "credit assignment" problem?). The method’s reliance on experience replay (not pure on-policy training, as incorrectly stated in the draft) avoids catastrophic forgetting during goal-agnostic training, but the paper should explicitly address how the experience buffer’s composition (e.g., fixed size, sampling strategy) prevents forgetting of past goal distributions.

**Additional Feedback**  
To further strengthen the paper, consider the following:  
1. **Clarify the contribution**: The abstract/introduction should explicitly state that the core innovation is *applying supervised future prediction to raw sensory input via vectorial measurements* for goal-agnostic control, rather than presenting it as a "distillation" of existing ideas.  
2. **Expand failure analysis**: The paper demonstrates strong generalization but does not explore *limits* of goal adaptation. For instance, how does performance degrade when test-time goals deviate significantly from the training distribution (e.g., extreme weightings like [0, 0, 100] vs. [0.5, 0.5, 1])? A quantitative analysis here would add depth.  
3. **Broaden empirical scope**: While Doom is compelling, testing on Atari (using ROM states as measurements) would better demonstrate the approach’s generality beyond 3D navigation. Even a single Atari experiment (e.g., Pong) would strengthen the claim of broader applicability.  
4. **Refine architecture description**: The normalization layer in Section 3.2 (Eq. 6) is critical but under-explained. A brief justification (e.g., "ensures action-specific predictions are centered around the expectation") would improve clarity.

**Questions for Authors**  
1. In Table 3, the model trained with goal sampling [-1, 1] (regime c) achieves slightly better generalization than [0, 1] (regime b). Could the authors elaborate on why allowing negative goal weights (e.g., "minimize frags") improves robustness?  
2. The experience buffer size (M) and update frequency (k) are not optimized. How sensitive is performance to these hyperparameters, and what values were selected as default?  
3. During competition training, the authors used D4-tx-L (a larger model on textured mazes). How much of the 50% performance gap over the runner-up (Lample & Chaplot, 2016) is attributable to model capacity vs. the proposed method?  

This work provides a compelling, empirically grounded alternative to reward-based RL for immersive control tasks. The clarity of the core idea, combined with its demonstrable success in a challenging real-world competition, makes it a valuable contribution to the field. With minor refinements to the presentation and analysis, it would be an excellent fit for ICLR.
