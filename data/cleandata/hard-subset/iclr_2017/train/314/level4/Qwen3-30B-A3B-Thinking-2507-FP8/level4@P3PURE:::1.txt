

This paper presents a novel deep reinforcement learning algorithm that achieves strong performance in 3D environments using raw sensory input, demonstrating superior generalization across diverse goals and environments. The approach, which won the Visual Doom AI Competition, leverages additional low-dimensional observations (e.g., ammo, health provided by the game engine) as supervised targets for prediction. Crucially, these predictions are conditioned on both a given goal vector (not learned) and the current action. The optimal action for a state is then determined by selecting the action that maximizes the predicted outcome relative to the goal. This differs fundamentally from successor feature representations, as the learning is fully supervised with no temporal-difference (TD) relationship between current and next-state predictions.

The authors effectively contextualize their work within prior literature on goal-driven function approximation and future state prediction (Section 2). Their key contributions include: (1) employing Monte Carlo estimation instead of TD learning, (2) utilizing low-dimensional "measurements" as supervised targets, (3) introducing parametrized goals, and (4) providing comprehensive empirical comparisons against relevant baselines. Beyond the Visual Doom results, the paper compellingly demonstrates the algorithm's ability to learn generalizable policies that adapt to limited goal changes without further training.

The paper is well-written, with clear exposition and convincing empirical results that will be of significant interest to the community. Two minor points warrant attention: First, the supervised training involves an on-policy approximation (assuming the remainder of the trajectory follows the current policy), yet it learns from a replay buffer containing trajectories generated by prior policy versions; this approximation should be explicitly discussed. Second, the algorithm relies on additional metadata specifying which sensory components to predict—metadata not required by comparison methods. The limitations of this dependency (e.g., potential failure in environments lacking such measurements) should be acknowledged more clearly.
