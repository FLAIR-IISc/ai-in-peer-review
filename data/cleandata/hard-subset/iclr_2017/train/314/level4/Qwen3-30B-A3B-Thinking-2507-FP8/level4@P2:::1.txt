

# Review

**Summary of Contribution**

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments by leveraging the cotemporal structure of sensory and measurement streams. The key innovation is reframing sensorimotor control as supervised learning, where the model predicts future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) conditioned on current sensory input, action, and goal. This approach enables training without extraneous supervision, supports learning from raw sensory input in complex environments, and allows for flexible goal specification at test time. The authors demonstrate that their method (termed "direct future prediction" or DFP) outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL approaches in challenging Doom-based scenarios, with a model trained using this approach winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition by more than 50% over the second-place submission. The paper also establishes that vectorial measurements provide a significantly richer training signal than scalar rewards, and that models trained with this approach generalize effectively across environments and goals.

**Decision**

Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of reinforcement learning by demonstrating that sensorimotor control can be effectively framed as supervised learning when provided with temporally dense, multidimensional supervision. The empirical results are compelling, the approach is theoretically well-motivated, and the work represents a significant advancement over prior methods in complex 3D environments.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's key strength lies in its clear demonstration that the rich temporal structure of measurement streams provides superior supervision compared to traditional sparse scalar rewards. The empirical results are particularly convincing in the challenging Battle and Battle 2 scenarios (D3 and D4), where DFP outperforms A3C by factors of 6 and 2.5, respectively. The paper effectively establishes that the vectorial measurements are crucial to this performance advantage through controlled ablation studies (Table 4). Additionally, the approach's ability to generalize to new goals without retraining (Table 3) demonstrates significant practical value beyond the specific competition setting.

The theoretical motivation is well-grounded in the literature, with clear connections to supervised learning, Monte Carlo methods, and related work on parametrized goals. The paper effectively distinguishes itself from prior work by focusing on direct future prediction (Monte Carlo) rather than temporal difference learning, and by demonstrating that this approach works well in realistic, complex environments with rich sensory input.

**Additional Feedback**

While the paper is strong, I offer the following suggestions for improvement:

1. The paper should more explicitly acknowledge the dependency on providing meaningful measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) that are not always available in general environments. This is a significant limitation compared to pure RL approaches that can work with raw sensory input alone. The authors should discuss scenarios where such measurements might not be available and how this might limit the approach's applicability.

2. The on-policy assumption with experience replay (Section 3.1) should be more thoroughly discussed. The paper uses a replay buffer with experiences generated by prior policy versions, which contradicts the pure on-policy training assumption. The authors should clarify how this affects the training dynamics and whether this could introduce bias.

3. The paper could better emphasize the simplicity of the approach compared to prior work. The winning entry in the Visual Doom competition was simpler than the second-place submission (which used reward shaping and extra supervision), yet outperformed it significantly. This simplicity is a major strength that could be highlighted more prominently.

**Questions for Authors**

1. How would the approach perform in environments where meaningful measurements are not provided by the environment (e.g., only raw visual input)? Could the measurement stream be learned from sensory input, or would this require substantial modifications to the approach?

2. In the ablation study (Table 4), the authors show that predicting multiple measurements significantly improves performance. However, the paper doesn't discuss how these measurements are selected or whether they could be automatically identified from sensory input. Could the approach work with automatically learned measurements rather than manually specified ones?

3. The paper states that the approach "supports training without a fixed goal and pursuing dynamically changing goals at test time." However, it's unclear how the goal vector is specified at test time. Would the user need to provide this goal vector, or could the system learn to infer it from the environment?
