

The paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments by leveraging the cotemporal structure of high-dimensional sensory streams and lower-dimensional measurement streams. It frames sensorimotor learning as supervised prediction of future measurements conditioned on current observations, actions, and goal vectors, eliminating the need for scalar rewards or extraneous supervision. The authors demonstrate that this method achieves state-of-the-art performance in complex 3D simulations, outperforms prior deep RL approaches by significant margins (including a 50%+ win in the Visual Doom AI Competition), and enables generalization across environments and dynamically specified goals without retraining.

Accept. The paper makes a compelling case for using vectorial measurements as a dense supervisory signal to replace sparse scalar rewards, and the empirical validation is exceptionally strong. The method's success in the competitive Visual Doom AI Competition provides decisive evidence of its practical impact.

The contribution is well-motivated by a thorough analysis of the limitations of traditional RL (sparse rewards, fixed goals) and the advantages of dense, temporally structured feedback. The authors clearly position their work within the literature, distinguishing it from prior supervised learning approaches (e.g., imitation learning), TD methods, and goal-conditioned frameworks (e.g., Schaul et al., 2015). The core innovation—treating sensorimotor control as direct future prediction of measurements using Monte Carlo estimation—directly addresses the paper's stated problem of learning from raw sensory input in complex environments. The results robustly support all key claims: the approach outperforms DQN, A3C, and DSR on all four scenarios (with 6x advantage in Battle scenarios), generalizes across environments (Table 2), and enables goal-agnostic training (Table 3). The ablation study (Table 4) conclusively demonstrates the necessity of vectorial measurements and multi-temporal prediction.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. To further strengthen it, consider clarifying the following points in the revision: (1) Explicitly state that the measurement stream (e.g., health, ammo) is a *pre-defined* component of the environment interface, not learned, and discuss its dependency on domain-specific knowledge (e.g., "This approach requires access to meaningful measurements like health/ammo; it may not generalize to environments lacking such structured feedback"). (2) Address the on-policy assumption in the training regime (Section 3.1), noting that the experience replay buffer samples from prior policies, while the Monte Carlo prediction assumes the current policy's trajectory. How does this impact convergence guarantees?

To better assess the training dynamics and theoretical foundations, please clarify: (1) How does the performance of the model trained with randomized goals (Section 4.3) compare to a model trained with goal vectors specifically optimized for the test goal? (2) In the ablation study (Table 4), what is the exact performance difference when predicting *only* the frag count (a scalar) versus all three measurements, and how does this compare to the scalar-reward baselines (DQN/A3C)?
