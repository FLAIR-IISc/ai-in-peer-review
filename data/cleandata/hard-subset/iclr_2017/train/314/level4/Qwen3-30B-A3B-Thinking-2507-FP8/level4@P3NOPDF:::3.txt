

This paper introduces an on-policy method for predicting intrinsic states in reinforcement learning, specifically designed for the vizDoom environment. The core innovation lies in training a model to predict sequences of triplets representing health, ammunition, and frags, weighted by user-specified goal triplets. This weighting mechanism guides exploration, enabling agents to optimize long-term goal achievement during inference. The approach secured victory in the 2016 vizDoom competition, demonstrating practical significance.

The experimental evaluation is robust and well-structured. The authors compare their method (DFP) against strong baselines: A3C, DQN, and DSR (Kulkarni et al., 2016). DFP consistently outperforms these methods across Doom scenarios, achieving superior or equivalent results where baselines plateau (e.g., A3C in scenario D1). An ablation study effectively validates the necessity of each component in the proposed architecture, confirming that the added complexity directly contributes to performance gains. The experimental design is thorough, with detailed results and supplementary material supporting reproducibility.

The work situates itself within established RL domains—predicting intrinsic motivation, auxiliary variables, and forward modeling. The revision adequately cites key prior work (e.g., Singh et al., 2004), though a more exhaustive literature survey would be ideal. The paper’s focus on a concrete, high-impact application (vizDoom competition) justifies its scope, and the methodological rigor outweighs minor gaps in prior work coverage.

A few minor issues require attention:
1. **Environment description**: The paper describes Doom as "3D," but it is fundamentally a 2D environment rendered in pseudo-3D (height is non-discriminative for actions). This should be clarified to avoid confusion.
2. **Notation clarity**: The symbol "P" in Equation (2) denotes prediction, not probability—a potential source of confusion. Explicitly defining it as "prediction weight" would improve readability.
3. **Equation (6)**: The double use of "j" (in different fonts) may mislead readers. Standardizing the index notation (e.g., using "i" for one variable) is recommended.
4. **Table presentation**: Units for metrics (e.g., frags, health) should be consistently repeated in Table 1 to accommodate heterogeneous measurements.

These points are purely editorial and do not undermine the paper’s contributions. The core methodology is sound, the experiments are compelling, and the vizDoom victory provides a strong real-world validation. While extending experiments to additional environments would strengthen generalizability, the paper’s focus on a well-defined, high-stakes benchmark is justified. The novelty lies in the *practical implementation* of weighted intrinsic state prediction as a scalable exploration strategy—not merely theoretical innovation—but this is sufficient for a "milestone" contribution in the context of the vizDoom competition.

The paper avoids common pitfalls: it is methodologically rigorous (clear ablations, reproducible appendix), avoids overclaiming (e.g., explicitly stating limitations), and delivers a tangible result (competition win). This combination of empirical strength, clarity, and real-world impact aligns perfectly with ICLR’s standards for publication. The minor notational and descriptive points noted above are easily resolvable in the camera-ready version.

I recommend **strong acceptance**. The work is a clear example of a "correct" and "milestone" paper: it solves a specific, challenging problem with a principled approach, delivers measurable results on a recognized benchmark, and provides sufficient detail for replication. While the domain (Doom) is narrow, the approach’s success here validates its potential for broader application, and the authors’ focus on a single, well-defined environment avoids dilution of impact. The minor weaknesses identified are not substantive enough to warrant rejection, especially given the paper’s demonstrable contribution to the field.
