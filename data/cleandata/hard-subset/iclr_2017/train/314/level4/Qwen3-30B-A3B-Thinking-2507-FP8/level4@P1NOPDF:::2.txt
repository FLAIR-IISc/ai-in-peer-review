

This paper introduces an on-policy deep reinforcement learning method incorporating auxiliary intrinsic state variables. While the approach is a specific instantiation of the universal value function (UVF) framework—appropriately cited—the primary technical contribution remains under-specified in the abstract and introduction. The authors consolidate multiple established techniques for 3D navigation, but this integration should be explicitly framed as the core contribution rather than implied.  

A significant gap is the absence of failure mode analysis. The paper does not address scenarios where the agent struggles to generalize to novel goals, such as when goal distributions shift significantly from training data. Additionally, as an on-policy method, the approach faces inherent limitations: catastrophic forgetting becomes particularly problematic when the agent fails to revisit goals from earlier training phases, as is typical in dynamic environments. This oversight weakens the practical viability claim.  

Given that the paper’s main contribution centers on integrating key ideas and demonstrating empirical benefits, extending evaluations to broader domains would strengthen the case. Testing on Atari environments—using game ROMs as sources of intrinsic rewards, for instance—would better validate the method’s generalizability beyond 3D navigation. Such results would contextualize the proposed framework against established baselines in more diverse settings.  

Despite these concerns, the empirical results clearly demonstrate the advantages of the underlying formulation. The experimental insights—such as the efficacy of auxiliary intrinsic variables in guiding exploration—hold tangible value for future agent design. The work provides a useful foundation, but the current presentation requires sharper articulation of its novelty and more rigorous evaluation of limitations.
