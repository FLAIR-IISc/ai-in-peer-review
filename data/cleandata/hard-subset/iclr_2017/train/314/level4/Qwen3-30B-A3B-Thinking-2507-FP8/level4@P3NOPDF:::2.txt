

This paper proposes an on-policy deep reinforcement learning method incorporating auxiliary intrinsic variables for 3D navigation tasks. While the approach integrates several established concepts, its core contribution requires clearer articulation. The method is correctly positioned as a special case of universal value function frameworks (with appropriate citations), yet the abstract and introduction should explicitly frame the *integration and distillation* of prior techniques—rather than presenting them as novel— as the primary technical contribution. This would better align the narrative with the paper's actual substance.

The empirical results demonstrate a clear performance advantage over baselines in the 3D navigation domain, providing valuable insights into the utility of the proposed auxiliary variables. These findings are well-supported and could offer actionable guidance for future agent design. However, the paper would benefit significantly from a more thorough analysis of limitations and failure modes. Specifically, it does not address under what conditions the agent struggles to generalize to novel goals (e.g., when goal distributions shift significantly from training data). This is critical for assessing the method's robustness.

Furthermore, as an on-policy algorithm, the approach inherently risks catastrophic forgetting. The paper does not discuss strategies to mitigate this, such as incorporating experience replay or periodic retraining on historical goals. Without such discussion, the practical viability of the method in long-term learning scenarios remains unclear, as the agent would likely degrade when encountering goals encountered far in the past without repeated exposure during training.

The experimental scope is another limitation. While the 3D navigation results are compelling, the paper does not test the core formulation on diverse domains like Atari, where intrinsic motivation has shown significant promise. A single experiment using game-specific ROMs as intrinsic state representations (e.g., leveraging game-specific features as auxiliary variables) could substantially strengthen the claim that the proposed framework generalizes beyond the specific navigation tasks studied. This would better validate the method's broader applicability and highlight its potential as a versatile component for future RL agents.

In summary, the paper makes a useful empirical contribution by demonstrating the efficacy of a unified framework for 3D navigation. The integration of existing ideas is valuable, but the contribution must be stated more precisely upfront. Addressing the limitations around generalization, catastrophic forgetting, and domain scope would significantly elevate the work. The empirical insights are promising, but the paper would be stronger with a more critical evaluation of the method's boundaries and a broader experimental validation. As currently presented, the method appears effective within its tested domain but lacks sufficient evidence to claim broader significance without addressing these gaps.
