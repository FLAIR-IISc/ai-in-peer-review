

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary
This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments by framing the problem as supervised learning using the cotemporal structure of sensory and measurement streams. The authors propose a method where an agent learns to predict future measurements (such as health, ammunition, and frag count) based on current sensory input, measurements, and a goal vector. This approach enables learning from raw sensory input without extraneous supervision, supports training without fixed goals, and allows for flexible goal specification at test time. The method is evaluated extensively in three-dimensional Doom simulations, where it outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning approaches, particularly on complex tasks. Most notably, a model trained with this approach won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming the second-best submission by over 50%.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution through its simple yet effective approach to sensorimotor control that outperforms more complex reinforcement learning methods in challenging environments. The experimental results are scientifically rigorous, and the approach is well-motivated within the literature.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper effectively addresses three key questions:

1. The specific problem tackled is sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex, dynamic 3D environments, with the goal of learning generalizable skills that can adapt to dynamically specified goals.

2. The approach is well-motivated by the observation that the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides rich supervision, which the authors position as a natural alternative to sparse scalar rewards in traditional reinforcement learning. The literature review thoroughly contextualizes this approach within supervised learning, Monte Carlo methods, and related work on vector-valued feedback and parameterized goals.

3. The paper strongly supports its claims through extensive experiments in the ViZDoom platform. The results demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art deep RL methods (DQN, A3C, DSR), particularly on challenging tasks (D3 and D4 scenarios). The ablation study confirms the importance of using vectorial measurements rather than scalar rewards, and the generalization experiments across environments and goals further validate the approach. The competition results provide compelling real-world validation.

## 4. Additional Feedback
The paper is well-written and technically sound, but I have a few suggestions to enhance clarity and precision:

- Clarify the description of Doom as a 3D environment. While the game presents a 3D perspective, it's technically a 2D environment with height not being a discriminative or actionable dimension. This distinction matters for the paper's claim about "three-dimensional environments."

- Consider revising notation in Equation (2) where "P" is used for prediction rather than probability (as is conventional), to avoid potential confusion.

- In Equation (6), the use of "j" for two different concepts (one in the equation, one in the text) may cause confusion. Consider using distinct notation.

- For greater clarity, include units in the results tables (particularly Table 1) since measurements are heterogeneous (health, frags, etc.).

- The paper mentions "vectorial measurements" but could more explicitly explain how these differ from standard reward signals in reinforcement learning, especially regarding the temporal density of supervision.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper claims the approach works in "three-dimensional environments," but Doom is fundamentally a 2D environment with 3D perspective. Could you clarify how this approach would generalize to true 3D environments where height is a discriminative dimension?

2. In the ablation study (Table 4), the authors show that predicting multiple measurements improves performance even when evaluated on a single measurement. How does this relate to the theoretical benefits of vector-valued feedback discussed in Section 2? Does this suggest the measurements are not entirely independent?

3. The paper demonstrates impressive generalization across environments, but it would be valuable to know whether the model maintains performance when tested in environments with different visual styles or object distributions not seen during training.
