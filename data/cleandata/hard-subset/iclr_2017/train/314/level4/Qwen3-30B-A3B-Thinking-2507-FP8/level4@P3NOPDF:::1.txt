

This paper presents a compelling advancement in deep reinforcement learning (RL) for complex 3D environments, demonstrated through its victory in the Visual Doom AI competition. The authors introduce an algorithm that leverages low-dimensional game-engine metadata (e.g., health, ammo) as supervised targets to learn goal-conditioned policies directly from raw sensory inputs. This approach enables superior generalization across diverse goals and environments compared to prior methods, with policies that adapt to novel goal configurations without retraining—a significant practical advantage.

The core innovation lies in conditioning a supervised prediction task on both a parametrized goal vector and the current action. The model predicts the outcome (e.g., future health level) given a goal and action, and the optimal action for a state is selected to maximize this predicted outcome toward the goal. Crucially, the learning paradigm is fully supervised (unlike successor features, which rely on temporal difference bootstrapping), avoiding TD error propagation while leveraging Monte Carlo estimation of the full trajectory. The authors effectively contrast this with relevant prior work in goal-driven RL (Section 2), highlighting their focus on Monte Carlo estimation, the use of engineered low-dimensional measurements, and parametrized goals as key contributions.

The empirical evaluation is robust and well-justified. Beyond the Visual Doom competition win, the paper demonstrates strong generalization: policies trained on one set of goals adapt immediately to new goals (e.g., "find health" vs. "find ammo") without additional training, even when goal specifications change subtly. Results across multiple environments confirm the algorithm’s superiority over state-of-the-art baselines in terms of sample efficiency and final performance. The writing is clear, the experiments are well-designed, and the results are highly compelling for the RL community.

Two minor points warrant clarification to strengthen the paper’s contribution and limitations:

1.  **On-policy assumption in replay buffer:** The supervised training assumes the trajectory remainder follows the current policy, yet it samples from a replay buffer containing episodes generated by prior policy versions. This creates a subtle mismatch between the on-policy learning objective and off-policy data collection. The authors should explicitly discuss this limitation and its potential impact on convergence or performance, as this is a common challenge in policy optimization with replay.

2.  **Dependency on engineered metadata:** The algorithm relies on game-provided low-dimensional measurements (ammo, health) as prediction targets. This is a significant advantage in the controlled Visual Doom environment but presents a limitation for broader applicability. The authors should more clearly articulate that this approach requires access to such domain-specific metadata, which may not be available in real-world sensory settings (e.g., raw camera inputs without internal state access). A brief discussion of potential workarounds or scenarios where this dependency could be mitigated would enhance the paper’s scope.

These points are minor and do not detract from the paper’s strong empirical results and clear contribution. The work effectively bridges the gap between goal-conditioned RL and practical generalization, offering a valuable alternative to TD-based methods. The Visual Doom victory provides a strong, concrete validation of the approach, and the generalization results are particularly impressive.

Overall, this is a well-executed, impactful paper that advances the state-of-the-art in goal-conditioned deep RL. The methodology is sound, the experiments are thorough, and the results are convincing. The minor clarifications suggested above would further solidify its contribution and applicability. I recommend acceptance.
