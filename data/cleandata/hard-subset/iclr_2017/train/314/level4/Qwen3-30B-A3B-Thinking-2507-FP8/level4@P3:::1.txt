

# Review for ICLR 2017

## Summary of Contributions
This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments that leverages the temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams to enable learning from raw experience without extraneous supervision. The authors frame sensorimotor control as a supervised learning problem where the agent predicts the effects of actions on future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count), rather than relying on sparse scalar rewards. The approach demonstrates significant advantages in complex 3D environments, particularly outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL methods on challenging tasks. Most notably, a model trained with this approach won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming the second-best submission by over 50% in previously unseen environments. The method also enables goal-agnostic training and generalization to dynamically specified goals at test time.

## Decision
Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by demonstrating that supervised learning with rich vectorial feedback can outperform traditional reinforcement learning approaches in complex 3D environments, supported by compelling empirical evidence.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper successfully addresses a significant challenge in RL: learning sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex environments. The key insight—that the temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides a rich supervisory signal—provides a well-motivated alternative to traditional RL with sparse rewards. This is particularly relevant in immersive environments where dense, multidimensional feedback is naturally available.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors conduct extensive experiments across four progressively challenging scenarios in the Doom environment, demonstrating that their approach (DFP) consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods (DQN, A3C, DSR), with increasingly significant margins as task complexity increases. The results are statistically sound, with multiple independent training runs reported. The generalization experiments across environments and goals further strengthen the case for the method's robustness.

The Visual Doom AI Competition win provides particularly compelling evidence. The authors' model achieved superior performance without the additional supervision (e.g., game engine knowledge) that their strongest competitor used, demonstrating the value of their approach in real-world competition settings.

## Additional Feedback
While the paper is strong, a few refinements would enhance its clarity and impact:

1. The distinction between sensory input and measurements could be clarified earlier. The paper states that "the measurement vector mt is distinguished from other sensations in two ways," but this distinction is somewhat artificial and could be better motivated. The authors might clarify why certain sensory elements are designated as measurements rather than being predicted.

2. The ablation study (Table 4) demonstrates the importance of vectorial feedback and multi-temporal prediction, but the paper could more explicitly discuss the limitations of this approach. For instance, how would the method perform in environments where such measurements are not naturally available? This limitation is noted in the draft review but should be more prominent in the main text.

3. The training regime uses a replay buffer with a small experience memory, which introduces a subtle discrepancy between the on-policy assumption of the supervised learning approach and the off-policy sampling. The authors should discuss this tension more thoroughly, as it could impact the theoretical foundation of the approach.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that "the measurement vector mt is distinguished from other sensations in two ways." Could you clarify why the authors chose to designate specific measurements (e.g., health, ammo) as the target for prediction rather than learning to predict the entire sensory stream? How would the method be adapted if the environment did not provide such measurements?

2. In the generalization experiments (Table 2), the model trained on D4-tx performs better than those trained on simpler environments. However, the paper does not discuss whether this improvement comes from the model architecture or from the increased diversity in the training environments. Could the authors elaborate on the relative contributions of these factors?

3. The paper mentions that the approach "supports training without a fixed goal." However, the training regime still uses a fixed goal distribution (uniformly random within [0,1] or [-1,1]). How would the method perform with a more complex goal distribution, such as one that follows the natural distribution of goals encountered in the environment?
