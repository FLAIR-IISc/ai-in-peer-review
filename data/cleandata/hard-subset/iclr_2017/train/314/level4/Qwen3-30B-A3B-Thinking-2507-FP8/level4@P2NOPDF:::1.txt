

This paper presents a novel deep reinforcement learning algorithm that achieves state-of-the-art performance in 3D visual environments, particularly excelling in the Visual Doom AI competition. The work builds upon recent advances in deep RL for large state spaces by introducing a method that leverages low-dimensional game-provided observations (e.g., health, ammo) as supervised targets for prediction. Crucially, predictions are conditioned on both a specified goal vector (externally defined, not learned) and the current action. The optimal action for a given state is then determined by selecting the action that maximizes the predicted outcome relative to the goal. This approach differs fundamentally from successor feature representations, as it employs supervised learning rather than temporal difference (TD) updates, eliminating the need for state-to-state prediction relationships.

The key contributions are well-articulated: (1) a shift from TD-based to Monte Carlo estimation for policy learning; (2) the strategic use of low-dimensional "measurements" as prediction targets; (3) parametrized goal conditioning; and (4) comprehensive empirical validation against relevant prior work, including the Visual Doom competition. The algorithm demonstrates strong generalization capabilities, learning policies that adapt to novel goals and environmental perturbations without additional training—a significant advancement for goal-directed RL.

The paper is clearly written, with compelling empirical results that validate the method's efficacy across diverse 3D environments. The Visual Doom competition win provides strong evidence of real-world applicability, and the generalization experiments further underscore the approach's practical value. The literature review in Section 2 adequately contextualizes the work within existing goal-driven RL and state-prediction frameworks.

Two minor points warrant attention for clarity and completeness:  
First, the supervised training implicitly assumes an on-policy trajectory for the Monte Carlo return (i.e., future rewards follow the current policy). However, the algorithm trains from a replay buffer containing episodes generated by prior policy versions. This off-policy sampling creates a subtle approximation error that should be acknowledged and discussed, as it may impact convergence guarantees.  
Second, the method relies on game-provided metadata (e.g., ammo counts) to define prediction targets—a critical dependency absent in comparable baselines. This limitation should be explicitly stated, particularly regarding scenarios where such measurements are unavailable (e.g., in real-world robotics with uninstrumented sensors). The authors should clarify whether this dependency is a fundamental constraint or if the approach could be adapted to infer such features from raw sensory input.

Overall, this is a strong contribution with clear technical innovation and robust empirical validation. The algorithm’s ability to generalize across goals and environments without retraining represents a meaningful step toward more adaptable RL systems. The minor concerns raised above are easily addressable in revision and do not undermine the paper’s core strengths. The work will be of significant interest to the RL community, particularly for applications requiring goal-conditioned policy learning in complex visual environments.
