

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments that leverages the cotemporal structure of high-dimensional sensory streams and lower-dimensional measurement streams to enable supervised learning without extraneous supervision. The authors demonstrate that their method, which trains models to predict future measurements based on current sensory input and goals, achieves state-of-the-art performance in complex three-dimensional simulations and successfully won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming sophisticated prior formulations by more than 50%.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The primary reasons for this decision are: (1) the approach demonstrates clear empirical advantages over existing deep reinforcement learning methods, particularly on challenging tasks requiring dynamic goal pursuit, and (2) the method provides valuable insights into how rich measurement streams can serve as superior supervisory signals compared to traditional scalar rewards.

The paper's experimental results are compelling and well-supported. The authors conduct thorough evaluations across four increasingly complex scenarios, demonstrating consistent outperformance of state-of-the-art baselines. The generalization capabilities across environments and goals are particularly impressive, with the model trained on one environment performing well in previously unseen variants. The approach's simplicity while achieving superior results represents a significant contribution to the field.

To strengthen the paper further, I suggest:
1. Clarifying the technical contribution in the abstract and introduction to better highlight what distinguishes this work from existing universal value function approaches.
2. Including an analysis of failure modes, particularly regarding the model's performance when generalizing to goals that are substantially different from those encountered during training.
3. Addressing the potential limitation of on-policy training with respect to catastrophic forgetting when dealing with a diverse set of goals over extended training periods.

I would appreciate clarification on the following points:
1. Under what specific conditions does the model struggle with generalizing to new goals, and how might these limitations be addressed?
2. Given the on-policy nature of the training approach, how does the authors' method mitigate the risk of catastrophic forgetting when the agent encounters a wide variety of goals over time?
3. Would the authors consider extending the experiments to other domains, such as Atari games, to demonstrate the broader applicability of their approach beyond the Doom environment?
