

Deep Reinforcement Learning (Deep RL), which employs deep neural networks for function approximation in reinforcement learning algorithms, has achieved notable success in solving reinforcement learning problems in large state spaces. This empirically driven work builds upon these advances by introducing a novel algorithm that demonstrates superior performance in novel 3D environments using raw sensory data and enables improved generalization across diverse goals and environments. Notably, this algorithm secured victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition.

The core innovation of the algorithm involves utilizing additional low-dimensional observations (e.g., ammo or health values provided by the game engine) as supervised targets for prediction. Crucially, this prediction is conditioned on a goal vector (provided externally, not learned) and the current action. After training, the optimal action for the current state is selected as the action that maximizes the predicted outcome relative to the specified goal. Unlike successor feature representations, this approach employs supervised learning without temporal difference (TD) relationships between predictions of the current and next states.

The authors comprehensively review prior work on predicting future states within RL and goal-driven function approximators in Section 2. The key contributions of this paper are: (1) a focus on Monte Carlo estimation rather than TD learning, (2) the use of low-dimensional 'measurements' as prediction targets, (3) parametrized goals, and (4) most significantly, a rigorous empirical comparison against relevant prior work.

Beyond the Visual Doom AI results, the authors demonstrate that their algorithm learns generalizable policies capable of responding to limited goal modifications without further training. The paper is well-written, and the empirical results are compelling, making it highly relevant to the community.

Minor suggestions for improvement:
1.  The supervised training involves an approximation due to an on-policy assumption: the Monte Carlo regression assumes the remainder of the trajectory follows the current policy, but the replay buffer samples episodes generated by prior policy versions. This discrepancy should be explicitly addressed.
2.  The algorithm relies on additional metadata (specifying which sensory components are predictive targets), which is not required by the baseline methods. The paper should more clearly acknowledge this dependency and discuss the limitation that the approach may not generalize to sensory environments lacking such provided measurements.
