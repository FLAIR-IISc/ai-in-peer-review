

Deep RL (employing deep neural networks as function approximators in reinforcement learning algorithms) has demonstrated significant success in solving reinforcement learning problems in large state spaces. This empirically driven work extends these frameworks by introducing a novel algorithm that achieves superior performance in novel 3D environments using raw sensory inputs and enables enhanced generalization across diverse goals and environments. Notably, the algorithm secured victory in the Visual Doom AI competition.

The core innovation involves utilizing additional low-dimensional auxiliary observations (e.g., ammo or health values provided by the game engine) as a supervised prediction target. Crucially, this prediction is conditioned on an explicitly provided goal vector (not learned) and the current action. Following training, the optimal action for a given state is selected as the action maximizing the predicted outcome relative to the goal. Unlike successor feature representations, this approach employs supervised learning without temporal difference (TD) updates between consecutive state predictions.

The authors comprehensively review relevant prior work on future state prediction and goal-driven function approximators in Section 2. The key contributions of this work include: (1) explicit reliance on Monte Carlo estimation (as opposed to TD learning), (2) the use of low-dimensional auxiliary measurements for prediction, (3) goal parametrization, and (4) notably, rigorous empirical comparisons against pertinent prior methods.

Beyond the Visual Doom AI results, the authors demonstrate that their algorithm learns generalizable policies capable of adapting to limited goal modifications without additional training. The paper is well-structured, with compelling empirical results that will attract considerable attention.

Minor suggestions for improvement:
1. The supervised training involves an approximation due to an on-policy assumption while learning from a replay buffer (Monte Carlo regression assumes the remainder of the trajectory follows the current policy, yet samples derive from episodes generated by prior policy versions). This limitation should be explicitly discussed.
2. The algorithm requires additional metadata specifying which sensory components to predict—unlike the baseline methods. The scope and limitations of this dependency (e.g., potential failure in sensory-rich environments lacking such measurements) should be clarified more prominently.
