

# Review for ICLR 2017

## Summary
This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments that leverages the cotemporal structure of sensory and measurement streams as a supervisory signal. Rather than relying on traditional reward-based reinforcement learning, the authors formulate the problem as supervised learning where the model predicts future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frags) based on current sensory input and a goal specification. The key insight is that this approach provides richer, temporally dense supervision compared to sparse scalar rewards. The method demonstrates impressive results in the Visual Doom AI Competition, winning the Full Deathmatch track against sophisticated baselines, and achieves state-of-the-art performance across multiple Doom scenarios. The paper makes a compelling case for the value of vectorial measurements over scalar rewards in complex 3D environments.

## Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning with a simple yet effective approach that outperforms state-of-the-art methods in challenging environments. The experimental validation is thorough, the approach is well-motivated by the literature, and the results are scientifically rigorous.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper successfully addresses two key challenges in reinforcement learning: sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments, and acquisition of general skills that can be flexibly deployed to accomplish dynamically specified goals. The authors clearly demonstrate how their approach overcomes these challenges by leveraging the temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams, which provides a rich supervisory signal for training. 

The experimental validation is particularly strong. The authors conduct comprehensive experiments across four progressively challenging Doom scenarios, demonstrating consistent superiority over DQN, A3C, and DSR baselines. In the most complex Battle and Battle 2 scenarios, their method (DFP) outperforms A3C by factors of 6 and 2.5, respectively. The ablation studies confirm that both the vectorial measurement approach and multi-temporal prediction are crucial to the method's success. Most impressively, their model won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming the second-best submission by over 50% without additional supervision or reward shaping.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with a thorough background section that carefully contextualizes the work relative to related approaches in supervised learning, reinforcement learning, and prediction-based methods. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from and improves upon prior work, particularly in addressing the limitations of sparse scalar rewards.

## Additional Feedback
While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance its clarity and impact:

1. The paper refers to Doom as a "3D environment" when it is technically a 2.5D environment (with height not being a discriminative or actionable dimension). Clarifying this would improve precision.

2. In Equation (2), the use of "p" for prediction may be confusing as it's not standard probability notation. Consider using "f" or another symbol to avoid confusion.

3. The double use of "j" (in different fonts) in Equation (6) could be misleading. Consider using distinct symbols for these different concepts.

4. Table 1 would benefit from clearly stating the units for each measurement (e.g., "health" vs. "frags") to improve readability.

5. The discussion of generalization across environments (Table 2) could be strengthened by including more specific examples of how the texture variations affected performance.

## Clarifying Questions
To further strengthen the paper and ensure confidence in the assessment, I'd appreciate clarification on:

1. The paper states "the measurement stream provides rich and temporally dense supervision" but doesn't quantify how much denser this supervision is compared to scalar rewards. Could the authors provide a more concrete comparison (e.g., number of supervision signals per time step)?

2. In Table 3, the "pacifist" goal (maximize ammo and health, minimize frags) results in negative health at the end of episodes. Is this expected behavior given the goal specification, or does it indicate a potential issue with the implementation?

3. The paper mentions that the model was trained using a variant of the D4-tx-L regime for the competition, but doesn't detail how this differed from the standard training procedure. Could the authors clarify these differences and why they were beneficial for competition performance?

The paper represents a significant contribution to the field with its simple yet effective approach to sensorimotor control. The experimental results are compelling, the method is well-motivated, and the paper is clearly written. The approach is not only a technical achievement (winning the Visual Doom competition) but also provides valuable theoretical insight into the nature of supervision in reinforcement learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
