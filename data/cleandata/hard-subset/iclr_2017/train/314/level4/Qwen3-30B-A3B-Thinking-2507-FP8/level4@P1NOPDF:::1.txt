

This paper presents a novel deep reinforcement learning (RL) algorithm that achieves strong performance in complex 3D environments using raw sensory input, demonstrating superior generalization across goals and environments. Crucially, it secured first place in the Visual Doom AI competition. The core innovation lies in using game-engine-provided low-dimensional state metrics (e.g., health, ammo) as supervised targets for prediction. These predictions are conditioned on a goal vector (explicitly specified, not learned) and the current action. The optimal action for a given state is then selected to maximize the predicted outcome relative to the goal. This approach differs fundamentally from successor feature representations, as it employs supervised learning without temporal-difference (TD) updates between states.

The authors effectively contextualize their work within prior research on goal-conditioned function approximation and future state prediction (Section 2). Their key contributions include: (1) leveraging Monte Carlo estimation instead of TD learning, (2) utilizing low-dimensional state metrics for supervision, (3) incorporating goal-parameterized predictions, and (4) providing rigorous empirical comparisons against relevant baselines. Beyond the Visual Doom results, the paper compellingly demonstrates that the learned policies generalize robustly to novel goals without additional training—a significant practical advantage.

The paper is well-written, with clear exposition and compelling empirical results that will interest the RL community. The methodology is sound, and the results are convincingly presented.

Two minor points warrant clarification:
1.  The supervised training involves an on-policy assumption (it assumes the remainder of the trajectory follows the current policy), yet it learns from a replay buffer containing trajectories generated by prior policy versions. This off-policy sampling introduces a potential approximation error that should be acknowledged and discussed.
2.  The algorithm relies on specific game-engine metrics (e.g., health, ammo) as auxiliary observations. This dependency is not present in the comparison baselines. The authors should more explicitly state the limitation: this approach may not generalize to environments where such structured metrics are unavailable or impractical to extract from raw sensory input.
