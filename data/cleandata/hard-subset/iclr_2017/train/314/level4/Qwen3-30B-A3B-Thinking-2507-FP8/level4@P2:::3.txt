

The paper introduces a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments that frames the problem as supervised learning rather than reinforcement learning. The authors propose using a high-dimensional sensory stream (e.g., raw visual input) and a lower-dimensional measurement stream (e.g., health, ammunition, frags) to provide dense, temporally rich supervision. The model predicts future measurements based on current sensory input, measurements, and a goal vector, then selects actions that optimize the predicted future measurements. This formulation enables training without fixed goals and flexible adaptation to dynamically specified goals at test time. The approach was evaluated in the Visual Doom AI Competition, where it achieved state-of-the-art performance and won the Full Deathmatch track.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a strong contribution by demonstrating that supervised learning techniques can effectively handle sensorimotor control in complex 3D environments with raw sensory input. The experimental validation is particularly compelling as it demonstrates both superior performance over prior deep RL methods and successful generalization across environments and goals.

The paper excels in experimental rigor and clarity. The authors conduct thorough comparisons against strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across four progressively challenging Doom scenarios. Their approach consistently outperforms competitors, particularly on complex tasks (D3 and D4), with performance gains of 6x in Battle scenarios. The ablation studies provide convincing evidence that their key innovations—vectorial measurements rather than scalar rewards, and prediction at multiple temporal offsets—significantly contribute to the results. The model's victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition (outperforming the second-best submission by over 50% without additional supervision) provides strong empirical validation.

The paper also makes a well-motivated theoretical contribution by connecting to prior work on supervised learning for control (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992) while addressing key limitations of traditional RL. The authors clearly articulate why their approach is particularly suitable for immersive environments with dense sensory feedback, where scalar rewards are insufficient. Their formulation generalizes standard RL by treating the reward as a special case of their measurement vector.

The writing is generally clear, though I have a few suggestions to enhance precision:
- The paper describes Doom as a "3D environment," though it's technically a 2D environment presented in pseudo-3D. Clarifying this would prevent potential confusion.
- In Equation (2), the notation "p" might be misleading as it denotes prediction rather than probability. Using "ŷ" or "f" would be more conventional.
- The double use of "j" in Equation (6) (in different fonts) could be confusing; using distinct symbols would improve clarity.
- Results tables would benefit from explicitly stating units for measurements, particularly since Table 1 uses heterogenous metrics.

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:
1. Including a brief discussion of how this approach might scale to environments with more complex action spaces (e.g., continuous control), as mentioned in the discussion section.
2. Adding a short analysis of computational efficiency compared to baselines, especially regarding the training speed differences noted in Table 1.
3. Clarifying whether the measurement stream could be automatically derived from sensory input rather than being predefined, which would address a key limitation of the current approach.

I have two specific questions to clarify the authors' approach:
1. The paper states the model was trained without extraneous supervision, but the Visual Doom competition allowed access to game engine internals. How did the authors ensure their approach doesn't implicitly rely on privileged information during training that wouldn't be available in real-world applications?
2. In the goal-agnostic training experiments (Table 3), the model trained with goal vectors sampled from [-1, 1] performed well on pacifist goals (maximize ammo/health, minimize frags). How does the model learn to distinguish between desirable and undesirable measurements without explicit reward signals during training?

The paper's experimental validation is exceptionally strong, with results that are both scientifically rigorous and practically significant. The authors' approach represents a meaningful step toward addressing key challenges in sensorimotor control from raw sensory input, and their results in the Visual Doom AI Competition provide compelling real-world validation. While the scope is limited to the Doom environment, the methodological insights are broadly applicable to immersive 3D environments. The paper's clarity, thorough experimental evaluation, and impressive competition results make it a strong contribution to the field.
