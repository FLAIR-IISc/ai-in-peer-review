

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments by leveraging the temporal structure between high-dimensional sensory inputs and lower-dimensional measurement streams. The authors propose a framework that transforms sensorimotor learning into a supervised learning problem, where the agent predicts future measurements based on current sensory input and a specified goal. This approach enables training without extraneous supervision, supports learning directly from raw experience, and allows flexible goal specification at test time. The method is evaluated extensively in 3D simulations based on the Doom game, demonstrating superior performance over state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods, particularly on challenging tasks. Notably, a model trained using this approach won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition in previously unseen environments, outperforming the second-best submission by over 50%.

I recommend rejection of this paper. The primary reason for rejection is that the paper fails to clearly establish the novelty of its contribution relative to existing work. While the empirical results are strong, the core technical approach appears to be a specific instantiation of the universal value function framework (Schaul et al., 2015) rather than a fundamentally new method.

The paper's main technical contribution is the application of supervised learning to sensorimotor control using measurement streams as the supervisory signal. However, the authors do not sufficiently distinguish their approach from the universal value function framework they cite. The claim that their method "departs from the reward-based formalization commonly used in RL" is misleading, as their framework is essentially a specific implementation of the universal value function approach where goals are defined in terms of intrinsic measurements. The paper does not provide a clear articulation of how their formulation advances beyond existing work in this space.

Additionally, the paper lacks critical analysis of the limitations of the proposed approach. The authors do not address important questions about failure modes, such as under what circumstances the model struggles to generalize to new goals. The claim that the approach supports "training without a fixed goal" is not fully substantiated, as the experiments with randomized goals during training show only modest improvements over fixed-goal training. The paper also fails to adequately address the practical concerns of using an experience memory rather than a fully off-policy approach, which could lead to issues with data efficiency and catastrophic forgetting.

While the empirical results are impressive, the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate why this approach is fundamentally superior to existing methods beyond the specific Doom domain. The authors do not provide results on other domains, such as Atari, where the approach might be tested with different intrinsic variables. This limits the generalizability of their claims and weakens the paper's contribution to the broader field.

For future work, the authors should more clearly articulate the novelty of their contribution relative to existing frameworks. They should provide a more detailed comparison with the universal value function framework of Schaul et al. (2015) and explicitly state how their approach advances beyond it. The paper would also benefit from a more thorough analysis of failure modes, including specific examples where the model struggles to adapt to new goals.

The authors should also provide a more comprehensive explanation of why multiple measurements provide a richer supervisory signal than a scalar reward. While Table 4 shows some benefits of vectorial feedback, the paper could better explain the specific mechanisms by which the measurement stream enhances learning in their particular setup.

I have three specific questions for the authors:
1. How does the proposed framework specifically advance beyond the universal value function framework of Schaul et al. (2015), which the authors cite as related work?
2. Can the authors provide more detailed analysis of the limitations of the goal-agnostic training regime, including specific examples where the model struggles to adapt to new goals?
3. How might the approach be extended to continuous action spaces, as suggested in the discussion, and what challenges might arise in such a generalization?

These clarifications would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and make it more suitable for publication.
