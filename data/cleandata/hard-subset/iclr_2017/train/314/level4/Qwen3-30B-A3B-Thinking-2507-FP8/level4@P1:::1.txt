

The paper presents a sensorimotor control approach for immersive 3D environments that frames learning as supervised prediction of future measurements (e.g., health, ammo) rather than reinforcement learning. The key innovation is using the temporal interplay between high-dimensional sensory input and low-dimensional measurements to provide dense, temporally rich supervision. The model predicts how actions affect future measurements, with the goal vector parameterizing desired outcomes. Crucially, the approach trains without fixed goals and adapts to new goals at test time. It achieved state-of-the-art results in the Visual Doom AI Competition (winning the Full Deathmatch track against complex baselines) and demonstrated strong generalization across environments and goals.

**Decision: Reject**  
The paper’s core contribution lacks sufficient novelty for ICLR. While the empirical results are compelling and the Doom competition win is notable, the fundamental approach—using environment-provided measurements as supervised targets for action prediction—is a minor adaptation of well-established supervised learning principles applied to a specific domain. The distinction from prior work (e.g., Schaul et al., 2015) is overstated, and the theoretical novelty is negligible.

**Supporting Arguments**  
1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Approach**: The paper’s "supervised learning" formulation is a direct extension of Jordan & Rumelhart (1992) and Sutton’s (1988) critiques of RL in environments with dense feedback. The use of vectorial measurements (vs. scalar rewards) and goal-parameterized prediction is explicitly covered by Schaul et al. (2015), which the authors cite but fail to properly contextualize. The claim that this "reduces sensorimotor control to supervised learning" is not new—imitation learning (LeCun et al., 2005) and model-based RL (Levine & Koltun, 2013) have long used similar paradigms. The paper’s contribution is primarily empirical, not conceptual.  

2. **Overstated Theoretical Claims**: The paper positions its method as "Monte Carlo" (vs. TD learning), but the training procedure (regression on experience replay) is standard supervised learning, not a novel Monte Carlo algorithm. The comparison to TD learning (Sutton, 1988) is misleading since the approach does not involve temporal difference updates. The authors also mischaracterize the relationship to successor features (Littman et al., 2001), which explicitly use TD updates. The theoretical justification is thin, relying on empirical results rather than new analysis.  

**Additional Feedback**  
- **Clarify the Role of Measurements**: The paper assumes measurements (e.g., health) are provided by the environment. This is a significant limitation—many real-world systems lack such structured feedback. Explicitly discuss scenarios where measurements are unavailable (e.g., raw pixels only) and whether the approach would fail. Compare to recent work on unsupervised measurement learning (e.g., Finn et al., 2016) to contextualize this dependency.  
- **Address the On-Policy Assumption**: The training uses experience replay (from prior policies) while the regression loss assumes on-policy trajectories. This creates a mismatch (the loss expects future rewards to follow the current policy, but samples come from older policies). Discuss this tension and how it might affect stability or convergence.  
- **Strengthen the Comparison to Schaul et al. (2015)**: The paper claims novelty over Schaul’s framework, but Schaul explicitly handles goal-parameterized value functions and vectorial feedback. Demonstrate how the current approach differs *in practice* (e.g., through ablation studies on Schaul’s setup) rather than merely citing it.  
- **Clarify "Goal-Agnostic" Training**: The results show models trained without fixed goals generalize to new goals, but the paper does not clarify how this differs from standard goal-conditioned RL (e.g., using a goal encoder). Explain why the *training* phase avoids goal specification (e.g., is it sampling goals during training?), and how this compares to methods like DQN with goal embeddings.  

**Questions for Authors**  
1. The paper states that "the model is trained without a fixed goal," but experiments use randomized goals during training (Section 4.3). How does this differ from standard goal-conditioned RL (e.g., Schaul et al., 2015), where goals are also sampled during training?  
2. The approach relies on environment-provided measurements (e.g., health). How would the method perform in a setting where such measurements are unavailable (e.g., only raw sensory input)? Could the model learn to predict measurements from sensory input alone?  
3. The training uses experience replay with a small memory (M most recent experiences). Is the performance sensitive to M? If M is increased to include older experiences, does the on-policy mismatch worsen?  

The empirical results are strong and the Doom competition win is impressive, but the work does not advance the *theoretical* or *methodological* foundations of the field. It is a competent application of existing ideas to a specific problem, not a novel contribution worthy of ICLR acceptance.
