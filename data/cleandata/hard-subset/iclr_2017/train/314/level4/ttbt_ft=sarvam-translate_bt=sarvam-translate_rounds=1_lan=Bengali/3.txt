This paper presents an on-policy approach for estimating future intrinsic measurements. All experiments were conducted within Doom (i.e., Doom), and instead of only estimating win/loss or the number of frags (score), the authors trained their model to estimate a triplet (a sequence of health, ammo, and frags) weighted by the "target" triplet (a sequence) they provided as input. Changing the weight of the target triplet is a way to guide/direct the search. During the experiment, only the long-term target can be maximized.

The results are impressive, as this model won the 2016 vizDoom competition. The experimental section of the paper seems accurate:

- A comparison of A3C with DFP and DSR (a recent similar approach from Kulkarni et al., 2016) is attempted. DFP performs better than other methods (or equals them when they reach a ceiling/optimum, e.g., A3C in the D1 scenario).
- There is an ablation study that supports the thesis that all the "extra complexity" of the paper model is useful.

Intrinsic motivation (Singh et al. 2004), auxiliary variables, and forward modeling predictions are well-studied areas of reinforcement learning. The version I read (December 4th revision) adequately references previous work, even if it is not entirely complete.

A few comments on the form (minor flaws):

- Doom is described as a 3D environment, whereas it is actually a 2D environment (height is not a discriminatory/effective dimension) presented in (fake) 3D.
- The use of "P" in (2) (and subsequently) can be misleading because it stands for prediction but not probability (as is usually the case with P).
- The double use of "j" in (6) (admittedly, with different fonts) can be misleading.
- The table of results may repeat units of measurement (especially since they are odd in Table 1).

I think this paper has a clear acceptance. One might argue that experiments could be run in different environments or that novelty is limited, but I think "right" (not nonsense, experimentally correct on Doom, with details for reproduction in the appendix) and "milestone" (vizDoom winner) papers should be published.
