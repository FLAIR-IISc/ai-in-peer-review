### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents an elegant and effective approach to sensorimotor control in complex, immersive 3D environments. The core idea is to reframe the control problem as one of supervised learning, departing from traditional reinforcement learning formulations. The proposed model, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), learns to predict the future values of a low-dimensional "measurement stream" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on the current high-dimensional sensory input (raw pixels), a potential action, and a goal vector. By training the agent to predict the consequences of its actions, it can select the action that is expected to best satisfy its current goal at test time.

The key contributions are:
1.  A specific, well-motivated formulation for sensorimotor control as a goal-conditioned supervised learning problem, which leverages the dense, multivariate feedback common in immersive environments.
2.  A concrete deep network architecture that effectively implements this idea, integrating perception, measurement, and goal inputs to predict future outcomes for all possible actions.
3.  Compelling experimental results in the challenging Visual Doom environment, demonstrating that this simpler approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods like A3C and DQN, especially on complex tasks.
4.  Strong evidence for the model's ability to generalize to unseen environments and to pursue dynamically specified goals that were not part of its training objective, supported by an impressive win in the Visual Doom AI Competition.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

My recommendation is to accept this paper. The decision is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Exceptional Empirical Performance:** The paper provides clear and convincing evidence that the proposed DFP method significantly outperforms sophisticated, state-of-the-art deep RL baselines on a set of challenging 3D navigation and combat tasks. The performance gap, particularly in the more complex "Battle" scenarios, is substantial. The victory in the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition serves as a powerful, independent validation of the method's effectiveness and generalization capabilities.

2.  **Valuable Conceptual Insight:** The work successfully demonstrates that for environments with rich, multi-dimensional feedback, reframing the control problem as direct future prediction via supervised learning is a highly effective alternative to value-function-based reinforcement learning. By synthesizing ideas from goal-conditioned learning and supervised prediction, the authors present a simple and scalable method whose success provides valuable insights that could influence the design of future agents for complex, interactive domains.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's claims are very well-supported by extensive and rigorous experiments.

*   **Superiority over Baselines:** The comparison in Figure 3 and Table 1 is stark. While methods like A3C and DQN perform well on simpler tasks, DFP shows a dramatic performance advantage (e.g., 6x more frags than A3C in scenario D3) as task complexity and the dimensionality of the measurement stream increase. The authors conducted a fair comparison by using a similar network architecture to DQN and performing a hyperparameter search for the baselines.

*   **Effective Generalization:** The experiments in Table 2 convincingly demonstrate the model's ability to generalize. Training on varied textures (D3-tx, D4-tx) allows the agent to perform well in unseen environments, a crucial capability for real-world application. The competition win, which was held in previously unseen environments, is the strongest possible evidence for this claim.

*   **Goal-Agnostic Learning:** The results in Table 3 are particularly interesting. They show that an agent can be trained without a fixed goal (using randomized goal vectors) and still perform nearly as well on a specific downstream task as an agent trained explicitly for that task. Furthermore, these goal-agnostic agents demonstrate far better flexibility in adapting to novel goals at test time (e.g., playing as a "pacifist"), supporting the claim that the model learns a general skill set.

*   **Importance of Multivariate Feedback:** The ablation study in Table 4 directly supports the paper's core motivation. It shows that predicting multiple measurements provides a significantly better learning signal than predicting a single scalar reward (frags), even when the agent is evaluated solely on that scalar metric. This validates the central hypothesis that leveraging the rich, dense feedback available in many environments is highly beneficial.

While the paper's core components (goal-conditioning, direct prediction) are not entirely novel in isolation, the main contribution lies in distilling and integrating these ideas into a cohesive and remarkably effective system. The authors clearly show the power of this synthesis on a challenging and relevant benchmark.

### **Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to further strengthen the paper and are not critical factors in my decision.

1.  **Clarity of Contribution:** The introduction and background sections do an excellent job of placing the work in the literature. However, the paper could be even stronger if it more explicitly framed its primary contribution as the successful synthesis, implementation, and large-scale validation of these existing ideas (direct prediction, vectorial feedback, goal parameterization) into a simple and powerful system that works exceptionally well on a complex problem. This would help manage reader expectations about theoretical novelty versus empirical and engineering contribution.

2.  **Analysis of Failure Modes:** The paper highlights the many successes of the approach, but a discussion of its limitations or failure modes would add significant depth. For instance, under what circumstances does the model struggle to generalize to new goals? Are there goals (e.g., those requiring very long-term planning or navigating complex causal chains) that this reactive, predictive model cannot achieve?

3.  **On-Policy Data and Forgetting:** The method trains on experience collected by the agent's current policy, which raises questions about catastrophic forgetting, especially in the randomized goal setting. If the agent does not train on a particular type of goal for a long time, will it forget how to achieve it? A brief discussion on how the experience memory and sampling strategy mitigate (or fail to mitigate) this would be insightful.

4.  **Applicability to Other Domains:** The approach is clearly well-suited to Doom. To broaden its perceived impact, it might be helpful to briefly discuss how it could be applied to other domains. For example, in the Atari domain, could the game's RAM state be used as the "measurement stream" to provide richer feedback than the scalar score alone?

### **Questions for Authors**

To help clarify my understanding and strengthen my assessment, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  Could you elaborate on the limits of the goal generalization shown in Table 3? For example, if you test on a goal vector that is anti-correlated with the common training goals (e.g., g = [-1, -1, -1]), does the agent trained on randomized positive goals still produce coherent behavior?

2.  The paper states it uses an experience memory of the "M most recent experiences." How was the size M chosen, and did you observe any sensitivity or issues related to catastrophic forgetting of skills for goals that were not sampled recently in the randomized-goal training regime?

3.  Your framework's strength seems to come from environments with rich, intrinsic measurements. Have you considered how this approach might compare to standard RL in environments where such measurements are not readily available and only a sparse, scalar reward exists? Does the advantage of DFP disappear in such settings?
