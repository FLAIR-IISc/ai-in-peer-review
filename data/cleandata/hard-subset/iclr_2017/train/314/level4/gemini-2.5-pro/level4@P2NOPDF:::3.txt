This paper introduces Direct Future Prediction (DFP), an on-policy method for reinforcement learning that predicts future values of a set of intrinsic game measurements. The experiments are conducted exclusively in the VizDoom environment. Instead of learning a value function for a single extrinsic reward like score, the proposed model learns to predict a future sequence of measurement triplets (health, ammunition, frags). This prediction is conditioned on a "goal" triplet provided as input, which acts as a dynamic weighting scheme for the different measurements. This mechanism serves a dual purpose: during training, varying the goal vector encourages diverse exploration, while at test time, the agent can act to maximize a specific, long-term objective.

The method's effectiveness is demonstrated by its impressive performance, notably winning the 2016 VizDoom competition. The paper's experimental section is rigorous and well-structured. It includes strong empirical comparisons against several relevant baselines, including A3C, DQN, and a related approach, DSR (Kulkarni et al., 2016). The results show that DFP outperforms or matches these strong baselines across several scenarios (e.g., matching the performance of A3C in the D1 scenario where performance has saturated). Furthermore, a comprehensive ablation study validates the contribution of each component of the proposed model, justifying its design complexity.

The authors situate their work within established research areas like forward modeling, auxiliary task prediction, and intrinsic motivation (Singh et al., 2004). The version of the paper I reviewed provides an adequate, though not exhaustive, review of prior work in these domains.

I have a few minor suggestions to improve the clarity of the presentation:

*   The paper describes Doom as a 3D environment. While visually presented in 3D, its game mechanics are more accurately characterized as 2.5D, as the vertical dimension is not a primary factor in navigation or combat strategy. Clarifying this could provide more precise context.
*   In Equation (2) and subsequent formulas, the notation "P" is used to represent a prediction. This could be momentarily confusing for readers accustomed to "P" denoting a probability distribution. Using a different symbol might enhance clarity.
*   In Equation (6), the index "j" is used twice with different fonts to represent distinct concepts. This overloading could be misleading; using different letters would prevent any potential ambiguity.
*   The results tables, particularly Table 1, present heterogeneous measurements. Including the units for each column would improve readability and prevent misinterpretation.

In conclusion, I recommend a clear accept for this paper. While one could argue for a broader experimental evaluation across different environments or debate the degree of conceptual novelty, the paper represents a significant empirical achievement. It is a well-executed, no-nonsense study that is experimentally sound and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility in the appendix. Papers that achieve milestone results, such as winning a major competition, and are presented with this level of empirical rigor are valuable contributions and deserve to be published and shared with the research community.
