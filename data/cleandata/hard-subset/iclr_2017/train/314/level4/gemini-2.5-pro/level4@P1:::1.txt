This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), that frames the problem as a supervised learning task. The key idea is to train a deep network to directly predict the future values of a low-dimensional stream of measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) from high-dimensional sensory input (e.g., raw pixels). This prediction is conditioned on both the agent's potential actions and a parameterized goal vector. At test time, the agent selects the action that is predicted to yield the best outcome according to its current goal. This formulation departs from standard reinforcement learning methods by using a rich, temporally dense, multi-dimensional signal for supervision instead of a sparse scalar reward, and by adopting a Monte Carlo-style prediction target rather than a bootstrapped one. The authors provide compelling empirical evidence in challenging 3D environments based on the game Doom, showing that their method significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL baselines. They also demonstrate the method's ability to generalize to unseen environments and to pursue dynamically specified goals without retraining. Notably, a model trained with this approach won the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong, empirically-driven paper that introduces a simple yet highly effective method for sensorimotor control. The primary reasons for my decision are:
1.  The proposed method convincingly outperforms strong, state-of-the-art deep RL baselines in complex, 3D first-person environments.
2.  The experimental results are thorough, well-communicated, and strongly support the claims of superior performance and generalization across both environments and goals.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's core strength lies in its extensive and convincing experimental evaluation. The authors compare their DFP algorithm against strong baselines, including DQN and A3C, across four scenarios of increasing difficulty. While performance is comparable on the simplest task, DFP demonstrates a substantial and widening advantage as the complexity of the navigation and combat tasks increases, outperforming A3C by a factor of 2.5-6 in the most challenging scenarios. This provides clear evidence for the effectiveness of the approach.

Furthermore, the paper successfully addresses the critical challenge of generalization. The experiments on randomly-textured mazes show that the learned policies can be applied to visually distinct, unseen environments. The results on goal-agnostic training are particularly impressive, demonstrating that an agent can be trained on randomized goals and then successfully pursue specific, new goals at test time with almost no loss in performance compared to an agent trained specifically for that task.

Finally, the ablation study provides valuable insight, confirming that both the use of a multi-dimensional measurement vector (as opposed to a single scalar reward) and the prediction of measurements at multiple future time steps are crucial for the method's success. The victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition serves as a powerful, real-world validation of the algorithm's capabilities, especially its ability to generalize to unseen environments against other competitive agents.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The paper is well-written and the empirical results are compelling. The following points are offered as constructive feedback to further strengthen the manuscript and are not part of my decision assessment.

*   The paper could benefit from a brief discussion on the implications of using an experience replay buffer. The training relies on Monte Carlo targets, which are calculated based on the outcomes of trajectories. This formulation implicitly assumes these outcomes are generated by the current policy. However, the replay buffer contains experiences gathered under previous versions of the policy, introducing an off-policy element to the training. While this approach clearly works well in practice, acknowledging and briefly discussing this potential discrepancy would be valuable.

*   The proposed method relies on access to a pre-defined set of low-dimensional "measurements" in addition to the raw sensory stream. This is a key part of the model and can be viewed as a form of domain knowledge or privileged information that the baseline methods, which operate solely on a scalar reward, do not require. I would encourage the authors to more explicitly discuss this distinction and the potential limitations of their approach. For instance, it would be helpful to address how the method might perform in domains where such meaningful, low-dimensional measurements are not readily available.
