This empirically driven work builds upon recent successes in deep reinforcement learning (RL), where deep neural networks are used as function approximators to solve problems in large state spaces. The authors introduce a novel algorithm that demonstrates superior performance and generalization in complex 3D environments, learning directly from raw sensory data. A key strength of the proposed method is its ability to generalize across different goals and environmental configurations. Notably, the algorithm was the winning entry in the Visual Doom AI competition, underscoring its practical effectiveness.

The core idea of the algorithm is to frame the learning problem as a supervised prediction task. The model learns to predict the future values of specific, low-dimensional measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) that are provided by the game engine. This prediction is conditioned on both the agent's current action and a specified goal vector, which is provided as input rather than learned. Once this predictive model is trained, the agent can select the optimal action at any given state by choosing the action that is predicted to maximize the outcome, as defined by the goal vector. This approach differs fundamentally from methods like successor feature representations in that the learning is purely supervised; it does not rely on temporal-difference (TD) learning or bootstrapping from the predictions of subsequent states.

The authors provide a thorough review of related work in Section 2, situating their approach relative to prior methods that involve predicting future states or using goal-conditioned value functions. The primary contributions of this paper can be summarized as: 1) the use of Monte Carlo estimation for supervised prediction targets, avoiding TD-based methods; 2) the novel idea of predicting low-dimensional, human-interpretable "measurements" instead of the full future state; 3) the use of a parameterized goal vector to enable generalization; and 4) a compelling empirical evaluation against relevant baselines.

Beyond the strong performance in the Visual Doom AI competition, the authors demonstrate that their method learns policies that generalize effectively. The trained agent can adapt to modifications in the goal vector at test time without requiring any additional training.

Overall, the paper is well-written, the methodology is clearly explained, and the empirical results are compelling. This work will be of significant interest to the deep reinforcement learning community.

**Minor Potential Improvements:**

*   The use of a replay buffer introduces a potential discrepancy in the training process. The Monte Carlo regression targets are calculated based on trajectories that are assumed to follow the *current* policy. However, the replay buffer contains trajectories generated by *previous* versions of the policy. This off-policy nature of the training data could be considered an approximation, and it would be beneficial for the authors to discuss its potential impact.
*   The proposed algorithm relies on the availability of specific, low-dimensional measurements (e.g., health, ammo) from the environment, which serve as the prediction targets. This is a form of privileged information that may not have been available to the baseline algorithms, potentially giving the proposed method an advantage. The authors should more explicitly acknowledge this and discuss the limitations of their approach in environments where such curated measurements are not provided.
