This empirically-driven paper builds on recent successes in Deep Reinforcement Learning, introducing a new algorithm that demonstrates superior performance and generalization in novel 3D environments using raw sensory data. Notably, the proposed algorithm was the winner of the Visual Doom AI competition and shows an ability to generalize across different goals and environments.

The core idea is to train a model to predict future low-dimensional measurements (e.g., ammunition or health, provided by the game engine) in a supervised manner. This prediction is conditioned on both a given goal vector and the current action. At test time, the agent selects the action that is predicted to maximize the outcome with respect to the specified goal. The authors note that, unlike successor representations, this learning process is purely supervised and does not involve a temporal-difference relationship between predictions at successive timesteps.

The authors provide a good overview of related work on future-state prediction and goal-conditioned function approximators. The main contributions of this paper are: the use of Monte Carlo estimation instead of temporal-difference learning for the predictions; the focus on predicting low-dimensional "measurements" rather than the full state; the use of parameterized goals to guide the agent; and, crucially, a strong empirical comparison against relevant prior work.

In addition to its strong performance in the Visual Doom AI competition, the algorithm learns generalizable policies that can respond to limited changes in the goal without requiring additional training. The paper is well-written, the empirical results are compelling, and the work should be of significant interest to the community.

Some minor potential improvements:

A potential weakness is an approximation made during training. The Monte Carlo regression assumes on-policy rollouts (i.e., that the remainder of the trajectory follows the current policy), yet the training data is sampled from a replay buffer containing episodes generated by previous policies. The implications of this on-policy/off-policy mismatch should be discussed.

The proposed algorithm relies on additional metadata specifying which parts of the sensory input are worth predicting—information that the compared algorithms do not use. This advantage, and the corresponding limitations of the approach (e.g., it may not be applicable in environments where such curated measurements are unavailable), should be more clearly acknowledged.
