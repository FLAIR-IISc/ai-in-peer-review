**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents an approach for sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments, framing the problem as a supervised learning task rather than traditional reinforcement learning. The core idea is to train an agent to predict future changes in a multi-dimensional stream of "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input, current measurements, a specified goal, and a potential action. By learning this predictive model from experience, the agent can select actions that are predicted to best satisfy its current goal, which is defined as a function of future measurements. The authors demonstrate that this method, which they call Direct Future Prediction (DFP), can be trained without a fixed goal and can generalize to new, dynamically specified goals at test time. The empirical evaluation in several complex Doom scenarios is extensive, showing that DFP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL baselines.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

Decision: Accept

The paper presents compelling empirical results for a simple and elegant formulation that warrants acceptance. The key reasons for this decision are:

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed method substantially outperforms strong, state-of-the-art deep RL baselines on a series of challenging 3D sensorimotor control tasks. The success in the Visual Doom AI Competition further validates the robustness and generalization capabilities of the approach in unseen environments.
2.  **Effective Use of Rich Feedback:** The paper makes a convincing case for leveraging dense, multi-dimensional measurement streams as a supervisory signal, moving beyond the standard sparse, scalar reward paradigm. The ablation study effectively demonstrates that this vectorial feedback is crucial to the model's success.

**Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The primary strength of this work lies in its impressive experimental results. The authors conduct a thorough evaluation across four increasingly difficult Doom scenarios. In the more complex "Battle" scenarios, DFP outperforms A3C by a significant margin (a factor of 2.5-6 in frag count), demonstrating its effectiveness in environments that require complex behaviors. The paper also provides strong evidence for the model's ability to generalize. Table 2 shows successful generalization across environments with different textures, and Table 3 shows that a model trained on randomized goals can perform nearly as well on a specific task as a specialist model, while also adapting effectively to novel, unseen goals (e.g., acting as a "pacifist").

The core idea of predicting future measurements is not entirely novel, and the authors appropriately place their work in the context of universal value functions and Monte Carlo methods. The contribution here is the successful distillation of these concepts into a practical and high-performing system for complex 3D environments. The proposed architecture, which splits the prediction into expectation and action streams, is well-motivated and appears effective. The ablation study in Table 4 provides clear support for the central claims of the paper: both the use of multiple measurements (vectorial feedback) and predicting at multiple future time steps are beneficial, validating the core design choices.

**Additional Feedback and Questions**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to the acceptance decision.

*   **Positioning the Contribution:** The paper effectively integrates several powerful ideas, and the method can be seen as a specific, practical instantiation of a universal value function approximator. While the authors cite the correct references, the contribution could be stated more clearly as a novel synthesis and successful application of these ideas to solve complex 3D control problems, which represents a significant engineering and empirical achievement.

*   **Analysis of Limitations:** The results on goal generalization are impressive. However, a discussion of the approach's limitations or failure modes would strengthen the paper. For instance, under what circumstances might the model fail to generalize to new goals? While training with randomized goals is effective, this relies on the training distribution covering the test distribution. It would be insightful to understand the model's behavior when presented with a goal that is fundamentally different from its training experience.

*   **Generalization to Other Domains:** Since a key argument is that the method is a general approach to sensorimotor control, demonstrating its effectiveness on other standard benchmarks would significantly bolster this claim. The Doom experiments are compelling, but applying the method to a domain like the Atari suite (perhaps using the game's RAM state as the measurement vector) would help clarify how broadly applicable the approach is, especially in environments where the set of intrinsic measurements is less obvious.

*   **Questions for the Authors:** Could you comment on the potential for catastrophic forgetting? The use of randomized goals during training is a key element for generalization. However, if the goal distribution for an agent were to shift over time (e.g., focusing on survival for a long period, then switching to aggression), would it retain its competence across all tasks without continuously re-training on a diverse set of goals from its past? A brief discussion of this potential limitation would be valuable.
