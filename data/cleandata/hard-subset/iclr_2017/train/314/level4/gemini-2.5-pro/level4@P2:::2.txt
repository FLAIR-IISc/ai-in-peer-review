### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which frames the problem as a supervised learning task rather than a traditional reinforcement learning one. The core idea is to leverage a rich, multi-dimensional stream of "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) in addition to the raw sensory input (e.g., visual frames). The model learns to predict future changes in these measurements, conditioned on its current state, a potential action, and a specified goal. By training the agent to predict the consequences of its actions, it can select the action that is predicted to best satisfy its current goal at test time. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach in the complex 3D environment of the game Doom. Their extensive experiments show that this method outperforms strong deep reinforcement learning baselines like A3C and DQN, particularly on challenging tasks. Furthermore, the paper provides compelling evidence that the trained agent can generalize to unseen environments and dynamically adapt to new goals not encountered during training. A model trained with this method notably won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Recommendation: Accept**

1.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides exceptionally strong and convincing empirical results. The proposed DFP method significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL baselines (A3C, DQN) across a series of increasingly difficult 3D navigation and combat scenarios. The victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition serves as a robust, independent validation of the model's effectiveness and generalization capabilities in unseen environments.

2.  **Compelling and Elegant Formulation:** The work successfully reframes the sensorimotor control problem in a way that elegantly sidesteps the challenge of sparse rewards. By using a dense, vectorial measurement stream as a supervisory signal, the model receives richer, more frequent feedback, which the ablation studies show is critical to its success. This goal-conditioned prediction framework is a valuable contribution that provides a distinct and effective alternative to conventional value-function or policy-gradient methods.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its rigorous experimental evaluation. The comparison against A3C and DQN in Section 4.2 is thorough, and the performance gap in the more complex "Battle" and "Battle 2" scenarios is striking (e.g., outperforming A3C by a factor of 6 in frags on D3). The ablation study in Table 4 convincingly demonstrates that both key components of the formulation—the use of vectorial feedback over a scalar reward and the prediction across multiple temporal offsets—are crucial for achieving this high level of performance. This directly supports the central claim that a dense, multivariate measurement stream is a superior training signal for complex tasks.

The paper's second major strength is the flexibility afforded by its goal-agnostic training regime. The experiments in Table 3 show that a model trained with randomized goals performs nearly as well on a specific task as a model trained exclusively for that task. More importantly, the randomly trained model generalizes far better to novel goals, such as adopting a "pacifist" strategy, demonstrating that the agent has learned a versatile and adaptable control policy rather than overfitting to a single objective.

The authors also do an excellent job of positioning their work within the existing literature. They correctly connect their approach to foundational ideas in universal value functions (Schaul et al., 2015) and supervised learning for control (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992). While the individual components (goal conditioning, future prediction) are not entirely new, their synthesis into the DFP framework and its successful application to a challenging, modern benchmark is a significant contribution.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   **Clarity of Contribution:** The paper's primary contribution appears to be the successful formulation and large-scale validation of a supervised prediction approach for complex control, effectively distilling and implementing ideas from prior work in a powerful new way. This is a strong contribution in itself. However, the abstract and introduction could be sharpened to more explicitly frame the contribution as this specific, highly effective synthesis and demonstration, rather than implying the invention of a fundamentally new algorithmic paradigm.

*   **Failure Modes and Limitations:** The goal generalization results are impressive. However, a discussion of the potential failure modes would strengthen the paper. For example, under what circumstances does the model fail to generalize to a new goal? Does performance degrade if the test-time goal is far outside the distribution of goals sampled during training?

*   **Generalization to Other Domains:** The results in Doom are excellent. To further bolster the claims of generality, it would be interesting to see results on other standard domains like the Atari suite. An experiment using the game's RAM state as the measurement vector `m_t` could provide valuable insights into how the DFP framework performs in a different context and whether it offers advantages over traditional RL methods there as well.

I have a few questions for the authors that would clarify my understanding:

1.  The paper uses an experience memory, which suggests a departure from a pure on-policy method. Could you clarify how this interacts with the training stability, especially when the goal distribution changes over time? For example, if the agent trains on one set of goals for an extended period and then switches to a new, disjoint set, does it suffer from catastrophic forgetting of how to achieve the older goals?

2.  The selection of measurements seems critical to the success of this method. How sensitive is the model's performance to this choice? For instance, what happens in the "Battle" scenario if an irrelevant or noisy measurement is added to the vector `m_t`, or if only the frag count is provided (as partially explored in the ablation)?
