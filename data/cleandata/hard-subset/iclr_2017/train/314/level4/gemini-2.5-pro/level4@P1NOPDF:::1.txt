Deep reinforcement learning (RL), which uses deep neural networks as function approximators, has had a number of successes in solving problems with large state spaces. This paper presents an empirically driven work that builds on these successes. It introduces a new algorithm that achieves strong performance in novel 3D environments using raw sensory data and demonstrates improved generalization across different goals and environments. Notably, this algorithm was the winner of the Visual Doom AI competition.

The key idea of the proposed algorithm is to use additional low-dimensional observations (e.g., ammo or health, which are provided by the game engine) as supervised targets for a prediction task. Crucially, this prediction is conditioned on both a user-specified goal vector and the current action. Once the model is trained, the optimal action for a given state can be chosen by selecting the action that maximizes the predicted outcome with respect to the specified goal. This approach differs from successor representations in that the learning is fully supervised, and there is no temporal difference (TD) relationship between the predictions for the current and next states.

The authors review a number of prior works in Section 2, covering both future-state prediction in RL and goal-conditioned function approximators. The key contributions of this work are its focus on Monte Carlo estimation (rather than TD methods), its use of low-dimensional 'measurements' for prediction, its framework for parameterized goals, and, perhaps most importantly, its thorough empirical comparison to relevant prior work.

In addition to the competitive results in the Visual Doom AI competition, the authors demonstrate that their algorithm learns generalizable policies that can adapt to moderate changes in the goal vector without any additional training.

The paper is well-written, and the empirical results are compelling. This work should be of significant interest to the deep RL community.

**Minor Points for Improvement:**

1.  The supervised training involves an approximation that should be discussed more explicitly. The Monte Carlo regression assumes that the remainder of a trajectory follows the current policy (an on-policy assumption). However, the training data is sampled from a replay buffer containing episodes generated by previous versions of the policy. This discrepancy between the on-policy assumption and the off-policy nature of the training data should be acknowledged and discussed.

2.  The proposed algorithm relies on access to specific, low-dimensional measurements (e.g., health, ammo) that serve as prediction targets. This use of 'metadata'—knowing which variables are important to predict—is a key difference from the compared algorithms, which operate on raw sensory input alone. The paper should more clearly state this requirement and discuss its limitations. For instance, the approach may not be directly applicable in environments where such structured, low-dimensional measurements are unavailable.
