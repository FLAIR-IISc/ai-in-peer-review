Deep reinforcement learning, which utilizes deep neural networks as function approximators, has demonstrated significant success in solving tasks within large state spaces. This empirically driven paper builds upon this line of work, introducing a novel algorithm that achieves superior performance and generalization in complex 3D environments using raw sensory inputs. A notable achievement of this work is its first-place performance in the Visual Doom AI competition.

The core technical contribution is an approach that uses auxiliary, low-dimensional observations provided by the environment (e.g., health, ammunition) as supervised prediction targets. This prediction is conditioned on both a user-specified goal vector and the agent's current action. During execution, the agent selects the action that is predicted to maximize the outcome with respect to the given goal. This formulation is distinct from methods like successor representations, as the learning process is fully supervised via Monte Carlo estimation and does not enforce a temporal difference relationship between the predictions of consecutive states.

The authors situate their work effectively relative to prior research in future state prediction and goal-driven function approximation. The key contributions of this paper are its focus on Monte Carlo estimation rather than temporal difference learning for this task, the use of low-dimensional measurements as prediction targets, the incorporation of parametrized goals, and, most significantly, the compelling empirical comparison against relevant prior work.

The experimental results are strong. Beyond the impressive performance in the Visual Doom AI competition, the authors demonstrate that their algorithm learns generalizable policies. These policies can adapt to moderate changes in the goal vector without requiring any additional training, highlighting a key advantage of the proposed method.

The paper is well-communicated, and the empirical results are convincing, making it a valuable contribution that should be of significant interest to the community. The following are suggestions for minor improvements:

1.  **On-Policy Assumption with Off-Policy Data:** The supervised training loop contains an approximation that should be explicitly discussed. The Monte Carlo regression for a state-action pair relies on the expectation of the trajectory's remainder, assuming it follows the *current* policy. However, training samples are drawn from a replay buffer, which contains episodes generated by *previous* versions of the policy. This introduces an off-policy element to an otherwise on-policy estimation, and the potential impact of this mismatch warrants discussion.

2.  **Reliance on Privileged Information:** The proposed algorithm relies on access to specific metadata—the low-dimensional game variables that serve as prediction targets—which are not available to the baseline algorithms used for comparison (e.g., A3C). This represents a significant difference in the information provided to the agents. The paper would be strengthened by a more prominent discussion of this dependency and its limitations. For instance, the authors should clarify that the approach, in its current form, may not be readily applicable to real-world or simulated environments where such curated measurements are not provided. Acknowledging this would provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's scope and applicability.
