This empirical paper builds upon recent successes in Deep Reinforcement Learning, which has proven effective for solving problems with high-dimensional state spaces. The authors introduce a novel algorithm that achieves superior performance and generalization across various goals and environments within new 3D domains, using raw sensory data as input. Notably, this method was the winning entry in the Visual Doom AI competition.

The central concept of the algorithm is to utilize auxiliary low-dimensional observations, such as health or ammunition provided by the environment, as targets for a supervised prediction task. This prediction is critically conditioned on both the current action and a provided, fixed goal vector. Once the predictive model is trained, the agent can select the optimal action for any given state by choosing the one that maximizes the predicted outcome in accordance with the desired goal. This approach differs from successor feature methods in that the learning is purely supervised and does not rely on a temporal-difference relationship between predictions for consecutive states.

The authors position their work relative to existing literature on future-state prediction and goal-conditioned learning, as reviewed in Section 2. The primary contributions of this work include the use of Monte Carlo estimation instead of TD-based methods, the strategic use of low-dimensional "measurements" as prediction targets, the incorporation of parametrized goals, and, most importantly, the thorough empirical evaluation against relevant prior work.

In addition to the competitive results, the authors demonstrate that their algorithm learns generalizable policies. These policies can adapt to moderate changes in the goal at execution time without requiring any retraining.

The paper is well-written, and the empirical results are persuasive, making this work of high interest to the community.

Some minor suggestions for improvement:

*   The supervised training process involves an approximation. Specifically, the Monte Carlo regression target assumes an on-policy trajectory expectation (i.e., that the future follows the current policy), yet the model is trained using data from a replay buffer, which contains episodes generated by previous versions of the policy. This discrepancy should be discussed.

*   The algorithm leverages additional metadata to identify which sensory inputs are worth predicting—an advantage not available to the baseline methods it is compared against. This point, and the resulting limitations of the approach (e.g., its potential inefficacy in environments where such specific measurements are not provided), should be stated more explicitly.
