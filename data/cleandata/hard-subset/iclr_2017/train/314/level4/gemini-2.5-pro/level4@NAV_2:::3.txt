This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which frames the learning problem as a supervised task of predicting future intrinsic measurements. The model is trained to predict a vector of future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frags) based on the current raw sensory input, current measurements, and a specified goal. This formulation provides a dense, rich supervisory signal from the agent's own experience, allows for training without a fixed objective, and enables the agent to pursue dynamically changing goals at test time by selecting actions that maximize the predicted outcome according to the current goal.

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. My decision is based on two primary reasons:
1.  The proposed method achieves impressive, state-of-the-art results on a series of challenging tasks, culminating in winning the Visual Doom AI Competition.
2.  The experimental evaluation is exceptionally thorough and methodologically sound, providing strong evidence to support the paper's claims regarding the benefits of vectorial feedback and goal-agnostic training.

The core strength of this work is its compelling empirical validation. The authors demonstrate that their relatively simple DFP model significantly outperforms established and sophisticated deep reinforcement learning baselines, including DQN and A3C, particularly on complex tasks that involve combat and navigation in 3D environments. The victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition is a milestone achievement, underscoring the model's effectiveness and its ability to generalize to previously unseen environments, a critical challenge in the field.

Furthermore, the paper's claims are substantiated by a comprehensive set of experiments. The ablation study in Table 4 clearly demonstrates the value of the key components of the proposed approach, showing that using a vectorial measurement stream is significantly more effective than a traditional scalar reward, and that predicting measurements at multiple future time steps is also beneficial. The experiments on goal-agnostic training convincingly show that the model can learn general skills without a predefined goal and adapt its behavior to novel objectives at test time, performing nearly as well as a model trained specifically for that task. While the concepts of forward modeling and predicting auxiliary variables are not new, the paper positions its work well within the literature and presents a simple, well-executed formulation that is shown to be highly effective. The inclusion of appendices with details on the architecture and baselines enhances the reproducibility of the work.

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the final version of the paper and are not critical to my assessment:

*   For technical accuracy, it may be worth noting that the Doom engine is often described as "2.5D" rather than fully 3D, as elements like height are not truly actionable dimensions in the same way as the 2D plane. This is a minor point but could add precision.
*   The notation `p` used for the prediction in Equation 2 could be slightly confusing, as `P` is conventionally used for probability distributions in machine learning literature. A different variable might enhance clarity.
*   In Section 3.2, the use of `j` for the index in the summation of Equation 6 and `j` for the joint input representation in the text immediately preceding it could be a source of minor confusion.
*   In the results tables, particularly Table 1, explicitly stating the units for each measurement (e.g., "Health (%)", "Frags (count)") in the column headers could improve readability, especially given the heterogeneous nature of the metrics.
