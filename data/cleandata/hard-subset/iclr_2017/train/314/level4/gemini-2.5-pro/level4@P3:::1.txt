### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in complex, 3D environments, which frames the problem as a supervised learning task rather than a traditional reinforcement learning one. The core idea is to leverage a low-dimensional "measurement" stream (e.g., health, ammunition) that is co-temporal with a high-dimensional sensory stream (raw pixels). The model, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), is trained to predict future changes in these measurements, conditioned on the current sensory input, current measurements, a potential action, and a parameterized goal vector. At test time, the agent selects the action that maximizes the predicted outcome according to its current goal.

This formulation provides a dense, multi-dimensional supervisory signal that proves highly effective for training. A key contribution is the demonstration that this approach allows for training a single, goal-agnostic policy that can be directed to pursue dynamically changing goals at test time without retraining. The authors conduct extensive experiments in the challenging Visual Doom environment, showing that their method significantly outperforms strong deep reinforcement learning baselines like A3C and DQN, particularly on more complex tasks. The method's effectiveness is compellingly validated by its victory in the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, which required generalization to previously unseen environments.

### **Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper with a clear and well-motivated contribution, backed by compelling empirical results. My decision to accept is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Novel and Effective Formulation:** The paper successfully reframes a challenging reinforcement learning problem into a supervised learning task. This simple yet powerful idea of directly predicting future measurements provides a richer training signal than a scalar reward, leading to superior performance and stability.
2.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Validation:** The claims are rigorously supported through extensive experiments that not only demonstrate state-of-the-art performance against relevant baselines but also include insightful ablation studies and tests of generalization across both environments and goals.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main strength lies in its elegant formulation and the strong empirical evidence supporting it. The authors motivate their departure from standard value-based RL methods by arguing that the sparse scalar reward signal is a bottleneck in complex, immersive environments where richer feedback is naturally available. By training the model to predict a vector of future measurements, the agent receives a much denser and more informative learning signal at every step. The results in Table 1 and Figure 3 clearly show that while DFP is competitive on simpler tasks, its advantage becomes substantial in the more complex "Battle" scenarios, where it outperforms A3C by a factor of up to 6.

Furthermore, the paper makes a significant contribution to learning flexible, goal-oriented behaviors. The idea of conditioning the prediction on a goal vector `g` is similar in spirit to work on Universal Value Functions, but the application here within a supervised, Monte Carlo-style prediction framework is novel. The experiments in Table 3 convincingly show that a model trained with randomized goals performs nearly as well on a specific task as a specialist model, while gaining the ability to generalize to entirely new, unseen goals (e.g., acting as a "pacifist") without any fine-tuning.

The experimental methodology is sound. The authors compare against strong, contemporary baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) and are transparent about their hyperparameter tuning process. The ablation study in Table 4 effectively isolates the benefits of the core contributions, confirming that using vectorial feedback is superior to a scalar reward and that predicting at multiple future time steps is beneficial. Finally, the win in the Visual Doom AI Competition serves as a powerful, unbiased validation of the approach's effectiveness and generalization capabilities in a competitive setting.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The paper is well-written and the work is of high quality. The following points are suggestions aimed at further strengthening the paper and are not critical to my decision.

1.  **On-Policy vs. Off-Policy Training:** The training procedure uses an experience replay buffer, meaning the future measurements (`f_i`) used as regression targets are generated by older policies. However, the Monte Carlo formulation implicitly assumes these futures are rollouts from the *current* policy. This off-policy nature of the training data could introduce a mismatch. While the empirical results show this is not a practical issue, a brief discussion of this potential discrepancy and why it does not seem to adversely affect performance would strengthen the paper's analysis.
2.  **Assumption of a "Measurement" Stream:** The proposed method relies on a pre-defined, low-dimensional "measurement" stream (e.g., health, ammo) that is distinct from the raw sensory input. This is a key part of the model's success but also a potential limitation. The baselines, in contrast, are typically trained on a single scalar reward derived from these same underlying game-state changes. It would be beneficial to explicitly discuss this distinction and the assumptions it entails more prominently. Clarifying that the DFP approach leverages additional structure in the observation space (the separation of `s_t` and `m_t`) would help frame the contribution and its limitations more precisely.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  The reliance on a curated measurement stream is a key design choice. Could you comment on the applicability of this method to environments where such low-dimensional, semantically meaningful measurements are not provided? For instance, could the model learn to identify or construct these measurements itself from the high-dimensional sensory stream?
2.  Could you elaborate on the potential mismatch from using an experience replay buffer—which contains trajectories from older policies—to generate supervised targets for a predictor that is then used by the current policy? Is there a risk of feedback loops or instability, and if so, how is it mitigated?
