This paper introduces an on-policy method designed to forecast future intrinsic measurements. The entirety of the experimental validation is conducted within the ViZDoom environment. Instead of predicting a singular outcome like a win/loss condition or overall score, the model is trained to forecast a sequence of measurement triplets corresponding to health, ammunition, and frags. These predictions are weighted by a user-specified "goal" triplet provided as an input. This framework allows for guided exploration by altering the weights of the goal triplet, while at test time, the agent acts to maximize this long-term objective.

The empirical results are compelling, a point reinforced by the model's first-place finish in the 2016 ViZDoom competition. The paper's experimental validation appears methodologically sound:
- The proposed DFP method is benchmarked against several key baselines, including A3C, DQN, and a comparison is made with the related DSR approach. DFP is shown to outperform or match the performance of these alternatives, particularly in scenarios where methods like A3C reach a performance ceiling (e.g., scenario D1).
- An included ablation study effectively justifies the model's components, demonstrating that its added complexity contributes meaningfully to its performance.

The work is well-situated within established reinforcement learning paradigms, such as intrinsic motivation, auxiliary task learning, and forward modeling. The reviewed version of the manuscript (dated December 4th) appropriately credits prior art, though the survey of related literature is not exhaustive.

Several minor points regarding the presentation:
- The description of Doom as a 3D environment is slightly inaccurate; it is more precisely a 2.5D environment where the vertical dimension is not a factor in gameplay.
- The use of "P" in Equation (2) and thereafter could be confusing, as it denotes "prediction" rather than the more conventional "probability."
- In Equation (6), the use of "j" in two different fonts for distinct variables may cause ambiguity.
- The results tables, especially Table 1 with its heterogeneous measurements, would benefit from explicitly restating the units for each metric to improve clarity.

In my view, this paper warrants a clear acceptance. While one might critique the work for its focus on a single experimental domain or for its incremental novelty, I believe its value as a methodologically sound and significant contribution is paramount. Papers that are "correct"—presenting robust, reproducible experiments (with implementation details in the appendix) and achieving a notable milestone like winning the ViZDoom competition—are essential for the field and merit publication.
