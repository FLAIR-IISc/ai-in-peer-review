This paper presents an on-policy deep reinforcement learning method augmented with auxiliary intrinsic variables for goal-oriented tasks. The approach is a specific implementation of a universal value function, and the authors appropriately reference prior work in this area. The primary technical contribution appears to be the successful distillation and integration of several existing ideas to solve complex 3D navigation problems. However, the paper would be strengthened if these contributions were more explicitly articulated in the abstract and introduction to better frame the work.

The evaluation would be more comprehensive with an analysis of the method's failure modes. For instance, under what circumstances does the model struggle to generalize to novel or dynamically changing goals? A potential conceptual issue also arises from the on-policy nature of the algorithm, which makes it susceptible to catastrophic forgetting. If the agent is not continuously exposed to a diverse set of goals, including those learned in the distant past, its performance on older tasks may degrade. The authors should discuss this potential limitation and any mechanisms in their training procedure designed to mitigate it.

Given that the paper's central contribution is the empirical demonstration of an integrated framework, its claims of generality would be significantly bolstered by results in other domains. Applying the method to different environments, such as the Atari suite (for example, by using the game's RAM state as intrinsic variables), would provide a more compelling case for its broader applicability.

Overall, the paper successfully demonstrates a clear empirical advantage for the proposed formulation on the chosen tasks. The experimental insights provided are valuable and could inform the development of future agents for complex, goal-conditioned problems.
