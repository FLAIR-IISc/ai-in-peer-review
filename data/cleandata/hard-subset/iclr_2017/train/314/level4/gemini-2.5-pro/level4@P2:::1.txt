### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel and effective approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which frames the learning problem in terms of supervised learning rather than traditional reinforcement learning. The core idea is to leverage a low-dimensional stream of "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) in addition to the high-dimensional sensory input (raw pixels). The model is trained to predict future measurements conditioned on the current state, a potential action, and a parameterized goal vector. At test time, the agent selects the action that maximizes the predicted outcome according to its current goal.

The authors make several significant contributions. They demonstrate that this supervised, Monte Carlo-style prediction approach can outperform state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods like A3C and DQN in complex, 3D first-person environments. A key advantage of this formulation is its ability to learn general skills without a fixed goal during training, allowing the agent to pursue dynamically specified goals at test time without retraining. The strength and generalization capabilities of this approach are compellingly demonstrated by its victory in the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, which was held in previously unseen environments.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper introduces a simple yet powerful reformulation of the sensorimotor control problem that yields state-of-the-art empirical results on challenging 3D tasks. The method's performance significantly surpasses that of more complex deep RL baselines.
2.  The model demonstrates impressive generalization, both to new goals not seen during training and to entirely new environments, as validated by its success in the Visual Doom AI Competition.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The primary strength of this work lies in its elegant reconceptualization of the learning problem. By replacing the often sparse, scalar reward signal of traditional RL with a dense, multivariate stream of measurements, the authors provide a rich supervisory signal that stabilizes and accelerates training. The experimental results strongly support this claim. In the complex "Battle" scenarios (D3 and D4), DFP outperforms the highly-regarded A3C baseline by a substantial margin (a factor of 6 in D3), highlighting the efficacy of the approach when richer feedback is available. The ablation study further solidifies this point, showing that predicting multiple measurements is significantly more effective than predicting a single scalar value (frags), even when frags are the sole evaluation metric.

The second major strength is the model's ability to learn flexible, goal-conditioned policies. The experiments in Section 4.2 show that a model trained with randomized goals performs nearly as well on a specific task as a model trained exclusively for that task. More importantly, the goal-agnostic model generalizes far better to novel objectives, such as adopting a "pacifist" strategy. This capability to decouple skill acquisition from the specification of a final goal is a significant step towards more general and reusable agents. This flexibility, combined with the model's capacity to generalize across visually distinct environments (Table 2), was powerfully validated by the win in the Doom AI competition, where the agent had to perform in environments it had never seen before. The paper is well-written, the experiments are thorough, and the empirical results are compelling and will be of significant interest to the community.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.*

1.  **On-policy Estimation with Off-policy Data:** The training procedure uses an experience replay buffer, meaning the Monte Carlo targets (`fi`) are calculated from trajectories generated by older versions of the policy. However, the supervised training implicitly assumes these targets are the result of following the *current* policy. This introduces a subtle off-policy element into a fundamentally on-policy estimation framework. While this does not appear to harm performance, it would strengthen the paper to explicitly acknowledge and briefly discuss this approximation.

2.  **Reliance on Curated Measurements:** The DFP approach's success hinges on the availability of a meaningful, low-dimensional measurement stream that is provided by the environment (in this case, the game engine). This is a stronger assumption than that of standard RL algorithms, which only require a scalar reward. It would be beneficial to more clearly position this as a key assumption of the method and discuss the potential limitations. For example, the paper could briefly touch upon how the approach might fare in environments where such explicit measurements are not available.

### **Questions for Authors**

1.  Following up on the feedback point regarding the replay buffer: Could you clarify why the off-policy nature of the training data does not seem to adversely affect the stability or performance of your supervised learning approach? Have you experimented with the size of the replay buffer or with a purely on-policy training scheme?

2.  The approach relies on a pre-defined set of measurements {mt}. How do you envision this method being applied in real-world scenarios, such as robotics, where clean, curated measurements might not be readily available or may need to be learned? Does this reliance on pre-defined measurements limit the generality of the approach compared to traditional RL frameworks?

3.  The ablation study shows that predicting measurements at multiple future time steps is beneficial. Could you provide more intuition on why this multi-timescale prediction helps? Does it function as a form of implicit regularization, or does it enable the agent to better model the environment's dynamics?
