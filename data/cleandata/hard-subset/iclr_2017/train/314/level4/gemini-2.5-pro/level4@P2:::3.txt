### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a novel approach for sensorimotor control in complex environments. The core idea is to reframe the control problem as a supervised learning task. Instead of maximizing a scalar reward signal, the agent learns to predict the future values of a low-dimensional stream of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) based on its high-dimensional sensory input (raw pixels). The agent's goal is provided as a weighted combination of these future measurements, allowing for dynamic goal-setting at test time. This formulation provides a rich, temporally dense supervisory signal derived directly from the agent's experience. The authors demonstrate their method in the challenging first-person environment of Doom, showing that it significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods. Notably, an agent trained with this approach won the Full Deathmatch track of the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that presents a simple yet powerful idea backed by impressive and thorough experimental validation. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Excellent Empirical Results:** The proposed DFP method substantially outperforms strong, state-of-the-art deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C) on a series of complex visuomotor control tasks. The success in the Visual Doom AI Competition on previously unseen maps provides compelling evidence of the method's effectiveness and generalization capabilities in a competitive setting.
2.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Design:** The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments that rigorously support its claims. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the benefits of using vectorial, multi-step predictions over a scalar reward. Furthermore, the experiments on goal-agnostic training and generalization across environments effectively showcase the flexibility and robustness of the proposed framework.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's claims are well-supported by a strong empirical foundation. The comparison with established deep RL baselines is fair and illuminating. In the more complex "Battle" and "Battle 2" scenarios, DFP outperforms A3C by a factor of 6 and 2.5, respectively, in terms of frag count. This highlights the advantage of the dense, multivariate supervisory signal provided by the measurement stream, especially when tasks involve multiple competing objectives.

The ablation study in Table 4 is particularly convincing. It systematically dismantles the model to show that both predicting multiple measurements (vectorial feedback) and predicting at multiple future time steps are critical to achieving peak performance. This directly supports the central thesis that a rich measurement stream is a more effective training signal than a single scalar reward for this class of problems.

The paper also successfully demonstrates two key advantages of its formulation: generalization and flexibility. The experiments in Table 2 show that training on varied textures leads to models that generalize well to novel environments. The goal-agnostic training results in Table 3 are equally impressive, showing that a single model trained on randomized goals can perform nearly as well on a specific task as a specialized model, while also being capable of pursuing novel and even contradictory goals (e.g., "pacifist") at test time. The detailed appendices provide sufficient information to promote reproducibility.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are suggestions for further strengthening the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Nomenclature and Clarity:**
    *   A minor point on terminology: The Doom engine is technically 2.5D (level geometry is 2D with height information) rather than fully 3D. While this does not diminish the complexity of the visual task, acknowledging this distinction could add precision.
    *   In the model description, the prediction `p_at` could perhaps be denoted differently (e.g., `ŷ_at`) to avoid any potential, however slight, confusion with the common use of 'P' or 'p' for probabilities in machine learning literature.
    *   For improved readability, consider adding the specific measurement units to the headers of the results tables (e.g., "Health (avg.)", "Frags (avg.)"), especially in Table 1 where results from different task objectives are presented together.

*   **Positioning in Literature:** The related work section is solid. To further strengthen it, you could briefly discuss the relationship between the DFP framework and other methods that use auxiliary prediction tasks or intrinsic motivation to enrich the learning signal, which is a common strategy for tackling sparse reward problems in RL.

### Questions for the Authors

Your responses to these questions would help clarify my understanding and strengthen the paper.

1.  The choice of temporal offsets (τ = 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32) seems crucial. How sensitive is the model's performance to this specific configuration? Was this set determined empirically, and have you explored other schemes, such as linear spacing or a longer prediction horizon?
2.  The goal vector `g` is an input to the network, which is a powerful feature of the model. Have you performed any analysis on the learned joint representation `j` to understand how it is modulated by `g`? Does the model learn distinct, goal-conditioned features in its intermediate layers?
3.  The paper notes that the distinction between the sensory stream `s_t` and measurement stream `m_t` is "somewhat artificial." Have you considered an extension where the model learns to predict future values of features learned directly from the sensory stream (e.g., via an autoencoder), thus removing the dependency on explicit, game-engine provided measurements?
