This empirically-driven paper builds on recent successes in Deep Reinforcement Learning (Deep RL), where deep neural networks serve as function approximators in large state spaces. The authors introduce a novel algorithm that demonstrates strong performance and generalization across various goals and environments, using raw sensory data from complex 3D worlds. Notably, this algorithm was the winner of the Visual Doom AI competition.

The key idea is to use additional low-dimensional observations provided by the environment (e.g., health, ammunition) as supervised targets for a prediction network. This prediction is conditioned on both a given goal vector and the current action. Once the network is trained, the agent can select the optimal action for any state by choosing the action that maximizes the predicted outcome according to the desired goal. Unlike approaches such as successor representations, the learning is fully supervised through Monte Carlo estimation, with no temporal difference (TD) relationship between the predictions of successive states.

The authors situate their work relative to prior methods in future-state prediction and goal-driven architectures. The primary contributions of this work are its focus on Monte Carlo estimation over TD learning, the use of low-dimensional "measurements" for prediction, the parameterization of goals, and, importantly, a compelling empirical comparison against relevant prior work. In addition to their competition-winning results, the authors show that their algorithm learns generalizable policies that can respond to limited changes in the goal without any further training. The paper is well-communicated, and the empirical results are convincing and of significant interest.

Some minor potential improvements:
1.  The supervised training involves an approximation that should be discussed. The Monte Carlo regression assumes an on-policy expectation for the remainder of a trajectory (i.e., that it follows the current policy), yet it learns from a replay buffer containing episodes generated by prior versions of the policy. The implications of this on-policy assumption with off-policy data should be addressed.
2.  The algorithm uses additional metadata—specifically, which low-dimensional game variables to predict—that the compared algorithms do not have access to. It would strengthen the paper to more clearly acknowledge this advantage and discuss the limitations of this approach, such as its applicability in environments where such curated measurements are not provided.
