### Paper Summary

This paper presents an approach for learning sensorimotor control in complex, immersive 3D environments by framing the problem as supervised learning. The key idea is to leverage the rich, temporally dense supervisory signal that arises from the cotemporal structure of a high-dimensional sensory stream (raw pixels) and a lower-dimensional measurement stream (e.g., health, ammunition). Instead of maximizing a scalar reward, the model is trained to predict future measurements conditioned on the current state, a proposed action, and a parameterized goal. This formulation allows the agent to be trained on its own experience without a fixed goal, and to pursue dynamically specified goals at test time by selecting actions that are predicted to best satisfy the current objective. The authors provide compelling empirical validation in challenging Doom scenarios, demonstrating that their method, Direct Future Prediction (DFP), significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning baselines. The effectiveness of the approach is further underscored by its victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition on previously unseen maps.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates a clear and significant performance advantage over well-established deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C) on a series of challenging 3D sensorimotor control tasks. The win in the Visual Doom AI competition provides powerful, independent validation of the method's effectiveness and generalization capabilities.
2.  **Valuable Formulation and Insights:** The work effectively distills and applies existing ideas in a simple yet powerful formulation. By framing control as a supervised prediction problem that leverages rich, multi-dimensional feedback, it provides a compelling alternative to standard scalar reward-based RL. The ablation studies convincingly support the central claims that vectorial feedback and multi-step prediction are critical to the method's success.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this paper lies in its extensive and well-executed experiments. The comparison against strong baselines like A3C is fair, with the authors making a clear effort to tune the baselines. DFP's performance gains are not marginal; it outperforms A3C by a factor of 2.5-6x on the more complex "Battle" scenarios. This highlights the practical value of the proposed approach in environments where dense, multi-dimensional feedback is available.

The paper's claims are well-supported by targeted experiments. The ablation study (Table 4) provides direct evidence for the superiority of using vectorial feedback over a scalar reward and predicting at multiple temporal offsets. Furthermore, the experiments on goal-agnostic training (Table 3) successfully demonstrate the model's ability to generalize to novel goals not seen during training, which is a significant advantage over methods trained for a single, fixed objective. The generalization experiments across different textures and environments (Table 2) further strengthen the claims of robustness.

While the conceptual building blocks (supervised learning for control, universal value functions) are not entirely novel, and the authors correctly cite the relevant literature (e.g., Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992; Schaul et al., 2015), the paper's contribution is in its specific, elegant formulation and the demonstration of its remarkable effectiveness on a difficult, modern benchmark. The paper convincingly argues that for a certain class of problems—immersive environments with intrinsic measurements—this direct prediction approach can be substantially more sample-efficient and performant than contemporary deep RL methods.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not part of the core assessment for my decision.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Clarifying Contribution:** While the related work is well-covered, the introduction could more explicitly state that the main contribution is the synthesis of these ideas into a simple, scalable architecture and the extensive empirical proof of its superiority on complex 3D tasks, rather than a fundamentally new learning paradigm.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the potential failure modes. Under what circumstances would this approach be less suitable than traditional RL? For example, in environments with extremely sparse measurements where long-term credit assignment is necessary, TD-based methods might have an advantage.
*   **Broader Empirical Scope:** The focus on Doom is excellent for demonstrating performance in immersive 3D environments. To broaden the paper's impact, it would be interesting to see results on other domains, such as the Arcade Learning Environment, using game-specific variables (e.g., score, lives, level) from RAM as the measurement stream. This could help delineate the range of problems where DFP is most effective.

**Questions:**
*   The paper notes that the training setup "departs from pure on-policy training" by using a small experience memory. Could you comment on the potential for catastrophic forgetting if the distribution of randomized goals were to shift significantly over a long training period? Does the agent need to continually revisit a wide range of goals to maintain its ability to generalize?
*   The goal generalization results are very promising. Did you observe any specific failure modes? For example, does the agent struggle to generalize to goals that represent non-linear or conflicting combinations of the base measurements (e.g., a goal that requires maximizing frags while minimizing ammunition usage)?
