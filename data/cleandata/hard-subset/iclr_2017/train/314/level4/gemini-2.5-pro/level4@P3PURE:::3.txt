This paper presents an on-policy method for directly predicting future intrinsic measurements from an environment. The experiments are conducted exclusively in the VizDoom environment, where the model is trained to predict a sequence of future measurement triplets (health, ammunition, frags). This prediction is conditioned on a "goal" triplet provided as input, which effectively weights the importance of each measurement. Modifying the weights of this goal vector provides a mechanism for guiding the agent's exploration. At test time, the agent acts to maximize the predicted long-term measurements according to a fixed goal.

The results are impressive, highlighted by the fact that this model won the 2016 VizDoom competition. The experimental evaluation is sound and thorough:
- The authors provide comparisons against strong baselines, including A3C, DQN, and a similar recent approach, DSR. The proposed Direct Future Prediction (DFP) method outperforms or matches the performance of these baselines, particularly in scenarios where methods like A3C reach a performance ceiling.
- An ablation study validates the utility of the model's components, justifying the design choices.

The paper is well-situated within established reinforcement learning domains such as intrinsic motivation, auxiliary task learning, and forward modeling. The version of the paper reviewed (December 4th revision) adequately references prior work, though the literature review is not exhaustive.

A few minor comments on the presentation:
- The paper describes Doom as a 3D environment. More accurately, it is a 2D environment with a pseudo-3D renderer, as height is not an actionable or discriminative dimension.
- The use of "P" in Equation (2) and subsequent equations for "prediction" could be confusing, as "P" typically denotes a probability distribution in this context.
- In Equation (6), the use of "j" for two different indices (distinguished only by font) could be misleading.
- The results tables, particularly Table 1, would be clearer if the units for the heterogeneous measurements were repeated in the table headers or captions.

Overall, this paper is a clear accept. While one could argue that the novelty is somewhat limited or that experiments could have been conducted on a broader range of environments, the paper makes a significant contribution. It presents a method that is methodologically sound, experimentally robust on a challenging benchmark, and highly reproducible thanks to the details provided in the appendix. The model's success in the VizDoom competition marks a milestone achievement. Such well-executed and impactful work warrants publication.
