### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel and effective approach for sensorimotor control in complex, immersive 3D environments. The core idea is to reframe the control problem as a supervised learning task, leveraging the rich, temporally-dense supervisory signal provided by the environment. Instead of relying on a sparse scalar reward, the proposed model learns to predict future changes in a low-dimensional "measurement stream" (e.g., health, ammunition) from raw, high-dimensional sensory input (e.g., pixels). This prediction is conditioned on both the agent's action and a parameterized goal vector. At test time, the agent selects the action that is predicted to best satisfy its current goal.

The authors provide compelling empirical evidence for their method, Direct Future Prediction (DFP), in a series of challenging scenarios within the ViZDoom environment. The key contributions are: (1) a simple and powerful formulation for goal-directed control based on supervised, Monte-Carlo-style future prediction; (2) demonstrating state-of-the-art performance against strong deep reinforcement learning baselines like A3C and DQN; and (3) showing remarkable generalization capabilities to unseen environments and dynamically changing goals without retraining. The method's success is powerfully underscored by its win in the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper with convincing results that will be of significant interest to the community. The key reasons for this decision are:

1.  **Exceptional Empirical Performance:** The proposed method significantly outperforms established and state-of-the-art deep RL baselines on complex 3D visuomotor control tasks. The simplicity of the approach relative to its high performance is particularly noteworthy.
2.  **Strong Generalization and Flexibility:** The paper demonstrates that a single trained model can generalize effectively across different environments and adapt to a variety of novel, dynamically-specified goals at test time, a key challenge in reinforcement learning.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's claims are thoroughly supported by a well-designed set of experiments. The comparison against strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across four scenarios of increasing difficulty clearly establishes the superiority of the DFP approach, especially in the more complex "Battle" environments where the multi-dimensional measurement stream is richest (Table 1, Figure 3).

The generalization experiments are particularly strong. Table 2 shows that training on environments with varied textures enables robust performance in entirely new settings. Furthermore, the goal-agnostic training results (Table 3) convincingly demonstrate the model's ability to learn a flexible policy that can be directed to pursue diverse—and even conflicting—goals without any fine-tuning. For instance, a model trained with randomized goals can perform nearly as well on a specific frag-maximization task as a model trained exclusively for that task, while also being capable of adopting a "pacifist" strategy when instructed.

Finally, the ablation study (Table 4) provides clear evidence for the core design choices, confirming that both the use of a vectorial measurement stream (vs. a scalar reward) and prediction at multiple temporal horizons are critical to the method's success. The victory in the Visual Doom AI competition serves as a powerful external validation of the method's real-world effectiveness against other competitive approaches.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The paper is well-written and the empirical work is excellent. The following points are intended as constructive feedback to further strengthen the work and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **On-Policy Assumption vs. Off-Policy Data:** The training procedure uses an experience replay buffer, which means the model is learning from data generated by older versions of the policy. However, the Monte Carlo regression targets implicitly assume the future trajectory follows the *current* policy. This introduces a slight off-policy learning dynamic. While the authors note they "have not observed any adverse effect," a brief discussion on this potential mismatch and why it does not seem to hinder performance would be a valuable addition.
*   **Reliance on Pre-defined Measurements:** A key strength and a potential limitation of the method is its reliance on a pre-defined, low-dimensional measurement stream that is distinct from the raw sensory input. This is an important structural assumption that the baseline methods do not make. It would be beneficial to more explicitly discuss the implications of this design choice. For example, clarifying that the approach is particularly well-suited for environments where such meaningful measurements are readily available, and acknowledging that its applicability might be more limited in scenarios where they are not.

### Questions for Authors

*   Could you elaborate on the interaction between the experience replay and the Monte Carlo prediction targets? Specifically, how sensitive is the training stability and final performance to the size of the experience memory and the frequency of policy updates?
*   The distinction between the sensory stream `s_t` and the measurement stream `m_t` is crucial. How did you select the measurements for your experiments, and have you investigated the model's sensitivity to this choice? For instance, how would performance in the "Battle" scenario change if the agent was only trained to predict its frag count, more closely mimicking a traditional scalar reward setup?
