This paper introduces Direct Future Prediction (DFP), an on-policy method that learns to predict future intrinsic game measurements. All experiments are conducted in the VizDoom environment, where the model is trained to predict a future vector of measurements (health, ammunition, and frags) rather than a single scalar value like a score. The agent's policy is controlled by a user-specified "goal" vector, which assigns weights to each of these measurements. By adjusting these weights, one can guide the agent's behavior and exploration. At test time, the agent selects actions that maximize the expected, goal-weighted sum of these future measurements.

The empirical results are impressive, underscored by the fact that this model won the 2016 VizDoom competition. The experimental section is sound and provides compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness:

*   The authors provide comparisons against strong baselines, including A3C, DQN, and a recent, related approach, DSR. The results show that DFP generally outperforms these methods, or matches their performance in scenarios where the baseline has reached an optimal policy (e.g., A3C in scenario D1).
*   A thorough ablation study validates the architecture, demonstrating that each component of the proposed model contributes positively to its overall performance.

The paper is well-situated within existing literature. The concepts of predicting auxiliary variables, forward modeling, and intrinsic motivation (Singh et al., 2004) are well-studied areas in reinforcement learning. The version of the paper reviewed (December 4th revision) adequately references prior work, providing sufficient context for the contributions.

**Minor Comments:**

*   The paper describes Doom as a 3D environment. While presented with a 3D perspective, it is technically a 2.5D or pseudo-3D environment, as the level map is two-dimensional and vertical movement/aiming is not a factor. This is a minor point, but clarifying it would improve technical precision.
*   In Equation (2) and thereafter, the notation 'P' is used for 'prediction'. This could be slightly confusing, as 'P' conventionally denotes probability in machine learning literature.
*   Similarly, in Equation (6), the use of the index 'j' in two different fonts to represent different variables could be potentially confusing for the reader.
*   For improved readability, the results tables could benefit from explicitly stating the units for each reported value, particularly in Table 1 where the measurements are heterogeneous.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend this paper for acceptance. While one might argue for experiments in more diverse environments or debate the degree of novelty, the paper's contributions are significant. It presents a well-executed, no-nonsense approach that is thoroughly validated on a challenging benchmark. The impressive, competition-winning results, combined with a sound experimental methodology and a commitment to reproducibility (evidenced by the detailed appendix), make this a milestone paper worthy of publication.
