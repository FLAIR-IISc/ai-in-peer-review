This paper presents an on-policy method that learns to predict future intrinsic measurements from the environment. All experiments are conducted in the VizDoom environment, where the model is trained to predict a sequence of triplets corresponding to health, ammunition, and frags. These predictions are conditioned on a "goal" triplet provided as input, which allows the agent's exploration to be guided by weighting the importance of each measurement. At test time, the agent acts to maximize the predicted long-term outcome based on a fixed goal.

The results are impressive, highlighted by the fact that this model won the 2016 VizDoom competition. The experimental section is sound and provides a thorough evaluation:
- The paper includes comparisons of the proposed method (DFP) with strong baselines, including A3C, DQN, and DSR. The results show that DFP generally outperforms these other approaches, or performs comparably in scenarios where baselines reach an optimal policy (e.g., A3C in the D1 scenario).
- A comprehensive ablation study is presented, which effectively demonstrates that the components of the proposed model are necessary for its performance.

The paper is well-situated within established domains of reinforcement learning, such as intrinsic motivation, auxiliary variable prediction, and forward modeling. The reviewed version (December 4th revision) adequately references prior work in these areas, even if the list of citations is not completely exhaustive.

A few minor suggestions for improving clarity and presentation:
- The paper describes Doom as a 3D environment. While presented with a 3D perspective, it is technically a 2.5D environment where height is not a fully actionable dimension. This is a minor point but worth clarifying for accuracy.
- The use of "P" in Equation (2) and onward denotes a prediction, which could be misleading since "P" conventionally represents a probability distribution in this context.
- In Equation (6), the variable "j" is used twice with different fonts, which could be confusing to the reader.
- The results tables, particularly Table 1 with its heterogeneous measurements, would be more readable if the units for each value were explicitly stated.

This is a strong paper that I recommend for acceptance. While one could argue for experiments in more diverse environments or that the conceptual novelty is incremental, the paper makes a significant contribution. It presents a "milestone" result as the winner of a major competition, is supported by a methodologically sound and well-documented experimental evaluation, and includes sufficient detail for reproducibility. Such papers, which demonstrate clear state-of-the-art performance on a challenging benchmark, are a valuable contribution to the field and warrant publication.
