This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem of direct future prediction (DFP). The core idea is to train an agent to predict future changes in a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) conditioned on its current sensory input and a potential action. This is in contrast to traditional reinforcement learning, which typically relies on maximizing a scalar reward signal. The model is trained purely from its own experience interacting with the environment, using the temporally dense, multi-dimensional measurement stream as a rich supervisory signal. A key feature of this framework is its ability to incorporate a "goal" vector as an input, which allows the agent to be trained without a fixed objective and to pursue dynamically specified goals at test time. The authors demonstrate their method in the challenging first-person visual environment of Doom.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a clear accept. The paper presents a straightforward and effective method that achieves state-of-the-art results on a difficult benchmark. The core contribution is supported by an extensive and methodologically sound set of experiments, and the results are significant enough to be considered a milestone for the ViZDoom environment.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its compelling empirical results. The proposed DFP model significantly outperforms several state-of-the-art deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C) on complex navigation and combat scenarios in Doom. The fact that a model trained with this approach won the Full Deathmatch track of the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming more complex models that used additional supervision, is a powerful testament to its effectiveness and generalization capabilities.

2.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The experimental design is rigorous and convincing. The authors provide:
    *   **Strong Baselines:** Comparisons are made against relevant and strong deep RL methods, demonstrating a clear performance advantage for DFP, especially in richer, more complex tasks.
    *   **Ablation Studies:** A well-conducted ablation study validates the central claims of the paper. It shows that both the use of a vectorial measurement stream (vs. a single scalar reward) and the prediction of measurements at multiple future time steps are crucial for achieving the reported performance.
    *   **Generalization Experiments:** The paper thoroughly investigates the model's ability to generalize across different goals and visual environments (e.g., randomly textured mazes), demonstrating the robustness of the learned skills.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The method is explained clearly, and the paper is well-written. The authors provide sufficient detail in the main text and appendices about their model architecture and training procedures to facilitate reproducibility.

4.  **Sound Positioning:** The paper adequately situates its contributions within the relevant literature, connecting to prior work on forward models, intrinsic motivation, and supervised approaches to control.

While one could argue that the novelty is incremental or that the evaluation could be extended to other domains beyond Doom, the paper's value is undeniable. It demonstrates that a simple, well-executed idea can push the state of the art. Such "milestone" papers, which are experimentally sound and establish a new level of performance on a challenging benchmark, are a valuable contribution to the community.

**Additional Feedback:**

The following are minor suggestions aimed at further improving the paper's clarity. They do not factor into my decision.

*   The description of Doom as a "three-dimensional environment" could be more precise. While visually presented in 3D, the gameplay mechanics are largely 2.5D, as height is not a fully independent dimension for agent navigation or interaction. Acknowledging this nuance might be helpful.
*   In Equation (2), the notation `pat` is used for the model's prediction. While the 'p' presumably stands for 'prediction', this could be momentarily confusing as 'P' or 'p' often denotes a probability in machine learning literature. Using a different variable might prevent this minor ambiguity.
*   In the results tables, particularly Table 1 where the performance metrics differ between scenarios, it would enhance readability to repeat the metric in each column header (e.g., "Avg. Health," "Avg. Frags").
