### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents an approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP). The core idea is to reframe the control problem as a supervised learning task. Instead of relying on a sparse, scalar reward signal common in reinforcement learning, the DFP agent learns to predict the future values of a rich, multidimensional stream of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) based on its current sensory input (raw pixels), current measurements, and a specified goal. The agent is trained by interacting with its environment, using the observed outcomes of its actions as the supervisory signal. This formulation allows for dense feedback during training and, notably, enables the agent to be trained without a fixed goal, allowing it to pursue dynamically specified goals at test time. The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation in the challenging Visual Doom environment, demonstrating that their approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods. The effectiveness of the model is further validated by its victory in the Full Deathmatch track of the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition, which was held in previously unseen environments.

### **Recommendation and Justification**

**Recommendation:** Accept

This is a strong paper with compelling results and a clear, well-executed central idea. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Exceptional Empirical Performance and Validation:** The proposed method is not just incrementally better; it dramatically outperforms strong deep RL baselines like A3C and DQN on complex, dynamic tasks. The victory in the Visual Doom AI competition provides powerful, independent validation of the approach's effectiveness and generalization capabilities, which is a rare and highly valuable form of evidence in a research paper.

2.  **Elegant and Effective Formulation:** The paper makes a convincing case for using a supervised, predictive framework for control when dense, multivariate feedback is available. The approach is conceptually simple yet powerful, and the design choices are well-justified through a thorough ablation study that confirms the benefits of both vectorial feedback and multi-step temporal prediction. The ability to train a single, goal-agnostic agent that can adapt its behavior to diverse objectives at test time is a significant contribution.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The claims made in the paper are robustly supported by extensive and well-designed experiments.

*   **Superiority over State-of-the-Art:** In the "Battle" and "Battle 2" scenarios, DFP outperforms the next-best method (A3C) by a factor of 6 and 2.5, respectively. This large performance gap on the most challenging tasks highlights the practical advantage of the DFP formulation, which leverages the richer supervisory signal from multiple measurements (health, ammo, frags).

*   **Rigorous Ablation Study:** The ablation study in Table 4 provides clear evidence for the paper's core theses. It demonstrates that predicting multiple measurements is substantially better than predicting a single scalar reward (frags), even when the agent is evaluated solely on that scalar metric. It also shows that predicting measurements at multiple future time steps is beneficial, supporting the architectural design.

*   **Demonstrated Generalization:** The paper provides strong evidence of generalization in multiple forms. First, the experiments in randomly textured environments (Table 2) show that the agent can learn policies that are not tied to specific visual textures. Second, and most impressively, the victory in the Visual Doom competition required generalizing to entirely new and unseen map layouts, a difficult challenge that the DFP model successfully overcame.

*   **Goal-Conditioned Flexibility:** The experiments on goal-agnostic training (Table 3) are a highlight. They show that an agent trained on randomized goals performs nearly as well on a specific task as an agent trained exclusively for it. More importantly, this goal-agnostic agent generalizes far better to novel goals at test time (e.g., switching from an aggressive to a "pacifist" strategy), demonstrating a level of behavioral flexibility not easily achieved with standard RL formulations.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions aimed at further strengthening the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Environment Terminology:** While "3D environment" is common parlance for Doom, it may be slightly more precise to describe it as a 2.5D environment, as there is no true z-axis for movement or aiming. This is a minor point of clarification.
*   **Clarity of Notation:** The notation is generally clear, but could be slightly refined. The use of `F` for the predictor and `p` for the prediction output is fine, but using `P` as an alternative symbol might momentarily confuse readers accustomed to `P` representing a probability distribution. Similarly, the use of `j` as both a summation index and a representation vector (`j`) in Equation 6, while distinguished by font, could be made more distinct to avoid any potential ambiguity.
*   **Table Readability:** In the results tables (e.g., Table 1), it would enhance readability to repeat the measurement unit in the column headers (e.g., "Avg. Health," "Avg. Frags"). This would make the table easier to interpret at a glance, especially since the primary evaluation metric changes between scenarios.
*   **Generality of the Approach:** The experiments in Doom are comprehensive and convincing. To further bolster the paper's claims of generality, it would be interesting to see a discussion on how this approach might translate to other domains, perhaps a robotic manipulation task or a different game genre, where the set of "intrinsic measurements" might be different or less obvious.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points to deepen my understanding of the work:

1.  The linear goal parameterization, `u(f; g) = g > f`, is elegant and clearly effective. Have you experimented with or considered more complex, non-linear goal functions? For instance, could this framework support goals like "keep health above a certain threshold" or "maximize frags while minimizing ammo consumption"?
2.  The choice of temporal offsets for prediction (`τ = {1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32}`) seems well-chosen. How sensitive is the model's performance to this specific set of offsets? Would a denser set of short-term predictions or a sparser set of longer-term ones significantly alter the learned behavior?
3.  The paper uses ε-greedy for exploration alongside, in some experiments, randomized goals. Could you elaborate on the interplay between these two mechanisms? To what extent does the pursuit of diverse, randomized goals during training provide a more structured and effective form of exploration than the undirected exploration from ε-greedy?
