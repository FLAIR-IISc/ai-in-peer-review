This paper presents an on-policy approach to estimating future underlying measures. All experiments were conducted within Doom (i.e., Doom), and instead of only estimating win/loss or the number of frags (score), the authors trained their model to estimate a three-dimensional (a sequence of health, ammo, and frags) weight that is weighted by the "target" three-dimensional (a sequence) they provided as input. Changing the target three-dimensional weight is a way to guide/direct the search. During the experiment, only the long-term target can be maximized.

The results are impressive, as this model won the 2016 Vizdom competition. The paper's experimental section seems accurate:

- An attempt has been made to compare A3C with DFP and DSR (a recent similar approach from Kulkarni et al., 2016). DFP performs better than other methods (or equal to them when they reach a ceiling/optimum, e.g., A3C in the D1 scenario).
- There is an ablation study that supports the thesis that all the "extra complexity" in the paper model is useful.

Internal motivation (Singh et al. 2004), auxiliary variables, and forward modeling predictions are well-studied areas of reinforcement learning. The version I read (revised on December 4) adequately references previous work, even if it is not entirely complete.

A few comments on the form (minor errors):

- Doom is described as a 3D environment, whereas it is actually a 2D environment (height is not a discriminatory/effective dimension) presented in (fake) 3D.
- (2) (and subsequently) the use of "p" can be misleading because it stands for prediction but not probability (as is usually the case with p).
- (6) The use of twice "j" (admittedly, with different fonts) can be misleading.
- The results table may repeat units of measurement (especially since they are strange in Table 1).

I think this paper has a clear acceptance. One might argue that the experiments could have been run in different environments or that the novelty is limited, but I think "correct" (not nonsense, experimentally correct on Doom, with details for reproduction in the appendix) and "milestone" (Vizdom winning) papers should be published.
