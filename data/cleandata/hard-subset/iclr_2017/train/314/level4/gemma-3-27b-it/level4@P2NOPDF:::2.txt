This paper introduces a novel on-policy deep reinforcement learning method that incorporates auxiliary intrinsic variables to enhance performance. The approach builds upon existing universal value function frameworks, and the authors appropriately acknowledge relevant prior work. While the core technical contribution appears to be a successful distillation and integration of established techniques – specifically tailored to the challenging domain of 3D navigation – this contribution could be more explicitly highlighted within the abstract and introduction. 

The paper demonstrates promising empirical results, but a more thorough analysis of the method’s limitations would strengthen the work. Specifically, exploring failure modes is crucial. Under what conditions does the agent struggle to generalize to novel or dynamically changing goal configurations? A key concern, inherent to on-policy methods, is the potential for catastrophic forgetting. The paper does not address how this is mitigated or whether performance degrades significantly when the agent is not continually retrained on previously encountered goals. This is a particularly relevant point given the continual learning nature of the 3D navigation task.

Given the authors position the contribution as an integration of key ideas, supported by empirical validation, it would be beneficial to assess the method’s performance beyond the 3D navigation domain. Extending the experiments to established benchmarks like Atari, perhaps utilizing game state information (e.g., ROM data) as intrinsic variables, would provide a broader understanding of the approach’s generalizability and relative strengths.

Despite these suggestions for improvement, this paper presents compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed formulation. The experimental results clearly demonstrate an empirical advantage, and the insights gained from this work are likely to be valuable for researchers developing future reinforcement learning agents. Overall, the paper is a worthwhile contribution to the field.
