The paper presents an on-policy method for predicting future intrinsic measurements. All experiments are conducted within the Doom environment (specifically, vizDoom), and, rather than simply predicting win/loss or the number of frags (score), the authors train their model to predict a sequence of triplets representing (health, ammunition, frags). These predictions are weighted by a sequence of “goal” triplets provided as input. Modifying the weights of the goal triplet provides a mechanism for performing and guiding exploration. At test time, the agent acts by maximizing long-term goal achievement.

The results are impressive – this model won the 2016 vizDoom competition. The experimental section of the paper appears rigorous: 
- Comparisons are made between DFP and A3C, DQN, and an attempt to compare with DSR (a recent, similar approach from Kulkarni et al., 2016). DFP outperforms the other approaches, or matches their performance when they reach a ceiling/optimum, as observed for A3C in scenario D1.
- An ablation study supports the claim that all the additions to the model contribute to its performance.

Predicting intrinsic motivation (Singh et al. 2004), utilizing auxiliary variables, and forward modeling are all well-studied areas within reinforcement learning. The version of the paper I reviewed (December 4th revision) adequately references prior work, although the coverage isn’t exhaustive.

I have a few minor comments regarding the presentation:
- Doom is described as a 3D environment, but is, in fact, a 2D environment presented with a (pseudo-)3D rendering. The height dimension is not discriminative or actionable.
- The use of “P” in equation (2) (and subsequently) could be misleading, as it denotes a prediction rather than a probability (which is the typical convention for ‘P’).
- The double use of “j” (albeit with different fonts) in equation (6) may cause confusion.
- The results tables could benefit from repeating the units of measurement, particularly given the heterogeneous nature of the data in Table 1.

Overall, I recommend accepting this paper. One could argue for experiments in additional environments or question the degree of novelty, but I believe that papers which are technically sound, experimentally validated (particularly on a challenging environment like Doom), and provide sufficient detail for reproducibility (as demonstrated in the appendix) deserve publication. Furthermore, the model’s status as the vizDoom competition winner marks it as a significant contribution.
