Deep reinforcement learning (RL), leveraging deep neural networks as function approximators, has achieved notable successes in RL problems with large state spaces. This empirically driven work builds upon these approaches, introducing a new algorithm that demonstrates improved performance in novel 3D environments from raw sensory data and facilitates better generalization across goals and environments. Significantly, this algorithm was the winner of the Visual Doom AI competition.

The key idea behind the algorithm is to utilize additional low-dimensional observations – such as ammo or health, provided by the game engine – as supervised targets for prediction. Critically, this prediction is conditioned on a goal vector (provided, not learned) and the current action. After training, the optimal action for a given state can be selected as the action that maximizes the predicted outcome with respect to the specified goal. Unlike successor feature representations, learning is supervised and does not rely on a temporal difference (TD) relationship between predictions of the current and next states.

The authors thoroughly review prior work in Section 2, covering both methods that predict future states as part of RL and goal-driven function approximation. The key contributions of this work are the focus on Monte Carlo estimation (in contrast to TD methods), the use of low-dimensional ‘measurements’ for prediction, the parametrization of goals, and, importantly, a comprehensive empirical comparison to relevant prior work.

Beyond the performance demonstrated in the Visual Doom AI competition, the authors demonstrate that their algorithm learns generalizable policies capable of responding to limited changes in the goal without requiring further training.

The paper is well-written and the empirical results are compelling, indicating it will be of significant interest to the community.

Some minor potential improvements could be considered:
The supervised training incorporates an approximation due to an on-policy assumption, despite learning from a replay buffer. Specifically, the Monte Carlo regression assumes the expectation of the remainder of the trajectory follows the current policy, yet samples are drawn from episodes generated by prior policy versions. This discrepancy should be discussed.
Furthermore, the algorithm leverages additional metadata – information about which parts of the sensory input are valuable to predict – that is not available to the compared algorithms. This, and the limitations of this approach (e.g., potential challenges in sensory environments lacking readily available measurements), should be discussed more explicitly.
