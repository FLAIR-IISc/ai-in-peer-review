Deep reinforcement learning (RL) with deep neural networks has achieved notable successes in solving RL problems with large state spaces. This empirically driven work builds on these approaches by introducing a new algorithm that outperforms existing methods in novel 3D environments using raw sensory data, and demonstrates improved generalization across goals and environments. Notably, a model trained with this algorithm won the Visual Doom AI competition.

The key idea of this algorithm is to utilize additional low-dimensional observations (such as ammo or health provided by the game engine) as supervised targets for prediction. Crucially, this prediction is conditioned on a goal vector (provided, not learned) and the current action. Once trained, the optimal action for a given state can be selected as the action that maximizes the predicted outcome according to the specified goal. Unlike successor feature representations, learning is supervised and there is no temporal difference (TD) relationship between predictions of the current and next states. 

The authors review relevant prior work in section 2, encompassing both prediction of future states within an RL framework and goal-driven function approximation. The primary contributions of this work are the focus on Monte Carlo estimation (rather than TD learning), the use of low-dimensional 'measurements' for prediction, parameterized goals, and, importantly, a comprehensive empirical comparison to existing methods.

In addition to the strong performance in the Visual Doom AI competition, the authors demonstrate that their algorithm learns generalizable policies capable of responding to limited changes in the goal without further training. 

The paper is well-written and the empirical results are compelling and likely to be of significant interest to the community.

Some minor potential improvements:
The supervised training relies on an on-policy assumption, yet it learns from a replay buffer. While using a replay buffer is common practice, this introduces a discrepancy as the Monte Carlo regression implicitly assumes the expectation of the remainder of the trajectory follows the current policy, while the buffer contains episodes generated by earlier versions of the policy. This should be discussed.
The algorithm leverages additional metadata – specifically, information identifying which aspects of the sensory input are valuable to predict – that is not available to the compared algorithms. The implications of this advantage, and the limitations of the approach (e.g., potential issues in sensory environments lacking readily available measurements), should be explicitly acknowledged.

---

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control that demonstrates strong empirical results, exceeding state-of-the-art methods in challenging environments and winning a competitive benchmark. The approach is well-motivated and the paper clearly articulates its contributions.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The results presented demonstrate a clear advantage over existing deep RL algorithms, particularly in complex environments. The win in the Visual Doom AI competition provides independent validation of the approach.
2.  **Novelty and Motivation:** The framing of sensorimotor control as a supervised learning problem, leveraging the rich supervisory signal provided by the correlation between sensory input, measurements, and actions, is a compelling and well-motivated idea. The focus on dense, multi-dimensional feedback, as opposed to sparse scalar rewards, is a significant contribution.
3.  **Generalization Ability:** The ability of the learned policies to generalize to new environments and goals without retraining is a noteworthy achievement and highlights the robustness of the approach.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   The discussion of the approximation introduced by using a replay buffer with Monte Carlo estimation could be expanded. Providing a brief analysis of the potential impact of this approximation would be beneficial.
*   A more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly concerning the requirement for meaningful “measurements,” would strengthen the paper. Exploring how the approach might be adapted to environments lacking such pre-defined measurements could be a valuable direction for future work.
*   The relationship between the proposed method and imitation learning could be further clarified. While the paper mentions imitation learning briefly, highlighting the differences and potential synergies could be insightful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of using a replay buffer in conjunction with the Monte Carlo regression? How does the potential bias introduced by this approximation affect the learning process and final performance?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the algorithm to the choice of measurements? Are there strategies for identifying or learning relevant measurements in environments where they are not immediately apparent?
3.  The paper states that the goal vector is "given, not learned." Could you elaborate on potential approaches for learning the goal vector itself, perhaps through exploration or interaction with the environment?
