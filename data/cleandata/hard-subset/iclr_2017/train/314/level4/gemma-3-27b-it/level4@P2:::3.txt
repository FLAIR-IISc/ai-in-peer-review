This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem focused on predicting future intrinsic measurements within an immersive environment – the game Doom. Instead of relying on traditional reward-based reinforcement learning, the authors train a model to predict sequences of (health, ammunition, frags) weighted by corresponding “goal” triplets provided as input. This allows for flexible exploration and goal-directed action selection at test time by maximizing long-term goal achievement.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The results are compelling, demonstrated by winning the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition, and the experimental validation is robust. The approach offers a promising alternative to standard reinforcement learning techniques, particularly in complex, visually-rich environments.

The paper provides a strong experimental foundation for its claims. The authors compare their Direct Future Prediction (DFP) approach to established deep reinforcement learning methods (A3C, DQN) and a recent related technique (DSR), consistently achieving superior or comparable performance. The ablation study effectively supports the design choices made in the model, highlighting the benefits of using multi-dimensional measurements and predicting measurements across multiple time steps. The experimental setup is well-defined and includes a clear description of the environments and hyperparameters used. The authors also demonstrate impressive generalization capabilities across different Doom environments. The paper's connection to prior work, including intrinsic motivation, auxiliary variables, and forward modeling, is adequately discussed, even if not exhaustive.

To further improve the paper, consider the following points:

*   **Environment Description:** Clarify that Doom, while *presented* in 3D, is fundamentally a 2D environment concerning agent movement and actionable dimensions.
*   **Notation:** Reconsider the use of "P" in equation (2) as it might be misinterpreted as probability. A different notation might improve clarity. Similarly, the dual use of "j" within equation (6), although typographically distinct, could potentially cause confusion.
*   **Table Clarity:** Enhance the clarity of results tables (e.g., Table 1) by explicitly repeating the units of measurement for each column. This avoids potential ambiguity, particularly as the measurements are heterogeneous.
*   **Goal Generalization:** Expand on the discussion regarding the ability to generalize to new goals. Provide more insight into the limitations of this generalization, and whether certain goal structures are more easily adapted to than others.
*   **Scalability:** While the success in Doom is notable, briefly discuss potential challenges and considerations when scaling this approach to more complex, real-world environments.

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors:

*   Could you elaborate on the selection criteria for the temporal offsets (τ1, ..., τn) used in the prediction? What motivated choosing those specific values, and how sensitive are the results to changes in these offsets?
*   The paper mentions training regimes with fixed and randomized goal vectors. Were there any noticeable differences in the *types* of strategies learned under these different training regimes?
*   Regarding the ablation study, what specific metrics were used to determine the relative importance of each component of the model? Can you provide more details on the sensitivity of performance to variations in these components?
*   Given the success with intrinsic measurements, have you considered exploring the use of learned representations of the sensory input (st) as additional measurements? Would this potentially bridge the gap toward predicting full sensory streams?
