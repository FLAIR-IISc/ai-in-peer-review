## Review of “Leveraging Monte Carlo Predictions for Goal-Directed Reinforcement Learning”

Deep reinforcement learning, utilizing deep neural networks as function approximators, has demonstrated significant success in solving reinforcement learning problems with large state spaces. This empirically grounded work builds upon these advances by introducing a novel algorithm that achieves superior performance in challenging, previously unseen 3D environments using raw sensory data. A key strength of this approach is its ability to generalize effectively across diverse goals and environments, as evidenced by its winning performance in the Visual Doom AI competition.

The core idea of the proposed algorithm centers around leveraging readily available, low-dimensional observations – such as ammo count or health status provided by the game engine – as supervised targets for prediction. Critically, this prediction is conditioned on both a goal vector (provided *a priori*, not learned) and the agent’s current action. After training, the optimal action for a given state is determined by selecting the action that maximizes the predicted outcome aligned with the specified goal. This differs from approaches employing successor feature representations; learning is fully supervised, foregoing the temporal difference (TD) relationship typically found between predictions for the current and next states.

The authors provide a thorough review of related work in Section 2, covering both prior efforts in predicting future states within a reinforcement learning context and techniques utilizing goal-driven function approximation. The principal contributions of this paper lie in its focus on Monte Carlo estimation (in contrast to TD learning), the strategic use of low-dimensional ‘measurements’ for prediction, the incorporation of parameterized goals, and, importantly, a rigorous empirical comparison to relevant prior work.

Beyond the compelling results in the Visual Doom AI competition, the authors demonstrate the algorithm’s capacity to learn generalizable policies capable of responding to limited changes in the goal without requiring further training. This adaptability is a significant benefit.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly communicates its key concepts. The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate a significant advancement in the field, making this work highly relevant and of considerable interest to the ICLR community.

However, a few points merit consideration. The supervised training process involves an implicit on-policy assumption, while the algorithm learns from a replay buffer. Specifically, the Monte Carlo regression relies on estimating the expected remainder of a trajectory, assuming this expectation aligns with the policy currently being evaluated. However, the replay buffer contains experiences generated by potentially earlier versions of the policy. A discussion of this potential discrepancy, and its impact on the learning process, would strengthen the paper.

Furthermore, the algorithm benefits from access to additional metadata – information specifying which aspects of the sensory input are valuable for prediction. This represents a form of prior knowledge that the compared algorithms do not possess. While the authors implicitly acknowledge this, a more explicit discussion of this advantage, and a consideration of the limitations of the approach when such measurements are unavailable (e.g., in purely sensory environments where such readily interpretable measurements are absent), would provide a more complete picture of the algorithm’s applicability. Exploring the sensitivity of performance to the quality or availability of these measurements could be valuable future work.
