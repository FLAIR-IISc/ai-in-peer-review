This paper presents an empirically strong contribution to deep reinforcement learning, building on recent successes in applying deep neural networks as function approximators in RL. The authors introduce a novel algorithm that demonstrates superior performance in navigating new 3D environments from raw sensory input, with a notable ability to generalize across different goals and environments – a capability underscored by its winning performance in the Visual Doom AI competition.

The core idea revolves around augmenting the standard RL framework with auxiliary supervised learning. Specifically, the algorithm leverages readily available, low-dimensional observations (e.g., ammo count, health status) as supervised targets for prediction. Crucially, these predictions are *conditioned* on both a specified goal vector (provided as input, rather than learned) and the agent’s current action. Once trained, the optimal action for a given state is determined by selecting the action that maximizes the predicted outcome, relative to the defined goal. This approach distinguishes itself from methods employing successor feature representations by utilizing supervised learning directly, thereby avoiding the temporal difference (TD) relationship inherent in predicting future states based on current state predictions.

The authors appropriately contextualize their work within a landscape of prior research, reviewing relevant efforts in both state prediction for RL and goal-conditioned function approximation (Section 2). The key contributions of this work lie in its emphasis on Monte Carlo estimation – as opposed to TD learning – the strategic use of low-dimensional “measurements” for prediction, the incorporation of parameterized goals, and, most significantly, its thorough empirical comparison with established baselines. 

Beyond the compelling results in Visual Doom AI, the paper convincingly demonstrates the algorithm’s capacity to learn policies that generalize effectively. These policies exhibit adaptability to limited changes in the goal specification *without* requiring further training, highlighting the potential for flexible, goal-directed behavior.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and the empirical results are both compelling and highly relevant to the deep RL community. It is likely to be of significant interest to researchers in the field.

However, there are a couple of points that could benefit from further discussion. The supervised training component relies on an on-policy assumption, yet learning occurs from a replay buffer. While Monte Carlo regression inherently considers the expected remainder of the trajectory under the current policy, the data within the replay buffer originates from episodes generated by *prior* policy iterations. The implications of this potential discrepancy should be addressed.

Furthermore, the algorithm leverages additional metadata – specifically, information identifying which aspects of the sensory input are relevant for prediction. It is important to acknowledge that this represents an advantage over the compared algorithms, which do not have access to this information. The paper should more explicitly discuss the limitations of this approach; namely, its potential ineffectiveness in environments lacking such pre-defined, informative measurements. Recognizing this constraint will provide a more nuanced understanding of the algorithm’s applicability and highlight potential avenues for future research exploring self-discovery of relevant features.
