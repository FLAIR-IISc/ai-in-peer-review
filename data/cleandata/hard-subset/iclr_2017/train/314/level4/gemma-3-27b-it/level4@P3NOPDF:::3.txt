This paper presents an on-policy reinforcement learning method for predicting future intrinsic measurements in the challenging environment of Doom (vizDoom). Unlike traditional reward-based approaches that focus on win/loss or score, the authors train their model to predict a sequence of triplets representing (health, ammunition, frags), weighted by corresponding “goal” triplets provided as input. This weighting scheme serves as a mechanism for guided exploration, and at test time, the agent can act by maximizing long-term goal achievement. The results are compelling, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance and culminating in a win at the 2016 vizDoom competition.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-presented. The authors provide comparisons against strong baselines including A3C, DQN, and a comparison with the related DSR approach (Kulkarni et al., 2016). DFP consistently outperforms these methods, or achieves comparable performance when baselines reach their asymptotic limits, as observed with A3C in scenario D1. Furthermore, a comprehensive ablation study effectively demonstrates the contribution of each component of the proposed model, validating the benefits of its added complexity. 

The work builds upon established concepts in reinforcement learning, namely intrinsic motivation (Singh et al., 2004), auxiliary variable prediction, and forward modeling. The December 4th revision appropriately cites relevant prior work, although the coverage could be even more exhaustive. 

I have a few minor comments regarding the presentation:

*   The description of Doom as a 3D environment is technically inaccurate. While presented in a pseudo-3D fashion, the environment is fundamentally 2D; height is not a discriminative or actionable dimension.
*   The notation “P” in equation (2) and subsequent equations might be misleading. While representing a prediction, it does not denote a probability, which is its conventional interpretation.
*   The dual use of the index “j” (using differing fonts) within equation (6) could potentially cause confusion.
*   The results tables, particularly Table 1, would benefit from explicitly repeating the units of measurement for each value, given the heterogeneity of the metrics presented.

Overall, I recommend accepting this paper. While one could reasonably suggest expanding the experimental scope to include diverse environments, or argue that the novelty is incremental, I believe that papers which are technically sound, experimentally validated – particularly on a challenging benchmark like Doom – and represent significant achievements (as evidenced by winning the vizDoom competition) deserve publication. This work is a “correct” and important contribution to the field.
