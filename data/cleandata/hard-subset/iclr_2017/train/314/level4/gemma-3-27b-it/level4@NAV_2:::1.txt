Deep RL (using deep neural networks for function approximators in RL algorithms) has achieved notable successes in solving RL problems with large state spaces. This empirically driven work builds upon these approaches, introducing a novel algorithm that demonstrates superior performance in unseen 3D environments utilizing raw sensory data, and facilitates better generalization across goals and environments. Significantly, this algorithm was the winning entry in the Visual Doom AI competition.

The core idea of the proposed algorithm is to leverage additional low-dimensional observations (e.g., ammo or health provided by the game engine) as supervised targets for prediction. Crucially, this prediction is conditioned on a goal vector (provided, not learned) and the current action. Once trained, the optimal action for a given state can be determined by selecting the action that maximizes the predicted outcome according to the specified goal. Unlike successor feature representations, learning is supervised, and there is no temporal difference relationship between predictions of the current and next states.

The authors thoroughly review related prior work in Section 2, encompassing both future state prediction within RL and goal-driven function approximation. The key contributions of this work lie in its emphasis on Monte Carlo estimation (as opposed to temporal difference learning), the utilization of low-dimensional ‘measurements’ for prediction, the incorporation of parameterized goals, and, importantly, the robust empirical comparison against relevant existing methods. 

Beyond the compelling performance in the Visual Doom AI competition, the authors demonstrate that their algorithm can learn generalizable policies capable of responding to limited changes in the goal without requiring further training.

The paper is well-written and the empirical results are convincing, making it a highly relevant and valuable contribution to the field.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control with strong empirical validation, including winning a competitive AI competition. The contributions are significant and the work is well-executed.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles a challenging problem – sensorimotor control from raw sensory input – and offers a compelling solution. The demonstrated performance in the Visual Doom AI competition provides strong evidence of the approach’s effectiveness. The ablation studies and analysis of generalization capabilities further support the claims made by the authors. The clear explanation of the algorithm and its connection to existing work contribute to the overall strength of the paper.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the Monte Carlo regression provides a stable learning signal, it implicitly relies on an on-policy assumption due to the use of a replay buffer. The expectation of the remainder of the trajectory is assumed to follow the current policy, but samples are drawn from episodes generated by prior policy versions. Discussing this approximation and its potential impact would be beneficial.

The algorithm benefits from access to additional metadata (specifically, the identification of which sensory inputs correspond to measurable quantities for prediction). It would be valuable to explicitly acknowledge this reliance and discuss potential limitations when applying this approach to environments where such measurements are not readily available.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential impact of the on-policy assumption inherent in using a replay buffer with the Monte Carlo regression? Are there strategies to mitigate any potential issues arising from this?
2.  How do you anticipate the performance of this approach in environments where defining informative “measurements” is difficult or impossible? Could you discuss potential extensions to handle such scenarios, perhaps by learning to identify relevant features from the raw sensory input?
3.  The network architecture is similar to DQN. What experiments did you perform to ensure the effectiveness of the specific network choices (e.g., the expectation/action stream split) and were there other architectures explored?
