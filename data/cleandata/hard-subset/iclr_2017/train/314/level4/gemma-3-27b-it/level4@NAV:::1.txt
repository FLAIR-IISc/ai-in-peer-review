Deep Reinforcement Learning, leveraging deep neural networks as function approximators, has demonstrated considerable success in reinforcement learning within complex state spaces. This work presents an empirically grounded algorithm that extends these approaches, achieving improved performance in previously unseen 3D environments using raw sensory input and exhibiting strong generalization capabilities across both goals and environments. The algorithm’s efficacy is underscored by its winning performance in the Visual Doom AI competition.

The core of the algorithm lies in utilizing readily available, low-dimensional observations – such as ammo count or health status provided by the game engine – as supervised targets for prediction. Crucially, this prediction is conditioned upon a given goal vector and the currently executed action. Following training, the optimal action for a given state is selected as the action that maximizes the predicted outcome with respect to the specified goal.  Distinct from methods employing successor feature representations, learning in this approach is fully supervised and lacks the temporal difference relationship characteristic of those techniques, avoiding reliance on predictions of subsequent states.

The authors thoroughly review related prior work in Section 2, encompassing both state prediction within RL frameworks and goal-conditioned function approximation. The primary contributions of this paper include the focus on Monte Carlo estimation in lieu of temporal difference learning, the strategic use of low-dimensional ‘measurements’ for prediction, the incorporation of parametrized goals, and, significantly, a comprehensive empirical evaluation against relevant existing methods. 

Beyond the compelling results from the Visual Doom AI competition, the authors demonstrate the algorithm's ability to acquire policies that generalize effectively, responding to limited alterations in goal specifications without requiring additional training. 

The paper is clearly written and the empirical findings are persuasive, making it a highly relevant and interesting contribution to the field. 

However, a few points could be strengthened. The supervised training process relies on an on-policy assumption, while learning utilizes a replay buffer. This discrepancy – the Monte Carlo regression assumes the expectation of the remaining trajectory aligns with the current policy, yet samples are drawn from episodes generated by earlier policy iterations – warrants further discussion.  Additionally, the algorithm benefits from supplemental metadata specifying which aspects of the sensory input are most valuable for prediction, a resource not available to the comparison algorithms. The implications of this, and the potential limitations of the approach when such measurements are absent (particularly in environments lacking clear, readily available measurements), should be addressed more explicitly.
