This paper introduces an on-policy method for predicting future intrinsic measurements. The experiments are conducted within the Doom environment (specifically, vizDoom), and instead of predicting traditional reward signals like win/loss or score (number of frags), the authors train their model to predict sequences of (health, ammunition, frags) triplets. These predictions are weighted by input “goal” triplets, and modifying these weights serves as a mechanism for exploration. At test time, the agent can act by maximizing long-term goal achievement. 

The results are compelling; this model achieved first place in the 2016 vizDoom competition. The experimental evaluation appears rigorous, including:
- Comparisons to A3C, DQN, and an attempt to compare with the related DSR approach (Kulkarni et al., 2016). DFP consistently outperforms these methods, or matches their performance when they reach an optimal level – as observed with A3C in scenario D1.
- An ablation study that validates the contribution of each component of the proposed model.

The work builds upon established areas of reinforcement learning research, including intrinsic motivation (Singh et al. 2004), auxiliary variable prediction, and forward modelling. The December 4th revision adequately cites relevant prior work, though the coverage is not exhaustive. 

I have a few minor comments regarding presentation:
- The paper describes Doom as a 3D environment, but it is more accurately a 2D environment presented with a pseudo-3D perspective, where height is not a meaningful dimension for action or perception.
- The notation "P" in equation (2) and subsequent equations may be misleading, as it denotes a prediction rather than a probability, which is the typical interpretation of 'P'.
- The repeated use of the index "j" (though in different fonts) within equation (6) could potentially cause confusion.
- Results tables would benefit from explicitly stating the units of measurement, particularly in Table 1 where the units are heterogeneous.

Overall, I recommend accepting this paper. While one could argue for experiments in additional environments or question the degree of novelty, I believe that papers which are technically sound, experimentally validated (especially on a challenging platform like Doom), and demonstrably successful – as evidenced by its vizDoom win – warrant publication.
