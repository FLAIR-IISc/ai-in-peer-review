This paper presents an approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, leveraging a high-dimensional sensory stream and a lower-dimensional measurement stream to provide a rich supervisory signal for learning. The approach uses supervised learning techniques to train a control model through interaction with the environment, without requiring extraneous supervision. The model learns to act based on raw sensory input and can pursue dynamically changing goals. Extensive experiments in 3D simulations based on Doom demonstrate superior performance compared to existing methods, with a winning entry in the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Reject.** While the paper presents promising results and demonstrates a compelling approach to sensorimotor control, the lack of thorough analysis of limitations and broader applicability prevents me from recommending acceptance at this time. The empirical success, while noteworthy, doesn’t fully justify the complexity of the proposed system without a deeper understanding of its failure modes and generalizability.

The paper tackles the challenging problem of sensorimotor control in complex 3D environments, specifically addressing the difficulties of learning from raw sensory input and adapting to dynamically changing goals. The approach is well-motivated, building upon established concepts in supervised learning, reinforcement learning, and predictive coding. The authors appropriately position their work within the existing literature, referencing key papers and outlining how their approach differs from and builds upon previous work (e.g., Jordan & Rumelhart, Sutton & Barto, Schaul et al.). The connection to the Universal Value Function framework is clearly articulated.

The paper supports its claims with extensive empirical results. The consistent outperformance of the proposed method across multiple Doom scenarios, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, is strong evidence of its effectiveness. The ablation studies further demonstrate the importance of both vectorial feedback and multi-step prediction. However, the analysis lacks depth in certain areas. The results, while impressive, are heavily concentrated on the Doom environment. While this provides a challenging testbed, it raises questions about the generalizability of the approach to other domains.  The comparison to baselines is reasonably thorough, but the discussion of limitations is limited.

Here are some additional points to help improve the paper:

*   **Clarify the contributions:** While the authors state that the approach distills existing ideas, the core novelty and specific contributions should be more explicitly articulated in the abstract and introduction. Is it the specific formulation, the architecture, or the combination of both that drives the performance gains?
*   **Address failure modes:** A discussion of scenarios where the model struggles would be valuable. Under what conditions does generalization to changing goals break down? Are there specific types of environments or goals that pose a particular challenge?
*   **Catastrophic forgetting:** The authors should acknowledge and address the potential for catastrophic forgetting inherent in on-policy methods. While the experiments involve continued training, the long-term stability of learned skills remains unclear.
*   **Broader applicability:**  Exploring the performance of the approach on other domains, such as Atari games (potentially using game state variables as intrinsic measurements), would strengthen the claims of generalizability.
*   **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** An analysis of the sensitivity of the approach to different hyperparameters would provide insights into its robustness and ease of use.
*   **Ablation Study on Architectures:** The ablation studies are focused on the measurement/prediction aspect. A corresponding investigation of the proposed network architecture (expectation and action streams) would be valuable. What is the impact of removing these components?

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   You mention that predicting full sensory streams is currently beyond your capabilities. What specific challenges prevent this, and what future work might address them?
*   The performance on the Full Deathmatch track is impressive. Can you elaborate on the differences between the environments used for training and testing, and how the model’s generalization ability was evaluated?
*   How does the computational cost of training and running the proposed model compare to the baselines? Is the improved performance achieved at a significant computational expense?
*   The text states: “the choice of TD versus Monte Carlo is to this day empirical”. Could you please elaborate on why TD learning continues to be preferred despite theoretical advantages of Monte Carlo in certain settings?
*   The description of the goal vector 'g' uses a linear combination. Are there any limitations to this form, and have you experimented with more complex, non-linear goal representations?
