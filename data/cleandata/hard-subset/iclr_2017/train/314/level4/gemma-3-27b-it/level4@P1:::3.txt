The paper presents an approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, framing it as a supervised learning problem of predicting future intrinsic measurements. Experiments are conducted within the Doom environment (specifically, the ViZDoom platform), where the model is trained to predict sequences of (health, ammunition, and frags), weighted by corresponding “goal” triplets provided as input. Adjusting these goal weights guides exploration, and at test time, the agent acts by maximizing long-term goal achievement. The results are compelling, highlighted by the model winning the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition. The experimental section appears rigorous, with comparisons to A3C, DQN, and an attempt to replicate and compare with DSR (a recent, related approach by Kulkarni et al., 2016). DFP consistently outperforms other methods, or reaches comparable performance when they attain optimal levels (as seen with A3C in scenario D1). An ablation study further validates the contribution of the proposed model’s components.

This work builds on well-established concepts in reinforcement learning, such as intrinsic motivation (Singh et al., 2004), auxiliary variables, and forward modeling. The current revision (December 4th) appropriately cites relevant prior work, though it might not be entirely exhaustive.

A few minor comments regarding presentation:
- While often referred to as 3D, the Doom environment is fundamentally 2D (height is not a discriminative or actionable dimension), presented with a pseudo-3D perspective.
- The notation “P” in equation (2) and subsequent equations, while representing prediction, could be misinterpreted as probability. Consider an alternative symbol.
- The dual use of the variable “j” (albeit in different fonts) within equation (6) may create confusion.
- Tables presenting results could benefit from explicitly stating the units for each measurement, particularly in Table 1, given their heterogeneous nature.

I recommend accepting this paper. While one could argue for the need for experiments in diverse environments or question the degree of novelty, I believe that papers demonstrating sound methodology, strong experimental validation (especially in a challenging environment like Doom), and a significant achievement (winning the ViZDoom competition) warrant publication. The work is "correct" (methodologically sound and experimentally validated) and represents a "milestone" achievement within the visual Doom benchmark.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that the goal vector `g` can be used to express any goal. Could you elaborate on any limitations or challenges encountered when representing more complex or abstract goals with this linear combination approach?
2.  In the ablation study, you demonstrate the importance of multi-dimensional feedback. Did you explore any techniques for automatically learning which measurements are most relevant for a given task or goal, rather than relying on predefined weights?
3.  Regarding the computational cost, how does the training time of DFP compare to the baseline methods when considering the number of simulation steps required to reach a comparable level of performance? The paper mentions training speed for DSR, but not for DFP, A3C, and DQN.
4.  The approach seems sensitive to the choice of temporal offsets (τ1…τn). Was any systematic exploration performed to determine optimal values for these offsets, or were they chosen heuristically? Could different tasks benefit from different offset configurations?
