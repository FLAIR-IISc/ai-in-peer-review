This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments, building on recent successes in deep reinforcement learning. The core idea is to reframe the problem as a supervised learning task by predicting future, low-dimensional “measurements” (e.g., health, ammunition) from raw sensory input (e.g., images), conditioned on the current action and a specified goal. This eliminates the need for a traditional reward function and enables learning without a fixed goal during training, allowing for flexible goal pursuit at test time. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL methods and showcasing impressive generalization capabilities.

**I recommend accepting this paper.** The approach is well-motivated, addresses a significant challenge in RL (sensorimotor control from raw sensory input), and is supported by compelling experimental evidence. The victory in the Visual Doom AI competition provides particularly strong validation.

The paper tackles the problem of learning effective control policies in complex, visually-rich environments, moving beyond traditional reward-based reinforcement learning. This is a well-defined and important problem, as it addresses key limitations of existing RL approaches when dealing with high-dimensional sensory input and dynamic goals. The authors successfully motivate their approach by highlighting the limitations of sparse rewards and proposing a more informative supervisory signal derived from the temporal correlation of sensory and measurement streams. The connection to prior work, including supervised learning perspectives on control and related methods like successor representations, is clearly articulated.

The empirical results are the strongest aspect of this paper. The authors provide a thorough comparison to several strong baseline methods across a range of challenging Doom environments. The consistent outperformance, particularly on complex tasks, provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of their approach. The generalization experiments, demonstrating the ability to adapt to novel environments and goals, are particularly noteworthy. The fact that their model won a challenging AI competition further validates the practical impact of this work. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the importance of both vectorial feedback and multi-step prediction.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **On-policy approximation:** The authors employ a replay buffer, introducing a potential on-policy approximation within their Monte Carlo regression framework. A brief discussion of this point, and its possible implications, would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Dependency on measurements:** The approach relies on the availability of informative, low-dimensional measurements. The paper could benefit from a clearer discussion of this limitation, and potential challenges in applying this technique to environments where such measurements are not readily available. It would be helpful to acknowledge that the algorithm leverages prior knowledge (knowing *which* sensory information to predict).
*   **Goal representation:** While the parametrization of goals is flexible, the current implementation uses a linear combination of future measurements. Exploring alternative goal representations might be a fruitful area for future research.
*  **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** A brief discussion about the sensitivity of the approach to hyperparameters would be helpful.



**Questions for the authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the impact of the on-policy approximation introduced by using a replay buffer? Did you experiment with different replay buffer sizes or sampling strategies to mitigate this effect?
*   How do you envision adapting this approach to environments where defining informative low-dimensional measurements is challenging or impossible?
*   Were there any attempts made to learn the goal vector itself, rather than providing it as input? If not, what are the primary challenges in learning goal representations in this framework?
*   How sensitive is the performance to the choice of temporal offsets (τ1, …, τn) used in the prediction task? Did you explore alternative strategies for selecting these offsets?
