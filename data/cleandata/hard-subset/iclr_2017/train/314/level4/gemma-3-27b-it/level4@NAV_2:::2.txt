This paper presents an approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, framing the problem as supervised learning through prediction of future measurements given raw sensory input. The method leverages the rich supervisory signal provided by the cotemporal structure of sensory and measurement streams, enabling learning without a fixed goal and facilitating adaptation to dynamically changing goals.

I recommend rejecting this paper in its current form. While the empirical results are compelling—demonstrating performance superior to state-of-the-art deep RL methods, including winning the Visual Doom AI Competition—the paper lacks sufficient clarity regarding the novelty of the proposed approach and a thorough analysis of its limitations.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its strong empirical validation. The demonstrated success in the challenging Doom environment, and particularly outperforming competitors in the Visual Doom AI Competition, is a significant achievement. The ablation studies convincingly demonstrate the benefits of vectorial feedback and multi-step prediction. However, the contribution feels incremental rather than fundamentally novel. The work builds on established ideas – supervised learning from experience, future prediction, and utilizing multi-dimensional feedback – and while the combination and application to this domain are valuable, the paper doesn't clearly articulate *what* new insight or technique enables the observed performance gains. The connection to existing work on universal value function approximators, though acknowledged, could be more deeply explored to position the contribution more accurately.

To improve the paper, I suggest the following:
*   **Clarify the technical contribution:** The abstract and introduction should explicitly state the novel aspects of the approach. Is it the specific formulation of the prediction task, the network architecture, the training regime, or the combination of these?
*   **Analyze failure modes:** Investigate scenarios where the model struggles to generalize to changing goals. This would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approach.
*   **Address catastrophic forgetting:** Discuss the potential for catastrophic forgetting inherent in on-policy learning and how this is mitigated (or not) in the presented experiments.
*   **Expand empirical evaluation:** Evaluate the approach on additional domains, such as Atari, to assess its broader applicability and provide a more comprehensive comparison to existing methods. Perhaps the game’s internal state variables could serve as intrinsic measurements.
*   **Provide more details on hyperparameter tuning:** A more thorough discussion of the hyperparameter search process and sensitivity analysis would strengthen the reproducibility of the results.

I have the following questions for the authors:
*   What specific design choices within the proposed approach contribute most significantly to the observed performance gains compared to standard deep RL baselines?
*   How does the performance of the model degrade as the time horizon for future measurement prediction is increased? Is there a point of diminishing returns?
*   Could you elaborate on the experience replay mechanism used during training? What is the size of the memory, and how are experiences sampled?
*   What is the computational cost of training this model compared to the baseline methods? Is the increased performance worth the additional computational resources?
