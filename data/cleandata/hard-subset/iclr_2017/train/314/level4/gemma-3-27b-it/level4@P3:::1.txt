## Review of “Sensorimotor Control with Predictive Measurements”

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, leveraging a combination of raw sensory input and lower-dimensional measurements to train a control model via supervised learning. The core idea is to predict the effect of actions on future measurements, conditioned on the current state and a specified goal. The method demonstrates strong empirical performance, notably winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, and exhibits promising generalization capabilities.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper tackles a challenging problem in reinforcement learning – sensorimotor control from raw sensory input – and proposes a compelling solution that outperforms existing methods. The results are strong, particularly the demonstrated success in the Visual Doom AI competition, and the paper is clearly written and well-motivated.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper effectively argues that framing sensorimotor control as a supervised learning problem, using the temporal correlation between sensory inputs, measurements, and actions, provides a powerful supervisory signal. This circumvents the challenges of sparse reward signals common in traditional reinforcement learning. The experimental validation is robust, including comparisons to state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods (DQN, A3C, DSR) across multiple Doom environments of increasing complexity. The consistently superior performance of the proposed method, particularly in more challenging scenarios, provides strong evidence for its effectiveness. Furthermore, the demonstrated generalization to unseen environments and varying goals reinforces the practical value of the approach. The ablation studies convincingly demonstrate the importance of both vectorial feedback (multiple measurements) and multi-step prediction horizons.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified to further strengthen the contribution. The authors acknowledge the use of an experience replay buffer, which introduces a slight deviation from a purely on-policy Monte Carlo approach. A more detailed discussion of the potential impact of this off-policy element would be beneficial. Specifically, how does the replay buffer affect the convergence properties and the stability of training?

The paper rightly highlights the advantage of utilizing readily available "measurements" (health, ammo, etc.). However, it would be valuable to explicitly discuss the limitations of this approach when such measurements are *not* directly provided by the environment. In such cases, how could the framework be adapted, perhaps by learning relevant measurements from the raw sensory input itself? Further exploration of this aspect could broaden the applicability of the proposed method. 

The choice of linear combinations for defining goals (equation 1) is a simplification. While sufficient for the experiments presented, a discussion of the potential benefits and drawbacks of more complex goal representations would be worthwhile. How does the choice of goal parametrization impact the performance and generalization ability of the model?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the impact of the experience replay buffer on the convergence and stability of the training process? Are there any techniques employed to mitigate potential issues arising from the off-policy nature of the learning?
2.  How would the proposed framework be adapted to scenarios where explicit measurements are not readily available? Would learning a latent measurement space from raw sensory input be a viable approach, and what challenges might arise in such a setting?
3.  The paper uses a linear combination for defining goals. What are the potential benefits and drawbacks of exploring more complex goal representations (e.g., non-linear functions)? Do you foresee any challenges in adapting the algorithm to handle such representations?
4.  In the ablation study, you found that multiple measurements significantly improved performance. Did you experiment with weighting the different measurements during training or prediction, and if so, what were the results?
