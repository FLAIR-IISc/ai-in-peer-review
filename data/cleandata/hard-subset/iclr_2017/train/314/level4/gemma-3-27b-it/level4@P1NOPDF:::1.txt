Deep reinforcement learning (RL), leveraging deep neural networks as function approximators, has achieved notable success in solving RL problems with large state spaces. This empirically driven work builds on these approaches by introducing a new algorithm that demonstrates improved performance in novel 3D environments using raw sensory data and exhibits better generalization across goals and environments. Significantly, this algorithm won the Visual Doom AI competition.

The key idea behind the algorithm is to utilize additional low-dimensional observations – such as ammo count or health, provided by the game engine – as a supervised target for prediction. Crucially, this prediction is conditioned on a goal vector (provided as input, not learned) and the current action. Once trained, the optimal action for a given state can be selected as the action that maximizes the predicted outcome with respect to the specified goal. Unlike successor feature representations, learning is fully supervised and does not rely on temporal difference (TD) relationships between predictions of the current and next states.

A number of prior works have explored both predicting future states as part of RL and employing goal-driven function approximators, as the authors review in Section 2. The key contributions of this work lie in its focus on Monte Carlo estimation (rather than TD learning), the use of low-dimensional ‘measurements’ for prediction, the parametrization of goals, and, perhaps most importantly, a thorough empirical comparison to relevant prior work. 

Beyond the strong performance in the Visual Doom AI competition, the authors demonstrate that their algorithm learns generalizable policies capable of responding to limited changes in the goal without requiring further training. 

The paper is well-written and the empirical results are compelling, making it a work of significant interest to the community.

I have a few minor suggestions for potential improvements:

The supervised training involves an approximation due to an on-policy assumption. While learning occurs from a replay buffer, the Monte Carlo regression assumes that the expectation of the remainder of the trajectory follows the current policy. However, episodes are sampled from prior versions of the policy. This discrepancy should be discussed.

The algorithm utilizes additional metadata – specifically, information about which parts of the sensory input are valuable to predict – that the compared algorithms do not have access to. I believe this advantage, and the limitations of the approach when such measurements are unavailable (e.g., in sensory environments lacking such explicit indicators), should be highlighted more clearly.
