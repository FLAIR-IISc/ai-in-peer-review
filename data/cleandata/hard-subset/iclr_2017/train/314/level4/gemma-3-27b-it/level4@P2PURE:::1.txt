Deep Reinforcement Learning (RL), leveraging deep neural networks as function approximators, has demonstrated significant success in RL problems with large state spaces. This empirically focused work builds upon these advances by introducing a novel algorithm that achieves improved performance in unseen 3D environments, operating directly from raw sensory data and exhibiting enhanced generalization across different goals and environments. Notably, this algorithm was the winning entry in the Visual Doom AI competition.

The core idea of the algorithm is to utilize readily available, low-dimensional observations – such as ammo count or health status provided by the game engine – as supervised targets for prediction. Critically, this prediction is conditioned on both a provided goal vector (fixed, rather than learned) and the current action. Following training, the optimal action for a given state is selected as the action that maximizes the predicted outcome with respect to the specified goal. Unlike successor feature representations, this learning process is supervised and does not rely on a temporal difference (TD) relationship between state predictions.

The authors review related prior work in section 2, encompassing both state prediction within RL and goal-directed function approximation. The key contributions of this paper lie in its emphasis on Monte Carlo estimation (as opposed to TD learning), the exploitation of low-dimensional ‘measurements’ for prediction, the use of parametrized goals, and, crucially, a thorough empirical comparison to relevant existing methods. 

Beyond the strong performance in the Visual Doom AI competition, the authors demonstrate that their algorithm learns generalizable policies capable of adapting to limited changes in the goal without requiring additional training. 

The paper is clearly written and presents compelling empirical results that are likely to be of substantial interest to the community.

Minor potential improvements:
The supervised training process incorporates an on-policy assumption, yet learning occurs from a replay buffer. The Monte Carlo regression relies on the expectation of the remainder of the trajectory aligning with the current policy; however, samples are drawn from episodes generated by earlier policy versions. This discrepancy should be discussed.
Furthermore, the algorithm utilizes supplementary metadata – information regarding which aspects of the sensory input are valuable for prediction – that is not available to the compared algorithms. This advantage, and the potential limitations of the approach when such measurements are unavailable (e.g., in sensory environments lacking such explicit signals), should be more explicitly acknowledged.
