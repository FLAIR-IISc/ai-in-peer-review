The paper presents an on-policy method for predicting future intrinsic measurements. All experiments are performed within the Doom environment (specifically, vizDoom), and instead of predicting win/loss or score (number of frags), the authors train their model to predict a sequence of triplets representing (health, ammunition, frags), weighted by a corresponding sequence of “goal” triplets provided as input. Modifying the weights of these goal triplets provides a mechanism for exploration and guidance. At test time, the agent acts by maximizing long-term goal achievement.

The results are impressive; this model won the 2016 vizDoom competition. The experimental section appears sound, with the following key features:
- Comparisons are provided between DFP and A3C, DQN, and an attempt to compare with DSR (a recent, similar approach from Kulkarni et al., 2016). DFP outperforms other approaches or achieves equivalent performance when they reach a ceiling or optimum, as observed with A3C in scenario D1.
- An ablation study supports the claim that the added complexity of the proposed model is beneficial.

Predicting intrinsic motivation (Singh et al. 2004), utilizing auxiliary variables, and forward modelling are well-established areas within reinforcement learning. The version of the paper I reviewed (December 4th revision) adequately references prior work, although the coverage isn’t exhaustive.

I have a few minor comments regarding presentation:
- The paper describes Doom as a 3D environment; however, it is more accurately characterized as a 2D environment projected in a (pseudo-)3D perspective, as the height dimension is not discriminative or actionable.
- The notation “P” in equation (2) and subsequent equations might be misleading, as it denotes a prediction but not a probability, which is the typical interpretation of ‘P’.
- The repeated use of the index “j” (albeit with different fonts) in equation (6) could be confusing.
- Results tables could benefit from explicitly repeating the units of measurement, particularly given the heterogeneity of units in Table 1.

Overall, I recommend accepting this paper. While one could argue for experiments in additional environments or question the extent of novelty, I believe that rigorous (no-nonsense, experimentally validated on Doom, with sufficient detail for reproducibility in the appendix) and impactful (vizDoom winner) papers warrant publication.
