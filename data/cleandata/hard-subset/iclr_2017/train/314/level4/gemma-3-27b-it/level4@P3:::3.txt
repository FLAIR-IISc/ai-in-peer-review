## Review for ICLR 2017

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, framing the problem as supervised learning through prediction of future intrinsic measurements. The core idea is to leverage the rich supervisory signal inherent in the cotemporal structure of sensory input and measurements, bypassing the need for explicit reward signals common in reinforcement learning. The approach is evaluated extensively in the challenging Doom environment (using the ViZDoom platform), demonstrating state-of-the-art performance and winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Decision: Accept**

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field, offering a compelling alternative to traditional reinforcement learning methods for complex sensorimotor control tasks. The impressive experimental results, particularly winning the Visual Doom AI Competition, provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The clarity of the presentation and thoroughness of the experimentation further support acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles a crucial problem in reinforcement learning: learning effective control policies directly from raw sensory input in complex, dynamic environments. The motivation for the approach is well-articulated, building on existing work in supervised learning, reinforcement learning, and prediction-based control. The authors effectively position their work within the relevant literature, highlighting the advantages of their method compared to existing approaches.

The experimental validation is comprehensive. The authors compare their method (DFP) against strong baselines (DQN, A3C, and DSR) across multiple scenarios of increasing difficulty. The results consistently demonstrate superior performance, particularly in complex tasks where DFP significantly outperforms other methods. The ablation studies provide valuable insights into the importance of different components of the proposed approach, strengthening the argument for its effectiveness. Furthermore, the generalization experiments and the success in the Visual Doom AI Competition demonstrate the robustness and practical relevance of the method. 

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and technical details. The network architecture is described with sufficient detail and the experimental setup is thoroughly documented. The inclusion of an appendix detailing implementation specifics enhances reproducibility.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

While the paper is strong, several minor points could further improve its clarity and impact:

*   **Environment Description:** The description of Doom as a "three-dimensional environment" is technically inaccurate. It is a 2D environment rendered with a pseudo-3D perspective. Clarifying this would improve precision.
*   **Notation:** The use of "P" for prediction in equation (2) could be ambiguous, given its common association with probability. A more descriptive variable name might reduce confusion. Similarly, the reuse of “j” with different fonts in equation (6) should be avoided.
*   **Table Clarity:** Tables, particularly Table 1, would benefit from explicitly stating the units of each measured quantity (e.g., health, ammunition, frags). This improves readability and avoids potential misinterpretations.
*   **Future Work Discussion:** Expanding on the discussion of future work could further highlight the potential impact of this research. Specifically, discussing how this approach might be combined with hierarchical reinforcement learning or methods for continuous action spaces would be valuable.
*   **Goal-Agnostic Training Analysis:** Providing a more detailed analysis of the variance in performance across different goal vectors in the goal-agnostic training experiments (Table 3) would be beneficial.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   The ablation study demonstrates the importance of predicting multiple measurements. Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with predicting more measurements? Is there a practical limit to the number of measurements that can be effectively predicted?
*   The paper mentions that predicting full sensory streams is currently beyond capabilities. What are the key challenges preventing this, and what future developments would be necessary to overcome them?
*   The success in the Visual Doom AI Competition is highlighted. Could you provide more details about the specific modifications or parameter tuning applied to the model for the competition, compared to the results presented in the main body of the paper?
