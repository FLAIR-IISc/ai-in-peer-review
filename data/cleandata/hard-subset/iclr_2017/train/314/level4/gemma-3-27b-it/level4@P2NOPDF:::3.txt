This paper introduces a novel on-policy reinforcement learning method focused on predicting future intrinsic state variables. The experiments are conducted within the challenging Doom environment (vizDoom), and notably, the model moves beyond traditional reward prediction—such as win/loss or score—to predict sequences of (health, ammunition, frags). These predictions are weighted by input "goal" triplets, providing a mechanism for guiding exploration; at test time, the agent optimizes for long-term goal achievement.

The reported results are compelling, highlighted by the model’s success in winning the 2016 vizDoom competition. The experimental evaluation appears thorough and well-structured. The authors provide comparisons against strong baselines, including A3C, DQN, and an attempt to replicate and compare against DSR (Kulkarni et al., 2016), a related recent work. DFP consistently outperforms these approaches, or achieves comparable performance when baselines reach their limits – as observed with A3C in scenario D1. Furthermore, a well-designed ablation study effectively demonstrates the value of the model’s architectural choices and complexities.

The work builds upon established research areas within reinforcement learning, including intrinsic motivation (Singh et al., 2004), auxiliary variable prediction, and forward modeling. The December 4th revision demonstrates a satisfactory awareness of relevant prior work, though the coverage isn’t exhaustive. 

I have a few minor comments regarding presentation:

*   The paper describes Doom as a 3D environment, which is technically inaccurate. While presented *as* 3D, the game is fundamentally 2D, as height is not a directly actionable or discriminative dimension.
*   The notation "P" in equation (2) and subsequent equations could be misleading. While used to denote prediction, it does not represent a probability, which is the typical interpretation of "P" in many contexts.
*   The dual use of the index "j" (presented with differing font styles) in equation (6) may introduce ambiguity.
*   The results tables, particularly Table 1, would benefit from explicitly repeating the units of measurement for each variable, given their heterogeneous nature.

Overall, I recommend accepting this paper. While it could be argued that broadening the experimental environments or emphasizing incremental novelty might be beneficial, I believe that papers which are technically sound, experimentally validated (particularly on a challenging environment like Doom), and demonstrably successful—as evidenced by its vizDoom competition win—deserve publication. This work represents a clear and valuable contribution to the field.
