This research paper presents a policy-based method for predicting future internal measures. All experiments were conducted in the game of Doom (i.e., within Doom itself), and instead of simply predicting win/loss or the number of frags (score), the authors trained their model to predict a triplet (sequence) of (health, ammunition, frags) weighted by a "target" triplet (sequence) provided as input. Changing the weight of the target triplet is a method that guides search. During testing, one can only maximize the long-term goal.

The results are impressive, as this model won the 2016 Doom competition. The experimental part of the paper seems solid:

- An attempt to compare A3C with DFP and DSR (recently similar approaches by Kulkarni et al., 2016). DFP performs better (or equally) than other approaches when they reach a limit/optimum, as is the case for A3C in scenario D1.
- There is an ablation study that supports the thesis that all the "extra complexity" of the paper's model is useful.
- Internal motivation (Singh et al. 2004), predicting auxiliary variables, and further modeling are well-studied areas of reinforcement learning. The version I read (December 4 revision) sufficiently referenced previous work, even if it was not exhaustive.
- Some comments on the form (nitpicks):
 - Doom is described as a 3D environment, while it is actually a 2D environment (height is not a differentiating/active dimension) presented in (fake) 3D.
 - The use of "P" in (2) (and later) can be misleading because it stands for prediction but is unlikely (as is usually the case for P).
 - The double use of "J" in (6) (assuming with different fonts) can be misleading.
 - The units of measurement in the results tables can be repeated (especially because they are odd in Table 1).

I think this paper is clearly acceptable. One could argue that the experiments could have been conducted in different environments or that the novelty is limited, but I think the "right" (no nonsense, experimentally solid on Doom, the appendix provides details for reproducibility) and "milestone" (Doom winner) research papers should be published.
