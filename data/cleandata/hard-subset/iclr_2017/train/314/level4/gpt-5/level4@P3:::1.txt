1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes a supervised-learning formulation for sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments. Instead of optimizing a scalar reward via TD methods, the agent predicts future values of a low-dimensional “measurement” vector (e.g., health, ammo, frags) conditioned on the current observation, a candidate action, and a goal vector. Actions are selected by choosing the action whose predicted future measurements best align with the goal. This approach:
- Uses Monte Carlo–style regression rather than TD, avoiding bootstrapping.
- Leverages dense, vector-valued feedback to stabilize and accelerate learning from raw sensory input.
- Trains without a fixed goal and supports goal-conditioned behavior at test time (including unseen goals).
- Achieves strong empirical performance in ViZDoom across tasks of increasing difficulty, generalizes across textures/environments, and won the Visual Doom AI Competition’s Full Deathmatch track.

The paper is clearly written, well-situated relative to RL, UVFA, and successor-representation work, and includes solid ablations (multi-measurement vs scalar reward; multi-horizon prediction).

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Strong and consistent empirical evidence that direct future prediction with vector-valued measurements is effective and competitive with state-of-the-art deep RL, especially on challenging 3D tasks and under goal changes.
- Conceptually clean, well-motivated reframing that enables goal-conditioned control and generalization without extraneous supervision or imitation data.

3. Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? Learning sensorimotor control from high-dimensional observations in complex 3D environments while remaining flexible to changing, parameterized goals.

Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? Yes. The authors motivate supervised direct future prediction when dense, multidimensional feedback is available, contrasting against TD’s advantages with sparse, delayed scalar rewards. The work is positioned against Monte Carlo vs TD debates, predictive representations, UVFA, and successor representations, and differentiates itself by (i) predicting intrinsic measurement vectors rather than full sensory streams and (ii) conditioning on goals during both training and action selection.

Do the results support the claims and are they rigorous? Largely yes.
- Comparative performance: The method (DFP) outperforms DQN, A3C, and DSR on more complex Doom scenarios (Battle/Battle 2), matches or exceeds baselines on simpler tasks, and trains efficiently. The competition win in unseen maps strengthens the generalization claim.
- Generalization: Models trained with texture variation generalize to new environments; goal-agnostic training yields policies that adapt at test time to new goals nearly as well as goal-specific training.
- Ablations: Predicting multiple measurements and multiple temporal offsets improves performance, supporting the central hypothesis that dense vector feedback is a superior learning signal compared to scalar rewards.
- Architecture: A goal-conditional predictor with action- and expectation-stream decomposition (akin to dueling networks) is a reasonable, transparent design choice.

Caveats: The approach relies on access to meaningful, low-dimensional measurements; this metadata may not be available in all domains. The training uses a small replay buffer with ε-greedy data while optimizing a policy-dependent Monte Carlo objective, introducing a mild off-policy approximation that deserves discussion. Nonetheless, empirical stability and performance are strong.

4. Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all points affect the accept decision)
- Clarify off-policy effects: The regression target assumes trajectories from the current policy, but data come from a replay buffer. Please discuss potential bias and why it appears benign here (e.g., small buffer, frequent policy updates), and whether you observed instability with larger buffers.
- Fairness of comparisons: You use the same linear combination of measurement deltas as scalar reward for baselines, which is good. Still, highlight explicitly that DFP leverages vector-valued supervision (predicting each dimension), whereas baselines receive only the scalarized reward; this likely contributes to DFP’s advantage. If feasible, include a baseline augmented with auxiliary prediction of the same measurements to isolate the benefit of the learning signal vs the control objective.
- Measurement selection and availability: Discuss how to choose measurements in new domains, what happens if measurements are noisy/incomplete, and limitations when only sparse rewards are available. An experiment with noisy or partially missing measurements would be informative.
- Sensitivity to goal parameterization: Report whether you normalize or constrain goal vectors g, and provide sensitivity to the choice of temporal offsets and their weights. How robust is performance to different horizon sets?
- Policy class and memory: The current model is reactive. Briefly quantify performance gains (if any) from adding recurrence or external memory on the harder tasks, or discuss anticipated trade-offs.
- Compute and sample efficiency: Include wall-clock training times and steps/sec per method for transparency, and summarize hyperparameter tuning effort (especially for A3C).
- Code and reproducibility: If possible, release code and training configs to facilitate adoption.

Questions for the authors
- Off-policy training: How large is the replay buffer relative to episode length, and how frequently are model parameters updated? Did larger buffers harm performance? Any empirical signs of bias (e.g., instability when ε decays)?
- Goal handling: Are goal vectors normalized or constrained during training/testing? How does performance change with different goal distributions during goal-agnostic training?
- Temporal offsets: How sensitive are results to the choice {1,2,4,8,16,32} and to the weighting of the last three steps? Have you tried longer horizons or learned weights?
- Measurement design: In domains without explicit statistics (e.g., real robots), what criteria guide measurement selection? Have you tried learning measurements as auxiliary features from pixels (e.g., via self-supervision) and then predicting those?
- Baselines with auxiliary tasks: Did you evaluate A3C/DQN augmented with auxiliary prediction of the same measurement vector? This would help attribute gains to signal richness vs objective structure.
- Generalization: In the texture-randomization setting, do agents overfit to layout? How does performance change with procedurally generated layouts, not just textures?
- Limits: How does DFP perform when measurements are very sparse or delayed (e.g., frags only)? Do benefits persist with fewer or noisier measurements?

Overall, this is a clear, well-executed paper with strong empirical results and a compelling, simple idea that enhances goal-conditioned control and generalization. I recommend acceptance.
