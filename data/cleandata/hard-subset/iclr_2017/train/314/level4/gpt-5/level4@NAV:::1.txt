Deep reinforcement learning (i.e., using deep neural networks as function approximators within RL) has achieved notable success in large state spaces. This empirically driven paper extends those approaches. It introduces a new algorithm that performs better in novel 3D environments from raw sensory inputs and exhibits improved generalization across goals and environments. Notably, the algorithm won the Visual Doom AI competition.

The central idea is to use additional low-dimensional observations (e.g., ammo or health provided by the game engine) as supervised prediction targets. Crucially, these predictions are conditioned on a goal vector (supplied, not learned) and the current action. After training, the action for the current state is selected as the one that maximizes the predicted outcome under the given goal. Unlike successor feature representations, the learning is purely supervised, with no temporal-difference relationship linking predictions across successive states.

There is substantial prior work on predicting future states within RL and on goal-driven function approximators, which the authors review in Section 2. The key contributions here are the emphasis on Monte Carlo estimation (rather than TD), the use of low-dimensional “measurements” as prediction targets, the parameterized goals, and, perhaps most importantly, the empirical comparisons to relevant prior methods.

Beyond the comparison with Visual Doom AI, the authors demonstrate that their algorithm learns generalizable policies that can respond, without additional training, to limited changes in the goal.

The paper is clearly communicated, the empirical results are compelling, and the work will be of significant interest.

Some minor potential improvements:
- The supervised training introduces an approximation: it assumes on-policy data while learning from a replay buffer. With Monte Carlo regression, the expectation over the remainder of the trajectory is treated as following the current policy, yet episodes are sampled from earlier policies. This should be discussed.
- The algorithm leverages additional metadata—information about which parts of the sensory input are worth predicting—that the compared methods do not use. This dependency, and its limitations (e.g., potential weakness in environments that do not provide such measurements), should be stated more clearly.
