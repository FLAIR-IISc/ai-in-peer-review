Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes a goal-conditioned, largely on-policy approach to sensorimotor control that predicts future intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) from high-dimensional sensory input and a goal vector, then selects the action that maximizes expected goal attainment. This reframes control as supervised prediction rather than reward-driven value estimation.
- The formulation naturally supports training without a fixed goal and pursuing dynamically changing goals at test time. It leverages dense, multidimensional supervision from the measurement stream, which the authors argue stabilizes and accelerates learning.
- The architecture combines perception, measurement, and goal modules, and splits the prediction head into expectation and action streams to better capture action-specific differences.
- Extensive experiments in ViZDoom show strong empirical results, including outperforming DQN, A3C, and DSR on increasingly difficult 3D tasks, generalization across textures/environments and goals, and a first-place result in the Visual Doom AI Competition. Ablations highlight the benefit of vectorial measurements and multiple temporal offsets.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) Clear and consistent empirical gains on challenging 3D visuomotor tasks, with informative ablations and generalization tests.
2) A simple, well-motivated formulation that unifies several existing ideas (e.g., goal-conditioned prediction akin to universal value function approximators) into a practical method with strong performance.

Supporting arguments
- Problem: The paper addresses learning visuomotor control from raw pixels in complex 3D environments while enabling dynamic, parameterized goals. This is clearly stated and important.
- Motivation and placement: The approach is positioned as a supervised, goal-conditioned alternative to reward-centric RL, closely related to universal value function approximators (UVFA) and Monte Carlo-style prediction. The paper cites appropriate prior work and articulates when dense, vectorial feedback can favor supervised prediction over TD methods. I agree with this placement, though I would appreciate a crisper articulation of what is technically novel versus what is a well-executed synthesis.
- Evidence and rigor: The Doom experiments are thorough, with consistent baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR), strong results on harder scenarios, and useful ablations (vectorial vs scalar feedback; multiple horizons). The competition result further supports external validity. That said, evaluation is confined to a single domain (ViZDoom); broader benchmarking would strengthen claims about generality.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Clarify contributions: The core novelty appears to be distilling goal-conditioned future prediction with vectorial measurements into a practical system that scales to 3D environments, together with an architectural head split and multi-horizon targets. Please state these contributions more explicitly in the abstract and introduction, and distinguish them from prior UVFA-style and Monte Carlo prediction ideas.
- Failure modes: Provide a systematic analysis of failure cases. Under what conditions does the method fail to generalize to changing goals (e.g., goal distributions at test time that are out of support, antagonistic trade-offs among measurements, or sparse/uninformative measurement streams)?
- On-policy training and forgetting: Because training is largely on-policy with a fixed or evolving goal distribution, what prevents catastrophic forgetting of earlier goals? How sensitive is performance to the distribution of goals encountered during training, and to revisiting older goals? An empirical study (e.g., goal cycling or hold-out goal families) would be very informative.
- Broader domains: Since the primary contribution is an integration that yields strong empirical advantages, additional domains would help establish generality. Atari seems natural; ROM/RAM variables could serve as intrinsic measurements. Even a small-scale study on a subset of games would strengthen the case.
- Comparisons to UVFA: A direct empirical comparison to a UVFA baseline (with similar architecture and capacity) would sharpen the claim that goal-conditioned future measurement prediction is especially effective.
- Measurement design: Discuss guidance for choosing measurements and their temporal offsets. How does performance vary with different measurement sets, offset schedules, or adding noisy/irrelevant measurements?
- Sample efficiency and stability: Report learning curves in wall-clock time and steps, and analyze the effect of replay/experience memory size and ε-greedy settings. This would help position the approach on efficiency versus TD-based methods.

Questions for the authors
1) Generalization to new goals: What is the relationship between the training-time goal distribution and test-time performance? Do you observe specific patterns where performance degrades (e.g., highly conflicting objectives or goals emphasizing unobserved offsets)?
2) Catastrophic forgetting: Did you quantify forgetting when returning to previously seen goals after long intervals? Would interleaving older goals or using a replay buffer stratified by goal mitigate this?
3) Off-policy usage: You mention a small experience memory. How sensitive are results to its size and sampling strategy? Could larger off-policy replay or goal relabeling further improve performance?
4) UVFA baseline: Can you provide results with a UVFA-style value function (same backbone, goal conditioning, and capacity) to isolate the benefit of predicting vectorial future measurements versus scalar value/reward?
5) Other domains: Do you have preliminary results on Atari or other simulators using ROM/RAM as intrinsic measurements? Any practical challenges you encountered?
6) Measurement choice: How robust is the method to noisy or partially informative measurements? Have you tried learning a subset of measurements or adding distractors?
7) Horizon design: You use multiple temporal offsets but only optimize the last three with specific weights. How sensitive is performance to this choice, and have you tried learning the weighting or offsets?

Overall, this is a strong empirical paper that convincingly demonstrates the benefits of goal-conditioned future measurement prediction in complex 3D environments. With clearer articulation of contributions, an analysis of failure modes and forgetting, and broader domain coverage, the paper would be even stronger.
