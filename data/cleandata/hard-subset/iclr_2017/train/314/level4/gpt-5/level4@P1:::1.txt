1. Summary of contributions
This paper proposes a simple and effective approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments by reframing action selection as supervised prediction of future “measurements” (e.g., health, ammo, frags) conditioned on the current observation, the candidate action, and a goal vector. Rather than learning via TD targets, the model is trained with Monte Carlo regression to predict low-dimensional, semantically meaningful measurement changes at multiple future horizons. At test time, the agent chooses the action whose predicted future measurements best align with the given goal. This yields a policy that can be trained without a fixed goal and can adapt to new, dynamically specified goals at test time. The approach performs strongly on ViZDoom tasks, generalizes across textures and goals, and won the Visual Doom AI Competition (Full Deathmatch) despite using a simpler model and less supervision than the runner-up.

2. Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Strong empirical performance and generalization: The method outperforms DQN, A3C, and DSR on challenging 3D control tasks, especially when multiple measurements are available, and demonstrates notable goal-conditioned generalization without retraining.
- Clear, well-motivated formulation: The supervised, goal-conditioned future prediction framing is simple, practical, and well-placed in the literature as a Monte Carlo alternative to TD-based RL, with convincing ablations showing why vectorial, temporally dense feedback helps.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and novelty: The paper targets learning from raw sensory streams in complex 3D environments while supporting dynamically specified goals. The key idea—predicting future changes in low-dimensional, intrinsic measurements conditioned on action and goal—is a clean way to exploit dense, multidimensional feedback. Unlike successor features and TD methods, learning is purely supervised with no bootstrapping across time steps.
- Relation to prior work: The paper situates itself well within Monte Carlo vs TD debates, UVFA/goal-parameterized value functions, multi-objective RL, and predictive representations. The departure from scalar rewards to vectorial measurement prediction is well justified, and the use of goal-conditioning connects naturally to UVFA while keeping training simple.
- Empirical rigor: Comparisons to DQN, A3C, and DSR on four ViZDoom scenarios show consistent advantages, particularly on the harder Battle tasks where three measurements are available. The study of generalization across textures/environments and across goals is compelling. Ablations demonstrate that (a) predicting multiple measurements improves even single-metric evaluation and (b) multi-horizon prediction helps. The competition win strengthens the case for practicality.
- Communication: The paper is clear and well structured; the architecture and training procedure are described succinctly, and the baselines are run carefully with reasonable hyperparameter searches.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not central to my decision)
- Off-policy training details: The training objective assumes trajectories follow the current policy, yet the model updates from a replay buffer populated by earlier policies. This mild on-policy approximation should be discussed: how large is the buffer, how quickly are parameters updated relative to data collection, and did you observe bias or instability when increasing buffer size? Any attempts at importance weighting or on-policy ablations would be informative.
- Use of auxiliary metadata: The approach leverages additional low-dimensional measurements provided by the environment (e.g., health, ammo), which many RL baselines do not directly use as supervised targets. Please make explicit that this is extra structure and discuss its implications for fairness of comparison. It would strengthen the work to include baselines augmented with the same measurements as auxiliary prediction tasks (e.g., UNREAL-style) or reward shaping, if feasible.
- Scope and limitations: Clarify scenarios where such intrinsic measurements are unavailable or noisy. What happens if only sparse scalar rewards exist? A brief discussion of potential extensions (e.g., learning measurement proxies from sensory features or predicting learned features) would help scope applicability.
- Goal vectors: The goal is provided, not learned. Please clarify how goals are normalized/scaled, how training-time goal distributions relate to test-time goals, and sensitivity to misspecified or adversarial goals. A short analysis of performance vs goal-vector magnitude and sparsity would be helpful.
- Partial observability and memory: The policy is reactive. Given that Doom is partially observable, a brief experiment with recurrence (e.g., LSTM) or a discussion of expected gains would add depth.

Questions for the authors
- Replay and policy mismatch: How sensitive is performance to replay buffer size and staleness? Did you try purely on-policy training or importance sampling to correct off-policy bias?
- Measurements: How robust is the method to the choice, dimensionality, and noise of the measurement vector? Do some measurements dominate learning, and how do you handle scaling/normalization across measurements and horizons?
- Auxiliary baselines: Have you evaluated A3C/DQN augmented to predict the same measurements as auxiliary tasks, or to use them for reward shaping, to assess how much of the gain comes from the supervised signal vs the overall framework?
- Goal specification: How were per-episode randomized goals sampled and normalized ([0,1] vs [-1,1])? How does performance vary with goal distributions unseen during training?
- Continuous actions and memory: Do you anticipate difficulties extending this approach to continuous control or adding recurrence? Any preliminary results?
- Compute and sample efficiency: Could you report training wall-clock time and sample efficiency relative to A3C under identical hardware constraints? How critical is the multi-horizon setup to efficiency?

Overall, this is a clear, well-motivated paper with strong empirical results and practical impact. I recommend acceptance.
