Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes direct future prediction (DFP), a goal-conditioned supervised learning approach to sensorimotor control. Instead of learning a value function for a scalar reward, the agent predicts future changes in a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) at multiple temporal offsets, conditioned on the current observation and a goal vector. Actions are selected by choosing the action whose predicted future measurements best align with the current goal.
- The key idea is to exploit the dense, multivariate measurement stream as supervision, turning control into a supervised prediction problem that supports training without fixed goals and enables dynamically specified goals at test time.
- Extensive experiments in ViZDoom across four scenarios show DFP matches or surpasses DQN and A3C, especially on the more challenging combat tasks. The method generalizes across textures and goals, benefits from vectorial measurements and multi-horizon prediction, and won the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition (Full Deathmatch) without extra supervision.

Decision: Accept
- Reason 1: Strong, reproducible empirical evidence that a simple, well-motivated predictive formulation outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL baselines on challenging 3D visual control tasks, and generalizes across environments and goals.
- Reason 2: The paper is clear, technically sound, and well-situated in the literature on predictive representations, multi-objective RL, and goal-conditioned value functions, while offering a practical architecture and thorough ablations.

Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: Learning sensorimotor control from raw visual input in immersive environments, with the ability to handle dynamically specified goals.
- Motivation and placement: The work bridges supervised learning and control by leveraging dense measurement streams, relating to predictive representations (GVFs), Monte Carlo methods, UVFA, and multi-objective RL. The paper makes a strong case for SL-style training when rich feedback is available and shows an effective instantiation at scale.
- Evidence for claims: 
  - Head-to-head comparisons with DQN, A3C, and an attempted DSR baseline. DFP matches A3C on the simplest task and substantially outperforms it on the harder combat scenarios (notably when multiple measurements are present).
  - Generalization experiments across textures and environments, including a higher-capacity variant.
  - Goal-randomized training enabling adaptation to new goals at test time with minimal loss on the main task and improved robustness to novel objectives.
  - Ablations demonstrating the benefits of vector-valued targets versus a scalar reward and the utility of predicting multiple temporal offsets.
  - External validation via the Visual Doom AI Competition, beating a stronger, more supervised baseline.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not critical to my decision)
- Environment description: Doom is often regarded as “2.5D” (height offers limited actionable variation). Clarify this nuance while keeping the emphasis on the complexity of first-person visual perception and dynamics.
- Notation clarity:
  - Using P for predictions can be confused with probability; consider a different symbol (e.g., F(·) or ŷ) in Eq. (2) and subsequent equations.
  - Avoid reusing the symbol j in different contexts (e.g., Eq. (6)); distinct symbols or subscripts would help.
- Units and heterogeneity: Reiterate units in all result tables, especially when combining heterogeneous measurements (health, ammo, frags), and ensure normalization choices are clearly stated near the results.
- On-policy vs off-policy: The method uses ε-greedy behavior with a short replay buffer. Briefly discuss any stability/efficiency trade-offs and why a small buffer works well here.
- Broader evaluation: While ViZDoom is a strong testbed, a brief discussion (or small experiment) in a different 3D environment would strengthen claims of generality.
- Limitations and extensions: Expand the discussion of memory, temporal abstraction, and continuous action spaces, and how DFP could integrate with these directions.
- Reproducibility: Centralize key hyperparameters (experience memory size, ε schedule, horizon and weighting for temporal offsets, optimizer settings, compute/training time) in the main text or a compact table to complement the appendices. Report variance across seeds for all baselines.

Questions for the authors
- Measurement design: How sensitive is performance to the choice and scaling/normalization of measurements? If some measurements are noisy or uninformative, does prediction degrade gracefully?
- Goal sampling: In goal-randomized training, how were goal vectors sampled and normalized relative to the measurement scales? Did you try curriculum strategies or structured goal distributions?
- Multi-horizon targets: You predict at 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32 steps with heavier weight on longer horizons. How sensitive are results to the set of offsets and their weights? Did you try learned horizon weights or discount-like schedules?
- Experience memory: What memory size worked best, and did larger buffers harm performance (e.g., due to non-stationarity of goals or policy drift)?
- Baseline tuning: For A3C/DQN, did you consider reward clipping, frame stacking, or different entropy regularization schedules? Any ablations to ensure the DFP advantage is not due to omitted stabilizers?
- Generalization: In the texture-generalization experiments, did you assess robustness to lighting changes, object variants, or partial observability (e.g., fog/smoke)?
- Computational cost: Please report wall-clock training time and hardware for DFP vs baselines to contextualize the “steps per day” numbers.
- Beyond discrete actions: Do you foresee any obstacles to adapting DFP to continuous action spaces (e.g., via action sampling or policy gradients guided by the predicted futures)?

Overall, this is a clear, well-executed paper with compelling results and a clean idea that is likely to influence future work on goal-conditioned control with rich sensory feedback. I recommend acceptance.
