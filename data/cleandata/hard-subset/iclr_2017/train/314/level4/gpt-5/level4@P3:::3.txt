Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning formulation for sensorimotor control in immersive environments. Instead of optimizing a scalar reward via TD or policy-gradient methods, the agent predicts vector-valued future measurement changes (e.g., health, ammo, frags) conditioned on the current observation, candidate action, and a goal vector. Acting reduces to selecting the action whose predicted measurements best align with the goal. This decouples training from a fixed reward, enables training with goal randomization, and supports dynamically specified goals at test time. The model operates directly on raw RGB input and low-dimensional measurements, with a simple yet effective architecture that separates an expectation stream from action-specific deviations.

Empirically, the method outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C) on ViZDoom scenarios, particularly on the more complex battle tasks with multiple measurements. The paper also demonstrates generalization across textures and environments, goal-agnostic training that transfers to new goals at test time, an ablation showing the benefits of multivariate and multi-horizon prediction, and a competition win in the Visual Doom AI challenge, all without additional supervision.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical results and clear scientific support: DFP consistently matches or surpasses state-of-the-art baselines, especially on challenging scenarios, and ablations support the core design choices.
- Conceptual clarity and practical impact: reframing control as supervised prediction over dense, intrinsic measurement streams is simple, well-motivated, and effective, enabling flexible goal specification and sample-efficient training.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets learning to act from raw sensory input in complex 3D-like environments while supporting variable goals—an important challenge. The shift from sparse scalar rewards to dense, vector-valued measurements is well motivated by the availability of intrinsic signals and the stabilizing effect of dense supervision.
- Relation to literature: The work is well situated with respect to TD vs. Monte Carlo, predictive representations, UVFA, multi-objective RL, and successor representations. It extends these ideas with goal-parameterized prediction of intrinsic measurements from rich sensory input in complex settings.
- Evidence for claims: Results cover four scenarios of increasing difficulty; DFP matches A3C on basic tasks and substantially dominates on battle tasks. Generalization across textures and to unseen environments is convincing, particularly when trained with diversity. Goal-agnostic training demonstrates the ability to adapt to unseen test-time goals with minimal performance loss compared to a goal-specific model. The ablation shows that multi-measurement and multi-horizon prediction materially improves performance, even when evaluation focuses on a single metric. The Visual Doom Competition win underscores robustness and competitiveness.
- Soundness and clarity: The approach is straightforward to implement, training details are provided, and comparisons use standard baselines with reasonable hyperparameter searches. The architectural decomposition (expectation vs. action stream) is sensible and empirically beneficial.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not central to my decision)
- Clarify “on-policy”: The training uses an ε-greedy policy with a small replay buffer and periodic updates, which departs from pure on-policy training. Please clarify the extent to which off-policy samples are used and whether performance is sensitive to replay size or update lag.
- Measurements and scaling: Elaborate on normalization/standardization of measurements and how scaling affects training stability and goal weighting. Are certain measurements sparser or more volatile, and how does the model handle that?
- Prediction horizons: The model predicts at multiple offsets and weights the last three. Provide a sensitivity analysis for horizon choices, weighting coefficients, and whether longer horizons or learned weights help.
- Partial observability and memory: The model is reactive (single frame). Comment on performance with frame stacks or recurrent memory, and whether memory is expected to further improve complex scenarios.
- Fairness of comparisons: DSR could only be evaluated on the basic task due to training speed. A brief discussion of sample efficiency and wall-clock considerations across methods would further contextualize advantages.
- Relation to UVFA and successor representations: Deepen the comparison to UVFA and DSR, clarifying how direct measurement prediction differs in practice (e.g., training targets, bias/variance, policy-dependence) and when each is preferable.
- Competition setup: Summarize any differences between the competition agent and the paper’s main D4-tx-L setup (e.g., hyperparameters, training diversity) to aid reproducibility.
- Minor style/notation:
  - Doom is often described as 3D; strictly, it is a 2.5D engine. Consider phrasing accordingly.
  - Using P in Eq. (2) to denote predictions may be confused with probability; consider a different symbol.
  - Avoid reusing j for different purposes (even with typographical differences) to reduce confusion.
  - Repeat units in result tables, especially when aggregating heterogeneous measurements (e.g., Table 1).

Questions for the authors
- Goal distribution: For randomized-goal training, what is the exact sampling distribution and normalization of g? How sensitive is performance and generalization to this choice?
- Measurement choice: How critical is the particular measurement set (health, ammo, frags)? Have you tried adding/removing measurements, or learned features as pseudo-measurements?
- Horizon and discounting: How does DFP compare to optimizing a discounted scalarized objective via learned discounting or by extending τ beyond 32? Could learned temporal weighting (instead of fixed coefficients) improve results?
- Off-policy tolerance: Since the predictor is trained via regression from replay, how robust is DFP to larger replay buffers or more off-policy data? Does mixing in expert or scripted trajectories help?
- Exploration: Beyond ε-greedy, did you explore goal-driven exploration via varying g during training to induce diverse behaviors? Any comparison to intrinsic curiosity or count-based methods?
- Architecture: How important is the expectation/action-stream decomposition vs. a single head, especially when the action set grows? Any performance changes with dueling-style aggregation variants?
- Generalization: In D3/D4 texture-transfer experiments, what is the failure mode when training only on a single texture (e.g., overfitting to color/contrast)? Did data augmentation help?
- Computational cost: Can you provide training wall-clock times and sample budgets per scenario for all methods to quantify efficiency gains from dense supervision?

Overall, this is a strong, well-executed paper with clear contributions and substantial empirical impact. I recommend acceptance.
