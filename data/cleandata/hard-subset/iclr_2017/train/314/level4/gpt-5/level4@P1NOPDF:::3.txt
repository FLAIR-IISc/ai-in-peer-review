This paper presents an on-policy method for predicting future intrinsic measurements. All experiments are conducted in Doom (specifically VizDoom). Instead of predicting only win/loss or frags (score), the authors train a model to predict sequences of triplets (health, ammunition, frags), weighted by input “goal” triplets. Adjusting the goal weights provides a mechanism to guide exploration; at test time, one can act by maximizing the long-term goal alone.

The results are impressive: this model won the 2016 VizDoom competition. The experimental section appears sound:
- The paper compares DFP with A3C, DQN, and attempts a comparison with DSR (a recent related approach by Kulkarni et al., 2016). DFP outperforms the other methods, or matches them when they reach a ceiling/optimum (as with A3C in scenario D1).
- An ablation study supports the claim that each component of the proposed model contributes meaningfully.

Intrinsic motivation (Singh et al., 2004), auxiliary tasks, and forward modeling are well-studied areas in reinforcement learning. The December 4th revision I read cites prior work adequately, even if not exhaustively.

Minor comments on form:
- Doom is described as a 3D environment, but it is effectively 2D (height is not a discriminative/actionable dimension) rendered in “fake” 3D.
- The use of “P” in equation (2) (and subsequently) may be misleading, as it denotes prediction rather than probability (its more common meaning).
- The reuse of “j” (albeit in different fonts) in equation (6) may be confusing.
- Results tables should repeat measurement units, especially since metrics are heterogeneous in Table 1.

Overall, I consider this a clear accept. One might argue for experiments in additional environments or question the degree of novelty, but this work is “correct” (no-nonsense, experimentally solid on Doom, with an appendix that supports reproducibility) and a “milestone” (VizDoom winner). Such papers merit publication.
