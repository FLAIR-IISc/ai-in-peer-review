This paper builds on the recent successes of deep reinforcement learning (RL) in large state spaces. It introduces an empirically driven algorithm that learns from raw sensory input and demonstrates superior performance in novel 3D environments, with improved generalization across both goals and environments. Notably, the method won the Visual Doom AI competition.

The core idea is to predict low-dimensional measurements provided by the environment (e.g., ammo, health) as supervised targets. These predictions are conditioned on both a provided, non-learned goal vector and the current action. After training, the agent selects the action that maximizes the predicted outcome under the specified goal. In contrast to successor feature representations, learning here is fully supervised with Monte Carlo targets; there is no temporal-difference (TD) linkage between predictions for the current and next states.

The authors review prior work on predicting future states within RL and on goal-conditioned function approximation (Section 2). The main contributions are: (i) a focus on Monte Carlo estimation rather than TD; (ii) leveraging low-dimensional “measurements” as prediction targets; (iii) parameterized goals; and (iv) a careful empirical comparison against relevant baselines.

Beyond the Visual Doom AI comparison, the paper shows that the learned policies can generalize to modest changes in the goal without additional training.

The paper is clearly written, and the empirical results are compelling and likely to be of significant interest.

Minor suggestions for improvement:
- The supervised training relies on an on-policy assumption while learning from a replay buffer. In the Monte Carlo regression, the expected remainder of a trajectory is assumed to follow the current policy, yet samples are drawn from episodes collected under earlier policies. This off-policy approximation should be discussed more explicitly.
- The method benefits from additional metadata: it knows which low-dimensional aspects of the sensory stream are worth predicting (the measurements), whereas competing methods do not. This advantage, and the potential limitations in settings where such measurements are unavailable, should be stated more clearly.
