1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes a simple, empirically driven alternative to standard deep RL for sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments. Instead of optimizing a TD-based objective, the method performs supervised learning to predict future values of low-dimensional measurements (e.g., health, ammo), conditioned on the current observation, the candidate action, and a goal vector (provided, not learned). At decision time, the agent selects the action that maximizes the predicted goal-weighted outcome. This direct, Monte Carlo-style future prediction avoids bootstrapping and does not impose a TD relationship between consecutive state predictions. The approach learns from raw visual input, generalizes across goals and environments, and achieves strong empirical results on Doom-based tasks, including winning the Visual Doom AI competition. The paper also situates the work within prior literature on predictive models and goal-conditioned function approximators.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (i) compelling empirical performance and generalization from raw sensory data, including a competition win, and (ii) a clear, simple alternative to TD-based deep RL that is well motivated and practically effective.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem: robust sensorimotor control from raw sensory streams in complex 3D environments with dynamically specified goals.
- Motivation and placement: the paper clearly contrasts TD-based RL with a Monte Carlo-style supervised alternative and connects to prior work on predictive representations and goal-conditioned value function approximation, while highlighting its differences (measurement prediction, action- and goal-conditioning, no TD).
- Technical approach: predict future changes in measurements as supervised targets, conditioned on action and goal; select actions by maximizing the predicted goal-weighted outcome. This leverages dense, low-dimensional feedback that many environments naturally expose.
- Evidence: strong comparisons to relevant deep RL baselines (e.g., DQN, A3C, DSR) on multiple Doom scenarios, showing improved performance, particularly on harder tasks. Demonstrated generalization to new goals without additional training. The Visual Doom AI competition result underscores external validity.
- Communication: the paper is clearly written and the empirical evaluation is thorough and relevant.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not central to the accept decision)
- Training regime: The supervised regression implicitly assumes on-policy trajectories for its Monte Carlo targets, yet the method learns from a replay buffer that mixes policies across training. Please discuss this approximation, its potential bias, and why it appears benign in practice.
- Comparative assumptions: The method benefits from “measurement” metadata (e.g., health, ammo) as explicit supervised targets that baselines may not exploit in the same way. It would help to more explicitly acknowledge this advantage and clarify limitations when such measurements are not available or are incomplete.

5. Questions for the authors
- Off-policy regression: How sensitive is performance to the degree of off-policyness in the replay buffer? Did you try purely on-policy updates or controlling buffer staleness, and with what effect?
- Measurements vs. raw reward: Can you quantify how much of the performance gain comes from using low-dimensional measurements as targets (versus a scalar reward) in otherwise similar architectures?
- Availability of measurements: In what settings might such measurements be unavailable or noisy, and how would the approach degrade or adapt (e.g., with learned measurement proxies)?
- Goal specification: The goal vector is provided, not learned. How is it set in practice at test time across your scenarios, and how sensitive is behavior to misspecified goals?
