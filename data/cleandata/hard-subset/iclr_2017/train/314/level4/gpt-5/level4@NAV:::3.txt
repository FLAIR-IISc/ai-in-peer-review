The paper introduces an on-policy approach for forecasting future intrinsic measurements. All experiments are conducted in Doom (specifically vizDoom). Rather than predicting only win/loss or the number of frags (score), the authors train a model to predict a sequence of triplets (health, ammunition, frags), each weighted by a corresponding sequence of input “goal” triplets. Modifying the goal weights provides a mechanism to guide exploration. At test time, the agent can act by maximizing the long-term goal alone.

The results are strong: this model won the 2016 vizDoom competition. The experimental section appears solid:
- There are comparisons of DFP against A3C, DQN, and an attempted comparison with DSR (a recent related method by Kulkarni et al., 2016). DFP outperforms the other methods (or matches them when they saturate/plateau, as with A3C on scenario D1).
- An ablation study supports the claim that each component of the model’s added complexity contributes meaningfully.

Predicting intrinsic motivation (Singh et al., 2004), auxiliary variables, and forward modeling are well-established themes in reinforcement learning. The version I reviewed (December 4 revision) suitably cites prior work, even if it is not fully comprehensive.

A few minor form-related comments:
- Doom is described as a 3D environment, but it is effectively a 2D environment (height is not a discriminative/actionable dimension) rendered in pseudo-3D.
- Using “P” in (2) (and subsequently) to denote prediction rather than probability may be confusing.
- The reuse of “j” (albeit in different fonts) in (6) can be misleading.
- Results tables could reiterate the measurement units, especially given the heterogeneous metrics in Table 1.

Overall, I consider this a clear accept. One might contend that additional environments should be tested or that the novelty is limited, but I believe papers that are correct (no-nonsense, empirically solid on Doom, with an appendix enabling reproducibility) and milestone (vizDoom winner) merit publication.
