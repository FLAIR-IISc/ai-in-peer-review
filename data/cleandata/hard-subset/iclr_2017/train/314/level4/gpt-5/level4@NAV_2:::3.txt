1. Summary of claims and contributions
- The paper proposes an (on-policy) approach that learns to predict future intrinsic measurements from raw sensory input in immersive environments. Concretely, rather than predicting a scalar reward or win/loss, the model forecasts sequences of measurement vectors (e.g., health, ammo, frags) and conditions these predictions on a goal vector provided as input. Varying the goal weights guides behavior during training, and at test time the agent acts by choosing the action that maximizes the long-term goal.
- The formulation turns sensorimotor control into supervised prediction of future measurements, enabling learning from interaction without external labels, and supports goal-parameterized control such that the agent can pursue new goals at test time.
- Extensive experiments in ViZDoom demonstrate strong performance: the method consistently outperforms or matches A3C and DQN (and reports an attempted comparison with DSR), shows meaningful generalization across environments and goals, and includes ablations indicating that the model’s “added complexity” is beneficial. A trained agent won the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition (Full Deathmatch), highlighting practical impact.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, clear empirical evidence: state-of-the-art performance on challenging Doom scenarios and a first-place finish in the Visual Doom competition.
- A simple, well-motivated formulation that leverages dense measurement streams to produce goal-conditional control, with solid ablations and comparisons.

3. Supporting arguments
- Comparative performance: Across four scenarios of increasing difficulty, the proposed direct future prediction (DFP) approach outperforms deep RL baselines or matches them when they saturate (e.g., parity with A3C in D1). In the more complex D3/D4 scenarios, DFP substantially exceeds A3C and DQN. The attempted DSR comparison is documented, acknowledging computational constraints.
- Ablations: The study supports key design choices. Predicting multiple measurements (versus a scalar like frags) and predicting across multiple temporal offsets both improve performance, even when evaluation focuses on a single metric, reinforcing the value of rich, temporally dense supervision.
- Generalization: Models trained with varied textures/environments generalize better to unseen mazes; higher-capacity variants further improve transfer. Goal-agnostic training (random goal vectors) yields models that nearly match task-specific training on the main objective and generalize better to novel goals.
- Methodological soundness: The architecture is kept close to DQN for fair comparison. The experimental setup, training/testing protocols, and reporting are careful and detailed; the December 4 revision appropriately situates the work in the literature on intrinsic signals, auxiliary prediction, and forward modeling, even if not fully exhaustive.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not decision-critical)
- Environment description: Doom is often characterized as “3D,” but gameplay is effectively 2.5D (height is not an actionable dimension). Clarifying this would avoid potential confusion.
- Notation:
  - Using “P” for prediction (instead of probability) in Eq. (2) and elsewhere may be misleading; consider an alternative symbol to prevent ambiguity.
  - The double use of “j” (albeit in different fonts) around Eq. (6) could confuse readers; renaming variables would help.
- Tables and units: Reiterate measurement units in the result tables (especially Table 1), since metrics are heterogeneous, to improve readability.
- Literature scope: The prior work discussion is adequate; a brief pointer to additional related strands (e.g., intrinsic motivation/auxiliary tasks/forward models like Singh et al., 2004) would further strengthen positioning.

Questions for the authors
- Goal conditioning:
  - How sensitive is test-time performance to the distribution over goal vectors used during training (e.g., [0,1] vs. [-1,1])? Are there failure modes when test goals lie outside the training distribution?
  - Did you try curriculum strategies over goal vectors to facilitate learning?
- Training dynamics:
  - How do the experience memory size and the update lag (k) affect stability and sample efficiency? Any observed trade-offs with more off-policy data?
  - How sensitive are results to the choice of temporal offsets and their weighting coefficients? Did alternative offset schedules (e.g., exponential) change outcomes?
- Architecture:
  - Can you quantify the contribution of the expectation/action-stream split beyond the ablation already shown (e.g., via additional controls without normalization)?
  - Did you explore lightweight memory (e.g., frame stacking or simple recurrence) to capture short-term dynamics, and if so, how did it interact with DFP?
- Scope and transfer:
  - How robust is performance to more severe domain shifts (lighting, enemy types, physics tweaks)? Any insights from failure cases?
  - Do you anticipate straightforward extensions to continuous action spaces, and what modifications to the action-stream would be required?
