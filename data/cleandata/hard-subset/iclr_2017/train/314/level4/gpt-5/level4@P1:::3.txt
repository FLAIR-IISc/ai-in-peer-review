Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a supervised-learning-based approach to sensorimotor control that predicts future intrinsic measurements conditioned on the current observation and a goal vector. Concretely, in ViZDoom the model predicts sequences of future triplets (health, ammunition, frags) at multiple temporal offsets, weighted by goal triplets provided as input. By changing goal weights during training, the authors can shape behavior and guide exploration; at test time, the agent acts by choosing the action that maximizes the long-term goal. The approach leverages dense, vector-valued feedback rather than a sparse scalar reward and supports dynamically changing goals at test time.

The experimental evaluation is extensive and convincing. The method (DFP) outperforms strong deep RL baselines (A3C, DQN) on increasingly challenging Doom scenarios and matches them when they saturate (e.g., A3C in D1). It shows compelling generalization across textures/environments and across goals, and an ablation study supports the claim that the added architectural and objective components matter (vectorial feedback and multi-horizon prediction). A model trained with this approach won the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition (Full Deathmatch), beating a strong LSTM DQN with extra supervision.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
1) Strong, reproducible empirical results that significantly outperform standard deep RL baselines on challenging tasks, culminating in a competition win.
2) A simple, well-motivated formulation that reframes control as supervised prediction over rich measurement streams, enabling goal-agnostic training and dynamic goals at test time.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Learning to act from raw sensory input in complex 3D-style environments and to flexibly pursue multiple goals is central to scalable RL. Using dense, multivariate measurements as supervision is a natural fit.
- Motivation and placement: The paper situates the approach relative to supervised vs TD learning, Monte Carlo methods, multi-objective RL, UVFAs, and predictive representations. The December 4 revision references relevant work on intrinsic motivation, auxiliary prediction, and forward modeling; while not exhaustive, it is adequate.
- Method clarity: The architecture cleanly separates perception, measurements, and goals, and uses an expectation/action-stream decomposition to sharpen action-conditioned differences. Training uses straightforward regression on experience gathered in the environment.
- Empirical support: Across four scenarios, DFP equals or surpasses A3C/DQN, with the largest margins in the more complex, multi-measurement settings (D3/D4). Generalization experiments (texture randomization) and goal-agnostic training demonstrate robustness and flexibility. The ablation study shows that vectorial feedback and multi-offset prediction both contribute substantially.
- Reproducibility: The paper provides implementation details and baselines, and the experimental setup appears careful and fair.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful; not central to my decision)
- Environment description: Doom is often described as 3D but is effectively a 2D environment rendered in pseudo-3D (height is not an actionable dimension). A brief clarification would avoid confusion.
- Notation:
  - Using P in Eq. (2) to denote “prediction” may be misread as a probability. Consider a different symbol (e.g., F or ŷ).
  - The double use of j (even with different fonts) in Eq. (6) can be confusing; renaming one instance would improve readability.
- Units in tables: Because measurements are heterogeneous (e.g., health, ammo, frags), repeating units/ranges in result tables (especially Table 1) would aid interpretation.
- Related work breadth: A short paragraph acknowledging adjacent lines on intrinsic motivation/auxiliary losses and forward modeling (beyond those already cited) would strengthen the positioning.
- Limitations: A brief discussion that the approach is reactive (no memory), tested in a single game, and designed for discrete actions would set expectations and motivate future work.

Questions for the authors
- Policy/training regime: The text describes the method as interacting ε-greedily and maintaining a small experience memory that “departs from pure on-policy training.” Could you quantify the impact of memory size M and update frequency k on stability and performance? How sensitive is DFP to these choices?
- Goal sampling: For goal-agnostic training, how sensitive are results to the distribution over goal vectors (e.g., [0,1] vs [-1,1])? Did you try curricula or structured goal sets, and do they improve generalization to unseen goals?
- Temporal offsets and weights: You predict at offsets {1,2,4,8,16,32} and weight the last three with coefficients (0.5, 0.5, 1). How robust is performance to these choices? Did you test other offset schedules or weighting schemes?
- Architecture details: What is the empirical contribution of the action-stream normalization (Eq. 6) beyond dueling-style separation? Any ablation isolating this component?
- Variance and seeds: How many independent runs are reported per condition, and what are the standard deviations for the final performance metrics? Reporting variance would help assess stability.
- Observations and memory: Are inputs single frames or stacked frames? Given the purely reactive design, do you anticipate gains from adding recurrent memory, especially in D3/D4?
- Competition setup: For the Full Deathmatch track, could you provide training compute, total steps, and any domain randomization used? This would help contextualize the win relative to other entrants.
- Environment realism: Since verticality is not actionable in Doom, do you expect similar gains in environments where height and physics are fully 3D and semantically important?

Overall, this is a clear, well-executed contribution that demonstrates a simple and effective alternative to standard deep RL formulations, with strong empirical validation. I recommend acceptance.
