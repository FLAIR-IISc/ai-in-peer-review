Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a goal-parameterized sensorimotor control framework that reframes action selection as supervised prediction of future intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) conditioned on the current sensory input, the candidate action, and a goal vector. Actions are selected by maximizing the predicted goal-weighted measurement changes.
- The model uses dense, vector-valued feedback rather than sparse scalar rewards, supports training without a fixed goal, and can pursue dynamically specified goals at test time.
- The architecture combines perception, measurement, and goal encoders with a prediction head split into expectation and action streams (dueling-style), predicting multiple future horizons.
- Extensive experiments in ViZDoom show strong gains over DQN and A3C, especially on harder combat scenarios; ablations highlight the benefits of multivariate, multi-horizon prediction; the method generalizes across textures and to new goals; and it won the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch track without extra supervision.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons
1) Strong empirical evidence that DFP outperforms competitive deep RL baselines on challenging 3D visuomotor tasks, with clear generalization to unseen environments and goals. 2) A simple, well-motivated formulation—predicting future measurements conditioned on goals—that is easy to implement, efficient to train, and practically impactful.

Supporting arguments
- Problem/approach fit: The paper identifies a setting where dense, intrinsic measurements are available and exploits them to replace sparse reward learning with supervised multi-horizon prediction. This is well motivated relative to UVFA, predictive representations, and Monte Carlo-style learning, and is appropriate for immersive environments with rich feedback.
- Empirical strength: Across four ViZDoom scenarios, DFP substantially exceeds A3C/DQN on the more complex tasks. The ablation study convincingly shows that (i) predicting multiple measurements is better than a single scalar target even when the evaluation metric is scalar, and (ii) multi-horizon targets help. Generalization results across textures and goals are solid, and the competition win provides external validation.
- Position in the literature: The framework is closely related to UVFA, GVFs, and Monte Carlo prediction, but the paper advances the field by demonstrating a practical, scalable instantiation from pixels with goal conditioning that achieves state-of-the-art performance in 3D environments. The architectural split into expectation/action streams and training with randomized goals contribute to effectiveness.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not central to the decision)
- Clarify novelty and scope: More explicitly contrast DFP with UVFA, successor representations (DSR), and GVFs. Highlight what is new (action-conditional, goal-parameterized, multi-horizon prediction of intrinsic measurement deltas; simple supervised objective; dueling-style head) versus reused.
- Failure modes and limitations: Discuss when DFP may struggle—e.g., when the measurement stream is sparse, weakly informative, or poorly aligned with goals; under severe partial observability; or with long-horizon, delayed consequences that exceed the predicted offsets. Include negative or borderline cases.
- Catastrophic forgetting and goal coverage: Because the policy follows the current goal distribution, analyze forgetting when the goal distribution shifts. Consider curricula over goals, replay that maintains goal diversity, or relabeling experiences with alternative goals to improve coverage.
- Broader evaluation: Add results on another domain (e.g., Atari) to test portability; use RAM or engineered indicators as measurements, or evaluate when only score is available. A recurrent variant (or frame stacking) would illuminate performance under partial observability.
- Sensitivity and ablations: Report sensitivity to (i) choice/number of measurements; (ii) horizons and their weights; (iii) ε schedule and replay size; and (iv) the expectation/action split versus a single head. Explore learning the horizon weights or discount as parameters.
- Baselines: Include a UVFA-style baseline trained on scalar reward with identical perception and compute; compare to A3C/DQN augmented with auxiliary prediction tasks. Ensure compute/training budgets are matched and report variance with confidence intervals.
- Practicality: Discuss how to select or construct measurements when only sparse rewards are available. Comment on extensions to continuous actions.
- Presentation: Fix citation formatting and minor typos; define “frags” on first use; release code and models if possible.

Questions for the authors
1) Measurement design: How sensitive is performance to which measurements are predicted and to their noise/scale? If only a sparse reward is available, can proxy measurements be learned or engineered, and how does DFP fare then?
2) Horizons: Why the specific offsets and weights (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32 with last three weighted)? Have you tried learning the weights or using a parametric discount? What is the effect of longer horizons?
3) Goal generalization: How far does the model extrapolate in goal space beyond the training distribution? Any observed pathologies for conflicting goals or extreme weights?
4) Replay and policy: You use a small experience memory and ε-greedy behavior. How sensitive are results to memory size and staleness? Would prioritized or goal-balanced replay help stability and prevent forgetting?
5) Architecture: What is the measured gain from the expectation/action split versus a single multilayer head when all else is equal? Any interactions with the number of actions?
6) Partial observability: Did you try frame stacking or recurrence (e.g., LSTM)? How does memory affect performance and goal generalization?
7) Robustness: How does DFP handle measurement drift or missing measurements at test time? Can the model adapt online to measurement definition changes?
8) Competition details: Please provide training details for the winning entry (data budget, randomization, hyperparameters) and clarify any differences from the paper’s main setup.

Overall, this is a timely, well-executed paper whose empirical findings and simple formulation should be valuable to the community.
