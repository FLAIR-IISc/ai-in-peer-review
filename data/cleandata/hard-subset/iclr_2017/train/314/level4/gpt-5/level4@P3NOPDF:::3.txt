Summary
The paper proposes an on-policy method that acts by predicting sequences of future intrinsic measurements rather than directly optimizing reward. In vizDoom, the agent predicts future tuples of (health, ammunition, frags) and conditions these predictions on a goal vector that specifies the relative importance of each measurement. Altering the goal weights provides a mechanism for guided exploration during training, while at test time the agent simply selects actions that maximize the long-term goal. This direct future prediction framework yields strong empirical performance and won the 2016 vizDoom competition.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results. The method outperforms DQN and A3C across several scenarios and matches A3C when the latter saturates (e.g., scenario D1). The attempted comparison to DSR (Kulkarni et al., 2016) is appreciated given its conceptual proximity.
- Careful experimental design. The study includes ablations showing that the proposed components materially contribute to performance, supporting the claim that the “added complexity” is justified.
- Clear problem formulation. Predicting task-relevant measurements conditioned on goals is a sensible alternative to value estimation in high-dimensional, partially observable environments like vizDoom.
- Adequate positioning. The December 4th revision appropriately situates the work relative to intrinsic motivation (e.g., Singh et al., 2004), auxiliary tasks, and forward modeling, even if the discussion is not exhaustive.
- Reproducibility and impact. The appendix provides implementation details, and the method’s competitive success (vizDoom winner) underscores its practical significance.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Scope of evaluation. All experiments are in a single domain (vizDoom). While this setting is challenging and diverse, broader validation would strengthen claims about generality.
- Perceived novelty. Elements of the approach connect to established ideas in auxiliary prediction and goal-conditioning. Some readers may view the contribution as an effective integration rather than a fundamentally new concept. Given the strong empirical results and clarity of the approach, this seems acceptable.

Presentation and minor comments
- Environment description. Doom is referred to as a 3D environment; more precisely, it is a 2D environment rendered to appear 3D, with height not being an actionable dimension.
- Notation. Using P in Equation (2) to denote prediction can be misleading because P commonly denotes a probability distribution. Consider using a different symbol (e.g., F or G) or explicitly clarifying at first use.
- Indexing. The double use of j in Equation (6), even with different fonts, is potentially confusing; renaming one index would improve readability.
- Tables. Reiterate the units for all reported measurements in the results tables, especially where metrics are heterogeneous (e.g., Table 1), to reduce ambiguity.

Overall assessment
This is a clear accept. The methodology is simple, coherent, and well-executed; the empirical evaluation is rigorous within the chosen domain; and the demonstrated impact (competition win) is compelling. While one could request experiments in additional environments or debate the degree of novelty, the paper strikes an excellent balance of correctness, clarity, and practical relevance. I recommend acceptance.
