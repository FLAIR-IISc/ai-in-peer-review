Summary
This paper proposes a supervised-learning formulation for sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments, termed direct future prediction (DFP). Instead of learning values via temporal-difference methods, the agent predicts future changes in a low-dimensional vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) conditioned on the current observation, candidate action, and a goal vector. At test time, it selects the action that maximizes the inner product between the goal and the predicted future measurements. This decouples learning from a fixed reward, enables goal-parameterized control, and leverages temporally dense supervision from measurement streams. The approach uses a simple deep architecture with separate streams for expected future measurements and action-specific deviations. Extensive experiments in ViZDoom show strong performance against DQN, A3C, and DSR, especially on difficult tasks; the method generalizes across environments and goals; ablations highlight the benefit of vectorial feedback and multi-horizon prediction; and a variant won the Visual Doom AI Competition’s Full Deathmatch track.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) A clear, well-motivated alternative to TD-based RL that reframes control as supervised prediction on dense, intrinsic measurements with goal conditioning; (2) compelling empirical results, including state-of-the-art performance and generalization across environments and goals, supported by careful ablations and a competitive win.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses learning to act from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments and adapting to changing goals. The choice to exploit dense, multidimensional measurement streams is well motivated and grounded in prior work on Monte Carlo methods, predictive representations, multi-objective RL, and UVFAs.
- Approach: The method is simple and principled: learn a function F(o, a, g) that predicts future measurement differences over multiple time offsets; act by maximizing g^T F. Training uses regression targets from experience without reliance on TD targets or extrinsic supervision.
- Empirical support: Across four ViZDoom scenarios, DFP matches or exceeds strong baselines, with especially large gains in more complex combat tasks. The architecture control (kept close to DQN) supports a fair comparison. Generalization experiments (texture randomization, cross-environment testing) and goal-agnostic training demonstrate robustness and adaptability. Ablations show clear benefits from (i) vector-valued measurements over scalar rewards and (ii) multi-horizon prediction. Training throughput and competition results further strengthen the claims.
- Clarity and presentation: The paper is well written, situates the contribution in the literature, and provides sufficient implementation detail to assess the approach.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not central to the decision)
- On-policy vs. replay buffer: The regression targets assume trajectories from the current policy, yet training draws from a finite replay memory. Please discuss potential bias from this policy mismatch, why it appears negligible here, and any observed sensitivity to buffer size or update lag.
- Reliance on measurement streams: The method uses additional metadata (game-provided measurements) that many RL baselines do not exploit. Please clarify the extent to which this constitutes an advantage, its availability in realistic settings, and how performance degrades when fewer or noisier measurements are available.
- Goal vector design: Provide guidance on choosing/normalizing goal vectors, handling conflicting objectives, and mitigating sensitivity to scale (e.g., health vs. ammo units). Report whether goal normalization or clipping is used at train/test time.
- Temporal offsets and weighting: Elaborate on the choice of time offsets and loss weights, and include sensitivity analyses.
- Architecture ablations: Since the predictor uses expectation/action streams (dueling-style), include an ablation isolating their contribution. Report calibration/error metrics for the predicted measurements.
- Limitations and scope: Emphasize the memoryless, discrete-action setting and discuss planned extensions to recurrent architectures and continuous control. Include more details on statistical variability (error bars) and seeding.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive is performance to the replay memory size, update frequency, and ε-greedy schedule? Did you observe signs of off-policy bias during training?
2. How were goal vectors sampled in goal-agnostic training, and how crucial is goal-conditioning during training for test-time generalization to new goals?
3. What criteria guided the selection of measurements to predict? How does performance change if some measurements are excluded or corrupted with noise?
4. How sensitive is the method to the choice and scaling of temporal offsets and their loss weights? Would a learned weighting across horizons help?
5. Can you provide an ablation without the expectation/action split to quantify its effect? Does the action-normalization layer materially improve learning stability?
6. In scenarios with only sparse scalar rewards and no auxiliary measurements, how would you adapt the approach? Are there proxy measurements you found effective?
7. For continuous actions, do you envision sampling actions to evaluate F or learning a differentiable policy that maximizes g^T F(o, a, g) w.r.t. a?
8. Did you assess calibration of the predicted measurements (e.g., ECE or reliability plots)? Could direct learning of u(f; g) (bypassing f) degrade generalization to new goals?
9. Beyond texture variation, have you tested generalization to new enemy types, dynamics, or weapon sets? What failures did you observe?
