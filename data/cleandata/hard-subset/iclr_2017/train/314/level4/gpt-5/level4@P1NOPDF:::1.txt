This empirically driven paper builds on recent successes in deep reinforcement learning with neural network function approximators. It proposes a new algorithm that operates on raw sensory input and achieves superior performance in novel 3D environments, while generalizing across goals and environments. Notably, it won the Visual Doom AI competition.

The central idea is to use additional low-dimensional observations (e.g., ammo or health provided by the game engine) as supervised prediction targets. Crucially, the prediction is conditioned on a goal vector (provided, not learned) and the current action. After training, the agent selects the action that maximizes the predicted outcome under the current goal. In contrast to successor feature representations, learning is fully supervised with Monte Carlo targets; there is no temporal-difference coupling between predictions at the current and next states.

The authors review related work on future prediction in RL and goal-conditioned function approximators (Section 2). The key contributions are: (i) a focus on Monte Carlo estimation rather than TD; (ii) predicting low-dimensional “measurements”; (iii) parameterized goals; and (iv) perhaps most importantly, a thorough empirical comparison to relevant baselines.

Beyond the Visual Doom AI results, the paper shows that the learned policies generalize to modest test-time goal changes without further training.

The paper is clearly written, and the empirical results are compelling and likely to be of significant interest.

Minor suggestions for improvement:
- Training uses supervised regression from a replay buffer while implicitly assuming on-policy trajectories. In particular, the Monte Carlo targets reflect returns from episodes generated by earlier versions of the policy, whereas the learning objective assumes behavior under the current policy. This off-policy Monte Carlo approximation should be discussed, including any potential bias or practical impact.
- The method leverages additional metadata—low-dimensional measurements that indicate which aspects of the sensory stream are worth predicting—that the compared algorithms do not use. The paper should more explicitly acknowledge this advantage and clarify limitations in settings where such measurements are not available or are difficult to define.
