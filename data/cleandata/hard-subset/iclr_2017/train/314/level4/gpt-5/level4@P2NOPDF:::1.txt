This paper builds on recent successes in deep reinforcement learning for high-dimensional state spaces by proposing an empirically driven algorithm that achieves strong performance from raw sensory input in novel 3D environments and generalizes across goals and environments. Notably, it won the Visual Doom AI competition.

The central idea is to predict future values of low-dimensional measurements (e.g., health, ammo) provided by the environment and to use these predictions for action selection. The predictions are conditioned on both the current action and a goal vector that is supplied rather than learned. After training, the agent selects the action that maximizes the predicted outcome under the current goal. Unlike successor features, the learning is purely supervised via Monte Carlo regression: there is no temporal-difference coupling between predictions for the current and next states.

The paper situates the work within prior research on predictive modeling in RL and goal-conditioned function approximators (reviewed in Section 2). Its key contributions are: (1) a focus on Monte Carlo estimation rather than TD learning; (2) the use of low-dimensional measurements as prediction targets; (3) parameterized goals that enable flexible behavior at test time; and (4) a thorough empirical comparison against relevant baselines. Beyond the competition results, the authors demonstrate that the learned policies can adapt at test time to limited goal changes without additional training, indicating meaningful generalization.

The paper is clearly written, the methodology is well motivated, and the empirical results are compelling. I expect the work to be of significant interest to the community.

Minor suggestions for improvement:
- Off-policy training with Monte Carlo targets: The supervised learning objective implicitly assumes on-policy rollouts, yet the algorithm trains from a replay buffer populated by trajectories generated under earlier policies. In effect, the Monte Carlo targets approximate expectations under the current policy using off-policy data. This approximation and its potential impact on bias or sample efficiency should be discussed, along with any mitigation (e.g., buffer refresh strategies or policy update schedules).
- Use of additional metadata: The method leverages environment-provided measurements that indicate which aspects of the state are important to predict. Baseline methods may not have access to equivalent side information, giving the proposed approach an advantage. The paper should more explicitly acknowledge this difference and discuss limitations in settings where such measurements are unavailable or expensive to extract, as well as potential workarounds (e.g., learning proxy measurements from pixels).
