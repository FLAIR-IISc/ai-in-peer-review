- Proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): predict multi-horizon future measurement vectors conditioned on observation, candidate action, and goal, replacing sparse reward bootstrapping with supervised regression.

- Architecture/process: simple, well-engineered network that processes pixels and measurements, separates expectation and action-specific components, trained via regression on ε-greedy experience.

- Empirical results: strong ViZDoom performance across four scenarios, matches or exceeds DQN/A3C, large gains in complex combat, generalizes to unseen textures/goals, and won the Visual Doom AI Competition.

- Decision: Accept — clear, well-motivated formulation with strong empirical evidence and a competition win.

- Problem significance: Tackles timely, challenging problem of control from raw pixels with dynamic goals, improving sample efficiency and flexibility via dense supervision.

- Positioning vs prior work: Distinct from UVFA/successor representations by predicting measurement trajectories (not scalar values), conditioning on goals throughout, and avoiding TD bootstrapping.

- Evaluation rigor: Uses established baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) with tuning, multiple seeds, training curves, ablations, and goal-agnostic training experiments.

- Limitations: Evaluation limited to Doom, model is reactive (no memory), and theoretical support is limited to intuition and empirical validation.

- Improve clarity: Sharpen and explicitly contrast DFP with UVFA and successor representations early in the paper.

- Report failure modes: Describe where DFP struggles (long-horizon delayed effects, heavy partial observability, rapidly changing goals).

- Generalization analysis: Quantify performance on test-time goals outside the training distribution or strongly antagonistic objectives.

- Measurement design: Discuss how to choose, normalize, and handle spurious/redundant or distractor measurements.

- Prediction calibration: Report prediction error metrics (e.g., MAE) and correlate prediction quality with control performance.

- Sensitivity studies: Vary temporal offsets and loss weights; test different ε schedules and experience memory sizes to assess robustness.

- Broader evaluation: Add experiments in other domains (e.g., Atari, TORCS) to strengthen generality claims.

- Memory/longer horizons: Discuss integrating recurrence or frame stacking and trade-offs when extending prediction horizons.

- Question — measurement sensitivity: How sensitive is learning to choice, scaling, normalization, and noisy/irrelevant measurements?

- Question — temporal offsets: How were τ and loss weights chosen; would learned temporal bases or exponential decay work better?

- Question — off-policy robustness: What happens with larger replay buffers or more off-policy training; any instability?

- Question — goal distribution: How does the training goal distribution affect test-time generalization, especially for goals outside the convex hull?

- Question — prediction quality: What are calibration/MAE numbers for measurement predictions and their relation to return?

- Question — partial observability/memory: Were frame stacking or LSTMs tried and did they help?

- Question — horizon limits: How does performance change when extending or shortening the prediction window beyond 32 steps?

- Question — continuous actions: Is there a practical extension to continuous action spaces?

- Question — baseline parity: Clarify compute budgets and frame rates across methods and whether wall-clock or sample efficiency explains performance gaps.

- Overall assessment: A clear, elegant formulation with strong empirical results; with clearer contribution articulation and deeper analysis of limitations, this will be a valuable reference for goal-parameterized control from rich sensory input.
