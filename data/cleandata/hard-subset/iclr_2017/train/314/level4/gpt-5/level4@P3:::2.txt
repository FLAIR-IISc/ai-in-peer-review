1. Summary and contributions
This paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning formulation of sensorimotor control that predicts future changes in a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) conditioned on the current observation, a candidate action, and a goal vector. The key idea is to exploit the cotemporal structure of rich sensory streams and dense, multidimensional measurements as supervision, replacing sparse reward bootstrapping with direct multi-horizon prediction. This goal-parameterized predictor enables training without a fixed goal and supports dynamically specified goals at test time by selecting the action whose predicted measurements best align with the current goal.

The authors present a simple, well-engineered architecture that processes pixels and measurements, separates expectation and action-specific components, and trains via regression over experience collected with an ε-greedy policy. Extensive ViZDoom experiments across four scenarios show strong empirical performance: DFP matches or exceeds DQN and A3C, with especially large gains in more complex combat scenarios; generalizes to unseen textures and goals; benefits from vectorial feedback and multi-horizon prediction in ablations; and won the Visual Doom AI Competition’s Full Deathmatch track without extra supervision.

2. Decision
Accept. The paper offers:
- A clear, well-motivated formulation that reduces control with dynamic goals to supervised prediction using dense measurement feedback.
- Strong, carefully presented empirical evidence, including generalization to unseen environments/goals and an external competition win.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Learning control from raw pixels in 3D environments with dynamically specified goals is timely and challenging. The paper addresses both sample efficiency (via dense supervision) and flexibility (goal conditioning).
- Motivation and placement: The work is well situated with respect to UVFA, successor representations, Monte Carlo vs TD learning, multi-objective RL, and video prediction. The main distinctions are (a) predicting future measurement vectors directly (rather than scalar returns or value functions), (b) conditioning on goals during training and testing, and (c) avoiding TD bootstrapping in favor of Monte Carlo-style supervised regression. This positioning is appropriate and well referenced.
- Empirical support and rigor: The evaluation spans tasks of increasing difficulty, uses established baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) with tuning, and reports both training curves and final performance with multiple random seeds. Ablations isolate the importance of vector-valued feedback and multi-horizon targets. The goal-agnostic training study convincingly demonstrates adaptation to new test-time goals. The competition result further corroborates real-world robustness.
- Limitations: Evaluation is confined to a single domain family (Doom), the model is reactive (no memory), and theory is limited to intuition and empirical validation. While these do not undermine the main claims, they bound the scope.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify and sharpen contributions early. Explicitly contrast DFP with UVFA and successor representations: DFP predicts measurement trajectories, not values, and uses goal-conditioning throughout, with no TD targets.
- Provide failure modes. Where does DFP struggle (e.g., long-horizon delayed effects beyond the prediction window, heavy partial observability, rapidly changing goals inducing nonstationarity)?
- Analyze generalization limits. Quantify performance when test-time goals are outside the distribution seen during training (e.g., strongly antagonistic objective weights).
- Expand measurement design discussion. How to choose measurements, normalize them, and mitigate spurious or redundant signals? What happens if distractor measurements are added?
- Report calibration/accuracy. Include prediction error metrics and their correlation with control performance to substantiate the supervision quality.
- Sensitivity studies. Vary temporal offsets and their loss weights; test different ε schedules and experience memory sizes to assess on-/off-policy robustness.
- Broaden domains if feasible. Even a compact Atari or TORCS study would strengthen claims of generality and clarify when dense measurement streams are available/useful.
- Memory and longer horizons. Briefly discuss incorporating recurrence or stacking frames, and the trade-offs when extending the prediction horizon.

Questions for the authors
- Measurement sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to the choice, scaling, and normalization of measurements? Does adding irrelevant or noisy measurements degrade learning?
- Temporal offsets: How were τ and loss weights chosen? Would a learned temporal basis or exponentially decayed targets perform comparably or better?
- Off-policy robustness: You use a small experience memory. What happens with larger replay (more off-policy training)? Any instability or degradation?
- Goal distribution: In goal-agnostic training, how does the training goal distribution shape test-time generalization? Have you tested goals outside the convex hull of training goals?
- Prediction quality: What is the calibration/MAE of future measurement predictions, and how does prediction accuracy relate to return?
- Partial observability and memory: Did you try frame stacking or LSTMs? Do they help in scenarios with occlusions or longer-term dependencies?
- Horizon limits: Many effects in Doom occur beyond 32 steps. How does performance change as you extend or shorten the prediction window?
- Continuous actions: Do you foresee a practical extension to continuous action spaces (e.g., predicting measurements conditioned on continuous action parameters)?
- Baseline parity: Can you clarify compute budgets and frame rates across methods, and whether wall-clock or sample efficiency drives the observed gaps?

Overall assessment
A clear, elegant formulation with strong empirical results and useful insights about leveraging dense, vector-valued measurements and goal conditioning. With improved articulation of contributions and analysis of limitations/failure modes, this work will be a valuable reference for goal-parameterized control from rich sensory input.
