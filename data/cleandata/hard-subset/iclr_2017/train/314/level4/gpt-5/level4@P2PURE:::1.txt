Deep RL (deep neural networks as function approximators in RL) has achieved a number of successes in large state spaces. This empirically driven paper builds on those advances. It introduces a new algorithm that performs better in novel 3D environments from raw sensory inputs and generalizes more effectively across goals and environments. Notably, this algorithm won the Visual Doom AI competition.

The key idea is to predict additional low-dimensional observations (e.g., ammo or health provided by the game engine) as supervised targets. Crucially, each prediction is conditioned on a goal vector (which is provided, not learned) and the current action. After training, the optimal action for the current state is chosen as the one that maximizes the predicted outcome under the given goal. Unlike successor feature representations, learning here is fully supervised, and there is no TD bootstrapping linking predictions of the current and next states.

Section 2 reviews relevant prior work on future-state prediction within RL and goal-driven function approximators. The main contributions are the emphasis on Monte Carlo estimation (rather than TD), the use of low-dimensional “measurements” as prediction targets, parameterized goals, and—perhaps most importantly—a thorough empirical comparison to strong baselines.

Beyond the Visual Doom AI comparison, the authors show that the algorithm learns policies that generalize and can adapt, without additional training, to limited changes in the goal.

The paper is clearly written, the empirical results are compelling, and the work will be of significant interest.

Some minor potential improvements:
- The supervised training introduces an approximation: it assumes on-policy data while learning from a replay buffer. With Monte Carlo regression, the expected remainder of the trajectory is assumed to follow the current policy, yet the samples come from episodes generated by earlier policies. This should be discussed.
- The algorithm leverages additional metadata (knowledge of which parts of the sensory input are worth predicting) that competing methods do not use. This, and the limitations of the approach—e.g., it may perform poorly when such measurements are unavailable—should be stated more explicitly.
