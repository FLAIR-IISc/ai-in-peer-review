Summary
This paper presents a deep reinforcement learning approach that leverages auxiliary, low-dimensional measurements (e.g., health, ammo) provided by the environment to predict future outcomes conditioned on both the current action and a goal vector. The method trains these predictions using Monte Carlo returns in a purely supervised fashion, without temporal-difference (TD) bootstrapping. At decision time, the agent selects the action that maximizes the predicted outcome under the current goal. The approach demonstrates strong performance in novel 3D, first-person environments from raw sensory input and exhibits generalization across goals and levels. Notably, the method won the Visual Doom AI competition.

Contributions and novelty
- A goal-parameterized predictive model trained via supervised Monte Carlo regression rather than TD learning.
- Exploitation of low-dimensional environment-provided measurements as prediction targets, decoupled from policy learning signals.
- A simple action-selection mechanism that uses the predictive model to optimize for arbitrary goal vectors at test time.
- Empirical comparisons to relevant deep RL baselines and demonstrations of limited zero-shot adaptation to new goals.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself well among model-free deep RL methods and goal-driven/value decomposition approaches, including work on learning to predict future measurements and successor features. A key difference from successor-feature methods is the absence of TD relationships between current and next-state predictions; learning is supervised from Monte Carlo returns, which simplifies training and avoids bootstrapping bias at the cost of potentially higher variance. The focus on low-dimensional measurements as prediction targets, rather than full-state prediction or purely reward-based learning, is a pragmatic and effective design choice in the studied domains.

Empirical evaluation
The experiments on 3D first-person tasks from raw pixels are compelling. The method outperforms strong baselines in Visual Doom-style environments and shows the ability to adjust behavior when test-time goals change, without further training. The inclusion of relevant baselines and an application with external validation (competition results) strengthens the empirical case.

Strengths
- Clear and well-communicated method with a simple training objective and decision rule.
- Strong empirical performance in challenging 3D environments from raw visual input.
- Demonstrated generalization to modified goal vectors at test time.
- Careful comparison to prior work and positioning within the literature.

Concerns and suggestions
- On-policy assumption vs. replay buffer: The supervised Monte Carlo regression implicitly assumes that the remainder of each trajectory reflects the current policy. However, training uses a replay buffer populated by earlier policy versions. This off-policy mismatch can introduce bias. The paper should explicitly discuss this approximation, its practical impact, and any mitigation (e.g., prioritization, policy versioning, or importance weighting), as well as whether performance degrades as the policy evolves.
- Reliance on privileged measurements: The approach uses additional metadata (low-dimensional measurements) provided by the environment that competing baselines may not access. This can confer a substantial advantage and may limit applicability in settings where such measurements are unavailable or expensive to define. The paper should (i) clearly articulate this assumption, (ii) justify its fairness in comparisons, and (iii) discuss limitations and potential workarounds (e.g., learning to infer measurements from observations, robustness to noisy or partial measurements).
- Goal specification: The goal vector is externally provided rather than learned. While this enables goal-conditioning, it also offloads part of the task design to the experimenter. A brief discussion of how goals might be acquired or adapted (e.g., via meta-learning, preference learning, or unsupervised discovery) would strengthen the broader impact.
- Scope of generalization: The reported zero-shot generalization to new goals is encouraging but appears limited. Additional analysis quantifying how far goals can deviate from those seen during training, and how performance scales with the dimensionality and diversity of measurements, would clarify the method’s robustness.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the particular choice and number of measurements? Have you evaluated ablations that remove or corrupt specific measurements?
- Do you observe measurable bias from off-policy Monte Carlo training via the replay buffer, and if so, how do you mitigate it?
- Can the approach be extended to settings without explicit measurements by learning a latent measurement space jointly with the predictor?
- How does sample efficiency compare to TD-based methods in these environments?

Overall assessment
This is a strong, empirically grounded paper that proposes a simple and effective predictive control approach for deep RL in high-dimensional, first-person settings. The method’s goal-parameterization and supervised Monte Carlo training are well-motivated and yield clear practical benefits, including some generalization to novel goals. The main caveats concern the reliance on privileged measurements and the off-policy nature of the replay-based training, both of which should be discussed more explicitly. Subject to clarifying these points, I recommend acceptance.
