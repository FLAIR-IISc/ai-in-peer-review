1. Summary of claims and contributions (positive)
The paper proposes an on-policy deep reinforcement learning approach augmented with auxiliary intrinsic variables (measurements) to drive sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments. Conceptually, it can be viewed as a concrete instantiation of universal value function ideas, with goals parameterized over future measurements. The main contribution is a careful distillation and integration of existing concepts into a practical system that learns from raw sensory streams and shows strong performance on challenging Doom-based tasks, suggesting clear empirical advantages of the proposed formulation.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (a) compelling empirical gains on difficult 3D control tasks, and (b) a well-executed synthesis of known ideas into a coherent framework that appears broadly useful.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses learning sensorimotor control from high-dimensional observations with dynamically changing goals, a setting where dense intrinsic measurements provide a natural supervisory signal. Positioning the method as a UVFA-style approach with goal-parameterized predictions is appropriate and well motivated.
- Relation to prior work: The connections to universal value function approximators and related literature are properly acknowledged. The contribution is less about introducing a wholly novel theoretical construct and more about consolidating and operationalizing known ideas for complex 3D navigation and combat.
- Empirical support: The method exhibits clear performance improvements over strong baselines in the presented Doom scenarios. Given the paper’s emphasis on integrating ideas to yield practical benefits, these results substantiate the core claims.
- Scope and limitations: The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of contributions and potential failure modes. It remains uncertain how robust the approach is to distributional shifts in goals over time, and on-policy training raises concerns about catastrophic forgetting of infrequently revisited goals.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not necessarily part of the decision)
- Clarify contributions: Make the claimed contributions more explicit in the abstract and introduction (e.g., what is methodologically new versus adapted, what aspects most drive the gains).
- Failure modes and robustness: Discuss where the approach struggles. Under what conditions does goal generalization degrade (e.g., conflicting measurement trade-offs, long-horizon dependencies, sparse or delayed effects on measurements)?
- On-policy stability: Provide analysis or experiments on catastrophic forgetting across goals. For instance, does performance on older goals deteriorate without continual rehearsal, and can simple mechanisms (e.g., goal-balanced sampling) mitigate this?
- Broader domains: Since the central claim is an effective integration yielding empirical advantages, consider demonstrating applicability beyond Doom (e.g., Atari), perhaps by using accessible intrinsic variables (e.g., ROM-exposed statistics) to test generality.

Questions for the authors
- Goal generalization: In practice, when do changing or out-of-distribution goals cause failures? Are there particular measurement combinations or horizon offsets that are especially brittle?
- Forgetting: Did you observe catastrophic forgetting of earlier goals during on-policy training? If so, what mitigation strategies (e.g., replay with goal balancing, periodic rehearsal of past goals) proved effective?
- Goal distribution: How are goals sampled during training, and how sensitive are results to this distribution? Would curriculum strategies over goal weights improve stability or final performance?
- Temporal offsets: How does performance vary with different sets or weightings of temporal offsets for future measurements? Are there trade-offs between short-horizon stability and long-horizon utility?
- Beyond Doom: Have you attempted to apply the approach to other domains (e.g., Atari) using proxy intrinsic variables? If not, what obstacles do you anticipate, and how might they be addressed?
