- Proposes a goal-conditioned, largely on-policy method that predicts future intrinsic measurements from pixels and a goal vector, selecting actions to maximize expected goal attainment.
- Frames control as supervised prediction of measurements rather than reward-driven value estimation.
- Supports training without fixed goals and pursuing dynamically changing goals at test time using dense, multidimensional measurement supervision.
- Argues vectorial, dense measurements stabilize and accelerate learning compared to scalar rewards or TD methods.
- Architecture combines perception, measurement, and goal modules, with prediction head split into expectation and action streams to capture action-specific differences.
- Uses multi-horizon prediction targets to improve performance.
- Extensive ViZDoom experiments show strong empirical gains, outperforming DQN, A3C, and DSR on hard 3D tasks.
- Demonstrates generalization across textures, environments, and goals, and won first place in the Visual Doom AI Competition.
- Ablations highlight benefits of vectorial measurements and multiple temporal offsets.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason 1: Clear and consistent empirical gains on challenging 3D visuomotor tasks with informative ablations and generalization tests.
- Key reason 2: Simple, well-motivated formulation that unifies existing ideas (e.g., UVFA, Monte Carlo prediction) into a practical, high-performing method.
- Problem statement: Tackles visuomotor control from raw pixels in complex 3D environments with dynamic, parameterized goals.
- Motivation/placement: Positioned as a supervised, goal-conditioned alternative to reward-centric RL; cites appropriate prior work but could clarify technical novelty vs synthesis.
- Evidence/rigor: Thorough Doom experiments and baselines support claims, though evaluation is limited to a single domain (ViZDoom).
- Clarify contributions: Authors should state explicit novelties—goal-conditioned future prediction with vectorial measurements, head split, and multi-horizon targets—versus prior UVFA/Monte Carlo ideas.
- Failure modes: Request systematic analysis of conditions where method fails (out-of-support goals, antagonistic measurements, sparse/uninformative streams).
- On-policy training & forgetting: Ask for investigation into catastrophic forgetting, sensitivity to goal distribution, and need for goal revisiting.
- Broader domains: Recommend additional benchmarks (e.g., Atari using ROM/RAM) to establish generality.
- Comparison to UVFA: Request direct empirical UVFA baseline with similar capacity to isolate benefits of vectorial future measurement prediction.
- Measurement design: Ask for guidance and experiments on choosing measurements, offsets, and effects of noisy/irrelevant measurements.
- Sample efficiency & stability: Recommend reporting learning curves in wall-clock time/steps and analysis of replay buffer size, ε-greedy settings, and sample efficiency relative to TD methods.
- Question 1: How does training-time goal distribution relate to test-time generalization and failure patterns?
- Question 2: Is catastrophic forgetting quantified when returning to previously seen goals, and can interleaving or stratified replay mitigate it?
- Question 3: How sensitive are results to experience memory size/sampling, and could larger replay or goal relabeling help?
- Question 4: Can the authors provide a UVFA-style baseline (same backbone and capacity) to isolate their method's advantage?
- Question 5: Any preliminary results or challenges applying the method to Atari or other simulators using ROM/RAM measurements?
- Question 6: How robust is the method to noisy or partially informative measurements, and have distractor measurements been tested?
- Question 7: How sensitive is performance to the chosen temporal offsets and weighting of multi-horizon targets, and have learned weights been tried?
