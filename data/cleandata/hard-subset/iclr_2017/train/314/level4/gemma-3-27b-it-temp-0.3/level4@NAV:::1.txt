Deep Reinforcement Learning, leveraging deep neural networks as function approximators, has demonstrated significant progress in tackling Reinforcement Learning problems with large state spaces. This work presents an empirically grounded algorithm that extends these approaches, achieving improved performance in unseen 3D environments using raw sensory input and exhibiting strong generalization capabilities across diverse goals and environments. The algorithm’s success is underscored by its winning performance in the Visual Doom AI competition.

The core innovation lies in utilizing readily available, low-dimensional observations – such as ammo count or health status provided by the game engine – as supervised targets for prediction. Critically, this prediction is conditioned on both a specified goal vector and the agent’s current action. Following training, the optimal action for a given state is selected as the action that maximizes the predicted outcome with respect to the desired goal.  Distinct from successor feature representations, this learning process is supervised and eschews the temporal difference (TD) relationship typically found between predictions for consecutive states.

The authors situate their work within the context of existing research on both state prediction within RL frameworks and goal-conditioned function approximation, providing a comprehensive review in section 2. The primary contributions of this paper are the emphasis on Monte Carlo estimation (in contrast to TD learning), the strategic use of low-dimensional ‘measurements’ for prediction, the incorporation of parameterized goals, and, crucially, a thorough empirical evaluation against relevant prior work.

Beyond the demonstrated success in Visual Doom AI, the authors demonstrate the algorithm’s ability to learn policies that generalize effectively, adapting to limited goal variations without requiring further training. 

The paper is clearly written and the empirical findings are convincing, making it a highly relevant contribution to the field.

However, a few points could be further addressed:
The supervised training incorporates an on-policy assumption, yet leverages a replay buffer. The Monte Carlo regression relies on the expectation of the remaining trajectory aligning with the current policy, while the buffer contains episodes generated by earlier policy iterations. This potential discrepancy warrants discussion.
Furthermore, the algorithm benefits from access to additional metadata – information guiding the prediction of relevant sensory input features – which is not available to the comparison algorithms.  The implications of this, and the potential limitations of the approach when such measurements are absent (e.g., in sensory environments lacking explicit measurements), should be more explicitly acknowledged.
