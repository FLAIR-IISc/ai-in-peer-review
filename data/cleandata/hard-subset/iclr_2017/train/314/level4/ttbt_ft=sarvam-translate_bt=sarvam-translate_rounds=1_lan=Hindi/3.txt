This research paper presents an on-policy method for estimating future internal measures. All experiments were conducted in the game of Doom (i.e., exactly in Doom), and instead of simply estimating win/loss or the number of frags (score), the authors trained their model to estimate a triplet (sequence) of (health, ammunition, frags), which was weighted by the "target" triplets (sequence) provided as input. Changing the weight of the target triplet is a way to explore/guide. At the time of testing, one can only maximize the long-term goal.

The results are impressive, as this model won the 2016 Doom competition. The experimental section of the paper seems solid:

- A comparison of DFP with A3C, DQN, and an attempt to compare with DSR (a recent similar approach by Kulkarni et al., 2016). DFP performs better than (or equal to) other approaches when they reach a limit/optimum, as is the case for A3C in scenario D1.
- There is an ablation study that supports the thesis that all the "extra complexity" of the paper's model is useful.
- Internal motivation (Singh et al. 2004), estimating auxiliary variables and forward modeling are well-studied areas of reinforcement learning. The version I read (December 4th revision) adequately references previous work, even if it is not fully detailed.
- Some comments on the form (nitpicks):
 - Doom is described as a 3D environment, while it is actually a 2D environment (height is not a differentiating/actionable dimension) presented in (fake) 3D.
 - The use of "P" in (2) (and later) can be misleading because it stands for prediction but not probability (as is usually the case for P).
 - The double use of "J" in (6) (assuming, with different fonts) can be misleading.
 - The units of measurement in the results tables can be repeated (especially because they are odd in Table 1).
I think this paper is clearly acceptable. One could argue that the experiments could have been conducted in different environments or that the novelty is limited, but I think the "right" (no nonsense, experimentally solid on Doom, the appendix provides details for reproducibility) and "milestone" (Doom winner) research papers should be published.
