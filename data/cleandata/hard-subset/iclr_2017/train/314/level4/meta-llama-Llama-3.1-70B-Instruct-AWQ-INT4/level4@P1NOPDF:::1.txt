Deep reinforcement learning (RL), which leverages deep neural networks as function approximators in RL algorithms, has achieved numerous successes in solving RL problems in large state spaces. This empirically driven work builds upon these approaches, introducing a novel algorithm that performs better in new 3D environments from raw sensory data and allows for improved generalization across goals and environments. Notably, this algorithm was the winner of the Visual Doom AI competition.

The key idea behind their algorithm is to utilize additional low-dimensional observations (such as ammo or health, provided by the game engine) as a supervised target for prediction. Importantly, this prediction is conditioned on a goal vector (which is given, not learned) and the current action. Once trained, the optimal action for the current state can be chosen as the action that maximizes the predicted outcome according to the goal. Unlike successor feature representations, learning is supervised, and there is no temporal difference (TD) relationship between the predictions of the current state and the next state.

There have been several prior works on predicting future states as part of RL and goal-driven function approximators, which the authors review in Section 2. The key contributions of this work are the focus on Monte Carlo estimation (rather than TD), the use of low-dimensional 'measurements' for prediction, the parameterized goals, and, perhaps most importantly, the empirical comparison to relevant prior work.

In addition to the comparison with Visual Doom AI, the authors demonstrate that their algorithm can learn generalizable policies that can respond to limited changes in the goal without further training.

The paper is well-communicated, and the empirical results are compelling, making it a significant contribution that will be of interest to the community.

Some minor potential improvements:

There is an approximation in the supervised training as it makes an on-policy assumption, but it learns from a replay buffer. With Monte Carlo regression, the expectation of the remainder of the trajectory is assumed to follow the current policy, but is being sampled from episodes generated by prior versions of the policy. This should be discussed.

The algorithm uses additional metadata (information about which parts of the sensory input are worth predicting) that the compared algorithms do not. I think this, and the limitations of this approach (e.g., it may not work well in a sensory environment if such measurements are not provided), should be mentioned more clearly.
