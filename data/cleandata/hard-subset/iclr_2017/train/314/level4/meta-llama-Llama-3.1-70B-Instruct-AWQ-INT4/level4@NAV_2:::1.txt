This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, leveraging a high-dimensional sensory stream and a lower-dimensional measurement stream to provide a rich supervisory signal for training a sensorimotor control model. The model is trained using supervised learning techniques, without extraneous supervision, and learns to act based on raw sensory input from a complex three-dimensional environment.

I decide to accept this paper, with two key reasons for this choice. Firstly, the paper introduces a new algorithm that performs better in novel 3D environments from raw sensory data and allows better generalization across goals and environments, as demonstrated by its win in the Visual Doom AI competition. Secondly, the paper provides a thorough empirical comparison to relevant prior work, including state-of-the-art deep RL methods, and shows that the proposed algorithm outperforms these methods in challenging tasks.

The key idea of the algorithm is to use additional low-dimensional observations as a supervised target for prediction, conditioned on a goal vector and the current action. This approach is distinct from traditional RL methods, which rely on scalar rewards or TD relationships between predictions of the current state and the next state. The authors also provide a comprehensive review of prior works in predicting future states as part of RL and goal-driven function approximators, highlighting the contributions of their work, including the focus on Monte Carlo estimation, the use of low-dimensional 'measurements' for prediction, and the parametrized goals.

The paper is well-communicated, and the empirical results are compelling, demonstrating the ability of the algorithm to learn generalizable policies that can respond to limited changes in the goal without further training. However, there are some minor potential improvements that could be discussed. For instance, the supervised training makes an on-policy assumption, but learns from a replay buffer, which assumes the expectation of the remainder of the trajectory follows the current policy, but is being sampled from episodes generated by prior versions of the policy. Additionally, the algorithm uses additional metadata that the compared algorithms do not, which may limit its applicability in certain sensory environments.

To further improve the paper, I would like to see a discussion on the limitations of this approach, including the potential impact of the additional metadata on the performance of the algorithm. I would also appreciate more details on the experimental setup, including the specific architectures used for the compared algorithms and the hyperparameter tuning process.

Some questions I would like the authors to answer to clarify my understanding of the paper and provide additional evidence to support their claims include:

* Can the authors provide more details on the specific architectures used for the compared algorithms, including the number of layers, layer sizes, and activation functions?
* How did the authors tune the hyperparameters for the compared algorithms, and what was the range of hyperparameters explored?
* Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup for the Visual Doom AI competition, including the specific tasks, environments, and evaluation metrics used?
* How do the authors plan to address the limitations of their approach, including the potential impact of the additional metadata on the performance of the algorithm?
* Can the authors provide more details on the potential applications of their algorithm, including its potential use in real-world robotic systems or other areas?
