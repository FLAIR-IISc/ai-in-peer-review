This paper presents an empirical work that builds upon existing deep reinforcement learning (RL) approaches to solve RL problems in large state spaces. The proposed algorithm demonstrates superior performance in novel 3D environments using raw sensory data and achieves better generalization across goals and environments. Notably, this algorithm was the winner of the Visual Doom AI competition.

The core idea of the algorithm is to utilize low-dimensional observations (such as ammo or health provided by the game engine) as a supervised target for prediction. This prediction is conditioned on a given goal vector and the current action. Once trained, the optimal action for the current state can be chosen as the action that maximizes the predicted outcome according to the goal. Unlike successor feature representations, learning is supervised, and there is no temporal difference (TD) relationship between the predictions of the current state and the next state.

The authors provide a thorough review of prior works on predicting future states as part of RL and goal-driven function approximators in section 2. The key contributions of this work are the focus on Monte Carlo estimation (rather than TD), the use of low-dimensional 'measurements' for prediction, the parametrized goals, and the empirical comparison to relevant prior work.

In addition to the comparison with Visual Doom AI, the authors demonstrate that their algorithm can learn generalizable policies that can respond to limited changes in the goal without further training.

The paper is well-communicated, and the empirical results are compelling, making it a significant contribution to the field.

Some minor potential improvements:

The supervised training procedure makes an on-policy assumption but learns from a replay buffer. This discrepancy arises because the Monte Carlo regression assumes the expectation of the remainder of the trajectory follows the current policy, whereas it is being sampled from episodes generated by prior versions of the policy. This should be discussed to provide clarity.

The algorithm utilizes additional metadata (information about which parts of the sensory input are worth predicting) that the compared algorithms do not. It would be beneficial to explicitly mention this and discuss the limitations of this approach, such as its potential ineffectiveness in sensory environments where such measurements are not provided.
