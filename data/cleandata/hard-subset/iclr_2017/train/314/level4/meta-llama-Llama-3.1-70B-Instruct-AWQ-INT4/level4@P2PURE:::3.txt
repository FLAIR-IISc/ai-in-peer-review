The paper presents an on-policy method for predicting future intrinsic measurements, with all experiments conducted in the game of Doom (specifically, vizDoom). Notably, the authors trained their model to predict sequences of triplets comprising health, ammunition, and frags, weighted by input "goal" triplets. This weighting mechanism allows for guided exploration by modifying the goal triplet weights. At test time, actions can be selected by maximizing the long-term goal.

The results are impressive, as this model won the 2016 vizDoom competition. The experimental section appears sound, featuring comparisons of the proposed method, DFP, with A3C, DQN, and an attempt to compare with DSR (a recent similar approach by Kulkarni et al., 2016). DFP outperforms other approaches, or matches their performance when they reach a ceiling or optimum, as observed with A3C in scenario D1. An ablation study supports the thesis that the added complexity of the paper's model is beneficial.

The paper operates within well-studied domains of reinforcement learning, including predicting intrinsic motivation (Singh et al., 2004), auxiliary variables, and forward modeling. The version reviewed (December 4th revision) adequately references prior work, although the list of references may not be exhaustive.

A few minor comments on the form:

* Doom is described as a 3D environment, but it is actually a 2D environment presented in a pseudo-3D format, as the height dimension is not discriminative or actionable.
* The use of "P" in equation (2) and subsequent equations may be misleading, as it represents prediction rather than probability, which is the conventional meaning of "P".
* The dual use of "j" (albeit with different fonts) in equation (6) may cause confusion.
* Results tables could benefit from repeating the units of measurement, particularly in Table 1, where the units are heterogeneous.

In my opinion, this paper is a clear accept. While one could argue that experiments on different environments or the novelty of the approach might be limited, I believe that papers that are "correct" (experimentally sound, with a clear and reproducible setup, as demonstrated in the appendix) and "milestone" (winning the vizDoom competition) should be published.
