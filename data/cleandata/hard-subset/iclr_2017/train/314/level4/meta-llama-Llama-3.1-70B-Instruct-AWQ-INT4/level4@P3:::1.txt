**Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, leveraging a high-dimensional sensory stream and a lower-dimensional measurement stream. The authors introduce a simple yet effective algorithm that uses supervised learning techniques to train a sensorimotor control model, without relying on extraneous supervision. The model learns to act based on raw sensory input from a complex three-dimensional environment, and it can pursue dynamically changing goals at test time. The approach is evaluated in immersive three-dimensional simulations based on the classical game Doom, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance and generalization across environments and goals.

**Supporting Arguments**

The empirical results presented in the paper are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in various scenarios.

The paper's use of supervised learning techniques to train a sensorimotor control model is a significant contribution, as it provides a novel and effective approach to learning from raw sensory input. The authors' focus on Monte Carlo estimation, the use of low-dimensional 'measurements' for prediction, and the parametrized goals are also key contributions that set their approach apart from prior work.

**Additional Feedback**

To further improve the paper, I suggest the following:

* Clarify the limitations of the approach, particularly with regards to the use of additional metadata (the information about which parts of the sensory input are worth predicting). This could be mentioned in the discussion or limitations section.

**Questions for the Authors**

To clarify my understanding of the paper and provide additional evidence, I would like the authors to answer the following questions:

* Can you provide more details on the architecture of the predictor network, particularly with regards to the choice of hyperparameters and the number of layers?
* How do you handle the case where the goal vector is not fixed during training, but rather changes dynamically? Can you provide more details on the randomized goal vector training regime?
* Can you provide more insights on the ablation study, particularly with regards to the importance of predicting multiple measurements and the effect of predicting measurements at multiple temporal offsets?
