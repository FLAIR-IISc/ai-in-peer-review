The paper presents an on-policy method for predicting future intrinsic measurements, with all experiments conducted in the game of Doom (specifically, vizDoom). Notably, the authors trained their model to predict sequences of triplets comprising health, ammunition, and frags, weighted by sequences of "goal" triplets provided as input. This weighting mechanism allows for exploration by modifying the weights of the goal triplet. At test time, the model can act by maximizing the long-term goal.

The results are impressive, particularly given that this model won the 2016 vizDoom competition. The experimental section of the paper appears sound, featuring comparisons between the proposed method, DFP, and other approaches such as A3C, DQN, and DSR (a recent similar approach from Kulkarni et al., 2016). DFP outperforms other methods or equals them when they reach a ceiling or optimum, as observed with A3C in scenario D1. Additionally, an ablation study supports the thesis that the added complexity of the paper's model is beneficial.

Predicting intrinsic motivation (Singh et al., 2004), auxiliary variables, and forward modeling are well-studied domains in reinforcement learning. The version of the paper I reviewed (December 4th revision) adequately references prior work, although it may not be completely exhaustive.

A few minor comments on the paper's form:

* Doom is described as a 3D environment, whereas it is actually a 2D environment (with height being a non-discriminative/actionable dimension) presented in a pseudo-3D format.
* The use of "P" in Equation (2) (and subsequent equations) may be misleading, as it stands for prediction rather than probability (which is the typical interpretation of "P").
* The double use of "j" (albeit with different fonts) in Equation (6) may be confusing.
* Results tables could benefit from repeating the units of the measurements, particularly as they are heterogeneous in Table 1.

In my opinion, this paper is a clear accept. While one could argue that experiments could be conducted on different environments or that the novelty is limited, I believe that "correct" (i.e., no-nonsense, experimentally sound on Doom, with an appendix providing details for reproducibility) and "milestone" (vizDoom winner) papers merit publication.
