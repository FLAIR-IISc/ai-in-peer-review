**Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions**

The paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, leveraging a high-dimensional sensory stream and a lower-dimensional measurement stream. The cotemporal structure of these streams provides a rich supervisory signal, enabling training a sensorimotor control model through supervised learning techniques without extraneous supervision. The model learns to act based on raw sensory input from a complex three-dimensional environment and can pursue dynamically changing goals at test time. The approach outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods in challenging tasks and demonstrates generalization across environments and goals.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

Based on the paper's contributions and empirical results, I recommend accepting the paper. The key reasons for this decision are:

1. The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated approach to sensorimotor control, leveraging the cotemporal structure of sensory and measurement streams to provide a rich supervisory signal.
2. The approach demonstrates state-of-the-art performance in challenging tasks and exhibits generalization across environments and goals, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

**Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's approach is well-motivated, building on the idea of using low-dimensional observations as a supervised target for prediction. The use of parametrized goals and Monte Carlo estimation is a key contribution, and the empirical comparison to relevant prior work is thorough and compelling. The paper's results demonstrate significant improvements over state-of-the-art deep RL methods, and the ability to learn generalizable policies that can respond to limited changes in the goal without further training is a notable achievement.

**Additional Feedback and Suggestions**

To further improve the paper, I suggest addressing the following points:

1. Discuss the approximation in the supervised training, as it makes an on-policy assumption but learns from a replay buffer.
2. Clearly mention the additional metadata used by the algorithm (information about which parts of the sensory input are worth predicting) and the limitations of this approach.

**Questions for the Authors**

To clarify my understanding of the paper, I would like the authors to address the following questions:

1. Can you provide more details on the approximation in the supervised training and how it affects the performance of the algorithm?
2. How do you plan to address the limitations of the approach, particularly in sensory environments where measurements are not provided?
