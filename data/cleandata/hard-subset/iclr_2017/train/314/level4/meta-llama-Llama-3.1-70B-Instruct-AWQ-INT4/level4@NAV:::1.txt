This submission presents a novel deep reinforcement learning (RL) algorithm that achieves state-of-the-art performance in complex 3D environments with raw sensory data. The algorithm's effectiveness in generalizing across goals and environments is noteworthy, as demonstrated by its victory in the Visual Doom AI competition.

The core idea behind this approach is to leverage low-dimensional observations (e.g., ammo or health) as a supervised target for prediction, conditioned on a predefined goal vector and the current action. This prediction enables the selection of the optimal action for the current state by maximizing the predicted outcome according to the goal. Unlike successor feature representations, this method employs supervised learning and eschews the temporal difference (TD) relationship between current and next state predictions.

The authors provide a comprehensive review of prior works on predicting future states in RL and goal-driven function approximators in Section 2. The key contributions of this work include the focus on Monte Carlo estimation, the use of low-dimensional measurements for prediction, parametrized goals, and a thorough empirical comparison with relevant prior work.

In addition to the Visual Doom AI competition, the authors demonstrate that their algorithm can learn generalizable policies that respond to limited changes in goals without further training. The paper is well-written, and the empirical results are compelling, making it a significant contribution to the field.

Some potential minor improvements to consider:

1. The supervised training approximation assumes an on-policy scenario, but it learns from a replay buffer. This discrepancy, which arises from the Monte Carlo regression assuming the remainder of the trajectory follows the current policy, should be discussed. Specifically, the expectation is sampled from episodes generated by prior policy versions.
2. The algorithm relies on additional metadata (information about which parts of the sensory input are worth predicting) not utilized by the compared algorithms. It would be beneficial to explicitly acknowledge this difference and discuss the limitations of this approach, such as its potential ineffectiveness in sensory environments lacking such measurements.
