This paper presents a novel algorithm for deep reinforcement learning (RL) that leverages low-dimensional observations as a supervised target for prediction. The algorithm has demonstrated state-of-the-art performance in novel 3D environments from raw sensory data, allowing for better generalization across goals and environments. Notably, this algorithm was the winner of the Visual Doom AI competition.

The core idea of the algorithm is to use additional low-dimensional observations, such as ammo or health provided by the game engine, as a supervised target for prediction. This prediction is conditioned on a goal vector, which is given and not learned, and the current action. Once trained, the optimal action for the current state can be chosen as the action that maximizes the predicted outcome according to the goal. Unlike successor feature representations, learning is supervised, and there is no temporal difference (TD) relationship between the predictions of the current state and the next state.

The authors build upon prior works that have explored predicting future states as part of RL and goal-driven function approximators, which are reviewed in Section 2. The key contributions of this work are the focus on Monte Carlo estimation, the use of low-dimensional 'measurements' for prediction, the parameterized goals, and, perhaps most importantly, the empirical comparison to relevant prior work.

In addition to the comparison with Visual Doom AI, the authors demonstrate that their algorithm can learn generalizable policies that can respond to limited changes in the goal without further training. The paper is well-communicated, and the empirical results are compelling, making it a significant contribution to the field.

Some minor potential improvements could be considered. The supervised training makes an on-policy assumption but learns from a replay buffer, which may introduce some bias. Specifically, the Monte Carlo regression assumes that the expectation of the remainder of the trajectory follows the current policy, but it is being sampled from episodes generated by prior versions of the policy. This should be discussed in more detail.

Furthermore, the algorithm uses additional metadata, such as information about which parts of the sensory input are worth predicting, that the compared algorithms do not. This, and the limitations of this approach, such as its potential failure in sensory environments where such measurements are not provided, should be mentioned more clearly. Nevertheless, the paper is well-written, and the empirical results are convincing, making it a valuable contribution to the field of deep RL.
