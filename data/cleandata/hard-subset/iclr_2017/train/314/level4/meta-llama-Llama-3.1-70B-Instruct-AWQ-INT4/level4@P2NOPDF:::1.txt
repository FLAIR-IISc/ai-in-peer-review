This paper presents an empirical study that builds upon recent successes in deep reinforcement learning (RL), which utilizes deep neural networks as function approximators in RL algorithms. The proposed algorithm demonstrates improved performance in novel 3D environments from raw sensory data and achieves better generalization across goals and environments. Notably, this algorithm was the winner of the Visual Doom AI competition.

The core idea of the algorithm is to leverage additional low-dimensional observations, such as ammo or health provided by the game engine, as a supervised target for prediction. This prediction is conditioned on a goal vector, which is given and not learned, and the current action. Once trained, the optimal action for the current state can be chosen as the action that maximizes the predicted outcome according to the goal. Unlike successor feature representations, learning is supervised, and there is no temporal difference (TD) relationship between the predictions of the current state and the next state.

The authors provide a comprehensive review of prior works in Section 2, which have explored predicting future states as part of RL and goal-driven function approximators. The key contributions of this work lie in its focus on Monte Carlo estimation (rather than TD), the use of low-dimensional 'measurements' for prediction, the parametrized goals, and, most importantly, the empirical comparison to relevant prior work.

In addition to the comparison with Visual Doom AI, the authors demonstrate that their algorithm can learn generalizable policies that can respond, without further training, to limited changes in the goal. The paper is well-written, and the empirical results are compelling, making it a significant contribution to the field.

Some potential improvements could be considered. Firstly, the supervised training approximation makes an on-policy assumption, but it learns from a replay buffer. Specifically, with Monte Carlo regression, the expectation of the remainder of the trajectory is assumed to follow the current policy, but is being sampled from episodes generated by prior versions of the policy. This discrepancy should be discussed. Secondly, the algorithm utilizes additional metadata, such as information about which parts of the sensory input are worth predicting, that the compared algorithms do not. The limitations of this approach, including its potential failure in sensory environments where such measurements are not provided, should be more clearly acknowledged.
