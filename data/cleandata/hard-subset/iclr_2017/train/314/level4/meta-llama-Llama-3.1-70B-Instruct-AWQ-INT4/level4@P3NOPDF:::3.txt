This paper presents an on-policy method for predicting future intrinsic measurements, with all experiments conducted in the game of Doom (specifically, vizDoom). Notably, instead of simply predicting win/loss or the number of frags (score), the authors trained their model to predict a sequence of triplets comprising health, ammunition, and frags, weighted by a sequence of "goal" triplets provided as input. This weighting mechanism allows for guided exploration by changing the weights of the goal triplet. At test time, actions can be taken by maximizing the long-term goal.

The results are impressive, as this model emerged as the winner of the 2016 vizDoom competition. The experimental section of the paper appears sound, featuring comparisons of the proposed method, DFP, with A3C, DQN, and an attempt to compare with DSR (a recent similar approach by Kulkarni et al., 2016). DFP outperforms other approaches or equals them when they reach a ceiling or optimum, as observed with A3C in scenario D1. Additionally, an ablation study supports the thesis that the added complexity of the paper's model is useful.

The concepts of predicting intrinsic motivation (Singh et al., 2004), auxiliary variables, and forward modeling are well-studied domains within reinforcement learning. The version of the paper I reviewed (December 4th revision) adequately references prior work, although it may not be completely exhaustive.

A few minor comments on the form:

- The description of Doom as a 3D environment is inaccurate, as it is essentially a 2D environment (with height not being a discriminative or actionable dimension) presented in a pseudo-3D format.
- The use of "P" in equation (2) and subsequent equations may be misleading, as it represents prediction rather than probability (which is the typical interpretation of "P").
- The double use of "j" (albeit with different fonts) in equation (6) may cause confusion.
- The results tables could benefit from repeating the units of measurement, particularly in Table 1, where the measurements are heterogeneous.

In conclusion, I believe this paper is a clear accept. While one could argue that experiments could be conducted in different environments or that the novelty is limited, I think that "correct" (no-nonsense, experimentally sound on Doom, with an appendix providing details for reproducibility) and "milestone" (vizDoom winner) papers warrant publication.
