This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, leveraging a high-dimensional sensory stream and a lower-dimensional measurement stream. The cotemporal structure of these streams provides a rich supervisory signal, enabling the training of a sensorimotor control model through interaction with the environment. The model is trained using supervised learning techniques, but without extraneous supervision, and learns to act based on raw sensory input from complex three-dimensional environments.

I recommend accepting this paper, with two key reasons for this choice. Firstly, the paper presents a well-motivated approach that addresses key challenges in sensorimotor control, including learning from raw sensory input in complex and dynamic environments. Secondly, the paper provides strong empirical evidence to support its claims, including extensive experiments in three-dimensional simulations and a winning entry in the Visual Doom AI Competition.

The paper's approach is well-placed in the literature, building on previous work in supervised learning, reinforcement learning, and sensorimotor control. The authors provide a clear and thorough review of relevant prior work, highlighting the key contributions of their approach, including the use of Monte Carlo estimation, low-dimensional 'measurements' for prediction, and parametrized goals.

The empirical results presented in the paper are compelling, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach in learning generalizable policies that can respond to changes in the goal without further training. The comparison with prior work, including the Visual Doom AI Competition, provides strong evidence for the approach's superiority in certain scenarios.

However, there are some minor potential improvements that could be addressed. Firstly, the paper assumes an on-policy assumption in the supervised training, but learns from a replay buffer. This approximation should be discussed, as it may have implications for the algorithm's performance. Secondly, the algorithm uses additional metadata, such as the information about which parts of the sensory input are worth predicting, that the compared algorithms do not. This should be mentioned more clearly, along with the limitations of this approach, such as its potential failure in sensory environments where such measurements are not provided.

Additional feedback to improve the paper includes providing more details on the architecture and training procedure, such as the specific neural network architecture used and the hyperparameters employed. Furthermore, it would be beneficial to include more analysis on the robustness of the approach to different types of noise or perturbations in the sensory input.

Questions to the authors include: (1) How do the authors plan to address the limitation of requiring additional metadata, such as the information about which parts of the sensory input are worth predicting? (2) Can the authors provide more details on the neural network architecture used and the hyperparameters employed in the experiments? (3) How do the authors plan to extend the approach to more complex scenarios, such as those involving multiple agents or dynamic environments?
