The paper presents an on-policy method for predicting future intrinsic measurements, with all experiments conducted in the game of Doom (specifically, vizDoom). Notably, the authors trained their model to predict a sequence of triplets consisting of health, ammunition, and frags, weighted by a sequence of "goal" triplets provided as input. By adjusting the weights of the goal triplet, the method allows for guided exploration. At test time, actions can be determined by maximizing the long-term goal.

The results are impressive, as this model won the 2016 vizDoom competition. The experimental section appears sound, featuring comparisons of the proposed method, DFP, with A3C, DQN, and an attempt to compare with DSR (a recent similar approach by Kulkarni et al., 2016). DFP outperforms other approaches or matches their performance when they reach a ceiling or optimum, as seen with A3C in scenario D1. Additionally, an ablation study supports the thesis that the added complexity of the paper's model is useful.

Predicting intrinsic motivation (Singh et al., 2004), auxiliary variables, and forward modeling are well-studied domains in reinforcement learning. The version of the paper I reviewed (December 4th revision) adequately references prior work, although it may not be completely exhaustive.

A few minor comments on the form:

* Doom is described as a 3D environment, whereas it is actually a 2D environment (with height being a non-discriminative/actionable dimension) presented in a 3D format.
* The use of "P" in equation (2) (and subsequently) may be misleading, as it stands for prediction rather than probability (as is normally the case for P).
* The double use of "j" (albeit with different fonts) in equation (6) may be misleading.
* Results tables could benefit from repeating the units of the measurements, particularly as they are heterogeneous in Table 1.

In my opinion, this paper is a clear accept. While one could argue that experiments could be conducted on different environments or that the novelty is limited, I believe that "correct" (no-nonsense, experimentally sound on Doom, with an appendix providing details for reproducibility) and "milestone" (vizDoom winner) papers warrant publication.
