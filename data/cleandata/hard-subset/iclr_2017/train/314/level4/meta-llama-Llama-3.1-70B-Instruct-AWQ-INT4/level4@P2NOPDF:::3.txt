The paper presents an on-policy method for predicting future intrinsic measurements, with all experiments conducted in the game of Doom (specifically, vizDoom). Instead of predicting simple outcomes such as win/loss or score, the authors trained their model to predict sequences of triplets comprising health, ammunition, and frags, weighted by input "goal" triplets. This weighting mechanism allows for guided exploration by modifying the goal triplet weights. At test time, the model acts by maximizing the long-term goal.

The results are impressive, as evidenced by the model's victory in the 2016 vizDoom competition. The experimental section appears sound, featuring comparisons between the proposed method (DFP) and other approaches, including A3C, DQN, and DSR (a similar approach by Kulkarni et al., 2016). DFP outperforms or matches the performance of other methods, particularly in scenarios where they reach a ceiling or optimum (e.g., A3C in scenario D1). An ablation study supports the claim that the added complexity of the proposed model is beneficial.

The paper's contributions are situated within well-studied domains of reinforcement learning, including predicting intrinsic motivation (Singh et al., 2004), auxiliary variables, and forward modeling. The version reviewed (December 4th revision) adequately references prior work, although the list of citations may not be exhaustive.

Some minor comments on the paper's form are noted:

* Doom is described as a 3D environment, whereas it is more accurately characterized as a 2D environment presented in a pseudo-3D format, as the height dimension is not discriminative or actionable.
* The use of "P" in equation (2) and subsequent equations may be misleading, as it represents prediction rather than probability, which is the conventional interpretation of "P".
* The double use of "j" in equation (6), albeit with different fonts, may cause confusion.
* Results tables would benefit from repeating the units of measurement, particularly in Table 1, where the units are heterogeneous.

Overall, this paper is a clear accept. While one could argue that experiments should be conducted in different environments or that the novelty is limited, the paper's sound experimental design, milestone achievement (winning the vizDoom competition), and adequate referencing of prior work make it a strong candidate for publication.
