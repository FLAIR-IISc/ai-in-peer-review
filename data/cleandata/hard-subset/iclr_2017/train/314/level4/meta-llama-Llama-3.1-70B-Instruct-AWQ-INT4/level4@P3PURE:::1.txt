This paper presents a novel deep reinforcement learning (RL) algorithm that achieves state-of-the-art performance in novel 3D environments from raw sensory data, demonstrating improved generalization across goals and environments. Notably, this algorithm was the winner of the Visual Doom AI competition.

The core idea of the algorithm is to leverage additional low-dimensional observations (e.g., ammo or health, provided by the game engine) as a supervised target for prediction. Crucially, this prediction is conditioned on a goal vector (given, not learned) and the current action. Once trained, the optimal action for the current state can be chosen as the action that maximizes the predicted outcome according to the goal. In contrast to successor feature representations, learning is supervised, and there is no temporal difference (TD) relationship between the predictions of the current state and the next state.

The authors build upon prior works in predicting future states as part of RL and goal-driven function approximators, which are reviewed in Section 2. The key contributions of this work are the focus on Monte Carlo estimation (rather than TD), the use of low-dimensional 'measurements' for prediction, the parametrized goals, and, perhaps most importantly, the empirical comparison to relevant prior work.

In addition to the comparison with Visual Doom AI, the authors demonstrate that their algorithm can learn generalizable policies that can respond, without further training, to limited changes in the goal. The paper is well-communicated, and the empirical results are compelling, making it a significant contribution to the field.

Some potential improvements and discussions are warranted. Firstly, the supervised training approximation relies on an on-policy assumption, but the algorithm learns from a replay buffer. Specifically, the Monte Carlo regression assumes the expectation of the remainder of the trajectory follows the current policy, but it is being sampled from episodes generated by prior versions of the policy. This discrepancy should be discussed.

Furthermore, the algorithm utilizes additional metadata (information about which parts of the sensory input are worth predicting) that the compared algorithms do not. The authors should more clearly acknowledge this difference and discuss the limitations of this approach, such as its potential failure in sensory environments where such measurements are not provided.
