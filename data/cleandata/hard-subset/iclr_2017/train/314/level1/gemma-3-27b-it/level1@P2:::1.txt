This paper presents a compelling approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem based on predicting future measurements from raw sensory input and current measurements. The core idea – leveraging the rich supervisory signal inherent in the co-temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams – is insightful and well-motivated, particularly in the context of immersive environments where sparse reward signals often hinder reinforcement learning. The successful application to Doom, culminating in winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, provides strong evidence of the approach's effectiveness.

**Decision: Accept** with enthusiasm. The paper tackles a significant problem in RL – learning complex control policies from raw sensory input – and offers a novel and effective solution that demonstrably outperforms state-of-the-art methods. The results are compelling and the contribution is substantial.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The performance in the Doom environment, and especially winning the Full Deathmatch competition against a strong baseline with additional supervision, provides exceptionally strong evidence supporting the claims. The consistent outperformance across multiple scenarios (D1-D4) reinforces the robustness of the approach. The ablation studies further solidify the importance of vectorial feedback and multi-step prediction.

2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional RL in complex sensorimotor tasks and provides a logical justification for framing the problem as supervised learning. The explanation of how the sensory and measurement streams provide a “rich supervisory signal” is convincing and directly addresses the challenges identified. The connection to prior work (Jordan & Rumelhart, Sutton) is appropriately made and used to position the contribution.

3. **Scientific Rigor:** The experimental setup appears well-controlled, with careful consideration given to baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) and hyperparameter tuning. The ablation studies demonstrate a systematic investigation of the key components of the proposed method. The reporting of results, including error bars (in figures), and the detailed description of the architecture, contribute to the rigor of the work. The comparisons with other submissions in the Visual Doom AI Competition are particularly valuable.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of decision):**

* **Clarify "Goal" Formulation:** While the paper explains the use of future measurements to represent goals, it could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of how the goal vector *g* directly influences the agent's behavior. A small example illustrating how different *g* vectors lead to different actions would be helpful.
* **Detailed Network Architecture:** While the network architecture is described, including a diagram illustrating the expectation and action streams might make the paper more accessible. Specifically, visualizing the normalization layer and its function would be useful.
* **Computational Cost:** A more detailed discussion of the computational resources required for training, compared to the baselines, would be valuable. This would allow readers to assess the practicality of the approach.
* **Generalization Analysis Depth:** While the generalization results are impressive, further analysis of *why* the model generalizes well (e.g., learned representations, invariance to texture) could strengthen the conclusions.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*  The ablation study shows that multiple future measurement predictions improve performance. Is there an optimal number of timesteps to predict, or does performance continue to improve with a larger horizon?
*  The paper mentions using a linear combination of future measurements to define goals. Have you explored other functional forms for *u(f;g)*, such as non-linear combinations or more complex functions?
*  Could you elaborate on the specific differences between the model that won the Visual Doom AI Competition and the one described in the paper? Were any significant modifications made during the competition?
*  The paper states that the agent is trained without “extraneous supervision.” Is any form of curriculum learning employed during training, for example, starting with simpler environments or tasks before moving to more complex ones?
