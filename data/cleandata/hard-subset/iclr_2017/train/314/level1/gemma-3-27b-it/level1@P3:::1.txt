This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem by predicting future measurements given current sensory input and actions, rather than relying on traditional reward-based reinforcement learning. The core idea – leveraging the rich supervisory signal inherent in the co-temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams – is compelling and well-motivated. The authors demonstrate impressive results in the challenging Doom environment, culminating in winning the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Decision: Accept** with enthusiasm. The paper tackles a significant problem in RL – learning from raw sensory input in complex environments – and offers a surprisingly effective and elegant solution. The strong empirical results, including winning a competitive benchmark, convincingly support the claims.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Novelty and Motivation:** The shift from reward maximization to direct future prediction is a significant departure from standard RL, particularly in raw sensory input scenarios. The motivation is clearly articulated: sparse rewards are problematic, while dense, multi-dimensional measurements provide a richer learning signal. This aligns well with insights from motor control in biological systems. The connection to Jordan & Rumelhart (1992) is well-placed, grounding the approach in prior work.
2. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are robust. The consistently superior performance of DFP over strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across increasingly complex Doom scenarios is compelling. The ablation studies further demonstrate the importance of the key components of the proposed approach (multi-dimensional measurements, multi-step prediction).
3. **Winning the Visual Doom AI Competition:** Winning the Full Deathmatch track against a strong field, *without* the extra supervision used by the second-place finisher, is a particularly strong piece of evidence. This shows the method’s generalizability to unseen environments and its competitive edge.
4. **Generalization and Goal Agnostic Learning:** Demonstrating generalization across textured environments and the ability to adapt to dynamically changing goals further strengthens the claims. The results showing competitive performance even when the goal isn't known during training are particularly noteworthy.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the approach. The architecture diagram is helpful.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are strong, a more thorough theoretical analysis would be valuable. A deeper discussion of why predicting measurements is an effective proxy for optimal control could strengthen the paper. Is there a connection to optimal control theory or dynamic programming principles that could be elaborated?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A sensitivity analysis of key hyperparameters (learning rate, network architecture details, temporal offset weighting) would be beneficial. How robust is the performance to changes in these parameters?
* **Computational Cost:** A more detailed discussion of the computational resources required for training would be helpful. How does the training time and memory usage compare to the baselines?
* **Qualitative Results:** Including some qualitative examples of the agent’s behavior (e.g., video clips of gameplay) would further enhance understanding and appreciation of the approach.
* **Measurement Selection:** The paper states that measurements are a subset of sensations. However, the criteria for selecting which sensations constitute measurements are not explicitly addressed. A discussion of this selection process, and potential strategies for automated measurement selection, would be valuable.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. **Regarding the choice of temporal offsets (τ1, ..., τn):** How sensitive is performance to the selection of these offsets? Did you explore different strategies for determining the optimal offsets (e.g., learning them)?
2. **Regarding the goal vector g:** In the goal-agnostic experiments, how were the random goal vectors generated? Was any form of regularization or normalization applied to prevent the vectors from becoming too large or skewed?
3. **Regarding computational cost:** Approximately how long did it take to train the winning model for the Visual Doom AI Competition, and what hardware resources were used?
4. **Regarding the action stream normalization (Eq. 6):** Was this normalization crucial for performance? Did you observe instability or divergence without it?
5. **Future Work:** Do you see potential for combining this approach with model-based RL techniques (e.g., using the learned predictor as a world model)?
