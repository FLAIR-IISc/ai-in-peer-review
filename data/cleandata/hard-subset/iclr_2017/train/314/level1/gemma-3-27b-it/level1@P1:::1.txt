This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem by predicting future measurements from sensory input and actions, rather than relying on traditional reward-based reinforcement learning. The core idea – leveraging the rich supervisory signal from the interplay between sensory streams and measurements – is well-motivated and potentially impactful, particularly in complex, immersive environments. The results are compelling, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance in Doom, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Decision: Accept**

The primary reason for acceptance is the strong empirical results, convincingly demonstrating the superiority of the proposed approach over existing methods on challenging tasks. The win in the Visual Doom AI Competition, against a strong baseline utilizing more complex models and additional supervision, is a particularly strong indicator of the method's effectiveness. Secondly, the paper clearly articulates a novel and well-reasoned approach that departs from conventional RL formulations and offers a potential solution to challenges in sensorimotor control.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper's arguments are largely supported by the experimental results. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the importance of both vectorial measurements and multi-step prediction. The generalization experiments show that the learned policies are not simply overfitting to specific environments. The comparison to strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) provides a solid benchmark. The authors effectively position their work within the existing literature, highlighting the limitations of traditional RL in complex environments and outlining the benefits of their supervised learning formulation. The architectural details are sufficiently described, allowing for potential replication. The ablation experiments clearly demonstrate the benefit of using multiple measurements and predicting them at several timesteps into the future. The win in the Visual Doom competition is a substantial and impressive accomplishment, and the authors appropriately highlight this result.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, some areas could be improved. 

*   **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are compelling, a more formal theoretical analysis of the conditions under which this approach will succeed would be valuable. Are there specific properties of the environment (e.g., observability, smoothness of dynamics) that make this method particularly well-suited?
*   **Goal Representation:** The linear combination of future measurements for defining goals seems somewhat arbitrary. Exploring more sophisticated goal representations or learning goal representations could be a fruitful avenue for future research. The work mentions the capability of the goal vector to represent any goal, but this is not fully explored.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A more thorough analysis of the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Cost:** A clearer discussion of the computational cost (training time, memory requirements) of the proposed approach compared to the baselines would be useful.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** Although quantitative results are impressive, providing some qualitative analysis of the agent’s behavior (e.g., visualizing what the agent is "looking at" or planning) would enhance understanding.

**Questions for Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that the choice of temporal offsets (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32) was made empirically. Was any systematic exploration of different offset combinations performed? If so, what were the results?
2.  Could you elaborate on the specific advantages of using the proposed network architecture (expectation and action streams) compared to a simpler architecture without these separate streams?
3.  The success in the Visual Doom AI Competition is significant. Could you provide more details about the specific environment configuration and the characteristics of the competing agents?
4.  The paper states that training with randomized goals generalizes better. Is there a relationship between the *range* of the random goal vectors and the degree of generalization? Specifically, how does increasing the range of the random goal vector impact performance across environments?
5.  While the approach avoids reward shaping, the choice of coefficients (0.5, 0.5, 1) in the loss function for health, ammunition, and frags can be seen as a form of weighting. How sensitive are the results to changes in these coefficients? Is there a method for learning these weights?
