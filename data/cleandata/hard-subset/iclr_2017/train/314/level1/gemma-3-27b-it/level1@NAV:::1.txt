## Review of "Sensorimotor Control in Immersive Environments"

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in complex 3D environments, framing the problem as supervised learning rather than traditional reinforcement learning. The core idea – leveraging the rich supervisory signal present in the co-temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams – is compelling and well-motivated. The authors convincingly argue that this approach circumvents the limitations of sparse reward signals often encountered in RL, enabling learning from raw sensory input without requiring pre-defined goals during training. The strong experimental results, culminating in a win at the Visual Doom AI Competition, are impressive and demonstrate the practical efficacy of the proposed method. 

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is significant, the presentation is clear, and the results are demonstrably strong. The success in the Visual Doom competition, particularly outperforming a significantly more complex model with extra supervision, is a particularly strong indicator of the method’s potential.

The paper provides a strong justification for its approach, grounding it in the limitations of existing RL paradigms and drawing parallels to established supervised learning principles. The extensive experimental evaluation in the Doom environment is well-designed, with comparisons to state-of-the-art deep RL methods and thorough ablation studies. The results consistently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method, especially in challenging scenarios and with dynamically changing goals. The clear articulation of the benefits—dense supervision, goal-agnostic learning, and generalization—contributes to the overall strength of the work.

However, a few points could enhance the paper. While the architecture is described, a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind specific design choices, particularly within the prediction module (expectation and action streams), would be valuable. Further clarification on the training regime, specifically the impact of the mini-batch size and experience replay buffer size, would also be beneficial. 

I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  Could you elaborate on the choice of temporal offsets (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, and 32) for the future measurements? Was this determined empirically, and how sensitive is the performance to these values?
2.  The paper mentions training with both fixed and randomized goals. Could you provide more insight into the distribution used for sampling random goals, and how this affects the learning process?
3.  Regarding the architecture, what is the intuition behind splitting the prediction module into expectation and action streams? What specific advantages does this architecture offer compared to a simpler single-stream approach?
4.  What computational resources were used for training the models (e.g., number of GPUs, training time)? This would provide context for the feasibility of replicating the results.
