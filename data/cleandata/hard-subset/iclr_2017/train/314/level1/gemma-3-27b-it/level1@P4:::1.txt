This paper presents an approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, framing the problem as supervised learning rather than traditional reinforcement learning. The key idea is to leverage the rich supervisory signal provided by the cotemporal structure of high-dimensional sensory streams and lower-dimensional measurement streams, predicting the effect of actions on future measurements. The authors demonstrate strong results in the Doom environment, even winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper tackles a significant problem – sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex environments – and proposes a novel and effective solution. The experimental results, particularly the win in a competitive setting, strongly support the claims.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty:** While supervised learning for control isn’t entirely new, the framing of the problem using paired sensory and measurement streams as a naturally occurring supervisory signal, and the avoidance of explicit reward shaping, is a significant contribution. The approach cleverly sidesteps the typical challenges of sparse rewards in complex environments.
* **Technical Soundness:** The methodology appears sound. The network architecture is well-defined, and the training procedure is clearly described. The ablation studies provide valuable insight into the contribution of different components (multi-dimensional measurements, multiple time offsets). The comparisons to strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) are appropriate and convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach.
* **Empirical Evidence:** The results are compelling. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on challenging Doom scenarios and winning the Visual Doom AI Competition are substantial achievements. The generalization experiments further strengthen the claims, showing the model’s ability to adapt to new environments and goals. The ablation studies add quantitative support for the design choices.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, approach, and results. The connection to existing literature is well-established, and the authors effectively position their work within the broader field of reinforcement learning.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Goal Representation:** The paper focuses on linear combinations of future measurements as the goal representation. Exploring other goal representations (e.g., non-linear functions, hierarchical goals) could be a valuable direction for future work.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of coefficients in the linear combination?
* **Temporal Abstraction:** As acknowledged by the authors, the model lacks temporal abstraction. Investigating mechanisms to incorporate hierarchical representations or memory could further improve performance and generalization.
* **Computational Cost:** The paper mentions training speed. A more detailed comparison of the computational resources required for training (e.g., GPU hours) relative to the baseline methods would be helpful.
* **DSR Implementation Details:**  The paper mentions challenges with replicating DSR's results due to training speed. Providing more detail on the specific configuration used for DSR (beyond stating it’s the author's implementation) would improve reproducibility.
* **Normalization Layer:** The normalization layer within the action stream (Equation 6) is a unique feature. Could the authors provide more intuition or justification for why this specific normalization scheme is beneficial? Are there other normalization strategies that were considered?



**Questions for the Authors:**

1. The paper states that the goal vector *g* can represent any goal. In practice, how are these goal vectors determined or learned? Is there a mechanism for automatically discovering meaningful goal representations?
2. The ablation study demonstrates the importance of predicting multiple measurements. Are there diminishing returns as the number of measurements increases? Is there an optimal subset of measurements to predict for a given environment or task?
3. The authors mention that the DFP approach is purely reactive. How might one extend the framework to incorporate a memory mechanism for planning or long-term dependencies?
4.  Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter search performed for the baseline methods (DQN, A3C, DSR)? Specifically, what ranges were explored for the learning rates and other key parameters?
