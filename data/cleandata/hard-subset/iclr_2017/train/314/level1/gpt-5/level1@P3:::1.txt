Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning formulation of sensorimotor control that predicts vectors of future measurement changes conditioned on current observation, candidate action, and a goal vector. Actions are selected by maximizing the inner product between predicted future measurements and the goal. Goals are parameterized and can be varied at test time.
- Architecturally, the model consumes raw pixels and low-dimensional measurements, fuses them with a goal embedding, and uses a dueling-style decomposition (expectation stream + action-advantage stream) to output action-conditional multi-horizon predictions.
- Training is via regression to Monte Carlo targets sourced from experience (short replay buffer, ε-greedy), without bootstrapping or external supervision. The targets comprise several temporal offsets, with greater weight on longer offsets.
- Extensive ViZDoom experiments show DFP is competitive on simple tasks and significantly outperforms DQN and A3C on harder, combat-style tasks, generalizes across textures/maps, adapts to test-time goals after goal-agnostic training, and benefits from vectorial measurements and multi-horizon prediction. The approach also won the Visual Doom AI Competition (Full Deathmatch track).

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong empirical performance and compelling demonstrations of goal-parameterized generalization in challenging 3D visuomotor tasks. (2) A simple, well-motivated formulation that bridges predictive representations and control, with clear advantages over scalar-reward RL when dense multivariate feedback is available.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets learning to act from raw sensory input in immersive 3D environments with dynamically specified goals—a timely and important problem. The motivation to exploit dense, vector-valued “measurements” as supervision is clear and contrasts well with sparse scalar rewards.
- Relation to literature: The method sits at a productive intersection of predictive representations (GVFs), universal value function approximators (UVFAs), and successor representations (DSR). Unlike DSR, which learns a (policy-dependent) successor of features, DFP directly regresses action-conditioned finite-horizon feature/measurement outcomes and performs greedy action selection conditioned on goals. This connection is valuable and largely correct: if utilities are linear in measurements, DFP approximates action-conditioned successor features (finite horizon, under the prevailing policy), enabling transfer to new goal weights. The paper cites the pertinent works and differentiates its setting and architecture reasonably well.
- Methodological soundness: The approach is simple and coherent. Using Monte Carlo multi-horizon targets removes bootstrapping instability and exploits dense feedback. The dueling-style split is plausible for learning fine action differences. The goal-conditioning is integrated end-to-end.
- Empirical rigor: The suite includes four tasks with increasing difficulty, ablations (vector vs scalar, single vs multiple horizons), generalization across textures/maps, and randomized-goal training with zero-shot goal adaptation. The competition win provides an external validation. Reported training speed and evaluation protocols are clear, and multiple seeds are used.
- Impact: Demonstrates that direct prediction of meaningful, intrinsic measurements can be a robust alternative to scalar-reward RL in rich perceptual environments, and that goal-parameterization yields flexible behavior without retraining.

Concerns and limitations (not blocking)
- Fairness of baselines: A3C and DQN implementations, hyperparameters, and architectural choices (e.g., recurrence, frame stacking) critically affect performance in partially observable 3D settings. The A3C baseline appears non-recurrent; stronger recurrent A3C/IMPALA-like baselines might close the gap. Clarifying whether DQN/A3C used frame stacks or LSTMs is essential. The DSR comparison is limited by training speed and tuning.
- Policy dependence and targets: The targets for action a are collected under the current behavior policy and thus entangle “take a at t, then follow evolving policy.” As the policy changes, targets shift. While this is akin to Monte Carlo policy iteration, a discussion of stability and off-policy effects (with replay) would strengthen the work.
- Horizon design: The choice of offsets and weights (1,2,4,8,16,32; last three weighted) is heuristic. Sensitivity analyses would be helpful. The induced utility is finite-horizon and non-exponentially weighted; relation to discounted returns is not fully analyzed.
- Architecture ablations: The benefit of the expectation/action split, goal and measurement embeddings, and normalization layers could be quantified via ablations.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Expand the conceptual link to successor features and UVFAs: formalize the conditions under which DFP yields generalized policy improvement for new goal vectors, and clarify the role of policy dependence in the predicted futures.
- Provide stronger baseline configurations: include recurrent A3C/LSTM with frame stacking; report sensitivity to entropy regularization and number of actors; ensure identical observation preprocessing across methods.
- Analyze data efficiency and wall-clock: report sample complexity and compute resources; show training curves in steps and hours; include learning curves for goal-agnostic training vs fixed-goal.
- Robustness/sensitivity: include ablations on (i) number and spacing of horizons, (ii) measurement subsets, (iii) replay size and ε-schedule, (iv) dueling vs monolithic head.
- Clarify evaluation: specify whether health/ammo/frags are normalized, frame rates, action repeat, and whether terminal penalties exist. Include confidence intervals.

Questions for the authors
1. Baselines: Did DQN/A3C use frame stacks or recurrence (LSTM)? If not, can you report results with recurrent A3C and/or stacked frames to address partial observability? Are observations and preprocessing identical across methods?
2. Targets and policy: For F(ot, a, g), do targets correspond to “take action a at t, then follow the current behavior policy”? If so, how sensitive is learning to policy drift given the replay buffer? Did you try importance weighting or smaller buffers?
3. Horizons: How sensitive are results to the chosen temporal offsets and the weighting of the last three? Have you compared to exponentially discounted aggregates to align with RL objectives?
4. Architecture: What is the marginal benefit of the expectation/action split and the action-normalization layer vs a single fully connected head? Please provide ablations.
5. Goal generalization: When training with randomized goals, what distribution over goal weights is used? How far can test-time goals extrapolate beyond the training distribution (e.g., large negative weights)?
6. Measurements: How critical is access to specific measurements (e.g., ammo, frags)? Can DFP work when only sparse scalar rewards are available, or when measurements are noisy/partial?
7. Stability and variance: Can you report training variance across seeds and discuss any failure modes (e.g., local policies that overfit specific textures or degenerate to ammo hoarding/pacifism under certain goals)?
