1) Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning formulation of sensorimotor control that predicts vector-valued future measurements conditioned on current sensory input and a goal vector, and selects actions by maximizing predicted goal utility. The approach reframes control as multi-step measurement prediction rather than reward-based value estimation, enabling training from dense, endogenous supervision without extraneous labels and allowing goal-agnostic training with dynamically specified goals at test time. The architecture integrates perception, measurement, and goal modules and uses a dueling-style split into expectation and action streams. Extensive ViZDoom experiments show strong performance over DQN/A3C/DSR, improved generalization to unseen textures, clear benefits of vectorial feedback and multi-horizon prediction, and a first-place finish in the Visual Doom AI competition’s Full Deathmatch track.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (a) A simple, well-motivated reformulation with clear conceptual impact (goal-conditioned supervised prediction using dense measurements) and (b) compelling empirical evidence of superiority and generalization in challenging 3D settings, including a competitive benchmark win.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem: Learning visuomotor control from raw input in complex 3D environments with dynamically changing goals.
- Motivation/placement: Well situated relative to RL (TD vs Monte Carlo), UVFAs, predictive representations, and multi-objective RL. The case for using dense, vectorial measurements as supervision is cogent and timely.
- Evidence/rigor: Comparisons against strong baselines (A3C, DQN), multiple seeds, long test rollouts, ablations (vector vs scalar, multi-horizon), cross-environment generalization, and a public competition result collectively support claims. Methodological clarity (goal randomization, multi-offset targets) strengthens the case. Limitations include focus on discrete actions, short prediction horizons, lack of memory, and limited theoretical analysis; baseline tuning for DSR is constrained. Nonetheless, the empirical story is strong and replicable in principle.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify sensitivity to the choice and scaling of measurements; discuss how to proceed when key measurements are unavailable or noisy.
- Analyze horizon length: how do performance and stability vary with larger τ or different weighting of offsets?
- Report statistical significance or confidence intervals on key tables; include variance across seeds for final metrics.
- Discuss the computational cost of predicting all actions per step and scalability to larger action spaces or continuous control.
- Consider adding memory (e.g., recurrent modules) and assessing partial observability.

Questions for the authors
- How is performance affected if the goal distribution at training markedly differs from test-time goals (e.g., sparse or adversarial g)?
- What is the minimum/necessary set of measurements for DFP to match A3C, and how robust is DFP to corrupted measurement channels?
- Did you compare to dueling DQN and UVFA baselines with similar architectural capacity?
- How large is the experience memory M and update lag k; how sensitive are results to these choices?
- Can DFP handle stochastic dynamics or delayed effects beyond 32 steps without degrading training stability?
