Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): a simple, supervised-learning formulation of sensorimotor control that predicts future intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) at multiple time offsets conditioned on current sensory input, current measurements, action, and a goal vector. Action selection is done by choosing the action whose predicted future measurements best align with the current goal.
- Key ideas: (i) exploit a dense, multidimensional measurement stream as supervision (rather than a sparse scalar reward), (ii) parameterize goals as linear combinations over predicted future measurement differences, enabling goal-agnostic training and dynamic goals at test time, (iii) a practical deep architecture with perception/measurement/goal encoders and a dueling-style expectation/action stream for action-conditional predictions.
- Empirically, DFP outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C, and DSR where feasible) in Doom-based 3D visuomotor tasks, with particularly large gains on the more complex combat scenarios. It shows generalization to unseen textures/environments and to new goal vectors, and won the Visual Doom AI competition on unseen maps.
- Ablations show that vectorial measurements and multi-horizon predictions both matter.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong, convincing empirical results in challenging 3D embodied control domains, including robust generalization and a public competition win; (2) A conceptually clear, well-motivated alternative to reward-centric TD/policy-gradient RL that leverages dense intrinsic measurements and goal-parameterization; (3) Careful ablations and comparisons suggesting the approach’s core choices are impactful.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles learning visuomotor control directly from raw, high-dimensional sensory input and intrinsic measurements without extraneous supervision. It argues convincingly that in immersive settings, dense, multidimensional feedback is natural and can be exploited via supervised prediction. The positioning w.r.t. RL, Monte Carlo prediction, UVFA, successor representations, and predictive representations is appropriate; the paper’s particular instantiation (vectorial measurements, goal-parameterized direct prediction, action-conditional network) is a coherent and pragmatic design.
- Method soundness: The formulation is simple and transparent: predict action-conditional future measurement deltas at multiple horizons from current observation and goal; act greedily wrt a goal-weighted sum of predictions. The use of an expectation/action stream echoes dueling networks; conditioning on goals aligns with UVFA. The training uses straightforward regression with experience replay and epsilon-greedy data collection; no fragile TD targets or value bootstrapping are required, which likely improves stability.
- Empirical support:
  - Across four Doom scenarios, DFP matches or surpasses A3C and DQN, with very large margins in the more complex, multi-measurement settings (D3/D4). Reporting over 50M steps for all major methods is fair, and the method trains efficiently in wall-clock time.
  - Generalization: training with many textures yields transfer to unseen textures; models trained with randomized goals generalize better to new goals.
  - Goal-agnostic training: models trained without knowing the eventual goal perform almost as well on that goal as a dedicated model and adapt to different test-time goals.
  - Ablation: vectorial measurements and multi-horizon targets both substantially improve final performance, even when evaluation focuses on a single metric (frags).
  - External validation: the Visual Doom AI competition win, outperforming a stronger, more complex baseline with extra supervision, is compelling.
- Rigor: The experiments are extensive for a 3D setting, include multiple baselines, seeds, and ablations, and detail training speeds and evaluation protocols. While theoretical analysis is limited, the empirical evidence is strong and carefully presented.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Fairness and clarity of baselines:
  - Please explicitly state whether baselines received the same observation tuple 〈st, mt〉 as DFP (i.e., health/ammo/frags as part of the state). This is crucial to isolate the contribution of the learning objective rather than privileged inputs.
  - A3C can benefit from an LSTM on partially observable 3D tasks. Consider reporting A3C-LSTM performance (with comparable observation inputs) for completeness.
  - Since DSR was only evaluated on D1 due to speed, it would help to add a successor-representation baseline (or UVFA) on D3/D4 in a simplified setting, or discuss expected behavior.
- Analysis:
  - Report prediction accuracy/calibration of future measurement predictions and its correlation with control performance.
  - Include an ablation without the dueling-style split to quantify its contribution.
  - Provide sensitivity to the choice and weighting of horizons (e.g., using more/other τ, or discount-style weights) and to experience memory size/update cadence.
- Generalization claims:
  - The “tx” experiments change textures but not geometry. Please include transfer to unseen layouts to substantiate “generalize across environments” more broadly.
  - For goal generalization, provide a denser sweep over the goal space (e.g., random goals at test) and show smooth performance interpolation.
- Scope and limitations:
  - Discuss when dense measurement streams are unavailable or partially observed; can auxiliary self-supervised signals approximate measurements?
  - Clarify the off-policy nature: predictions are of outcomes under the behavior policy after taking the chosen action. A brief discussion of potential bias/mismatch and why it works well empirically would be valuable.

Questions for the authors
1. Do all baselines receive the same measurement vector mt as part of their observation, or only rewards? If not, can you provide results where baselines also observe mt?
2. How are measurements normalized and scaled in practice, and how sensitive is DFP to these choices?
3. What exactly is the target policy for the predicted futures: “take action a, then follow the current ε-greedy policy”? If so, how does the evolving policy affect target stationarity, and did you try importance weighting or policy-free rollouts for targets?
4. How critical is the dueling-style expectation/action decomposition? Do single-head predictors reach similar performance?
5. Did you evaluate A3C with an LSTM and/or frame stacking for partial observability? If so, how does it compare?
6. Can DFP incorporate continuous actions (e.g., via parameterized action-conditional predictors), and have you tried this in simpler continuous-control domains?
7. How far can you push goal generalization? For example, can a model trained with goals sampled in [−1, 1] extrapolate to unseen weightings emphasizing very long-horizon effects?
8. In D4/D4-tx, can you show transfer to unseen map topologies (new mazes) to complement the texture transfer?

Overall, this is an elegant, well-executed paper with clear contributions and strong empirical validation. I recommend acceptance.
