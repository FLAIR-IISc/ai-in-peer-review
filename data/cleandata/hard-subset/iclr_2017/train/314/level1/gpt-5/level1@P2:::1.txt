Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a simple supervised-learning formulation for sensorimotor control that predicts action-conditional future “measurements” (e.g., health, ammo, frags) from raw sensory input and a goal vector. Actions are chosen by maximizing the predicted goal-weighted future measurements. Key claims: (1) dense, multivariate measurement prediction provides a rich supervisory signal that stabilizes and accelerates training compared to scalar rewards; (2) parameterizing goals enables training without a fixed goal and flexible goal changes at test time; (3) the approach outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C) on challenging 3D ViZDoom tasks, generalizes across environments, and won the Visual Doom AI Competition. The paper is clear, well-motivated, and provides ablations showing the value of multiple measurements and multi-horizon prediction.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical evidence that a simple supervised prediction objective, conditioned on goals, can outperform state-of-the-art deep RL on challenging 3D visuomotor tasks, with compelling additional demonstrations (generalization across textures/goals; competition win).
- Conceptually novel and well-placed: reframes control as supervised prediction over intrinsic measurements, connecting to UVFA/PSR, while contributing a practical, scalable instantiation with dense supervision and a tailored architecture.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem tackled: Learning sensorimotor control directly from raw sensory streams in complex 3D environments, with the added desideratum of flexible test-time goals. This is a significant and timely challenge.

2) Motivation and placement in literature: The paper argues that when dense, multidimensional feedback exists, SL over future measurements can be preferable to TD-based RL. The framing is related to UVFAs, generalized value functions, and Monte Carlo prediction, but differs by directly predicting action-conditional future intrinsic measurements at multiple horizons and selecting actions by a goal-weighted projection. The related work situates the approach among RL, UVFA, successor representations, and predictive representations; the contribution is a concrete, effective formulation and architecture for high-dimensional sensory input.

3) Support for claims and rigor:
- Comparisons: DFP vs DQN, A3C (and DSR where feasible) over four scenarios. DFP matches A3C on easy tasks and strongly dominates on harder ones (6x frags in D3; 2.5x in D4). Training budgets and reward definitions are aligned; hyperparameters are tuned for baselines. Reporting includes training curves and final performance over large test rollouts.
- Generalization: Training on varied textures improves cross-environment transfer; a higher-capacity model further improves performance.
- Goal flexibility: Models trained with randomized goals nearly match dedicated models on the “main task” and generalize better to new goals, supporting the goal-conditioning claim.
- Ablations: Show that vector-valued measurements and multi-horizon prediction both contribute substantially, even when evaluation is on a single metric.
- Practicality: High training throughput on ViZDoom and success in an external competition further support relevance.

Potential limitations/concerns (do not outweigh merits):
- Off-policy/statistical consistency: DFP predicts future measurements under the data-collection policy, not a fixed target policy; yet empirically this instability does not manifest. A brief discussion of policy mismatch and sensitivity to replay size/refresh rate would strengthen rigor.
- Baseline completeness: DQN variant appears non-dueling, while DFP uses an expectation/action-stream decomposition akin to dueling networks; a dueling DQN baseline would test whether architectural differences explain part of the gains. Likewise, an A3C-LSTM baseline could address partial observability.
- Long-horizon credit assignment: The approach relies on dense measurements and fixed short-to-mid horizons; performance in sparse/delayed reward domains remains unclear.
- Statistical reporting: More runs or confidence intervals would help quantify variability; currently most plots average over three seeds.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify the learning target: specify precisely how fi is computed (n-step sums of measurement deltas over τ ∈ {1,2,4,8,16,32} with weights (0.5,0.5,1) on the last three) and discuss why weighting only recent horizons works best.
- Analyze sensitivity to choices: measurement set, horizon set, coefficients, replay buffer size, update frequency k, and ε schedule.
- Fairness of baselines: add dueling/double/prioritized DQN and an A3C-LSTM variant; report their throughput. If infeasible, discuss why and provide references indicating expected gaps.
- Provide learning curves with shaded variance and report compute budgets (wall-clock, hardware) and frames per second for each method.
- Discuss applicability beyond ViZDoom: when measurements are scarce or costly to define; possibilities for learning measurements from representation learning; extension to continuous actions.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is DFP to the choice of temporal offsets and the weighting of horizons? Did you try learned weights or exponentially decayed weights to better approximate discounted returns?
2) How large is the off-policy bias introduced by using a small replay buffer and a changing policy? Did you evaluate performance versus buffer size or more on-policy variants?
3) Can you report results with dueling DQN (and/or double/prioritized) and A3C-LSTM to isolate method vs architecture/memory effects?
4) How are measurement vectors chosen in practice? Could DFP be extended to predict learned latent “measurements” that are useful for multiple goals?
5) How does performance change as goals become non-linear in f (e.g., u(f; g) non-linear or constraints)? Any preliminary experiments?
6) What is the effect of much longer horizons or sparser measurement feedback? Does performance degrade gracefully, and could eligibility traces or n-step returns mitigate this?
7) Will you release code, trained models, and environment configs to facilitate reproducibility?
