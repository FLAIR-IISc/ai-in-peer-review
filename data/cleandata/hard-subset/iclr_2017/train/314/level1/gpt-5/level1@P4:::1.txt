1. Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning formulation of sensorimotor control that predicts future changes in vectorial “measurements” (e.g., health, ammo, frags) conditioned on current sensory observations, the candidate action, and a goal vector. Actions are selected by maximizing the inner product between the goal vector and the predicted future measurements.
- The approach enables dense supervision from the measurement stream rather than sparse scalar rewards, supports training without a fixed goal, and allows goal-conditioned control at test time.
- A specific neural architecture is presented that predicts action-conditioned measurement futures via separate expectation and action-difference streams, akin to dueling networks.
- Extensive experiments on ViZDoom (four scenarios, including complex battle tasks) show strong empirical performance, often substantially outperforming deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C; DSR partially) in both learning speed and final performance. The method also generalizes across textures and goals and won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition without extra supervision.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical evidence of substantial gains over competitive deep RL baselines in challenging 3D visuomotor tasks, with clear ablations and generalization studies.
- A conceptually simple, well-motivated shift from sparse scalar reward learning to dense vectorial measurement prediction with goal conditioning, advancing clarity and flexibility of goal-directed control.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: Learning sensorimotor control from raw high-dimensional sensory inputs in complex 3D environments, with the ability to pursue varying goals at test time.
- Motivation and placement: The paper convincingly motivates that dense, vectorial feedback enables supervised prediction to be effective and stable versus sparse-reward TD learning. It relates to Monte Carlo methods, multi-objective RL, UVFA, and successor representations. The distinctions—direct prediction of action-conditioned future measurements, explicit goal parameterization, and practical architecture for rich sensory inputs—are articulated and empirically validated.
- Evidence and rigor: 
  - Comparisons: DFP vs DQN, A3C across four scenarios; DSR on a simpler task due to runtime constraints. Training budgets and implementation details are described, and for A3C some hyperparameter search was performed. Results consistently favor DFP, especially on the complex battle tasks where vectorial measurements are richer.
  - Generalization: Cross-texture training/testing shows improved transfer when trained on diverse appearances; larger-capacity models further improve performance.
  - Goal-agnostic training: Models trained with randomized goals nearly match single-goal training on the main task and generalize better to new goals.
  - Ablations: Demonstrate benefits of multiple measurements and multiple temporal offsets, supporting the core hypothesis about dense multivariate supervision.
  - Competitive validation: Winning the Visual Doom AI competition adds external validation under unseen environments.
- Technical soundness: The learning objective (supervised regression to future measurement differences) is straightforward and consistent. The architecture is plausible and justified; normalization in the action stream mirrors dueling architectures. The training protocol with small replay and epsilon-greedy is standard. While no theoretical guarantees are provided, the empirical methodology is careful and the claims are supported by the experiments presented.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not necessarily part of the decision)
- Baseline parity and tuning: Provide more detail on compute budgets, wall-clock training time, and hyperparameter sweeps for all baselines. Include variance/error bars and multiple seeds for all methods in final evaluations to quantify statistical significance.
- Measurement design: Discuss how to select or construct measurement vectors in domains where informative intrinsic measurements are less readily available, and implications if measurements are partial or noisy.
- Horizon sensitivity: Analyze the effect of temporal offsets and loss weighting on performance and stability; report sensitivity to the choice of offsets and to using more than the last three for the loss.
- Action-space scalability: Clarify the number of composite actions in D3/D4 and discuss computational scaling when the discrete action set grows; outline strategies for large/continuous action spaces (e.g., action sampling, parametric policies, or actor-critic hybrids).
- Memory and partial observability: Since the model is reactive, include experiments with frame stacking or recurrent modules, and quantify gains on tasks requiring longer-term memory.
- Off-policy considerations: The method uses a small replay buffer; report whether larger buffers or prioritized sampling affect stability/performance.
- Reproducibility: Include precise architecture hyperparameters, optimizer settings, pre-processing, and training schedules in the main text or a consolidated appendix, and release code if possible.

Questions for the authors
- Measurement stream:
  - How sensitive is DFP to the choice and dimensionality of measurements? Have you tested with incomplete or noisy measurements, or with auxiliary learned measurements (e.g., learned features) as targets?
  - Can the method handle categorical measurements (beyond stating it could)? Any empirical results with mixed continuous/categorical targets?
- Temporal offsets and horizon:
  - What is the impact of extending the prediction horizon beyond 32 steps or adjusting the loss weights? How does performance degrade with shorter/longer horizons?
  - Did you try learning the offset weighting end-to-end?
- Action space:
  - What is the exact number of actions in each scenario (combinatorial sub-action count), and how does inference cost scale? Have you tried sub-action factorization in the predictor to reduce complexity?
  - Do you have preliminary results on continuous action domains or with action sampling during decision time?
- Baseline fairness:
  - For A3C, what network capacity and input preprocessing were used relative to DFP? Did you try multi-step returns or auxiliary tasks to match the dense supervision advantage?
  - Could you provide standard deviations and per-seed results for the final evaluations?
- Generalization:
  - In the texture-transfer experiments, do policies trained with randomized goals (during training) further improve cross-environment generalization versus fixed-goal training?
  - Beyond texture, have you tested layout changes or enemy behavior variations?
- Competition model:
  - What differences exist between the competition-winning model and the D4-tx-L setup (e.g., architecture, training schedule, offsets, goal distribution)?

Overall, the paper is clear, technically sound, and presents a notably novel and effective perspective on goal-conditioned control through direct prediction of vectorial futures.
