This paper presents Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a novel and effective approach for sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments. The core idea is to frame the control problem as supervised learning, where the agent learns to predict future changes in a low-dimensional "measurement" stream (e.g., health, ammunition) based on its high-dimensional sensory input (raw pixels) and a potential action. This formulation leverages the rich, dense supervisory signal from the environment's state variables, sidestepping the challenge of sparse rewards common in reinforcement learning. A key contribution is the model's ability to be trained without a fixed objective, allowing it to pursue dynamically specified goals at test time. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of their method in challenging scenarios using the ViZDoom platform, where it significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning baselines.

I strongly recommend to **Accept** this paper. The two key reasons for this decision are:
1. The proposed DFP formulation is a simple, elegant, and powerful alternative to standard reinforcement learning that is exceptionally well-suited for complex environments with rich, multi-dimensional feedback.
2. The claims are substantiated by extensive and rigorous experiments, including strong baseline comparisons, insightful ablation studies, and compelling external validation through a competition victory.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its effective reformulation of the control problem. By treating sensorimotor learning as a prediction task, it capitalizes on temporally dense, multivariate signals that are often available but collapsed into a single scalar reward in typical RL setups. The empirical evidence is convincing. DFP not only surpasses strong baselines like A3C by a large margin on complex "Battle" tasks but the authors also systematically validate their design choices. The ablation study clearly demonstrates that both vectorial feedback (vs. a scalar reward) and multi-step temporal predictions are critical to its success. Furthermore, the experiments on goal-agnostic training and generalization to unseen environments (validated by winning the Visual Doom AI Competition) provide powerful proof of the method's flexibility and robustness.

The following points are intended to help improve the paper further:
*   The paper acknowledges the distinction between the sensory and measurement stream is somewhat artificial. It would be beneficial to discuss the principles for selecting these measurements. How does one decide what should be predicted versus what should be conditioned upon, especially in novel environments?
*   The selection of temporal offsets for prediction (`τ`) appears to be a critical hyperparameter. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the model to these values or the intuition behind their selection would strengthen the paper.

I would appreciate clarification on the following points:
*   In the goal-agnostic training regime, is the randomly generated goal vector `g` provided as input to the policy for action selection during experience collection, or is it only used as an input when training the predictor `F` on the collected data?
*   Could you comment on the expected performance of DFP in environments where the measurement stream is much sparser, effectively approaching a traditional scalar reward? At what point would the advantages over a method like A3C diminish?
