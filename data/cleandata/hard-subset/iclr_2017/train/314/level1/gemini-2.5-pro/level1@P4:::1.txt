### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which reframes the problem as a supervised learning task. The agent learns to act by predicting the future values of a low-dimensional "measurement" stream (e.g., health, ammunition) based on its high-dimensional sensory input (raw pixels), current measurements, and a specified goal. The core idea is that the cotemporal structure of the sensory and measurement streams provides a dense and rich supervisory signal, which is used to train a deep network to predict the outcome of its actions. This formulation allows the agent to be trained without a fixed objective and to pursue dynamically changing goals at test time by selecting actions that are predicted to best satisfy the current goal. The authors conduct extensive experiments in challenging 3D environments based on the game Doom, demonstrating that their approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods like DQN and A3C. The paper further substantiates its claims with experiments showing effective generalization across different environments and goals, an ablation study highlighting the benefits of vectorial feedback, and a victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition, which serves as strong independent validation of the method's effectiveness.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Sound Formulation:** The paper proposes a simple yet powerful reformulation of the sensorimotor control problem. By framing it as direct prediction of future measurements under a supervised learning paradigm, it effectively leverages dense, multidimensional feedback signals that are common in immersive environments, moving beyond the often-sparse scalar rewards of traditional reinforcement learning. This is a conceptually significant and well-motivated contribution.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The claims are supported by a rigorous and extensive set of experiments. The proposed method is shown to substantially outperform strong, state-of-the-art baselines on a series of increasingly complex tasks. The paper's empirical support is further strengthened by thorough ablation studies, compelling generalization results across both environments and goals, and a decisive win in a competitive, public benchmark.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-placed within the literature, acknowledging early work on supervised learning for control (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992) and relating its goal-conditioned nature to more recent work like Universal Value Functions (Schaul et al., 2015). However, its specific formulation—learning to act by directly predicting a vector of future measurements conditioned on a goal—is novel. The distinction between a high-dimensional sensory stream and a lower-dimensional measurement stream is a key conceptual component that makes this prediction task tractable and effective.

The technical soundness of the work is demonstrated through its robust experimental methodology.
*   **Baselines and Scenarios:** The comparison against strong and widely-recognized deep RL methods (DQN, A3C) on four Doom scenarios of increasing complexity provides a convincing case for the proposed method's superiority, especially on the more challenging "Battle" tasks where DFP's advantage is most pronounced. The authors report a fair comparison process, including hyperparameter tuning for the baselines.
*   **Ablation Study:** The ablation study in Table 4 provides clear evidence for two of the paper's central claims: that using a vector of measurements is superior to a single scalar reward (even when the evaluation metric is that same scalar), and that predicting measurements at multiple future time steps is beneficial. These controlled experiments validate crucial design choices of the DFP model.
*   **Generalization:** The experiments on goal-agnostic training (Table 3) successfully demonstrate the model's ability to learn general skills that can be flexibly directed towards novel goals at test time. Furthermore, the experiments involving randomized textures (Table 2) show that the learned policies can generalize to visually distinct, unseen environments.
*   **Competition Result:** Winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition is a particularly strong piece of evidence. This success in unseen environments against other competitive agents provides external validation that the method is not just effective in a controlled research setting but is also robust and state-of-the-art in practice.

The network architecture is sensible, incorporating an effective technique from recent literature (the dueling network structure) to better distinguish the value of different actions. The clarity of the paper is high, with the model and experiments being well-described and the results clearly presented.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Clarification on Goal Vector Implementation:** The paper would benefit from a more concrete example of how goals are encoded in the vector `g`. For instance, in the goal-agnostic experiments (Table 3), how is a test-time goal like "Maximize frags" (a scalar objective) translated into the full goal vector `g` that has the same dimensionality as the concatenated future measurement differences `f`? A brief explanation would improve reader understanding of the practical implementation.
*   **Relationship to Model-Based RL:** The approach involves predicting the future consequences of actions, which is the defining characteristic of model-based RL. While DFP predicts only a small subset of the state (the measurements) rather than the full sensory input, a more explicit discussion positioning the work within the spectrum of model-free and model-based methods could strengthen the paper's theoretical framing.
*   **Dependence on Measurements:** The approach's success seems predicated on the availability of a meaningful, low-dimensional measurement stream. It would be valuable to briefly discuss the potential limitations of the approach in scenarios where such measurements are unavailable, noisy, or difficult to define. This would help delineate the problem class for which this method is most suitable.

### 5. Questions for Authors

*   The training loss in Equation 4 is a regression loss on the predicted future measurements `f_i`. The goal vector `g_i` is an input to the predictor `F` but does not appear in the loss function itself. Could you clarify the mechanism by which the network learns to use the goal `g` to modulate its predictions? Is the learning signal purely implicit, arising from the fact that training examples with different goals push the network to find a representation that disentangles goal- Mfrom state-action dynamics?
*   In the goal-agnostic training results (Table 3), the model trained with a fixed goal (regime 'a') still demonstrates some, albeit limited, ability to generalize when tested on different goals. Since the goal input `g` was constant during its training, how can it generalize at all? Does the goal module `G(g)` learn a trivial mapping in this case, and the generalization comes from the other modules, or is there another explanation?
