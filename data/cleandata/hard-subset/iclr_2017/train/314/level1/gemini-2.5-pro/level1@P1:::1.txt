### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a novel and effective approach to sensorimotor control in complex, immersive 3D environments. The core contribution is to frame the control problem as a supervised learning task rather than a traditional reinforcement learning one. The agent learns to predict a vector of future "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input (raw pixels), its current measurements, a goal specification, and a potential action. By learning this predictive model from its own experience, the agent can select actions that are predicted to best achieve its current goal. This formulation cleverly uses the rich, multi-dimensional data stream available in many environments as a dense supervisory signal, bypassing the need for a scalar reward function.

The key benefits demonstrated are threefold. First, the method excels in challenging 3D environments, significantly outperforming state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning baselines like A3C. Second, the model can be trained without a fixed goal, and at test time it can flexibly pursue dynamically specified goals it has never seen before. Third, the resulting policies demonstrate impressive generalization to entirely new environments. The strength of the approach is compellingly validated by winning the Full Deathmatch track of the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition, which was held in previously unseen environments.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper with a clear and well-executed core idea, supported by an exceptionally thorough and convincing set of experiments. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Elegant and Powerful Formulation:** The paper proposes a simple yet powerful alternative to the standard reinforcement learning paradigm. By reframing sensorimotor control as a supervised prediction problem, it elegantly sidesteps challenges associated with sparse rewards and value function instability. The ability to decouple policy learning from a fixed goal by parameterizing the goal as an input to the network is a significant advantage, enabling remarkable flexibility and generalization.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results and Rigorous Evaluation:** The empirical evidence supporting the paper's claims is comprehensive and compelling. The proposed DFP method is shown to substantially outperform strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C) on a suite of increasingly difficult 3D tasks. The claims of generalization are not just asserted but rigorously tested through controlled experiments on unseen environment textures and novel goals. The victory in the Visual Doom AI competition serves as an outstanding, unbiased validation of the method's effectiveness in a challenging, competitive setting.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strength lies in its clear motivation, sound methodology, and extensive experimental validation.

The motivation to move beyond a scalar reward to a richer, multi-dimensional measurement stream is well-argued and highly relevant for complex, interactive environments. The authors correctly identify that for an agent in an immersive world, feedback is often dense and multi-faceted, a fact that traditional RL formulations do not fully exploit. The proposed DFP method directly leverages this structure.

The experimental setup is exemplary. The authors evaluate their method on four Doom scenarios of increasing complexity, which clearly showcases the performance gap between DFP and baselines as the tasks become more challenging (Table 1, Figure 3). In the most complex "Battle" scenarios, DFP outperforms the next-best method (A3C) by a factor of 2.5-6x in terms of frags, a dramatic improvement.

The paper’s claims about generalization are robustly supported.
*   **Generalization across environments:** The experiments in Table 2, where models are trained on mazes with randomized textures and tested on unseen ones, provide direct evidence of generalization. This is further substantiated by the win in the Visual Doom AI competition's Full Deathmatch track, which explicitly tested generalization to unknown maps. This result is particularly impressive given that the second-place entry reportedly used a more complex recurrent model with additional supervision (ground-truth enemy detection).
*   **Generalization across goals:** The experiments in Table 3 are excellent. They show that a model trained on randomized goals can perform nearly as well on a specific task as a model trained specifically for that task. More importantly, it generalizes far better to novel goals, such as adopting a "pacifist" strategy, demonstrating that the agent has learned a flexible, goal-conditioned model of the environment's dynamics.

Finally, the ablation study (Table 4) is crucial, as it confirms the central hypotheses of the paper: using a vectorial measurement stream is significantly better than a scalar reward (even when the evaluation metric is scalar), and predicting measurements at multiple future time steps provides a richer learning signal.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Relation to Model-Based RL:** The approach has a strong model-based flavor, as it involves predicting future outcomes. However, it does not predict the full sensory state (`s_t+τ`), only the measurement vector (`m_t+τ`). It would be beneficial to include a short discussion positioning DFP on the spectrum between model-free and model-based methods. This would help readers better contextualize the work.
*   **Defining Measurements:** The distinction between the sensory stream `s_t` and the measurement stream `m_t` is described as "somewhat artificial". This is a key design choice. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the method to what is included in `m_t` would be valuable. For example, how would the system perform if some measurements were noisy or irrelevant? This could point towards interesting future work on learning which measurements are important to predict.
*   **Architecture Details:** The dueling-style architecture for splitting the prediction into expectation and action streams is an interesting choice. While the motivation is provided, a small ablation study on the impact of this specific architectural component could further strengthen the paper's claims about its importance.

### **Questions for the Authors**

Answering these questions would clarify some details and strengthen my confidence in the assessment.

1.  **Sensitivity to Temporal Offsets (τ):** The temporal offsets for future prediction (1, 2, 4, ..., 32 steps) seem to be a key hyperparameter. How sensitive is the performance to this specific choice of exponentially increasing offsets? Did you experiment with other schemes?
2.  **Long-Term Planning:** The model predicts up to 32 steps into the future. How do you see this approach handling tasks that require much longer-term planning, where the consequences of an action might not be reflected in the measurement stream until hundreds of steps later? Does this formulation have inherent limitations compared to value-based methods that can theoretically propagate information over arbitrary time horizons?
3.  **Goal Representation:** The goal vector `g` is concatenated with the sensory and measurement features early in the network. What was the rationale for this choice versus other ways of incorporating the goal, such as the multiplicative gating used in some conditional models or a Universal Value Function-style combination at a later stage?
