### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a novel and effective approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments. The core contribution is the reframing of the control problem as a supervised learning task, moving away from the conventional reinforcement learning (RL) paradigm of maximizing a scalar reward. The agent learns to predict the changes in a low-dimensional stream of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) at various future time steps, conditioned on its current high-dimensional sensory input (raw pixels), a potential action, and a goal representation. This goal is elegantly defined as a desired weighting over future measurements, allowing a single trained model to pursue dynamically changing objectives at test time without retraining. The authors provide compelling empirical validation in the challenging ViZDoom environment, demonstrating that their simpler DFP model significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods like A3C, especially on complex tasks. Furthermore, the paper shows strong generalization capabilities, both to novel goals specified at test time and to entirely new environments, culminating in a win at the Visual Doom AI Competition.

### **Decision: Accept**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. My decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  The paper presents an elegant and well-motivated alternative to standard value-based RL methods. By leveraging dense, multi-dimensional feedback streams, it effectively sidesteps the common challenge of sparse rewards in complex environments.
2.  The empirical results are exceptionally strong and rigorously presented. The comprehensive experiments, including superior performance over strong baselines, successful goal and environment generalization, and a victory in a competitive challenge, provide convincing evidence for the method's efficacy and robustness.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this work lies in its simple yet powerful formulation. Traditional RL methods often struggle with credit assignment when rewards are sparse and infrequent. This paper makes the insightful observation that many realistic environments provide a continuous, multi-faceted stream of information that can serve as a rich supervisory signal. By training the model to directly predict these future measurements (a regression task), the learning process becomes more stable and efficient, as demonstrated by the superior performance over A3C, particularly in the complex "Battle" scenarios (Table 1). The performance gap, with DFP outperforming A3C by a factor of 2.5-6 in frag count, is a testament to the benefits of this dense supervision.

A second major strength is the seamless integration of goal-conditioning. By including the goal vector `g` as an input to the network, the model learns a single, versatile policy that can be directed to pursue different objectives at test time. The experiments in Table 3 are particularly compelling: a model trained with randomized goals performs nearly as well on a specific task as a specialized model trained only for that task. More importantly, the goal-agnostic model generalizes far better to novel objectives, such as behaving like a "pacifist." This capability to learn general skills that can be flexibly deployed is a significant step forward for creating more adaptable agents.

Finally, the empirical validation is thorough and convincing. The authors not only compare against relevant and strong baselines (DQN, A3C) but also conduct well-designed experiments to support each of their core claims. The ablation study (Table 4) clearly demonstrates the value of using both vectorial feedback over a scalar signal and predicting at multiple temporal horizons. The most impressive result is the victory in the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition. Winning a competition in previously unseen environments against other state-of-the-art agents is a powerful, independent validation of the model's generalization capabilities and practical effectiveness, lending significant weight to the paper's claims.

### **Additional Feedback and Suggestions for Improvement**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Defining Measurements vs. Sensory Input:** The paper notes the distinction between sensory input `s_t` and measurements `m_t` is "somewhat artificial." While the operational difference is clear, a more principled discussion on how one would make this distinction in a new domain would be valuable. Is it primarily about dimensionality, semantic content, or the ability to define goals over them? Expanding on this could help others apply the DFP framework more broadly.
*   **Exploration:** The agent uses a standard ε-greedy exploration strategy. Given that the model predicts outcomes for all actions, it seems well-suited for more sophisticated exploration techniques, such as those based on uncertainty or disagreement in the predicted futures. A brief discussion of this potential extension would be welcome.
*   **Scope and Limitations:** The work is demonstrated in a simulated environment where clean measurements are readily available. It would strengthen the paper to briefly discuss the challenges and potential adaptations required to apply DFP in real-world settings (e.g., robotics), where measurements might be noisy, partially observable, or need to be learned from raw sensory data themselves.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points to solidify my understanding:
1.  **Goal Representation:** The goal vector `g` is used as an input to the network `F` and is also used to select the best action via the dot product `g^T * F(...)`. This design choice seems to imply the network is trained to predict future measurements in a way that is already aligned with the goal objective. Could you elaborate on the intuition here? Have you explored training a goal-agnostic predictor `F(o_t, a; θ)` and only applying the goal at test time via `argmax_a g^T * F(o_t, a; θ)`?
2.  **Training Stability:** The paper mentions that the agent's policy is updated every `k` new experiences. What value of `k` was used, and how sensitive is the training process to this parameter?
3.  **Temporal Offsets:** The set of future time steps `τ` for prediction seems to have been chosen manually. How sensitive is the final performance to this choice? For example, would performance degrade significantly if only longer-term horizons were used for prediction?
