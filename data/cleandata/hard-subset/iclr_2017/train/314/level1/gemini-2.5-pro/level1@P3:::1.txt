### **Review of "Sensorimotor Control in Immersive Environments"**

**1. Summary of Claims and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel and compelling approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which reframes the problem as a supervised learning task. Instead of relying on a scalar reward signal as is common in reinforcement learning, the agent leverages a high-dimensional sensory stream (raw pixels) and a lower-dimensional, multi-variate measurement stream (e.g., health, ammunition). The core idea is to train a deep network to predict the changes in future measurements for each possible action, conditioned on the current sensory input, current measurements, and a parameterized goal vector. At test time, the agent simply selects the action predicted to yield the best outcome according to its current goal.

The key contributions are:
1.  A new formulation for learning control that uses the rich, temporally dense signal from multi-dimensional measurements as direct supervision, moving away from the sparse rewards that often challenge traditional RL.
2.  A flexible framework that decouples the agent's world knowledge (predicting future measurements) from its objectives, enabling it to be trained without a fixed goal and to pursue dynamically specified goals at test time.
3.  Strong empirical validation in the complex 3D environment of Doom, demonstrating that DFP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL baselines like A3C and DQN, especially on more complex tasks.
4.  Demonstration of impressive generalization capabilities, both to unseen visual environments (validated by winning the Visual Doom AI Competition) and to novel goals not encountered during training.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is an excellent paper presenting a simple, elegant, and highly effective idea with strong empirical backing.

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Formulation:** The paper's main strength lies in its reformulation of the control problem. By leveraging intrinsic, multi-dimensional measurements as a dense supervisory signal, it provides a powerful alternative to standard RL that is particularly well-suited to complex, immersive environments where such measurements are naturally available. This approach neatly sidesteps many of the credit assignment and sample-efficiency issues associated with sparse rewards.

2.  **Exceptional Empirical Results and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental results are comprehensive and highly convincing. The proposed DFP method doesn't just incrementally improve upon strong baselines like A3C; it outperforms them by a very large margin (e.g., a 6x increase in frags in the "Battle" scenario). The success is further validated by a win in a public competition in unseen environments, extensive ablation studies confirming the importance of the core ideas (vectorial feedback, multiple time horizons), and a compelling demonstration of zero-shot generalization to novel goals.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The decision to accept is based on the significant conceptual contribution and its thorough empirical validation.

The conceptual shift from value-function or policy optimization to direct future prediction is a significant one. The authors correctly position this in the historical context of SL vs. RL for control (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992) and argue convincingly that for environments rich in feedback, direct prediction is a more natural fit. The formulation where an action `a_t` is chosen by `argmax_a g^T F(o_t, a, g; θ)` is particularly elegant, as it cleanly separates the learned model `F` (which predicts what will happen) from the goal `g` (which specifies what the agent wants to happen). This decoupling is the key to the impressive goal-generalization results shown in Table 3, where an agent trained on randomized goals can instantly adopt novel behaviors like "pacifist" or "ammo-waster" at test time. This is a significant step towards more flexible and general AI agents.

The experimental section is exemplary. The comparison against strong, well-tuned baselines (A3C, DQN) on a series of increasingly difficult tasks clearly establishes the superiority of the proposed method. The ablation study in Table 4 provides crucial scientific support for the central claims: both the use of vectorial feedback over a scalar reward and the prediction of multiple future time steps are shown to be critical for performance. This rigor adds significant weight to the paper's conclusions. Finally, the victory in the Visual Doom AI competition serves as a powerful, unbiased external validation of the method's robustness and generalization ability, lending the work a level of real-world credibility that is often missing in purely academic benchmarks.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended as constructive suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not critical to the acceptance decision.

*   **Connection to Model-Based RL:** The paper frames the approach as "supervised learning for control," which is accurate. However, it bears a strong resemblance to a specific form of model-based RL. The predictor `F` is essentially learning a dynamics model in the space of measurements, i.e., `p(m_{t+τ} | s_t, m_t, a)`. Explicitly discussing this connection would better situate the work within the broader RL literature and could highlight its relationship to other model-based approaches, potentially clarifying its advantages (e.g., avoiding the difficulty of predicting high-dimensional pixel spaces by focusing on a compact, task-relevant measurement space).
*   **Stability of Training:** The paper mentions that using an experience memory (an off-policy component) had no adverse effects. This is an interesting observation, as off-policy learning can be unstable for many RL algorithms. It would be valuable to speculate on why DFP might be more stable. Is it because the regression targets are grounded in actual, observed future measurements, rather than bootstrapped from the network's own, possibly inaccurate, value estimates (as in Q-learning)? A brief discussion of this point would be insightful.
*   **Linearity of Goal Representation:** The utility function is assumed to be linear (`g^T f`). While effective, it would be useful to discuss the implications of this assumption. How might the framework be extended to handle non-linear objectives (e.g., maintaining health above a certain threshold)? Does feeding `g` as an input to the network `F` allow it to learn non-linear interactions even if the final action selection uses a linear dot product? A little more intuition on this aspect would be welcome.

**5. Questions for Authors**

*   Could you provide more intuition on the role of the goal vector `g` during training? Since `g` is an input to the network `F`, the network learns to make predictions that are conditioned on the goal. However, the loss function is a simple regression error `||F - f||^2` which does not explicitly depend on `g`. How does the network learn to correctly interpret `g` such that the `argmax_a g^T F(...)` operation at test time yields goal-directed behavior?
*   In the goal-agnostic training experiments (Table 3), the models trained with randomized goals show remarkable generalization but are slightly outperformed on the primary task (`(0.5, 0.5, 1)`) by the specialist model trained only on that goal. Do you view this as a fundamental trade-off between specialization and generalization, or do you believe this performance gap could be closed with, for instance, a larger model capacity or more diverse goal sampling during training?
