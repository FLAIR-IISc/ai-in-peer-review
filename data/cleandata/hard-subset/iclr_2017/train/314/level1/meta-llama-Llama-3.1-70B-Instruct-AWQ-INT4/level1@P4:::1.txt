**Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions**

This paper presents an approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, which utilizes a high-dimensional sensory stream and a lower-dimensional measurement stream. The authors propose a novel formulation that reduces sensorimotor control to supervised learning, without the need for extraneous supervision. The approach is based on the idea that the cotemporal structure of the sensory and measurement streams provides a rich supervisory signal, enabling the training of a sensorimotor control model. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through extensive experiments in three-dimensional simulations, using the classical first-person game Doom. The results show that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods, particularly on challenging tasks, and that the trained models generalize across environments and goals.

**Decision: Accept**

The main reasons for this decision are:

1. **Well-motivated approach**: The authors provide a clear and well-motivated formulation of the sensorimotor control problem, which is grounded in the idea of utilizing the cotemporal structure of the sensory and measurement streams as a supervisory signal.
2. **Strong empirical results**: The authors present extensive experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach in outperforming state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods, particularly on challenging tasks.

**Supporting Arguments for the Reasons**

The authors' approach is well-motivated because it:

* Provides a clear and intuitive formulation of the sensorimotor control problem, which is grounded in the idea of utilizing the cotemporal structure of the sensory and measurement streams as a supervisory signal.
* Departs from the traditional reward-based formalization commonly used in reinforcement learning, and instead uses a more general and flexible framework that can handle multiple goals and measurements.

The empirical results presented by the authors are strong because they:

* Demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach in outperforming state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods, particularly on challenging tasks.
* Show that the trained models generalize across environments and goals, which is a critical aspect of sensorimotor control.

**Additional Feedback and Suggestions**
