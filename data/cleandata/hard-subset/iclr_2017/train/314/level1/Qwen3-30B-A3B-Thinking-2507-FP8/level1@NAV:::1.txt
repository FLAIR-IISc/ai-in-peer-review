

This paper presents an innovative approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments that leverages both high-dimensional sensory streams and lower-dimensional measurement streams to provide rich supervisory signals. The authors propose a framework where learning is framed as supervised prediction of future measurements rather than traditional reinforcement learning with sparse rewards. The key contribution is demonstrating that this approach enables learning from raw sensory input without extraneous supervision, supports training without fixed goals, and allows flexible goal pursuit at test time. The authors validate their approach through extensive experiments in the Doom environment, showing superior performance compared to state-of-the-art deep RL methods, particularly on complex tasks. Their model won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition in previously unseen environments.

I recommend acceptance with the following key reasons: (1) The approach is well-motivated and properly situated within the literature, clearly addressing limitations of traditional RL in complex environments; (2) The experimental results are scientifically rigorous, with thorough comparisons to strong baselines, demonstration of generalization across environments, and validation through a major competition.

The paper provides compelling evidence that vectorial measurements provide a significantly richer training signal than scalar rewards, as demonstrated through ablation studies showing the advantage of predicting multiple measurements at multiple temporal offsets. The authors' demonstration of goal-agnostic training and successful generalization to new goals is particularly impressive.

To further strengthen the paper, I suggest clarifying the relationship between this work and the recent "successor representations" literature (Kulkarni et al., 2016b), as the authors cite this work but don't explicitly connect their framework to it. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed approach compared to standard RL methods.

I would like to ask the authors: How does your approach handle environments where the measurement stream is not explicitly provided by the environment (e.g., where measurements must be inferred from sensory input)? And could you provide more insight into why the randomized goal training regime (b,c) outperforms fixed goal training (a) in the goal-agnostic experiments?
