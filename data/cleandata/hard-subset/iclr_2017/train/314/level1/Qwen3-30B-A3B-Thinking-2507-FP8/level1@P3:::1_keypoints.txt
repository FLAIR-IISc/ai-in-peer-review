- Proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), reframing RL as supervised learning by predicting future low-dimensional measurements from raw sensory input, actions, and goals.  
- Demonstrates DFP outperforms DQN, A3C, and DSR on ViZDoom 3D tasks and won the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch by >50% over a strong baseline.  
- Shows DFP can train without a fixed goal and generalizes to new goals at test time.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Strength: Addresses learning from raw sensory input and flexible, goal-specified skills with a well-motivated, literature-grounded approach.  
- Strength: Rigorous experimental evaluation across four scenarios with clear empirical advantages, especially on complex tasks (D3, D4).  
- Strength: Simple method needing no reward shaping or complex architectures; ablations show vectorial feedback outperforms scalar rewards.  
- Strength: Aligns with prediction-based RL trends while offering a simpler direct formulation.  
- Main limitation: Lacks theoretical analysis explaining why DFP outperforms standard RL; needs discussion of limitations and failure cases.  
- Feedback: Provide more detail/justification for architecture choices (expectation vs action streams) and the normalization layer.  
- Feedback: Clarify relationship to predictive state representations (PSRs) and how DFP differs or relates.  
- Feedback: Clarify the claim of training "without extraneous supervision" given use of ViZDoom internal measurements (health, ammo, frags).  
- Feedback: Ablation study limited to Doom—discuss whether vectorial feedback benefits generalize to other domains with harder-to-obtain measurements.  
- Question: Why does goal-agnostic training with goal vectors sampled from [-1,1] perform worse than [0,1], and what are the practical implications?  
- Question: How might the approach evolve to incorporate full sensory-stream prediction (given recent advances) while retaining measurement-stream benefits?  
- Question: How would DFP perform in domains where meaningful measurements are hard to define or measurement-goal relationships are complex?
