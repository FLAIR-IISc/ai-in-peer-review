

# Peer Review

## 1. Summary of Paper Claims and Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control that reframes learning to act as supervised learning rather than reinforcement learning. The authors propose leveraging the cotemporal structure of sensory and measurement streams to provide rich, dense supervision for training. Their approach, called Direct Future Prediction (DFP), uses raw sensory input (e.g., visual input from a 3D environment) along with a lower-dimensional measurement stream (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) to predict future measurements based on current inputs and goals. The key contributions include: (1) a simple yet effective formulation for sensorimotor control that avoids sparse scalar rewards; (2) strong empirical results demonstrating superior performance over state-of-the-art deep RL methods (DQN, A3C, DSR) across increasingly complex Doom scenarios; (3) evidence of generalization across environments and goals without retraining; and (4) winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming a more complex baseline by over 50% in previously unseen environments.

## 2. Decision

Accept. This paper presents a well-motivated, rigorously evaluated approach that makes a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the approach is properly positioned within the literature and offers a compelling alternative to traditional RL when rich sensory feedback is available; (2) the empirical results are robust, showing consistent performance improvements across multiple challenging tasks, with strong evidence of generalization and goal flexibility.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's motivation is particularly strong. It correctly identifies a key limitation of traditional RL: the reliance on sparse scalar rewards that may not reflect the natural learning environment of organisms. By leveraging the inherent structure of sensory and measurement streams, the authors present a theoretically sound alternative that is grounded in decades of prior work on supervised learning for control (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992) while making a clear distinction from Monte Carlo methods (Sutton, 1995) through their specific formulation.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors evaluate their approach across four increasingly challenging Doom scenarios, comparing against multiple strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR). The results consistently show DFP outperforming baselines, with particularly dramatic improvements in the more complex Battle scenarios where multiple measurements are available. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the importance of vectorial feedback and multiple temporal offsets. The Visual Doom competition win provides compelling external validation of the approach's effectiveness.

The paper also makes a significant contribution to the understanding of how to effectively utilize dense sensory feedback. The goal-agnostic training experiments are particularly compelling, demonstrating that models trained without fixed goals can generalize to new goals with minimal performance loss, which is a major advantage over traditional RL approaches.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, these improvements would make it even better:

- The connection to predictive state representations (PSR) could be strengthened. The paper mentions PSR in the background but doesn't explicitly position DFP within this framework. A clearer articulation of how DFP relates to or extends PSR would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

- The limitations section could be expanded. While the paper notes the model is purely reactive, it doesn't discuss specific scenarios where this limitation would be problematic or how future work could address it (e.g., through memory mechanisms).

- The architecture details for the prediction module could be clearer. The normalization layer in equation (6) is critical to the approach but could be explained more thoroughly to help readers understand why it's necessary and how it improves performance.

- The paper mentions the model was trained using "raw experience" but doesn't explicitly state whether the training was completely unsupervised (only using experience collected by the agent) or whether any human demonstrations were used. Clarifying this would strengthen the claim of "without extraneous supervision."

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that the measurement stream "provides rich and temporally dense supervision" while traditional RL uses "occasional scalar reward." Could the authors provide a more precise characterization of the information-theoretic advantage of the measurement stream over scalar rewards in their specific setting?

2. In the goal-agnostic training experiments (Table 3), models trained with goals from [-1, 1] (regime c) generally outperformed those trained with goals from [0, 1] (regime b) on the pacifist test goal. Why did this occur, and what does it suggest about the relationship between the training distribution and the model's ability to generalize to goal directions?

3. The paper mentions that the measurement stream is "lower-dimensional" than the sensory stream. Could the authors provide specific dimensionality numbers for their experiments (e.g., the number of measurements versus pixels in the image) to help readers better understand the scope of the problem?

4. The paper notes that the approach "does not require a fixed goal at training time, and pursuing dynamically changing goals at test time." Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of this for real-world applications where goals may change frequently during deployment?
