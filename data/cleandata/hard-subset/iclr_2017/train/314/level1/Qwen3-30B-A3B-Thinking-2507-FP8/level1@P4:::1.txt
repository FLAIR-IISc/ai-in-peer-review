

1. Summary: The paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments that frames learning to act as a supervised learning problem rather than reinforcement learning. The approach leverages both high-dimensional sensory input (e.g., visual input) and lower-dimensional measurements (e.g., health, ammunition levels) to create a rich supervisory signal. The authors demonstrate that predicting future measurements based on current sensory input, measurements, and goals enables effective control without requiring a fixed goal during training or extraneous supervision. The method achieves state-of-the-art performance in complex 3D environments based on the Doom game, outperforming sophisticated deep RL models, and successfully generalizes across environments and goals. The model won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming the second-best submission by over 50%.

2. Decision: Accept. The paper presents a well-motivated approach with strong empirical evidence supporting its claims. The technical approach is sound, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods in challenging environments.

3. Supporting arguments: The paper effectively addresses a key challenge in RL - learning sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex environments. The authors provide a thorough literature review that places their work within the context of supervised learning, reinforcement learning, and related approaches. Their formulation using vectorial measurements rather than scalar rewards is well-justified and supported by a compelling argument about the natural structure of immersive environments. The experiments are comprehensive, with four increasingly difficult scenarios in the Doom environment, ablation studies demonstrating the importance of vectorial feedback, and generalization tests across different environments. The results consistently show that the proposed method outperforms strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) in complex tasks, with the most significant advantage appearing in scenarios with multiple measurements. The competition win provides strong real-world validation of the approach. The paper provides sufficient technical detail about the model architecture and training procedure to be reproducible.

4. Additional feedback: The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of the normalization layer in the action stream (Equation 6), including why it's necessary and how it impacts learning dynamics. The authors could provide more insight into the limitations of their purely reactive model (e.g., how it might perform in tasks requiring longer-term planning) and potential extensions to address this. The discussion of why vectorial measurements outperform scalar rewards could be strengthened with more theoretical insight beyond the empirical results.

5. Questions for authors:
- Could you provide more intuition about why the normalization layer (Equation 6) is necessary and how it improves the model's ability to distinguish between action outcomes?
- In the competition, you mentioned using a "variant of the D4-tx-L regime." Could you clarify what specific modifications were made to the standard D4-tx training setup for this competition entry?
- The paper claims to operate without "extraneous supervision," but the measurements (health, ammo, etc.) are provided to the model. How do you distinguish these from "extraneous" supervision, and would the approach work with only raw sensory input (without any measurements)?
- The paper demonstrates generalization across environments and goals, but could you elaborate on the practical limits of goal flexibility (e.g., are there specific goal types that the model struggles with)?
