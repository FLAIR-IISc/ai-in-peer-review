

# Peer Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control that reframes reinforcement learning as a supervised learning problem. The authors propose using a high-dimensional sensory stream (raw visual input) and a lower-dimensional measurement stream (agent state information) to provide rich, temporally dense supervision for learning to act. The key insight is that the cotemporal structure of these streams enables training a sensorimotor control model without requiring a scalar reward signal. The approach, termed "Direct Future Prediction" (DFP), trains a model to predict future measurements based on current sensory input, actions, and goals. The authors demonstrate that DFP outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods (DQN, A3C, DSR) on challenging 3D environments in the ViZDoom platform, particularly when multiple measurements are available. The approach also enables training without a fixed goal and generalizes to new goals at test time. Notably, a DFP model won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming a strong baseline by over 50%.

## 2. Decision

Accept with minor revisions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a compelling contribution that addresses two key challenges in RL: learning from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments and acquiring flexible skills for dynamically specified goals. The core insight is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature, building on Jordan & Rumelhart (1992) and Sutton (1988) while addressing modern RL challenges. The experimental evaluation is rigorous and thorough, with careful comparisons to multiple strong baselines across four increasingly difficult scenarios. The results demonstrate clear advantages of the approach, particularly on complex tasks (D3 and D4), where DFP outperforms A3C by factors of 6 and 2.5. The generalization experiments across environments and goal-agnostic training results further strengthen the claim that the approach provides a more robust and flexible framework for sensorimotor control.

The paper's key strengths include its simplicity (no need for reward shaping or complex architectures), the clear empirical demonstration of superiority over baselines, and the successful competition results. The authors convincingly show that vectorial feedback provides a significant advantage over scalar rewards, as evidenced by the ablation studies. The approach also aligns with the broader trend of using prediction-based methods in RL (Oh et al., 2015; Kulkarni et al., 2016b) while offering a simpler, more direct formulation.

The main limitation is the lack of theoretical analysis explaining why the approach works better than standard RL, though this is understandable given the empirical nature of the contribution. The paper could benefit from more explicit discussion of potential limitations and failure cases.

## 4. Additional Feedback

- The paper should provide more detail on the architecture choices in Section 3.2, particularly why the network splits into expectation and action streams. A brief justification of the normalization layer would help readers understand its importance.

- The discussion of the relationship to predictive state representations (PSRs) could be strengthened. While the paper mentions PSRs in the background, it doesn't clearly position how DFP relates to or differs from this established framework.

- The paper claims to train "without extraneous supervision," but the use of the ViZDoom platform's internal measurements (health, ammo, frags) could be seen as a form of supervision. Clarify how this differs from the "extraneous supervision" the authors claim to avoid.

- The ablation study (Table 4) is limited to the Doom environment. It would strengthen the contribution to discuss whether the benefits of vectorial feedback would generalize to other domains where measurements might be less readily available.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In the goal-agnostic training experiments (Table 3), the model trained with goal vectors sampled from [-1,1] (regime c) performs slightly worse than regime b (sourced from [0,1]). Can you provide more insight into why this might be the case, and how this affects practical implementation?

2. The paper states that "predicting full sensory streams is beyond our capabilities" but cites recent work (Kalchbrenner et al., 2016; van den Oord et al., 2016) that has made significant progress in this area. How do you envision your approach evolving to incorporate full sensory prediction while maintaining the benefits of the measurement stream?

3. The paper demonstrates strong performance on the Doom environment but doesn't address whether the approach would work on tasks where meaningful measurements are difficult to define. Could you discuss potential challenges in domains where measurements aren't readily available or where the relationship between measurements and goals is complex?
