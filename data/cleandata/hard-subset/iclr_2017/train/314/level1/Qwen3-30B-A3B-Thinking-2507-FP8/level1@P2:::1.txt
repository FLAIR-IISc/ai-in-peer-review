

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments by leveraging the cotemporal structure between high-dimensional sensory streams and lower-dimensional measurement streams. The authors propose a framework that transforms sensorimotor control into a supervised learning problem, where the agent predicts future measurements based on current sensory input, measurements, and a goal. The key contribution is demonstrating that this simple approach outperforms sophisticated reinforcement learning methods on complex 3D tasks, generalizes across environments and goals, and won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition. The paper makes a compelling case that vectorial measurements provide richer supervision than scalar rewards, enabling more effective learning in complex environments.

## 2. Decision

Accept.

The paper makes a strong case for a novel and effective approach to sensorimotor control that is both theoretically sound and empirically validated. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the paper provides a clear, well-motivated alternative to traditional reinforcement learning that addresses important limitations in the field, and (2) the empirical results are compelling, scientifically rigorous, and demonstrate significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution is well-motivated. The authors clearly identify two key challenges in sensorimotor control: learning from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments and acquiring generalizable skills that can flexibly handle dynamically specified goals. They position their work as a departure from traditional reward-based RL, arguing that the measurement stream provides richer, temporally dense supervision compared to sparse scalar rewards. This is well-supported by the literature review, which cites Jordan & Rumelhart (1992) and Sutton (1988) as foundational work that suggests supervised learning may be more appropriate when dense multidimensional feedback is available.

The paper's empirical evaluation is thorough and scientifically rigorous. The authors conducted extensive experiments across four increasingly challenging Doom-based scenarios, comparing their approach (DFP) to three strong baselines (DQN, A3C, and DSR). The results consistently show DFP outperforming these baselines, with particularly significant improvements in more complex scenarios (D3 and D4), where DFP outperforms A3C by factors of 6 and 2.5 respectively. The ablation study further supports the importance of using vectorial measurements rather than scalar rewards, and the generalization experiments across environments demonstrate the approach's robustness.

The Visual Doom AI Competition win provides strong real-world validation. The fact that their model won against a strong baseline (Lample & Chaplot, 2016) that used additional supervision from the game engine without such assistance is particularly compelling evidence of the approach's effectiveness.

The paper is well-placed in the literature. It correctly situates its work in relation to prior efforts in supervised learning for sensorimotor control, vector-valued feedback, and goal-parameterized learning. The authors effectively connect their approach to Schaul et al. (2015) while highlighting their specific contributions in handling realistic sensory and measurement streams.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance its impact:

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach. For instance, how does the method scale to environments with significantly more complex sensory inputs (e.g., full 3D point clouds rather than 2D images)? What are the specific constraints on the types of measurements that can be used effectively?

2. The training procedure involves a small experience memory and off-policy training, but the authors don't thoroughly explore how sensitive their results are to these hyperparameters. A brief sensitivity analysis would strengthen the claims.

3. The paper could more explicitly contrast the computational efficiency of their approach with traditional RL methods. The training speed is mentioned in Table 1, but a more detailed comparison would help readers understand the practical advantages.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper mentions that the approach is "purely reactive" with "no explicit facilities for memory." How might incorporating memory mechanisms (e.g., LSTMs or other recurrent structures) further improve performance, and what would be the trade-offs in terms of model complexity and training efficiency?

2. In the goal-agnostic training experiments, the authors show that models trained with random goal vectors generalize better. However, the paper doesn't explain why this is the case. Could the authors provide more theoretical insight into how training with diverse goals leads to better generalization?

3. The paper claims that "the measurement stream provides rich and temporally dense supervision that can stabilize and accelerate training." Could the authors provide more specific evidence about how the training dynamics differ between their approach and traditional RL methods (e.g., learning curves, variance in performance)?
