This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, framing the problem as supervised learning rather than traditional reinforcement learning. The core idea is to leverage the rich supervisory signal present in the cotemporal structure of high-dimensional sensory streams and lower-dimensional measurement streams. By predicting the effect of actions on future measurements, the agent learns to act without explicit rewards, demonstrating impressive performance – even winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is both conceptually insightful and empirically successful, offering a compelling alternative to standard RL methods, particularly in complex, visually-rich environments. The strong results, including winning a competitive AI challenge, provide convincing evidence for the efficacy of the proposed method.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of traditional RL in complex sensorimotor tasks and positioning their approach as a solution. The experimental setup is thorough, utilizing the challenging Doom environment and comparing against state-of-the-art baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR). The results consistently demonstrate superior performance, especially in scenarios with richer measurement streams. The ablation studies further strengthen the argument for the importance of multivariate feedback and multi-step prediction. The generalization experiments are also a strong point, showing the model's ability to adapt to unseen environments.

However, a few points could be addressed to further improve the paper. The architecture of the predictor network (Figure 1) is described but could benefit from more detail regarding layer sizes and activation functions. While the authors mention the use of mini-batches, the batch size isn’t specified. Further clarification on the hyperparameter tuning process for the baselines would also be helpful. Finally, while the paper acknowledges limitations, a discussion of potential failure modes or scenarios where this approach might *not* be suitable would be valuable. 

I would like to ask the authors: Could you elaborate on the choice of temporal offsets (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32)? Was this determined empirically, or is there a theoretical justification for these specific values? Also, how sensitive is the performance to the choice of the goal weighting coefficients (e.g., 0.5, 0.5, 1)?
