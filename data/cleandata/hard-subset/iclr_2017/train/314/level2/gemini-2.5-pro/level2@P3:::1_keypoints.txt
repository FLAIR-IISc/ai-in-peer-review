- Proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): reframe control as supervised learning by predicting future multi-dimensional measurements from sensory input, goal, and action.  
- Uses dense, self-generated supervisory signal instead of sparse scalar rewards.  
- Supports goal-agnostic training to learn general environment dynamics usable for novel, test-time goals.  
- Validated extensively in Doom 3D environment, outperforming strong RL baselines (DQN, A3C) on complex tasks.  
- Demonstrates generalization across environments and to goals not seen during training.  
- Real-world validation: won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Elegant, well-motivated supervised alternative to standard RL that addresses sparse scalar-reward limitations.  
- Key reason 2: Exceptionally thorough and convincing experiments, including rigorous baselines, generalization tests, and external competition success.  
- Conceptual strength: simplicity and ability to avoid credit-assignment and instability issues of value-based RL.  
- Empirical strength: substantial performance gains, especially in complex "Battle" scenarios, supporting the benefit of vectorial measurements.  
- Goal-agnostic training shows near-specialist performance on specific tasks while enabling pursuit of different/opposing goals.  
- Ablation study confirms importance of vectorial feedback over scalar signals and multi-horizon prediction.  
- Paper quality: well-written, clearly structured, and well-situated in literature.  
- Suggestion: discuss connections to model-based RL, highlighting prediction of low-dimensional measurements vs. full state models.  
- Suggestion: provide more implementation details on the goal-conditioning module (how goal vector is processed and integrated).  
- Suggestion: discuss sensitivity and rationale for chosen temporal prediction offsets (1,2,4,8,16,32).  
- Suggestion: acknowledge dependence on pre-defined measurement stream and speculate on extensions where measurements must be learned.
