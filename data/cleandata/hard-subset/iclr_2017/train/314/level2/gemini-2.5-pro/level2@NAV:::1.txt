This paper presents Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a novel approach for sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments. The core contribution is framing the control problem as supervised learning, departing from traditional reinforcement learning formulations. The agent learns to predict future changes in a low-dimensional "measurement" stream (e.g., health, ammo) based on its high-dimensional sensory input (raw pixels), a potential action, and a goal vector. By selecting actions that lead to the best-predicted future according to its current goal, the agent can be trained without a fixed reward function and can adapt to dynamically changing objectives at test time. The authors claim this method outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL baselines in complex tasks and generalizes effectively to new environments and goals.

I recommend this paper for acceptance. The two primary reasons are: 1) The proposed method demonstrates a significant and well-supported performance improvement over strong, established baselines in challenging, dynamic environments. 2) The work convincingly addresses the important problem of goal-agnostic learning, showing that the agent can learn general skills and adapt to novel goals not seen during training.

The empirical evaluation strongly supports these claims. In complex "Battle" scenarios within the Doom environment, DFP outperforms A3C by a factor of 2.5-6x in terms of frags, a remarkable improvement. The method's success in winning the Visual Doom AI Competition, which was held in unseen environments, provides compelling real-world evidence of its robustness and generalization capabilities. Furthermore, the experiments on goal-agnostic training clearly show that a model trained with randomized goals performs nearly as well on a specific task as a specialized model, while generalizing far better to novel objectives. The ablation study effectively isolates the benefits of using multi-dimensional measurements over a single scalar reward, reinforcing the paper’s core hypothesis.

For future improvement, the authors could elaborate on the conceptual distinction between "sensory input" and "measurements." While functionally clear, a more principled discussion on how to define this split in new domains would be valuable. The discussion could also benefit from exploring the limitations of a purely reactive model in tasks that require long-term planning or memory.

I have a couple of questions for the authors to clarify my understanding:
1. The training process uses an experience memory, which departs from pure on-policy methods. How sensitive is the model's performance to the size of this memory and the frequency of policy updates?
2. The current goal formulation is linear (`g>f`). Have you considered non-linear goal functions, and what new types of behaviors or complex objectives might they enable?
