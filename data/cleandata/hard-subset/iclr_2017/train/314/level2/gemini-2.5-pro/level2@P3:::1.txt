**Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel approach for learning sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex, immersive environments. The core idea is to reframe the control problem as a supervised learning task. Instead of relying on a scalar reward signal, the agent learns to predict future values of a multi-dimensional measurement stream (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input, measurements, goal, and a potential action. This formulation provides a dense and rich supervisory signal that is generated directly from the agent's interaction with the environment. A key advantage of this framework is its ability to support goal-agnostic training, allowing the agent to learn a general model of its environment's dynamics that can be flexibly applied to pursue novel, dynamically specified goals at test time.

The work provides extensive empirical validation in the challenging 3D environment of Doom. The proposed method, Direct Future Prediction (DFP), is shown to significantly outperform strong deep reinforcement learning baselines, including DQN and A3C, particularly on complex tasks involving combat and navigation. The paper further demonstrates the model's ability to generalize across different environments and to adapt to a variety of goals it was not explicitly trained for. The effectiveness of the approach is underscored by its victory in the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, where it was deployed in previously unseen environments.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

Decision: **Accept**

This is a strong paper with a clear and compelling contribution. The decision to accept is based on two key reasons:
1.  The work proposes an elegant and well-motivated alternative to the standard reinforcement learning paradigm for control in environments with rich, multi-dimensional feedback. The supervised learning formulation is simple, effective, and directly addresses the limitations of sparse scalar rewards.
2.  The claims are substantiated by an exceptionally thorough and convincing set of experiments. The rigorous comparison against state-of-the-art methods, the demonstration of generalization across both environments and goals, and the external validation from a competition win provide strong evidence for the method's effectiveness.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main strength lies in its conceptual simplicity and empirical power. By framing the problem as one of predicting future measurements, the work sidesteps many of the challenges associated with credit assignment and instability in value-based reinforcement learning. This is particularly relevant for the target domain of immersive 3D environments, where an agent receives a constant stream of meaningful, multi-modal feedback that is often discarded or condensed into a single scalar reward in typical RL setups.

The experimental evaluation is exemplary. The performance gains over strong baselines like A3C are substantial, especially in the more complex "Battle" scenarios (Table 1), which strongly supports the hypothesis that leveraging vectorial measurements is beneficial. The goal-agnostic training experiments (Table 3) are particularly impressive, demonstrating that an agent can learn a versatile policy that performs nearly as well on a specific task as a specialist agent, while also being capable of pursuing entirely different, even opposing, goals. This is a significant step toward more flexible and general intelligent agents. The ablation study (Table 4) provides clear evidence for the importance of the two key design choices: using vectorial feedback over a scalar signal and predicting at multiple future time steps.

Finally, the success in the Visual Doom AI Competition provides a powerful, real-world validation of the method’s robustness and generalization capabilities, which are often difficult to assess in a purely academic setting. The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and properly situated within the relevant literature.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended to help strengthen the paper further and are not critical to the acceptance decision.

*   **Connection to Model-Based RL:** The approach involves predicting the future consequences of actions, which has strong conceptual ties to model-based reinforcement learning. The distinction is that the "model" here predicts a low-dimensional measurement vector rather than the full, high-dimensional sensory state. A brief discussion of this connection would be valuable, as it could help place the work more broadly and highlight its relationship to learning predictive world models.
*   **Goal Conditioning Mechanism:** The paper mentions a "goal module G(g)" in the architecture, but provides few details on its implementation and how it enables generalization to new goals. Elucidating this mechanism would be helpful. For example, is the goal vector simply processed by a fully-connected layer and concatenated? A more detailed description or diagram of how the goal vector influences the network's predictions would enhance the reader's understanding of this crucial component.
*   **Sensitivity to Temporal Offsets:** The choice of temporal offsets (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32 steps) for future prediction is an important hyperparameter. While the ablation study shows that using multiple offsets is beneficial, some discussion on the sensitivity to these specific values or the intuition behind their selection (e.g., the exponential spacing) would be a useful addition.
*   **Dependence on Measurements:** The method's success relies on the availability of a meaningful, pre-defined measurement stream `m_t`. It would be valuable to briefly acknowledge this limitation in the discussion section and perhaps speculate on how the approach could be extended to scenarios where such measurements are not provided and must be learned from the raw sensory stream.
