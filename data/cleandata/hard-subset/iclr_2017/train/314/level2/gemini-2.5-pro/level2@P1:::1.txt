### Summary

This paper presents an approach for learning sensorimotor control in complex, immersive 3D environments. The core idea, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), is to reframe the control problem as a supervised learning task. Instead of maximizing a scalar reward signal as in standard reinforcement learning, the agent learns to predict a vector of future "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input, measurements, and a specified goal. Actions are then chosen by selecting the one that leads to the best-predicted future according to the current goal. This formulation provides a dense, multi-dimensional training signal derived directly from experience. The authors conduct extensive experiments in challenging first-person scenarios using the ViZDoom platform. They demonstrate that their approach significantly outperforms strong deep RL baselines like DQN and A3C, particularly on complex tasks. Furthermore, they show that the agent can be trained without a fixed goal and can generalize effectively to pursue dynamically specified goals at test time. The strength of the approach is compellingly demonstrated by its victory in the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed DFP method demonstrates exceptional performance, decisively outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL methods (A3C, DQN) on challenging 3D visuomotor control tasks. The performance gap is particularly large in the more complex "Battle" scenarios. The reported win in the Visual Doom AI Competition provides a very strong, independent validation of the method's effectiveness and robustness in unseen environments.

2.  **Novel and Effective Problem Formulation:** The paper's formulation of control as goal-conditioned future prediction is elegant and compelling. By replacing a sparse scalar reward with a dense, vectorial measurement stream, the method provides a rich supervisory signal that appears to stabilize and accelerate learning. The experiments on goal-agnostic training successfully show that this framework can learn general skills that are applicable to a variety of goals, a key challenge in reinforcement learning.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's primary strength lies in its comprehensive and convincing experimental evaluation. The authors compare DFP against highly relevant and strong baselines on a series of tasks with increasing difficulty, clearly showing the advantages of their method. The ablation study in Table 4 provides rigorous support for the central claims of the paper: that using vectorial feedback is superior to a scalar reward, and that predicting measurements at multiple future time steps is beneficial. This analysis validates the key design choices of the DFP framework.

The generalization experiments are another highlight. The ability of the model trained with varied textures to generalize to new environments (Table 2) and the ability of the model trained with randomized goals to generalize to new objectives (Table 3) are significant results. The latter is particularly interesting, as the model trained without a fixed goal performs nearly as well on the primary task as a specialized model, while gaining the flexibility to pursue entirely new goals like "pacifist" or "ammo drainer". This demonstrates a promising path toward more flexible and general-purpose agents.

The architecture, while building on existing ideas like dueling networks, is well-suited to the task and clearly described. The connection to the broader literature, including historical work on supervised control and modern work on universal value functions, is well-articulated and places the contribution in a proper context.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for the decision.

*   **The Sensory vs. Measurement Distinction:** The paper introduces a distinction between the raw sensory stream `s_t` and the measurement vector `m_t`. While this is a practical and effective choice, the conceptual boundary between them feels somewhat artificial, as the paper acknowledges. It would be beneficial to include a discussion on the principles for selecting these measurements. For example, how sensitive is the method to the choice of what is included in `m_t`? Would the approach work if the goal was not easily expressed by an available measurement (e.g., a purely visual goal like "go to the blue pillar")?
*   **Comparison to Model-Based RL:** The approach has elements of model-based RL, as it involves predicting future outcomes. A more explicit discussion comparing and contrasting DFP with traditional model-based approaches would be enlightening. DFP appears to occupy a pragmatic middle ground by predicting only a low-dimensional vector of task-relevant measurements rather than the full next state, which could be a key reason for its success. Highlighting this could further sharpen the paper's contribution.
*   **Stochasticity:** The use of an L2 regression loss implies an assumption that the distribution of future outcomes is unimodal. It would be useful to briefly discuss how the method is expected to behave in highly stochastic environments where a given state-action pair might lead to very different outcomes.

### Questions for Authors

To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  In the randomized goal training experiments, is the ε-greedy policy for data collection also conditioned on the randomly sampled goal for that episode? That is, does the agent act to maximize `g_i^T F` during collection, and then the experience tuple is stored with that same `g_i`?
2.  Could you clarify the dimensionality of the goal vector `g`? The paper states it has the same dimensionality as `f`, the vector of future measurement differences. In the "Battle" scenario, there are 3 measurements and 6 temporal offsets, which seems to imply `f` is 18-dimensional. Does the goal vector `g` also have 18 dimensions, allowing the agent to specify a different weight for each measurement at each future time step?
3.  How was the set of temporal offsets (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32 steps) chosen? Have you investigated the sensitivity of the model's performance to this hyperparameter?
