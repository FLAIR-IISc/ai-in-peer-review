- Decision: Accept — well-motivated supervised formulation for sensorimotor control with strong empirical validation and competition win.

- Novelty & motivation: Proposes predicting action effects on future measurements as an alternative to sparse-reward RL, leveraging dense co-temporal sensory/measurement signals.

- Strong empirical results: Large performance gains over state-of-the-art deep RL in Doom, validated by winning the Visual Doom AI Competition and ablation studies.

- Generalization ability: Demonstrates robustness and transfer across different Doom environments, indicating good representation learning.

- Clear presentation: Paper is well-written with a clear explanation of method, experiments, and relation to prior work.

- Theoretical analysis needed: Lacks formal analysis of convergence and how the approach relates to supervised learning guarantees.

- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Needs more detailed discussion and experiments on sensitivity of the proposed method to hyperparameters.

- Computational cost: Requires a thorough analysis of training time and memory usage compared to baselines.

- Goal representation: Currently uses linear combinations of future measurements; exploring richer goal representations could be informative.

- Visualization of representations: Adding visualizations (e.g., activations) would help interpret how sensory input is processed and predictions formed.

- Continuous actions: Clarify challenges and potential solutions for extending the approach to continuous action spaces.

- Limitations of linear goals: Discuss scenarios where linear combination of future measurements may be inadequate and possible alternatives.

- Temporal offsets sensitivity: Explain sensitivity to choice of temporal offsets (τ1,...,τn) and whether other selection strategies were explored.

- Replay buffer details: Provide replay buffer size and analysis of its impact on training dynamics.

- Measurement design: Discuss whether there is a principled way to design or select measurements (mt) to further improve learning and generalization.
