This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, framing the problem as supervised learning rather than reinforcement learning. The core idea is to leverage the rich supervisory signal present in the cotemporal structure of high-dimensional sensory streams and lower-dimensional measurement streams. By predicting the effect of actions on future measurements, the agent learns to control its environment without needing explicit rewards, and can adapt to dynamically changing goals. The approach was successfully demonstrated in the challenging Doom environment, even winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is innovative and provides a compelling alternative to traditional reinforcement learning paradigms, particularly in scenarios with dense, multi-dimensional feedback. The strong empirical results, including winning a competitive AI challenge, provide strong support for the claims. 

The paper convincingly argues that the proposed method overcomes limitations of traditional RL, such as sparse rewards and difficulties in generalization. The authors clearly explain the rationale behind their approach, placing it within the broader context of the field. The experimental setup appears rigorous, with comparisons to state-of-the-art methods and ablation studies that demonstrate the importance of different components. The generalization results are particularly encouraging, suggesting the model learns robust and transferable skills. 

However, a few points could be addressed to further strengthen the paper. The architecture of the predictor network (Figure 1) could benefit from more detailed explanation, particularly the reasoning behind the expectation and action streams. While the ablation studies are helpful, a more in-depth analysis of the effects of different temporal offsets for prediction would be beneficial. Also, the reliance on Doom as the sole test environment, while impressive given the competition win, might be seen as a limitation. Exploring the method’s performance on other, diverse immersive environments would strengthen the generalizability claim.

I would like to know more about the hyperparameter tuning process for each of the baselines. How sensitive was the performance of the baselines to changes in learning rate? Also, could the authors elaborate on the specific differences in the model complexity (number of parameters) between their approach and the winning submission in the Visual Doom AI Competition, given that their model was simpler? Finally, what computational resources were required for training?
