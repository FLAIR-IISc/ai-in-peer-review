This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, framing it as a supervised learning problem rather than traditional reinforcement learning. The key idea is to leverage the rich supervisory signal present in the co-temporal structure of high-dimensional sensory streams and lower-dimensional measurement streams. The authors demonstrate strong results in the Doom environment, even winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Decision: Accept**

The core contribution – reframing sensorimotor control as a supervised learning problem using future prediction of measurements – is insightful and demonstrably effective. The results are compelling, particularly the win in the Visual Doom AI Competition, providing strong evidence of the approach’s superiority over existing methods. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation and benefits of the proposed method.

**Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Novelty and Insight:** The approach of utilizing direct future prediction based on measurements, instead of relying on sparse rewards, is a significant departure from standard RL and presents a fresh perspective on sensorimotor control.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The performance in the Doom environment consistently outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods. The victory in the Visual Doom AI competition provides especially strong validation, as it demonstrates generalization to unseen environments. The ablation studies are well-designed and provide further support for the key components of the proposed approach.
*   **Clear Motivation and Justification:** The paper effectively argues why the proposed approach is well-suited for environments with dense, multi-dimensional feedback, contrasting it with traditional RL's reliance on sparse scalar rewards.
*   **Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-organized, with a clear flow of ideas and a comprehensive overview of related work. The architectural details are sufficiently described, and the experimental setup is clearly explained.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Discussion of Limitations:** While the paper presents strong results, a more in-depth discussion of the limitations of the approach would be valuable. For example, what types of environments might this approach *not* be well-suited for? What are the challenges in defining appropriate "measurements" for different tasks?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning for the baselines. Providing some insight into the sensitivity of *their* approach to key hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, network architecture) would strengthen the results.
*   **Computational Cost:** A brief discussion of the computational resources required to train the models would be helpful.
*   **Generalization beyond Doom:** While the generalization across randomly textured Doom environments is demonstrated, exploring generalization to entirely different simulated environments would further strengthen the claims.
*   **Mathematical Formalism:** While the presentation is clear, a slightly more rigorous mathematical formulation of the training objective and prediction module might enhance the clarity for some readers.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Regarding the choice of temporal offsets (τ1, …, τn), what is the rationale behind selecting those specific values (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, and 32)? Were other combinations explored, and if so, what were the results?
*   In the ablation study, you found that predicting multiple measurements significantly improves performance. Is there a point of diminishing returns as the number of predicted measurements increases?
*   Could you elaborate on the types of goals that might be difficult to express in terms of future measurements, and how the approach might be adapted to handle such scenarios?
*   You mention that the presented approach can generalize to new goals not specified during training. Is there a limit to how far "out-of-distribution" the new goals can be before performance degrades significantly?
*   The model won the full deathmatch competition against a team employing substantial additional supervision. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of the specific types of supervision used by the winning model and the baseline approach, and how this difference may have impacted the results?
