This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, framing the problem as supervised learning by leveraging the rich supervisory signal provided by the co-temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams. The core idea – predicting the effect of actions on future measurements – is compelling and well-motivated, particularly in contexts where sparse rewards are problematic for traditional reinforcement learning. The results, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, are impressive and strongly support the claims made.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper tackles a significant problem in RL – learning from raw sensory input – and proposes a well-motivated and empirically validated approach. The strong experimental results, particularly the win in the Visual Doom AI Competition, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The paper offers a fresh perspective on sensorimotor control, moving away from reward-based RL towards a supervised learning formulation. The rationale for this approach – the richness of multi-dimensional, temporally dense measurements – is clearly articulated and convincingly argued.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments in Doom demonstrate significant performance improvements over state-of-the-art deep RL methods, especially in challenging scenarios. The success in the Visual Doom AI Competition provides particularly strong external validation. The ablation studies further bolster the claims by demonstrating the importance of both vectorial measurements and multi-step prediction.
* **Generalization Ability:** The experiments demonstrating generalization across different Doom environments highlight the robustness of the learned representations. This is a crucial aspect of practical applicability.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach, experimental setup, and results. The connection to relevant prior work is also well-established.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Theoretical Analysis:** While the empirical results are compelling, a more formal theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the proposed method would strengthen the paper. Specifically, discussing how the approach relates to, or differs from, traditional supervised learning guarantees would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the approach to various hyperparameters would be helpful. While the paper mentions hyperparameter tuning for the baselines, a similar analysis for the proposed method would increase confidence in its robustness.
* **Computational Cost:** A more thorough analysis of the computational resources required for training would be beneficial. This could include a comparison of training time and memory usage across different scenarios and baselines.
* **Goal Representation:** The paper focuses on linear combinations of future measurements as goals. Exploring more complex goal representations and their impact on performance would be an interesting extension.
* **Visualization of Learned Representations:** Including visualizations of the learned representations (e.g., activation patterns in the network) could provide insights into how the model is processing sensory input and predicting future measurements.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you elaborate on the challenges of extending this approach to handle continuous action spaces?
* What are the limitations of using the linear combination of future measurements to define goals? Are there scenarios where this representation might be inadequate?
* How sensitive is the performance of the model to the choice of temporal offsets (τ1, ..., τn)? Did you explore other strategies for selecting these offsets?
*  The paper mentions the use of an experience replay buffer. What is the size of this buffer and how does it impact the training process?
* Given the success with DFP, do you believe there's a principled way to design the measurements (mt) to further improve learning and generalization, beyond just what's readily available in the environment?
