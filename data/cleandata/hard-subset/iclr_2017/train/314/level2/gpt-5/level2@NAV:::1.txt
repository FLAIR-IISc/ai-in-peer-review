Summary and claims: The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning formulation of sensorimotor control that predicts future changes in intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) conditioned on current sensory input and a goal vector. The key claims are: (1) dense, vector-valued measurement streams provide rich supervision that stabilizes and accelerates training compared to scalar reward RL; (2) goal-parameterization enables training without a fixed goal and test-time adaptation to new goals; (3) DFP outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) on challenging 3D Doom scenarios and generalizes across environments and goals, with additional evidence from winning the Visual Doom AI competition.

Decision: Accept. Reasons: (i) a simple, well-motivated reformulation yielding strong empirical gains on realistic 3D tasks; (ii) convincing demonstrations of goal-conditioned control and generalization, supported by ablations.

Supporting arguments: The problem—learning visuomotor control from raw pixels with dynamically specified goals—is important. The approach is situated in prior work (UVFA, predictive representations, Monte Carlo methods) and the departure from sparse scalar rewards is well motivated. Experimental support is substantial: four scenarios of increasing difficulty, comparisons against tuned DQN/A3C, generalization to unseen textures, goal-agnostic training, and ablations showing the benefits of vectorial feedback and multi-horizon prediction. Training is efficient due to fast simulation, and results are averaged over multiple runs. Limitations include: incomplete statistical rigor (no error bars/significance), uneven baseline coverage (limited DSR due to speed), dependence on accessible, informative measurements and linear goal composition, purely reactive control without memory (partial observability), and evaluation confined to discrete actions and a single domain. Nonetheless, the performance gains and the competition result make the contribution compelling.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper, not central to the decision):
- Report variance/confidence intervals and perform significance tests; include learning curves with shaded uncertainty.
- Clarify and justify key choices (temporal offsets, loss weights, replay size, k-update schedule) and provide sensitivity analyses.
- Discuss sample efficiency vs A3C in reward-per-environment-step, not just wall-clock.
- Evaluate robustness to noisy/partial measurements and to sparser feedback; include a domain beyond Doom if possible.
- Explore non-linear goal parameterizations and memory (e.g., LSTM) for partial observability; comment on continuous actions.
- Clean up and complete the references.

Questions for authors:
- How sensitive is performance to the choice and scaling of measurements and to the goal distribution used in training?
- What are M and k in the experience memory regime, and how do they affect stability?
- Do results degrade if g changes mid-episode, and how is action selection handled then?
- Can you report variance across seeds and per-task sample-efficiency curves?
- How does DFP perform when only a sparse scalar reward is available?
