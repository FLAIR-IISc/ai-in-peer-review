1. Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning formulation of sensorimotor control that predicts future values of vectorial measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) conditioned on current observations, action, and a goal vector. Actions are chosen by greedily maximizing the inner product between the goal and predicted future measurement changes. Key attributes:
- Replaces sparse scalar rewards with dense, multi-dimensional measurement streams as supervision.
- Supports goal-parameterized control: no fixed goal during training is required; at test time, arbitrary goals expressed over future measurements can be pursued.
- Simple, reactive architecture combining a perception module and an “expectation + action stream” prediction head (dueling-style).
- Extensive experiments in 3D Doom-based environments show strong performance vs. DQN, A3C, and DSR, particularly on harder tasks; evidence of generalization across environments (textures/mazes) and across goals; ablations demonstrate benefits of vector measurements and multi-horizon predictions. The approach also won the Visual Doom AI Competition (Full Deathmatch).

2. Decision
Accept. Reasons: (i) A clear, well-motivated re-framing of control as supervised multi-step measurement prediction with goal-parameterization, and (ii) compelling empirical results on challenging 3D domains, including generalization across environments and goals, that substantially outperform strong baselines.

3. Supporting arguments
Strengths
- Novelty and clarity: The paper articulates a simple but powerful idea—predict meaningful, intrinsic measurements and act greedily with respect to a test-time goal. This bridges SL and control without hand-crafted rewards or imitation data, and is well contextualized vis-à-vis Monte Carlo methods, UVFA, and multi-objective RL.
- Strong empirical support: On D3/D4 (Battle tasks), DFP significantly outperforms A3C and DQN, with convincing training curves and large test-time margins. The generalization studies (D3/D4 vs. D3-tx/D4-tx) and competition win add credibility.
- Goal-parameterization: Experiments show that goal-agnostic training performs comparably to goal-specific training on the main task and generalizes better to unseen goals.
- Ablations: Demonstrate that vector-valued supervision and multi-horizon prediction materially improve performance, even when evaluation is on a single metric (frags).

Limitations and concerns
- Fairness and scope of baselines: The key advantage stems from dense, multi-dimensional supervision. A3C/DQN are trained on scalar shaped rewards; although the scalar reward matches the same linear combination, A3C could also benefit from auxiliary prediction heads (e.g., predicting the measurement components or pixel-control/feature-control). Comparison to such “auxiliary-task” actor-critic baselines (e.g., UNREAL-style) would strengthen the claim that the win is due to the DFP formulation rather than just auxiliary dense supervision. Relatedly, evaluation against UVFA-like goal-conditioning with RL training would be informative.
- Horizon and delayed credit: DFP uses finite short horizons (1–32 steps, with heavier weight on the latest 3). This may limit applicability to tasks with sparse or very delayed rewards. The paper acknowledges this, but a discussion of failure modes or performance degradation as horizons increase would be useful.
- Exploration: The method uses ε-greedy exploration; no mechanism addresses hard-exploration regimes. It would help to clarify how sensitive performance is to ε schedules and whether better exploration (e.g., goal-conditioned exploration, count-based bonuses) improves results.
- Partial observability and memory: All methods use a single frame (feed-forward convnet). Since Doom can be partially observable, including a recurrent baseline (A3C-LSTM) and/or adding memory to DFP could clarify whether DFP’s advantage persists under stronger baselines.
- Off-policy stability: The method is trained with a small replay buffer and an evolving behavior policy (off-policy Monte Carlo). Although the results are stable, a brief analysis of stability/sensitivity to buffer size/batch size would increase confidence.
- Reproducibility: Many implementation details are in appendices; public code or more hyperparameter detail (optimizer settings, preprocessing, frame-skip, action repeat) would aid reproducibility.

4. Additional feedback and suggestions (to help improve the paper)
- Comparisons to auxiliary-task actor-critic: Add a baseline where A3C predicts the same measurement vector (multi-head) with an auxiliary loss alongside policy/value learning, and/or pixel/feature control. This isolates whether vectorial dense supervision per se accounts for most gains.
- Architectural ablations: Report variants without the dueling-style expectation/action split and without the action-stream normalization to quantify their contribution. Also test multi-step horizons vs. single-step-only predictions with matched compute.
- Sensitivity to goal parametrization: Explore scaling of g (magnitude) and sparsity (e.g., one-hot goals) at test time; assess calibration of prediction heads. Plot u(f; g) error vs. achieved returns across g to show alignment.
- Horizon design: Vary temporal offsets and loss weights to show robustness; consider longer horizons or learned discounting; discuss computational cost vs. performance.
- Sample efficiency: Include learning curves vs. environment steps (not wall-clock) to assess sample efficiency relative to A3C/DQN.
- Exploration: Briefly discuss whether randomized goals during training implicitly aid exploration (curricula over g), and whether curriculum over g improves learning speed.
- Continuous actions: Provide a short discussion on integrating DFP with continuous action spaces (e.g., predicting measurements conditioned on parameterized actions and optimizing via CEM/gradient-based action selection).
- Minor: Clean up several references (formatting, missing years, inconsistent author strings).

Questions for the authors
- Can you report results for an A3C baseline augmented with auxiliary prediction of the measurement components used by DFP (and possibly other auxiliary tasks)? How close does it get?
- How sensitive is DFP to the choice and scaling of measurement channels? If one measurement is noisy or weakly relevant, does it hurt learning?
- What is the effect of longer prediction horizons (e.g., up to 128/256 steps) and different weighting schemes? Does performance saturate?
- How large is the experience memory M, and how do performance and stability change with M, batch size, and update frequency k?
- Did you try recurrent DFP (e.g., ConvLSTM) or A3C-LSTM baselines? If so, how do results change on D3/D4?
- In goal-agnostic training, what is the distribution over g? How far can you extrapolate at test time beyond the training range without loss of control quality?
- Could you release code and environment configs to facilitate replication?
