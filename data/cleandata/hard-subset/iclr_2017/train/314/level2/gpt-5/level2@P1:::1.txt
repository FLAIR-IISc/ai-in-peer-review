Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning formulation of sensorimotor control that predicts action-conditional future changes in a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) at multiple horizons, conditioned on a goal vector g. Action selection maximizes the inner product g^T f, enabling both goal-agnostic training and goal-conditioned control at test time.
- A simple yet well-structured architecture processes sensory input, measurements, and goals; prediction is split into an expectation stream and an action-advantage stream (inspired by dueling networks) to better capture action differences.
- Extensive experiments in ViZDoom across four scenarios show competitive or superior performance to strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C), with especially large gains on more complex tasks with richer measurements. The approach demonstrates generalization across environments (texture variations), across goals (randomized-goal training), and includes ablations showing the benefits of vectorial feedback and multi-horizon prediction. A variant won the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch track.
- The work advances a practical and scalable alternative to reward-based RL when dense, multidimensional measurements are available, highlighting a promising supervised-learning path to sensorimotor control with raw pixels.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) Strong empirical performance and generalization with a simple, scalable method; particularly impressive gains on complex 3D tasks and success in an external competition.
2) Clear, well-motivated reframing of control as supervised prediction of task-relevant future measurements, with goal-conditioning that supports training without a fixed goal and flexible deployment.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets two salient challenges: pixel-based control in rich 3D environments and flexible goal specification. The argument that dense, multidimensional measurement streams can provide richer supervision than sparse scalar rewards is compelling and well supported by ablations.
- Placement in literature: The connections to UVFA (Schaul et al.), predictive representations, Monte Carlo methods, and dueling architectures are discussed. The specific instantiation—predicting vectorial future measurements conditioned on goals and using that for control—is novel and practical. The relation to successor representations (Kulkarni et al.) is noted; DFP’s direct prediction without value decomposition offers a distinct trade-off.
- Method and correctness: The approach uses pure Monte Carlo targets of future measurement deltas at several horizons, avoiding bootstrapping instabilities. The all-actions prediction with loss applied to the executed action seems plausible and compatible with shared representation learning. The goal-conditioning is straightforward and experimentally validated.
- Empirical rigor: 
  - Baselines: DQN and A3C are appropriate; hyperparameter tuning is described. DSR comparisons are limited by speed, but the primary comparisons to DQN/A3C are the core evidence.
  - Scenarios: Increasing difficulty, clear metrics, and multiple runs. Testing on 1M steps provides stable estimates. Goal-agnostic training results are particularly convincing: models trained with randomized goals nearly match dedicated models and generalize far better to novel goals.
  - Ablations: Demonstrate that multiple measurements and multiple horizons both matter. Cross-texture generalization and the larger-capacity variant strengthen the claims.
- Practicality: The method trains quickly with simple infrastructure (thousands of FPS simulation), which is a notable advantage over many RL methods.

Additional feedback and questions (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Clarify supervision details:
  - When predicting for all actions, is the regression loss applied only to the executed action’s head, or is there any auxiliary loss for non-executed actions (e.g., via counterfactuals or off-policy estimates)?
  - How are targets computed for horizons that cross episode termination (padding, truncation, or absorbing states)? How are measurement deltas defined upon death?
  - How are measurements normalized and are target scales balanced across dimensions and horizons? How sensitive are results to the horizon weights (0.5, 0.5, 1) and to the set {1,2,4,8,16,32}?
- Fairness and breadth of baselines:
  - Given the partially observable nature of Doom, could you report A3C-LSTM (recurrent) results, even if DFP is reactive, to understand how much memory could close the gap?
  - UVFA-style Q-learning/policy-gradient with goal-conditioning would be a useful additional baseline to isolate the effect of direct measurement prediction versus value learning with goals.
  - Please report variance across seeds and statistical significance for key comparisons.
- Goal-conditioning and measurements:
  - How sensitive is performance to the choice and number of measurements? For example, if some measurements are noisy or weakly correlated with success, does DFP remain robust?
  - The linear goal assumption is elegant; have you tried nonlinear goal maps u(f; g) or learning a goal embedding jointly?
  - In randomized-goal training, what is the distribution over g, and how critical is sampling from [−1,1] versus [0,1]? Any curriculum effects?
- Architecture and ablations:
  - Provide an ablation of the expectation/action split (dueling-style) within DFP to quantify its contribution in this setting.
  - Could multi-step rollouts (predicting over longer windows or via sequence models) or light recurrence improve performance on tasks with delayed consequences?
- Exploration and data efficiency:
  - The method uses ε-greedy with a small replay buffer. Have you evaluated alternative exploration strategies (e.g., entropy regularization, count-based bonuses, curiosity) or larger buffers? Any off-policy issues observed?
  - Report wall-clock training time for all methods, not only steps/day, to emphasize the practical speed advantage.
- Generalization:
  - The cross-texture results are strong. Have you examined transfer to layouts with different topology or to tasks with different measurement semantics?
  - Any evidence that models trained with randomized goals generalize better across environments as well as across goals?

Overall, this is a clear, original, and empirically strong submission. It proposes a simple idea that works remarkably well and opens an appealing direction for goal-conditioned control with dense measurement feedback. I recommend acceptance.
