Summary
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a goal-conditioned sensorimotor control framework that replaces scalar reward prediction with supervised prediction of future intrinsic measurements. Observations are decomposed into a high-dimensional sensory stream (e.g., images) and a low-dimensional measurement stream (e.g., health, ammo, frags). Given an observation, candidate action, and goal vector, a deep network predicts changes in future measurements at multiple temporal offsets; the agent then selects the action that maximizes the goal-weighted prediction. The approach enables training without fixed goals and supports dynamically specified goals at test time. An architecture with separate “expectation” and “action-difference” streams improves discrimination among actions. Experiments in ViZDoom across four scenarios show strong performance relative to DQN, A3C, and DSR, especially on challenging tasks with richer measurement vectors. The paper also reports generalization across textures and environments, goal-agnostic training that transfers to new goals, an ablation supporting the benefits of vector-valued and multi-horizon predictions, and a win in the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch track without extra game-engine supervision.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) A clear, well-motivated alternative to value-based RL that leverages dense, vectorial feedback to deliver strong empirical gains on challenging 3D visuomotor tasks; (2) Practical and conceptually appealing support for dynamic goal specification and cross-goal generalization, validated by extensive experiments and a competitive benchmark win.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The work targets sensorimotor learning from raw sensory input with flexible goals—an important setting where dense, multidimensional measurements are often available. Framing control as supervised prediction of future measurements is well motivated by prior analyses contrasting SL, TD, and Monte Carlo methods, and by literature on multi-objective RL and goal-parameterized value functions.
- Approach and novelty: The central idea—predicting multi-horizon future measurement vectors conditioned on goals and actions, followed by greedy selection—is simple, scalable, and distinct from standard TD-based methods. The explicit separation of sensory input from measurements, vector-valued goal conditioning u(f; g)=g^T f, and the dueling-style architecture adapted for multi-measurement prediction constitute a coherent design. The formulation is closely related to UVFA and successor representations, but differs by directly predicting future measurement changes at multiple fixed horizons without bootstrapping.
- Empirical support: Across four Doom scenarios, DFP consistently matches or exceeds strong deep RL baselines; gains are substantial in “Battle” and “Battle 2” where multiple measurements are informative (up to 6× A3C). The generalization study (texture/randomized maps) shows meaningful transfer when trained with environment variation; a higher-capacity model further improves results. Goal-agnostic training nearly matches task-specific training for the main goal and generalizes better to new goals. Ablations indicate that vector-valued supervision and multi-horizon targets both contribute significantly. The competition result provides independent validation in previously unseen environments against strong LSTM-based agents using shaped rewards and extra supervision.
- Rigor: The paper provides training details, baselines from published implementations, and reports best results over reasonable hyperparameter searches. Comparisons use equivalent reward/measurement definitions where applicable. Multiple random seeds and long evaluation runs are used.

Additional feedback and questions (to help improve the paper)
Essential clarifications
- Relationship to UVFA and successor representations: Provide a more explicit comparative analysis. In what settings would successor features or generalized value functions approximate the same object, and when does direct multi-horizon prediction have advantages or disadvantages (e.g., bias/variance trade-offs, compounding error, coverage of long horizons)?
- Scope and assumptions: Discuss the dependence on accessible, informative measurements. How sensitive is performance to the choice and scaling of measurements, inclusion of irrelevant/noisy channels, or partial/unreliable measurement streams?
- Horizon design: Justify the choice of temporal offsets and loss weighting (only last three offsets with coefficients 0.5, 0.5, 1). Include a sensitivity analysis over horizon sets and weights to establish robustness and guide practitioners.

Nice-to-have extensions (optional given time/resources)
- Fairness and compute: Report wall-clock and sample efficiency comparisons to A3C/DQN under matched hardware and environment step budgets, as well as the effect of the ε schedule and replay buffer size on DFP stability and performance.
- Myopic action selection: The policy is 1-step greedy with respect to multi-horizon predictions. Analyze whether multi-step rollout with the learned predictor (model-based lookahead) improves performance, and characterize when one-step greediness suffices.
- Memory and partial observability: Doom scenarios are partially observable. Report results with a recurrent extension or frame stacking to assess the contribution of temporal context to prediction accuracy and control.
- Continuous control: Comment on applicability to continuous actions (e.g., predicting measurements for sampled actions with gradient-based refinement), and any early evidence.
- Calibration and error analysis: Provide calibration plots or error decomposition for measurement predictions across horizons and actions; analyze failure cases where predictions are accurate but the induced policy underperforms, or vice versa.

Questions
- How does performance change when adding extraneous measurements (e.g., irrelevant counters) or injecting noise into measurements? Are predictions and policies robust?
- What is the effect of goal distribution during goal-agnostic training (e.g., uniform on [0,1] vs. [−1,1]) on learned representations and downstream goal generalization?
- Are the multi-horizon targets redundant or complementary? Do shorter horizons mainly aid stability while longer ones drive task reward?
- How sensitive is DFP to action space granularity? Would action branching (factorized action heads) further help in scenarios with combinatorial sub-actions?
- Can the method recover conventional discounted-return behavior via an appropriate choice of goal vector, and how closely does it match Q-learning on synthetic discounted tasks?

Overall, the paper delivers a compelling, simple alternative to deep RL that is empirically strong, well explained, and practically meaningful. With clarifications on the relation to prior goal-conditioned/value-function work and a brief robustness analysis of design choices (measurements and horizons), this work merits acceptance.
