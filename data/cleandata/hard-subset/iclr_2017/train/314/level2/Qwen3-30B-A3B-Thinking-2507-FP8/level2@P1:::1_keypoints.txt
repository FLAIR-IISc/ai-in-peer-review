- Introduces Direct Future Prediction (DFP), using cotemporal structure of high-dimensional sensory streams and low-dimensional measurement streams to enable supervised action selection without scalar rewards.
- Argues convincingly that temporal interplay between sensory input and measurements provides rich supervision for learning in complex 3D environments.
- Evaluated extensively in the Doom environment, achieving state-of-the-art performance and strong generalization across scenarios.
- Recommendation: accept; key strengths include the motivation for vectorial measurements, thorough experiments, and a decisive competition win (Full Deathmatch) outperforming the runner-up by >50%.
- Major contribution: reframes sensorimotor control as supervised learning when rich, temporally dense measurements are available.
- Empirical evidence shows vectorial measurements substantially outperform scalar rewards, especially on challenging tasks.
- Ablation study (Table 4) demonstrates that predicting multiple measurements at multiple temporal offsets yields significant benefits.
- Generalization experiments (Table 2) and competition results validate robustness and effectiveness on previously unseen environments.
- Background section is well-written and situates the work relative to Monte Carlo methods, multi-objective decision-making, and parameterized goals.
- Novelty: formulating direct prediction of future meaningful measurements in complex 3D environments builds on prior supervised-control work but is distinct and effective.
- Suggest strengthening theoretical justification for why DFP outperforms traditional RL and why vectorial measurements provide superior supervision to scalar rewards.
- Recommend a clearer comparison and differentiation from the successor representation framework (Schaul et al., 2015) and value function approximations.
- Advise adding a discussion of limitations, e.g., performance in environments with sparse or less informative measurement streams.
- Two author questions: (1) how the model handles test-time goals completely outside the training goal range, and (2) how the approach generalizes when the number or dimensionality of measurements changes significantly.
