- Novel formulation (DFP): frame sensorimotor control as supervised learning by predicting future measurements from raw sensory input and current measurements.  
- Main contribution: predicting future measurements provides dense supervision, removing need for scalar rewards and reward shaping.  
- Enables training without fixed goals and flexible goal adaptation at test time via vectorial goal specification.  
- Strong empirical results in ViZDoom: state-of-the-art on challenging scenarios (Battle, Battle 2).  
- Practical validation: won Full Deathmatch track of Visual Doom AI Competition, >50% better than second place.  
- Argues vectorial measurements give richer, denser supervision than sparse scalar rewards, contrasted with standard RL.  
- Ablation studies (Table 4) empirically show benefits of multiple measurements and multiple temporal offsets.  
- Generalization experiments across textured environments (Table 2) demonstrate robustness to visual variation.  
- Method is well-grounded in prior supervised control and vector-feedback literature while distinct in applying to raw 3D visual input.  
- Experimental design is rigorous: four progressively harder scenarios, fair baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR), extensive hyperparameter tuning.  
- Results are statistically sound (averaged over three runs) with clear metrics (frags, health) reported for training and testing.  
- Training procedure and architecture are described sufficiently for reproducibility (Sections 3.1, 3.2).  
- Decision: Accept — significant contribution showing supervised, measurement-prediction can outperform RL in complex 3D environments.  
- Feedback 1: Clarify the definition and criteria for designating variables as "measurements" versus sensory input.  
- Feedback 2: Include failure cases and limitations (e.g., when measurements are uninformative about goals).  
- Feedback 3: Soften the claim that "DFP dominates" by acknowledging DQN/A3C perform reasonably in simpler scenarios, indicating the advantage scales with complexity.  
- Question 1: How does the method handle goals not expressible as a linear combination of future measurements — is the linear goal formulation (Eq.1) a fundamental limitation?  
- Question 2: Could incorporating learned feature representations (e.g., pre-trained networks) for sensory input reduce dimensionality and improve scalability?
