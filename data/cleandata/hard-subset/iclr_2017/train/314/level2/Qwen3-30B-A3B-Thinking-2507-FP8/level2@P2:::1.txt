

# Review for ICLR 2017

## Summary
The paper proposes "Direct Future Prediction" (DFP), an approach to sensorimotor control that frames learning to act as a supervised learning problem using a rich supervisory signal from the cotemporal structure of sensory and measurement streams. Instead of relying on sparse scalar rewards as in traditional reinforcement learning, DFP trains a model to predict future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) based on current sensory input, measurements, and a goal. The model then selects actions that best achieve the predicted outcome. The authors claim DFP outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods on complex Doom-based tasks, generalizes across environments and goals, and won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

## Decision
Reject. While the paper presents an interesting experimental approach with strong empirical results, the contribution lacks sufficient novelty and the experimental comparison is not sufficiently rigorous.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's main claim is that "the formulation of sensorimotor learning from raw experience as supervised learning is rare." However, this claim is overstated. The paper cites Sutton et al. (2011) on predictive state representations and Oh et al. (2015) on predicting future frames, but fails to clearly distinguish DFP from these well-established approaches. The core idea of using future prediction for control is not novel, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why their specific formulation provides a significant theoretical or practical advantage over prior predictive control methods.

The experimental comparison with baselines is problematic. The authors train DFP for 50 million steps but only train DSR (the most comparable baseline) for approximately 1 million steps (10 days), which is a significant difference in training time. This makes the performance comparison unfair, as the baselines were not given sufficient training time to reach their full potential. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't address how it impacts the conclusions.

The paper claims to "learn without a fixed goal at training time," but the ablation study doesn't provide sufficient evidence for this claim. The model still requires some form of goal specification during training (even if randomized), and the paper doesn't demonstrate that the model can handle completely unseen goals at test time without any prior exposure to similar goals.

## Additional Feedback
1. **Novelty Clarification**: The paper should more thoroughly position itself against prior work on predictive representations and future prediction for control. A detailed comparison with Sutton et al. (2011) and Oh et al. (2015) would strengthen the contribution statement.

2. **Experimental Fairness**: The training time discrepancy with DSR needs to be addressed. Either provide a fair comparison with equal training time, or explicitly acknowledge this limitation and its impact on the results.

3. **Theoretical Justification**: The paper provides empirical evidence that vectorial feedback works better than scalar rewards, but lacks a theoretical explanation for why this is the case. A brief theoretical analysis would significantly strengthen the paper.

4. **Limitations Section**: The paper should include a more detailed discussion of the approach's limitations. For example, how would DFP perform in environments where the goal cannot be expressed as a linear combination of measurements? What are the constraints on the measurement stream?

## Questions for Authors
1. How does the DFP approach differ from the predictive state representation framework of Sutton et al. (2011) in a way that justifies the novelty claim? What specific aspects of their formulation provide advantages over prior work?

2. The paper claims to "learn without a fixed goal at training time," but the model still requires goal specification during training (even if randomized). How does the paper handle completely unseen goals at test time without any prior exposure to similar goals?

3. The ablation study shows that predicting multiple measurements improves performance, but the paper doesn't explain why this is the case. Is it because the measurements are correlated, or is there something inherent about multiple measurements that provides better supervision?
