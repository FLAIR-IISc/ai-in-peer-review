

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments by framing it as supervised learning using a high-dimensional sensory stream and a lower-dimensional measurement stream. The key contributions are: (1) using the cotemporal structure of sensory and measurement streams as a rich supervisory signal for training without extraneous supervision, and (2) enabling training without fixed goals while supporting dynamically changing goals at test time. The authors demonstrate that their method outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning approaches in complex 3D environments, particularly on challenging tasks involving multiple measurements.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a compelling case for a significant paradigm shift in sensorimotor control, and the experimental results provide strong support for the core claims. The approach won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming the second-best submission by over 50% without additional supervision. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the benefits of vectorial measurements over scalar rewards, and the generalization across environments is particularly impressive.

The experimental design is rigorous, with multiple scenarios of increasing complexity, thorough comparisons to relevant baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR), and comprehensive ablation studies. The paper's theoretical grounding in supervised learning and its connection to prior work on temporal-difference learning and multi-objective decision-making is well-executed.

To strengthen the paper, I suggest clarifying the relationship between the measurement stream and the environment's actual dynamics. Specifically, how do the authors ensure that the measurement stream captures all relevant state information for the task? Additionally, while the paper mentions the limitations of being purely reactive, a more detailed discussion of how memory mechanisms could integrate with this framework would be valuable.

The authors should also provide more concrete examples of how the goal vector g is defined in practice for different tasks, beyond the linear combination used in experiments. This would help readers understand the practical implementation of the "dynamically changing goals" claim.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances our understanding of sensorimotor control and provides a simple yet effective alternative to reinforcement learning for complex 3D environments. The results are convincing and the approach has clear potential for broader application.
