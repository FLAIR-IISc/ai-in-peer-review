- Proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): supervised learning to predict future measurements from sensory input and goals, then choose actions to optimize predicted outcomes.  
- Claims DFP outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL on Doom tasks and won the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch.  
- Decision: Reject—interesting empirical results but insufficient novelty and experimental rigor.  
- Novelty overstated: core idea of using future prediction for control is not clearly distinguished from prior work (Sutton et al. 2011, Oh et al. 2015).  
- Paper fails to convincingly argue specific theoretical or practical advantages of their DFP formulation over established predictive-control methods.  
- Experimental comparison is unfair: DFP trained for 50M steps while the closest baseline (DSR) was only trained for ~1M steps, biasing results.  
- Acknowledging the training-time discrepancy is insufficient; the paper must address its impact or provide fairer comparisons.  
- Claim of learning without fixed goals at training is weak: training still uses goal specification (even if randomized) and generalization to truly unseen goals is not demonstrated.  
- Ablation study does not adequately show the model can handle completely unseen goals at test time.  
- Lacks theoretical justification for why vectorial (multi-measurement) feedback outperforms scalar rewards.  
- Recommend clearer positioning against prior predictive-representation and future-prediction work, plus detailed comparisons to Sutton et al. and Oh et al.  
- Recommend addressing experimental fairness (equal training budgets) and adding a theoretical analysis explaining vectorial feedback benefits.  
- Suggest adding a limitations section discussing cases where goals aren’t linear combinations of measurements and constraints on the measurement stream.  
- Questions for authors: How exactly does DFP differ from predictive state representations? How are completely unseen goals handled at test time? Why does predicting multiple measurements improve performance—correlation or inherent advantage?
