

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control called "direct future prediction" (DFP), which leverages the cotemporal structure of high-dimensional sensory streams and lower-dimensional measurement streams to enable supervised learning for action selection without scalar reward signals. The authors convincingly argue that the temporal interplay between sensory input and measurements provides rich supervision for learning to act, particularly in complex 3D environments. The approach is evaluated extensively in the Doom environment, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance across multiple scenarios and strong generalization capabilities.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The key strengths are the compelling motivation for using vectorial measurements rather than scalar rewards, the thorough experimental validation across four increasingly complex scenarios, and the impressive results in the Visual Doom AI Competition where the approach won the Full Deathmatch track by outperforming the second-best submission by over 50%.

The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that sensorimotor control can be effectively framed as a supervised learning problem when rich, temporally dense measurements are available. The authors provide strong empirical evidence that vectorial measurements substantially improve performance over scalar rewards, particularly on challenging tasks. The ablation study in Table 4 clearly demonstrates that predicting multiple measurements at multiple temporal offsets provides a significant advantage. The generalization experiments across different environments (Table 2) further strengthen the case for the approach's robustness. The competition results provide particularly convincing validation, as the approach was evaluated in previously unseen environments without additional supervision.

The background section is well-written and effectively positions the work within the literature, particularly clarifying the relationship between their approach and Monte Carlo methods, as well as distinguishing it from related work on multi-objective decision-making and parameterized goals. The authors correctly identify that while supervised learning for control has been considered previously (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992), their specific formulation using direct future prediction of meaningful measurements in complex 3D environments is novel and effective.

I suggest the authors strengthen the theoretical justification for why this approach works well compared to traditional RL methods. While they mention the connection to Monte Carlo methods, a more detailed explanation of why vectorial measurements provide better supervision than scalar rewards would be valuable. The paper states that "a multidimensional stream of sensations is a more appropriate model for an organism that is learning to function in an immersive environment" but could provide more concrete reasoning.

The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of the relationship between their approach and the "successor representation" framework (Schaul et al., 2015), which they mention as "most closely related" but don't thoroughly differentiate from. Specifically, how does their direct prediction of future measurements differ from the value function approximation in successor representations?

I also recommend adding a brief discussion of potential limitations. For instance, how might the approach perform in environments where the measurement stream is less informative or more sparse? While the Doom environment provides rich measurements, it's worth acknowledging that not all environments will offer such well-defined measurements.

For the authors, I have two questions to help clarify the paper:
1. The paper states that the approach "supports training without a fixed goal and pursuing dynamically changing goals at test time." Could the authors elaborate on how the model handles situations where the goal vector during testing is completely outside the range of goals seen during training?
2. In the Battle scenarios (D3 and D4), the agent has access to three measurements (health, ammunition, and frag count). How would the authors expect the approach to generalize to environments where the number of measurements changes significantly, or where measurements have different dimensionalities?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field. The results are impressive, the approach is novel, and the experimental validation is thorough. The paper addresses a significant challenge in RL - learning from raw sensory input in complex environments - with a simple yet effective solution. The authors have demonstrated that supervised learning techniques can be effectively applied to sensorimotor control when rich, temporally dense measurements are available, and their results provide compelling evidence for this approach.
