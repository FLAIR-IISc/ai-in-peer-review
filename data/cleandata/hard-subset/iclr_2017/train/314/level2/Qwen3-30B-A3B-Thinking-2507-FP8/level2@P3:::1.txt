

The work presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments by framing the problem as supervised learning using a cotemporal stream of sensory input and lower-dimensional measurements. The key contribution is the demonstration that predicting future measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) from raw sensory input and current measurements provides sufficient dense supervision for effective control, eliminating the need for scalar rewards or reward shaping. This formulation enables training without fixed goals and flexible goal adaptation at test time. The approach, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), is evaluated extensively in ViZDoom environments, achieving state-of-the-art results in challenging scenarios (Battle, Battle 2) and winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition by outperforming the second-best submission by over 50%.

The work makes a compelling case for its approach. It clearly positions itself against the standard RL paradigm by arguing that vectorial measurements provide richer, denser supervision than sparse scalar rewards, which is empirically validated through ablation studies (Table 4) showing significant performance gains from using multiple measurements and multiple temporal offsets. The competition win provides strong real-world validation beyond controlled benchmarks. The generalization experiments across textured environments (Table 2) further demonstrate robustness. The method is well-motivated by prior work on supervised learning in control (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992) and vector-valued feedback (Schaul et al., 2015), while clearly distinguishing itself through its application to complex 3D visual environments with raw sensory input.

The paper provides sufficient evidence to support its claims. The experimental design is rigorous: four progressively challenging Doom scenarios, fair comparison against three strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR), extensive hyperparameter tuning for baselines, and controlled ablation studies. The results are statistically sound (averaged over three runs), with clear performance metrics (frags, health) reported across training and test phases. The generalization and goal-agnostic experiments directly address the core claim about flexible goal pursuit. The training procedure (Section 3.1) and architecture (Section 3.2) are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.

Decision: Accept. The work makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that supervised learning with vectorial measurements can outperform sophisticated RL methods in complex 3D environments, providing a simpler, more effective paradigm for sensorimotor control. The evidence is robust, the problem is clearly articulated, and the results are compelling.

Additional feedback to strengthen the paper:
1. Clarify the definition of "measurements" in the introduction. The paper distinguishes sensory input (st) and measurements (mt), but the distinction appears somewhat arbitrary (e.g., why is health a measurement but not the visual input? Why not predict health directly from pixels?). Explicitly state the criteria for designating a variable as a measurement (e.g., "variables directly controllable by the agent's actions" or "variables whose change correlates with goal achievement").
2. Include failure cases. The paper reports strong success in Doom, but it would strengthen the argument to briefly discuss scenarios where the approach might underperform (e.g., environments where measurements are not informative about future states or goals). This would provide a more balanced perspective on the method's limitations.
3. In Section 4.2, the claim that "DFP dominates the other approaches" in Battle/Battle 2 needs slightly more nuance. The performance gap is large, but the paper should acknowledge that DQN and A3C still achieve non-trivial results in simpler scenarios (D1, D2), suggesting the approach's advantage scales with environment complexity. This would better contextualize the contribution.

Key questions for authors:
- How does the method handle environments where the goal cannot be expressed as a linear combination of future measurements (e.g., complex multi-objective goals)? Is the linear goal formulation (Equation 1) a fundamental limitation?
- The paper states that "predicting full sensory streams is beyond our capabilities" (Section 3), yet the model uses raw visual input. Could the approach benefit from incorporating learned feature representations (e.g., from a pre-trained network) for the sensory stream, potentially reducing the dimensionality of st and improving scalability?
