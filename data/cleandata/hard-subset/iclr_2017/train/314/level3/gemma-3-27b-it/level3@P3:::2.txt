This paper presents an on-policy deep reinforcement learning approach for sensorimotor control in 3D environments, specifically utilizing the Doom game environment. The core idea is to reframe reinforcement learning as a supervised learning problem by predicting future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) given current sensory input, actions, and goals. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results, surpassing state-of-the-art deep RL methods and even winning the Visual Doom AI Competition. The work effectively leverages readily available intrinsic signals within the environment to facilitate learning without relying on sparse, externally defined rewards.

**Accept** – The paper demonstrates a clear empirical advantage over existing methods in a challenging domain and provides useful insights into the benefits of rich, temporally dense feedback for reinforcement learning. While the approach isn’t fundamentally novel in its components, its successful integration and demonstration are significant.

The paper’s strength lies in its strong experimental validation. The results consistently show superior performance compared to DQN, A3C, and DSR, particularly in more complex scenarios. The win at the Visual Doom AI Competition is a compelling demonstration of the method’s practical effectiveness. The ablation studies convincingly demonstrate the importance of both vectorial feedback and multi-step prediction. The analysis of generalization across environments is also a valuable contribution. The authors effectively utilize the ViZDoom platform and present a thorough empirical evaluation.

However, the paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of its contributions. While the authors acknowledge that the approach builds upon existing ideas (universal value function approaches, supervised learning for control), the abstract and introduction could more clearly articulate *how* this work advances the field. Is it the specific combination of techniques, the scale of the environment, the emphasis on intrinsic measurements, or a novel architectural element? A more focused framing would strengthen the paper. Additionally, a detailed analysis of failure modes would be beneficial. What types of scenarios or goals does the model struggle with? How does performance degrade when the environment changes drastically? Exploring the limitations of the approach is crucial for understanding its applicability. Finally, the inherent limitation of on-policy learning – catastrophic forgetting – deserves further attention. While the authors mention revisiting goals, a discussion of how this is practically addressed, or potential mitigation strategies, would be valuable. Expanding the experiments to other domains beyond Doom (as suggested by the authors themselves – e.g., Atari with ROM states as intrinsic variables) would broaden the appeal and demonstrate the generalizability of the approach.

Here are some specific questions for the authors:

1. **Contribution Clarification:** Could you elaborate on the key novelty of your approach? Is it the specific architecture, the emphasis on intrinsic measurements, the integration of supervised learning and RL principles, or something else? A clearer articulation of the core contribution would be helpful.
2. **Catastrophic Forgetting:** On-policy learning is susceptible to catastrophic forgetting. Beyond simply revisiting goals, what mechanisms or techniques were employed to mitigate this issue during training, particularly with randomized goals? 
3. **Failure Mode Analysis:** Can you provide examples of situations or goals where the model consistently fails? What are the characteristics of these scenarios, and what factors contribute to the failures?
4. **Goal Representation:** The goal representation `g>f` is relatively simple. Have you experimented with more complex goal representations (e.g., non-linear functions, hierarchical goals)? If so, how did they impact performance?
5. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** How sensitive is the performance of the method to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the learning rate and the coefficients for the different temporal offsets in the loss function?
6. **Computational Cost:** While training speed is reported for baselines, what is the computational cost (GPU hours, etc.) of training the best-performing DFP models? This would provide a more complete picture of the practical feasibility of the approach.
7. **Generalization to Novel Actions:** How well does the model generalize to novel action spaces, or environments with a different set of available actions than those seen during training?
