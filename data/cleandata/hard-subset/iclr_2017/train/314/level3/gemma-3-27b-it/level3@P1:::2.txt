## Review of “Sensorimotor Control with Predictive Representations”

This paper presents an approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, specifically focusing on learning from raw sensory input and dynamically changing goals. The core idea is to reframe the problem as supervised learning by predicting the effect of actions on future measurements, leveraging the rich supervisory signal provided by the cotemporal structure of sensory and measurement streams. The authors demonstrate compelling results in the Doom environment, outperforming state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper presents a solid and well-executed study demonstrating a novel and effective approach to sensorimotor control. The empirical results are convincing, and the contribution—while largely based on integrating existing ideas—is significant in its practical application and demonstrable success in a challenging environment. The win in the Visual Doom AI competition is a particularly strong indicator of the approach’s effectiveness.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper is clearly written and well-motivated. The authors effectively position their work within the existing literature on reinforcement learning, supervised learning, and related approaches like imitation learning and model-based RL. They convincingly argue for the benefits of their approach – particularly the ability to learn without a fixed goal and the richer, temporally dense supervision provided by the measurement stream compared to sparse rewards.

The experimental results are thorough and well-presented. The authors demonstrate consistent outperformance over strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across multiple Doom scenarios of increasing difficulty. Crucially, the results show generalization to new environments (through the texture randomization experiments) and to dynamically changing goals. The ablation studies provide valuable insights into the importance of both multivariate measurements and predicting at multiple temporal offsets. The success in the Visual Doom AI Competition adds substantial weight to the claims.

The architecture of the predictor network, while drawing inspiration from existing approaches like DQN, is well-justified and appears appropriately designed for the task. The detailed description of the training procedure and hyperparameter settings allows for reproducibility.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong overall, several points could be addressed to further improve it. 

Firstly, the abstract and introduction could be more explicit about the key novelty of the work. The paper acknowledges it's built upon existing ideas – framing the problem as supervised learning isn't entirely new – but the specific contribution of distilling these ideas and achieving state-of-the-art performance in a complex environment deserves more emphasis. Highlighting the key architectural choices and how they contribute to the success would also be beneficial.

Secondly, a discussion of the limitations and failure modes of the approach would be valuable. Under what conditions does the model struggle to generalize to new goals? Are there specific types of environments or tasks where the approach is less effective? Analyzing these scenarios would provide a more nuanced understanding of the method’s strengths and weaknesses. 

A potential concern is the on-policy nature of the training. Catastrophic forgetting could be an issue if goals from the distant past are not revisited during training. While the authors mention experience replay, its impact on mitigating this issue could be explicitly discussed. Exploring strategies to address this, such as periodic rehearsal of past goals, would be a valuable extension.

Expanding the experiments to other domains beyond Doom would strengthen the claim of broader applicability. Investigating the performance of the approach on Atari games, for example, using the game’s ROM as the source of intrinsic measurements, would be a compelling demonstration.

Finally, the parameter selection for the goal vector (τ1, . . . , τn and the coefficients (0.5, 0.5, 1)) could be discussed further. Was a sensitivity analysis performed? How do different choices impact the model’s performance?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the key differences between your approach and existing universal value function approaches? While you cite relevant work, a more detailed comparison would be helpful.
2.  What failure modes have you observed in the Doom environment, and under what conditions does the model struggle to generalize to new goals?
3.  How does the on-policy nature of the training affect the model’s ability to retain knowledge of previously encountered goals, and what strategies (if any) are employed to mitigate catastrophic forgetting?
4.  Have you explored alternative choices for the temporal offsets (τ1, . . . , τn) and the coefficients used in the goal function? If so, what impact did these choices have on performance?
5.  Do you have any insights into why the multivariate measurement stream is so crucial to the success of the approach? What properties of the measurements contribute to the improved performance compared to a scalar reward?
