This paper proposes a novel on-policy method, Direct Future Prediction (DFP), for sensorimotor control in immersive environments. Instead of learning from scalar rewards, DFP learns to predict future intrinsic measurements, leveraging the rich supervisory signal present in the cotemporal structure of sensory input and measurement streams. The approach was successfully applied to the challenging VizDoom environment, achieving state-of-the-art results and winning the 2016 VizDoom AI Competition.

**Accept** – The experimental results, particularly winning the VizDoom competition, are compelling evidence of the method’s efficacy. The clarity of the approach and its grounding in established learning paradigms, despite a novel twist, justify publication.

The core contribution lies in framing sensorimotor control as a supervised learning problem focused on predicting future measurements rather than optimizing for a reward. This is a well-motivated departure from traditional reinforcement learning, especially in environments with dense, multi-dimensional feedback. The paper effectively positions itself within the existing literature, drawing connections to intrinsic motivation, auxiliary tasks, and forward modeling. The experimental setup is robust, employing a challenging environment (VizDoom) and demonstrating strong performance against established baselines (A3C, DQN, and a DSR attempt). The ablation study further strengthens the claims by showing the importance of the proposed model components. The success in the VizDoom competition provides significant validation.

Despite the strong positive assessment, several points could improve the paper. Firstly, while the authors acknowledge Doom's presentation as "3D," clarifying that it is fundamentally a 2D game presented with pseudo-3D rendering would enhance accuracy. Secondly, the notation could be refined. The use of “P” in equation (2) is misleading, as it represents a prediction and not a probability. Additionally, the double use of “j” in equation (6) may cause confusion; a different variable name would improve readability. Finally, the results tables would benefit from repeating units for heterogeneous measurements, aiding in direct comparison. For example, when reporting health, ammunition, and frags, explicitly stating the units (e.g., health points, rounds, count) in the column headers would be beneficial.

I have several questions for the authors:

1.  **Goal Vector Initialization:** How are the goal vectors (g) initialized in the randomized goals training regime? Is there any sensitivity to the initial distribution of goal weights?
2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While ablation studies are performed, a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the approach to hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, mini-batch size, network architecture) would strengthen the results.
3.  **Computational Cost:** While training speed is reported for baselines, a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources required for DFP training (e.g., GPU type, training time) would be valuable for reproducibility.
4.  **Generalization to Other Environments:** The paper demonstrates good generalization within Doom environments. Could the authors comment on the expected performance of DFP in substantially different environments (e.g., a robotic manipulation task) and the potential modifications needed to adapt the approach?
5.  **Impact of Temporal Offset Selection:** How was the selection of temporal offsets (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, and 32) determined? Was any exploration of alternative offset schedules performed, and if so, what were the results?
