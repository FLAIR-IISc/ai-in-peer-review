This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, framing the problem as supervised learning rather than traditional reinforcement learning. The core idea – predicting low-dimensional measurements conditioned on goals and actions – is compelling, and the experimental results, including winning the Visual Doom AI Competition, are impressive. The paper is well-written and clearly communicates its contributions.

**Accept** – This paper presents a significant advancement in deep reinforcement learning, particularly for environments with rich sensory input and clear, measurable goals. The strong empirical results, coupled with a novel approach that deviates from standard RL paradigms, warrant acceptance.

The approach’s strength lies in its effective use of readily available supervisory signals. By focusing on predicting measurements (health, ammo, etc.), the authors circumvent the challenges of sparse rewards common in RL. This enables stable and efficient learning directly from raw sensory data, a crucial step towards more general and robust AI agents. The generalization across environments and goals demonstrated is particularly noteworthy, suggesting the learned policies possess a degree of adaptability not often seen in RL. The performance in the Visual Doom AI Competition provides strong validation of the approach’s practicality and effectiveness. The authors clearly position their work within the existing literature, acknowledging prior attempts and articulating the novelty of their contribution – specifically, the focus on Monte Carlo estimation, low-dimensional measurements, parametrized goal conditioning, and comprehensive empirical comparisons.

However, two minor concerns merit consideration. First, the use of a replay buffer introduces an on-policy assumption violation. While the authors acknowledge this, a more detailed discussion of its potential impact on convergence and stability would strengthen the paper. The replay buffer enables learning from past experiences that weren’t collected using the *current* policy, creating a mismatch. Addressing this could involve analyzing the buffer's impact through ablation studies or discussing the benefits of off-policy learning in this context.

Second, the method relies on access to “extra metadata” – the specific measurements to predict. The authors correctly note this limitation, but it should be emphasized further. The advantage over competing algorithms stems, in part, from knowing *which* signals are informative.  The paper should explicitly discuss the implications when such metadata is unavailable or difficult to define – what happens when only raw sensory input is available, and no readily apparent measurements exist? This limitation is critical for assessing the broader applicability of the method.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper would benefit from a discussion of the sensitivity of the approach to hyperparameters. Were extensive hyperparameter sweeps conducted? How robust is the performance to changes in learning rate, network architecture, or other key parameters?
*   **Ablation Study Expansion:** While an ablation study is included, expanding it to explore the impact of different measurement combinations could be insightful. Does performance degrade linearly with fewer measurements? Are some measurements more crucial than others?
*   **Goal Representation:** The use of linear combinations of future measurements to define goals is a reasonable starting point. However, exploring more complex goal representations (e.g., non-linear functions, hierarchical goals) could unlock further improvements in flexibility and generalization.
*   **Computational Cost:** While training speed is mentioned in the baselines section, a more detailed analysis of the computational resources required for training would be helpful, particularly considering the scale of the simulations.
*   **Visualizations:**  More detailed visualizations of the learned representations within the network (e.g., activation maps) could provide further insight into how the model is processing sensory information and making predictions.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the replay buffer, did you observe any instability or convergence issues that could be attributed to the off-policy nature of the learning? Were any techniques (e.g., importance sampling) considered to mitigate the impact of this approximation?
2.  How does the performance of the method degrade when access to the "extra metadata" (measurements) is limited or unavailable? Are there strategies for identifying informative measurements automatically from raw sensory data?
3.  Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process? What strategies were used to select the optimal hyperparameters, and how sensitive is the performance to changes in these parameters?
4.  What is the computational cost of training the model, and how does it scale with the complexity of the environment and the number of actions?
5.  Are there any specific failure cases or limitations of the approach that you observed during your experiments?
