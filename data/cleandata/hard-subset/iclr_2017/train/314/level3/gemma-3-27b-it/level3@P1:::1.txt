## Review of "Sensorimotor Control with Prediction of Measurements"

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, framed as supervised learning through the prediction of low-dimensional measurements conditioned on actions and goals. The approach demonstrably outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods in the challenging domain of Doom, achieving particularly strong results in generalization across environments and goal adaptation. Notably, a model trained using this method won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, a significant achievement.

**Decision: Accept** with enthusiasm. The paper tackles a relevant problem – learning complex sensorimotor skills from raw sensory input – and provides a compelling solution with strong empirical validation. The successful performance in the Visual Doom AI Competition is a particularly convincing demonstration of the method’s effectiveness. 

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of recasting sensorimotor control as a supervised prediction problem is well-motivated. The authors effectively argue that the temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides a rich supervisory signal, obviating the need for sparse, hand-engineered rewards. This is a particularly valuable contribution, given the challenges associated with reward design in complex environments. The emphasis on learning from raw experience, without relying on imitation or hand-crafted features, is also a strength. 

The experimental results are robust and thoroughly presented. The comparisons to established deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) are comprehensive, and the ablation studies effectively demonstrate the importance of the key design choices, namely the use of vectorial measurements and multi-step future prediction. The generalization experiments and the successful performance in the Visual Doom AI competition provide compelling evidence of the method’s practical utility and robustness.  The fact that the method outperformed a significantly more complex model with additional supervision in the competition highlights the efficiency and elegance of the proposed approach.

The authors clearly and concisely explain the method, providing sufficient detail to allow for replication. The network architecture is well-described, and the training procedure is clearly outlined. The related work section is comprehensive and appropriately contextualizes the contribution within the broader field of reinforcement learning and sensorimotor control.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong overall, a few minor points could be addressed to further enhance its clarity and impact. 

*   **On-Policy vs. Off-Policy Training:** The use of a replay buffer introduces an off-policy element into what is fundamentally an on-policy supervised learning framework. While this is a common practice, a brief discussion of the potential implications of this approximation – such as the introduction of bias or the need for careful regularization – would be beneficial.
*   **Dependency on Measurements:** The method relies on access to informative, low-dimensional measurements (e.g., health, ammo). The authors acknowledge this, but a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach in scenarios where such measurements are unavailable or difficult to obtain would strengthen the paper. How would the approach perform if only raw sensory input were available? 
*   **Goal Representation:** The use of linear combinations of future measurements as the goal representation is relatively simple. Exploring more sophisticated goal representations could potentially lead to further improvements in performance and generalization.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the method to key hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, batch size, network architecture) would be valuable.
*   **Computational Cost:** A comparison of the computational cost (training time, memory usage) of the proposed approach relative to the baselines would provide a more complete picture of its practical advantages and disadvantages.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of the method to the choice of temporal offsets (τ1, ..., τn) used for future measurement prediction? Did you perform an ablation study to investigate the optimal number and spacing of these offsets?
2.  You mention the potential for extending the method to incorporate more sophisticated goal representations. Could you elaborate on potential directions for future research in this area?
3.  The method appears to rely on the availability of informative measurements. What strategies might be employed to learn useful measurements directly from raw sensory input, thereby extending the applicability of the approach to scenarios where measurements are not readily available?
4.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources required to train the models, including training time and memory usage, particularly in comparison to the baseline methods?
