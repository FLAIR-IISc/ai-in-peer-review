This paper presents an approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, specifically utilizing the Visual Doom AI Competition as a testbed. The core idea is to frame the learning problem as supervised prediction of future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) given current sensory input and actions, thereby sidestepping the complexities of traditional reward-based reinforcement learning. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming approaches with more complex architectures and additional supervision.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper addresses a challenging problem in reinforcement learning – learning from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments – and proposes a compelling solution. The results are strong, convincingly demonstrating the efficacy of the proposed approach, and the winning performance in a competitive benchmark provides significant validation.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of traditional reinforcement learning approaches in the context of complex, visually rich environments. The authors effectively position their work within the existing literature, referencing relevant work on supervised learning, reinforcement learning, and related approaches like temporal difference learning and universal value function approximators. The connection to prior work is thoughtfully discussed and acknowledges the contribution as an integration and distillation of existing ideas rather than a completely novel algorithmic breakthrough, which is honest and appropriate.

The empirical evaluation is extensive and well-designed. The authors compare their approach (DFP) to several strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across multiple Doom scenarios of increasing difficulty. The results consistently show DFP outperforming the baselines, particularly in the more challenging scenarios. The ablation studies convincingly demonstrate the importance of both multivariate measurements and prediction at multiple temporal offsets.  The generalization experiments, showing robustness to changes in environment textures, are particularly noteworthy. Finally, the success in the Visual Doom AI Competition serves as a strong independent validation of the approach’s effectiveness.  The inclusion of training speeds for each method is also appreciated.

The model architecture is clearly described and justified. The use of a perception module, measurement module, and goal module, along with the expectation and action streams, is well-explained and appears to be a sensible design choice. The connection to prior work, like Wang et al. (2016), is appropriately acknowledged.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong overall, some areas could be improved. The abstract and introduction could more clearly articulate the specific contribution of the work. While the authors acknowledge that the approach builds on existing ideas, highlighting the unique combination and application of these ideas, and their resulting benefits, would strengthen the paper.

The paper mentions the potential for catastrophic forgetting due to on-policy training. Exploring this further – perhaps with experiments showing performance degradation when revisiting older goals – would be valuable.  Analyzing failure modes, and identifying specific situations where the model struggles to generalize to new goals, would also strengthen the analysis. This would provide insights into the limitations of the approach and suggest directions for future research.

The authors could consider expanding the experimental evaluation beyond the Doom environment. While Doom provides a compelling and challenging testbed, demonstrating the applicability of the approach to other domains (e.g., Atari games using the game’s ROM state as the “measurements”) would significantly broaden the impact of the work.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing specific temporal offsets (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32)? Was any experimentation done with different offset combinations, or other ways of weighting future measurements?
2.  The ablation study demonstrates the importance of predicting multiple measurements. Was any investigation done into feature selection – i.e., identifying which measurements are most crucial for performance?
3.  The paper mentions the experience replay memory size (M). How sensitive are the results to the size of this memory?
4.  The competition-winning model is described as a variant of D4-tx-L. Could you provide more details on the architectural differences between D4-tx-L and the standard model described in the paper, and explain why these changes were beneficial?
5.  Regarding catastrophic forgetting, have you considered any techniques to mitigate this issue, such as periodic replay of experiences from earlier goals?
