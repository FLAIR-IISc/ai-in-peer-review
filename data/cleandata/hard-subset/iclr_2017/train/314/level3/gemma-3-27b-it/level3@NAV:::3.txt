## Review of “Sensorimotor Control via Direct Future Prediction”

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control, DFP, which departs from traditional reward-based reinforcement learning by directly predicting future intrinsic measurements instead. The core idea – leveraging the rich supervisory signal provided by the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams – is well-motivated and intuitively appealing. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results in the challenging VizDoom environment, even winning the 2016 competition, and convincingly show performance gains over established methods like A3C, DQN, and a DSR baseline. The ablation study further solidifies the importance of the proposed model components. 

I **accept** this paper for publication. The experimental validation is exceptionally strong, the method achieves a milestone (winning the VizDoom competition), and the approach offers a compelling alternative perspective on sensorimotor learning. While not necessarily radically novel in its individual components, the specific combination and application to this problem space are significant.

The paper effectively situates itself within the existing literature, acknowledging relevant work in intrinsic motivation, auxiliary tasks, and forward modeling. The experiments are well-designed and the results are clearly presented. The success in the VizDoom competition provides a strong, independent validation of the approach. The ablation study provides clear evidence that each component of the architecture contributes meaningfully to the overall performance. 

However, several minor improvements could further enhance the paper. The description of Doom as “three-dimensional” is inaccurate; it's presented in 2D with fake 3D effects. The use of “P” in equation (2) is misleading as it denotes prediction, not probability. Additionally, the double use of “j” in equation (6) could be clarified. Finally, results tables would benefit from repeating units for heterogeneous measurements (e.g., displaying “health %”, “ammo count”, “frags” in each column header).

I have a few questions for the authors:

1.  The paper mentions the potential for extending the model with memory-based architectures. Could you elaborate on how you envision integrating a memory component (e.g., LSTM) into the DFP framework, and what challenges you anticipate?
2.  The training regimes with randomized goals yielded promising results. Were any strategies employed to ensure exploration of the goal space during training, or did the agent effectively discover useful goals on its own?
3.  The performance gap between DFP and the baselines appears substantial. Could you offer any insights into *why* DFP performs so well, beyond simply the benefits of dense, multivariate feedback? Is it the specific architecture, the training procedure, or an interaction between the two?
