This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, framing the problem as supervised learning through prediction of future measurements conditioned on actions and goals. The authors demonstrate compelling results in the Doom environment, outperforming state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition. The core idea of leveraging the cotemporal structure of sensory and measurement streams as a supervisory signal is insightful and well-executed.

**Decision:** Accept with minor revisions. The paper addresses a challenging problem with a clear and effective solution, supported by strong experimental evidence. The performance gains, particularly in the challenging Doom environments and the competition win, are significant and convincingly demonstrate the value of the proposed approach.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles a significant problem in machine learning: sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex, dynamic environments. While reinforcement learning has made progress, it often struggles with sparse rewards and the exploration challenge. This work offers a compelling alternative by reformulating the problem as supervised learning, leveraging the readily available information contained in measurements (health, ammo, etc.). The motivation is clear and well-placed within the existing literature, contrasting the approach with traditional RL and highlighting the advantages of dense, multidimensional feedback. 

The experimental results are particularly strong. The consistent outperformance across multiple Doom scenarios, including the more complex Battle tasks, is impressive. The ablation studies convincingly demonstrate the importance of both vectorial feedback (multiple measurements) and predicting at multiple temporal offsets. The generalization experiments show that the learned policies are robust and adaptable to new environments. The win at the Visual Doom AI Competition provides independent validation of the approach’s effectiveness.  The thorough comparison to existing methods (DQN, A3C, DSR) demonstrates a clear advantage for the proposed method.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and results. The architecture is well-described and the implementation details, while relegated to the appendix, are sufficient to understand the approach.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **On-Policy vs. Replay Buffer:** The paper utilizes a replay buffer despite framing the learning process as supervised Monte Carlo, which inherently suggests an on-policy approach. This discrepancy isn’t explicitly discussed. A brief discussion of the implications of using an off-policy component (the replay buffer) and its potential impact on convergence or sample efficiency would strengthen the paper.
*   **Measurement Dependence:** The method relies on identifying informative measurements to predict. The paper acknowledges this but could be strengthened by explicitly discussing the limitations of the approach when such measurements are not readily available or are noisy. A discussion of how the method might be extended to learn measurements directly from the raw sensory input would be valuable.
*   **Goal Representation:** The choice of a linear combination for the goal function `u(f; g)` is reasonable, but the paper could briefly explore the potential benefits of more complex goal representations, potentially using learned embeddings.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the approach to key hyperparameters, particularly the learning rate and the size of the replay buffer, would be helpful.
*   **Further Generalization Tests:** While the generalization experiments are good, exploring generalization to different *types* of environments (e.g., different game genres) could further demonstrate the robustness of the approach.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the impact of using a replay buffer within a Monte Carlo-based learning framework? Are there any observed differences in performance or convergence compared to a purely on-policy approach?
2.  How would the approach perform in scenarios where informative measurements are difficult to define or are unavailable? Have you considered strategies for learning relevant measurements directly from the raw sensory input?
3.  What is the sensitivity of the approach to the learning rate and replay buffer size? Were any specific strategies used for tuning these hyperparameters?
4.  The paper demonstrates generalization across textured environments. Have you considered evaluating generalization to fundamentally different environments (e.g., different games, simulated robotic tasks)?
5.  Regarding the goal representation: was any consideration given to exploring more complex goal representations, such as learned embeddings, rather than linear combinations? If so, what were the reasons for choosing the linear representation?
