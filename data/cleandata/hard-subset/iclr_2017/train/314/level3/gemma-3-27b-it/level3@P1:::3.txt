## Review of “Sensorimotor Control with Predictive Measurements”

**1. Summary of Claims**

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, framing the problem as supervised learning rather than reinforcement learning. The core idea is to predict future intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) given current sensory input, action, and goal. This prediction serves as a supervisory signal, enabling the agent to learn effective control policies without explicit rewards. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach—dubbed Direct Future Prediction (DFP)—in the challenging Doom environment, outperforming state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods, including A3C and DQN, and winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition. Crucially, the method demonstrates generalization to unseen environments and can adapt to dynamically changing goals.

**2. Decision**

**Accept**. The paper presents a strong and compelling contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on a challenging task, and the winning performance in the Visual Doom AI Competition further validates the approach. The framing of sensorimotor control as supervised prediction is a potentially impactful shift in perspective. 

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper’s key strength lies in its empirical validation. The authors conducted extensive experiments in the Doom environment, systematically comparing DFP to established RL algorithms. The performance gains observed, particularly in complex scenarios and generalization tests, are significant and statistically persuasive. The ablation study provides valuable insights into the importance of the various components of the proposed model, justifying the design choices made. 

Furthermore, the success in the Visual Doom AI Competition is a strong endorsement of the method’s practical applicability and robustness. Beating a competitor utilizing a more complex model *and* additional supervision is a testament to the efficacy of the DFP approach. The ability to learn without a fixed goal and adapt to dynamically changing goals at test time is a particularly noteworthy achievement, addressing a key challenge in RL. The clear articulation of the motivation, linking it to the limitations of traditional RL frameworks, is also commendable. 

The work is well-situated within the existing literature, referencing relevant papers on supervised learning, reinforcement learning, and predictive representations. The authors acknowledge related works and clearly articulate how their approach differs and contributes to the field.

**4. Additional Feedback**

While the paper is excellent overall, a few points could be addressed to further improve its clarity and impact:

*   **Terminology:** The description of Doom as a “three-dimensional” environment is technically inaccurate; it is a 2.5D environment presented with a 3D perspective. Clarifying this would improve precision.
*   **Notational Clarity:** The use of “p” in equation (2) is potentially misleading as it commonly denotes probability. A different symbol might be more appropriate to avoid confusion. Similarly, the repeated use of “j” in equation (6) could be improved for clarity.
*   **Presentation of Results:** In the tables presenting experimental results, include units for each measurement (e.g., "Health (%)", "Ammo (units)", "Frags (count)") to ensure easier interpretation, especially as the units are not necessarily consistent across all measurements.
*   **Environment Diversity:** While the experiments are thorough within the Doom environment, the generalization tests still rely on variations *within* Doom. Exploring generalization to fundamentally different environments would strengthen the claim of broad applicability.
*   **Computational Cost:** A brief discussion of the computational cost of training DFP compared to the baseline methods would be valuable.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind choosing the specific temporal offsets (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32) for predicting future measurements? Was any sensitivity analysis performed on these values?
*   In the ablation study, you demonstrate the importance of predicting multiple measurements. Were there any interactions observed between the predicted measurements? For example, did predicting health improve performance when predicting frags, and vice versa?
*   The paper mentions that the goal vector can represent exponential decay. Could you provide a more detailed example of how this would be used in practice, perhaps with a specific scenario in Doom?
*   Could you discuss the limitations of this approach? Are there specific types of environments or tasks where you anticipate it would be less effective?
*   You mentioned that the presented approach outperformed a model from the visual doom competition, but that the competitor used additional supervision. Could you provide some additional details regarding the nature of the additional supervision utilized by the competitor to assess the difficulty of the comparison?
