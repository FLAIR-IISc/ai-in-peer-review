This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, framing the problem as supervised learning through prediction of future intrinsic measurements rather than relying on traditional reward-based reinforcement learning. The core idea—leveraging the rich, temporally dense information within the interplay of sensory input and agent state (measurements)—is compelling and demonstrably effective, as evidenced by strong empirical results, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a sound approach, thoroughly evaluated, that achieves state-of-the-art results on a challenging benchmark. The success in the Visual Doom AI Competition further strengthens the validity and significance of the work.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles a significant problem in reinforcement learning: learning effective sensorimotor control directly from raw sensory input in complex environments. The proposed approach is well-motivated, building on the limitations of sparse reward signals in traditional RL and the potential benefits of leveraging rich, intrinsic feedback. The authors convincingly argue that the cotemporal structure of sensory streams and measurements provides a valuable supervisory signal, enabling learning without a fixed goal at training time and facilitating adaptation to dynamically changing goals.

The empirical evaluation is robust and comprehensive. The authors compare their approach (DFP) against strong baselines (DQN, A3C, and DSR) across a range of Doom scenarios of increasing difficulty. DFP consistently outperforms these methods, particularly in more complex environments where the availability of multiple measurements proves advantageous. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the importance of both vectorial feedback (multiple measurements) and multi-step prediction, validating the core design choices. The generalization experiments, showing performance in randomly textured environments, and the success in the Visual Doom AI Competition, provide strong evidence for the robustness and adaptability of the proposed approach. The performance advantage over the second-place submission in the competition – a more complex model with additional supervision – is particularly noteworthy.

The authors clearly articulate their contributions, situating their work within the broader landscape of reinforcement learning, supervised learning, and related areas such as forward modeling and intrinsic motivation. The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the model architecture and training procedure.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where further clarification or refinement could enhance its impact:

*   **Environment Description:** The paper refers to Doom as a “three-dimensional” environment. While the game *presents* a 3D perspective, it is fundamentally a 2.5D game. Acknowledging this would improve precision.
*   **Notational Clarity:** The use of "p" in equation (2) to denote the predicted values is somewhat misleading, as it commonly signifies probability. A different variable name might avoid confusion. The reuse of 'j' in equation (6) could also be clarified.
*   **Table Presentation:** In the results tables, the units for the different measurements (health, ammunition, frags) are not consistently presented. Including units (e.g., “health (%), ammo (units), frags (count)”) would improve readability.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While ablation studies are provided, a brief discussion of the sensitivity of performance to key hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, network architecture) would be valuable.
*   **Future Work:** The authors briefly mention potential extensions (memory, hierarchical organization, continuous actions). Expanding on these ideas, and perhaps discussing their potential challenges, would strengthen the discussion section.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The ablation study demonstrates the benefit of predicting multiple future time steps. Was there a point of diminishing returns with increasing the number of predicted time steps, or did performance continue to improve with more distant predictions?
2.  The paper highlights the success of DFP in adapting to new goals. How does the performance of DFP compare to that of a model specifically trained on each new goal? Is there a trade-off between the ability to generalize to new goals and the performance on specific goals?
3.  The winning submission to the Visual Doom AI Competition utilized a more complex model and additional supervision. Could you elaborate on the specific advantages of your simpler approach, and whether the additional supervision utilized by the second-place submission represents a significant advantage in other scenarios?
4.  The paper refers to "randomized goals" in training. What specific distribution was used for generating the goal vectors, and how might different distributions impact the resulting performance?
