- Paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): supervised learning to predict low-dimensional future measurements conditioned on goals and actions for sensorimotor control in 3D environments.
- DFP uses dense, vectorial feedback and avoids temporal-difference updates and scalar rewards common in RL.
- Key technical contributions: Monte Carlo estimation, goal parameterization, and multi-step prediction formulation.
- Empirical claim: DFP outperforms state-of-the-art RL baselines and won the Visual Doom AI competition.
- Decision: Accept.
- Novelty and generalization: Introduces a supervised approach that generalizes across changing goals and unseen environments.
- Empirical strength: Strong, rigorous results with extensive comparisons to DQN, A3C, DSR and ablations validating core components.
- Motivation: Well-grounded in literature; addresses sparse rewards and generalization by leveraging dense multivariate feedback.
- Adaptability: Demonstrated ability to adapt to new goals without retraining.
- Feedback — On-policy assumption: Replay buffer introduces off-policy elements; discuss impact on convergence and stability.
- Feedback — Metadata limitations: Reliance on low-dimensional measurements raises questions about performance when such metadata is missing or noisy.
- Feedback — Memory and temporal abstraction: Lacks recurrent/ hierarchical components; suggest discussing integration with memory or temporal abstraction methods.
- Question — Measurement availability: How does DFP perform when low-dimensional measurements are unavailable or must be inferred from raw sensory input?
- Question — Computational trade-offs: What are the sample-efficiency and scalability trade-offs versus TD-based methods, especially for larger action spaces?
- Question — Continuous actions: Can the architecture be adapted to continuous action spaces and what performance impacts are expected?
