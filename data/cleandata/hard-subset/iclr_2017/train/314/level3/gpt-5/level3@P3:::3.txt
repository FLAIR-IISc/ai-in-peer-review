1) Summary of contributions
The paper introduces Direct Future Prediction (DFP), an on-policy, goal-conditioned sensorimotor control method that predicts future intrinsic measurements rather than scalar rewards. Instead of estimating values or policies via Bellman targets, DFP trains a supervised predictor F(o, a, g) of goal-weighted future measurement changes at multiple horizons and selects actions that maximize the inner product with a goal vector g. This turns control into supervised learning driven by dense, multivariate feedback (e.g., health, ammo, frags), enabling: (i) fast, stable training from raw pixels and measurements, (ii) goal-agnostic training and test-time goal switching, and (iii) simple, greedy action selection that maximizes long-term goals.

Experiments in ViZDoom predict sequences over triplets (health, ammunition, frags) at multiple time offsets; actions at test time maximize g^T F. Goal vectors can be changed to guide exploration and behavior. An architecture with perception, measurement, and goal modules, and a dueling-style split (expectation vs action streams) supports accurate multi-action, multi-horizon predictions.

Empirically, DFP achieves strong results: it outperforms or matches DQN and A3C in several scenarios, shows clear gains with multi-measurement signals, generalizes across textures and goals, and won the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition (Full Deathmatch) without extra supervision. Ablations indicate that vectorial measurement prediction and multi-horizon prediction materially contribute to performance. The paper situates DFP among work on intrinsic motivation, auxiliary tasks, forward modeling, and UVFAs; the references are appropriate though not exhaustive.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, carefully executed empirical results with compelling wins over strong baselines (A3C, DQN) and a public competition victory; good ablations and generalization studies.
- Clear, simple formulation with a practical training signal (dense multivariate measurements) that enables goal-agnostic training and test-time goal selection; high reproducibility and training speed.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem and approach: The paper targets sensorimotor control from pixels with dynamic goals. Predicting future intrinsic measurements aligns the training signal with what matters for control, sidestepping sparse reward and bootstrapping instability.
- Motivation and placement: The method is well motivated as a Monte-Carlo-style, supervised alternative when dense feedback exists, connected to UVFAs, predictive representations, and auxiliary/forward models. The literature coverage is adequate for ICLR 2017.
- Empirical rigor:
  - Benchmarks: Four ViZDoom scenarios of increasing difficulty; clear training/testing protocols; multi-horizon targets; consistent evaluation metrics.
  - Comparisons: DFP matches A3C on Basic, surpasses it on Navigation, and strongly dominates in Battle and Battle 2 (A3C appears to hit a ceiling in one scenario). DSR performs worse and is slower.
  - Generalization: Training on multi-texture variants improves transfer to new textures; higher-capacity variants further improve results.
  - Goals: Goal-randomized training yields near-parity on the main goal and better generalization to new goals (e.g., pacifist, ammo-drain).
  - Ablations: Predicting multiple measurements and multiple horizons both help; the added architectural structure (dueling-style split) appears beneficial.
  - External validation: Winning the 2016 competition in unseen maps underscores robustness and practical impact.
- Reproducibility and soundness: Architecture, horizons, losses, and baselines are described in sufficient detail; training is fast (thousands of FPS) and episodes are well specified. Although a small replay buffer departs slightly from pure on-policy, the approach is essentially on-policy in spirit and practice.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve; not central to the accept decision)
- Clarify “3D” vs “2D presented in fake 3D.” Doom is rendered as pseudo-3D; a brief clarification would avoid nitpicks.
- Notation:
  - Equation (2): The symbol p (or P) is used for predictions, which may be confused with probability. Consider F or y for predictions, reserving p for probabilities.
  - Equation (6): The double use of “j” (as joint input and within normalization) may mislead; rename the joint input to φ or h, and keep indices distinct.
- Tables/units: When reporting heterogeneous measurements (health, ammo, frags), repeat units/scales in every table to aid readability; normalize or clearly denote normalization (e.g., “normalized health ∈ [0,1]”).
- On-policy claim: Since you keep a small experience memory, briefly discuss why this does not induce off-policy bias in practice and report sensitivity to memory size.
- Baselines: Note that A3C can benefit from auxiliary tasks; acknowledge this and emphasize the apples-to-apples setup used here. A short comparison/contrast with UVFAs (Schaul et al.) trained on the same goals would strengthen the positioning.
- Limitations and scope: Call out the limited environment diversity beyond Doom, lack of memory/recurrence, and discrete action restriction; consider a short discussion of how DFP could be extended to recurrent or continuous-action settings.

Questions for the authors
- Goal sampling: How sensitive is performance to the distribution over training goals (e.g., [0,1] vs [-1,1])? Does curriculum over goals help?
- Measurement design: How were measurements normalized/scaled across scenarios? Did you try learned measurement embeddings or additional intrinsic measurements (e.g., velocity, damage taken)?
- Multi-horizon targets: Why choose the specific offsets and terminal weighting (0.5, 0.5, 1)? How does performance vary with these choices?
- Exploration: Beyond ε-greedy, did goal variation itself materially aid exploration? Any evidence that changing goal weights during training improves coverage?
- Architecture: Can you quantify the gain from the expectation/action-stream split and action-normalization relative to a single-head predictor across all actions?
- Off-policy buffer: What is the maximum buffer size before you observe degradation (i.e., becoming too off-policy)?
- Goal switching online: Can the agent switch goals mid-episode and respond immediately? Any experiments with rapidly changing goals?
- Statistical robustness: Are the improvements significant across seeds for the hardest scenarios? Please include variance/error bars for the final evaluations.
