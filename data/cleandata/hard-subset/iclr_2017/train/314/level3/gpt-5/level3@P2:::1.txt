Summary of contributions
- Problem: Learning sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments and enabling test-time flexibility to pursue dynamically specified goals.
- Idea: Reformulate control as supervised prediction of low-dimensional intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) into the future, conditioned on current observation, candidate action, and a goal vector. Select actions by maximizing predicted goal utility.
- Method: A deep network predicts future measurement differences at multiple temporal offsets for all actions, using an expectation/action-stream decomposition (akin to dueling networks). Training is Monte-Carlo regression on experience, without TD bootstrapping or extraneous supervision.
- Results: Strong empirical performance on ViZDoom scenarios (D1–D4), with particularly large gains on challenging tasks versus DQN and A3C; generalization across textures/environments and goals; goal-agnostic training that adapts to new goals at test time; ablations showing benefits of vectorial measurements and multi-horizon prediction. The approach won the Visual Doom AI Competition (Full Deathmatch).

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) Simple, well-motivated formulation that cleanly connects supervised prediction with goal-conditioned control, positioned relative to UVFAs and successor features, and grounded in longstanding Monte Carlo vs TD discussions.
2) Compelling, thorough empirical evidence: consistent improvements over strong RL baselines on visually rich 3D tasks, successful cross-goal and cross-environment generalization, and a public competition win.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? The paper targets learning visuomotor policies from scratch in immersive 3D worlds and enabling post-training goal changes. It addresses sparse/unstable RL learning by exploiting dense, vector-valued intrinsic measurements.
- Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The work thoughtfully revisits SL vs TD learning in settings with dense multidimensional feedback, connects to Monte Carlo estimation, multi-objective RL, UVFAs, successor representations, and predictive state representations. The goal conditioning and measurement-centric framing are clearly articulated and justified.
- Do results support claims? The comparisons to DQN and A3C are carefully run, with multiple runs and hyperparameter searches for baselines. The largest gains occur when multiple measurements are available, matching the method’s premise. Ablations isolate contributions of vectorial feedback and multi-horizon prediction. Generalization experiments (texture randomization and unseen maps) and goal-agnostic training substantiate adaptability claims. The competition result strengthens external validity.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual advance: act via direct future prediction of intrinsic measurements, avoiding bootstrapping instabilities.
- Elegant architecture and training procedure; efficient training leveraging dense supervision.
- Strong empirical coverage: multiple tasks of increasing difficulty, environment and goal generalization, ablations, and an external benchmark (competition).
- Practical significance: demonstrates that explicit measurement prediction can be a robust alternative to TD-based deep RL in complex 3D settings.

Weaknesses and concerns (non-blocking)
- On-/off-policy mismatch: Although the method is presented as supervised on-policy Monte Carlo, a replay buffer and periodically updated policy introduce off-policy elements. The paper notes no adverse effects, but a brief analysis/discussion of potential bias or stability implications would improve rigor.
- Assumption of available measurement streams: The approach leverages low-dimensional intrinsic measurements as prediction targets. Many RL benchmarks expose only rewards. While the paper partially addresses this via ablations (scalar vs vector), clearer discussion of applicability when such measurements are unavailable, or how to learn surrogate measurements, would help clarify scope and fairness relative to reward-only baselines.
- Limited theoretical treatment: No guarantees on policy improvement or horizon truncation effects; however, the empirical evidence is strong.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Clarify fairness of comparisons: Explicitly state exactly which signals each baseline receives (reward vs full measurement vector) and discuss how reward shaping or auxiliary losses might change baseline performance. Consider reporting a variant where baselines receive the same measurement vector (e.g., multi-objective reward) to further isolate the algorithmic advantage.
- Provide more detail on the Monte Carlo targets fi: exact windowing/discounting, handling of terminations, and whether normalization of measurements changes over training. Include confidence intervals or standard errors over seeds in plots/tables.
- Expand ablations: remove the expectation/action-stream split; vary the set of temporal offsets; evaluate the impact of goal conditioning during training vs test-only; and compare to successor features trained with Monte Carlo returns.
- Discuss limitations: likely failure modes on sparse-reward tasks without rich measurements, long-horizon credit assignment beyond the last offsets, and memoryless architecture limitations in partially observable settings. A brief note on extending to recurrent models or continuous control would be useful.
- Implementation notes: Provide pseudocode and key hyperparameters (e.g., experience memory size, update frequency k, epsilon schedule) in the main text or a concise appendix table for reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1) Off-policy aspects: How sensitive is performance to the size of the replay buffer and the update lag k? Did you observe bias when increasing the buffer or slowing updates? Any benefit from importance weighting?
2) Target construction: How exactly are fi computed near episode ends and deaths? Are measurements normalized per-scenario or globally, and fixed throughout training?
3) Goal sampling: In the randomized-goals regime, how did you choose the sampling ranges ([0,1] vs [-1,1])? How sensitive are results to this distribution, and do you observe coverage issues or conflicting gradients?
4) Horizon choice: Why the specific offsets {1,2,4,8,16,32} and the weighting of the last three? Did you try learned weights or longer horizons, and how does this affect stability and performance?
5) Architecture ablations: What is the quantitative impact of removing the expectation/action split (i.e., a single head predicting per-action futures)? Any experiments with recurrence to mitigate partial observability?
6) Measurement availability: If only a scalar reward is provided, can auxiliary measurements be learned (e.g., via representation learning) to retain the benefits of dense supervision? Any preliminary results?
7) Baseline parity: Did you run A3C/DQN with auxiliary measurement prediction or multi-task heads as additional signals? If so, how did they compare?
8) Sample efficiency: How does DFP’s sample efficiency compare to A3C when matched on wall-clock and hardware? Are your gains primarily from better credit assignment or from richer supervision?

Overall, this is a strong, timely paper with a simple idea that yields substantial practical benefits. I recommend acceptance.
