- Problem: Learning visuomotor control from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments while enabling test-time flexibility for dynamically specified goals.

- Idea: Reformulate control as supervised prediction of low-dimensional intrinsic measurements into the future, conditioned on observation, candidate action, and a goal vector, selecting actions by maximizing predicted goal utility.

- Method: Deep network predicts future measurement differences at multiple temporal offsets for all actions using an expectation/action-stream decomposition; trained by Monte-Carlo regression on experience without TD bootstrapping.

- Results: Strong empirical gains on ViZDoom D1–D4 versus DQN/A3C, good generalization across textures/maps/goals, benefits from vectorial and multi-horizon predictions, and won the Visual Doom AI Competition (Full Deathmatch).

- Decision: Accept.

- Key reason 1: Simple, well-motivated supervised-prediction formulation that connects to UVFAs, successor features, and Monte Carlo vs TD debates.

- Key reason 2: Compelling, thorough empirical evidence with consistent improvements over strong RL baselines and external validation via a competition win.

- Supporting point: Targets learning visuomotor policies from scratch in immersive 3D worlds and enabling post-training goal changes using dense, vector-valued intrinsic measurements.

- Placement in literature: Thoughtful framing linking SL vs TD, multi-objective RL, successor representations, and predictive state representations; goal-conditioning/measurement-centric framing is well justified.

- Empirical rigor: Careful baseline comparisons, multiple runs/hyperparameter searches, ablations, and generalization experiments substantiate claims.

- Strength: Clear conceptual advance—acting via direct future prediction of intrinsic measurements avoids bootstrapping instabilities.

- Strength: Elegant architecture and efficient training leveraging dense supervision.

- Strength: Broad empirical coverage — multiple difficulty levels, ablations, environment/goal generalization, and an external benchmark.

- Strength: Practical significance—demonstrates measurement prediction as a robust alternative to TD-based deep RL in complex 3D settings.

- Weakness: On-/off-policy mismatch from using a replay buffer and periodic policy updates—needs discussion of bias/stability implications.

- Weakness: Assumes availability of low-dimensional intrinsic measurements; unclear applicability to reward-only benchmarks or how to learn surrogate measurements.

- Weakness: Limited theoretical treatment—no guarantees on policy improvement or effects of horizon truncation.

- Feedback: Clarify fairness of comparisons by stating exactly which signals baselines receive and consider running baselines with the same measurement inputs or auxiliary losses.

- Feedback: Provide explicit details on Monte Carlo targets fi (windowing/discounting), handling of terminations, measurement normalization, and include confidence intervals or standard errors.

- Feedback: Expand ablations—remove expectation/action split, vary temporal offsets, test goal conditioning during training vs test-only, and compare to successor features trained with Monte Carlo returns.

- Feedback: Discuss limitations and likely failure modes (sparse-reward tasks, long-horizon credit assignment, partial observability) and note possible extensions (recurrence, continuous control).

- Feedback: Add pseudocode and key hyperparameters (experience memory size, update frequency k, epsilon schedule) for reproducibility.

- Question 1: How sensitive is performance to replay buffer size and update lag k, and was any bias observed when increasing buffer or slowing updates; any benefit from importance weighting?

- Question 2: How are fi computed near episode ends/deaths, and are measurements normalized per-scenario or globally and fixed during training?

- Question 3: How were goal sampling ranges chosen in randomized-goals, how sensitive are results to this distribution, and are there coverage/conflicting gradient issues?

- Question 4: Why choose offsets {1,2,4,8,16,32} and weight the last three; were learned weights or longer horizons tried and how do they affect stability/performance?

- Question 5: What is the quantitative impact of removing the expectation/action split and are there experiments with recurrence for partial observability?

- Question 6: If only scalar reward is available, can auxiliary measurements be learned to retain dense supervision benefits; any preliminary results?

- Question 7: Were A3C/DQN baselines run with auxiliary measurement prediction or multi-task heads, and how did those variants compare?

- Question 8: How does DFP’s sample efficiency compare to A3C when matched on wall-clock/hardware, and are gains due to better credit assignment or richer supervision?

- Overall: Strong, timely paper with a simple idea that yields substantial practical benefits; recommend acceptance.
