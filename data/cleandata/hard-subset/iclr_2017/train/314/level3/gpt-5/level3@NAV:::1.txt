1. Summary
This paper tackles goal-directed sensorimotor control from raw high-dimensional input in complex 3D environments. It proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): a deep goal-conditioned predictor that, given current sensory input, low-dimensional measurements, and a goal vector, predicts future changes in measurements for each action. Actions are chosen by maximizing the predicted goal utility. Learning is cast as supervised Monte Carlo regression rather than TD or successor features. Key contributions include (i) focusing on Monte Carlo estimation with dense multivariate feedback, (ii) the use of intrinsic low-dimensional measurements as prediction targets, (iii) parametrized goal conditioning enabling test-time goal changes, and (iv) comprehensive empirical comparisons. The method outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) in novel 3D Doom tasks, generalizes across environments/goals, and won the Visual Doom AI competition. The paper is clearly written with compelling results and ablations.

2. Decision
Accept. Reasons: (1) Strong, broad empirical gains with a simple, well-motivated idea that advances goal-conditioned control; (2) Clear evidence of generalization across environments and goals, including a competitive win in previously unseen maps.

3. Supporting arguments
- Sound problem placement in deep RL: relates to UVFA, multi-objective RL, and Monte Carlo vs TD. The departure from scalar rewards to vector measurements is well justified by dense supervision.
- Empirical rigor: four scenarios with increasing difficulty, comparisons to tuned baselines, ablations (vector vs scalar targets; multi-horizon prediction), environment texture randomization, and goal-agnostic training demonstrating policy responsiveness to goal changes without retraining.
- Practical significance: simple architecture, competitive training throughput, and strong zero-shot adaptation to new goal weights.
- The competition win substantiates external validity under adversarial multi-agent conditions.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify the on-policy assumption: training uses supervised returns but a replay buffer introduces off-policy data; include sensitivity to buffer size and a pure on-policy variant.
- Discuss reliance on extra metadata (choice of measurements). When such measurements are unavailable, what are viable proxies or learned latent measurements? Provide limitations and guidance.
- Include confidence intervals across seeds and a per-scenario compute budget to strengthen claims of robustness and fairness.
- Contrast more explicitly to successor features/UVFA with generalized policy improvement, both conceptually and empirically.
- Analyze sensitivity to goal distributions used during training and to noisy/delayed measurements.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the choice and dimensionality of measurement vectors? What happens with missing, noisy, or delayed measurements?
- Can you report ablations on replay buffer size and an on-policy-only variant to quantify any bias?
- How does DFP compare to successor features with GPI on these tasks under matched architectures?
- Does training with randomized goals reduce sample efficiency for a specific downstream goal?
- How does the method scale to larger or continuous action spaces?
- Any evidence that the predicted measurements are calibrated, and does calibration affect action selection robustness?
