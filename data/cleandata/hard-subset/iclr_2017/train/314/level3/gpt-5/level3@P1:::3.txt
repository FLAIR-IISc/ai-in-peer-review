1. Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a goal-conditioned sensorimotor control method that reframes acting as supervised learning. Instead of estimating value functions or policies from sparse scalar rewards, DFP predicts short- to mid-horizon changes in a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) given the current observation, candidate action, and a goal vector. At test time, the agent selects actions that maximize the inner product between the goal and the predicted future measurement changes. This enables training without a fixed goal and flexible goal specification at test time. The model ingests raw visual input and measurements, and uses a simple perception trunk with an action-conditional prediction head split into an expectation stream and an action-differentiating stream.

Empirically, DFP matches or outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) on ViZDoom tasks of increasing difficulty, and a DFP-trained agent won the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition’s Full Deathmatch track in unseen environments. The paper presents ablations (vector vs scalar feedback, multi-horizon prediction, architectural split) and generalization studies across textures/environments and across test-time goals. The approach is simple, sample-efficient in wall-clock time, and well-positioned relative to work on Monte Carlo methods, goal-conditioned value functions, and predictive representations.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, consistent empirical superiority on challenging 3D visuomotor tasks, with compelling generalization to new goals and environments, and a competition win against strong baselines.
- A clear, well-motivated formulation that leverages dense multivariate measurements to turn control into supervised prediction, with solid ablations that support the design choices.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets learning to act from pixels in immersive environments with dynamically specified goals. The critique that scalar rewards are sparse/noisy in such settings is persuasive; using dense intrinsic measurements as supervisory signals is well-motivated and connects to predictive representations and multi-objective RL.
- Method: The goal-conditioned predictor F(o, a, g; θ) produces per-action multi-step measurement deltas, and action selection is greedy w.r.t. g. The expectation/action-stream decomposition (dueling-inspired) is a reasonable inductive bias to capture average futures versus action-specific differences. While training employs ε-greedy data with a small replay buffer (thus not strictly on-policy), the learning signal is straightforward supervised regression from experience, avoiding the instabilities of bootstrapping.
- Evidence: Across Basic, Navigation, Battle, and Battle 2 scenarios, DFP outperforms A3C and DQN notably in the more complex multi-measurement tasks; A3C appears to hit a ceiling where DFP keeps improving. Generalization experiments with texture/domain randomization show substantial transfer when trained with sufficient appearance diversity. Goal-agnostic training (randomized goals per episode) yields performance close to a goal-specific model on the primary task, and much better adaptation to new goals. Ablations demonstrate that (i) vector-valued prediction beats scalar-reward prediction even when evaluation is on a single metric; (ii) multi-horizon prediction helps over single-horizon. The Visual Doom competition result substantiates external validity.
- Rigor and placement: Baselines are reasonably strong (author-provided DQN/DSR and a solid A3C implementation with hyperparameter searches), and comparisons use matched inputs and rewards. The literature discussion covers TD vs Monte Carlo, UVFA, predictive representations, and recent deep RL in 3D environments. While not exhaustive, it is adequate for ICLR.
- Limitations: Experiments are confined to Doom-like tasks and discrete actions; the model is reactive (no memory) and lacks theoretical guarantees. Nonetheless, the empirical breadth and clarity are above the bar.

4. Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not central to the decision)
- Clarity and notation:
  - Equation (2): using p for “prediction” may be mistaken for probability. Consider y or v. Also, clarify the “a” subscript (pat) earlier to denote per-action predictions.
  - Equation (6): avoid overloading j; consider distinct symbols for inputs vs indices to reduce confusion.
  - Tables: for heterogeneous measurements, repeat units/normalization descriptions in captions and columns to ease reading.
- Wording: Doom is rendered with a 2.5D engine; consider phrasing as “immersive first-person 3D visuals” to avoid pedantry.
- Training regime: You note departure from pure on-policy training; specify replay buffer size and update cadence prominently, and report sensitivity to buffer size. Provide full ε schedules and τ weighting coefficients; a short hyperparameter appendix table would aid reproducibility.
- Exploration: The randomized-goal training likely serves as structured exploration. A brief analysis (e.g., visitation statistics or ablation without goal randomization) would strengthen this claim.
- Compute and efficiency: Reporting wall-clock time, frames per second, and GPU/CPU usage for DFP vs baselines would highlight the practical advantages.
- Broader scope: A discussion on extending to continuous control (e.g., action sampling over parameterized actions) and adding memory (e.g., recurrent modules) would be valuable, perhaps with a small pilot result.

Questions for the authors
- Off-policy aspects: How sensitive is performance to the replay buffer size and update lag (k)? Did you test purely on-policy (no replay) training, and with larger buffers?
- Horizons and weighting: How robust are results to the choice of temporal offsets and their loss weights? Did you try learned or goal-dependent weighting over horizons?
- Measurement design: How critical is the choice of measurement vector? What happens if you include noisy or partially irrelevant measurements, or exclude ammo/health?
- Goal space: How do you choose goal distributions during training? Have you tried curriculum distributions that emphasize harder goals or anti-goals to improve robustness?
- Exploration: Can you quantify how randomized goals affect state-action coverage versus a fixed-goal ε-greedy baseline?
- Memory and partial observability: Did you test recurrent variants of DFP? In environments with occlusions/long-term dependencies, does memory provide clear gains?
- Continuous actions: Any preliminary results on applying DFP to continuous control (e.g., via action sampling or predicting futures for parameterized actions)?
- Theoretical perspective: Can you relate DFP’s supervised Monte Carlo target to UVFA/Monte Carlo value estimation formally, especially regarding bias/variance trade-offs and convergence under function approximation?
