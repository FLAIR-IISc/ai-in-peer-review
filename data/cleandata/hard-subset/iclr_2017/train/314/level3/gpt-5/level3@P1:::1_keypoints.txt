- Proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): predict future low-dimensional measurement vectors conditioned on observation, candidate action, and goal for action selection.
- Reframes control as supervised Monte Carlo regression instead of temporal-difference reinforcement learning.
- Uses dense, multivariate, multihorizon supervision (measurement deltas at multiple offsets) rather than sparse scalar rewards.
- Key contribution: goal-conditioned predictor that handles raw sensory input and parameterized goals.
- Key contribution: practical architecture separating expectation stream and action-specific prediction stream.
- Key contribution: empirical finding that predicting multiple measurements at multiple horizons improves learning.
- Key contribution: generalization across environments and to test-time goals specified only at inference.
- Key contribution: strong empirical results in ViZDoom, including winning the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch.
- Decision: Accept.
- Reason: Novel, well-positioned formulation emphasizing dense vector feedback and goal conditioning.
- Reason: Compelling empirical evidence with consistent, often large gains over strong deep RL baselines.
- Problem addressed: visuomotor control from raw pixels with changing test-time goals, improving sample efficiency and stability by using intrinsic measurement streams.
- Motivation/literature placement: well-motivated, contrasts SL vs RL, Monte Carlo vs TD, and relates to UVFA, multi-objective RL, successor representations.
- Performance: competitive on simple Doom tasks and substantially outperforms A3C/DQN on harder tasks where multiple measurements are available.
- Generalization: models trained on randomized textures generalize to unseen textures; goal-agnostic training adapts to multiple test-time goals.
- Ablations: vector multihorizon prediction outperforms scalar reward prediction; multiple temporal offsets help.
- External validation: competition win against engineered baselines lends credibility.
- Rigor: uses public implementations, multiple runs, substantial training budgets, and hyperparameter tuning; DSR comparison limited by compute.
- Feedback — measurement availability: clarify applicability when measurement streams are unavailable, noisy, or partial; consider baselines that use measurements as auxiliary targets.
- Feedback — off-policy aspects: discuss or test off-policy bias from ε-greedy data and small replay buffer (e.g., buffer size, sampling, importance corrections).
- Feedback — statistical reporting: add variance/error bars, statistical tests, sample-efficiency curves, and wall-clock comparisons.
- Feedback — sensitivity analyses: explore sensitivity to horizon choices, loss weightings, and corrupted/withheld measurements.
- Feedback — broader applicability: discuss/experiment with continuous actions, partial observability (LSTM), and long-horizon credit assignment limits.
- Questions — sparse rewards: how would DFP perform with only a sparse scalar reward or learned surrogate measurements?
- Questions — off-policy sensitivity: how sensitive is performance to replay buffer size, sampling strategy, and importance sampling?
- Questions — horizons & weighting: how were temporal offsets and weights chosen; any experiments with longer or learned horizons?
- Questions — goal conditioning: what training goal distributions worked best and does negative weighting help generalization?
- Questions — architecture ablations: what's the empirical role of expectation/action-stream decomposition and action-stream normalization?
- Questions — baselines with auxiliary tasks: how do A3C/DQN augmented to predict measurements as auxiliary losses compare?
- Questions — robustness & exploration: sensitivity to noisy/delayed/missing measurements and exploration strategies beyond ε-greedy?
- Questions — scaling & transfer: results for continuous control or larger action spaces and ability to adapt to different measurement sets via fine-tuning?
