Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a goal-conditioned sensorimotor control approach that learns to act by predicting short-horizon changes in intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) from raw visual input. Rather than optimizing a scalar reward via a Bellman backup, the model directly predicts future measurement vectors conditioned on the current observation, candidate action, and a goal vector, then greedily chooses the action whose predicted measurements best align with the goal.
- Architecturally, the predictor uses a perception module and “expectation + action” streams (inspired by dueling networks) to capture average outcomes and action-dependent deviations. Training reduces to supervised regression on experiences collected via an ε-greedy policy, with a small replay buffer (near on-policy).
- Empirically, the method achieves strong performance on multiple ViZDoom tasks, substantially outperforming DQN and A3C on the harder scenarios, generalizes to new textures and to dynamically specified goals at test time, and won the Visual Doom AI Competition (Full Deathmatch) without extra supervision.
- Ablations show benefits of vectorial measurement prediction over scalar reward prediction and of using multiple temporal offsets. The formulation offers a simple, stable alternative to standard deep RL when dense intrinsic measurements are available.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong and convincing empirical results on challenging 3D environments, including clear advantages over strong baselines and successful cross-goal generalization.
- A clean, practical distillation of ideas from goal-conditioned RL (UVFA), multi-objective RL, and Monte Carlo prediction into an approach that is easy to implement and yields impactful insights for future agents.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem tackled: Learn robust sensorimotor control from raw pixels in complex 3D environments and support dynamic, test-time goals without retraining. The paper specifically targets the setting where dense, low-dimensional intrinsic measurements accompany high-dimensional sensory streams.

2) Motivation and placement: The paper is well motivated: dense measurement streams offer richer supervision than sparse scalar rewards, making direct prediction attractive. The work is well placed with respect to UVFA, multi-objective RL, predictive state representations, and Monte Carlo vs TD debates. The relationship to UVFA and successor representations is acknowledged; the paper frames a specific instantiation focused on predicting intrinsic measurements rather than value functions. While the algorithmic novelty is limited (largely an integration and reparameterization of existing concepts), the formulation is crisp and pragmatic.

3) Do results support claims?
- Correctness and rigor: The experimental design is sound, with multiple scenarios of increasing difficulty, comparison to strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR), and ablations. The training/test separation for texture randomization, and the competition result, lend credibility.
- Claims substantiation: The method clearly outperforms prior work on the harder scenarios, generalizes to held-out textures, and adapts to unseen goals. The ablations convincingly show the value of vectorial, multi-offset prediction.
- Caveats: Novelty is mainly empirical/practical rather than theoretical. Analysis of failure modes, robustness to more diverse goal distributions, and broader domains is limited. The training protocol is close to on-policy with a small buffer; potential catastrophic forgetting of under-sampled goals is not analyzed.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify the contribution: Position the work explicitly as a goal-conditioned, supervised alternative to TD-based RL, an instantiation of UVFA-like conditioning that predicts intrinsic measurements. Make explicit what is novel (measurement-centric target, dense supervision, empirical findings and design insights) versus prior art.
- Failure modes and limits: Provide a qualitative/quantitative analysis of where the approach struggles: e.g., occlusions, partial observability, long-horizon credit assignment, rapid goal switching, rare-event measurements, adversarial textures, and OOD goals (weights outside the training range).
- Goal distribution and forgetting: Discuss and test how training with fixed vs randomized goals impacts catastrophic forgetting. Consider simple mechanisms (replay diversification, goal rehearsal, coverage metrics, elastic weight consolidation) and report stability as goals drift.
- Baselines and fairness: Include steps-to-threshold/sample-efficiency curves in addition to final performance. Report variance across seeds. Where possible, add UVFA-style goal-conditioned baselines and successor features/representations to isolate the benefit of direct measurement prediction.
- Broader applicability: Add results on at least one non-Doom domain (e.g., a subset of Atari where intrinsic counters can be approximated; or a continuous-control proxy with discretized actions) to demonstrate generality. Even partial evidence would strengthen impact.
- Uncertainty and loss shaping: Consider robust losses (Huber), per-dimension normalization/weighting, or heteroscedastic regression to handle heterogeneous measurement scales and noise. Calibration of predicted futures could help decision quality.
- Memory and hierarchy: Since tasks are partially observable, try a recurrent variant to test gains. Discuss compatibility with hierarchical skills and temporal abstraction.
- Continuous actions: Comment on extending to continuous actions (e.g., learned actor that maximizes g^T F(o,a,g;θ), CEM, or gradient-based action refinement).

Questions for the authors
- Novelty/relationship: In what precise sense does predicting intrinsic measurements differ from UVFA or successor features beyond the target representation? Can you formalize advantages or trade-offs?
- On-/off-policy and forgetting: With a small replay memory and randomized goals, did you observe catastrophic forgetting or policy oscillations when the goal distribution changed? Any metrics tracking goal coverage or retention?
- Goal generalization: How far can the model extrapolate to goal vectors outside the training range (e.g., negative weights or larger magnitudes)? Any systematic sweeps?
- Sample efficiency: How does DFP’s steps-to-threshold compare to A3C on the hard tasks, normalized by wall-clock and environment steps?
- Measurement choice: How sensitive is performance to the set of measurements chosen? If a useful measurement is omitted or noisy, how does this impact control?
- Temporal offsets: How were the offsets and weights chosen? Did you try learned weights or curriculum over horizons?
- Uncertainty: Are the predictions calibrated? Would modeling predictive uncertainty improve action selection under noisy dynamics?
- Recurrent models: Did you test adding memory (e.g., LSTM) to mitigate partial observability? If tried, what were the effects?
- Broader domains: Do you anticipate any obstacles applying DFP to domains without explicit intrinsic counters? What proxies would you use?
