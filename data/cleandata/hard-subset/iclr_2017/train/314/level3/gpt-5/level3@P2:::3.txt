Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a simple but effective sensorimotor control approach that reduces action selection to supervised learning by predicting future intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) under candidate actions and selecting the action that maximizes a goal-weighted combination of those predictions. Goals are parameterized and can be changed at test time.
- The model operates on raw visual input plus a low-dimensional measurement stream and trains from interaction alone (no human demonstrations or extraneous supervision). It introduces an architecture with perception, measurement, and goal modules, and a dueling-style prediction head (expectation + action streams) to predict action-conditional future measurement vectors at multiple temporal offsets.
- Extensive experiments in ViZDoom show DFP matches or outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C), demonstrates generalization across textures and to novel goals, and benefits from vectorial, multi-horizon prediction. A DFP-based agent won the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch track.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical evidence that a supervised, goal-parameterized future-prediction approach can outperform state-of-the-art deep RL on challenging 3D visuomotor tasks, including a competitive benchmark (competition win).
- Clear, well-motivated formulation with practical advantages (dense supervision, goal flexibility), backed by ablations and generalization studies that support the core claims.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Learning sensorimotor control from raw high-dimensional sensory input in immersive 3D environments, while supporting dynamic goals specified at test time. The paper reframes control as predicting future intrinsic measurements conditioned on action and goal.
- Is the approach well motivated and positioned? Yes. The paper connects to supervised forward models, Monte Carlo methods, multi-objective RL, and universal value function approximators, arguing that dense multidimensional feedback favors supervised prediction over TD-style bootstrapping. The related work is appropriate and frames the contribution within auxiliary tasks/predictive representations and goal-conditioned value approximation.
- Do the results support the claims?
  - Performance: Across four scenarios, DFP equals A3C on the easiest task and substantially outperforms A3C and DQN on Navigation and both Battle scenarios (notably large margins where measurements are richer). The competition result further corroborates real-world strength against sophisticated alternatives.
  - Goal flexibility: Goal-agnostic training with randomized goals yields near-parity on the main goal at test time and better generalization to new goals than training with a single fixed goal.
  - Generalization: Training on texture-varied environments transfers to novel textures; increased capacity improves further.
  - Ablations: Predicting vectorial measurements and multiple temporal offsets both contribute meaningfully; even when evaluated on a single metric (frags), multi-measurement prediction improves performance.
  - Scientific rigor: Experiments describe training/testing protocols, offsets, weighting, ε-greedy schedule, experience memory, and baseline tuning; comparisons use shared observation/action setups and comparable architectures to DQN for fairness. While DSR comparisons are limited by speed, this is candidly reported.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not central to the accept decision)
- Clarify environment description: Doom is often characterized as 2.5D (2D map with a 3D renderer). Consider phrasing as “immersive first-person 3D rendering” to avoid pedantry.
- Notation and presentation:
  - In Eq. (2), using p for “prediction” can be mistaken for probability; consider y or f̂. In Eq. (6), avoid overloading indices/variables (e.g., the double use of A and j) and state explicitly that w = |A|.
  - Repeat units or normalize scales clearly in tables that mix heterogeneous measurements; ensure axis labels and legends state whether values are raw or normalized changes.
  - Define the normalization of measurements (e.g., ammo/health scaling) in the main text (not only in appendices) for readability.
- Baselines and fairness:
  - A3C details are thorough; DSR is limited by speed. Consider adding a note on wall-clock training time and sample efficiency (steps to reach X performance) for DFP vs A3C/DQN to make the practical advantages more explicit.
  - Discuss variance across seeds and include error bars in key plots.
- Deeper analysis:
  - Provide sensitivity to goal vector scaling and to the choice/weights of temporal offsets; include a brief study of how τ and their loss weights affect performance/latency of behavioral effects.
  - Report the effect of experience memory size (on/off-policy gap) and ε schedule on stability/performance.
  - Discuss robustness to noisy or partially missing measurements (a likely issue in real systems).
- Scope and limitations (expand briefly in Discussion):
  - The approach benefits from dense, informative measurements; address how it behaves with sparser or delayed measurements, and whether auxiliary learned measurements (learned features) could substitute.
  - Note implications for partial observability and memory; a short experiment with a recurrent variant would strengthen the case.

Questions for the authors
1. Measurement design:
   - How sensitive is DFP to the choice and scaling of measurements? If one measurement dominates in magnitude, does it overshadow others despite goal weights? Do you normalize per-dimension during training and at test time?
   - Have you tried adding learned auxiliary measurements (e.g., latent features) to f to further densify the signal?
2. Temporal offsets:
   - Why choose τ = {1,2,4,8,16,32} and loss weights (0.5, 0.5, 1) on the last three? How does performance change with different horizons/weightings, or when optimizing a single long-horizon τ?
3. Policy and data:
   - You maintain a small experience memory and update every k steps with ε-greedy exploration. How sensitive are results to memory size, update frequency, and ε schedule? Any signs of instability or overfitting to recent experience?
4. Baselines and metrics:
   - Can you report sample efficiency (steps to reach N frags) and wall-clock training time for DFP vs A3C, to complement final performance?
   - Did you evaluate a recurrent A3C baseline (LSTM) to address partial observability parity with your reactive model?
5. Goal generalization:
   - How far can test-time goals extrapolate beyond the training goal distribution (e.g., negative weights, extreme trade-offs)? Any failure modes when g emphasizes very long-horizon outcomes?
6. Robustness:
   - How does DFP perform if some measurements are noisy, delayed, or occasionally missing? Can the model learn to downweight unreliable measurements?
7. Action space:
   - Predicting futures for all action combinations can become large as sub-actions grow. Have you explored factorized action heads or continuous-action extensions?

Overall, this paper is a clear, well-executed step showing that goal-parameterized, supervised future prediction with dense measurement streams is a powerful alternative to standard deep RL in immersive visuomotor control.
