Summary and contributions
- This paper positions itself squarely within deep RL, but reframes sensorimotor control with deep function approximators as supervised prediction rather than TD-based value learning. The core idea is to predict low-dimensional future measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) conditioned on the current observation, action, and a goal vector, and then select actions that maximize g^T predicted-measurements. The learning objective is supervised Monte Carlo regression on future measurement differences at multiple horizons, not TD or successor features.
- The authors introduce Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a simple, scalable goal-conditioned predictor that operates from raw first-person RGB and measurement streams in 3D Doom environments. The architecture combines perception, measurement, and goal encoders with a dueling-style decomposition to separate expected outcome from action-specific deltas.
- Empirically, DFP outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) in four ViZDoom scenarios, particularly in the more complex battle mazes with richer measurement streams. Policies generalize across textures and to new goals at test time without retraining, and an extended model won the Visual Doom Full Deathmatch competition (unseen environments), outperforming a strong LSTM-DQN agent that used extra supervision.
- Key contributions: the Monte Carlo training focus; using low-dimensional measurements as the prediction target; explicit goal conditioning; careful empirical comparisons, ablations (measurement vector vs scalar reward, multiple horizons), and compelling generalization studies.

Decision: Accept
Reason: A simple, well-motivated formulation with strong empirical performance and clear communication. The goal-conditioned supervised prediction approach is impactful and shows superior results and generalization in challenging, visually rich 3D domains.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles sensorimotor control from raw inputs in complex 3D environments and flexible goal pursuit. The motivation to exploit dense, multidimensional feedback via supervised learning is well-grounded in prior work (UVFA, predictive representations, Monte Carlo vs TD) and is clearly distinguished from standard TD/policy-gradient methods and successor features.
- Methodological soundness: The formulation is coherent and minimal: multihorizon prediction of measurement differences, linear goal utility, action selection via argmax over predicted goal attainment. The architecture choice (perception + measurement + goal encoders; dueling-like decomposition) is sensible and aligns with the objective.
- Empirical support: Extensive experiments in multiple Doom scenarios, generalization across textures/environments, goal-agnostic training to goal-conditioned testing, and ablations substantiate the claims. The competition win adds external validation.
- Scientific interest: The work challenges the dominance of TD learning in deep RL by showing that Monte Carlo supervised prediction over informative measurements can be highly competitive, especially when supervision is dense.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- On-policy vs replay buffer: The training objective assumes on-policy Monte Carlo targets but experience is sampled from a replay buffer. While the buffer is “small,” please quantify its size and analyze sensitivity to buffer size and policy shift. A short discussion on the bias introduced and why it appears benign here would strengthen the methodology section.
- Reliance on measurement metadata: DFP leverages privileged knowledge of which measurements to predict, while some baselines only see rewards. Please make this assumption explicit upfront, discuss settings where such measurements are unavailable, and clarify how DFP would fare if restricted to reward-only signals (you provide a useful ablation; expanding on limitations and workarounds such as learning latent measurements would be helpful).
- Fairness of baselines: You note limited DSR tuning and slower training. For A3C/DQN, provide more detailed hyperparameter/search settings, compute budgets, and seeds. Reporting mean ± std across runs and significance tests would increase rigor.
- Goal distributions: The goal-agnostic training results are compelling. It would help to discuss how the choice of training-goal distribution affects generalization (e.g., importance of including negative weights; curriculum across goal magnitudes).
- Scalability and applicability: Briefly discuss the applicability of DFP to domains with sparse scalar rewards (e.g., success/failure tasks), continuous actions, or where measurements are expensive/noisy. A short comparison to successor features/UVFAs in terms of sample efficiency and compositionality would add perspective.

Questions for the authors
- How large is the replay buffer relative to episode length, and how often are targets recomputed? Did you try purely on-policy updates, and with what effect?
- How sensitive is performance to the set of predicted horizons and their loss weights? Would a learned weighting or longer horizons help?
- Measurement selection: If you remove one measurement at train time but still evaluate frags, how much does performance drop? Can DFP learn useful latent “measurements” (e.g., via auxiliary representation learning) when explicit ones are missing?
- Goal conditioning: How well does the model extrapolate to goal vectors outside the training range (e.g., larger magnitudes, different trade-offs)? Any calibration plots of g^T prediction vs realized outcomes?
- Comparison to successor features: Have you compared DFP to learned successor features with generalized policy improvement in the same setups? What are the computational/sample-efficiency trade-offs you observe?
- Memory and partial observability: Did you evaluate adding recurrence (e.g., LSTM) on top of DFP? If so, does it improve performance in the maze and battle tasks?
- Continuous actions: Any preliminary results adapting DFP to continuous control via, e.g., sampling candidate actions or learning a policy head that maximizes g^T F?

Overall, this is a clear, thoughtful, and empirically strong paper that will be of significant interest to the deep RL community. The minor concerns are addressable and do not detract from the main contributions.
