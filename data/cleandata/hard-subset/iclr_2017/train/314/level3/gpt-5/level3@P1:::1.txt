Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning approach to sensorimotor control that predicts future values of a low-dimensional measurement stream (e.g., health, ammo, frags) conditioned on the current observation, candidate action, and a goal vector. Action selection chooses the action that maximizes predicted utility under the specified goal. This reframes control as supervised Monte Carlo regression rather than TD learning, enabling dense, multivariate supervision without extraneous labels. Key contributions include: (i) a goal-conditioned predictor that handles raw sensory input and parameterized goals; (ii) a practical architecture that separates expectation and action-specific prediction; (iii) empirical evidence that predicting multiple measurements at multiple horizons improves learning; (iv) generalization across environments and across goals specified only at test time; and (v) strong empirical results in ViZDoom, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition against strong baselines that used more complex models and additional supervision.

Decision: Accept
Reasons: (1) Strong, novel formulation that rethinks control with dense vector feedback and goal conditioning, well positioned in the literature. (2) Compelling, carefully executed empirical evidence showing consistent and often large gains over strong deep RL baselines, plus cross-goal and cross-environment generalization and a competition win.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Learning visuomotor control from raw pixels in complex, dynamic 3D environments, with the ability to pursue changing goals at test time without retraining. The approach addresses sample efficiency and stability challenges associated with sparse scalar rewards by leveraging dense, intrinsic measurement streams.
- Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? Yes. The paper connects SL vs RL trade-offs (dense vector feedback favors SL), contrasts Monte Carlo vs TD considerations, and situates the work alongside UVFA, multi-objective RL, and successor representations. The choice to predict measurement deltas at multiple horizons aligns with predictive representation work and with practical limitations of modeling full sensory streams.
- Do the results support the claims?
  - Performance: Across four Doom scenarios (increasing difficulty), DFP is competitive on simple tasks and substantially outperforms A3C/DQN on harder ones, especially where multiple measurements are available. The training speed and stability appear favorable.
  - Generalization: Models trained on texture-randomized mazes generalize well to unseen textures; higher-capacity variants improve further. Goal-agnostic training yields policies that adapt to multiple test-time goals nearly as well as goal-specific training on the primary task and significantly better on novel goals.
  - Ablations: Predicting vector measurements outperforms scalar reward prediction even when evaluated on a single metric; multiple temporal offsets help, supporting the core hypothesis about dense, multihorizon supervision.
  - External validation: Winning the Visual Doom AI Competition against stronger-engineered baselines gives additional credibility.
  - Rigor: Comparisons use public implementations, multiple runs, and substantial training budgets; hyperparameter tuning is described. While DSR comparisons are limited by compute, A3C/DQN baselines are appropriately tuned for the setting.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not decisive)
- Fairness and assumptions: DFP leverages per-step measurement vectors as supervised targets. While these are available to players/agents in Doom, note that many RL benchmarks only expose scalar rewards; clarify applicability when such measurement streams are unavailable, noisy, or partial. Consider adding baselines that also consume these measurements as auxiliary prediction targets to isolate the benefit of the DFP objective vs auxiliary learning.
- On-/off-policy aspects: Training uses ε-greedy data with a small replay buffer while the predictor is updated off-policy. Provide a discussion or empirical check on potential off-policy bias (e.g., varying buffer sizes, prioritized sampling) and whether importance corrections matter.
- Statistical reporting: Include variance/error bars on final evaluations and statistical tests where feasible. Report sample efficiency curves (performance vs environment steps) and wall-clock comparisons.
- Sensitivity analyses: Explore sensitivity to the choice of horizons, loss weighting across horizons, and goal distributions used during training. Show how performance degrades if some measurements are withheld or corrupted.
- Broader applicability: Discuss adaptation to continuous action spaces and to partially observable settings with memory (LSTM), and provide preliminary results if possible. Clarify any limitations regarding long-horizon credit assignment beyond the largest prediction offset.

Questions for the authors
- Measurement availability: How would DFP perform if only a sparse scalar reward were available? Can auxiliary learned measurements (e.g., learned features) substitute effectively for game-provided measurements?
- Off-policy effects: How sensitive is performance to the replay buffer size and sampling strategy? Did you try importance sampling or other corrections, and what was the impact?
- Horizons and weighting: How were the temporal offsets and their weights chosen? Have you compared more/longer horizons or learned horizon weighting?
- Goal conditioning: In goal-agnostic training, what distributions over goals worked best? Does training with negative weights (penalizing some measurements) consistently improve generalization to diverse test-time goals?
- Architecture: What empirical role does the expectation/action-stream decomposition and action-stream normalization play relative to a monolithic head? Any ablations on this design?
- Baselines with auxiliary tasks: If A3C/DQN are augmented to predict the same measurements as auxiliary losses (without using them for action selection), how does the gap change?
- Robustness: How sensitive is DFP to noisy or delayed measurement streams? What happens if measurements are missing sporadically?
- Exploration: Beyond ε-greedy, did you try entropy regularization, curiosity, or goal-randomization schemes to improve exploration in harder mazes?
- Scaling: Do you have results for continuous control (e.g., DFP with DDPG-style action selection) or for tasks with larger action spaces?
- Transfer: Can a DFP model trained with one measurement set be adapted to a different measurement set without retraining from scratch (e.g., via fine-tuning or a modular head)?
