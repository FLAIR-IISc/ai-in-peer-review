Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes a simple but effective framework for sensorimotor control that replaces reward-centric RL with supervised prediction of future intrinsic measurements conditioned on goals. Given raw sensory input (e.g., images), a compact measurement vector (e.g., health, ammo, frags), and a goal vector, the model predicts action-conditioned future measurement changes at multiple horizons and selects actions that maximize goal-weighted predicted outcomes. This goal-conditioned, vector-valued supervision enables training without a fixed goal and adaptation to new goals at test time. The architecture couples a standard perception stack with a prediction head split into expectation and action streams (akin to dueling networks), trained with Monte Carlo targets and ε-greedy data collection. Extensive experiments in ViZDoom show strong gains over DQN and A3C, particularly on challenging combat scenarios with multiple measurements, generalization to unseen textures/layouts, effective goal-agnostic training, and a win in the Visual Doom AI Competition. Ablations indicate that multi-measurement and multi-horizon prediction substantially improve performance. The paper is well written, clearly positioned in the literature (SL vs. TD, UVFA, predictive representations), and offers a compelling empirical case for dense, vectorial supervision.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons: (1) Strong empirical evidence that direct future prediction with vectorial intrinsic measurements yields large, practical gains over strong deep RL baselines in complex 3D settings, including generalization and competition results. (2) Conceptually clean, well-motivated framework that unifies goal-conditioned control and dense supervision, with clear connections to UVFA and dueling architectures.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Learning visuomotor control from raw pixels in rich 3D environments while supporting dynamically specified goals. This is timely and important, addressing two challenges: sparse/unstable credit assignment in RL from pixels and goal-conditional control.
- Motivation and placement: The paper carefully contrasts TD-based RL with SL in settings with temporally dense, multidimensional feedback. It relates to Monte Carlo methods, multi-objective RL, and especially UVFA (goals as input), while highlighting its distinctive choice to predict measurement vectors directly (rather than value functions) and to optimize goal-weighted predictions at test time. The dueling-like head is a sensible architectural refinement.
- Do results support claims?
  - Head-to-head comparisons vs. DQN/A3C on Basic/Navigation/Battle/Battle2 are favorable and consistent across seeds, with especially large margins on multi-measurement combat tasks (up to 6x). Training speed is competitive, aided by dense supervision.
  - Generalization: Training across texture variants yields transfer to unseen textures and new mazes; higher-capacity models further improve robustness.
  - Goal-agnostic training: Models trained with randomized goals nearly match fixed-goal training on the main task and generalize better to novel goals (including pacifist or ammo-drain objectives).
  - Ablations: Vectorial measurements and multi-horizon prediction both contribute significantly, supporting the central hypothesis that dense, multivariate supervision helps.
  - External validation: Winning the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming a strong LSTM-DQN with additional supervision, adds credibility.
- Scientific rigor: The setup is described in detail (measurement definitions, horizons, weighting, action sets). Baselines use author or public implementations with reasonable hyperparameter sweeps. The paper is transparent about replay size, ε-schedules, and evaluation protocols. While more domains would be ideal, the experimental breadth within Doom is strong for ICLR standards at the time.

Additional feedback to help improve the paper (not central to the accept decision)
- Clarify novelty: The contributions are primarily conceptual integration and empirical demonstration. Be explicit about the relationship to UVFA (Schaul et al., 2015): your method can be seen as a UVFA over a structured feature f (future measurement deltas) with direct supervised targets. Discuss pros/cons versus learning V or Q directly under goal-parameterization.
- Failure modes: Provide analysis where DFP struggles—e.g., long-horizon credit assignment with partial observability, sparse measurement streams, or when relevant measurements are not directly aligned with behaviorally critical events. Include negative results or qualitative failures.
- Goal distribution and forgetting: On-policy training with changing goals risks catastrophic forgetting of earlier goals. Consider empirical analysis of retention across the goal space, sensitivity to the training goal distribution, and mechanisms such as goal-aware replay, coverage regularization, or periodic rehearsal.
- Broader applicability: Add experiments beyond Doom to show generality, e.g., Atari using a measurement vector constructed from RAM/score/lives/time, or a robotics simulator where proprioceptive signals provide measurements. Even small-scale results would strengthen claims of generality.
- Partial observability and memory: Since DFP is reactive, include a simple recurrent extension (e.g., LSTM) and report whether memory improves results on mazes or adversarial tracking.
- Analysis details: Report variance across seeds for all methods, sensitivity to horizon set and weighting, and the effect of adding irrelevant measurements (robustness to noisy or redundant channels).

Questions for the authors
- UVFA connection: Can you formalize the equivalence/differences between DFP and UVFA with Q(s,a,g)? Under what assumptions is maximizing g^T F(o,a,g) equivalent to maximizing a goal-conditioned action-value?
- Goal sampling: How are training goals sampled and normalized? How sensitive is performance and generalization to the goal distribution, scaling of g, and the choice of measurement normalization?
- Horizons: Why weight only the last three offsets, and how sensitive are results to the set {1,2,4,8,16,32} and their weights? Does including longer horizons help in Navigation/Battle2?
- Replay and on-/off-policy: You use a small replay buffer. What happens with larger buffers or prioritized replay across goals? Do you observe forgetting of infrequent goals over long training?
- Measurement design: How do choices of measurement subset affect performance? Does adding irrelevant or delayed measurements degrade learning, and do you need feature selection or sparsity?
- Partial observability: Have you tried recurrent encoders or stacking more frames? Do they help in Battle2 or goal generalization?
- Baseline fairness: For A3C and DQN, can you provide compute budgets and training wall-clock parity? How do results change under equalized environment steps and hyperparameter search budget?
- Robustness and safety: What is the impact of ε-schedule and exploration strategy on generalization to new goals? Could you incorporate goal-directed exploration signals during training?
