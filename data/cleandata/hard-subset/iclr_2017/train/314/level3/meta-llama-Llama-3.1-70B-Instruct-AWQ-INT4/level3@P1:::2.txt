The paper presents an approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, utilizing a high-dimensional sensory stream and a lower-dimensional measurement stream. The authors demonstrate that the cotemporal structure of these streams provides a rich supervisory signal, enabling training a sensorimotor control model by interacting with the environment. The model is trained using supervised learning techniques, but without extraneous supervision. It learns to act based on raw sensory input from a complex three-dimensional environment. The presented formulation enables learning without a fixed goal at training time, and pursuing dynamically changing goals at test time.

I decide to accept this paper with two key reasons: (1) the approach demonstrates a clear empirical advantage over state-of-the-art deep RL methods, particularly on challenging tasks, and (2) the authors provide useful experimental insights for future agents.

The paper's main technical contribution appears to be distilling and integrating existing ideas rather than a novel algorithmic advance. However, the authors successfully demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach in immersive three-dimensional simulations based on the classical game Doom. The results show that the presented approach outperforms sophisticated prior formulations, particularly on challenging tasks. The results also demonstrate that trained models successfully generalize across environments and goals.

One potential issue with the approach is that on-policy training can cause catastrophic forgetting if goals from the distant past are not revisited. The authors may want to consider analyzing failure modes and conditions where the model struggles to generalize to changing goals. Additionally, expanding experiments to other domains (e.g., Atari using ROM as intrinsic variables) would help demonstrate broader applicability.

To improve the paper, the authors could clarify their contributions in the abstract and introduction, highlighting what is novel about their approach. They could also provide more analysis of the limitations of their method and potential avenues for future work.

Some questions I would like the authors to answer:

* How do the authors plan to address the issue of catastrophic forgetting in on-policy training?
* Can the authors provide more details on the architecture of the predictor network, including the specific layers and activation functions used?
* How do the authors choose the temporal offsets τ1, . . . , τn of predicted future measurements, and what is the effect of different choices on the performance of the model?
* Can the authors provide more analysis of the generalization performance of the model across different environments and goals?

Overall, the paper presents a well-motivated approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, and the authors provide useful experimental insights for future agents. With some additional analysis and clarification, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
