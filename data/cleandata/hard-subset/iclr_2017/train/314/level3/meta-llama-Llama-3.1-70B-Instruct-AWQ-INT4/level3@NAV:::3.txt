**Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, which utilizes a high-dimensional sensory stream and a lower-dimensional measurement stream. The approach, called Direct Future Prediction (DFP), predicts future intrinsic measurements rather than rewards, and uses a parameterized function approximator to learn from raw sensory input and measurements. The paper claims that DFP outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods in challenging tasks, and demonstrates strong empirical results, including winning the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

Based on the review, I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The two key reasons for this decision are:

1. **Experimental soundness and reproducibility**: The paper presents extensive experiments in immersive three-dimensional simulations, including comparisons to state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods, and demonstrates strong empirical results. The experimental setup and results are well-documented, making it easy to reproduce the results.
2. **Milestone status**: The paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control, which has the potential to advance the field of reinforcement learning and robotics. The results, including winning the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition, demonstrate the approach's effectiveness and impact.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's approach is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem they are trying to solve. The paper also situates itself among prior work on intrinsic motivation, auxiliary tasks, and forward modeling, and cites relevant literature adequately.

The experimental results are impressive, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the results, including ablation studies and comparisons to other methods. The paper also demonstrates the approach's ability to generalize across environments and goals.

**Additional Feedback and Suggestions**

To improve the paper, I suggest the following:

* Address the minor form issues, such as describing Doom as a 3D game when it is actually a 2D game presented in a fake 3D environment.
* Clarify the notation, such as using "P" for prediction instead of probability, and avoiding the double use of "j" in equation (6).
* Consider repeating units for heterogeneous measurements in the results tables.
* Provide more details on the architecture and training procedure, such as the number of layers and the learning rate schedule.

**Questions for the Authors**

To clarify my understanding of the paper and provide additional evidence, I would like the authors to answer the following questions:

* Can you provide more details on the experience memory and how it is used to update the predictor?
* How did you select the temporal offsets τ1, …, τn for predicting future measurements?
* Can you provide more information on the goal-agnostic training regime and how it is used to adapt to varying goals at test time?
* How do you plan to extend the approach to continuous action spaces and more complex environments?
