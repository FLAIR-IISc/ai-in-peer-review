This paper presents an approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, utilizing a high-dimensional sensory stream and a lower-dimensional measurement stream. The authors demonstrate that the cotemporal structure of these streams provides a rich supervisory signal, enabling training a sensorimotor control model through supervised learning techniques without extraneous supervision. The model learns to act based on raw sensory input from a complex three-dimensional environment and can pursue dynamically changing goals at test time.

I decide to accept this paper with two key reasons for this choice. First, the authors provide a clear and well-motivated approach to sensorimotor control, grounded in the literature and leveraging existing ideas in a novel way. Second, the paper supports its claims with extensive experiments in three-dimensional simulations based on the classical first-person game Doom, demonstrating a clear empirical advantage over prior formulations.

The approach presented in the paper is well-motivated, drawing on existing work in supervised learning, reinforcement learning, and sensorimotor control. The authors provide a clear and concise overview of the relevant literature, highlighting the key challenges in sensorimotor control and the limitations of existing approaches. The proposed method is a special case of universal value function approaches, and the authors acknowledge and build upon prior work in this area.

The experimental results presented in the paper are extensive and demonstrate a clear advantage over prior formulations. The authors evaluate their approach in four scenarios of increasing difficulty, comparing it to state-of-the-art deep RL methods. The results show that the presented approach outperforms prior methods, particularly on complex tasks, and that the use of vectorial measurements provides a significant advantage over conventional scalar rewards.

However, I would like to see further analysis of failure modes and conditions where the model struggles to generalize to changing goals. Additionally, I note a conceptual issue with the on-policy training approach, which can cause catastrophic forgetting if goals from the distant past are not revisited. I suggest that the authors expand their experiments to other domains, such as Atari using ROM as intrinsic variables, to demonstrate broader applicability.

To improve the paper, I provide the following feedback:

* Clarify the main technical contribution of the paper, as it appears to be a distillation and integration of existing ideas rather than a novel algorithmic advance.
* Provide additional analysis of failure modes and conditions where the model struggles to generalize to changing goals.
* Address the conceptual issue with on-policy training and consider alternative approaches that can mitigate catastrophic forgetting.
* Expand experiments to other domains to demonstrate broader applicability.

I would like the authors to answer the following questions to clarify my understanding of the paper and provide additional evidence:

* Can you provide more details on the architecture of the future predictor network and how it was configured to be as close as possible to the DQN model?
* How did you select the temporal offsets τ1, . . . , τn of predicted future measurements, and what was the rationale behind using only the latest three time steps to contribute to the objective function?
* Can you provide more information on the experience memory and how it was used to update the parameters of the predictor used by the agent?
* How did you evaluate the importance of vectorial feedback versus a scalar reward, and what were the results of this evaluation?
