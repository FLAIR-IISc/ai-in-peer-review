This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, leveraging a high-dimensional sensory stream and a lower-dimensional measurement stream. The authors utilize the cotemporal structure of these streams as a rich supervisory signal, enabling the training of a sensorimotor control model through interaction with the environment. The model is trained using supervised learning techniques, without extraneous supervision, and learns to act based on raw sensory input from a complex three-dimensional environment. The approach allows for learning without a fixed goal at training time and pursuing dynamically changing goals at test time.

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reason for this decision is that the paper presents a well-motivated approach that is grounded in the literature, and the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their approach, and the empirical evaluations are thorough and well-executed.

The paper tackles a specific question/problem in the field of sensorimotor control, which is a key challenge in reinforcement learning. The authors propose a novel approach that departs from the traditional reward-based formalization commonly used in RL. Instead, they consider a stream of sensory input and a stream of measurements, and use the cotemporal structure of these streams as a supervisory signal. This approach is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how it addresses the challenges of sensorimotor control in immersive environments.

The paper supports its claims through extensive experiments in three-dimensional simulations based on the classical first-person game Doom. The results demonstrate that the proposed approach outperforms sophisticated prior formulations, particularly on challenging tasks. The authors also evaluate the generalization of the learned model across environments and goals, and the results show that the model can effectively pursue new goals not specified during training.

One potential limitation of the paper is that the experiments are conducted in a single environment (Doom), which may not be representative of all immersive environments. However, the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, and the results are convincing. Additionally, the paper situates itself well among prior work on intrinsic motivation, auxiliary tasks, and forward modeling, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how their approach relates to these areas.

In terms of presentation, the paper is well-written and clear. However, there are some minor issues with notation and terminology. For example, the use of "P" in equation (2) is misleading, as it is used for prediction, not probability. Additionally, the double use of "j" in equation (6) may cause confusion. These issues are minor, and do not detract from the overall quality of the paper.

To improve the paper, I suggest that the authors consider adding more diversity to the environments used in the experiments. Additionally, the authors may want to consider evaluating their approach in more complex scenarios, such as those involving multiple agents or dynamic environments. However, these are minor suggestions, and the paper is strong as it stands.

Overall, I believe that this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of sensorimotor control and reinforcement learning, and I recommend accepting it for publication.
