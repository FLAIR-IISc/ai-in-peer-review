**Summary**

The paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, leveraging a high-dimensional sensory stream and a lower-dimensional measurement stream. The cotemporal structure of these streams provides a rich supervisory signal, enabling training a sensorimotor control model through supervised learning techniques. The model learns to act based on raw sensory input from a complex three-dimensional environment and generalizes across environments and goals. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through extensive experiments in three-dimensional simulations based on the classical first-person game Doom, outperforming sophisticated prior formulations and winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Decision**

I recommend accepting this paper. The key reasons for this choice are:

1. The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, building on empirical successes using deep function approximators.
2. The authors provide compelling empirical results, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach in outperforming prior methods in novel 3D environments from raw sensory data and generalizing across goals and environments.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's core idea of supervising prediction of low-dimensional measurements conditioned on a given goal vector and the current action is innovative and well-supported by the experimental results. The use of Monte Carlo estimation, low-dimensional measurements, and parametrized goal conditioning are key contributions that distinguish this work from prior approaches. The authors also demonstrate the ability of learned policies to respond to limited goal changes without additional training, which is a significant advantage over traditional reinforcement learning methods.

The paper is well-communicated, and the experimental results are thorough and convincing. The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of their approach, including comparisons to prior work and ablation studies. The winning performance in the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition further validates the effectiveness of their approach.

**Minor Concerns and Suggestions**

While the paper is strong overall, there are two minor concerns that should be addressed:

1. The supervised training approach makes an on-policy assumption, but the authors use a replay buffer, which is an approximation. This should be discussed in more detail, and the authors should provide more insight into the implications of this approximation.
2. The method relies on extra metadata (which measurements to predict) unavailable to compared algorithms. The limitations of this approach when such measurements are absent should be more clearly noted.

**Additional Feedback**

To further improve the paper, the authors could consider the following suggestions:

* Provide more details on the architecture and implementation of the model, including the specific choices of hyperparameters and the reasoning behind them.
* Discuss the potential applications and extensions of their approach, including the possibility of using continuous actions and more complex goal structures.
* Consider including more visualizations and illustrations of the model's performance, such as videos or screenshots of the agent's behavior in the Doom environment.

**Questions for the Authors**

To clarify some aspects of the paper, I would like the authors to answer the following questions:

* Can you provide more insight into the choice of low-dimensional measurements and how they were selected for the experiments?
* How do you plan to address the limitation of requiring extra metadata (which measurements to predict) in future work?
* Can you discuss the potential implications of using a replay buffer in the supervised training approach, and how this affects the on-policy assumption?
