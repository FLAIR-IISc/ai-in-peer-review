**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, leveraging a high-dimensional sensory stream and a lower-dimensional measurement stream. The method, called Direct Future Prediction (DFP), utilizes a parameterized function approximator to predict future measurements based on current observations, actions, and goals. The approach is trained using supervised learning techniques, without extraneous supervision, and demonstrates strong empirical results in various scenarios, including the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Decision**

Based on the paper's contributions, experimental soundness, and milestone status, I recommend acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments**

1. **Well-motivated approach**: The authors provide a clear and well-motivated departure from traditional reinforcement learning (RL) methods, which often rely on sparse scalar rewards. By utilizing a rich supervisory signal from the cotemporal structure of sensory and measurement streams, the approach addresses key challenges in sensorimotor control.
2. **Strong empirical results**: The paper presents extensive experiments in immersive three-dimensional simulations, demonstrating that DFP outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods, including DQN, A3C, and DSR. The approach also shows strong generalization across environments and goals.
3. **Sound experimental design**: The authors conduct a thorough evaluation of their approach, including comparisons to prior work, ablation studies, and analysis of the importance of different model components.

**Additional Feedback**

1. **Notational issues**: The use of "P" in equation (2) is misleading, as it is used for prediction, not probability. Additionally, the double use of "j" in equation (6) may cause confusion.
2. **Minor form issues**: Doom is described as a 3D game, although it is presented in a fake 3D environment.
3. **Presentation suggestion**: Results tables should repeat units for heterogeneous measurements to improve clarity.
4. **Future work**: The authors may consider exploring the application of their approach to continuous action spaces, as well as integrating memory-based models to enhance the reactive nature of the current model.

**Questions for the Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the architecture of the predictor network, including the specific configuration used in the experiments?
2. How do you handle the case where the goal vector is not fixed during training, and the agent needs to adapt to varying goals at test time?
3. Can you elaborate on the importance of predicting measurements at multiple temporal offsets, and how this affects the performance of the learned model?
