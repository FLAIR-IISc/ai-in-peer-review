**Summary of the Paper's Contributions**

The paper presents an approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments, utilizing a high-dimensional sensory stream and a lower-dimensional measurement stream. The method, called Direct Future Prediction (DFP), trains a model to predict future measurements based on raw sensory input, without extraneous supervision. The approach enables learning without a fixed goal at training time and pursuing dynamically changing goals at test time. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of DFP in immersive three-dimensional simulations based on the classical game Doom, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL methods, particularly on challenging tasks. The results also show that trained models generalize across environments and goals.

**Decision**

Based on the paper's contributions and the experimental results, I recommend accepting the paper.

**Reasons for the Decision**

1. **Strong empirical results**: The paper presents extensive experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in immersive three-dimensional simulations. The results show that DFP outperforms or matches state-of-the-art deep RL methods, particularly on challenging tasks.
2. **Clear and well-motivated approach**: The paper presents a clear and well-motivated approach to sensorimotor control in immersive environments. The authors provide a thorough explanation of the method and its components, and the approach is well-situated among prior work on intrinsic motivation, auxiliary tasks, and forward modeling.

**Supporting Arguments**

1. **Effective use of intrinsic measurements**: The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of using intrinsic measurements to guide exploration and learning. The approach enables the model to learn without a fixed goal at training time and pursue dynamically changing goals at test time.
2. **Strong generalization performance**: The results show that trained models generalize across environments and goals, which is a critical aspect of sensorimotor control in immersive environments.
3. **Clear and thorough presentation**: The paper presents a clear and thorough explanation of the method, its components, and the experimental results. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the results and discuss the implications of the approach.

**Additional Feedback**

1. **Minor form issues**: The paper describes Doom as a 3D game, although it is 2D presented in fake 3D. This is a minor issue, but it should be corrected for accuracy.
2. **Notational issues**: The notation "P" in equation (2) is misleading, as it is used for prediction, not probability. Additionally, the double use of "j" in equation (6) may confuse readers. These notational issues should be addressed to improve the clarity of the paper.
3. **Presentation suggestion**: Results tables should repeat units for heterogeneous measurements to improve readability.
4. **Environment diversity**: While the paper presents extensive experiments in the Doom environment, it would be beneficial to demonstrate the approach in other environments to further establish its generalizability.

**Questions for the Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the architecture of the predictor network, including the specific layers and activation functions used?
2. How did you select the temporal offsets for predicting future measurements, and what is the effect of using different offsets on the performance of the model?
3. Can you provide more information on the experience memory and the sampling process used to train the model?
4. How does the approach handle situations where the goal is not expressible in terms of future measurements, and what are the implications of this limitation?
