### **Summary**

This paper presents Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a novel approach for sensorimotor control in complex 3D environments using raw visual inputs. The work positions itself within the deep reinforcement learning landscape but departs from traditional value-based or policy-gradient methods. The core idea is to frame the control problem as a supervised learning task. The agent learns a deep network to predict the future values of a low-dimensional vector of measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on the current sensory input, a proposed action, and a parameterized goal vector. At test time, the agent selects the action that is predicted to best satisfy the current goal.

The key contributions are: (1) formulating the learning problem as supervised Monte Carlo regression rather than temporal-difference learning, leveraging a dense, multi-dimensional signal from the environment; (2) a goal-conditioning mechanism that allows a single trained model to pursue dynamically changing goals without retraining; and (3) a comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating state-of-the-art performance. The method is shown to significantly outperform strong deep RL baselines like A3C and DQN on challenging tasks in the ViZDoom environment. The paper's claims are powerfully substantiated by its victory in the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, where it generalized successfully to previously unseen environments.

### **Decision**

**Accept.**

This is an excellent paper presenting a simple, elegant, and highly effective new approach to a challenging problem. The two primary reasons for my decision are:
1.  The novel framing of sensorimotor control as a supervised prediction problem is a compelling alternative to mainstream RL algorithms, backed by strong motivation and clear execution.
2.  The empirical results are exceptionally strong and convincing, not only outperforming state-of-the-art methods in controlled experiments but also demonstrating real-world success in a competitive setting.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main strength is its thoughtful and effective reformulation of the control problem. Instead of relying on a potentially sparse scalar reward and Bellman updates, the DFP model leverages a rich, temporally dense stream of vectorial measurements. This provides a much stronger supervisory signal, which the results suggest leads to more stable and effective learning, especially in complex scenarios (D3 and D4). This idea is well-motivated and connects to earlier work (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992) that is often overlooked in modern deep RL.

The second major strength is the quality and thoroughness of the empirical evaluation. The authors compare DFP against very strong baselines (A3C, DQN, DSR) across a suite of tasks with increasing difficulty. The performance gains, particularly a 6x improvement over A3C in the "Battle" scenario, are significant and clearly demonstrate the method's practical advantages. Beyond raw performance, the paper provides compelling evidence for the model's flexibility. The goal-agnostic training experiments show that the agent can learn general skills and adapt to new, unseen goals at test time, performing nearly as well as a specialist agent trained on a fixed goal. This is a crucial capability for building more general agents. The victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition serves as an outstanding external validation of the method's robustness and ability to generalize. Finally, the ablation study effectively isolates the contributions of using vectorial feedback and multi-step prediction, reinforcing the paper's core claims. The work is also very well-written and easy to follow.

### **Additional Feedback**

The paper is very strong, but its technical discussion could be slightly improved by addressing the following points. These are intended as constructive suggestions and do not detract from my decision to accept.

1.  **On-Policy Training with Off-Policy Data:** The training procedure is described as a supervised Monte Carlo regression. The target `fi` (the observed future measurement difference) is an outcome of the agent's current policy. This makes the training an on-policy method. However, the use of an experience replay buffer means the model is trained on data collected by older policies, which is an off-policy technique. While the paper notes this departure "from pure on-policy training" has no observed adverse effects, a brief discussion of this potential mismatch would be valuable. Acknowledging this approximation and hypothesizing why it works well (e.g., due to the policy changing slowly or the inherent robustness of supervised regression) would strengthen the paper's technical rigor.

2.  **Dependence on Curated Measurements:** The method's success hinges on the availability of a semantically meaningful, low-dimensional measurement vector `mt`. This gives the DFP agent access to information that is not directly available to the baseline methods, which only receive a single scalar reward derived from these measurements. This is a perfectly valid design choice, but the paper could more explicitly frame it as a key assumption and a potential limitation. A clearer discussion of the scenarios where DFP is most applicable (i.e., where such intrinsic measurements are available) and its potential limitations when they are not would provide readers with a more complete understanding of the method's scope.

### **Questions for Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on why the off-policy sampling from the experience replay buffer does not appear to destabilize the supervised, on-policy Monte Carlo training? Is the approach simply robust to this mismatch, or is there another factor at play, such as the slow update schedule for the acting policy?

2.  The reliance on a pre-defined vector of meaningful measurements is a key aspect of the model. Have you considered scenarios where such measurements are not available? Do you have any thoughts on how the measurement vector `mt` might be learned in an end-to-end fashion, for instance, by using an auxiliary unsupervised learning objective to derive a compact state representation?
