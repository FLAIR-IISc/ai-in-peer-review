### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel and compelling approach to sensorimotor control in complex 3D environments, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP). The core idea is to reframe the problem of learning to act from a reinforcement learning perspective to a supervised learning one. Instead of relying on a scalar reward signal, the model learns to predict future changes in a low-dimensional stream of measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) conditioned on the current sensory input, the current measurements, and a parameterized goal vector. Action selection at test time is then a matter of choosing the action that is predicted to yield the best outcome according to the specified goal. The paper demonstrates that this approach, trained via Monte Carlo-style regression on raw experience, significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods like A3C and DQN in challenging first-person shooter scenarios within the ViZDoom environment. Key contributions include the formulation of sensorimotor control as a goal-conditioned future prediction problem, the demonstration of strong generalization across both unseen environments and dynamically specified goals, and a thorough empirical validation. The significance of the work is underscored by the fact that a model trained with this method won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition. The paper is well-written, the method is clearly explained, and the results are of significant interest to the community.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novelty and Simplicity:** The paper presents a simple yet powerful alternative to the dominant temporal-difference learning paradigm in deep reinforcement learning. By framing the problem as supervised prediction of vectorial measurements, the authors provide a fresh perspective that is well-motivated and demonstrates remarkable effectiveness. The goal-conditioning mechanism is an elegant way to train a single, flexible agent capable of pursuing diverse objectives.
2.  **Compelling Empirical Results:** The experimental validation is extensive and convincing. The proposed DFP method does not just achieve incremental gains but dramatically outperforms strong, widely-used baselines (A3C, DQN) on complex, dynamic 3D tasks. The ability of the model to generalize to new environments and unseen goals, coupled with its success in the Visual Doom AI Competition, provides powerful evidence of its robustness and practical utility.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this work lies in its successful departure from conventional reward-based reinforcement learning. Instead of learning a value function or a policy from a potentially sparse, scalar reward, DFP leverages a dense, multi-dimensional stream of measurements as a rich supervisory signal. The ablation study (Table 4) provides strong support for this design choice, clearly showing that using vectorial feedback (health, ammo, frags) is significantly better than using a scalar reward (frags only), and that predicting at multiple future time steps is also beneficial. This confirms the intuition that a richer, more descriptive feedback signal can stabilize and accelerate learning in complex domains.

The goal-conditioning aspect is another significant contribution. The experiments in Table 3 convincingly show that a model can be trained in a "goal-agnostic" manner (using randomized goal vectors) and still perform nearly as well on a specific task as a model trained exclusively for that task. More importantly, these goal-agnostic models generalize far better to novel goals at test time, such as adopting a "pacifist" strategy, without any additional training. This capability represents a meaningful step towards creating more flexible and general-purpose agents.

Finally, the empirical results are state-of-the-art and rigorously presented. The comparison against A3C, a very strong baseline, shows performance improvements of up to 6x in the "Battle" scenario. The victory in the Visual Doom AI competition is a particularly strong result, as it validates the method's performance against other competitive approaches in unseen environments, and did so without the extra supervision or architectural complexity employed by the second-place entry.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision to accept.

*   The training procedure uses an experience replay buffer, which is characteristic of off-policy methods. However, the supervised learning formulation seems implicitly on-policy, as the future measurements `f_i` are the result of executing the policy that was active at that time. Using a replay buffer means the model is trained on data from older, potentially very different policies. While this clearly works well in practice, it would strengthen the paper to briefly discuss this approximation and any potential instabilities or challenges that might arise from this on-policy/off-policy mismatch.
*   The method's success seems closely tied to the availability of a meaningful, low-dimensional measurement stream provided by the environment. This gives the DFP agent access to richer information than the baseline agents, which only receive a synthesized scalar reward. While this is a core part of the proposed framework, it would be beneficial to more explicitly discuss the limitations of the approach. For instance, how might this method be adapted to domains where such clean, hand-picked measurements are not available?

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following questions in their rebuttal:

1.  Could you comment on the use of an experience replay buffer for what is effectively a supervised, Monte Carlo-style prediction task? Did you experiment with purely on-policy training, and if so, how did it compare?
2.  The reliance on a pre-defined, low-dimensional measurement stream seems crucial to the method's success. How do you envision this approach applying to domains where such 'measurements' are not readily available or must be learned? Would the agent simply try to predict aggregations of its raw sensory input?
3.  The choice of temporal offsets {1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32} feels somewhat heuristic. Could you comment on the sensitivity of the model's performance to this choice? For example, would a denser or sparser set of prediction horizons significantly alter the results?
