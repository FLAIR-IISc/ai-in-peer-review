This paper presents an approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which frames the problem as a supervised learning task. The agent learns to predict future values of a low-dimensional "measurement" stream (e.g., health, ammunition) based on its current high-dimensional sensory input (raw pixels), a proposed action, and a goal vector. The goal vector specifies a desired combination of future measurements, allowing the model to be trained without a fixed objective and to pursue dynamically changing goals at test time. By acting greedily with respect to its predictions, the agent learns complex behaviors in immersive 3D environments. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments in the ViZDoom platform that their approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning baselines like A3C and DQN, especially on complex tasks. Furthermore, the work shows strong generalization capabilities across different environments and to novel goals not seen during training, culminating in a first-place finish in the Visual Doom AI Competition.

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are:
1.  The proposed method, while conceptually simple, demonstrates a significant and compelling empirical advantage over more complex, state-of-the-art reinforcement learning algorithms in challenging 3D visuomotor control tasks.
2.  The paper provides valuable experimental insights, particularly through its ablation studies and goal-generalization experiments, which effectively support the central claims that vectorial, multi-step future prediction is a more powerful learning signal than a scalar reward.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The empirical results are the standout strength of this work. In the complex "Battle" and "Battle 2" scenarios (Table 1), DFP outperforms the strong A3C baseline by a large margin (a factor of 6 and 2.5 in frags, respectively). This is a substantial improvement. The victory in the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition provides a powerful, independent validation of the method's effectiveness and its ability to generalize to unseen environments.

The paper is well-situated within the literature and correctly identifies its relationship to prior work, such as universal value functions (Schaul et al., 2015) and Monte Carlo methods. While the main technical contribution is arguably a distillation and effective integration of existing ideas rather than a fundamentally new algorithm, the specific formulation is elegant and its successful application to such a complex domain is a significant contribution in itself. The paper makes a strong case that for environments with rich, intrinsic measurement streams, reframing the control problem as direct supervised prediction can be more effective and efficient than traditional value-based or policy-gradient methods.

The ablation study in Table 4 provides rigorous support for the core design choices. It clearly demonstrates that both using a vectorial measurement stream (over a scalar reward) and predicting at multiple future temporal offsets contribute significantly to the final performance. The experiments on goal-agnostic training (Table 3) are also compelling, showing that the agent can learn general skills without a fixed goal and adapt proficiently to novel objectives at test time, including ones that are contrary to typical gameplay (e.g., the "pacifist" goal).

**Additional Feedback:**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

1.  **Clarification of Contribution:** The abstract and introduction could be slightly refined to more clearly position the contribution. While the approach is novel in its specific formulation and application, its core ideas build on established principles. Highlighting the contribution as a successful synthesis and large-scale validation of a supervised prediction framework for complex control, which demonstrates surprising advantages over mainstream RL, might be more precise.

2.  **Analysis of Failure Modes:** The paper convincingly shows when the model succeeds, but an analysis of its failure modes would be instructive. For instance, under what conditions does the generalization to new goals break down? Are there types of goals (e.g., those requiring long-term planning beyond the prediction horizon or those anticorrelated with all training goals) for which the model struggles? Including such an analysis would provide a more complete picture of the method's capabilities and limitations.

3.  **Potential for Catastrophic Forgetting:** The goal-agnostic training regime with randomized goals is very interesting. However, given that the training is largely on-policy (collecting data with the current policy), there is a potential risk of catastrophic forgetting. If the distribution of sampled goals were to shift over time, the network might forget how to achieve goals from the distant past. The paper mentions using a small experience memory, but a brief discussion of this potential issue and how the current setup mitigates it would strengthen the work.

4.  **Broader Applicability:** The Doom environment is an ideal testbed for this method because it provides an explicit, low-dimensional set of "measurements" (health, ammo, frags). It would be beneficial for the authors to discuss the applicability of DFP to other domains where such variables are not readily available. For example, could this approach be used in Atari games by treating certain parts of the game's RAM as the intrinsic measurement vector? Demonstrating or even just discussing the path to broader applicability would increase the paper's impact.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential for catastrophic forgetting in the randomized goal training regime? Is the experience memory sufficient to ensure the agent remains proficient across the full spectrum of training goals, or did you observe any degradation in performance for less-frequently sampled goals?
2.  Regarding the generalization to new goals at test time, have you identified any characteristics of goals for which the trained models perform poorly? For example, does performance suffer if a test goal is far outside the convex hull of the goals seen during training?
3.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the choice of temporal offsets {τ_n}? Was the selection of {1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32} based on empirical tuning, and does the model's effectiveness depend heavily on having this exponential spacing?
