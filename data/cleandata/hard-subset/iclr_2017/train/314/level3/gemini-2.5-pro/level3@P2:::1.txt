### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel and effective approach to sensorimotor control in complex, immersive 3D environments. The core idea is to reframe the control problem as a supervised learning task, moving away from the conventional reinforcement learning paradigm. The proposed model, Direct Future Prediction (DFP), learns to predict future changes in a low-dimensional stream of measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on the current high-dimensional sensory input (raw pixels), a potential action, and a parameterized goal vector. At test time, the agent selects the action that is predicted to maximize the outcome according to its current goal.

The key contributions are:
1.  A simple and powerful learning algorithm based on Monte Carlo-style supervised regression, which proves highly effective for learning directly from raw experience.
2.  Compelling empirical evidence showing that DFP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods like DQN and A3C on challenging first-person shooter tasks in the VizDoom environment.
3.  A demonstration of impressive generalization capabilities, where a single trained model can adapt to dynamically changing goals at test time without retraining and can also generalize across previously unseen environments.
4.  Strong external validation of the method's effectiveness through its victory in the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

The paper is well-written, the method is clearly explained, and the experimental results are extensive and persuasive.

### Decision: Accept

The primary reasons for this decision are:
1.  **Outstanding Empirical Results:** The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance on a set of challenging 3D visuomotor control tasks, substantially outperforming well-established deep RL baselines. The win in the Visual Doom AI competition provides a powerful, independent validation of the method's strength and generalization capabilities.
2.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The paper provides a well-motivated alternative to traditional value-based or policy-gradient RL. By framing control as direct prediction of multi-dimensional, goal-conditioned outcomes, the approach elegantly handles temporally dense feedback and allows for flexible, zero-shot adaptation to new goals, which is a significant and desirable capability.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's central claim is that for environments with rich, multi-dimensional feedback, reframing sensorimotor control as a supervised prediction problem can be more effective than traditional RL. The experiments provide strong support for this claim. In the complex "Battle" scenarios (D3 and D4), DFP outperforms the state-of-the-art A3C by a factor of 2.5-6 in terms of frag count. The ablation study in Table 4 convincingly demonstrates that using vectorial feedback is critical to this success; a model trained to predict only a scalar reward (frags) performs significantly worse, supporting the authors' core motivation.

Furthermore, the paper tackles the important challenge of acquiring general skills that can be deployed for various goals. The experiments in Table 3 are particularly compelling. A model trained with randomized goals performs nearly as well on a specific benchmark task as a model trained specialistically for that task. More importantly, the goal-agnostic training enables the agent to generalize far better to novel goals at test time, such as adopting a "pacifist" strategy, showcasing a level of flexibility not easily achieved with standard RL agents. This goal-conditioning mechanism is a significant contribution.

Finally, the success of the DFP agent in the Visual Doom AI Competition is a standout result. Winning a competition on previously unseen maps against other highly-tuned agents is a testament to the robustness and generalization power of the learned policies. The fact that it outperformed a more complex, recurrent model that used additional supervisory signals from the game engine further highlights the effectiveness and data-efficiency of the proposed supervised learning formulation.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

1.  **On-Policy Objective with Off-Policy Data:** The model is trained via supervised regression to predict the future outcomes of actions taken by the agent's current policy. This is conceptually an on-policy objective. However, the training procedure uses an experience replay buffer, which is an off-policy mechanism that samples from the agent's past experiences. This introduces a potential mismatch, as the predictor is trained on data from older policies to make predictions for the current one. While this approximation clearly works very well in practice, it would be beneficial for the authors to acknowledge and briefly discuss this aspect. Why do you believe this mismatch does not negatively impact performance? Is it because the policy changes slowly, or the buffer size is kept small?

2.  **Dependence on Explicit Measurements:** The method's strength relies on the availability of a meaningful, low-dimensional measurement stream that is provided by the environment. The baselines, in contrast, are only given a single scalar reward derived from these measurements. This could be seen as providing the DFP model with a form of privileged information. It would strengthen the paper to more explicitly discuss the scope and limitations of the approach in this regard. How would the DFP framework be applied in domains where such clean, intermediate measurements are not available? Does this requirement constrain its generality compared to standard RL formulations? Clarifying this would help readers better understand the class of problems for which DFP is best suited.
