This paper introduces Direct Future Prediction (DFP), an elegant and effective approach to sensorimotor control that frames the problem as supervised learning rather than traditional reinforcement learning. The core contribution is a model that learns to predict future values of a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frags) conditioned on the current state, a potential action, and a goal vector. By treating goal specification as an input, the model can be trained without a fixed objective and later deployed to pursue dynamically changing goals by selecting actions that maximize the desired future measurements according to the current goal.

I recommend a clear accept for this paper. The primary reasons are its outstanding empirical validation and the novelty of its formulation, which presents a compelling alternative to reward-based methods in measurement-rich environments.

The paper’s claims are substantiated by a rigorous set of experiments in the challenging VizDoom environment. The DFP model is shown to outperform or match strong contemporary baselines, including A3C and DQN, particularly in more complex "Battle" scenarios where A3C's performance plateaus. The method's success is powerfully underscored by its victory in the 2016 VizDoom AI Competition, demonstrating robust generalization to unseen environments. Furthermore, an insightful ablation study confirms that key components of the proposed model—namely, the use of vectorial measurements over a scalar reward and predictions at multiple temporal offsets—are crucial for its performance. The paper is also well-positioned within the literature, connecting its ideas to prior work on intrinsic motivation, forward models, and universal value functions.

For the final version, I offer the following suggestions for improvement. The authors could enhance presentation clarity by repeating the units for heterogeneous measurements in the results tables. There are also minor notational ambiguities that could be addressed: the use of "P" for prediction in equation (2) could be confused with probability, and the repeated index "j" in equation (6) might be slightly confusing. Finally, while not critical, noting that Doom is a pseudo-3D environment would add technical precision.

I would be interested in the authors' thoughts on the following questions:
1. The success of DFP seems tied to the availability of a dense, meaningful measurement stream. How do you envision this approach being applied to environments with sparser or less obvious intrinsic measurements, such as puzzle-solving tasks?
2. The paper demonstrates effective goal-agnostic training with randomized goal vectors. How sensitive is the final performance to the distribution from which these goals are sampled during training?
