**Summary**
This paper presents a compelling method for sensorimotor control in complex 3D environments, framing the problem as a supervised learning task of direct future prediction. The approach trains an on-policy agent to predict future changes in a stream of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its sensory input, current measurements, and a specified goal. The key contribution is showing that this simple formulation, which learns from raw experience without a traditional scalar reward, can achieve state-of-the-art performance. The experiments, conducted in challenging Doom scenarios, demonstrate that the agent not only outperforms sophisticated deep RL baselines but also generalizes effectively to unseen environments and dynamically changing goals.

**Decision: Accept**
I recommend accepting this paper for two primary reasons. First, the empirical results are exceptionally strong and well-supported, showing a clear advantage over established methods in a challenging domain. Second, the work provides valuable experimental insights by successfully distilling and integrating existing concepts into a simple and effective framework for goal-conditioned agents.

**Supporting Arguments**
The paper's claims are convincingly supported by extensive experiments. The proposed Direct Future Prediction (DFP) model significantly outperforms strong baselines like A3C and DQN, particularly in complex scenarios. The ablation study effectively validates the core design choices, confirming the benefits of using vectorial, multi-step measurement predictions over a single scalar reward. The work is well-positioned within the literature, correctly identifying its connection to universal value function approximators (Schaul et al., 2015). While the core algorithm is not entirely novel, its main contribution lies in the elegant application and empirical validation of these ideas, demonstrating their power for complex 3D control tasks. This practical demonstration and the insights gained are a significant contribution to the field.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**
The following points are intended as constructive feedback to further strengthen the paper:
*   **Clarifying Contribution:** The abstract and introduction could more clearly frame the contribution as a novel and powerful integration of existing ideas rather than a fundamentally new algorithm.
*   **Analysis of Failure Modes:** The analysis would be enhanced by a discussion of the model's limitations. An investigation into the conditions where the agent fails to generalize to new goals would provide a more complete picture.
*   **Generality:** To demonstrate broader applicability, it would be interesting to see experiments in other domains. For example, the framework could be tested on Atari games using portions of the RAM state as the intrinsic measurement stream.
*   **Catastrophic Forgetting:** The on-policy nature of the training combined with randomized goals raises concerns about catastrophic forgetting. A brief discussion on whether the agent might forget skills related to goals not recently encountered would strengthen the paper.

**Questions for the Authors**
*   Could you elaborate on the conditions or types of goals where your model struggles to generalize?
*   Given the on-policy training, have you investigated the potential for catastrophic forgetting of skills related to goals that are not sampled for extended periods? How crucial is the experience buffer in mitigating this?
