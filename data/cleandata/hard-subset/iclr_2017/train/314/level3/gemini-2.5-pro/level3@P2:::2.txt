### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a compelling approach for training agents to perform sensorimotor control in complex, immersive 3D environments. The core idea is to reframe the control problem as a supervised learning task. Instead of relying on a sparse, scalar reward signal, the agent learns to predict future changes in a multi-dimensional stream of "measurements" (e.g., health, ammo) based on its current sensory input (raw pixels), current measurements, and a specified goal. The goal is represented as a vector defining a desired combination of future measurements. This "Direct Future Prediction" (DFP) framework allows the agent to be trained from its own experience without external supervision, supports training without a fixed goal, and enables the pursuit of dynamically changing goals at test time. The authors provide extensive empirical validation in the Visual Doom environment, demonstrating that their method significantly outperforms strong deep reinforcement learning baselines like A3C and DQN, particularly on more complex tasks. A key result is that an agent trained with this method won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, highlighting its effectiveness and generalization capabilities in unseen environments.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates a clear and significant performance advantage over state-of-the-art deep RL methods on a set of challenging 3D visuomotor control tasks. The results are thorough, well-presented, and backed by a win in a competitive setting, providing strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2.  **Valuable Experimental Insights:** The work provides important insights into the value of using dense, multi-dimensional feedback for training agents. The ablation study convincingly shows that predicting a vector of future measurements is substantially more effective than predicting a single scalar reward, even when the evaluation metric is scalar. This finding is a useful contribution to the field.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's main strength lies in its comprehensive and convincing experimental results. In four Doom scenarios of increasing difficulty, the proposed DFP agent consistently and substantially outperforms DQN and A3C, which are very strong baselines. The performance gap widens on the more complex "Battle" scenarios, where DFP leverages the richer, multi-dimensional measurement stream (health, ammo, frags) to great effect. The victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition, particularly in the "Full Deathmatch" track held in unseen environments, is a powerful testament to the model's ability to learn generalizable skills.

While the core algorithmic components (predicting future outcomes given a goal) build upon existing ideas in the literature, such as Universal Value Function Approximators (Schaul et al., 2015), the paper does a good job of citing this prior work. The contribution here is the successful distillation and integration of these concepts into a simple, practical, and highly effective model for a complex and relevant problem domain. The specific architecture, training regime, and demonstration of its power in raw-pixel 3D environments is a noteworthy engineering and scientific achievement.

Furthermore, the experiments on goal-agnostic training are excellent. They show that an agent can be trained on randomized goals and later generalize effectively to specific, unseen goals at test time, performing nearly as well as an agent trained specifically for that single goal. This is a crucial step towards creating more flexible and general-purpose agents. The ablation study clearly isolates and validates the central hypothesis of the paper: that a rich, vectorial measurement stream provides a superior training signal compared to a conventional scalar reward.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Clarifying the Contribution:** The abstract and introduction could be slightly refined to more precisely frame the contribution. While the approach is powerful, it can be viewed as a specific instantiation of a more general class of goal-conditioned value-function methods. Clarifying that the key contribution is the successful synthesis of these ideas into a practical system and the empirical demonstration of the power of dense, vectorial supervision in complex 3D worlds would strengthen the paper's positioning.
*   **Analysis of Failure Modes:** The paper demonstrates impressive successes, but an analysis of the model's failure modes would add significant depth. For example, are there certain types of goals the model struggles to generalize to? What happens when the prediction of future measurements is inaccurate? A short discussion on the limitations and potential failure cases would be very insightful.
*   **Broader Applicability:** The DFP framework seems general. To further demonstrate its breadth, it would be interesting to see it applied to other domains. For instance, many Atari games provide intrinsic variables in the game's RAM. An experiment showing that DFP can be applied in this setting (using RAM values as the measurement stream) could compellingly argue for the generality of the approach beyond 3D navigation tasks.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  The model is trained on-policy (or with a small experience buffer) with goals that can be randomized per episode. Have you investigated the potential for catastrophic forgetting, where the agent's competence on goals from the distant past degrades after training on a new distribution of goals? If so, how might the framework be adapted to mitigate this?
2.  Could you provide some qualitative analysis on the failure modes of the agent? For example, in the goal-agnostic training experiments (Table 3), the "Pacifist" and "Drain Ammo" goals are pursued less optimally than the others. Is there an intuition for why certain goal combinations are harder to learn or generalize to?
3.  The paper frames the approach as an alternative to the standard RL formulation. While empirically superior in these domains, the core concept of learning a function `F(state, action, goal)` to predict future outcomes is conceptually similar to learning a universal value function `Q(state, action, goal)`. Could you further elaborate on what you see as the key conceptual difference or advantage of direct future prediction over a goal-conditioned Q-learning framework, beyond the use of a vectorial measurement stream instead of a scalar reward?
