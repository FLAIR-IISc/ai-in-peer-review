### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel and effective approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which frames the problem as a supervised learning task rather than a traditional reinforcement learning one. The core idea is to train an agent to predict the future values of a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) conditioned on its current sensory input, action, and a specified goal. The goal is represented as a weighting of the desired changes in future measurements. This formulation provides a dense, multi-dimensional supervisory signal that proves highly effective for training. At test time, the agent selects the action that is predicted to best satisfy its current goal.

The authors evaluate DFP in the complex, first-person 3D environment of VizDoom. The experimental results are comprehensive and impressive, showing that DFP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning baselines, including A3C and DQN, particularly on more challenging tasks. A key strength of the method is its ability to train without a fixed goal and generalize to new, dynamically specified goals at test time. The success of this approach is powerfully validated by its victory in the Full Deathmatch track of the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition, where it performed in previously unseen environments.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper is a clear accept. The decision is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Outstanding Empirical Results:** The proposed DFP method achieves state-of-the-art performance in a challenging and widely recognized benchmark. The comprehensive comparison against strong baselines like A3C, the thorough ablation studies, and especially the win in a competitive tournament provide exceptionally strong evidence for the effectiveness of the approach.
2.  **Elegant and Flexible Formulation:** The paper's formulation of control as goal-conditioned future prediction is both simple and powerful. It effectively leverages dense, vectorial feedback from the environment and enables a remarkable degree of flexibility, allowing the trained agent to pursue varied and even contradictory goals without retraining. This addresses a significant challenge in developing general-purpose agents.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's claims are rigorously supported by extensive experimentation. The comparison to A3C and DQN across four scenarios of increasing difficulty clearly demonstrates the superiority of DFP. In the complex "Battle" and "Battle 2" scenarios, DFP outperforms the next-best method (A3C) by a factor of 6 and 2.5, respectively. This is a substantial margin. The ablation study in Table 4 further strengthens the claims by demonstrating that both key components of the method—the use of vectorial feedback over a scalar reward and the prediction of measurements at multiple temporal offsets—contribute significantly to performance. The success in the VizDoom competition is a powerful, real-world validation of the model's robustness and generalization capabilities, especially since it outperformed a competitor that used additional supervision.

The flexibility of the goal-agnostic training is another major strength. The experiments in Table 3 show that a model trained with randomized goals can perform nearly as well on a specific task as a model trained specialistically for that task. More importantly, it generalizes far better to novel goals, such as adopting a "pacifist" strategy. This capability of decoupling training from a specific goal is a significant step towards more general and adaptable agents. The approach is well-motivated and properly situated within the literature, drawing connections to prior work on supervised learning for control, Monte Carlo methods, and universal value function approximators, while presenting a concrete, high-performing implementation.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions for improving the paper and are not critical to my decision. They are offered to help strengthen the final version.

1.  **Notational Clarity:**
    *   In Equation (2), the function `F` outputs a prediction, but its result is denoted `p_at`. The subscript `at` is slightly confusing; `p_a` might be clearer since the prediction is for a single action `a`.
    *   In the architecture description for Equation (6), the term `A_i(j)` appears on both sides of the normalization equation. It may be clearer to distinguish the pre-normalized and post-normalized values, for instance by using `A'_i(j)` for the pre-normalized value on the right-hand side.

2.  **Presentation of Results:** In Table 3, the measurements (Ammo, Health, Frags) have different scales and units. While the goal is clear, repeating the units or providing context in the caption for each sub-column could improve readability.

3.  **Minor Factual Clarification:** The paper repeatedly describes Doom as a "three-dimensional" environment. While it was revolutionary for its time, technically its engine uses 2.5D rendering (a 2D map with height information). This is a minor nitpick but mentioning this would add technical precision.

4.  **Scope of Generalization:** The experiments convincingly show generalization across textures and goals within the Doom environment. To make an even stronger case for the generality of the DFP framework, demonstrating its effectiveness in a different domain, such as a robotics simulator with different physics and measurements, would be a valuable future contribution.

### Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate clarification on the following points to deepen my understanding of the work:

1.  **Sensitivity to Measurements:** The measurement stream (`m_t`) is a core component of the model. How sensitive is performance to the choice of measurements? For example, in the "Battle" scenario, what would happen if you removed "health" from the measurement vector but still included it in the goal vector? Does the model learn to infer the missing measurement, or does performance degrade gracefully?
2.  **Choice of Temporal Offsets:** The temporal offsets for future prediction were set to {1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32}. How was this set chosen? How sensitive is the model to these specific values? Is there a principled way to determine an optimal set of offsets for a given environment or task?
3.  **Relationship to Reward Shaping:** The use of a dense, vectorial measurement stream feels conceptually related to dense reward shaping in traditional RL. Could you elaborate on the key differences and advantages of the DFP formulation compared to an RL agent (like A3C) trained with a heavily engineered, dense reward signal composed of the same measurements (e.g., `r_t = 0.5 * delta(ammo) + 0.5 * delta(health) + 1.0 * delta(frags)`)?
4.  **Complexity of Goal Functions:** The paper uses a simple linear goal `g > f`. Have you explored more complex goal functions? For instance, could the agent learn to pursue non-linear goals, such as maintaining health above a certain threshold while maximizing frags? How might this affect the architecture and training?
