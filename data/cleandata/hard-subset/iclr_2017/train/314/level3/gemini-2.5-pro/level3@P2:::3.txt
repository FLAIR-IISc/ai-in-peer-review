### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a novel and effective approach to sensorimotor control in complex, immersive environments. The core contribution is to frame the control problem as a supervised learning task, moving away from the traditional scalar reward paradigm of reinforcement learning. The model learns to predict future values of a low-dimensional stream of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) based on the high-dimensional sensory input (raw pixels), a proposed action, and a goal vector. This formulation provides a dense, rich supervisory signal from the agent's own experience. A key advantage is the ability to train a single, goal-agnostic policy that can be directed to pursue dynamically specified goals at test time by simply changing the input goal vector. The authors conduct extensive experiments in the challenging VizDoom environment, demonstrating that DFP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods like A3C and DQN, particularly on complex combat scenarios. The strength and generalization of the approach are compellingly validated by its victory in the Full Deathmatch track of the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition, which was held in previously unseen environments.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Clear Accept**

1.  **Exceptional Empirical Results:** The paper presents strong, well-documented empirical evidence of the method's effectiveness. The DFP agent not only outperforms strong contemporary baselines (A3C, DQN) by a significant margin on complex tasks but also won a competitive AI challenge, demonstrating its robustness and ability to generalize to unseen environments. This constitutes a milestone result for learning control in visually rich, first-person environments.

2.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Formulation:** The approach of reframing control as a supervised prediction problem using dense, multi-dimensional measurements is elegant and well-motivated. The paper makes a convincing case for this paradigm in immersive environments where such measurement streams are naturally available. The claims are well-supported by a thorough ablation study that validates the key design choices—namely, the use of vectorial feedback over a scalar reward and the prediction of measurements at multiple future time steps.

### Supporting Arguments

The decision is primarily supported by the strength and rigor of the experimental evaluation. The authors systematically compare DFP against relevant and powerful baselines across four scenarios of increasing difficulty. The performance gap in the more complex "Battle" scenarios (D3 and D4) is particularly striking, with DFP outperforming the next best method, A3C, by a factor of 2.5-6 in terms of frags. This is not a marginal improvement but a step-change in capability. The victory in the VizDoom competition serves as an independent, unbiased validation of the method's superiority and generalization power against a field of strong competitors.

Furthermore, the paper's core claims are directly tested and validated. The experiment on "Generalization across environments" (Table 2) shows that training on varied textures leads to robust policies. The "Goal-agnostic training" experiment (Table 3) is a powerful demonstration of the model's flexibility. An agent trained with randomized goals performs nearly as well on a specific task as a specialist agent trained only for that task, while also generalizing far better to novel goals (e.g., acting as a "pacifist").

Finally, the ablation study (Table 4) provides clear evidence for the paper's central theses. It quantitatively shows that predicting multiple measurements is significantly better than predicting a single scalar reward (even when the evaluation metric is that same scalar), and that predicting a sequence of future measurements is better than predicting only a single point in the distant future. This systematically justifies the added complexity of the DFP framework and reinforces the idea that dense, multi-dimensional feedback is a more effective learning signal for these tasks. The paper is also well-situated in the literature, correctly connecting its ideas to prior work on supervised approaches to control, universal value functions, and predictive state representations.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Technical Accuracy:** For precision, it might be worth noting that the classic Doom engine renders a 2.5D world rather than a fully 3D one, though it is presented from a first-person 3D perspective. This is a very minor point and does not detract from the work.
*   **Presentation Clarity:** In tables with heterogeneous data (e.g., Table 3), repeating the measurement units (frags, ammo, health) in the column headers for each training regime would enhance readability and prevent any potential ambiguity for the reader.
*   **Notation:** The notation is mostly clear, but two minor points could be improved. First, using `p` for "prediction" (as in `pat`) is common but can be slightly ambiguous in a machine learning context where it often denotes probability. Second, in Equation (6), the term `Ai(j)` is redefined in terms of itself. Using a different notation, such as `A'_i(j)`, for the normalized output would more clearly distinguish it from the pre-normalization value.

**Questions:**
1.  The experiments are impressively thorough but are confined to the Doom environment. Could you comment on the potential challenges or necessary adaptations for applying DFP to environments with different characteristics, such as robotics simulators with more complex physics (e.g., MuJoCo) or other types of game engines?
2.  The goal vector `g` is a key component of the model's input. How sensitive is performance to the relative scaling of the components within `g` and the measurements `m`? For instance, if health ranges from 0-100 and frags from 0-10, does this require careful normalization for the goal-directed action selection to be effective?
3.  The paper describes a training setup that is largely on-policy but utilizes a small experience memory. Have you experimented with a fully off-policy version of DFP? If so, what were the results? If not, could you speculate on the potential stability challenges of using data from a behavior policy that might diverge significantly from the target policy over time?
