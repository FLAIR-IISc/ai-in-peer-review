### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents Direct Future Prediction (DFP), an approach for training sensorimotor control agents in immersive 3D environments. The core idea is to reframe the control problem as a supervised learning task. Instead of maximizing a scalar reward, the agent learns to predict future changes in a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input, current measurements, a specified goal, and a potential action. The goal itself is defined as a linear combination of these future measurements. At test time, the agent simply selects the action that is predicted to yield the best outcome according to the current goal. This formulation allows the agent to be trained without a fixed objective and to generalize to dynamically changing goals at runtime. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach in the challenging Visual Doom environment, showing that DFP significantly outperforms strong deep reinforcement learning baselines like A3C and DQN, particularly on complex tasks. A model trained with this method also won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, providing strong evidence of its generalization capabilities to unseen environments.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides compelling empirical evidence that the proposed DFP method significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL baselines on a set of complex, visually rich 3D tasks. The success in the Visual Doom AI competition is a particularly strong result, demonstrating effective generalization in a competitive setting.
2.  **Effective Formulation and Integration:** The work successfully reframes the control problem in a way that leverages dense, multi-dimensional feedback from the environment. While the approach builds on existing concepts like Universal Value Functions, its specific formulation, implementation, and application to this domain represent a valuable and insightful contribution that clearly advances the state of the art for this class of problems.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's claims are well-supported by a thorough set of experiments. The comparison against A3C and DQN across four scenarios of increasing difficulty clearly demonstrates the practical advantages of the DFP approach (Figure 3, Table 1). The performance gap widens on the more complex "Battle" scenarios, which supports the central hypothesis that a rich, vectorial measurement stream is a more effective training signal than a single scalar reward.

The paper's placement within the literature is excellent. It correctly identifies its connection to the Universal Value Function Approximator (UVFA) framework (Schaul et al., 2015) and appropriately situates its supervised, Monte-Carlo-style prediction approach relative to dominant TD-learning methods. The architectural design, which incorporates a dueling-style structure to separate action-independent and action-dependent predictions, is well-motivated and likely contributes to its success.

The generalization experiments are a key strength. The ability to train with randomized goals and then successfully pursue specific, unseen goals at test time (Table 3) is a significant step towards more flexible agents. This goal-agnostic training capability is a primary benefit of the proposed framework. Furthermore, the experiments showing generalization across randomly textured environments (Table 2) and the victory in the Doom competition underscore the robustness of the learned policies.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Clarification of Contribution:** The main contribution appears to be a very effective distillation and integration of existing ideas (goal-conditioning, future prediction as a supervised target, dueling architectures) into a coherent and high-performing system, rather than a fundamentally new algorithm. The abstract and introduction could be slightly revised to more explicitly frame the contribution in this light. This would set clearer expectations and highlight the impressive engineering and conceptual synthesis that led to the strong results.
*   **Analysis of Failure Modes:** The paper focuses on the method's successes, but an analysis of its limitations would provide deeper insight. For instance, under what conditions does the model fail to generalize to a new goal? It is plausible that if a goal requires a long sequence of actions that was rarely explored during training, the greedy one-step prediction might fail. Illustrating such failure cases would provide a more complete understanding of the agent's capabilities.
*   **Potential for Catastrophic Forgetting:** The paper mentions that the agent's policy changes as it collects new experiences. This on-policy nature, even with an experience buffer, raises the concern of catastrophic forgetting. If the agent is trained on a distribution of goals and that distribution shifts, will it forget how to accomplish goals from the distant past? A brief discussion of this potential issue would be welcome.
*   **Demonstrating Broader Applicability:** The experiments are confined to the Doom domain. To further strengthen the paper's claims of generality, it would be highly beneficial to demonstrate the method's effectiveness in another domain. For example, applying DFP to Atari games using the RAM state as the measurement vector (`m_t`) could provide powerful evidence that the framework is a general tool for goal-conditioned reinforcement learning.

### Questions for Authors

*   Could you provide more intuition on the selection of temporal offsets for future prediction (1, 2, 4, ..., 32 steps)? How sensitive is the performance to this choice? Have you experimented with predicting only a single, distant future state?
*   The results in Table 3 show that goal-agnostic training performs nearly as well on the primary task as the dedicated model trained with a fixed goal. Does this imply that the agent is effectively learning a set of primitive skills for controlling the measurements, and the goal vector `g` primarily serves as a selector over these skills at test time?
*   In the experiments with randomized goals, were the goal vectors `g` for each episode stored in the experience memory alongside the state-action tuples? If so, how does this interact with the on-policy nature of the learning algorithm?
