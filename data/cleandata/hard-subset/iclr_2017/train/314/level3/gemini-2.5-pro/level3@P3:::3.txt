### **Review**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a novel on-policy method for sensorimotor control in immersive environments. The core idea is to train an agent to predict future changes in a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frags) rather than optimizing a policy to maximize a scalar reward. The model takes the current sensory input, current measurements, and a "goal" vector as input to predict the future measurements for each possible action. The goal vector, which is a set of weights over the predicted measurements, allows the agent's behavior to be dynamically guided at test time. By selecting actions that maximize the goal-weighted predicted outcome, the agent can pursue different objectives without retraining. The method is validated in the VizDoom environment, where it demonstrates strong empirical performance, outperforming strong deep RL baselines like A3C and DQN. The paper's claims are further substantiated by winning the Full Deathmatch track of the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition, which tested generalization in unseen environments.

**2. Recommendation: Clear Accept**

This is a strong paper that should be accepted. The primary reasons are its excellent empirical results on challenging tasks and the clear demonstration of generalization to new goals and unseen environments. The proposed method is simple, effective, and represents a milestone for learning complex behaviors directly from raw pixels in 3D environments.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

This paper successfully addresses three key questions for a high-quality publication:

*   **What is the specific question/problem tackled by the paper?**
    The paper tackles the challenging problem of learning flexible sensorimotor control from high-dimensional visual input in complex, dynamic 3D environments. It specifically addresses how an agent can learn general skills that can be repurposed for dynamically changing goals, moving beyond the standard fixed-reward reinforcement learning paradigm.

*   **Is the approach well motivated, including being well-placed in the literature?**
    Yes, the approach is very well motivated. The authors argue that for agents in immersive environments, a dense, multi-dimensional stream of intrinsic measurements provides a richer supervisory signal than a sparse, scalar reward. This intuition is compelling and provides a solid foundation for their method. The paper does a good job of situating itself within the literature, connecting to concepts from intrinsic motivation, forward modeling, and universal value function approximators. Citations are adequate and place the work in proper context, acknowledging both historical precedents and contemporary related work.

*   **Does the paper support the claims?**
    The claims are exceptionally well-supported by rigorous and comprehensive experiments.
    *   **Strong Empirical Results:** The comparative experiments against established baselines (A3C, DQN, and an attempt at DSR) are convincing. DFP significantly outperforms these methods in the more complex "Battle" scenarios, demonstrating its effectiveness where other state-of-the-art methods struggle or plateau.
    *   **Ablation Study:** The ablation study in Table 4 is crucial. It clearly demonstrates that both key components of the method—using vectorial feedback (multiple measurements) and predicting at multiple future temporal offsets—are beneficial. This justifies the design choices and added model complexity over simpler alternatives.
    *   **Generalization and Flexibility:** The goal-agnostic training experiments are a highlight, showing that an agent can learn general skills and then adapt its behavior to diverse, even contradictory, test-time goals (e.g., playing as a "pacifist"). This is a significant step towards more flexible AI. The crowning achievement is winning the Visual Doom AI competition, which serves as unbiased, third-party validation of the method's robustness and ability to generalize to completely unseen environments.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions to further strengthen the manuscript and are not critical to the acceptance decision.

*   **Minor Factual Inaccuracy:** The paper repeatedly refers to Doom as a "three-dimensional" environment. While it provides an immersive first-person perspective, the original Doom engine is technically pseudo-3D (or 2.5D), using 2D maps with variable height information. This is a minor point, but mentioning it would be more precise.
*   **Notational Clarity:** A couple of notational choices could be improved for clarity.
    *   In Equation (2), `pat = F(...)`, the use of "p" for "prediction" is a bit unfortunate, as "P" is ubiquitously used for "probability" in the machine learning literature. Using a different symbol might prevent momentary confusion.
    *   In Equation (6), the index `j` is used to represent the joint input representation from Equation (5) and also as a summation index within the equation itself (`∑k=1 Ak(j)`). Using a different letter for the summation index (e.g., `k`) would avoid this ambiguity.
*   **Presentation of Results:** In tables reporting heterogeneous measurements like Table 3 (Ammo, Health, Frags), repeating the measurement name in the header for each training regime (a, b, c) would improve readability. Currently, the reader has to track which column corresponds to which measurement across the table.

**5. Questions for Authors**
*   How sensitive is the model's performance to the specific choice of temporal offsets (`τ`) for future prediction? Was any search performed over these values, or were they chosen based on heuristics?
*   The DFP framework relies on a set of human-defined "intrinsic measurements." How do you envision this approach extending to environments where such measurements are not readily available? Could they be learned in an unsupervised fashion, for example, from an autoencoder's latent space?
