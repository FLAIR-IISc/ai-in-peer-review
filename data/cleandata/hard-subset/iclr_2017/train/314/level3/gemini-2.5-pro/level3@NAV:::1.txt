This paper presents a novel approach for sensorimotor control in complex 3D environments, framing the deep reinforcement learning problem as a supervised learning task. The core contribution is an algorithm that learns to predict a vector of future, low-dimensional game measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on the current sensory input, action, and a specified goal vector. Instead of optimizing a policy based on a scalar reward, the agent selects actions that are predicted to maximize the desired outcome according to its current goal. The authors provide compelling empirical validation in challenging Visual Doom scenarios, demonstrating that their method, Direct Future Prediction (DFP), significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL baselines like A3C and DQN. Furthermore, they show the learned policies can generalize to new goals and previously unseen environments, underscored by their model winning the Visual Doom AI Competition.

I recommend to **Accept** this paper. The primary reasons are (1) the elegant and effective reframing of the control problem, which provides a strong alternative to traditional value-function-based RL in measurement-rich environments, and (2) the exceptionally strong and thorough empirical results that convincingly support the paper’s claims.

The supporting arguments for this decision are robust. The paper is well-positioned within the literature, drawing a clear distinction between its Monte Carlo-style supervised prediction and dominant temporal-difference methods. The experimental results are comprehensive, including rigorous comparisons against strong baselines, ablation studies that validate key design choices (vectorial feedback, multi-step prediction), and impressive generalization experiments. The win in a major AI competition serves as a powerful, real-world validation of the method's effectiveness and practicality. The work is of significant interest to the community, as it demonstrates that for a certain class of problems, simpler supervised techniques can outperform more complex RL algorithms.

To further improve the paper, I would suggest the following points for consideration. First, the paper notes that its supervised training departs from a pure on-policy setup by using an experience replay buffer. A brief discussion on the potential implications of this approximation—for instance, any potential bias introduced by using data from older policies to train a predictor for the current one—would strengthen the analysis. Second, the method’s reliance on a pre-defined set of meaningful measurements gives it access to information unavailable to the baseline algorithms, which only receive a unified reward signal. Clarifying this as a potential limitation or scope condition would be beneficial.

I would like the authors to answer the following questions:
1. Could you elaborate on the potential effects of using an experience replay buffer? Does sampling from a buffer of older experiences introduce any observable bias in the future predictions, and how might this affect training stability?
2. How sensitive is the model’s performance to the specific choice of measurements provided? For example, in the "Battle" scenario, what would happen if the "frag count" measurement was withheld?
