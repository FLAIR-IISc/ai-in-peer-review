

This paper presents an intriguing approach to sensorimotor control that reframes the problem as supervised learning rather than reinforcement learning. The authors propose a method that utilizes both high-dimensional sensory input (e.g., visual observations) and lower-dimensional measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) to create a rich supervisory signal. The key insight is that the temporal interlock between sensory and measurement streams provides sufficient information for effective learning without requiring extraneous supervision. The model learns to predict future measurements based on current observations, measurements, and a goal specification, then selects actions that maximize predicted goal achievement. The approach is evaluated extensively in 3D Doom environments, demonstrating clear empirical advantages over state-of-the-art deep RL methods, with the authors' model winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

Decision: Accept. The paper makes a compelling empirical contribution that demonstrates significant performance gains in complex 3D environments, particularly where previous RL methods struggle. The approach is well-motivated and supported by thorough experimentation, showing strong generalization capabilities across environments and goals.

Supporting arguments: The paper's main contribution lies in its practical demonstration of how rich, temporally dense supervision from measurement streams can significantly outperform sparse scalar rewards in complex immersive environments. The experimental results are robust, with the method outperforming DQN, A3C, and DSR by substantial margins (6x in Battle scenario), and the competition win provides strong validation. The approach elegantly handles the challenge of learning without fixed goals during training while adapting to dynamically specified goals at test time. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the importance of vectorial measurements over scalar rewards and multiple temporal offsets. The paper's thorough comparison with relevant baselines and careful experimental design provide strong scientific rigor.

The paper successfully addresses key challenges in sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments, which has been a persistent challenge for traditional RL approaches. The method's simplicity and effectiveness in a challenging domain like Doom make it a valuable contribution to the field. The authors provide clear evidence that the measurement stream provides superior supervision compared to traditional RL reward signals.

Additional feedback: The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of the novelty in the abstract and introduction. While the method builds on concepts from universal value function approaches (Schaul et al., 2015), the paper should more explicitly clarify how this formulation differs and why it's particularly effective in immersive 3D environments. The current presentation sometimes reads as a distillation of existing ideas rather than a novel algorithmic contribution. Clarifying this in the introduction would strengthen the paper's impact.

The paper should also address the potential for catastrophic forgetting during on-policy training, as noted in the key points. The authors use a small experience memory, but it's unclear how they handle the potential issue of forgetting goals from earlier training periods. A brief discussion of this limitation and potential mitigation strategies would strengthen the paper.

Additionally, the authors could provide more detailed analysis of failure modes, particularly regarding goal generalization. The paper shows the approach works well for most goal variations, but it would be valuable to understand the specific conditions under which it struggles to generalize to new goals. A failure case analysis would provide deeper insights into the method's limitations.

The paper would be strengthened by a brief exploration of the approach's applicability beyond Doom. While the authors mention potential extensions to continuous actions, a short discussion of how the method might transfer to other domains (e.g., Atari using ROM as intrinsic variables) would help demonstrate broader relevance.

Questions for authors:
1. The paper mentions the model generalizes across environments and goals, but could you provide more specific examples of goal configurations where generalization fails? What are the boundary conditions for effective goal generalization?
2. Given the on-policy training approach with a limited experience memory, how do you handle the potential for catastrophic forgetting of goals from earlier training periods? Is there a specific mechanism to ensure goals from different training phases are appropriately represented?
3. The paper focuses exclusively on Doom, but how might this approach be adapted to environments where meaningful measurements are less readily available or more ambiguous? Could the method learn to identify appropriate measurements from raw sensory input?
