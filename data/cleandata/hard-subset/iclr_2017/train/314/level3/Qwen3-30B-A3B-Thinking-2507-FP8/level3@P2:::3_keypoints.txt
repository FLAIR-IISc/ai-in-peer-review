- Proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a method that predicts future measurements from sensory input, current measurements, and a goal vector to learn sensorimotor control.  
- DFP enables learning from raw experience without extraneous supervision and supports dynamically specified goals at test time.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance, outperforming DQN, A3C, and DSR across ViZDoom scenarios and winning the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition by a large margin.  
- Argues that cotemporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides richer supervision than sparse scalar rewards.  
- Well-situated in related work on supervised control, vector-valued feedback, and future prediction.  
- Ablation study shows vectorial feedback and multiple temporal offsets are key to the method’s success.  
- Shows generalization across different environments and the ability to handle changing goals at test time.  
- Real-world validation via competition win shows simpler DFP can outperform more complex architectures with extra supervision.  
- Minor factual correction: Doom is technically a 2D engine rendered in fake 3D and should be described more accurately.  
- Notational issues: clarify use of "P" in Eq. (2) and the double use of index "j" in Eq. (6).  
- Results tables should state units for heterogeneous measurements (e.g., normalized health, frag counts).  
- Suggest discussing limitations of ViZDoom for generalization to diverse 3D environments.  
- Question: How does DFP handle goals not expressible as linear combinations of measurements (non-linear goals)?  
- Question: Why does predicting multiple measurements improve performance even when evaluating a single measurement—what’s the theoretical explanation?  
- Question: How would adding memory mechanisms affect DFP’s performance and what are the trade-offs?  
- Overall recommendation: strong, simple, and effective approach with rigorous experiments — recommend acceptance.
