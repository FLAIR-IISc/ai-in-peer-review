

This paper introduces Direct Future Prediction (DFP), an on-policy reinforcement learning method that predicts future intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frags) rather than relying on scalar rewards. The approach leverages the cotemporal structure of sensory and measurement streams to provide dense supervision for learning sensorimotor control from raw experience. By training a predictor to anticipate future measurements given current observations, goals, and actions, the method enables flexible goal pursuit without requiring fixed training objectives. The authors demonstrate that DFP outperforms state-of-the-art methods (A3C, DQN, DSR) in challenging vizDoom scenarios, particularly when multiple measurements are available, and achieved victory in the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by demonstrating that vectorial future prediction provides superior supervision compared to scalar rewards for complex 3D environments. The empirical results are compelling, with DFP achieving significant performance gains (up to 6x over A3C) in the most challenging scenarios while requiring less complex architecture than competing approaches. The ablation study effectively validates the importance of key components, and the method's ability to generalize to unseen environments and adapt to new goals at test time represents a substantial advance.

Minor issues include describing Doom as "3D" when it's 2D rendered in fake 3D, and notational inconsistencies in equations (2) and (6). The presentation could be improved by repeating measurement units in results tables. However, these do not undermine the paper's strong scientific contribution.

To further strengthen the work, the authors might clarify how the goal-weighting mechanism specifically enables exploration during training, and provide more detail on how the normalization layer in equation (6) affects learning dynamics. The competition results are particularly impressive given that DFP required no additional supervision beyond what was available to all competitors. The method's simplicity combined with its strong empirical performance makes it a valuable milestone for the field.
