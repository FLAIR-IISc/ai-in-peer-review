

**Review of "Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control in Immersive Environments"**

1. **Summary of Contributions**: The paper introduces a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments by framing the problem as supervised learning of future measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frag count) conditioned on the current state, a goal vector, and an action. The core insight is that the temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides a rich supervisory signal, allowing the agent to learn to act based on raw sensory input without extraneous supervision. The method outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL approaches in challenging Doom-based scenarios, demonstrates strong generalization across environments and goals, and won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition. The authors present a compelling case for using vectorial measurements rather than scalar rewards, and show that the approach can handle dynamically changing goals at test time without retraining.

2. **Decision**: Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning by demonstrating that sensorimotor control in complex environments can be effectively learned using supervised learning techniques when provided with dense, multidimensional feedback. The empirical results are strong, the method is well-motivated, and the approach has been validated through a competition win.

3. **Supporting Arguments**: The paper addresses a meaningful challenge in RL: learning sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments without sparse rewards. The approach is well-placed in the literature, with thoughtful connections to prior work on supervised learning for control, Monte Carlo methods, and goal-conditioned learning. The experimental results are scientifically rigorous, with thorough comparisons to state-of-the-art methods (DQN, A3C, DSR) across four increasingly difficult Doom scenarios. The method shows consistent improvements over baselines, particularly in the more complex Battle scenarios where it outperforms A3C by factors of 6 and 2.5 in D3 and D4, respectively. The generalization experiments across environments and goals are particularly convincing, showing that the model can adapt to new tasks without retraining. The competition win in the Visual Doom AI Competition (outperforming the second-best submission by over 50%) provides strong external validation of the approach's effectiveness.

4. **Additional Feedback**: The paper is well-written and clearly communicates its contributions. To further strengthen the work, the authors might consider:
   - Discussing the on-policy assumption of supervised learning in more depth, particularly given they use a replay buffer (an approximation of off-policy training). It would be valuable to understand how this impacts training dynamics and whether the method would work without experience replay.
   - More clearly articulating the limitations of the approach when the relevant measurements are not available (e.g., if the environment doesn't provide structured measurements like health or ammo). The authors note this as a minor concern, but it's worth elaborating on in the paper.
   - Providing more details on the computational efficiency of the method compared to baselines, particularly since the paper mentions training speeds (e.g., DFP trains at 1.5M steps/day vs. DSR's 1M steps/10 days).

5. **Questions for Authors**:
   - How sensitive is the method to the choice of measurements? The paper assumes these measurements are provided (e.g., health, ammo), but in some environments, these might not be available or might require additional processing. Could the method learn to predict these measurements from raw sensory input, or would it require additional supervision?
   - The paper uses a replay buffer with a small experience memory, which departs from pure on-policy training. Could the authors share more details about how this affects training stability and convergence, and whether the method would work without experience replay?
   - The paper mentions that the method was the winner of the Visual Doom AI Competition. Could the authors provide more details about the specific training regime used for the competition entry (e.g., whether it used the D4-tx-L architecture mentioned in the paper)?
