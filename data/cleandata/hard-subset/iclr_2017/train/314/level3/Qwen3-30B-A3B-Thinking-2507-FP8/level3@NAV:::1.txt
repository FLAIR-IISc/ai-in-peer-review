

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments by framing the problem as supervised learning of future measurements conditioned on current state, action, and goal. The authors introduce a direct future prediction (DFP) algorithm that outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods in complex Doom environments, demonstrates strong generalization across goals and environments, and won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition. The key innovation lies in using low-dimensional measurements (e.g., health, ammo) as supervisory signals rather than scalar rewards, enabling effective learning from raw sensory input while supporting flexible goal-directed behavior.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a compelling contribution by demonstrating that supervised Monte Carlo regression can outperform traditional RL approaches in challenging 3D environments, with significant empirical validation across multiple difficulty levels. The Visual Doom competition win provides strong real-world validation of the approach's effectiveness. The paper is well-communicated, with clear motivation, thorough experiments, and convincing ablation studies showing the benefits of vectorial measurements and multi-step prediction.

The minor concerns raised in the key points are valid but do not undermine the main contribution. The authors should explicitly discuss the tension between the on-policy assumption and replay buffer usage, and more clearly acknowledge the limitation that their method relies on metadata (measurements) not available to compared algorithms. However, these points can be addressed in a revision without affecting the paper's core contribution.

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:
1. Adding a more detailed discussion of the measurement selection process and how it might generalize to environments where such measurements aren't naturally available
2. Providing a clearer comparison of the computational efficiency of DFP versus the baselines
3. Including a brief analysis of the robustness of the approach to noisy measurements

I would like to ask the authors to clarify how their method would handle environments where the relevant measurements are not explicitly provided by the environment, and whether the approach could be extended to learn these measurements from raw sensory input through self-supervision.
