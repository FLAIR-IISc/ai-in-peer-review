

# Review for ICLR_2017 Paper

## 1. Summary of Contribution

The paper introduces Direct Future Prediction (DFP), an on-policy method that shifts sensorimotor control from traditional reward-based reinforcement learning to predicting future intrinsic measurements. Instead of optimizing for a scalar reward, DFP treats the agent's measurements (health, ammunition, frags) as a multidimensional stream and learns to predict how different actions will affect these measurements over time. This approach enables training without a fixed goal and allows the agent to pursue dynamically specified goals at test time by selecting actions that maximize predicted future measurements. The method achieved strong empirical results, winning the Full Deathmatch track of the 2016 VizDoom AI Competition, and outperformed state-of-the-art deep RL methods (A3C, DQN, and DSR) on challenging tasks in immersive 3D environments. The paper demonstrates that vectorial feedback provides a richer supervisory signal than scalar rewards, particularly in complex scenarios with multiple measurements.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper demonstrates significant empirical progress in sensorimotor control for complex environments, with results that are scientifically rigorous and reproducible. The method's ability to win the VizDoom competition and outperform established baselines on challenging tasks represents a clear milestone in the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Question 1: What specific problem does the paper tackle?**  
The paper addresses two key challenges in reinforcement learning: (1) sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments, and (2) acquiring general skills that can flexibly pursue dynamically specified goals. By shifting from scalar rewards to predicting multidimensional measurements, DFP provides a richer supervisory signal that stabilizes and accelerates training while enabling goal flexibility.

**Question 2: Is the approach well motivated and situated in the literature?**  
Yes. The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation by connecting to supervised learning principles, Monte Carlo methods, and prior work on vector-valued feedback. It clearly situates itself within the literature on intrinsic motivation, auxiliary tasks, and forward modeling, citing relevant work while acknowledging limitations of prior approaches. The motivation is compelling: traditional RL's reliance on sparse scalar rewards is ill-suited for immersive environments where rich, temporally dense feedback (health, ammunition, etc.) naturally exists.

**Question 3: Does the paper support its claims with correct and rigorous evidence?**  
Yes. The empirical evaluation is thorough, with four progressively challenging Doom scenarios demonstrating DFP's superiority over A3C, DQN, and DSR. The ablation study confirms that vectorial feedback and multi-temporal prediction are beneficial. The method's competition success (winning by >50% over the second-place submission) provides strong real-world validation. The experiments are well-designed with appropriate baselines, multiple training regimes, and extensive generalization tests across environments. The paper demonstrates that DFP outperforms state-of-the-art methods on challenging tasks where others reach performance ceilings.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further improve the paper:

- **Minor formatting issues:** The paper describes Doom as "3D" when it's a 2D game rendered in fake 3D. This should be corrected for technical accuracy.
- **Notational clarity:** Equation (2) uses "P" for prediction, which is misleading as it's not probability. Consider using "F" or another symbol. Equation (6) uses "j" for both the input representation and an index, which could confuse readers.
- **Presentation improvement:** Results tables should explicitly repeat units for heterogeneous measurements (e.g., "frags" vs. "health") to avoid ambiguity.
- **Clarification request:** The paper mentions "the use of vectorial measurements instead of a scalar reward is beneficial" but doesn't explicitly quantify how much of the performance gain is due to the vectorial nature versus other components. A brief analysis of this decomposition would strengthen the claims.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field with a simple yet powerful approach that achieves state-of-the-art results on challenging tasks. The experimental soundness, reproducibility (with clear implementation details), and milestone status (winning the VizDoom competition) justify publication. The minor issues noted above are easily addressable and do not undermine the paper's substantial contribution.
