

# Review of "Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper presents an innovative approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments by leveraging the cotemporal structure of sensory and measurement streams. The authors propose training a model to predict future measurements based on current sensory input, measurements, and goals, which can then be used to select actions that optimize the predicted future measurements. The method is well-motivated by the observation that many real-world systems provide rich, multidimensional measurements rather than sparse scalar rewards. The approach demonstrates clear empirical advantages over state-of-the-art deep RL methods in the challenging Doom environment, with the model winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition by a significant margin (over 50% better than the second-place submission). The paper provides valuable insights into the benefits of vectorial measurements over scalar rewards and shows strong generalization across environments and goals.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a compelling empirical case for the effectiveness of the proposed approach in a challenging 3D navigation task, with results that are both significant and well-documented. The insights about the advantages of vectorial measurements over scalar rewards are particularly valuable for future work.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main strength is its strong empirical results demonstrating clear advantages in complex navigation tasks. The Visual Doom AI Competition win provides compelling real-world validation of the approach. The method is well-placed in the literature, correctly positioning itself as a special case of universal value function approaches (Schaul et al., 2015) and providing appropriate citations to related work. The experiments are well-designed, with controlled ablation studies demonstrating the importance of vectorial measurements and multiple temporal offsets.

However, the technical contribution is primarily an integration of existing ideas rather than a novel algorithmic advance. The paper should clarify this distinction more explicitly in the abstract and introduction, as the current presentation may overstate the novelty of the approach. The authors have made a useful contribution by demonstrating how these concepts can be applied effectively to a challenging 3D navigation task, but the core methodological ideas are not entirely new.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. The paper should more clearly acknowledge that the main contribution is the distillation and application of existing ideas rather than a novel algorithmic advance. This clarification should be made in the abstract and introduction to set appropriate expectations for readers.

2. The paper would benefit from a more thorough analysis of failure modes, particularly regarding generalization to changing goals. The authors should provide examples of situations where the model struggles to generalize, and discuss why these failures occur.

3. The conceptual issue of catastrophic forgetting in on-policy training (when goals from the distant past are not revisited) should be addressed more thoroughly. The authors should discuss how this limitation might affect performance in more complex environments and what strategies could be used to mitigate it.

4. Expanding experiments to other domains (e.g., Atari using ROM as intrinsic variables) would strengthen the case for broader applicability. The authors should consider this as a future direction.

5. The paper should more explicitly discuss how their approach relates to Schaul et al. (2015) and other universal value function approaches, clarifying that their method is a special case of this broader framework.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How would you characterize the novelty of your approach relative to Schaul et al. (2015) and other universal value function approaches? What specific aspects of their framework does your method leverage?

2. Can you provide more details about the failure modes of your approach when tested with changing goals? Under what conditions does the model struggle to generalize?

3. The paper mentions that "on-policy training can cause catastrophic forgetting if goals from the distant past are not revisited." How does your approach mitigate this issue, and what evidence do you have that this is not a significant problem in your experiments?

4. Have you considered applying this approach to other domains beyond Doom (e.g., Atari with ROM as intrinsic variables)? If not, what would be the main challenges in doing so?
