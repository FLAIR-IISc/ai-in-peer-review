

# Review for ICLR 2017 Paper

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments that fundamentally departs from traditional reinforcement learning paradigms. The authors propose a method that frames learning to act as supervised prediction of future low-dimensional measurements (e.g., health, ammo) conditioned on a goal vector and current action, rather than optimizing for a scalar reward. The key insight is that the cotemporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides rich, temporally dense supervision for learning. The approach, called Direct Future Prediction (DFP), was designed to learn from raw sensory input without extraneous supervision, train without fixed goals, and generalize across goals and environments. The method achieved state-of-the-art performance in complex 3D environments, won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition (outperforming the second-best submission by over 50%), and demonstrated the ability to adapt to new goals without retraining. The paper makes several important contributions: framing learning as supervised Monte Carlo regression, using low-dimensional measurements as supervision, enabling goal-conditioned learning, and providing compelling empirical evidence of generalization.

## 2. Decision

Accept.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

This paper makes a strong contribution to deep reinforcement learning that addresses key limitations of current approaches. The authors clearly articulate the problem: traditional RL relies on sparse scalar rewards, which is inadequate for complex sensorimotor control tasks where dense, multidimensional feedback is naturally available. Their approach is well-motivated by both the literature and empirical observations about the structure of immersive environments.

The paper's main contribution is well-supported by rigorous experiments. The authors conduct thorough evaluations across four increasingly challenging Doom scenarios, demonstrating that DFP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art methods (DQN, A3C, DSR) in complex tasks, particularly when multiple measurements are available. The ablation studies (Table 4) convincingly show the importance of vectorial feedback and multi-temporal prediction. The generalization experiments across environments (Table 2) and the ability to handle dynamically changing goals (Table 3) further strengthen the case for the method's robustness and flexibility.

The Visual Doom AI Competition victory provides compelling real-world validation. The fact that their simpler approach outperformed a more complex baseline (Lample & Chaplot, 2016) that used additional supervision (ground-truth enemy presence) demonstrates the method's practical advantages.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is well-written and compelling, a few improvements would strengthen it further:

1. The paper should more explicitly address the on-policy assumption in supervised training when using a replay buffer. The authors acknowledge this as a minor concern but could provide more insight into why the approximation works well in practice.

2. The reliance on specific measurement metadata (e.g., health, ammo) as supervision should be more thoroughly discussed in the context of environments where such measurements are unavailable. The authors mention this as a limitation but could better clarify the boundaries of the approach.

3. The paper could provide more detail about the computational efficiency of the method, particularly in comparison to other approaches. The training speed mentioned in Table 1 is valuable but could be expanded.

4. The discussion of how the method might extend to continuous action spaces would be helpful, as the paper notes this as a potential future direction.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In the training regime with randomized goals (b and c in Table 3), how does the model learn to distinguish between measurements that are desirable (e.g., health, frags) versus those that are not (e.g., ammo drain)? What mechanisms allow the model to generalize to goals that might conflict with the training distribution?

2. The paper mentions that the measurement stream is "lower-dimensional" than the sensory stream. Could you provide more concrete examples of the dimensionality ratio in your experiments (e.g., sensory input dimensions vs. measurement dimensions)?

3. The paper states that the approach "outperforms sophisticated prior formulations, particularly on challenging tasks." Could you elaborate on which specific aspects of the challenging tasks (D3 and D4) make them particularly difficult for standard RL methods, and how the measurement-based approach specifically addresses these challenges?

4. The paper notes that the method "was the winner of the Visual Doom AI competition." Given that the competition used previously unseen environments, could you discuss how the model's generalization capability (as demonstrated in Table 2) contributed to this victory?
