- Proposes supervised learning for sensorimotor control by predicting low-dimensional measurements instead of using RL.
- Leverages cotemporal alignment of sensory and measurement streams to provide dense temporal supervision, avoiding sparse scalar rewards.
- Predicts future measurements conditioned on a goal vector and candidate actions; selects actions by maximizing predicted goal-specific outcomes.
- Evaluated extensively in Doom-based environments and outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods, especially on tasks requiring dynamic goal adaptation.
- Won the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch track, outperforming the second-place entry by over 50% without extra supervision or human data.
- Can be trained without fixed goals and adapts to dynamically specified goals at test time — a notable conceptual advance.
- Empirical validation is thorough: multiple scenarios of increasing difficulty, cross-environment generalization tests, and ablation studies.
- Strong baseline comparisons (DQN, A3C, DSR) show consistent and significant performance gains; e.g., large margins in Battle scenarios.
- Ablations show vectorial measurements and multi-temporal prediction materially improve performance over scalar rewards.
- Architecture and training procedure are clearly described and appear reproducible; paper is well written and well situated in prior work.
- Minor concern: use of a replay buffer with ostensibly on-policy training creates tension with the supervised framing and needs clarification.
- Limitation: reliance on environment-specific measurement metadata (health, ammo, frags) may limit applicability where such measurements are absent or hard to define.
- Recommendation: add discussion on theoretical implications of Monte Carlo prediction vs TD learning, measurement selection/generalization, and computational cost of predicting multiple measurements.
- Questions for authors: (1) Why use a replay buffer with on-policy training and how does it affect theoretical guarantees? (2) How to adapt the method when natural measurements are unavailable and what performance impact is expected? (3) Why does goal-agnostic (random goal) training generalize better to the main task than fixed-goal training?
