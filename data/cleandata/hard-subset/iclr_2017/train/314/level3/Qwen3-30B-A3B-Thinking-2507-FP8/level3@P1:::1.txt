

The paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments that frames learning as supervised prediction of low-dimensional measurements rather than traditional reinforcement learning. The authors introduce a method that leverages the cotemporal structure of sensory and measurement streams to provide rich, temporally dense supervision, eliminating the need for sparse scalar rewards. The key innovation lies in predicting future measurements conditioned on a goal vector and current action, with action selection performed by choosing the action that maximizes the predicted outcome for the specified goal. The approach is evaluated extensively in Doom-based environments, demonstrating superior performance over state-of-the-art deep RL methods, particularly on complex tasks requiring dynamic goal adaptation. Most significantly, a model trained with this approach won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming the second-best submission by over 50% without additional supervision or human gameplay data.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of embodied AI by demonstrating that supervised learning techniques can achieve superior performance in complex 3D environments compared to traditional RL approaches. The method's ability to learn without fixed goals during training and adapt to dynamically specified goals at test time represents a significant conceptual advance. The empirical validation is thorough, with comprehensive experiments across multiple scenarios of increasing difficulty, generalization tests across environments, and ablation studies that convincingly demonstrate the importance of vectorial measurements and multi-temporal prediction.

The paper's empirical results are compelling and well-supported. The Visual Doom AI Competition victory provides strong real-world validation of the approach's effectiveness. The comparison against multiple strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across four progressively challenging scenarios shows consistent and significant performance advantages, particularly in the more complex Battle scenarios where the model outperforms A3C by factors of 6 and 2.5. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate that the vectorial measurement approach provides a substantial advantage over scalar rewards, and the generalization results across different environments and textures further validate the model's robustness. The architecture and training procedure are clearly described, and the authors provide sufficient detail to reproduce their results.

The paper is well-written and clearly communicates its contribution. The background section thoughtfully situates the work within the broader literature on supervised learning for control, RL, and goal-conditioned learning. The distinction between sensory input and measurements is well-motivated, and the approach's relationship to prior work (notably Schaul et al., 2015) is clearly articulated.

A minor concern is the use of a replay buffer with on-policy training, which creates a subtle tension with the supervised learning framing. The authors should clarify how this implementation choice affects the theoretical grounding of their approach. Another point for consideration is the reliance on specific measurement metadata (health, ammo, frags) that might not be available in all environments. The authors should more explicitly discuss the limitations of their approach when such measurements are unavailable or difficult to define.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend adding a brief discussion of the theoretical implications of using Monte Carlo prediction versus TD learning in this context, and clarifying how the measurement selection process might generalize to other environments where such natural measurements are less apparent. The authors should also consider discussing the potential computational overhead of predicting multiple measurements compared to single-scalar reward approaches.

Questions for the authors:
1. In the training procedure, you use a replay buffer with on-policy training. How does this choice impact the theoretical guarantees of your supervised learning approach, and why was this implementation chosen over pure on-policy training?
2. The paper relies on specific measurements (health, ammo, frags) that are naturally available in the Doom environment. How would you adapt this approach to environments where such natural measurements are not available or difficult to define, and what would be the expected performance impact?
3. In the goal-agnostic training experiments (Table 3), the model trained with random goal vectors (regime b) performed better than the fixed goal regime (a) on the main task. Could you elaborate on why the model trained with goal-agnostic objectives generalizes better to the main task than the specifically trained model, given that the main task was the training goal for regime (a)?
