

This paper introduces a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments by framing learning as supervised prediction of future measurements rather than traditional reinforcement learning. The authors present an on-policy method that utilizes a rich stream of intrinsic measurements (health, ammunition, frag count) alongside visual input to predict future states, enabling training without a fixed goal and flexible goal adaptation at test time. The method demonstrates significant empirical advantages over state-of-the-art deep RL approaches in complex Doom navigation tasks, with the model winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

Decision: Accept (with minor revisions)

The paper effectively addresses the problem of learning sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments, with clear empirical advantages demonstrated through extensive experiments. The approach is well-motivated and properly situated within the literature, with appropriate citations to related work on universal value functions (Schaul et al., 2015) and supervised learning for control (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992). The results are scientifically rigorous, with multiple experiments demonstrating superior performance and generalization capabilities.

However, the paper would benefit from clearer articulation of its technical contribution, as the method appears to be a specific application of existing universal value function concepts rather than a novel algorithmic advance. The authors should explicitly clarify how their formulation differs from prior work in the abstract and introduction. Additionally, the paper lacks analysis of failure modes - it would be valuable to understand specific conditions where the model struggles to generalize to changing goals. The on-policy training approach also raises concerns about catastrophic forgetting, which the authors should address.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend expanding the experiments to demonstrate broader applicability (e.g., using ROM as intrinsic variables in Atari environments) and providing more detailed analysis of failure cases. The authors should also address the conceptual issue of on-policy training potentially causing catastrophic forgetting when goals from distant past are not revisited. These additions would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and impact.
