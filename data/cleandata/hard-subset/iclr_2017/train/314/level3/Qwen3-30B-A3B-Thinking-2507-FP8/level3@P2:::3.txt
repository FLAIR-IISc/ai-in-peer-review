

**Review for ICLR 2017**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments that leverages the cotemporal structure of sensory and measurement streams to provide rich supervision for learning. The authors propose a method called Direct Future Prediction (DFP) that trains a model to predict future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frags) based on current sensory input, measurements, and a goal vector. This approach enables learning from raw experience without extraneous supervision, supports training without a fixed goal, and allows flexible deployment of learned skills to dynamically specified goals at test time. The authors demonstrate that their method outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL approaches in challenging 3D environments, winning the Full Deathmatch track of the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition by a significant margin (over 50% better than the runner-up).

**2. Decision**

Accept. The paper makes a strong empirical contribution with a clear, well-executed experimental validation of a simple yet effective approach to sensorimotor control that outperforms sophisticated baselines in a challenging domain. The method's success in the Visual Doom AI Competition provides compelling real-world validation.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper successfully addresses a significant challenge in reinforcement learning: learning sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments. The authors provide a clear motivation for their approach by contrasting it with traditional RL, arguing that the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides richer supervision than sparse scalar rewards. The method is well-placed in the literature, with appropriate discussion of related work on supervised learning for control, vector-valued feedback, and future prediction.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous and compelling. The authors conducted extensive experiments across four increasingly challenging scenarios in the ViZDoom platform, comparing their approach against DQN, A3C, and DSR. The results consistently show that DFP outperforms these baselines, particularly in the more complex scenarios (Battle and Battle 2), where it achieves performance improvements of 6x and 2.5x over A3C. The ablation study provides strong evidence that the vectorial feedback and multiple temporal offsets are key to the method's success. The authors further demonstrate the method's generalization capabilities across different environments and its ability to handle dynamically changing goals at test time.

The paper's real-world validation through winning the Visual Doom AI Competition is particularly impressive. The authors' model achieved superior performance to the runner-up (Lample & Chaplot, 2016), which employed a more complex architecture with additional supervision from the game engine, demonstrating that their simpler approach is more effective.

**4. Additional Feedback**

* The paper describes Doom as a 3D environment, though it's technically a 2D environment presented in a fake 3D perspective. Clarifying this would improve accuracy.

* There are minor notational issues: the use of "P" in equation (2) is misleading (it's used for prediction, not probability), and the double use of "j" in equation (6) may confuse readers. These should be clarified.

* In the results tables, it would be helpful to explicitly state units for heterogeneous measurements (e.g., "health (normalized)", "frags (count)") to improve readability.

* While the paper demonstrates strong performance in the Doom environment, the authors might consider discussing the limitations of this specific environment for generalizing to more diverse 3D settings.

**Questions for Authors**

1. The paper claims that the approach can handle "dynamically changing goals at test time." How does the method handle goals that are not expressible as linear combinations of the measurements (e.g., complex goals requiring non-linear transformations of measurements)?

2. In the ablation study (Table 4), the authors show that predicting multiple measurements improves performance even when evaluated on just one measurement. What is the theoretical explanation for this phenomenon, and how might it generalize to other domains?

3. The paper mentions that the model is purely reactive with no explicit memory. How might incorporating memory mechanisms (e.g., as in Oh et al., 2016) affect the performance of the DFP approach, and what would be the trade-offs?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field of sensorimotor control. The method is simple yet effective, the experiments are well-designed and comprehensive, and the results are compelling. The paper's success in the Visual Doom AI Competition provides particularly strong validation of the approach. I recommend acceptance.
