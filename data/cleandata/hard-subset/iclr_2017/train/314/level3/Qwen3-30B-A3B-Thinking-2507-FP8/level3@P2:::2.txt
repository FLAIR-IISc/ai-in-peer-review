

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents an approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments that utilizes both high-dimensional sensory input and lower-dimensional measurement streams. The authors frame sensorimotor control as a supervised learning problem where the agent predicts future measurements based on current sensory input, measurements, and a goal. The method trains using raw experience without extraneous supervision, allowing the model to learn to act based on visual input in complex environments. The paper demonstrates that this approach outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods in the Visual Doom AI Competition, particularly on challenging tasks requiring dynamic goal pursuit. The work is well-motivated, with strong empirical results showing clear advantages over prior methods.

## 2. Decision

Accept.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a compelling empirical case for its approach, with the model winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition by outperforming the second-best submission by over 50%. The results are scientifically rigorous, with extensive experiments across four increasingly challenging scenarios in the Doom environment. The paper properly compares against relevant baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) and demonstrates both superior performance and strong generalization across environments and goals.

The key strength of this work is its clear empirical advantage over state-of-the-art methods, particularly in complex tasks where the use of vectorial measurements provides a richer supervisory signal than scalar rewards. The paper effectively demonstrates that this approach can learn without fixed goals at training time and generalize to dynamically changing goals at test time, which addresses an important challenge in reinforcement learning.

While the main technical contribution appears to be a distillation and integration of existing ideas rather than a novel algorithmic advance, the paper's strong empirical results and clear demonstration of the benefits of vectorial measurements in this specific context provide significant value to the field.

## 4. Additional Feedback

The paper would be strengthened by more explicit clarification of its specific contributions relative to existing work, particularly in the abstract and introduction. While the paper cites appropriate prior work (including Schaul et al.'s universal value function framework), it would benefit from a clearer statement of how this work specifically advances the field beyond previous approaches.

I would also appreciate seeing an analysis of failure modes and conditions where the model struggles to generalize to changing goals. The paper demonstrates strong success in many scenarios, but understanding the boundaries of the approach would provide deeper insights.

The on-policy training regime used in the paper raises potential concerns about catastrophic forgetting when goals from the distant past are not revisited. A brief discussion of how the experience memory mechanism mitigates this issue would strengthen the paper.

Finally, while the Doom environment is compelling, the paper would benefit from experiments demonstrating broader applicability to other domains. For instance, testing the approach on Atari games using ROM states as intrinsic variables could provide stronger evidence of the approach's general utility.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that the method is "essentially a special case of universal value function approaches." Could you clarify the specific differences between your approach and Schaul et al.'s framework that make your formulation novel and beneficial for this particular problem?

2. In the ablation study (Table 4), the performance advantage of predicting multiple measurements is significant even when evaluating by a single measurement. Could you provide more insight into why this is the case? Does this suggest that the model is learning useful representations beyond the specific measurement being predicted?

3. The paper notes that the model was trained without fixed goals, allowing it to generalize to new goals at test time. However, it doesn't discuss scenarios where the model might struggle with certain goal combinations. Could you share any examples where the model performs poorly with specific goal vectors, and what might be the underlying reasons for these failures?
