

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments by leveraging the cotemporal structure of sensory and measurement streams to provide rich supervisory signals, effectively reframing the problem as supervised learning rather than reinforcement learning. The authors propose a method called Direct Future Prediction (DFP) that trains agents to predict future measurements (such as health, ammunition, and frags in Doom) based on current sensory input and a goal vector, without requiring sparse scalar rewards. The model learns to select actions that maximize the predicted future measurements, enabling flexible goal specification during test time. The approach demonstrates compelling empirical results, winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition in previously unseen environments, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL methods by over 50% in that competition.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that simple supervised learning techniques can outperform sophisticated RL methods in complex 3D environments when provided with rich, temporally dense measurement streams. The experimental results are scientifically rigorous, with thorough comparisons against multiple strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across increasingly challenging scenarios, and the ablation studies convincingly support the importance of vectorial measurements and multi-temporal prediction.

The paper's key strengths include: (1) a clear and well-motivated problem formulation that addresses critical challenges in RL (sensorimotor control from raw input and flexible goal specification); (2) compelling empirical results showing DFP outperforms prior methods by substantial margins, particularly on complex tasks; (3) strong evidence of generalization across environments and goals; (4) clear demonstration of the approach's practical utility through competition success; and (5) careful attention to experimental design and implementation details.

The paper's positioning within the literature is appropriate, citing relevant work on supervised learning for control, vector-valued feedback, and goal specification. While the paper mentions the "3D" nature of Doom, it's worth noting that the game uses 2D sprites rendered in a pseudo-3D perspective, which is a minor but accurate point to clarify.

For additional improvements, I suggest: (1) clarifying the notation in equations (2) and (6) where "P" and "j" are used in potentially confusing ways; (2) including measurement units consistently in the results tables (e.g., specifying "frags" vs "health points"); (3) providing more detail on the implementation of the randomized goals regime to clarify how goal vectors are generated during training.

To further strengthen the paper, I would like to ask the authors to clarify: (1) How the performance of DFP would scale to environments with more complex goal specifications (beyond the linear combinations tested in the paper); (2) Whether the success of the method depends on the specific choice of measurements (e.g., whether using only health would be sufficient for all scenarios, or whether additional measurements are necessary for complex tasks); (3) Whether the approach could be extended to continuous action spaces, as suggested in the discussion, and if so, what modifications would be necessary.

The paper makes a strong case for the value of rich measurement streams as supervision for sensorimotor control, demonstrating that this simple approach can outperform sophisticated RL methods in challenging 3D environments. The experimental validation is thorough, the results are compelling, and the method has been successfully deployed in a competitive setting. Despite the limited diversity of environments tested (all Doom-based), the paper makes a clear contribution that advances the field of embodied AI. The method is reproducible, the experiments are well-designed, and the results provide a solid foundation for future work in this direction.
