## Review of "Sensorimotor Control via Direct Future Prediction"

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which eschews traditional reward-based reinforcement learning in favor of predicting future intrinsic measurements directly from sensory input. The core idea – leveraging the rich supervisory signal inherent in the temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams – is compelling and well-motivated. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results, notably winning the 2016 Visual Doom AI Competition, and convincingly show DFP outperforms or matches established methods like A3C, DQN, and a DSR attempt. The ablation study further strengthens the claim that the proposed model components contribute meaningfully to performance.

**Accept** – The paper presents a significant contribution to the field, demonstrating a successful alternative to reward-based RL in a complex environment. The strong empirical validation, including a competition win, and the clear presentation of the method justify publication.

The approach is well-motivated by the limitations of sparse reward signals in complex environments and is appropriately situated within the existing literature on intrinsic motivation, auxiliary tasks, and forward modeling. The authors adequately cite relevant work, though a more exhaustive review of related prediction-based control methods might be beneficial. The experimental setup is rigorous, and the results are presented clearly. The ablation study provides valuable insight into the importance of the proposed components. 

However, a few points could improve the paper. The description of Doom as “3D” is inaccurate; it’s a 2.5D environment presented with 3D effects. The use of “P” in equation (2) is misleading, as it represents a prediction, not a probability. The double use of “j” in equation (6) could cause confusion. Finally, the results tables would benefit from repeating units for each measurement to improve readability (e.g., including "%" for health or "frags" for frag count).

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors:

1.  The paper mentions the potential for extending the model with memory mechanisms. Could you elaborate on how you envision integrating such mechanisms (e.g., LSTMs) into the current architecture, and what challenges you anticipate?
2.  The ablation study demonstrates the importance of predicting multiple future time steps. Is there a diminishing return as the prediction horizon increases? Was any effort made to optimize the selection of temporal offsets (τ1…τn)?
3.  While the competition win is impressive, the environments used were specific to Doom. How do you anticipate the performance of DFP transferring to other, significantly different, simulated or real-world environments? What modifications, if any, would be necessary?
