

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper presents a bold and novel approach to sensorimotor control by reframing reinforcement learning as a supervised learning problem. The authors propose using a stream of vectorial measurements (rather than scalar rewards) as the supervisory signal for learning to act in complex environments. Their key insight is that the interlocked temporal structure of sensory input and measurement streams provides rich, temporally dense supervision that can stabilize and accelerate training. The approach, called Direct Future Prediction (DFP), trains a model to predict the effect of actions on future measurements, with the agent selecting actions that best match the desired goal. The paper demonstrates that this simple approach outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods in complex 3D environments (based on the Doom game), particularly in scenarios with richer measurement streams. Notably, DFP won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming a strong baseline by over 50% without additional supervision.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

This paper introduces a genuinely novel conceptual framework that challenges the traditional RL paradigm. The core idea of using vectorial measurements as the supervisory signal rather than scalar rewards is both bold and well-motivated, offering a significant shift in perspective on sensorimotor control. The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods in challenging environments, with the added benefit of supporting dynamically specified goals at test time without retraining. While the approach is limited to discrete action spaces and Doom-based environments, the paper's contribution is sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant acceptance.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's key strength is its conceptual novelty. The authors correctly identify a fundamental limitation in traditional RL—reliance on sparse scalar rewards—and propose a simple yet powerful alternative that leverages the natural structure of sensory and measurement streams. This is not just a minor modification to existing methods but a different way of framing the learning problem. The paper positions this idea well within the literature, referencing relevant work on supervised learning for control (Jordan & Rumelhart), Monte Carlo methods, and multi-objective decision-making, while clearly distinguishing their contribution.

The empirical validation is strong within the scope of their experiments. The results across four increasingly difficult scenarios demonstrate consistent advantages of DFP, with particularly impressive gains in the most complex environments (D3 and D4). The ablation study clearly shows the importance of vectorial feedback and multi-temporal prediction, supporting their core hypothesis. The Visual Doom AI Competition win provides compelling real-world validation that goes beyond standard benchmarking.

While the paper could benefit from more detailed theoretical analysis of why their approach works better than traditional RL (beyond the empirical results), the reviewer is willing to overlook this given the paper's strong conceptual contribution and empirical validation. The method is remarkably simple yet effective, and the paper successfully demonstrates that this simple approach can outperform more complex RL methods.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper further, consider:

1. **Theoretical Justification**: While the empirical results are strong, adding a brief theoretical analysis of why vectorial measurements provide better supervision than scalar rewards would strengthen the paper. This could be a short section discussing the information-theoretic advantages of the proposed approach.

2. **Broader Context**: The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of how this approach relates to other recent work on predictive state representations (e.g., Sutton et al., 2011) and successor representations (Kulkarni et al., 2016b), which also use prediction for control.

3. **Limitations and Future Work**: While the paper mentions limitations in the discussion, it would be helpful to explicitly state what environments or tasks the approach might struggle with, and how the limitations could be addressed in future work.

4. **Implementation Details**: Some implementation details (like the experience memory size and the choice of temporal offsets) could be better justified, as these choices could impact the results.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper mentions that the measurement stream is "the part of the observation that the agent will aim to predict." How would you handle cases where the agent needs to predict multiple types of measurements that are not clearly distinguishable from raw sensory input? 

2. In the ablation study (Table 4), the paper shows that predicting multiple measurements improves performance even when evaluated by only one measurement. Could you provide more insight into why this is the case? Is it because the measurements are correlated, or is there a more fundamental reason related to the learning signal?

3. The paper states that "natural supervision is provided by the cotemporal structure of the sensory and measurement streams." Could you elaborate on how this differs from the supervisory signal provided by the environment in traditional RL, and why it's more effective for learning?
