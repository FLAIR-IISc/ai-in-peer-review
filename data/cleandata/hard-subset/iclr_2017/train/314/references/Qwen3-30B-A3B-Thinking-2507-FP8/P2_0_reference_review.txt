

# Review of "Direct Future Prediction: A Simple Approach to Sensorimotor Control"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control that reframes reinforcement learning as a supervised learning problem. The authors argue that instead of relying on sparse scalar rewards, an agent can learn from the temporal structure between raw sensory input and a stream of meaningful measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count). Their "Direct Future Prediction" (DFP) approach trains a model to predict how actions will affect future measurements, allowing the agent to select actions that best achieve desired goals. The paper makes several key contributions: a theoretically grounded alternative to traditional RL, empirical validation showing superior performance over state-of-the-art RL methods on complex 3D environments, and demonstration of generalization across environments and goals without retraining. The approach was successfully deployed to win the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**. The paper makes a compelling contribution that addresses a meaningful limitation in current RL approaches, with strong empirical validation that directly supports its claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Question 1: Specific problem tackled**
The paper clearly identifies a critical limitation in current RL approaches: the difficulty of learning sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex environments with sparse rewards. The authors correctly note that while RL has achieved impressive results, it struggles with the "acquisition of general skills that can be flexibly deployed to accomplish a multitude of dynamically specified goals" in immersive 3D environments. Their solution—using the temporal structure between sensory input and measurements as a rich supervision signal—directly addresses this problem.

**Question 2: Approach motivation and literature placement**
The paper is exceptionally well-motivated and placed within the literature. It properly situates itself within the historical context of supervised learning for action (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992), acknowledges the dominance of TD learning (Sutton, 1988), and clearly identifies the gap where dense vectorial feedback is available. The connections to related work (Schaul et al., 2015; Konidaris et al., 2012) are appropriate and highlight how this work extends existing frameworks. The authors correctly note that while some approaches use prediction (e.g., Oh et al., 2015), their specific formulation using vectorial measurements for goal-directed behavior is novel.

**Question 3: Supporting claims**
The paper provides robust empirical validation that directly supports its claims. The experiments are well-designed and thorough:
- Comparison against three strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across four progressively challenging Doom scenarios
- Ablation studies showing the importance of vectorial measurements and multiple temporal offsets
- Generalization experiments across 100 randomly textured environments
- A real-world competition win (Visual Doom AI Competition) where DFP outperformed the second-best submission by >50%

The results are statistically significant (multiple independent runs) and the performance gains are substantial (e.g., DFP outperforms A3C by 6× in complex Battle scenarios). The paper properly acknowledges limitations (discrete action spaces, need for measurement stream) while demonstrating the approach's effectiveness where traditional RL struggles.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these improvements would enhance its impact:

1. **Clarify measurement stream definition**: The paper assumes measurements are available, but doesn't sufficiently explain how these are defined for new environments. A brief discussion of whether measurements are hand-crafted or can be automatically identified would strengthen the claim of generalizability.

2. **Address computational complexity**: The paper mentions training speed (millions of steps/day), but doesn't compare computational requirements with baselines. Given that DSR was much slower to train, it would be helpful to discuss the trade-off between training time and performance.

3. **Expand discussion of limitations**: While the paper acknowledges discrete actions as a limitation, it could more thoroughly discuss how the approach would need to be adapted for continuous control, which is common in robotics.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How would the approach handle environments where the "meaningful measurements" are not readily available or require significant processing to extract? Would the approach still work if the measurements had to be learned from the sensory stream?

2. In the competition, the authors won without using extra supervision (unlike the second-place submission that used ground-truth enemy presence). Could you elaborate on how the measurement stream provided sufficient information to learn the necessary representations without this additional supervision?

3. The paper demonstrates strong generalization across environments, but doesn't explore how the model would handle completely novel goal types not seen during training. How would the approach handle a goal that requires predicting measurements not included in the training measurements (e.g., a new metric like "time spent in safe zones")?

This paper presents a compelling alternative to traditional RL that addresses a real limitation in the field. The theoretical motivation is sound, the empirical results are robust, and the approach has demonstrated clear practical value. The paper makes a significant contribution that will likely inspire future work on alternative approaches to sensorimotor control in complex environments. I recommend acceptance.
