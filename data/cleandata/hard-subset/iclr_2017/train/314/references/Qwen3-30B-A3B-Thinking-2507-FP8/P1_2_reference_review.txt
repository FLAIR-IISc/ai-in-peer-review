

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control that reframes reinforcement learning as a supervised learning problem using vectorial measurements rather than scalar rewards. The authors propose Direct Future Prediction (DFP), where an agent learns to predict future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) given current sensory input, measurements, and a goal. The agent then selects actions that maximize predicted future measurements. The paper claims this approach provides richer, temporally dense supervision compared to traditional RL, supports training without a fixed goal (enabling adaptation to dynamically specified goals at test time), and outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods on complex 3D tasks. The authors demonstrate significant improvements in the Visual Doom AI Competition, with their model winning the Full Deathmatch track by outperforming the second-best submission by over 50%.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance**. The key reasons for this decision are: (1) the paper addresses a significant limitation in current RL approaches for complex sensorimotor tasks with a well-motivated and theoretically grounded approach, and (2) the empirical results are robust, showing substantial improvements over strong baselines on challenging tasks, with thorough ablation studies supporting the core claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a compelling case for the importance of rich supervisory signals in sensorimotor learning. The theoretical foundation is strong, with careful connection to prior work on supervised learning for control (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992), Monte Carlo methods, and predictive state representations. The motivation for using vectorial measurements rather than scalar rewards is particularly well-justified by the limitations of current RL approaches in complex 3D environments.

The empirical results are exceptionally strong and well-executed. The approach demonstrates clear superiority on increasingly complex Doom scenarios, with the most significant improvements on tasks where multiple measurements are available (D3 and D4). The winning performance in the Visual Doom AI Competition (outperforming a strong baseline by over 50%) is particularly compelling evidence of the approach's effectiveness. The ablation studies (Table 4) effectively demonstrate the importance of vectorial feedback and multi-temporal predictions, supporting the paper's core claims.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, with careful consideration of related work in multi-objective decision-making, parameterized goals, and predictive representations. The comparison to state-of-the-art baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) is thorough and fair, with the authors acknowledging computational differences between methods (e.g., DSR training is much slower).

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- **Clarify goal representation**: The paper mentions the goal vector as a linear combination of future measurements, but provides limited concrete examples of how this is implemented in practice. Adding a specific example of how the goal vector would be defined for a particular task would strengthen the presentation.

- **Address measurement acquisition**: The paper claims "training on raw experience without extraneous supervision," but in the Doom environment, the measurements (health, ammo, frags) are provided by the game engine. This could be seen as "extraneous supervision" from the perspective of a truly autonomous agent. The paper should clarify whether the measurements are considered part of the environment's natural state (thus not "extraneous") or if they represent a form of external supervision that would be unavailable in a truly autonomous system.

- **Discuss memory limitations**: The paper mentions the approach is "purely reactive" with no explicit memory, but doesn't discuss how this limitation might affect performance on tasks requiring longer-term planning. A brief discussion of this limitation and potential future work to address it would strengthen the paper.

- **Specify competition metric**: The paper states the model "outperformed the second best submission by more than 50%," but doesn't clarify whether this is a 50% improvement in average frags, or some other metric. Clarifying this would improve the paper's scientific rigor.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In the Visual Doom AI Competition, the second-best submission (Lample & Chaplot, 2016) used "extra supervision from the game engine" (e.g., ground-truth knowledge of enemy presence). How would your approach perform if you were not allowed to use the game engine's internal measurements (e.g., if you had to infer health, ammo, and frags from visual input alone)?

2. The paper states the agent is trained with either a fixed goal or randomized goals. How does the performance change when the goal is a complex function of measurements (e.g., a non-linear combination) rather than a simple linear combination?

3. The paper mentions the approach supports "learning without a fixed goal" and "pursuing dynamically specified goals at test time." Can you provide specific examples of goals that the model successfully handles that would be difficult for traditional RL approaches (e.g., a goal that requires balancing multiple conflicting measurements)?

This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning for complex sensorimotor control. The approach is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and empirically validated with strong results. The work has clear potential to influence future research on learning from raw sensory input in complex environments.
