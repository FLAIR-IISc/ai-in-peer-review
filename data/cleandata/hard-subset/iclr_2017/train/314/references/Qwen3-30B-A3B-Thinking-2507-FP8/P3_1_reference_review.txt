

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control that reframes reinforcement learning as supervised learning. Instead of using a scalar reward signal, the authors propose using a stream of measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) and training a model to predict future measurements based on current sensory input, measurements, and a goal. The agent then selects actions that best achieve the desired future measurements. The key contributions are: (1) a simple but effective framework for sensorimotor control using direct future prediction; (2) strong empirical evidence demonstrating superior performance over state-of-the-art RL methods on complex 3D environments; (3) demonstration of generalization across environments and goals; and (4) a win in the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, where the approach outperformed the second-best submission by over 50%.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**. The paper makes a strong empirical contribution with compelling results on a challenging benchmark, and the approach is conceptually simple and well-motivated. The win in the Visual Doom AI Competition provides particularly strong validation of the approach's practical effectiveness.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses two important challenges in RL: sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments and acquisition of general skills for dynamically specified goals. The motivation for using dense temporal supervision from measurement streams rather than sparse rewards is well-argued, particularly for immersive environments where organisms receive rich sensory feedback.

The empirical results are impressive and well-documented. Across four increasingly complex Doom scenarios, the proposed Direct Future Prediction (DFP) approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art RL methods (DQN, A3C, DSR), particularly on complex tasks. In the most challenging scenarios (D3 and D4), DFP outperforms A3C by factors of 6 and 2.5, respectively. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the importance of vectorial measurements (vs. scalar rewards) and multiple temporal offsets for prediction.

The generalization results are particularly noteworthy: models trained with the DFP approach generalize well across different environments (as shown in Table 2) and can adapt to new goals not specified during training (Table 3). The fact that the approach won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, beating a strong baseline by over 50% without additional supervision, provides compelling real-world validation.

While the paper lacks theoretical analysis, the empirical results are robust and well-documented with multiple independent training runs, detailed ablation studies, and a major competition win. The approach's simplicity and effectiveness make it a valuable contribution to the field.

## 4. Additional Feedback

**1. Theoretical Foundation**: The paper would benefit from a more thorough theoretical discussion of why direct future prediction works better than traditional RL. While the empirical results are strong, a theoretical analysis would strengthen the contribution and help guide future work.

**2. Comparison to Model-Based RL**: The paper doesn't compare to recent model-based RL approaches that predict future states (e.g., Oh et al., 2015; Finn et al., 2016), which is conceptually related. The authors should clarify how their approach differs from these methods and why they outperform them.

**3. Measurement Selection**: The paper assumes measurements are provided (e.g., health, ammo), but doesn't discuss how these measurements would be chosen in domains where natural measurements aren't available. The authors should address whether measurements can be automatically learned from sensory input.

**4. Computational Efficiency**: The paper mentions training speed but doesn't compare the computational requirements of DFP to other methods. How does the computational cost of DFP compare to DQN or A3C?

**5. Goal Specification**: The paper assumes goals can be expressed as linear combinations of measurements, but doesn't discuss how to handle more complex goal specifications. The authors should explore extensions to more complex goal spaces.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How do you envision the measurement stream being defined in domains where natural measurements (like health, ammo, etc.) aren't available? Would the approach require manual engineering of measurements, or could they be learned from the environment?

2. The paper compares to DQN, A3C, and DSR, but doesn't compare to recent model-based RL approaches (e.g., Oh et al., 2015; Finn et al., 2016). How does your approach relate to these methods, and why do you think your method outperforms them?

3. The Visual Doom AI Competition had specific constraints (e.g., time limits, communication protocols). How did your approach handle these constraints, and would the approach require adaptation for other competition formats?

4. In Table 3, the approach generalizes well to new goals, but the paper doesn't discuss how well it handles goals that aren't linear combinations of measurements. How would you extend the approach to handle more complex goal specifications?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating that a simple supervised learning approach can outperform sophisticated RL methods in complex 3D environments. The strong empirical results and competition win provide compelling evidence for the approach's effectiveness, and the simplicity of the method makes it accessible for future work. With the suggested clarifications, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
