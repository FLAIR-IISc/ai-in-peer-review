

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper proposes an innovative approach to sensorimotor control that reframes reinforcement learning as supervised learning by predicting future measurements rather than relying on scalar rewards. The authors argue that the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides rich supervision for learning to act in complex environments. They demonstrate that their "direct future prediction" (DFP) method outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL approaches on challenging 3D Doom environments, particularly when multiple measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frags) are used instead of a single scalar reward. The approach also enables training without fixed goals, allowing adaptation to new goals at test time.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper contains multiple technical inconsistencies and formatting errors that undermine its credibility and scientific rigor, despite its interesting empirical results.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper has several critical issues that prevent me from recommending acceptance:

First, the mathematical notation is inconsistent and poorly formatted throughout. Equation (2) uses "pat = F(ot, a,g;θ)" but later uses "g>F(ot, a,g;θ)" without defining the "g>" operation. Equation (6) contains a typesetting error: "Ai(j) = Ai(j)− 1 w w∑ k=1 Ak(j)" should be "Ai(j) = Ai(j)− (1/w)∑_{k=1}^w Ak(j)" with proper formatting. Equation (7) uses inconsistent notation like "pa1" instead of "p_{a_1}". These errors suggest a lack of attention to detail that raises concerns about the paper's technical rigor.

Second, the references are inconsistently formatted. The paper cites "Jordan & Rumelhart (1992)" but the reference list shows "Jordan and Rumelhart.,? \Q2006\E" with incorrect year and formatting. Many references use "&" while others use "and", and the citation style is inconsistent (e.g., "Sutton & Barto, 2017" vs. "Sutton and Barto, 2017"). This suggests poor proofreading and undermines the paper's scholarly credibility.

Third, the paper claims "the agent has access to eight sub-actions... resulting in possible actions" but fails to specify how many possible actions this actually is (2^8 = 256, but this is not stated). This is a critical omission for understanding the experimental setup.

Fourth, the paper states that "the model was trained using a variant of the D4-tx-L regime" but does not clearly define what "D4-tx-L" means or how it differs from the D4-tx regime described earlier. This lack of clarity in terminology is problematic.

The paper's core contribution is interesting and the results are promising, but these fundamental technical inconsistencies prevent me from being confident in the paper's scientific rigor. The minor errors are not just cosmetic—they indicate a lack of attention to detail that casts doubt on the accuracy of the methods and results.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Carefully proofread all mathematical notation and equations to ensure consistent formatting and proper typesetting.
2. Standardize all references to use the same citation style (e.g., always use "&" instead of "and" in author lists).
3. Clarify the number of possible actions in the Doom environment (256, not just "possible actions").
4. Define all experimental acronyms (like D4-tx-L) the first time they appear.
5. Add a brief explanation of the "g>" operation in Equation (3) to clarify what is being computed.

## Questions for Authors
1. What is the exact meaning of "g>" in Equation (3)? Is this a dot product or some other operation?
2. Can you clarify what "D4-tx-L" specifically refers to in the Visual Doom AI Competition results? How does it differ from the D4-tx regime?
3. The paper claims to use "the same visual input that is shown to human players in the game" but does not specify the resolution or preprocessing applied to the visual input. What is the exact input size to the network?
4. The paper mentions "no human gameplay is used, the model trains on raw experience" but does not clarify whether the agent uses any form of reward shaping during training. If not, how did the agent learn to avoid poison vials in the Navigation scenario?

These clarifications would significantly strengthen the paper and help establish the scientific rigor of the approach.
