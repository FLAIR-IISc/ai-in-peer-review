- Proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): replace scalar rewards with measurement streams and train a model to predict future measurements to choose actions.
- Argues DFP reduces sensorimotor control to supervised learning using temporally rich sensory+measurement structure.
- Shows DFP outperforms deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) in complex 3D Doom scenarios, especially with richer measurement streams.
- Demonstrates strong generalization across environments and goals.
- Won the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch, outperforming the second-best entry by >50%.
- Decision: Accept — significant contribution showing supervised approach can surpass RL for sensorimotor control.
- Acceptance reasons: well-motivated solution to sparse rewards and rigorous, consistent empirical gains over strong baselines.
- Key insight: measurement streams provide denser supervision than scalar rewards, challenging the necessity of RL in these tasks.
- Empirical evaluation: four increasing-complexity scenarios, large improvements (e.g., 6x in D3, 2.5x in D4) and thorough ablation studies.
- Ablation results: vectorial feedback and multi-horizon (multiple temporal offsets) prediction are critical to success.
- Competition win provides independent validation and robustness even versus methods using extra engine supervision.
- Request to clarify theoretical distinction from prior future-prediction work, emphasizing novelty of parameterized goals and vectorial measurements on raw input.
- Suggest expanding discussion of limitations (scaling to very high-dimensional or task-irrelevant measurement streams).
- Suggest providing more intuition or theoretical analysis for why vectorial measurements improve learning and stability.
- Questions for authors: explain specific mechanisms by which vectorial measurements stabilize/accelerate training.
- Question: how would DFP perform if baselines had the same extra game-engine supervision?
- Question: how does performance degrade as test-time goals deviate from training goal distribution and is there a degradation limit?
