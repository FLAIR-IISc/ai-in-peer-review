

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control by reframing it as a supervised learning problem rather than relying on traditional reward-based reinforcement learning. The key insight is that the temporal structure of sensory input and measurement streams provides rich, temporally dense supervision for learning. Instead of optimizing for a scalar reward, the agent predicts future measurements based on current sensory input, measurements, and a goal vector. This "direct future prediction" (DFP) approach enables learning from raw experience without extraneous supervision, supports flexible goal specification at test time, and leverages multidimensional measurements rather than sparse scalar rewards. The authors demonstrate significant performance improvements over state-of-the-art deep RL methods in complex 3D environments, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition by over 50% against the second-place submission.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper presents a well-motivated, technically sound approach that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of sensorimotor control, with strong empirical evidence supporting its claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper effectively addresses a meaningful problem: the limitations of traditional RL in complex 3D environments with sparse rewards. The approach is well-placed in the literature, with careful discussion of related work on supervised learning for control (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992), Monte Carlo methods (Sutton, 1995), and goal-conditioned learning (Schaul et al., 2015). The paper provides strong empirical validation across four increasingly challenging scenarios in the Doom environment, with consistent performance improvements over multiple baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR). The ablation studies (Table 4) provide compelling evidence for the importance of vectorial measurements rather than scalar rewards. The real-world validation through winning the Visual Doom AI Competition further strengthens the claims, particularly since the approach outperformed a more complex model that used additional supervision.

The technical execution is rigorous: the model is clearly defined (Section 3), the experimental setup is well-described (Section 4.1), and the results are presented with appropriate statistical reporting (three independent runs, large testing sets). The generalization experiments (Tables 2-3) demonstrate the approach's flexibility across environments and goals, addressing a key challenge in RL.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper would benefit from more explicit theoretical grounding. While the connection to Monte Carlo methods is mentioned, a more formal analysis of why predicting future measurements provides better supervision than scalar rewards would strengthen the contribution.
- The network architecture (Figure 1) is described but could be more clearly presented with specific dimensions and layer details to improve reproducibility.
- The "goal-agnostic training" results (Table 3) are compelling but could be more thoroughly analyzed. A deeper exploration of how different training regimes affect generalization performance would add value.
- The paper mentions the model's "reactive" nature (Section 5) as a limitation, but doesn't clearly discuss how this might impact performance on tasks requiring longer-term planning. A more explicit discussion of this limitation and potential extensions would be helpful.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. Could you provide a more formal theoretical justification for why predicting future measurements (rather than just future rewards) leads to better learning? Why does the temporal structure of the measurement stream provide a superior supervisory signal compared to scalar rewards?

2. In the ablation study (Table 4), the authors show that predicting multiple measurements improves performance even when evaluated on only one measurement. Could you elaborate on why this is the case? Is it due to the correlation between measurements, or is there another mechanism at play?

3. The paper states that "the measurement stream has lower dimensionality" than sensory input. Could you provide specific dimensionality numbers for the experiments (e.g., number of measurements vs. pixels in the input) to help readers understand the scale of the problem?

4. The authors won the Visual Doom AI Competition. Could you provide more details on the training setup (e.g., total simulation steps, training duration) that led to this success, to help readers understand the computational requirements of the approach?
