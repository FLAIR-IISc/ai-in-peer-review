

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control that reframes learning to act as a supervised learning problem rather than a reinforcement learning problem. The authors argue that traditional RL's reliance on sparse scalar rewards is limiting, particularly for complex 3D environments. Instead, they propose using a stream of measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) that provide rich, temporally dense supervision. The key insight is that the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides a natural supervisory signal for predicting the effects of actions on future measurements. The authors train a model to predict future measurements given current sensory input, measurements, and a goal, then select actions that best match the desired future measurements. The approach, called Direct Future Prediction (DFP), demonstrates strong performance across multiple Doom scenarios, outperforms state-of-the-art RL methods (DQN, A3C, DSR), and won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition with a 50% margin over the second-best submission. The paper also demonstrates that the approach generalizes across environments and goals without requiring goal-specific training.

## 2. Decision

Accept

Key reasons: 
1. The paper presents a well-motivated, novel approach that effectively addresses key limitations of traditional RL (sparse rewards, fixed goals) in complex 3D environments.
2. The experimental results are comprehensive, scientifically rigorous, and convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the approach over state-of-the-art methods, particularly on complex tasks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's contribution is well-placed in the literature. It builds on Jordan & Rumelhart's (1992) early observation that supervised learning can be advantageous when temporally dense feedback is available, and extends this insight to modern sensorimotor control problems. The authors effectively situate their work within the broader RL literature, discussing connections to Monte Carlo methods, multi-objective decision-making, and parameterized goals. The theoretical motivation is sound and addresses a genuine limitation of current RL approaches.

The empirical results are particularly compelling. The paper evaluates the approach across four increasingly difficult scenarios in the Doom environment, demonstrating consistent performance gains over state-of-the-art baselines. The win in the Visual Doom AI Competition is especially convincing, as it demonstrates real-world effectiveness in a challenging, competitive setting without additional supervision. The ablation study (Table 4) provides strong evidence that vectorial measurements provide a more effective training signal than scalar rewards, and that predicting at multiple temporal offsets is beneficial.

The paper also makes a strong case for the approach's ability to handle dynamically specified goals at test time without requiring goal-specific training, which is a significant practical advantage. The generalization experiments across different environments (Table 2) further demonstrate the robustness of the approach.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong overall, I recommend the following improvements to enhance clarity and impact:

1. **Clarify the relationship to predictive state representations (PSRs)**: The paper mentions connections to predictive representations (Sutton et al., 2011) but doesn't clearly explain how the proposed approach differs from and advances this literature. A brief comparison would strengthen the theoretical positioning.

2. **Expand discussion of limitations**: The paper mentions the model is purely reactive with no explicit memory, but doesn't discuss how this limitation might affect performance in more complex tasks requiring long-term planning. A short discussion of this limitation and potential future work to address it would make the paper more balanced.

3. **Streamline architecture description**: The architecture details in Section 3.2 are quite technical. Consider moving some of the more implementation-specific details to an appendix to improve readability of the main text.

4. **Provide more insight on measurement selection**: The paper assumes measurements are provided, but doesn't discuss how to select appropriate measurements in new environments. A brief discussion of how to identify meaningful measurements for a given task would be valuable for practitioners.

## 5. Questions for Authors

To better understand the approach and its implications, I would like to ask the following questions:

1. In the Visual Doom AI Competition, the Full Deathmatch track used environments not seen during training. How do the authors believe their approach generalizes to environments where the relationship between sensory input and measurements is significantly different from the training environments?

2. The paper demonstrates that vectorial measurements outperform scalar rewards, but how does the approach handle environments where the appropriate measurements are not readily available or require complex computation from raw sensory input? Would the authors expect to see similar performance gains if measurements had to be estimated from raw input?

3. The paper mentions that the approach won the competition without using additional supervision (unlike the second-place submission which used ground-truth enemy presence information). How much of the performance gain is due to the measurement-based approach versus the specific implementation details of the DFP model?

4. The authors note that the model is purely reactive with no explicit memory. How might the approach be extended to incorporate memory mechanisms (e.g., LSTM) to handle tasks requiring longer-term planning, and what would be the expected performance impact?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating that sensorimotor control can be effectively framed as a supervised learning problem using rich measurement streams. The results are compelling, the approach is novel, and the work has clear practical implications for complex 3D environments. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to ICLR.
