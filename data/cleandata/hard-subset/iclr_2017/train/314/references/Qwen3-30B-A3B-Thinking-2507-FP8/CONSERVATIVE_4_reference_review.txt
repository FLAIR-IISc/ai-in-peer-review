

# Review

## 1. Summary of Paper Claims
The paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control in reinforcement learning called "direct future prediction" (DFP). The core idea is to replace the traditional scalar reward signal with a vector of measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) and train a model to predict future measurements based on current observations, actions, and goals. The authors claim this approach provides "rich and temporally dense supervision" that stabilizes and accelerates training compared to traditional RL. They demonstrate that DFP outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods (DQN, A3C, DSR) on four increasingly complex Doom-based tasks, particularly when multiple measurements are available. The authors further claim that DFP generalizes across environments and can adapt to new goals without retraining.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper presents an interesting variation of existing approaches but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of significant advantages over established methods. The experimental validation is limited, and the claims of "no extraneous supervision" and "outperforms state-of-the-art" are not convincingly supported.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution is not sufficiently novel or rigorously validated for ICLR. The core idea of using multiple measurements as a supervisory signal is a minor variation of existing work (e.g., Schaul et al., 2015) with no strong theoretical justification for why vectorial feedback should outperform scalar rewards in the settings studied. The authors cite Jordan & Rumelhart (1992) as motivation, but this work predates modern deep RL and doesn't provide a strong theoretical basis for their specific approach.

The experimental validation has several critical weaknesses:
- The comparison with DSR is fundamentally unfair, as DSR was only trained for 10 days (roughly 1 million steps) while other methods were trained for 50 million steps. This is a major methodological flaw that undermines the claimed performance advantage.
- The paper doesn't sufficiently address how the measurements (health, ammo, frags) are obtained without additional supervision from the game engine. The authors claim "no human gameplay is used" and "the model trains on raw experience," but the measurements are clearly provided by the game engine, which constitutes additional supervision. This contradicts their claim of "no extraneous supervision."
- The Doom environment is not a standard benchmark for RL, and the paper doesn't compare to more recent state-of-the-art methods beyond 2016 (the year of the competition).
- The paper claims "the presented approach won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition," but doesn't provide sufficient detail about the competition setup or how the measurements were obtained during competition, making it impossible to assess whether the claimed "no extraneous supervision" holds.

## 4. Additional Feedback and Questions
The paper would benefit from addressing these issues to strengthen its claims:

1. Provide a more rigorous theoretical justification for why vectorial measurements should outperform scalar rewards in the specific environments studied. The current motivation is insufficient.

2. Re-run experiments with fair training time comparisons for all methods. The current comparison with DSR is unconvincing due to the significant difference in training time.

3. Clarify the "no extraneous supervision" claim. Explain precisely how the measurements (health, ammo, frags) are obtained without additional game-engine supervision, and how this differs from the "reward shaping" used by the Lample & Chaplot (2016) baseline.

4. Compare with more recent state-of-the-art methods beyond 2016, as the paper's claims are based on results from a 2016 competition.

5. Provide more detail about the hyperparameters used for all baselines, not just the ones the authors chose to optimize. The paper states they "report the best results we were able to obtain" but doesn't provide the complete hyperparameter search space.

6. Why does the paper use a fixed goal during training for the "fixed goal" regime but random goals for the other regimes? What is the theoretical basis for this choice?

7. In the ablation study (Table 4), the authors claim "predicting multiple measurements significantly improves performance," but the metric used for evaluation (frags) is only one of the three measurements. Why does predicting only frags (the scalar reward) perform worse than predicting all three measurements, even when evaluating only on frags? This needs clearer explanation.

The paper presents an interesting idea that could be valuable with more rigorous validation, but as currently presented, it lacks the methodological rigor and comprehensive validation required for publication at ICLR. The claims of significant performance improvements over established methods are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided.
