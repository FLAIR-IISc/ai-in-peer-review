

# Review of "Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control that frames it as a supervised learning problem rather than a reinforcement learning problem. The authors argue that the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides a rich supervisory signal, enabling an agent to predict the effects of actions on future measurements. This approach departs from traditional RL's scalar reward formalization and instead uses a vector of measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frag count) as the target for prediction. The key contributions include: (1) a formalization of sensorimotor control as direct future prediction, (2) a model architecture that predicts future measurements given current sensory input, measurements, and a goal, and (3) empirical demonstration that this approach outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods in complex 3D environments, particularly when multiple measurements are used.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes an interesting conceptual contribution with potential value, but the empirical validation is insufficient to support the strong claims made, particularly regarding the superiority of the approach over state-of-the-art methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core idea is conceptually sound and well-motivated, drawing appropriately from historical context (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992) and recent work on predictive representations. The distinction between sensory input and measurements is well-articulated, and the claim that multidimensional measurements provide richer supervision than scalar rewards is plausible.

However, the empirical evidence falls short of supporting the paper's claims for several reasons:

1. **Inadequate comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper compares to DQN, A3C, and DSR, but DSR is a weaker baseline (only trained on the "Basic" scenario due to slow training) and the comparison lacks sufficient detail about hyperparameter tuning. The paper states they "report the best results we were able to obtain" for baselines, but doesn't provide evidence of thorough hyperparameter search or ablation studies to confirm these are optimal.

2. **Lack of statistical significance**: The results are presented as averages over three independent runs, but the paper doesn't provide statistical significance tests for the observed performance differences. This is particularly concerning given the claim of "more than 50% improvement" in the competition.

3. **Insufficient analysis of failure modes**: The paper demonstrates success on the Doom environments but doesn't analyze cases where the approach fails or compare against more recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., PPO, SAC, or more recent Doom-specific approaches).

4. **Overstated claims about generalization**: The generalization results across environments (Table 2) show promising results, but the paper claims "the model can effectively pursue new goals not specified during training" without providing sufficient evidence. The goal-agnostic training results (Table 3) are limited to one scenario and don't demonstrate robustness across multiple goal types.

5. **Theoretical grounding is weak**: While the paper references Monte Carlo methods and predictive representations, it doesn't provide a theoretical analysis of why their approach would outperform RL methods in the settings they consider.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen this paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, including recent algorithms like PPO, SAC, and more recent Doom-specific approaches. Provide a clear justification for why these weren't included.

2. Perform statistical significance tests for all performance comparisons (e.g., t-tests with p-values) to demonstrate that the observed differences are meaningful.

3. Include ablation studies that isolate the contribution of each key component (vectorial measurements, multiple temporal offsets, etc.) with proper statistical analysis.

4. Provide a more detailed analysis of failure modes and limitations of the approach, particularly in scenarios where it underperforms.

5. Include a theoretical analysis of the relationship between the prediction framework and traditional RL, explaining why their approach should outperform RL methods in the specific settings they consider.

6. Clarify the relationship between their approach and the "Universal Value Function Approximators" (Schaul et al., 2015) framework, which appears closely related but isn't properly contrasted.

## Questions for Authors

1. How did you determine that the DSR baseline was trained for 10 days (1 million steps) was sufficient for fair comparison, given that the paper states "this time was sufficient to significantly exceed the number of training steps reported in the experiments of Kulkarni et al. (2016b)" but not sufficient to "approach the number of steps afforded by the other approaches"?

2. The paper claims "the presented approach outperformed the second best submission, which employed a substantially more complex model and additional supervision during training, by more than 50%." However, the paper doesn't provide the exact performance metrics for the second-place submission (Lample & Chaplot, 2016). Could you provide these numbers to better contextualize the claimed improvement?

3. In Table 3, the model trained with a fixed goal (a) performs nearly as well as models trained with randomized goals (b,c) on the main task. What evidence do you have that the model trained with randomized goals generalizes better to new goals than the fixed-goal model, given that the fixed-goal model appears to perform similarly on the main task?

4. The paper states "the model trains on raw experience, by interacting with the environment without extraneous supervision." However, the ViZDoom platform provides access to internal game state information (e.g., health, ammo), which could be considered "extraneous supervision." How does this differ from the supervision used in imitation learning or model-based RL?
