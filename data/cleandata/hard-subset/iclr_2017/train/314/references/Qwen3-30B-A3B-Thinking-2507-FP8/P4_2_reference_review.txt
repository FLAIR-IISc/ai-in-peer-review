

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel framework for sensorimotor control that reframes reinforcement learning as a supervised learning problem. Instead of relying on sparse scalar rewards, the authors introduce a stream of measurements (lower-dimensional state information) alongside raw sensory input. Their key insight is that the temporal structure between sensory input and measurements provides a rich supervisory signal for learning. The approach, called Direct Future Prediction (DFP), trains a model to predict future measurements based on current sensory input, measurements, and a goal vector. The paper demonstrates that this framework outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods in complex 3D environments, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition. The authors provide compelling evidence that vectorial measurements provide a more effective learning signal than scalar rewards, and that their approach supports flexible goal specification and generalization across environments.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution with strong empirical validation in a challenging domain.

Key reasons:
1. The approach is well-motivated and clearly situated within the literature, addressing key challenges in RL with a novel formulation grounded in historical work.
2. The empirical results are scientifically rigorous and provide strong evidence for the claims, including winning a major competition.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a compelling case for its approach. It correctly identifies the limitations of traditional RL in complex 3D environments (sparse rewards, difficulty with raw sensory input) and provides a well-justified alternative. The literature review is thorough, situating the work in relation to historical supervised learning approaches (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992), Monte Carlo methods, vector-valued feedback, and parameterized goals (Schaul et al., 2015).

The experimental validation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors evaluate on four increasingly difficult scenarios (D1-D4) using the ViZDoom platform, comparing against three strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR). The results demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines in the more complex scenarios (D3 and D4), with DFP outperforming A3C by factors of 6 and 2.5 respectively. The ablation study (Table 4) directly supports the claim that vectorial measurements provide a significant advantage over scalar rewards. The competition results provide strong real-world validation, with the model winning the Full Deathmatch track against a strong baseline (Lample & Chaplot, 2016) that used additional supervision.

The paper's claims are well-supported by the evidence. The experiments demonstrate not only superior performance but also important properties like generalization across environments (Table 2) and the ability to handle dynamically specified goals (Table 3), which are significant contributions to the field.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- Consider adding statistical significance tests for the performance differences between DFP and baselines to strengthen empirical claims.
- Clarify the training time comparison with DSR (which was trained for only 1 million steps vs. 50 million for others), and discuss how results might change with comparable training time.
- The paper could provide more insight into why vectorial measurements work better than scalar rewards in these environments. A deeper analysis of learning dynamics would strengthen the contribution.
- The distinction between sensory input and measurements is noted as "somewhat artificial" but could be better justified with examples from the experiments showing how this distinction is implemented in practice.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- Can you provide more details about the statistical significance of the performance differences between DFP and the baselines (particularly in the more complex scenarios D3 and D4)?
- The paper mentions that DSR was trained for only 1 million steps (vs. 50 million for others), which may make the comparison unfair. How would the results change if DSR was trained for the same number of steps as the other methods?
- In Table 4, predicting only frags (a scalar reward) performs worse than predicting multiple measurements. Given the same network architecture, could you elaborate on why predicting multiple measurements is beneficial beyond just having more information?
- The paper claims the approach "won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, which took place in previously unseen environments." Could you provide more details about the specific challenges of these unseen environments and how the model's generalization capabilities specifically addressed them?
