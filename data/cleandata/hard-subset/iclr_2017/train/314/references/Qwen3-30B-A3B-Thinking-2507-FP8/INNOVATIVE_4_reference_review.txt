

# Review

## Summary of Contribution

This paper presents a bold and novel approach to sensorimotor control in complex 3D environments by reframing the problem as supervised learning rather than reinforcement learning. The authors propose that the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides rich supervision for learning to act, eliminating the need for sparse scalar rewards. Instead of maximizing a reward signal, the agent learns to predict future measurements given current sensory input, measurements, and a goal. This approach, called Direct Future Prediction (DFP), has two key advantages: it provides temporally dense supervision that stabilizes and accelerates training, and it supports training without fixed goals while enabling flexible deployment of learned skills at test time. The paper demonstrates that DFP outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods in complex 3D environments, particularly on challenging tasks requiring navigation, object recognition, and interaction with dynamic adversaries. Notably, their model won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition by outperforming the second-best submission by over 50%.

## Decision

Accept. The paper introduces a fundamentally new conceptual framework for sensorimotor control that challenges the dominant reinforcement learning paradigm. Its novelty and boldness in rethinking the problem as supervised learning with vectorial measurements rather than scalar rewards, combined with strong empirical results showing significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods, make it a compelling contribution.

## Supporting Arguments

The paper's main strength is its conceptual novelty. The authors correctly identify a key limitation of current RL approaches: the assumption of sparse scalar rewards is inadequate for environments where rich, temporally dense feedback is naturally available. By reframing sensorimotor control as supervised learning using the temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams, they introduce a new direction for research that could have broad implications beyond the specific domain they evaluate.

The empirical results are compelling and well-supported. The authors demonstrate that DFP significantly outperforms DQN, A3C, and DSR on increasingly complex Doom scenarios, with performance gaps widening on more challenging tasks. The ablation studies provide strong evidence for their core claims about the benefits of vectorial measurements versus scalar rewards and multiple temporal offsets. The fact that their simple approach won a major competition against more complex models is particularly impressive.

The paper is well-placed in the literature. The authors provide a thoughtful review of related work, situating their approach in relation to supervised learning, Monte Carlo methods, and previous work on vector-valued feedback and parameterized goals. They correctly identify the historical context of the supervised learning vs. reinforcement learning debate and make a strong case for revisiting supervised learning when rich feedback is available.

While the paper doesn't provide a full theoretical analysis of why their approach works better than RL methods, the reviewer is willing to overlook this for the sake of the paper's significant conceptual novelty. The empirical evidence is robust enough to support their main claims.

## Additional Feedback

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach. For instance, how well would DFP perform in environments where the relationship between measurements and future states is highly nonlinear or where measurements are not readily available?

2. The paper mentions that "the model trains on raw experience, by interacting with the environment without extraneous supervision," but it's worth clarifying what exactly constitutes "extraneous supervision" in this context. The measurements used as supervision are part of the environment's natural feedback, but they are still a form of structured supervision that might not be available in all real-world settings.

3. The connection between the proposed approach and recent work on predictive state representations (PSRs) could be strengthened. PSRs also leverage the temporal structure of observations to predict future states, and there's potential for synergy between these ideas.

4. The paper claims "the agent can be trained to predict the effect of different actions on future measurements" but doesn't explicitly discuss how the training process handles the exploration-exploitation trade-off in the context of prediction learning. This is a potential concern for the method's scalability.

## Clarifying Questions

1. The paper states that "the goal vector used at test time need not be identical to any goal seen during training." How sensitive is the performance to the similarity between training and test goals? What is the maximum "distance" between training and test goals for which the model still performs well?

2. In the ablation study (Table 4), the paper shows that predicting multiple measurements improves performance even when evaluated on a single measurement. Can the authors provide insight into why this is the case? Is it simply because the model learns richer representations of the environment, or is there something more specific about the relationships between measurements?

3. The paper mentions that "the measurement stream provides rich and temporally dense supervision that can stabilize and accelerate training." Could the authors provide more specific evidence for this claim beyond the performance results? For instance, could they show training curves demonstrating faster convergence or more stable learning?
