

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper proposes a fundamentally new approach to sensorimotor control that challenges the standard reinforcement learning paradigm. Instead of using a scalar reward signal, the authors frame learning as predicting future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) from current sensory input and actions. The core insight is that the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides rich, temporally dense supervision that can stabilize and accelerate learning. The approach treats sensorimotor control as a supervised learning problem where the agent predicts the effect of actions on future measurements, enabling flexible goal specification at test time without retraining. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on complex Doom environments, winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition by more than 50% over the second-best submission. This is a bold, conceptually simple, and potentially paradigm-shifting approach that challenges the dominance of reward-based RL.

## 2. Decision

Accept

Key reasons: (1) The paper introduces a genuinely novel conceptual framework for sensorimotor control that fundamentally departs from standard RL, and (2) The empirical results are compelling, showing significant performance gains over strong baselines on challenging tasks where traditional RL struggles.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

This paper excels in novelty, which I prioritize as a reviewer. The core idea—framing sensorimotor control as supervised learning of future measurements instead of optimizing a scalar reward—is a striking departure from the standard RL paradigm. While supervised learning for control has been considered before (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992), the authors' specific formulation using vectorial measurements and their demonstration of state-of-the-art performance in complex 3D environments makes this a significant contribution. The paper clearly articulates why this approach is superior to traditional RL in environments where dense, multidimensional feedback is available (e.g., physical systems or immersive games), addressing a key limitation of current RL methods.

The experimental results are impressive and directly support the paper's claims. The approach consistently outperforms strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across increasingly complex Doom scenarios, with particularly dramatic gains in the most challenging tasks (Battle and Battle 2). The 50% win margin in the Visual Doom AI Competition is especially compelling evidence of the approach's practical value. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate that vectorial measurements provide a significant advantage over scalar rewards, and the generalization experiments across environments and goals provide strong evidence of the model's flexibility.

The paper's main strength is its conceptual boldness—it doesn't just incrementally improve RL but proposes an alternative paradigm. While the methodological details are somewhat sparse (e.g., network architecture details in the appendix), this is acceptable given the paper's focus on the novel conceptual contribution rather than methodological refinement. The experimental validation is sufficient to support the core claims.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, consider:

- A clearer articulation of the theoretical justification for why this approach works better than RL in these environments. While the paper mentions the "rich and temporally dense supervision," a more formal discussion of the learning dynamics would strengthen the argument.

- A more detailed comparison of the computational efficiency between the proposed method and baselines. The paper notes that DSR was trained significantly slower, but it would be helpful to quantify the computational cost of the proposed method relative to the baselines.

- A discussion of limitations: How would this approach handle environments where the measurement stream is not well-defined or where the relationship between actions and measurements is highly non-stationary?

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims the approach "outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL models, particularly on complex tasks." Could you provide more specific analysis of why the approach excels on complex tasks but performs similarly to baselines on simpler tasks (D1)? Is this due to the richer feedback becoming more important as task complexity increases?

2. In Table 1, DFP achieves 97.5% health in D1, while DQN achieves 89%. However, in Figure 3, the curves show DQN and DFP converging to similar performance. Could you clarify this discrepancy? Is the 97.5% figure based on a different training configuration or evaluation metric?

3. The paper states that "the measurement stream provides rich and temporally dense supervision that can stabilize and accelerate training." Could you provide evidence of this claim beyond the final performance metrics? For example, do you observe faster convergence or more stable training curves?

4. In the discussion of the ablation study (Table 4), the authors note that "predicting multiple measurements significantly improves the performance of the learned model, even when it is evaluated by only one of those measurements." Could you elaborate on why this is the case? Is there a specific mechanism by which predicting multiple measurements helps with the primary task?
