

# Review

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims

The paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control in reinforcement learning that reframes the problem as supervised learning rather than traditional RL. Instead of using a scalar reward signal, the authors propose that the agent should predict future values of a stream of measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) given current sensory input, measurements, and a goal. The agent then selects actions that maximize the predicted future measurements. The authors claim this approach outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods on several Doom-based tasks, demonstrates better generalization across environments and goals, and won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition by over 50% compared to the second-best submission.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper presents an interesting empirical finding but fails to provide sufficient evidence to justify its claims, particularly against more recent and relevant baselines, and lacks theoretical justification for why this approach should work better than established RL methods.

## 3. Supporting arguments

The paper's main contribution is a re-framing of sensorimotor control as a supervised learning problem using measurement prediction instead of reward-based learning. While the idea has some conceptual merit, the paper falls short in several critical aspects:

First, the comparison to baselines is not sufficiently rigorous. The authors compare to DQN (2015), A3C (2016), and DSR (2016), but these are not the state-of-the-art methods for the tasks they evaluate. More recent, stronger baselines like PPO, SAC, or D4PG would be expected in a modern RL paper. The paper's claim of "outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL models" is undermined by the choice of baselines, which are not representative of the current field.

Second, the training time discrepancy between DFP (50 million steps) and DSR (only 1 million steps) makes the comparison unfair. The authors acknowledge DSR trains much slower (1 million steps/day vs. DFP's 10 million steps/day), but they don't account for this in their analysis. They report DSR results after only 10 days of training, which is insufficient for a fair comparison to DFP, which was trained for 50 million steps.

Third, the paper lacks theoretical justification for why this approach should work better than standard RL. The authors cite some relevant background (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992; Sutton, 1988) but don't provide a clear argument for why their approach should overcome the fundamental challenges that have made RL dominant in complex control tasks. The paper presents empirical results without explaining why this method should generalize beyond the specific Doom scenarios tested.

Fourth, the Visual Doom AI Competition win is presented as a major achievement, but the paper provides insufficient details about the competition setup, the second-best submission (Lample & Chaplot, 2016), and how the comparison was conducted. The claim of "more than 50% better" performance is vague without context about the scoring system and standard deviation of results.

## 4. Additional feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Compare against more recent and stronger baselines that represent the current state-of-the-art in deep RL for 3D environments.

2. Conduct a fairer comparison by ensuring all methods are trained for comparable numbers of steps or by reporting results at equivalent training time.

3. Provide a more thorough theoretical analysis of why their approach should work better than standard RL, particularly in complex, high-dimensional environments.

4. Provide more detailed information about the Visual Doom AI Competition results, including the exact scoring metric, the performance of the second-best submission, and the training details for all competing models.

5. Expand the ablation study to include more comprehensive analysis of why vectorial measurements outperform scalar rewards.

## 5. Questions for the authors

1. Why did you choose to compare against DQN, A3C, and DSR rather than more recent state-of-the-art methods like SAC, PPO, or D4PG, which have become standard for continuous control tasks?

2. How did you determine that 50 million training steps for DFP was sufficient for fair comparison, given that DSR was only trained for 1 million steps? Would you expect DSR to catch up with DFP if given more training time?

3. Can you provide more details about the second-best submission in the Visual Doom AI Competition (Lample & Chaplot, 2016)? Specifically, what was the architecture, training procedure, and how much additional supervision was used?

4. The paper claims that "the use of vectorial measurements instead of a scalar reward is beneficial" based on Table 4, but the improvement appears modest (e.g., 20% increase in frags in D3-tx). Can you explain why this seemingly small improvement translates to such large performance gains in the main experiments?

5. How does your approach handle the challenge of sparse rewards, which is one of the key advantages of RL over supervised learning approaches according to Jordan & Rumelhart (1992)?
