

# Review of "Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes an innovative approach to sensorimotor control that reframes reinforcement learning as a supervised learning problem using temporal prediction of measurements rather than scalar rewards. The authors argue that the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides a rich supervisory signal, allowing the agent to predict the effect of actions on future measurements. The key contributions include: (1) a novel framework for sensorimotor control using vectorial measurements instead of scalar rewards, (2) empirical evidence demonstrating superior performance on complex 3D environments compared to state-of-the-art deep RL methods, (3) demonstration of generalization across environments and goals, and (4) a simple yet effective architecture for learning from raw experience.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The primary reasons are: (1) the claimed novelty is not sufficiently differentiated from existing work on predictive state representations and model-based RL, and (2) the experimental evaluation contains significant limitations that undermine confidence in the claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core idea—using future predictions for control—has strong connections to established work. While the authors cite Schaul et al. (2015) and Sutton et al. (2011), they don't clearly articulate how their specific formulation differs from these prior approaches. The "expectation stream" and "action stream" architecture (Section 3.2) appears to be a minor architectural variation rather than a fundamental conceptual advance. The paper's claim that "the use of vectorial measurements instead of a scalar reward is beneficial" is somewhat trivial, as more information should generally improve prediction performance.

The experimental evaluation has several critical limitations that prevent confident assessment of the claims. Most notably, the comparison to baselines is not fair: DSR was only trained for 10 days (approximately 1 million steps), while DQN and A3C were trained for 50 million steps. This explains the significant performance gap reported in Table 1. The paper states that "the training procedure for DSR is much slower," but doesn't justify why this disparity was permitted. For A3C, the authors mention they "trained 20 models with random learning rates" but don't clarify if they selected the best-performing model or averaged results. Without knowing if optimal hyperparameters were used for all methods, the comparison is questionable.

The generalization experiments (Table 2) are limited to only 10 test environments, which is insufficient to draw strong conclusions about generalization capability. The ablation study (Table 4) shows that predicting multiple measurements helps, but doesn't isolate whether this is due to the vectorial nature of the measurements or simply having more prediction targets. The paper would benefit from an additional ablation where a single measurement is predicted with multiple temporal offsets.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Clearly differentiate their work from Schaul et al. (2015) and Sutton et al. (2011) by providing a detailed comparison of the specific architectural and algorithmic differences. What exactly makes their approach novel?

2. Conduct a fairer comparison with baselines by training all methods for the same number of steps or providing a detailed analysis of how training duration affects performance. Alternatively, report results normalized by training time.

3. Expand the generalization experiments to include more environments and provide statistical significance testing for the reported performance differences.

4. Provide a more detailed analysis of the ablation study, specifically addressing whether the vectorial measurement format provides a benefit beyond simply having more prediction targets.

5. Include more theoretical justification for why vectorial measurements would be beneficial in this specific setting, beyond the empirical evidence.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. How does your approach specifically differ from Schaul et al.'s (2015) universal value function approximator framework, which also uses goal-parameterized predictions?

2. Why did you choose a fixed set of temporal offsets (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32) rather than learning the optimal temporal horizon? How might the performance change with different temporal horizons?

3. In your ablation study (Table 4), could the improvement from using vectorial measurements be due to simply having more prediction targets rather than the specific nature of the measurements themselves? Could you run an additional ablation where you predict a single measurement with multiple temporal offsets?

4. Your results show that randomized goal training (regime b) outperforms fixed goal training (regime a) on the main task (Table 3). What is your interpretation of this phenomenon, and how does it relate to the theoretical claims about goal-agnostic training?

The paper presents an interesting idea with promising empirical results, but the novelty claims are not sufficiently supported, and the experimental evaluation contains significant limitations that prevent confident assessment. With the suggested revisions, particularly a clearer articulation of novelty and a fairer comparison with baselines, this work could make a valuable contribution to the field.
