

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

The paper introduces a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments by reframing the problem as supervised learning rather than reinforcement learning. The authors propose that instead of using the traditional scalar reward signal, agents should learn to predict the effect of actions on future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count), with the measurement stream providing a rich, temporally dense supervisory signal. This Direct Future Prediction (DFP) approach reduces sensorimotor control to a supervised learning problem while supporting learning from raw experience without extraneous data. The paper demonstrates that DFP outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL models on complex tasks in 3D environments, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition by more than 50% over the second-best submission. The paper further shows that the approach generalizes across environments, handles dynamically specified goals at test time, and benefits from vectorial measurements rather than scalar rewards.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution by providing a simple yet powerful alternative to traditional reinforcement learning for sensorimotor control in complex 3D environments. The experimental results are compelling, the approach is well-motivated, and the paper addresses a fundamental question about the nature of supervision in sensorimotor learning.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in addressing three key aspects of a strong ICLR submission:

First, the problem is well-defined and important. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of traditional RL approaches for complex sensorimotor control tasks, particularly the sparsity of scalar rewards in immersive environments. Their insight that the temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides rich supervision is both novel and well-motivated, with a thorough literature review that positions their work within the broader context of supervised vs. reinforcement learning (citing Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992; Sutton, 1988, etc.).

Second, the approach is scientifically rigorous and well-supported by empirical evidence. The paper presents four increasingly difficult scenarios in the Doom environment, with careful comparison to three strong baselines (DQN, A3C, and DSR). The results are compelling: DFP significantly outperforms baselines in complex scenarios (D3 and D4), with a 6x advantage over A3C in Battle (D3) and a 2.5x advantage in Battle 2 (D4). The ablation studies demonstrate the importance of vectorial measurements and multiple temporal offsets. The Visual Doom AI Competition results provide particularly convincing evidence, as DFP won against a strong competitor that used additional supervision from the game engine.

Third, the paper provides valuable theoretical insights about the nature of supervision in learning to act. The authors demonstrate that their approach can handle dynamically specified goals without retraining, a significant advantage over traditional RL methods. This is shown through the "goal-agnostic training" experiments, where models trained without knowing the goal in advance performed nearly as well as dedicated models trained for specific goals.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, I offer these suggestions to further enhance its quality:

- The paper mentions the model is "purely reactive" but doesn't thoroughly discuss the implications of this limitation. Adding a brief discussion of how memory mechanisms or hierarchical skills might be integrated with DFP would strengthen the paper's forward-looking perspective.

- The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of how the goal vector is used in the objective function. Specifically, the paper states that "any goal that the agent will pursue can be defined as maximization of a function u(f;g)," but doesn't clarify how the goal vector g is constructed for different test-time goals.

- The computational complexity of DFP compared to RL methods could be better addressed. While the paper mentions training speed, it would be valuable to discuss inference time and memory requirements, as these are critical for real-world applications.

## 5. Questions for Authors

To help me better understand the paper and its contribution:

1. In the goal-agnostic training experiments, how exactly are the goal vectors sampled from [0,1] and [-1,1]? Specifically, does the model see the same distribution of goals during training as it encounters during testing?

2. The paper mentions that DFP outperformed the second-best submission in the Visual Doom AI Competition by more than 50%. Could you provide more detail on how this performance was measured (e.g., average frags per game, win rate, etc.)?

3. The paper states that "predicting measurements at multiple future times is also beneficial." Could you provide more insight into why this is the case? Is it simply that more temporal information helps, or is there a specific advantage to the temporal structure of the predictions?

4. In the architecture section, the paper mentions "a small experience memory" but doesn't specify the size (M). How did the authors determine this hyperparameter, and how sensitive is the performance to its value?

5. The paper compares to DSR, which was trained on only 1 million steps due to slow training speed. Could the authors speculate on how much better DSR might perform with more training time, and whether this would close the gap with DFP?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The experimental results are compelling, the approach is well-motivated, and the paper addresses an important question about the nature of supervision in sensorimotor learning. The paper is well-suited for ICLR.
