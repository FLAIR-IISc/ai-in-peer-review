

# Review of "Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control"

## 1. Summary of Claims
The paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control by framing it as a supervised learning problem rather than reinforcement learning. The authors argue that the temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides rich supervision for learning to act in complex environments. Instead of using a scalar reward signal, their method trains an agent to predict future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) given current sensory input, measurements, and a goal. The approach claims to outperform state-of-the-art deep RL methods on challenging 3D environments, demonstrate generalization across environments and goals, and show that vectorial measurements provide significant advantages over scalar rewards. The paper presents compelling empirical results on the ViZDoom platform, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

## 2. Decision
Reject. Two key reasons: (1) The theoretical justification for why the approach outperforms RL is insufficiently developed, and (2) the comparison to some baselines (particularly DSR) is not entirely fair due to significant differences in training speed.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a strong empirical claim that their approach outperforms RL baselines, and the results in Table 1 and Figure 3 are indeed impressive. However, the paper lacks a clear explanation for *why* their method works better. The authors claim the "dense, multidimensional supervision" is the key advantage, but they don't provide theoretical arguments or detailed empirical analysis to support this beyond the results themselves. A reader could reasonably question whether the performance gain stems from the prediction framework itself, the vectorial measurements, or simply the fact that they're using supervised learning rather than RL. The paper doesn't isolate these factors.

Additionally, the comparison to DSR is problematic. The authors note that DSR was trained much slower (1 million steps per day vs. 50 million for others), which means they didn't have time to properly tune it. This makes the comparison less meaningful, as the DSR results might not represent the best possible performance of that method. For a fair comparison, they should have trained DSR for the same number of steps as the other methods. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't address how it might affect their conclusions.

The ablation study (Table 4) shows that vectorial measurements are better than scalar rewards, but the paper doesn't provide a clear explanation for why this is the case. Is it simply because they provide more information, or is there something more fundamental about the structure of the supervision signal? The paper doesn't adequately address this.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- **Theoretical Justification:** Provide a more detailed theoretical argument for why the approach works better than RL. Consider analyzing the information content of the supervision signal and how it relates to the learning process.
- **Fair Comparisons:** Re-run the DSR comparison with comparable training time to ensure a fair comparison. If DSR was limited by training speed, the comparison is not valid.
- **Limitations Discussion:** Discuss the limitations of the approach more thoroughly, particularly regarding environments where measurements aren't provided or are difficult to extract. How would the approach handle cases where the relevant measurements need to be inferred from raw sensory input?
- **Ablation Study:** Include a more detailed ablation study to isolate the specific components contributing to the improved performance (e.g., the prediction framework vs. the vectorial measurements).
- **Architecture Complexity:** Discuss the computational complexity and training time of the proposed method compared to baselines, especially considering the different training speeds mentioned for DSR.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why vectorial measurements provide better supervision than scalar rewards? Is it simply more information, or is there something more nuanced about the structure of the supervision signal?
2. How would your approach scale to environments where the relevant measurements are not directly observable or require inference from raw sensory input?
3. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational complexity and training time of your method versus the baselines, particularly considering the different training speeds mentioned for DSR?

While the paper presents an interesting approach with strong empirical results, the lack of theoretical justification and the questionable comparison to DSR prevent it from meeting ICLR's high standards for theoretical contribution. With more rigorous theoretical grounding and fairer comparisons, this work could be significantly strengthened.
