

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes an innovative approach to sensorimotor control that frames reinforcement learning as a supervised learning problem using a stream of measurements rather than scalar rewards. The authors argue that by predicting future measurements based on current sensory input, measurements, and a goal, agents can learn to act effectively in complex 3D environments without relying on sparse reward signals. The approach is evaluated on the Doom environment, with claims of outperforming state-of-the-art methods, winning the Visual Doom AI Competition, and demonstrating generalization across environments and goals. The work presents a compelling alternative to traditional RL approaches and has significant potential for advancing embodied AI.

## 2. Decision

**Reject**

The paper makes an interesting contribution but contains several significant technical and methodological flaws that undermine the validity of its claims. The most critical issues are the lack of statistical significance testing for the main results and the unfair comparison to baseline methods due to different training times.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core claim is that "the presented approach outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL models, particularly on complex tasks." However, the evidence supporting this claim is significantly weakened by two major issues:

First, the paper does not provide statistical significance testing for the performance differences between the proposed method and baselines. This is a critical omission in a machine learning paper, as it's impossible to determine whether the reported performance differences are meaningful or due to random variation in the training process. For example, in Table 1, the paper reports that DFP outperforms A3C by 25 percentage points in the Navigation scenario, but without statistical testing, we cannot determine if this difference is significant.

Second, the comparison to the DSR baseline is fundamentally unfair. The paper states that "DSR is much slower and can only process roughly 1 million simulation steps per day" and "we were only able to evaluate DSR on the Basic scenario and were not able to perform extensive hyperparameter tuning." The paper then reports DSR's performance after 10 days of training (10 million steps), while DQN, A3C, and DFP were trained for 50 million steps. This means the baselines were trained for 5 times as many steps as DSR, making the comparison invalid. The authors should have either trained DSR for the same number of steps or adjusted their claims accordingly.

Additional issues include:

- Inconsistent notation (e.g., "pat" in Equation 2 vs. "p" in Equation 7)
- Missing definition of "fi" in the loss function (Equation 4)
- Unclear explanation of how the goal vector is generated during training
- Inconsistent metric reporting (health for D1/D2 vs. frags for D3/D4)
- Formatting errors in references (e.g., "Kushner.,? \Q2003\E" instead of "Kushner, 2003")

These issues collectively undermine the scientific rigor of the paper, making it impossible to fully trust the reported results.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, the authors should address the following issues:

1. Perform statistical significance testing for all performance comparisons. The authors should report p-values or confidence intervals for the performance differences between their method and baselines.

2. Re-evaluate the comparison to DSR to ensure a fair comparison (same number of training steps). If this is not feasible, the authors should explicitly state that DSR was not trained to the same extent as the other methods and adjust their claims accordingly.

3. Clarify the notation throughout the paper. Define all symbols upon first use and maintain consistent notation.

4. Provide a clearer explanation of how the goal vector is generated during training, especially for the randomized goal regimes.

5. Standardize the metrics reported across all scenarios (e.g., report health, ammo, and frags for all scenarios rather than switching to frags for D3/D4).

6. Fix reference formatting inconsistencies (e.g., "Kushner.,? \Q2003\E" should be "Kushner, 2003").

7. Clarify the relationship between the measurement stream and the sensory input. The paper claims to use "raw visual input" but then states the agent is provided with "health and ammunition levels," which are not raw visual input.

8. Provide more detail about the "100 randomly textured versions" of the mazes and how the textures were generated, as this is critical for evaluating the generalization claims.

## Questions for Authors

1. Could you provide the statistical significance values (p-values) for the performance differences reported in Table 1 and Figure 3? Without this information, it's impossible to assess whether the claimed improvements are meaningful.

2. Why was DSR trained for only 10 days (10 million steps) while the other methods were trained for 50 million steps? How would the results change if DSR were trained for the same number of steps as the other methods?

3. In the "Goal-agnostic training" section, when the paper states "the third regime makes no assumptions as to whether the measured quantities are desirable or not," how does the agent learn to distinguish between desirable and undesirable states without this information?

4. The paper claims to use "raw visual input," but also states the agent is provided with "health, ammunition, and frag count." How do these measurements relate to the raw visual input? Are they extracted from the game engine or computed from the visual input?
