

# Peer Review: "Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control that reframes reinforcement learning as a supervised learning problem. Instead of using sparse scalar rewards, the authors propose that agents should predict future values of meaningful measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) based on current sensory input and goal. The key insight is that the temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides rich supervisory signal, enabling learning from raw experience without extraneous data. The approach, termed "Direct Future Prediction" (DFP), outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods on complex 3D environments in the Visual Doom platform, particularly on tasks with richer measurement streams. The authors demonstrate strong generalization across environments and goals, and show that vectorial measurements provide significant advantages over scalar rewards.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.** This is a strong contribution that addresses a fundamental limitation in reinforcement learning (sparse rewards) with a simple, elegant approach that achieves state-of-the-art results. The paper is well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated, and makes a clear contribution to the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

The paper's main strengths are:

- **Strong theoretical motivation**: The authors effectively connect their work to Jordan & Rumelhart (1992) and Sutton (1988), who argued that supervised learning can be advantageous when temporally dense multidimensional feedback is available. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for their approach.

- **Compelling empirical results**: The experiments across four increasingly complex Doom scenarios demonstrate clear performance advantages, particularly in the more challenging "Battle" scenarios where vectorial measurements provide significant benefits. The 50% performance improvement over the second-best submission in the Visual Doom AI Competition is especially convincing.

- **Thorough evaluation**: The paper includes ablation studies showing the importance of vectorial measurements and multiple temporal offsets, generalization experiments across environments, and goal-agnostic training experiments that demonstrate the approach's flexibility.

- **Simple yet effective architecture**: The DFP model is straightforward and comparable in complexity to standard RL baselines, making it easy to implement and understand.

The paper effectively demonstrates that for sensorimotor control in complex 3D environments, the rich supervisory signal provided by measurement streams enables learning that surpasses traditional reward-based RL.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, these additions would further strengthen it:

- **Clarify the "extraneous data" claim**: The paper states the model "trains on raw experience without extraneous data," but uses the game's built-in measurements (health, ammo, etc.) as part of the input. These measurements are natural to the environment, not extraneous data, but this distinction needs clarification to avoid confusion.

- **Expand theoretical justification**: While the paper references Jordan & Rumelhart (1992), it lacks a detailed theoretical argument for why predictive future measurements are superior to reward signals in this context. A brief analysis of how the measurement stream provides more informative gradients or better exploration would strengthen the foundation.

- **More detailed comparison with the second-best submission**: The paper claims a 50% performance improvement over the second-best submission in the Visual Doom competition, but doesn't provide enough detail about the baseline approach (Lample & Chaplot, 2016) to fully appreciate this achievement. A brief comparison of their method's architecture and training procedure would help contextualize the results.

- **Clarify goal specification**: The paper mentions that "the goal vector used at test time need not be identical to any goal seen during training," but doesn't explain how the goal vector is specified in practice for different tasks. A brief example of how to convert task specifications into goal vectors would be helpful.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims that "the measurement stream provides rich and temporally dense supervision that can stabilize and accelerate training" compared to sparse scalar rewards. Could you provide a more detailed theoretical explanation of why this specific predictive approach leads to better training dynamics than traditional RL methods?

2. How does your approach relate to model-based RL methods that also use prediction (e.g., predicting next states or rewards)? What specific properties of your predictive framework make it more effective for sensorimotor control in complex environments?

3. In the goal-agnostic training experiments, you show that models trained without knowing the goal generalize well to new goals. Could you elaborate on why this is the case? Is it because the measurement stream contains information about the goal, or is there something about the training process that enables this generalization?

4. The paper mentions that "predicting full sensory streams is beyond our capabilities," but references recent work on predictive sensory prediction (Kalchbrenner et al., 2016; van den Oord et al., 2016). How do you see your approach evolving as predictive sensory prediction becomes more feasible, and would this change your framework?

This is a well-executed, impactful paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. The experimental results are robust and the approach is elegant and practical. With minor clarifications, this paper would be an excellent addition to ICLR.
