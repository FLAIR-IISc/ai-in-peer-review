

# Peer Review: "Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control"

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control that reframes the problem as supervised learning rather than traditional reinforcement learning. The authors propose using a stream of measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) as a rich supervisory signal instead of relying on sparse scalar rewards. The core insight is that by predicting future measurements based on current sensory input, actions, and goals, an agent can effectively learn to act in complex environments. The approach, called Direct Future Prediction (DFP), trains a model to predict the effect of actions on future measurements, then selects actions that optimize the predicted future measurements. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments in the Doom environment that DFP outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods, particularly in complex scenarios with multiple measurements. The paper also shows that DFP generalizes across environments and can handle dynamically specified goals at test time without requiring goal-specific training.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**. The paper makes a compelling contribution to the field with strong empirical evidence supporting the claim that vectorial measurements provide a superior supervisory signal compared to scalar rewards for sensorimotor control in complex environments.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in several key areas:

First, the problem is well-motivated and clearly positioned within the literature. The authors effectively review historical work on supervised learning for control (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992) and contrast it with modern RL approaches, clearly articulating why their approach is appropriate for environments with rich, temporally dense feedback. This is particularly valuable given the increasing availability of such feedback in real-world sensorimotor tasks.

Second, the empirical evidence is robust and comprehensive. The authors conduct experiments across four increasingly complex scenarios in the Doom environment, comparing against three strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across multiple metrics. The results consistently show DFP outperforming baselines, especially in the more complex scenarios (D3 and D4) where multiple measurements are available. The performance gap is substantial - DFP outperforms A3C by a factor of 6 in D3 and 2.5 in D4. 

Third, the ablation studies provide compelling evidence for the key claims. The authors demonstrate that vectorial feedback is significantly more effective than scalar rewards (predicting only frags), and that predicting measurements at multiple temporal offsets improves performance. The generalization experiments across environments (D3-tx/D4-tx) and goal-agnostic training experiments further support the authors' claims about the approach's flexibility and robustness.

Fourth, the paper's practical impact is significant. The authors won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming the second-best submission by more than 50% without using additional supervision from the game engine. This real-world validation of their approach is particularly convincing.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong overall, there are a few areas where the authors could enhance their contribution:

- The paper mentions that the model is purely reactive (no explicit memory), but doesn't discuss how this limitation might be addressed in future work. A brief discussion of how memory-based approaches could be integrated with DFP would strengthen the paper's future directions.

- The comparison with predictive state representations (PSRs) from Littman et al. (2001) would be valuable. While the paper mentions PSRs in the background, it doesn't clearly articulate how their approach differs from or builds upon this line of work.

- The paper states that the model "outperformed the second best submission by more than 50%," but doesn't provide details about the second-best submission's approach. A brief description of what the second-best method did (e.g., "it used an LSTM with reward shaping") would help readers understand the significance of the performance gap.

- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach. For instance, how would DFP handle environments where the measurement stream is not informative about the desired behavior?

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In the scenarios with multiple measurements (D3 and D4), how do the authors ensure the agent learns to use all measurements rather than focusing solely on the most salient one (e.g., frags)? Would the approach still work if some measurements were irrelevant to the goal?

2. The paper mentions that the approach works with discrete action spaces, but how would the authors expect it to scale to continuous action spaces? Would the "action stream" architecture need modification, and if so, how?

3. In the goal-agnostic training experiments, the authors show that models trained with random goals generalize well to new goals. However, how does the performance compare to a model trained on a mixture of goals with known distributions (e.g., goals that match the test-time distribution)? Is random goal training optimal, or are there better training strategies?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating that supervised learning approaches can outperform traditional RL when rich feedback is available. The empirical results are compelling, the methodology is rigorous, and the approach is simple yet effective. The paper deserves to be accepted at ICLR.
