

# Review of "Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control that challenges the conventional reinforcement learning paradigm by replacing the scalar reward with a stream of measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count). The authors propose that the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides a rich supervisory signal, allowing sensorimotor control to be reduced to a supervised learning problem. Their method, called Direct Future Prediction (DFP), trains a model to predict how actions affect future measurements, then selects actions that maximize the predicted goal-relevant measurements. The paper demonstrates that DFP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods (DQN, A3C, DSR) in complex 3D environments like Doom, particularly in scenarios with richer measurement streams. The approach also shows strong generalization across environments and goals, and won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition by outperforming the second-best submission by over 50%.

## 2. Decision

**Accept.** The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating that a simple supervised learning approach can outperform sophisticated deep RL methods in complex sensorimotor tasks. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the approach is well-motivated and addresses a meaningful limitation in current RL methods (sparse rewards), and (2) the empirical results are scientifically rigorous, consistent, and demonstrate substantial performance gains over strong baselines.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's contribution is particularly compelling because it challenges the field's assumption that RL is necessary for sensorimotor control. The authors convincingly argue that the measurement stream provides richer, temporally denser supervision than traditional scalar rewards, which explains why their approach outperforms RL methods in complex scenarios. This insight has important theoretical implications for how we view reinforcement learning problems.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors evaluate their approach across four increasingly complex Doom scenarios, comparing against three strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) on both performance metrics and generalization capabilities. The results consistently show that DFP outperforms the baselines, with the largest gains (6x in D3, 2.5x in D4) in the most challenging scenarios where multiple measurements are provided. The ablation study (Table 4) further supports the authors' claims by demonstrating that vectorial feedback and predicting at multiple temporal offsets are crucial for the approach's success.

The Visual Doom AI Competition win provides strong independent validation of the approach's effectiveness. The fact that DFP outperformed a strong baseline (Lample & Chaplot, 2016) that used additional supervision from the game engine demonstrates the robustness and efficiency of their method.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. **Clarify the theoretical distinction from prior work on future prediction.** While the paper cites relevant literature (Oh et al., 2015; Littman et al., 2001), it would benefit from a clearer explanation of how DFP differs from these approaches. Specifically, how does the use of parameterized goals and vectorial measurements in the context of raw sensory input represent a novel contribution?

2. **Expand the discussion of limitations.** The paper mentions that the approach is purely reactive, but it would be valuable to discuss other limitations more explicitly. For example, how would the approach scale to environments with very high-dimensional measurement streams? What happens when the measurement stream is not directly related to the task? A brief discussion of these limitations would strengthen the paper's credibility.

3. **Provide more intuition about why vectorial measurements work better.** While the paper states that vectorial measurements provide "rich and temporally dense supervision," a brief theoretical analysis or more intuitive explanation of why this leads to better learning would help readers understand the core insight.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "the measurement stream provides rich and temporally dense supervision that can stabilize and accelerate training." Could you provide more specific intuition about why vectorial measurements provide better supervision than scalar rewards? What mechanisms (e.g., reduced variance in gradient estimates) do you believe are responsible for the improved training stability?

2. In the Visual Doom AI Competition, the second-best submission used additional supervision from the game engine (ground-truth knowledge of enemies). How do you think your approach would perform if the baselines had access to the same level of additional supervision? Would your approach still outperform them?

3. The paper demonstrates that models trained with randomized goals generalize better to new goals. How does performance degrade as the test-time goal moves further from the distribution of goals seen during training? Is there a limit to how far the goal can deviate before performance degrades significantly?

This is a strong contribution that challenges fundamental assumptions in reinforcement learning and provides a simple yet highly effective alternative for sensorimotor control. The paper is well-written, the experiments are rigorous, and the results are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the theoretical contribution and address the limitations more explicitly.
