

# Review of "Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes an alternative approach to reinforcement learning (RL) for sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments. Instead of using a scalar reward signal, the authors frame the problem as supervised learning where the agent predicts future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) based on current sensory input and proposed actions. The authors claim that this approach provides richer supervision than traditional RL, enables training without a fixed goal, and generalizes better across environments and tasks. They evaluate their method on the Visual Doom AI Competition tasks, reporting significant performance improvements over established deep RL methods (DQN, A3C, DSR) and winning the Full Deathmatch track.

## 2. Decision

**Reject**

The key reasons for rejection are: (1) insufficient empirical validation of the core claim that vectorial measurements provide superior supervision to scalar rewards, and (2) inadequate experimental rigor in comparison to established baselines.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

While the paper's core idea has some conceptual merit and the empirical results appear promising at first glance, the paper fails to meet ICLR's standards for methodological rigor. The primary issues are:

First, the comparison to baselines lacks proper experimental controls. The paper trains DSR for only 1 million steps (10 days) while DQN, A3C, and DFP are trained for 50 million steps. This is a critical flaw as DSR's training time is orders of magnitude shorter, making the comparison inherently unfair. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't compensate by providing additional evidence that the 10-day training period was sufficient for DSR to converge. Without this, the claimed 50% performance advantage cannot be validated.

Second, the paper claims their approach "outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL models, particularly on complex tasks" but fails to provide sufficient statistical evidence. The results are reported as averages over three training runs, but the paper doesn't provide standard deviations or confidence intervals, making it impossible to assess whether the observed differences are statistically significant. The paper also doesn't conduct proper ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the vectorial measurement approach versus other architectural choices.

Third, the paper makes strong claims about generalization and goal-agnostic training, but the experiments don't adequately support these claims. For instance, the generalization experiments in Table 2 show modest improvements in generalization when training on varied textures, but the paper doesn't demonstrate that this is specifically due to the vectorial measurement approach rather than other aspects of the model or training procedure.

The paper's theoretical grounding is also weak. While it cites historical work on supervised learning for control (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992), it fails to address why previous attempts to use supervised learning for control from raw experience haven't been successful, or why this specific approach would overcome those challenges. The paper doesn't provide any theoretical analysis of why vectorial measurements would provide better supervision than scalar rewards.

## 4. Additional Feedback and Questions

**For improvement:** To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Re-run all experiments with equal training time for all methods (at least 50 million steps for DSR), or provide a detailed analysis showing that DSR's training is sufficiently efficient.
2. Include statistical significance tests for all performance comparisons.
3. Conduct a more thorough ablation study to isolate the contribution of the vectorial measurement approach from other architectural choices.
4. Provide more detailed implementation details for baselines to ensure fair comparison.

**Questions for authors:**
1. How much additional training time would be required for DSR to match the 50 million steps of the other methods? Could the authors provide an estimate of how much performance would improve with equivalent training time?
2. The paper claims their method "outperformed the second best submission... by more than 50%." Please provide the exact metric and values for both the winning model and the second-best submission to allow for proper assessment of this claim.
3. In the goal-agnostic training experiments (Table 3), the authors report that models trained with random goal vectors (b,c) perform nearly as well as those trained with a fixed goal (a). How much more training data was required for the goal-agnostic models to achieve this performance? Was the training time comparable?
4. The paper claims that "predicting multiple measurements significantly improves the performance of the learned model." However, in Table 4, the model that predicts only frags (a scalar reward) performs better than the baseline in some cases. How do the authors reconcile this apparent contradiction with their main claim?
