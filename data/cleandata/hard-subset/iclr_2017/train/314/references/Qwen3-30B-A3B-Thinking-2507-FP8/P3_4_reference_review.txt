

# Peer Review: "Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control"

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control that reframes the problem as supervised learning rather than reinforcement learning. The authors argue that traditional RL's reliance on sparse scalar rewards is suboptimal for immersive environments where agents receive rich, multidimensional sensory feedback. Instead, they propose predicting future values of meaningful measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) from current sensory input and measurements. The model is trained to predict how different actions affect future measurements, and at test time selects actions that best achieve the specified goal. The approach, called Direct Future Prediction (DFP), demonstrates significant performance improvements over state-of-the-art deep RL methods (DQN, A3C, DSR) in complex 3D environments, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field by challenging the conventional RL paradigm for sensorimotor control. The approach is well-motivated by the limitations of sparse scalar rewards in realistic environments, and the empirical results are robust and significant, particularly in complex scenarios where the advantage of vectorial feedback is most pronounced.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Novelty and Literature Positioning (Key Strength):** The paper successfully positions its approach within the historical context of supervised versus reinforcement learning. It properly credits Jordan & Rumelhart (1992) and Sutton (1988) for earlier insights about when supervised learning might be preferable, and effectively connects to modern work on vectorial feedback and goal-conditioned learning (Schaul et al., 2015). The authors make a convincing case that the rich, temporally dense measurements in immersive environments provide a natural supervisory signal that has been underutilized in current RL frameworks.

**Rigorous Experimental Validation (Key Strength):** The paper provides exceptionally thorough empirical validation. The four progressively challenging Doom scenarios (D1-D4) allow for a clear demonstration of the approach's scalability. The comparison to multiple strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) is fair and comprehensive, with careful attention to training details and hyperparameter tuning. The ablation studies (Table 4) effectively demonstrate the importance of vectorial measurements and multiple temporal predictions. The generalization experiments across environments (Table 2) and the real-world validation through winning the Visual Doom AI Competition provide compelling evidence of the approach's robustness and practical significance.

**Clear Technical Contribution:** The architecture is well-designed, particularly the split into expectation and action streams (Equations 6-7), which elegantly handles the prediction of future measurements across different actions. The training procedure (Section 3.1) is clearly described, and the authors make a thoughtful choice to use a small experience memory rather than pure on-policy training.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

**Clarify Goal Generalization:** The paper claims the model can "pursue dynamically specified goals at test time," but the details of how this works for completely novel goals are not fully explained. Please clarify how the model handles goals that were not seen during training (e.g., a new measurement not used in training) and provide additional evidence of this capability.

**Expand Theoretical Discussion:** While the paper is primarily empirical, adding a brief theoretical analysis of why vectorial feedback might outperform scalar rewards in this setting would strengthen the contribution. For instance, how does the information-theoretic advantage of multiple measurements translate to faster convergence?

**Address Computational Efficiency:** The paper notes that DSR was trained for only 10 days due to slow training speed, but doesn't provide a clear comparison of computational resources used for each method. Including training time comparisons would help readers understand the practical efficiency of the approach.

**Discuss Limitations More Fully:** The paper should explicitly address limitations. For example, what types of tasks would this approach struggle with? How does it handle environments where measurements are not directly observable or where the relationship between measurements and goals is highly nonlinear?

## Questions for Authors

1. In the goal-agnostic training experiments (Table 3), the model trained with random goal vectors (regimes b and c) performs nearly as well as a model trained for the specific goal (regime a). Could you elaborate on why this is the case, and whether the model learns to prioritize certain measurements over others during training?

2. The paper states that "the use of vectorial measurements instead of a scalar reward is beneficial" (Table 4). Can you explain why predicting multiple measurements improves performance even when the evaluation metric is a single measurement (e.g., frags)? Is this due to the additional information provided by the other measurements, or is there a more subtle effect?

3. The authors note that the model is "purely reactive" with "no explicit facilities for memory." How do you envision integrating memory mechanisms (e.g., recurrent networks) with this approach, and what would be the expected benefits for more complex tasks requiring longer-term planning?

4. In the Visual Doom AI Competition, your model won by "more than 50%." Could you provide the exact performance metrics (e.g., average frags per game) for both your model and the second-place submission to better quantify the significance of this result?

5. The paper uses a fixed set of temporal offsets (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32 steps) across all experiments. How sensitive is the model's performance to the choice of these offsets, and would the results generalize to different temporal prediction horizons?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating that a simple supervised learning approach can outperform sophisticated RL methods in complex sensorimotor tasks. The results are robust, the methodology is sound, and the implications for future research are clear. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points above.
