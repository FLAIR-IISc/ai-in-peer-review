

# Peer Review: Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control that fundamentally departs from traditional reinforcement learning (RL) formalization. Instead of using a scalar reward signal, the authors propose using a stream of vectorial measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) as a dense supervisory signal for learning. The core idea is to train a model to predict future measurements given current sensory input, measurements, and a goal, thereby reducing sensorimotor control to a supervised learning problem. The authors claim that this approach: (1) provides richer temporal supervision than sparse scalar rewards; (2) supports training without fixed goals, enabling flexible deployment to dynamically specified goals at test time; and (3) achieves state-of-the-art performance in complex 3D environments, particularly in the Doom game benchmark. The approach, called Direct Future Prediction (DFP), was demonstrated to win the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming the second-best submission by over 50%.

## 2. Decision: Accept

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work presents a simple yet powerful idea that significantly advances our understanding of sensorimotor control, with compelling empirical results that demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art RL methods. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by showing that dense vectorial feedback can be more effective than sparse scalar rewards in complex 3D environments.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

**Theoretical Contribution and Motivation (Strong):** The paper is well-motivated and properly situated in the literature. The authors correctly identify a key limitation of traditional RL—sparse scalar rewards—and provide a compelling argument for why dense vectorial feedback can be more effective. Their connection to Jordan & Rumelhart (1992) and the historical context of supervised learning for sensorimotor control is well-placed. The paper effectively argues that their approach is not merely a minor variation but a fundamentally different way of framing the learning problem.

**Empirical Rigor and Results (Very Strong):** The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors compare their approach to three strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across four progressively more difficult scenarios. The results consistently show significant advantages for DFP, particularly in the more complex environments (D3 and D4), where DFP outperforms A3C by factors of 6 and 2.5, respectively. The ablation study effectively demonstrates the importance of using vectorial measurements rather than scalar rewards and predicting at multiple temporal offsets. The victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition, where DFP outperformed a substantially more complex model by over 50%, provides particularly strong empirical validation.

**Generalization and Flexibility (Strong):** The paper's demonstration of generalization across environments (Table 2) and the ability to handle dynamically specified goals at test time (Table 3) are significant contributions that go beyond typical RL work. The paper convincingly shows that models trained without knowing the eventual goal can perform nearly as well as models trained specifically for that goal.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, these improvements would make it even better:

1. **Clarify the "raw experience" claim:** The paper states it trains "on raw experience" but actually uses the game's internal measurement system (health, ammo, etc.). This is an important distinction that should be clarified to avoid misrepresenting the approach's data requirements.

2. **Provide more insight into why this works:** While the empirical results are compelling, the paper could provide deeper theoretical analysis of why direct future prediction of measurements is so effective compared to other supervised approaches (e.g., inverse dynamics models). A brief discussion of the conditions under which this approach would fail would also strengthen the paper.

3. **Expand the theoretical connection to existing work:** While the paper references Schaul et al. (2015), it could better clarify how their approach differs from and extends the Universal Value Function Approximators framework. The connection to predictive state representations (Littman et al., 2001) could also be strengthened.

4. **Clarify the 50% performance difference:** The paper states that DFP "outperformed the second best submission by more than 50%," but it's unclear whether this refers to a 50% improvement in performance or a 50-point difference in frags. This should be clarified.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper claims that the measurement stream provides "rich and temporally dense supervision." How do you quantify this advantage? Could you provide a comparison of training dynamics (e.g., learning curves with different learning rates) to demonstrate how the dense supervision stabilizes and accelerates training?

2. In the goal-agnostic training experiments (Table 3), you show that models trained with random goal vectors generalize better. Could you elaborate on how the goal vector is actually used during training? Specifically, how are the goal vectors generated for each episode in the randomized goal regime?

3. The paper states that "the agent is provided with three measurements: health, ammunition, and frag count" in D3 and D4. How would this approach scale to environments where the appropriate measurements are not provided by the game engine, but must be learned from raw sensory input? What would be the implications for the approach's generalizability beyond the Doom environment?

4. The paper mentions that "predicting full sensory streams is beyond our capabilities" but references recent work on predicting future frames (Oh et al., 2015; Kalchbrenner et al., 2016). Could you discuss how your approach might integrate with these methods in the future, and whether predicting sensory features might eventually blur the distinction between sensory and measurement streams as you suggest in the discussion?
