

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control in reinforcement learning that shifts from traditional reward-based formulations to prediction of future measurements. The authors argue that the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides rich supervisory signals, allowing sensorimotor control to be framed as a supervised learning problem. Their method, called Direct Future Prediction (DFP), trains a model to predict the effect of actions on future measurements, enabling action selection without a fixed goal during training. The paper demonstrates that this approach outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods in complex three-dimensional environments, particularly when multiple measurements are available, and shows strong generalization across environments and goals.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating that supervised learning techniques can be effectively applied to complex sensorimotor control problems with raw sensory input, and provides compelling empirical evidence supporting this claim.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses a meaningful problem in reinforcement learning: the challenges of learning sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex, dynamic environments. The motivation is well-placed in the literature, with a thorough review of prior work on supervised learning approaches to action, Monte Carlo methods, and vector-valued feedback. The authors correctly identify the gap between traditional RL formulations and the needs of immersive, complex environments.

The claims are well-supported by scientifically rigorous experiments. The paper compares DFP against strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across four increasingly challenging scenarios in the Doom environment. The results consistently show DFP outperforming baselines, particularly in more complex tasks (Battle and Battle 2 scenarios) where multiple measurements are available. The performance gains are substantial: DFP outperforms A3C by a factor of 6 in the Battle scenario and by 2.5 in Battle 2. The ablation studies convincingly demonstrate the importance of vectorial measurements and multiple temporal offsets.

The paper's contribution is scientifically significant as it provides empirical evidence that supervised learning can be a viable alternative to traditional RL in complex environments, challenging the dominance of TD-learning and policy gradient methods. The Visual Doom AI Competition results further strengthen the claims, as DFP won the Full Deathmatch track against a strong baseline that used additional supervision.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
While the paper is strong, several improvements would enhance its clarity and impact:

1. The paper should more explicitly clarify the relationship between the proposed approach and existing work on predictive state representations and successor representations. While the paper mentions these connections, a more detailed comparison would help situate the contribution.

2. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach. For instance, how does DFP handle environments where measurements are not readily available or where the relationship between actions and measurements is highly non-Markovian?

3. The architecture details in Section 3.2 are somewhat dense. A clearer diagram of the network architecture would aid understanding, especially the interaction between the expectation stream and action stream.

4. The paper claims to train without extraneous data, but it would be helpful to quantify the amount of data required compared to standard RL approaches, as this is an important practical consideration.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. In the Visual Doom AI Competition, your model won the Full Deathmatch track without using the internal game engine knowledge that the second-place model utilized. Could you clarify what specific aspects of your approach allowed it to outperform the second-place model despite lacking this additional supervision?

2. The paper mentions that the model is purely reactive, with no explicit facilities for memory. How do you expect this limitation to impact performance in tasks requiring long-term planning or where the current state alone is insufficient for optimal action selection?

3. In the goal-agnostic training experiments (Table 3), the model trained with random goal vectors (regimes b and c) generalizes better than the fixed goal model. Could you elaborate on why regime c (sampling from [-1, 1]) performs better than regime b (sampling from [0, 1])? Is this due to the model learning to distinguish between desirable and undesirable measurements?
