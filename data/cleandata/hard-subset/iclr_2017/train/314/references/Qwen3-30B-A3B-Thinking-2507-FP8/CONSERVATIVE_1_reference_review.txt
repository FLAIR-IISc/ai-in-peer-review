

# Review for ICLR

## Summary of Contribution
The paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control in reinforcement learning by framing the problem as supervised learning rather than traditional reward-based RL. The authors argue that when an agent receives a stream of measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) in addition to sensory input, it can predict the effect of actions on future measurements and select actions that optimize these predictions. They claim this approach, which they term "Direct Future Prediction" (DFP), outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods on complex tasks in the Doom environment, particularly when multiple measurements are used. The paper further claims that the approach generalizes across environments and goals without requiring fixed goal specification during training.

## Decision
Reject. The paper fails to provide sufficient empirical evidence to support its claims of general superiority over established RL methods, and the validation is limited to a narrow domain with insufficient comparison to recent state-of-the-art approaches.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's primary claim is that its approach outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods, particularly on complex tasks. However, the empirical validation is fundamentally limited in several critical ways:

1. **Narrow Scope of Evaluation**: The experiments are confined to the Doom environment with only four scenarios, despite the paper claiming general applicability. The Doom environment, while a common benchmark, represents a very specific domain (first-person shooter games) and does not demonstrate generalization to other environments or tasks that would be necessary to support the broader claim of "sensorimotor control in complex and dynamic three-dimensional environments."

2. **Inadequate Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The paper compares to DQN, A3C, and DSR, but these are not the current state-of-the-art methods. More recent, highly effective algorithms like PPO, SAC, and DDPG (which have significantly outperformed A3C in many domains) are not included in the comparison. The authors cite Mnih et al. (2016) for A3C but fail to acknowledge that the field has advanced considerably since then.

3. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper includes minimal ablation studies to isolate the contribution of key components. For example, Table 4 shows that predicting multiple measurements improves performance, but the paper doesn't systematically test how different measurement combinations affect performance or whether the approach would work with measurements that don't correlate well with task success.

4. **Insufficient Evidence for Generalization Claims**: The paper claims generalization across environments and goals, but the experiments are limited to variations of the Doom environment with different textures. These are superficial variations that don't test true generalization to fundamentally different environments or tasks.

5. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: While the paper cites Jordan & Rumelhart (1992) and Sutton (1988), it doesn't provide a theoretical foundation for why this approach should work better than traditional RL in complex environments. The paper presents empirical results without explaining why the method should be expected to work, making the claim feel more like an empirical observation than a principled advance.

## Additional Feedback
To strengthen this work, the authors should:

1. Expand the empirical evaluation to include multiple environments beyond Doom (e.g., Atari games, robotics simulators, or other 3D environments) to demonstrate broader applicability.

2. Include comprehensive comparisons with more recent state-of-the-art RL methods, not just the ones cited in the paper.

3. Conduct thorough ablation studies to understand the specific contributions of different components (e.g., number of measurements, temporal offsets, goal specification).

4. Provide a more detailed description of the Visual Doom AI Competition results, including the number of participants, the statistical significance of the victory, and how their approach compares to other submissions.

5. Address the limitations of the approach, such as how it would handle environments where measurements are not readily available or where measurements don't correlate with task success.

## Questions for Authors
1. How does your approach compare to more recent state-of-the-art RL methods like PPO, SAC, and DDPG, which have demonstrated superior performance on a variety of tasks?

2. Can you provide more details about the Visual Doom AI Competition, including the number of participants, the statistical significance of your victory, and how your performance compares to the second-place submission?

3. What specific environments or tasks would be particularly challenging for your approach, and why?

4. How would you modify your approach to handle situations where the measurements are not readily available or where they don't correlate with task success?

5. Can you provide ablation studies showing the effect of using different combinations of measurements (e.g., health only vs. health and ammunition) on performance across different tasks?

The paper presents an interesting empirical observation but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its broader claims of general superiority over established methods. Without more rigorous validation across a diverse set of environments and tasks, the contribution lacks the necessary scientific rigor to be considered for publication at ICLR.
