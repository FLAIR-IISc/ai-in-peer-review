

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Contribution

The paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control in complex 3D environments by reframing reinforcement learning as a supervised learning problem. Instead of relying on sparse scalar rewards, the authors propose using a stream of measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) that provide dense, temporally rich supervision. The core idea is that agents can be trained to predict how actions will affect future measurements, with the goal defined in terms of these measurements. This "Direct Future Prediction" (DFP) approach enables learning from raw sensory input without extraneous supervision, supports flexible goal specification at test time, and demonstrates superior performance compared to state-of-the-art deep RL methods. The authors validate their approach through extensive experiments in the Doom environment, showing significant performance gains, strong generalization across environments, and the ability to handle dynamically specified goals.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

Key reasons for acceptance: 
1. The paper makes a compelling contribution by demonstrating that a simple supervised learning approach can outperform sophisticated deep RL methods in complex 3D environments.
2. The experimental results are rigorous, well-designed, and provide strong evidence for the claims, including winning a major competition with a significant margin.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is exceptionally well-motivated and positioned within the literature. The authors effectively cite relevant work by Jordan & Rumelhart (1992) and Sutton (1988) to establish that supervised learning is appropriate when dense feedback is available, while acknowledging that TD learning has dominated in sparse reward settings. Their formulation is a natural extension of this line of thinking to realistic, complex environments with rich sensory feedback.

The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The authors conduct four increasingly challenging scenarios in the Doom environment, showing consistent performance advantages of DFP over DQN, A3C, and DSR. The most compelling evidence is the 50% performance advantage over the second-best submission in the Visual Doom AI Competition's Full Deathmatch track—a significant real-world validation. The ablation studies (Table 4) provide strong evidence that vectorial measurements and multi-temporal prediction are key to the approach's success.

The paper also makes a meaningful contribution to the understanding of what constitutes effective supervision in learning to act. By demonstrating that the cotemporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides natural supervision, they address a fundamental question about the nature of learning signals in embodied agents.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where the authors could improve clarity and impact:

- The theoretical justification for why prediction of future measurements works so well could be strengthened. While they reference previous work on predictive state representations, explicitly connecting their method to existing theoretical frameworks (e.g., predictive state representations, successor representations) would deepen the contribution.

- The architecture description (Section 3.2) could benefit from a clearer explanation of why the two-stream prediction module (expectation and action streams) is necessary. A brief comparison to a simpler architecture would help readers understand the design choices.

- The paper mentions that the approach "can be extended in multiple ways" but doesn't provide specific examples of these extensions. A brief discussion of potential extensions (e.g., how to incorporate memory, handle continuous actions) would strengthen the impact.

- The paper states that "the use of vectorial measurements instead of a scalar reward is beneficial" but could more explicitly discuss the limitations of their approach. For example, how might the method handle environments where meaningful measurements are not readily available?

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that the measurement stream provides "rich and temporally dense supervision." How does this differ from the concept of "intrinsic rewards" used in curiosity-driven exploration, and why might your approach be superior?

2. In Table 4, the ablation study shows that predicting only the "frags" measurement (a scalar reward) significantly underperforms the full approach. Could you elaborate on why predicting a single measurement (even if it's the most important one) is insufficient compared to predicting multiple measurements?

3. The authors mention that "the measurement stream has lower dimensionality" than sensory input. How did you determine which measurements to include in this stream? Is there a principled way to select these measurements for a new environment?

4. The paper claims the approach "supports training without a fixed goal." How does this compare to the goal-conditioned RL approaches by Schaul et al. (2015) and others? What specific advantages does your approach offer over these methods?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating that a simple supervised learning approach can outperform state-of-the-art RL methods in complex 3D environments. The experimental results are compelling, the motivation is strong, and the approach has clear potential for broader application. I recommend acceptance to ICLR.
