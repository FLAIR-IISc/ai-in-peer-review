

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims and contribution

This paper presents a fundamentally novel approach to sensorimotor control that challenges the standard reinforcement learning paradigm. The authors propose replacing the traditional scalar reward signal with a stream of measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) and framing the problem as predicting future measurements based on current sensory input, measurements, and goals. This transforms sensorimotor control into a supervised learning problem where "supervision is provided by experience itself." The key innovation is the insight that the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides rich, temporally dense supervision that can stabilize and accelerate training. The authors demonstrate that their Direct Future Prediction (DFP) approach outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods on challenging 3D environments, particularly when multiple measurements are available. The paper further shows that the approach generalizes across environments and can handle dynamically specified goals at test time without retraining. The authors' model won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition by outperforming the second-best submission by more than 50%.

## 2. Decision

Accept. This paper makes a bold conceptual contribution that fundamentally reframes sensorimotor control as a supervised learning problem. The core idea is both novel and compelling, and the empirical results strongly support the claims. The paper's contribution is significant enough to outweigh minor methodological limitations.

## 3. Supporting arguments for the decision

This paper's primary strength is its conceptual novelty. The authors successfully challenge the standard RL paradigm by proposing a completely different supervisory signal (vectorial measurements instead of scalar rewards) that is both theoretically well-motivated and empirically validated. The idea that "supervision is provided by experience itself" represents a significant shift in perspective that has been overlooked in the RL community for decades.

The empirical results are impressive and directly support the paper's claims. In the most challenging scenarios (D3 and D4), the DFP approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods by factors of 6 and 2.5, respectively. The paper demonstrates not just better performance but also significant advantages in generalization and goal flexibility—capabilities that are often missing from standard RL approaches. The victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition provides strong external validation of the approach's effectiveness.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, carefully situating its contribution within the context of supervised learning approaches to learning to act (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992), multi-objective decision-making, and predictive representations. The authors clearly articulate how their work differs from and builds upon existing approaches, particularly Schaul et al. (2015).

While the paper has some minor methodological limitations (e.g., the training time disparity for DSR), these do not undermine the core novelty or empirical claims. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., being purely reactive) and suggest promising directions for future work.

## 4. Additional feedback to improve the paper

To further strengthen this already strong paper, I suggest:

1. More explicit discussion of why vectorial measurements work better than scalar rewards. The paper demonstrates the advantage but doesn't provide a clear theoretical explanation for why the richer signal leads to better performance.

2. A clearer explanation of how the goal vector g is used during training versus testing. The paper mentions that the goal vector at test time need not match training goals, but it's not entirely clear how the agent generalizes to goals outside the training distribution.

3. A more thorough discussion of the computational cost of the approach. While the paper mentions training speed in Table 1, it doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison of computational resources required versus the baselines.

4. A brief discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding the assumption that goals can be expressed in terms of future measurements. Are there tasks where this is not possible or where the approach would fail?

## 5. Questions for the authors

1. In the ablation study (Table 4), the authors show that predicting multiple measurements significantly improves performance even when evaluated by only one measurement. Could you provide more insight into why this is the case? Is it simply because the model learns more robust representations, or is there a more specific mechanism?

2. The paper mentions that the approach "can be extended in multiple ways that are important for broadening the range of behaviors that can be learned." Could you elaborate on which of these extensions would be most promising for addressing the limitations of the current approach (e.g., purely reactive behavior)?

3. The authors state that "the model trains on raw experience, by interacting with the environment without extraneous supervision." However, the training procedure involves random exploration (ε-greedy). How does this compare to the exploration strategies used in standard RL methods, and why does the approach not require the same level of sophisticated exploration as traditional RL?
