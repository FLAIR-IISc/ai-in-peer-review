

# Review of "Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control in complex 3D environments that reframes reinforcement learning as a supervised learning problem. The key insight is that the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides a rich supervisory signal. Instead of relying on sparse scalar rewards, the agent learns to predict the effects of actions on future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count in the Doom environment). The authors demonstrate that this approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods in challenging 3D environments, particularly when multiple measurements are available. The work is validated through comprehensive experiments in the ViZDoom platform, including a winning submission in the Visual Doom AI Competition's Full Deathmatch track.

## 2. Decision

**Accept.** The paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of reinforcement learning by demonstrating that sensorimotor control can be effectively framed as a supervised learning problem when provided with rich, temporally dense measurement streams. The empirical results are robust and well-supported by multiple lines of evidence including comparative experiments, generalization studies, and ablation analyses.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses a meaningful problem in RL: learning from raw sensory input in complex, dynamic 3D environments. The authors correctly identify key limitations of traditional RL approaches, particularly the sparsity of scalar rewards that can hinder learning in immersive environments. Their proposed solution is well-motivated by prior work on supervised learning for action (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992) and predictive representations (Sutton et al., 2011), while clearly distinguishing their approach from existing methods.

The empirical evaluation is particularly strong. The authors design four progressively more challenging scenarios in the Doom environment, demonstrating consistent outperformance over state-of-the-art baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across all tasks, with particularly significant advantages in complex scenarios (D3 and D4). The 50%+ performance gap over the second-place submission in the Visual Doom AI Competition is compelling evidence of the approach's practical value.

The paper makes a strong case for the importance of vectorial measurements versus scalar rewards through both empirical results and ablation studies (Table 4). The results clearly show that predicting multiple measurements (even when only one is used for evaluation) significantly improves performance over predicting a single scalar reward. Similarly, predicting measurements at multiple temporal offsets provides additional benefits.

The generalization experiments across environments (Table 2) and the ability to generalize to new goals without retraining (Table 3) further strengthen the paper's claims about the approach's flexibility and robustness.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend the following:

1. **Clarify the measurement vs. sensory distinction**: The paper acknowledges that the measurement stream is "somewhat artificial" but doesn't sufficiently explain why certain sensations are designated as measurements rather than raw sensory input. A more detailed explanation of this design choice would help readers understand the practical implementation.

2. **Provide more insight into why the approach works**: While the paper argues that richer supervision is beneficial, a deeper analysis of why this specific prediction-based approach outperforms traditional RL would strengthen the theoretical contribution. For instance, how does the model's ability to predict multiple measurements simultaneously provide advantages that traditional value-based methods cannot capture?

3. **Expand the discussion of limitations**: The paper acknowledges the approach is purely reactive with no explicit memory, but doesn't explore how this limitation might affect performance on tasks requiring long-term planning. A more thorough discussion of this limitation and potential pathways for future work would be valuable.

4. **Clarify the Visual Doom competition results**: The paper states the approach "outperformed the second best submission by more than 50%," but doesn't provide the exact performance metrics. Including these would strengthen the claim and allow for more precise comparison.

## Questions for Authors

1. In the Visual Doom AI Competition, the second-place submission (Lample & Chaplot, 2016) used additional supervision from the game engine (ground-truth enemy presence). How would your approach perform if it also incorporated this additional supervision? Would it still outperform the second-place submission by 50%+?

2. The paper shows that predicting multiple measurements improves performance even when only one measurement is used for evaluation. Could you provide more insight into why this is the case? Is it simply because the network learns better representations of the environment, or is there something more specific about the relationships between the measurements?

3. The paper mentions that the approach "can be extended in multiple ways" including memory-based models and hierarchical organization of skills. How would you expect these extensions to interact with the direct future prediction framework? Would they be straightforward to integrate, or would they require significant architectural changes?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating that sensorimotor control can be effectively framed as a supervised learning problem when provided with the right supervisory signal. The empirical results are strong, the approach is well-motivated, and the work has clear practical significance as evidenced by the Visual Doom AI Competition win. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the ICLR conference.
