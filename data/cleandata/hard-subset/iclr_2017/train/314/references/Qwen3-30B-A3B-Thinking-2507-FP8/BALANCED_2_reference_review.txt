

# Review of "Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper presents an innovative approach to sensorimotor control that reframes reinforcement learning as a supervised learning problem using measurement streams rather than scalar rewards. The authors propose a framework where an agent predicts future values of meaningful measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) based on current sensory input and a goal vector, then selects actions that best achieve the desired future state. The approach, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), is evaluated in immersive 3D environments using the ViZDoom platform. The authors claim their method outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods (DQN, A3C, DSR) in complex scenarios, demonstrates strong generalization across environments, and effectively handles dynamically specified goals at test time. Notably, their DFP model won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition by outperforming the second-best submission by over 50%.

## 2. Decision

**Reject**

Key reasons for rejection: The paper makes a compelling empirical claim but lacks sufficient theoretical grounding and fails to adequately distinguish its approach from related work. The experimental evaluation, while impressive, does not fully address fundamental questions about why this approach works better than traditional RL, and the generalization claims are limited by the narrow scope of the experiments.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main weakness lies in its insufficient theoretical justification and inadequate differentiation from existing work. While the authors cite Schaul et al. (2015) as related work, they fail to clearly articulate how their specific formulation differs and why it should be expected to outperform existing methods. The claim that "our work deals with the more general setting of vectorial feedback and parameterized goals" lacks substantive theoretical support. The paper's reliance on Monte Carlo methods as justification is superficial, as they don't provide convergence guarantees or theoretical analysis of why vectorial feedback should improve performance.

The experimental evaluation, while extensive within the Doom domain, has significant limitations. The paper tests exclusively on a single platform (ViZDoom) with a narrow range of environments. This severely limits the generalizability of their claims. The paper also doesn't address how their approach would perform on tasks where meaningful measurements aren't readily available or where the measurement space is high-dimensional and noisy.

The paper's strongest claim is that DFP "outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL models." However, the comparison is not entirely fair: DSR was trained for only 10 days (1 million steps) while the others were trained for 50 million steps. The paper states this was "not sufficient to approach the number of steps afforded by the other approaches," which undermines the comparison. The paper also doesn't provide sufficient statistical significance testing for the performance differences.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen this work for future submission, the authors should:

1. Provide a more rigorous theoretical analysis of why vectorial feedback should improve learning. This could include formal guarantees about convergence or sample complexity advantages.

2. Expand the experimental evaluation to include at least one substantially different domain (e.g., robotics simulation, other game platforms) to demonstrate broader applicability beyond Doom.

3. Conduct a more thorough ablation study on the impact of measurement selection. The paper claims "predicting multiple measurements significantly improves performance," but doesn't investigate how different measurement choices affect performance, which is crucial for real-world applicability.

4. Address the fairness of the comparison with DSR by either training DSR for the same number of steps or explicitly stating the training time discrepancy and its impact on the results.

5. Clarify the relationship with Schaul et al. (2015) and other relevant work. The paper claims to be "most closely related" to Schaul et al. but doesn't explain how their formulation differs or why it should be expected to outperform the existing framework.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "the measurement vector mt is distinguished from other sensations in two ways." Could you clarify why it's necessary to make this distinction, and what would happen if the agent were trained to predict the entire sensory stream instead of a subset?

2. The paper claims generalization across environments, but the experiments only use randomly textured versions of the same mazes. How would the approach perform when tested on completely different environment layouts (e.g., different maze structures or entirely different game mechanics)?

3. In Table 4, the authors report that predicting multiple measurements improves performance even when evaluated by only one measurement. Could you provide more insight into why this occurs? Is it related to the internal representations learned by the network?

4. The paper mentions "the use of vectorial measurements instead of a scalar reward is beneficial," but doesn't explain how to select meaningful measurements for a new domain. How would an agent determine which measurements to predict for a new task where domain knowledge is limited?
