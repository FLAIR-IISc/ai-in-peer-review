

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contribution

The paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control in immersive 3D environments by framing it as supervised learning rather than reinforcement learning. The authors claim that by predicting future measurements (health, ammo, frags) instead of relying on scalar rewards, their method achieves superior performance in complex 3D environments like Doom. They introduce "Direct Future Prediction" (DFP), which uses a deep network to predict future measurements given current sensory input, measurements, and a goal. The paper claims DFP outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods (DQN, A3C, DSR) in four increasingly complex Doom scenarios, with particularly strong results in the most challenging Battle scenarios (D3 and D4). The authors further claim that their approach generalizes well across environments and goals, and that the vectorial measurement signal provides a richer supervisory signal than traditional scalar rewards.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes a significant methodological claim but fails to provide rigorous evidence to support it, particularly in comparison to established baselines under fair training conditions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main claim is that vectorial measurement prediction outperforms scalar reward-based RL. However, the evidence provided is methodologically flawed in several critical ways:

First, the training conditions for the baselines are not comparable. The paper trains DFP for 50 million steps (20 days at 2.5M steps/day), while DSR is only trained for 10 days (1 million steps). A3C trains much faster (5M steps/day), but the paper doesn't provide a fair comparison by training all methods to the same number of steps. This is a fundamental flaw in the experimental design, as the performance gap could be largely explained by differences in training time rather than methodological superiority.

Second, the paper fails to properly address the question of whether the measurements themselves constitute a form of "extraneous supervision" that would not be available in a real-world setting. In the Doom environment, the measurements (health, ammo, frags) are provided by the game engine - essentially the same way rewards are provided in standard RL. The paper's claim that it "trains on raw experience without extraneous supervision" is misleading, as the measurements are equally "extraneous" as a reward signal.

Third, the paper does not provide sufficient theoretical justification for why vectorial prediction should outperform scalar reward-based methods. The authors cite historical work by Jordan & Rumelhart (1992) but fail to properly contextualize why their specific approach would work better than standard RL in complex environments. The paper acknowledges that TD learning has dominated the field for good reason (Sutton, 1995), but provides no convincing argument for why their approach would overcome these challenges.

Fourth, the paper's claim that their method "generalizes across environments and goals" is not thoroughly validated. The generalization experiments (Table 2) show some improvement, but the paper doesn't demonstrate that the method works in truly novel environments beyond the Doom test set, which is a key requirement for generalization claims in RL.

Finally, the paper's claim that their method "outperforms the second best submission by more than 50%" in the Visual Doom AI Competition is misleading. The second-best submission (Lample & Chaplot, 2016) used "reward shaping and extra supervision from the game engine," which the paper claims to avoid. However, the paper itself uses measurements that are essentially equivalent to reward shaping, so the comparison is not meaningful.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Re-run all experiments with comparable training times for all methods (e.g., train all methods for 50 million steps) to ensure a fair comparison.

2. Clarify whether the measurements used in their approach are truly "natural" or constitute a form of extraneous supervision. If they are equivalent to reward signals, the paper should reframe its contribution as a specific implementation of reward-based learning rather than a fundamental departure from RL.

3. Provide a theoretical analysis explaining why vectorial prediction would outperform scalar reward-based methods in complex environments, rather than relying solely on empirical results.

4. Demonstrate generalization to environments beyond the Doom platform to support claims about broader applicability.

5. Include ablation studies that test the impact of different measurement dimensions and temporal offsets to better understand what aspects of their approach are truly beneficial.

## Questions for Authors

1. How do you justify the claim that the measurement stream provides "rich and temporally dense supervision" when the measurements themselves are essentially equivalent to a reward signal in standard RL? Isn't the measurement stream just a different form of reward?

2. Why does the paper claim "no human gameplay is used" and "the model trains on raw experience" when the measurements (health, ammo, frags) are provided by the game engine and are essentially a form of extraneous supervision?

3. How do you address the fact that the training time for DFP (20 days) is significantly longer than for A3C (10 days for 50M steps), yet the paper claims DFP outperforms A3C? Would A3C achieve similar performance if trained for 20 days?

4. Can you provide evidence that the measurements used in your approach would be available in a real-world sensorimotor control task (e.g., a physical robot), where health and ammo measurements would not be directly observable?

5. Why does the paper cite "sharp negative examples" for TD learning (Bertsekas, 2010) but not address how these might affect the proposed approach?
