

# Review

## Summary of Contribution
The paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control by framing reinforcement learning as supervised learning where the agent predicts future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) rather than maximizing scalar rewards. The authors argue that the temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides a richer supervisory signal than traditional sparse rewards, enabling effective learning from raw experience. They evaluate their "Direct Future Prediction" (DFP) approach on the Doom game environment, reporting superior performance over DQN, A3C, and DSR baselines, particularly in complex scenarios with multiple measurements. The paper also demonstrates generalization across environments and the ability to adapt to new goals at test time.

## Decision
Reject

## Key Reasons for Rejection
1. The paper contains multiple critical technical inconsistencies that undermine its claims.
2. The experimental results lack sufficient precision and scientific rigor to support the strong claims made.

## Supporting Arguments

The paper's core claim—that predicting future measurements provides a superior learning signal to scalar rewards—is undermined by multiple technical inconsistencies. First, the paper repeatedly refers to "vectorial measurements" while in all experiments, the measurements (health, ammunition, frags) are scalar values. The authors claim "the measurement stream has lower dimensionality" but provide no justification for why these three scalars constitute a "vector" in the mathematical sense. This fundamental terminology error weakens the paper's central premise.

Second, the paper states "the agent has access to eight sub-actions" but fails to specify what these actions are, and then claims "any combination of these sub-actions can be used, resulting in possible actions" without providing the number of possible actions. This omission makes it impossible to evaluate the action space properly.

Third, the paper's claim that DFP "outperforms A3C at test time by a factor of 6 in D3" is ambiguous and scientifically unsound. Is this a 6x improvement in performance (e.g., 60% to 360%) or a 6 percentage point improvement? The paper uses inconsistent terminology throughout, switching between "factor of 6" and "25 percentage point advantage" without clear definitions.

Fourth, the paper claims to have "won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition... by more than 50%," but provides no exact numerical results. The authors state the second-place submission "employed a substantially more complex model and additional supervision during training," but fail to provide the actual performance metrics of the winning model versus the second-place model.

The paper also contains numerous minor but pervasive formatting inconsistencies: "threedimensional" (without space) appears multiple times, inconsistent citation styles (some with "et al.", some without), and unclear references to figures/tables. While these might seem minor, they collectively indicate a lack of attention to detail that undermines the paper's scientific credibility.

## Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Clarify the terminology: Either explain why scalar measurements constitute a "vector" in their framework, or revise the terminology to match their implementation (e.g., "multiple scalar measurements" instead of "vectorial measurements").

2. Provide precise experimental results: Replace ambiguous statements like "factor of 6" with exact performance metrics (e.g., "6.0× higher average frags per episode").

3. Complete the description of the action space: Specify the eight sub-actions and the total number of possible actions.

4. Include exact competition results: Provide the exact win margin in the Visual Doom AI Competition (e.g., "DFP achieved 32.1 frags per episode versus the second-place model's 21.3").

5. Address the theoretical justification: Explain why the measurement stream provides better supervision than scalar rewards, beyond empirical results.

## Clarifying Questions

1. If the measurements are scalar values (health, ammo, frags), how do they constitute a "vector" as claimed in the paper? If they're not a vector, how should the paper's title and central claim be revised?

2. The paper claims "the agent has access to eight sub-actions" but doesn't specify what they are. Could the authors clarify the action space and provide the total number of possible actions?

3. The paper states "the presented approach won the Full Deathmatch track... by more than 50%." What was the exact performance difference (e.g., average frags per episode) between the winning model and the second-place submission?

4. The paper claims "the model generalizes across environments" but doesn't provide sufficient evidence. How much does performance degrade when moving from the training environment to a novel environment with different textures?

These questions are essential to verify the paper's claims and ensure the results are scientifically rigorous. Without clear answers, the paper's claims cannot be properly evaluated.
