

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control in complex 3D environments by reframing the problem as supervised learning rather than reinforcement learning. The key insight is that the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides rich supervisory signals, allowing the agent to predict future measurements rather than relying on sparse scalar rewards. The authors demonstrate that their "Direct Future Prediction" (DFP) approach outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods on challenging tasks in immersive 3D environments, particularly when multiple measurements are available. The approach also supports learning without a fixed goal and generalizes to dynamically specified goals at test time. The paper presents empirical evidence from experiments in the ViZDoom platform and reports winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution by demonstrating that supervised learning approaches can effectively handle sensorimotor control in complex environments with rich feedback, outperforming traditional RL methods. The experimental results are rigorously presented and provide compelling evidence for the approach's superiority.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper clearly addresses a significant challenge in RL: learning from raw sensory input in complex environments. The problem statement is well-defined, and the approach is well-motivated by a thorough literature review that situates the work within the broader context of RL and supervised learning. The authors effectively argue that when dense multidimensional feedback is available (as in the ViZDoom environment), supervised learning can be more effective than traditional RL.

The technical approach is sound and well-executed. The paper's core idea of predicting future measurements rather than maximizing a scalar reward is logically consistent and supported by the experiments. The architecture, including the expectation and action streams, is well-justified and enables the model to learn fine differences between actions.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and scientifically rigorous. The authors compare against three strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across four increasingly difficult scenarios, demonstrating consistent outperformance by DFP, particularly in complex tasks with richer measurement streams. The generalization experiments across environments and the ability to handle dynamically specified goals provide strong evidence for the approach's robustness and flexibility. The winning performance in the Visual Doom AI Competition provides additional compelling evidence.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by demonstrating that a simple supervised learning approach can outperform sophisticated RL methods in the right setting, challenging the conventional wisdom that RL is necessary for sensorimotor control.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
While the paper is strong, the following suggestions could further strengthen it:

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach. For example, how does the method scale to environments with significantly higher-dimensional measurement streams? What are the specific conditions under which the approach would not be applicable?

2. The paper mentions that the model is "purely reactive" with "no explicit facilities for memory," and suggests integrating memory-based models as future work. A brief discussion of why the current approach works well without explicit memory (compared to methods that use LSTM or other memory mechanisms) would be valuable.

3. The ablation study (Table 4) shows the importance of multiple measurements and multiple temporal offsets, but the paper could provide more insight into why these factors matter. A brief theoretical explanation or analysis would strengthen the empirical findings.

4. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how the goal vector is parameterized during training and testing, particularly regarding the scaling of the goal vector and how it relates to the measurement scales.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper reports that DFP outperformed the second-best submission in the Visual Doom AI Competition by more than 50%. Could the authors provide more specific details about the second-best submission's architecture and training approach, particularly regarding the "reward shaping and extra supervision from the game engine" mentioned?

2. In the experiments, DFP was trained with multiple measurements (health, ammunition, frag count) but evaluated primarily on frag count. How would the performance change if the evaluation metric was health or ammunition instead? The paper mentions that predicting multiple measurements improves performance even when evaluated on a single measurement, but a more direct analysis of this would be helpful.

3. The paper states that the measurement stream provides "rich and temporally dense supervision." Could the authors provide a quantitative analysis of how the density of supervision in their approach compares to the typical RL setting (e.g., number of supervision signals per episode)?

4. In the architecture section, the paper mentions that the action stream is normalized to have zero mean. What was the empirical impact of this normalization, and would the approach work without it?
