

# Peer Review: "Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control that reframes reinforcement learning as a supervised learning problem. The authors argue that the temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides rich supervision, allowing agents to predict future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) given current sensory input, measurements, and a goal. This "Direct Future Prediction" (DFP) approach eliminates the need for scalar rewards, enables training without fixed goals, and supports flexible deployment to dynamically specified goals at test time. The authors demonstrate that DFP outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods (DQN, A3C, DSR) on increasingly complex Doom-based scenarios, with particularly significant gains in environments with richer measurement streams. They further demonstrate strong generalization across environments and goals, and validate their approach by winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a compelling contribution by providing a simple yet effective alternative to traditional RL that addresses key challenges in sensorimotor control. The approach is well-motivated, the experiments are rigorous, and the results are impressive.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

The paper makes several strong contributions:

First, it successfully challenges the dominant RL paradigm by demonstrating that supervised learning can be effective for sensorimotor control when dense, multidimensional feedback is available. This is well-positioned in the literature, as the authors properly cite prior work by Jordan & Rumelhart (1992) and Sutton (1988) that suggested similar approaches could be advantageous in certain settings. The paper's key insight—that the measurement stream provides richer supervision than sparse scalar rewards—aligns well with empirical evidence from developmental psychology (Adolph & Berger, 2006).

Second, the experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The four Doom scenarios progressively increase in complexity, allowing the authors to demonstrate that DFP's advantages become more pronounced as the environment and task complexity increase. The results showing DFP's superiority over baselines (particularly in the Battle scenarios where it outperforms A3C by factors of 6 and 2.5) are compelling. The ablation study (Table 4) provides strong evidence that vectorial measurements and multi-temporal predictions are key to the approach's success.

Third, the generalization experiments (Tables 2 and 3) are particularly convincing. The ability to train without a fixed goal and generalize to new goals at test time is a significant advantage over traditional RL methods, and the results demonstrate this capability robustly.

Finally, the competition results provide independent validation of the approach's effectiveness, as the DFP model won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition by more than 50% over the second-best submission.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, the following improvements would enhance its quality:

1. **Clarify the training procedure**: The paper mentions using a small experience memory and updating the predictor after every k new experiences. This is an important detail that affects reproducibility. The authors should provide more specific details about k, the size of the experience memory, and why this approach was chosen over standard experience replay. The Appendix should contain complete implementation details.

2. **Address the DSR comparison more thoroughly**: The paper states that DSR was trained for only 10 days (1 million steps) compared to 50 million steps for the other methods. This is a significant disadvantage that should be acknowledged, and the authors should clarify whether they believe DSR's performance would improve with more training time.

3. **Clarify the "50% better" claim**: The paper states the DFP model "outperformed the second best submission by more than 50%." This should be quantified (e.g., 50% more frags, 50% higher win rate) for scientific precision.

4. **Discuss limitations more thoroughly**: The paper briefly mentions that the model is purely reactive with no explicit memory. The authors should discuss how this limitation might affect performance in tasks requiring long-term planning or where context from past observations is crucial.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In Table 3, the model trained with goal vector sampled from [-1, 1] (regime c) performs worse than regime b (uniform [0,1]) on the main task (first row). However, regime c generalizes better to new goals. How do you explain this trade-off between main task performance and generalization ability?

2. The paper mentions that "the use of vectorial measurements instead of a scalar reward is beneficial." How would the authors characterize the relative importance of the different measurements (health, ammo, frags) for different tasks? For example, would the approach work equally well if only one measurement were provided?

3. The architecture (Figure 1) uses a normalization layer on the action stream to force the average predictions to zero. This seems to be a key innovation. Could you provide more intuition about why this normalization is important for learning?

4. The paper claims that "the model trains on raw experience, by interacting with the environment without extraneous supervision." However, the agent uses a fixed goal during training (in the single goal regime). How would the authors handle the case where the agent has no prior knowledge of any goal at all during training?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating that sensorimotor control can be effectively framed as supervised learning when rich measurement streams are available. The empirical results are strong, the approach is novel, and the implications for future work are significant. With the suggested minor revisions, this paper would make an excellent contribution to ICLR.
