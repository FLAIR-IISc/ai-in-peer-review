

# Review of "Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control by reframing reinforcement learning as supervised learning using measurement streams rather than scalar rewards. The authors argue that the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides rich supervisory signals, reducing sensorimotor control to a supervised learning problem. They demonstrate that their approach, which predicts future measurements given current sensory input, measurements, and a goal, outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods on complex 3D environments in the Doom game. The paper makes a compelling case for using vectorial measurements instead of scalar rewards and demonstrates generalization across environments and goals.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper contains methodological inconsistencies and lacks proper statistical reporting that undermine confidence in the results.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's core contribution has merit, but several methodological inconsistencies and omissions prevent me from recommending acceptance.

First, there is a critical dimensionality mismatch in the model description. The authors define f = 〈mt+τ1 −mt, ..., mt+τn −mt〉 (Section 3), which has dimensionality n (number of temporal offsets). However, in their experiments, they use three measurements (ammo, health, frags) and claim to predict "multiple measurements," yet the dimensionality of f should be 3×n (not n), which contradicts the paper's description. The paper states "The dimensionality of pat matches the dimensionality of f and g," but doesn't clarify this crucial detail. This inconsistency makes it difficult to understand the actual model architecture.

Second, the paper lacks proper statistical reporting. It mentions "each data point is computed from 3×50,000 steps of testing" and "each presented curve averages information from three independent training runs," but provides no standard deviations or confidence intervals. Without these, it's impossible to assess the statistical significance of the reported performance differences, particularly the "25 percentage point advantage" in the Navigation scenario.

Third, there are inconsistencies in describing the measurements across scenarios. The paper states for D1 and D2 "the only measurement provided to the agent in these scenarios is health," but for D3 and D4, they mention "the agent is provided with three measurements: health, ammunition, and frag count." However, D1 and D2 are supposed to be simpler versions of D3 and D4, yet they don't include the same measurements. This creates confusion about what the model actually sees in different scenarios.

Fourth, the ablation study (Table 4) does not properly test the claim that "predicting multiple measurements significantly improves the performance of the learned model, even when it is evaluated by only one of those measurements." The table only compares the full model (predicting three measurements at six temporal offsets) with a model that predicts only one measurement (frags), but doesn't show a model that predicts multiple measurements but is evaluated on only one measurement.

Finally, the paper claims their model won "the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition" by "more than 50%," but doesn't provide sufficient detail about the competition setup or how the 50% margin was calculated. Without knowing the exact metrics and how the comparison was made, this claim is difficult to verify.

## 4. Additional Feedback

The authors should address these issues to strengthen the paper:

1. Clarify the dimensionality of f and how it relates to the number of measurements and temporal offsets. Provide a precise mathematical description of the model's input and output dimensions.

2. Add statistical measures (standard deviations, confidence intervals) to all performance metrics to demonstrate statistical significance.

3. Ensure consistency in describing the measurements across all scenarios. Either D1/D2 should include the same measurements as D3/D4, or the authors should clearly explain why they differ.

4. Conduct a proper ablation study for the multiple measurements claim. Specifically, show results for a model that predicts multiple measurements but is evaluated only on one measurement.

5. Provide more details about the Visual Doom AI Competition, including how the 50% margin was calculated and the specific metrics used for comparison.

6. Fix minor formatting inconsistencies throughout the paper, including inconsistent hyphenation of "three-dimensional" (sometimes written as "threedimensional" without a hyphen) and reference formatting issues in the bibliography.

7. Clarify the ε-decay schedule for the ε-greedy policy in Section 3.1, as this is important for reproducibility.

These issues collectively indicate a lack of attention to detail that prevents me from being confident in the paper's claims. While the approach is promising, the methodological inconsistencies and lack of statistical rigor make it difficult to assess the true contribution of the work.
