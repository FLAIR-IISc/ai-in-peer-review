- Proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): reframing sensorimotor control as supervised prediction of future measurements from sensory input, current measurements, and a goal vector.  
- Shows DFP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods in complex 3D environments and generalizes across environments and goals.  
- Recommendation: Accept — novel, well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated with strong empirical evidence.  
- Tackles two core RL challenges: raw sensorimotor control in 3D and learning general skills for dynamically specified goals.  
- Strong literature placement and motivation, arguing temporally dense measurement streams offer richer supervision than sparse scalar rewards.  
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation against DQN, A3C, and DSR across four Doom scenarios with large performance gains (notably 6x and 2.5x in hardest scenarios).  
- Ablation studies validate the importance of vectorial measurements and predicting at multiple temporal offsets.  
- Demonstrates practical success: won Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch, beating second place by >50% without extra supervision.  
- Evidence of generalization: models transfer between environments and support goal-agnostic training/adaptation at test time.  
- Suggests adding a formal theoretical analysis to explain why direct future prediction outperforms traditional RL.  
- Recommends a more thorough discussion of limitations (discrete actions, lack of memory, scaling to continuous actions and real-world inputs).  
- Requests comparison of computational efficiency and training requirements versus baselines to support claims of simplicity.  
- Asks how the approach would change if full future sensory prediction were feasible and whether measurement streams would become redundant.  
- Asks for insight on why predicting multiple measurements improves performance on single-measurement tasks (correlation vs. richer state representation).  
- Asks whether any reward shaping or external/game-engine supervision was used during training and how this differs from prior methods.
