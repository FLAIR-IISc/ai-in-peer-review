

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contribution
The paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control that reframes reinforcement learning as supervised learning by predicting future measurements rather than relying on scalar rewards. The authors argue that the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides rich, temporally dense supervision for learning. Their Direct Future Prediction (DFP) method trains a model to predict how different actions will affect future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count), then selects actions that best achieve the current goal. The approach is evaluated on the Visual Doom AI platform across four increasingly complex scenarios, demonstrating consistent performance improvements over state-of-the-art deep RL methods, particularly in complex environments with richer measurement streams. The paper also shows strong generalization across environments and goals, with the DFP model winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition by outperforming the second-best submission by more than 50%.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that a simple supervised learning approach can outperform sophisticated deep RL methods in complex 3D environments. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the approach is well-motivated by the literature and addresses a genuine gap in the field regarding dense multidimensional feedback, and (2) the empirical results are scientifically rigorous and consistently demonstrate advantages over strong baselines across multiple challenging scenarios.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's motivation is exceptionally strong. The authors provide a thorough literature review that clearly positions their work in relation to prior supervised learning approaches (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992), temporal-difference learning (Sutton, 1988), and related work on vector-valued feedback and parameterized goals (Schaul et al., 2015). They compellingly argue that traditional RL's reliance on sparse scalar rewards is inappropriate for environments with dense, multidimensional feedback, which better models natural sensorimotor learning. The theoretical foundation for their approach is sound and well-justified.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and scientifically rigorous. The authors test their approach across four increasingly difficult scenarios in the Doom environment, comparing against three strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across multiple metrics. The results consistently show DFP outperforming baselines, with particularly significant gains in complex scenarios (2.5x better than A3C in Battle 2). The ablation study (Table 4) provides convincing evidence that vectorial measurements provide a significant advantage over scalar rewards. The generalization experiments across environments and goals further demonstrate the robustness of the approach. The Visual Doom AI Competition result provides independent validation of the approach's effectiveness.

The paper's methodology is well-executed. The authors carefully design experiments to isolate the effects of different components (e.g., vectorial measurements vs. scalar reward), report results with proper statistics (averaging across multiple training runs), and provide sufficient implementation details for reproducibility. The training and testing procedures are clearly described, and the comparison to baselines is fair and thorough.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with Schaul et al. (2015), as the authors mention being "most closely related" but do not precisely clarify how their specific formulation advances this prior work. A clearer distinction would strengthen the contribution statement.

- While the paper mentions that the model is purely reactive, it would be helpful to discuss the computational cost of the approach relative to the baselines in more detail. Understanding whether the performance gains come with significant computational overhead would be valuable for practitioners.

- The paper states that the model "trains on raw experience, by interacting with the environment without extraneous supervision." However, the goal vector is specified during training (either fixed or randomized). Clarifying how this differs from traditional RL (where the reward function is also defined in advance) would strengthen the paper's claims about the novelty of the approach.

## Questions for Authors
1. In the ablation study (Table 4), predicting multiple measurements improves performance even when evaluated only on one measurement. Could the authors elaborate on why this occurs? Is it primarily due to the richer supervision signal, or do the additional measurements provide useful intermediate representations that improve learning?

2. The Visual Doom AI Competition result shows DFP winning by >50% over the second-best submission, which used additional supervision from the game engine. Could the authors provide more insight into how much of this performance gap is attributable to the core DFP approach versus the specific training regime (D4-tx-L) used in the competition?

3. The paper claims the approach "supports training without a fixed goal and pursuing dynamically specified goals at test time." However, the goal vector is still specified during training (either fixed or randomized). Could the authors clarify how this differs from traditional RL, where the reward function (which can be viewed as a goal specification) is also defined in advance?
