

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

The authors present a novel approach to sensorimotor control that reframes reinforcement learning as a supervised learning problem. Instead of relying on a sparse scalar reward signal, their method uses a stream of sensory input (e.g., raw visual data) and a stream of lower-dimensional measurements (e.g., health, ammunition levels) to predict the effect of actions on future measurements. The key insight is that the interlocked temporal structure of these streams provides rich, temporally dense supervision, enabling effective learning from raw experience without extraneous data. The authors demonstrate that this approach, called "Direct Future Prediction" (DFP), outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods on complex 3D environments (Doom), particularly when multiple measurements are available. They also show that DFP generalizes across environments, handles dynamically specified goals at test time, and achieves superior performance in the Visual Doom AI Competition, winning the Full Deathmatch track by over 50% over the second-place submission.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating that a simple supervised learning approach can outperform sophisticated deep RL methods in complex sensorimotor control tasks. The key reasons for acceptance are:

1. The paper presents a well-motivated, conceptually clear alternative to traditional RL that addresses important limitations in the field (sparse rewards, difficulty with raw sensory input).

2. The empirical results are robust, scientifically rigorous, and demonstrate clear advantages of the proposed approach across multiple challenging tasks, with strong performance in a real-world competition.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's core contribution is compelling and well-supported. The authors successfully address a fundamental limitation of current RL approaches: the sparsity of reward signals. By recognizing that the temporal structure of measurements provides rich supervision, they transform a challenging RL problem into a more tractable supervised learning problem. This insight is well-motivated by the literature and aligns with earlier work (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992) that suggested supervised learning could be advantageous when dense feedback is available.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors systematically compare their approach against three strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across four progressively more complex Doom scenarios. The results consistently show DFP outperforming baselines, particularly in scenarios with richer measurements (D3 and D4), which directly supports their claim that vectorial measurements provide a better training signal than scalar rewards. The ablation study in Table 4 provides strong evidence that both multi-measurement prediction and multi-temporal offset prediction are beneficial.

The Visual Doom AI Competition results are particularly impressive. Winning by over 50% against a strong baseline that used additional supervision (ground-truth knowledge of enemy presence) demonstrates the practical value of the approach. This is not just a theoretical contribution but a method that achieves state-of-the-art performance in a real-world challenge.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with clear connections to related work on predictive representations, multi-objective decision-making, and parameterized goals. The authors correctly identify the gap their work fills: most RL approaches have focused on scalar rewards, while their method leverages the rich, multidimensional feedback naturally available in many environments.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few improvements could enhance its impact:

1. **Clarify the theoretical foundation**: The paper mentions that the approach "reduces sensorimotor control to supervised learning" but doesn't provide a theoretical justification for why this works better than RL in the settings they study. A brief discussion of how the temporal structure of measurements enables more effective learning would strengthen the paper.

2. **Address potential limitations**: The paper focuses on environments where measurements are explicitly provided. In real-world applications, these measurements might not be readily available. A brief discussion of how this approach could be extended to learn measurements from raw sensory input (e.g., using self-supervised learning) would make the contribution more broadly applicable.

3. **Expand the discussion of related work**: While the paper references relevant literature, it could more explicitly position itself against recent work on predictive state representations (PSRs) and successor representations (e.g., Schaul et al., 2015; Kulkarni et al., 2016b), which also aim to predict future states.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. In the Visual Doom competition, the second-place submission (Lample & Chaplot, 2016) used ground-truth enemy presence information during training. How would you expect your approach to perform if the enemy presence information was not available during training (i.e., only raw visual input was provided)? Would the advantage of your method over their approach be larger or smaller in this setting?

2. The paper demonstrates that models trained with randomized goals generalize better than those trained with fixed goals. However, the paper doesn't explore whether there's an optimal distribution for sampling goal vectors during training. Could you discuss whether there might be a more principled way to sample goals beyond uniform random sampling?

3. The paper mentions that the measurement stream provides "rich and temporally dense supervision" compared to sparse scalar rewards. Could you elaborate on how this dense supervision specifically helps with the learning process (e.g., faster convergence, more stable learning, better exploration) compared to sparse rewards?

4. The architecture uses a normalization layer to ensure the action stream predictions average to zero. How sensitive is the performance to the specific implementation of this normalization layer? Would the approach work with a different normalization strategy or without it entirely?

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of reinforcement learning, with a clear insight, thorough evaluation, and impressive results. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the ICLR proceedings.
