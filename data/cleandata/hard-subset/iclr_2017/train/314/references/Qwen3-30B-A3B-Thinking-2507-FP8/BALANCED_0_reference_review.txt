

# Review of "Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control that reframes reinforcement learning as a supervised learning problem. Instead of relying on scalar rewards, the authors use a stream of multidimensional measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) that provide dense temporal supervision. The core idea is that agents can predict future measurements based on current sensory input, measurements, and a goal, then select actions that optimize these predictions. The approach is evaluated on the Visual Doom AI Competition environments, with claims of outperforming state-of-the-art RL methods, demonstrating generalization across environments, and supporting goal-agnostic training.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The key reasons are that the experimental comparisons lack sufficient rigor and the paper fails to properly contextualize its contributions within the broader RL literature.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main claim—that their supervised learning approach outperforms state-of-the-art RL methods—is not adequately supported by the experimental evidence. Several critical issues undermine this claim:

First, the comparison to DSR (Deep Successor Representations) is fundamentally unfair. DSR was trained for only 1 million steps (10 days) while DQN, A3C, and DFP were trained for 50 million steps. The authors acknowledge this discrepancy but do not address its implications for the results. This is a major methodological flaw that invalidates the comparison.

Second, the paper fails to compare against more recent and relevant baselines that were becoming standard at the time (e.g., PPO, A2C, or other efficient on-policy methods). Instead, they rely on older methods like DQN (2015) and A3C (2016), which were already being surpassed by newer approaches by the time of their submission.

Third, the competition claim lacks sufficient detail. The paper states their model "won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition" and "outperformed the second best submission by more than 50%," but provides no context about the number of competitors, the specific metrics used, or how this compares to other submissions. Without this information, the claim lacks scientific rigor.

Fourth, the ablation study (Table 4) does not isolate the contribution of the vectorial measurement approach. The performance gains could be due to the specific network architecture rather than the core idea of using multiple measurements.

Finally, the paper lacks discussion of limitations. For example, how does the approach perform when the measurement stream is sparse or ambiguous? What happens when the goal cannot be expressed as a linear combination of measurements?

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen the paper, I recommend the following changes:

1. **Fairer experimental comparisons**: Train all methods for equivalent numbers of steps and report results on standardized benchmarks. Compare against more recent methods like PPO or A2C, which were becoming standard by 2016.

2. **More detailed competition results**: Provide specific metrics (e.g., average frags, win rate), number of competitors, and how the results compare to other submissions. Explain why their approach outperformed the second-best submission.

3. **Better ablation study**: Isolate the contribution of the vectorial measurement approach by keeping the network architecture constant and only varying the prediction target (scalar reward vs. vector of measurements).

4. **Limitations and failure cases**: Discuss scenarios where the approach might not work well (e.g., when measurements are not informative or when goals cannot be expressed as linear combinations).

5. **Theoretical grounding**: While the paper is primarily empirical, a brief discussion of why vectorial measurements provide better supervision could strengthen the theoretical motivation.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper claims to have "outperformed the second best submission by more than 50%." Can you provide the exact performance metrics (e.g., average frags) for both your model and the second-best submission?

2. Why was DSR only trained for 1 million steps while other methods were trained for 50 million steps? Was this a limitation of the DSR implementation or a deliberate choice to make the comparison fair?

3. How does your approach compare to more recent methods like PPO or A2C, which were becoming standard in RL at the time of your submission?

4. Can you provide examples of environments where your approach would fail due to limitations in the measurement stream or goal specification?

This paper has a compelling core idea, but the lack of rigorous experimental validation and contextualization within the broader RL literature prevents it from making a convincing contribution to the field. With significant revisions to address the experimental weaknesses, the paper could potentially make a valuable contribution.
