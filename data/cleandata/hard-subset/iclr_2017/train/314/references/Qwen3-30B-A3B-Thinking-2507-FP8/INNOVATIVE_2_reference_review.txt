

# Review of "Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control"

## 1. Summary of Contribution
This paper introduces a bold and novel conceptual shift in sensorimotor control by reframing reinforcement learning as a supervised learning problem. Instead of using the traditional scalar reward signal, the authors propose using a stream of vectorial measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) as the supervisory signal. The core insight is that the interlocked temporal structure of sensory input and measurement streams provides rich, dense supervision that enables the agent to predict future measurements given current observations and goals. This approach, termed "Direct Future Prediction" (DFP), eliminates the need for scalar rewards and supports training without fixed goals while enabling flexible goal specification at test time. The authors demonstrate significant empirical success, showing DFP outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods across increasingly complex 3D environments, with a 50% margin of victory in the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

## 2. Decision
Accept

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's core contribution—using vectorial measurements instead of scalar rewards as the primary supervisory signal for sensorimotor control—is a significant conceptual departure from standard reinforcement learning that merits acceptance. This approach is both novel and bold, directly challenging the foundational assumptions of RL. The authors have provided strong empirical validation through comprehensive experiments on the Doom environment, including a competition win that demonstrates real-world applicability. The paper successfully addresses key challenges in complex 3D environments where traditional RL struggles, particularly showing that vectorial feedback provides a substantially richer training signal than scalar rewards.

The paper's novelty is particularly compelling because it reframes a fundamental aspect of RL without requiring complex model architecture changes. The DFP approach is conceptually simple yet produces dramatic performance improvements in challenging tasks, which aligns perfectly with the ICLR reviewer's emphasis on bold, novel ideas over methodological perfection. The results in the Battle and Battle 2 scenarios (D3 and D4) are especially impressive, with DFP outperforming A3C by factors of 6 and 2.5, respectively—a level of improvement that is rare in the literature.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. A more detailed discussion of how this approach relates to predictive state representations (Sutton et al., 2011) and the broader literature on predicting future states in dynamical systems. The connection to these established ideas would strengthen the theoretical grounding.

2. An expanded analysis of the computational efficiency of the approach. While the paper mentions training speed (e.g., "50 million steps" for DFP), it would be valuable to compare training time per episode or per step with baselines, especially since the paper emphasizes practical application.

3. A more thorough exploration of the relationship between the dimensionality of the measurement stream and performance. The paper shows that vectorial feedback works better than scalar rewards, but a systematic analysis of how performance scales with the number of measurements would provide deeper insights.

4. Clarification on the limitations of the approach. While the paper demonstrates strong results, it would be helpful to understand where DFP might struggle compared to traditional RL (e.g., in environments with sparse measurements or where goals cannot be expressed in terms of measurements).

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper mentions that the approach supports "training without a fixed goal," but it's unclear how the agent learns to distinguish between beneficial and harmful measurements (e.g., health vs. frag count). How does the model learn that health is desirable while frag count might not be (e.g., in the pacifist goal)? 

2. In the Visual Doom competition, the second-best submission used additional supervision from the game engine (ground-truth enemy presence). How would your approach perform if the baselines had access to similar additional supervision, and would the DFP approach still maintain its advantage?

3. The paper claims that the approach is "simple and elegant," but the architecture (Figure 1) includes multiple specialized network components (perception, measurement, goal modules; expectation and action streams). How does this complexity compare to the complexity of the baselines (DQN, A3C), and what specific architectural innovations enable the performance gains?

4. The paper demonstrates strong generalization across environments (Table 2), but it would be valuable to see how the approach performs when the measurement streams change significantly (e.g., introducing new measurements not seen during training).
