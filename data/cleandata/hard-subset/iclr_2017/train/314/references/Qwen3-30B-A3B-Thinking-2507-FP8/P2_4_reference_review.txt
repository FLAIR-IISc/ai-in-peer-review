

# Review: "Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control in Complex Environments"

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control that fundamentally shifts from the traditional reward-based reinforcement learning framework to a supervised learning framework based on predicting future measurements. The authors propose that rather than optimizing for a sparse scalar reward, agents should learn to predict the effects of actions on a stream of meaningful measurements (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) that provide rich, temporally dense supervision. The core contribution is a simple yet effective model that predicts future measurements based on current sensory input, measurements, and a goal vector. The authors demonstrate that this approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods in complex 3D environments, particularly when vectorial measurements are used instead of scalar rewards, and that the trained models generalize well across environments and goals.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field with a novel approach that is well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated, and backed by compelling empirical evidence. The key reasons for acceptance are the paper's strong theoretical justification for departing from traditional RL and its impressive empirical results across multiple challenging tasks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in addressing the three key review questions:

**1. Specific problem tackled**: The authors clearly identify two key challenges in RL: (1) sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments, and (2) acquisition of general skills for dynamically specified goals. They propose a solution that reframes sensorimotor control as a supervised learning problem where the agent predicts future measurements based on current observations, actions, and goals.

**2. Motivation and literature placement**: The paper provides an excellent literature review that situates their work precisely. They cite Jordan & Rumelhart (1992) who argued that SL can be advantageous when dense feedback is available, and they carefully distinguish their work from related approaches like Schaul et al. (2015) and Monte Carlo methods. The motivation is compelling - they argue that the temporally dense stream of measurements provides richer supervision than the sparse scalar rewards common in traditional RL, which is particularly relevant for immersive environments like Doom.

**3. Support for claims**: The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and scientifically rigorous. The authors compare their approach (DFP) against three strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across four progressively challenging Doom scenarios. The results are striking: DFP significantly outperforms baselines, particularly in complex scenarios (D3 and D4) where it achieves performance advantages of 6x and 2.5x over A3C. The ablation studies convincingly demonstrate the importance of vectorial measurements (vs. scalar rewards) and predicting at multiple temporal offsets. Most impressively, their model won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming the second-best submission by over 50% without using additional supervision.

The paper also provides strong evidence for generalization: models trained on one environment generalize well to others, and the approach supports goal-agnostic training where models can adapt to new goals at test time without retraining.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these suggestions would further enhance its quality:

1. **Theoretical justification**: The paper could benefit from a more formal analysis of why direct future prediction works better than traditional RL. A simple theoretical argument about the relationship between the prediction task and the underlying dynamics would strengthen the motivation.

2. **Limitations discussion**: The paper acknowledges limitations (discrete actions, no explicit memory) but could more thoroughly discuss the approach's limitations in real-world applications, such as how it would scale to continuous action spaces or more complex sensory inputs.

3. **Computational efficiency**: While the paper mentions training speed, it would be valuable to compare the computational requirements of DFP with baselines, especially since the authors claim their approach is "simpler" than competitors like Lample & Chaplot (2016).

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "predicting full sensory streams is beyond our capabilities" but cites recent work by Kalchbrenner et al. (2016) and van den Oord et al. (2016) on this topic. How would the approach change if future sensory prediction were feasible? Would the measurement stream become redundant?

2. The ablation study (Table 4) shows that predicting multiple measurements improves performance even when evaluated on a single measurement. Can the authors provide more insight into why this is the case? Is it because the measurements are correlated, or does predicting multiple measurements provide a better overall representation of the state?

3. The paper mentions that the approach was "trained on raw experience" without human gameplay, but the paper doesn't clarify whether any form of reward shaping or external supervision was used during training. How does this approach differ from methods like the one by Lample & Chaplot (2016), which used "extra supervision from the game engine"?

This paper makes a compelling contribution to RL that challenges conventional wisdom while delivering strong empirical results. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and rigorously evaluated. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the ICLR program.
