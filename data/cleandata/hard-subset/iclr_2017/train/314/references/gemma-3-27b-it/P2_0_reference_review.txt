## ICLR Review

**1. Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem rather than traditional reinforcement learning. The core idea is to leverage the rich, temporally dense information present in simultaneous sensory and measurement streams to predict the effect of actions on future measurements. This circumvents the need for sparse, scalar rewards, enabling learning from raw experience and facilitating adaptation to dynamically specified goals. The authors demonstrate compelling results in the Doom environment, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL methods, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a strong contribution with a clear, well-motivated approach and convincing empirical results. The success in the Visual Doom AI Competition is particularly noteworthy.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The framing of sensorimotor control as a supervised learning problem, leveraging interlocked sensory and measurement streams, is a refreshing and well-motivated departure from traditional RL. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of sparse reward signals in complex environments and position their approach as a solution.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are impressive.  Outperforming existing deep RL methods across multiple Doom scenarios, and *winning* a competitive benchmark, provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The ablation studies further strengthen the claim that vectorial feedback and multi-step prediction are beneficial.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and methodology. The architecture description (Figure 1) and experimental setup are detailed enough for reproduction.
* **Generalization and Flexibility:** Demonstrating generalization across environments (D3-tx and D4-tx) and the ability to adapt to dynamically specified goals (Table 3) showcases the robustness and practical value of the approach.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Theoretical Grounding:** While the empirical results are compelling, a more thorough discussion of the theoretical underpinnings would be valuable. Specifically, exploring the relationship to established supervised learning theory (e.g., generalization bounds) and potential convergence properties would strengthen the paper.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  A brief discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., learning rate, network architecture choices) would be helpful. While the ablation studies cover some aspects, a more systematic analysis of the impact of key hyperparameters would be beneficial.
* **Comparison to Imitation Learning:** The paper mentions imitation learning briefly.  A more detailed comparison, highlighting the key differences between the proposed approach and existing imitation learning techniques, would be insightful.  Specifically, how does this approach address the distribution shift problem often encountered in imitation learning?
* **Computational Cost:** While the training speed is mentioned, providing more detailed information on computational resources required (e.g., number of GPUs, training time) would be useful for readers considering implementing this approach.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding generalization (Table 2):** The performance improvement from training in D4 to D4-tx is significant. Is this primarily due to the increased diversity of textures, or are there other factors contributing to this improvement (e.g., the larger network capacity)?
* **Regarding dynamic goals (Table 3):** When the agent is trained with randomized goals, does the distribution of these goals have a noticeable effect on performance?  For instance, is the agent better at adapting to goals similar to those seen during training?
* **Regarding theoretical properties:** Have you investigated any theoretical properties of this approach, such as sample complexity or convergence rates? If so, could you briefly summarize those findings?
* **Regarding scalability:** How well does this approach scale to environments with even higher dimensionality and more complex action spaces? What modifications, if any, would be necessary to address these challenges?
