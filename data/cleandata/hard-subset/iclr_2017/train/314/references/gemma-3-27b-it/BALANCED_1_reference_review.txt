This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem rather than the traditional reward-based reinforcement learning paradigm. The core idea is to leverage the rich, temporally dense information present in the interlocked streams of sensory input and internal measurements to predict the effect of actions on future measurements. This cleverly bypasses the need for sparse reward signals often encountered in complex environments. The authors demonstrate strong performance in the Doom environment, even winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Reject.** While the approach is promising and the experimental results are impressive, the paper lacks sufficient clarity in several areas, particularly regarding the justification for design choices and a more thorough ablation study. The core contribution, while insightful, feels somewhat underexplored, and the presentation doesn't fully convince of its generality or robustness beyond the specific Doom setup.

The primary reason for rejection stems from a lack of detail and justification. For example, the choice of temporal offsets (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, and 32) feels arbitrary. What is the rationale for selecting these specific offsets? How sensitive are the results to different choices of offsets? Similarly, the weighting scheme (0.5, 0.5, 1) for the future measurements is not clearly explained. More importantly, the paper doesn’t adequately discuss *why* predicting measurements – rather than, say, directly predicting actions – is effective. What theoretical considerations underpin this choice? While the results speak for themselves, a deeper understanding of the mechanism at play would significantly strengthen the contribution.

Secondly, while the ablation study examines the importance of vectorial feedback and multiple temporal offsets, it’s limited. A more comprehensive ablation exploring the network architecture (e.g., the expectation/action stream split) or different prediction horizons would be valuable. The comparison to prior work is strong, but the authors don't thoroughly analyze *why* their method outperforms the others. Is it solely due to the richer supervision signal, or are there other factors at play?

Finally, the discussion section highlights potential extensions, which suggests the authors themselves recognize avenues for further work. However, addressing some of these points within the paper would elevate its quality and impact.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Clarify the motivation for predicting measurements:** Expand on why predicting future measurements is a suitable proxy for learning effective control policies. Connect this more explicitly to the idea of internal models and forward dynamics.
*   **Justify design choices:** Provide a more rigorous justification for the choice of temporal offsets and weighting schemes. Explore their sensitivity through ablation studies.
*   **Expand the ablation study:** Include ablations on the network architecture (expectation/action stream) and the impact of different prediction horizons.
*   **Analyze the failure cases:** Discuss scenarios where the approach struggles and potential reasons for the failures.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** Explore any theoretical connections to existing work on supervised learning, prediction error minimization, or information-theoretic approaches to control.
*   **Generalizability beyond Doom:** While the Doom environment is a compelling testbed, the paper would benefit from discussing the potential limitations of the approach and its applicability to other domains.
*   **Elaborate on the goal vector:** Explain the choice of linear combinations for expressing goals. Can other function approximators be used for u(f; g)? What are the trade-offs?
*   **Further details on the competition setup:** Provide more specifics on the visual doom competition. What were the constraints and the exact evaluation metrics?

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  Why did you choose to predict *measurements* rather than directly predicting actions? What is the underlying principle that makes this approach effective?
2.  What is the justification for the specific temporal offsets (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32) used in the prediction horizon? How sensitive are the results to different choices of offsets?
3.  The weighting scheme (0.5, 0.5, 1) for the future measurements seems arbitrary. What is the reasoning behind this choice, and how does it impact performance?
4.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of *why* your approach outperforms the baseline methods? Is it simply the denser supervision signal, or are there other contributing factors?
5.  What are the main limitations of this approach, and what types of environments or tasks would it be less suited for?
