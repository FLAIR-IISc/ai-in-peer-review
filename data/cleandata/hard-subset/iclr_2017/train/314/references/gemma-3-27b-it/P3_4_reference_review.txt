## ICLR 2024 Review: Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control

**1. Summary:**

This paper presents a compelling approach to sensorimotor control that shifts away from traditional reward-based reinforcement learning (RL) and towards a supervised learning framework. The core idea is to train an agent to predict the effects of its actions on future measurements, using the interleaved streams of sensory input and measurements as a rich supervisory signal. This formulation allows for learning from raw experience, dense supervision, and flexible goal specification. The authors demonstrate strong performance in the challenging Doom environment, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL methods and even winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.  The work’s strength lies in its conceptual simplicity, strong empirical validation, and insightful analysis of the benefits of vectorial feedback.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with enthusiasm. This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning, offering a promising alternative to traditional methods, particularly in complex, immersive environments. The empirical results are impressive, and the analysis provides strong justification for the proposed approach.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The paper tackles a fundamental challenge in RL – learning effective control from raw sensory input in complex environments.  The departure from reward-based learning is well-motivated by the limitations of sparse reward signals and the natural abundance of multi-dimensional sensory feedback in real-world scenarios. The authors effectively position their work within the broader RL landscape, referencing relevant literature and highlighting the advantages of their approach.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The results in Doom are genuinely impressive.  Consistently outperforming established RL algorithms (DQN, A3C, DSR) across multiple scenarios demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method. The win in the Visual Doom AI Competition – surpassing a strong baseline with more complex architecture and additional supervision – provides particularly compelling evidence of the method's practical utility and generalization capability. 
* **Ablation Studies & Analysis:** The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the importance of both vectorial feedback (multiple measurements) and predicting at multiple temporal offsets. These insights contribute meaningfully to understanding *why* the method works, not just that it does. The analysis of generalization to different textured environments is also insightful and strengthens the claims about robustness.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The architecture is clearly explained and illustrated. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail, allowing for reproducibility.

**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are strong, a more formal theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the proposed method would be valuable.  While the authors acknowledge the connection to Monte Carlo methods, exploring the conditions under which this approach converges and its relationship to temporal difference learning would strengthen the paper.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A more thorough discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity would be beneficial. How robust is the performance to changes in the learning rate, mini-batch size, and other key parameters? Providing some insight into the parameter tuning process would enhance the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
* **Computational Cost:** A detailed comparison of the computational cost (training time, memory requirements) of the proposed approach versus the baselines would be helpful. While the results show superior performance, understanding the trade-offs in terms of computational resources is important for practical application.
* **Limitations:** A more explicit discussion of the limitations of the approach would be valuable. For example, what types of environments or tasks might be less suitable for this method? What are the potential challenges in applying it to even more complex, real-world scenarios?

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the goal vector 'g':**  How sensitive is performance to the choice of the goal vector?  Did you explore any automated methods for learning or adapting the goal vector during training?
* **Regarding the architecture:**  Why was the specific architecture chosen (e.g., the separation into expectation and action streams)?  Were other architectural variations explored, and if so, what were the results?  
* **Regarding scalability:**  How does the performance of this approach scale with the dimensionality of the sensory input (e.g., higher-resolution images) and the number of actions?
* **Regarding real-world application:** Do you foresee any major challenges in applying this approach to real-world robotic control tasks?  Specifically, how might the noise and uncertainty in real-world sensor data affect the performance of the model?



Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The proposed approach is innovative, well-motivated, and empirically validated.  I strongly recommend it for acceptance to ICLR.
