This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem by predicting future measurements from raw sensory input and actions, rather than relying on traditional reward-based reinforcement learning. The core idea—leveraging the rich supervisory signal present in the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams—is conceptually interesting and potentially valuable, particularly for environments where sparse rewards are problematic. The authors demonstrate promising results in the Doom environment, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL methods and winning a competition track.

**Reject.** While the empirical results are encouraging, the paper lacks sufficient justification for the design choices and a thorough ablation study to isolate the contributions of different components. The success in Doom, while impressive, doesn't automatically translate to a broadly applicable methodology, and the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate generalizability beyond the specific experimental setup. The relative simplicity of the presented approach, despite achieving strong results, demands significantly more rigorous analysis to establish its robustness and understand its limitations.

The primary concern lies in the lack of comprehensive comparison and ablation. The authors compare against DQN, A3C, and DSR, but the details of these implementations (provided in the appendix) are insufficient to fully assess the fairness of the comparison. A3C, in particular, is known to be sensitive to hyperparameter tuning; the described search process feels cursory. More importantly, the ablation study (Table 4) is too limited. Simply demonstrating that multiple measurements *are* beneficial doesn’t explain *why* they are, or how crucial each measurement is. A more granular analysis of the contribution of each measurement and the impact of different temporal offsets is needed. The impact of the specific network architecture (Figure 1), particularly the split expectation/action streams, remains unclear. Is this architecture essential for performance, or can similar results be achieved with a simpler network? 

Furthermore, the justification for the choice of predicting *future measurements* instead of directly predicting actions is not fully convincing. The authors argue this reduces the problem to supervised learning, but the future prediction step introduces a new complexity. A direct action prediction model, trained with the same data, should be included as a baseline. The claim that the approach works well with dynamically specified goals needs further substantiation; the generalization experiments (Table 2) show promise but are limited to variations of the same Doom environments.

The reliance on Doom as the sole testbed is a concern. While a compelling environment, Doom is still a relatively narrow domain. The approach's efficacy in more complex and diverse environments remains untested. The authors acknowledge this and mention potentially extending the work to other domains, but this is not demonstrated.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the connection to existing SL methods:** The paper frames the approach as “supervised learning.” However, it’s not a standard SL problem. The target variables (future measurements) are inherently noisy and depend on the agent’s actions. A more nuanced discussion of how this differs from traditional SL is needed.
*   **Investigate the impact of goal representation:** The linear goal representation (u(f;g)) is a simple choice. Explore the use of more complex goal representations (e.g., learned embeddings) to see if performance can be further improved.
*   **Address the potential for compounding errors:** Predicting future measurements over multiple time steps is prone to error accumulation. The authors don’t adequately address this issue. Investigating techniques to mitigate this, such as recurrent architectures or more robust prediction models, would be beneficial.
*   **Provide more detail on the training process:** The experience replay buffer size and update frequency (k) are not clearly defined. These parameters can significantly impact performance.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the hyperparameter tuning process for A3C and DQN? Specifically, what range of hyperparameters were explored, and how were the best parameters selected?
*   Why was a linear function chosen for the goal representation? Have you considered using more complex, non-linear functions, or learned embeddings?
*   Have you experimented with predicting actions directly, as opposed to predicting future measurements? If so, how does the performance compare?
*   How sensitive is the performance to the choice of temporal offsets (τ1, ..., τn)? What happens if you use fewer or more offsets, or if you choose them randomly?
*   What is the computational cost of the proposed approach compared to the baseline methods? Is the increased performance worth the additional computational resources?
