This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem of predicting future measurements given current sensory input and action. Departing from traditional reward-based RL, the authors leverage the rich, temporally dense information present in measurement streams to guide learning, achieving impressive results in the Doom environment, even winning a competition against more complex and conventionally trained agents.

**Reject.** While the results are compelling and the core idea is refreshingly different, the paper feels more like a proof-of-concept with a strong empirical demonstration than a fully fleshed-out research contribution. The novelty is high, but the analysis lacks depth and the limitations aren't thoroughly addressed.

The primary strength lies in the demonstrated performance. Outperforming state-of-the-art methods, particularly with a simpler model and without reward shaping, is significant. Winning the Visual Doom AI Competition is a concrete achievement. The framing of sensorimotor control as future prediction is a conceptually appealing and potentially impactful shift. The ablation studies, while limited, do support the claim that vectorial feedback is valuable.

However, several concerns prevent an acceptance. First, the architectural details, while described, lack justification. Why this specific network structure? What alternatives were considered? The explanation feels somewhat ad-hoc. While the authors mention prior work on similar network architectures (Wang et al., 2016), the rationale for *this* specific instantiation within their framework is unclear. Second, the hyperparameter tuning process isn’t detailed enough. The description of comparing different learning rates for the baselines, while appreciated, lacks similar detail for their own approach. This makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the results.  Third, the ablation studies are too limited. While they show the importance of vectorial feedback and multi-step prediction, they don't investigate the sensitivity of the approach to changes in the goal representation (the ‘g’ vector). How does performance change with different goal parameterizations? Fourth, the generalization results, while positive, are presented without sufficient detail regarding the texture randomization process. Is the randomization purely cosmetic or does it affect the underlying dynamics of the environment?  Finally, the paper lacks a deeper theoretical discussion. While the authors briefly touch upon connections to temporal difference learning, a more rigorous analysis of the convergence properties and potential advantages/disadvantages compared to established RL methods would be valuable. The link to Monte Carlo methods feels similarly underdeveloped.

Here are some suggestions to improve the paper:

*   **Elaborate on architectural choices:** Provide a more detailed justification for the network architecture, including an exploration of alternative designs and their performance.
*   **Provide a detailed hyperparameter tuning process:** Document the hyperparameter search strategy and the rationale behind the chosen values.
*   **Conduct more comprehensive ablation studies:** Investigate the sensitivity of the approach to different goal representations, different sensory input modalities, and different prediction horizons.
*   **Provide a more thorough description of the texture randomization process:** Explain how the textures were randomized and how this affects the environment dynamics.
*   **Expand the theoretical discussion:** Provide a more rigorous analysis of the convergence properties and potential advantages/disadvantages of the proposed approach compared to established RL methods.
*   **Discuss limitations:** The paper would be strengthened by a more honest discussion of the limitations of the approach. For instance, it is less clear how well the approach would scale to very high-dimensional state spaces or complex action spaces.

I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind the choice of six temporal offsets for future prediction? Was this determined empirically, or based on theoretical considerations?
2.  You mention that the goal vector ‘g’ can represent any goal expressible in terms of future measurements. Could you provide examples of goals that are *difficult* to represent using this framework? What types of goals are unsuitable for this formulation?
3.  The ablation study shows improved performance with multiple measurements.  However, how does the computational cost scale with the dimensionality of the measurement vector? Is there a point where adding more measurements becomes detrimental due to increased complexity?
4.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the coefficients used to weigh the different temporal offsets in the loss function? Did you experiment with different weighting schemes?
