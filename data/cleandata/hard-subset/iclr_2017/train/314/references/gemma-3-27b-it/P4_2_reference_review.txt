This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem of predicting the effects of actions on future measurements, rather than the traditional reward-based reinforcement learning paradigm. The core idea – leveraging the rich supervisory signal present in the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams – is compelling and well-motivated. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results, notably winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming a significantly more complex model with additional supervision.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a promising approach and demonstrates impressive empirical results, the writing lacks sufficient clarity and rigor in certain areas, hindering a complete assessment of the technical contribution. Specifically, the description of the experimental setup and ablation studies feels somewhat cursory and lacks the detail needed for reproducibility.

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of clarity regarding experimental details. The paper mentions multiple ablation studies (Table 4) but doesn't clearly define the baselines or provide sufficient justification for the specific choices made. The success in the Visual Doom AI Competition is highlighted, but details about the competition environment, evaluation metrics, and the precise configuration of the winning model are limited. While a link to the competition is provided, the specifics crucial for understanding the magnitude of the achievement are missing from the paper itself.

Secondly, the connection to existing work, while acknowledged, lacks sufficient depth. The paper mentions relevant approaches but doesn’t fully articulate how the proposed method differs and improves upon them. For example, the discussion of Schaul et al. (2015) feels superficial; a more nuanced comparison would strengthen the paper’s contribution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Clarity of Approach:** The core idea of predicting future measurements as a form of supervised learning is clearly explained in the introduction.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The performance on the Doom environment, including winning the Visual Doom AI Competition, provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the approach.
*   **Motivation:** The motivation for moving away from sparse reward signals to denser measurement streams is well-articulated and aligns with known challenges in RL.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The inclusion of ablation studies demonstrates an attempt to understand the contribution of different components.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Experimental Details:** Provide a more detailed description of the experimental setup, including hyperparameter settings, network architectures (with layer sizes and activation functions), optimization algorithms, and training procedures. A clear description of the evaluation metrics used is also crucial.
*   **Reproducibility:** Consider releasing the code to facilitate reproducibility and further research.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** Elaborate on the baselines used, including a more detailed comparison of their architectures, training procedures, and hyperparameter settings.
*   **Ablation Study Details:** Provide a more thorough explanation of the ablation studies, justifying the choices made and interpreting the results more deeply. Explain *why* removing certain components negatively impacts performance.
*   **Visual Doom Competition Details:** Include more specifics about the competition environment (map layouts, adversary behavior, scoring rules) and evaluation protocols.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** While the paper focuses on empirical results, exploring the theoretical properties of the proposed approach (e.g., convergence guarantees, sample complexity) could further strengthen its contribution.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** Acknowledge potential limitations of the approach, such as its reliance on well-defined measurements and its applicability to environments with sparse or noisy measurements.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed description of the network architecture used in the experiments, including the number of layers, the size of each layer, and the activation functions used?
2.  What were the specific hyperparameters used for training the different models (learning rate, batch size, discount factor, etc.)?
3.  Can you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing the specific temporal offsets (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32) for predicting future measurements?
4.  Could you provide more details on the Visual Doom AI Competition environment and evaluation metrics? Specifically, what were the map layouts and adversary behaviors used in the Full Deathmatch track?
5.  What is the computational cost of the proposed approach compared to the baseline methods? How does the training time scale with the complexity of the environment?
