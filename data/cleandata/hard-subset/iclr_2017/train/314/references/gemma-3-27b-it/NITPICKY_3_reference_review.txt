This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem by predicting future measurements from sensory input and actions, rather than relying on the traditional reward-based reinforcement learning paradigm. The core idea – leveraging the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams – is intuitively appealing and potentially addresses limitations of sparse reward signals in complex environments. The authors demonstrate promising results in the Doom environment, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL models and winning a competition track.

**Reject.** While the approach shows promise and achieves impressive empirical results, the presentation and rigor are significantly lacking, riddled with inconsistencies, imprecise language, and a superficial engagement with related work. The multitude of minor flaws, when aggregated, undermine confidence in the validity and generalizability of the findings.

The paper suffers from a lack of methodological precision. The “temporal offsets” (τ) are introduced without clear justification for the specific choices (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32). Why these values? Is there a sensitivity analysis? The weighting of future measurements (0.5, 0.5, 1) also feels arbitrary. The network architecture (Figure 1) is mentioned but lacks critical details – number of layers, filter sizes, activation functions (beyond LReLU) are not specified, hindering reproducibility. The description of the baselines is vague. It is stated that "the best results" are reported, but details of hyperparameter search and tuning are minimal. The ablation study is similarly weak, only removing components without exploring alternative configurations or analyzing their individual contributions in depth. The notion of "randomized goals" lacks clarity. What distribution is used, and why?  The claim of outperforming a significantly more complex model in the competition is presented without detailed comparison of model sizes or computational costs. The paper uses ambiguous terms like “measurements” without clearly delineating them from raw sensory input, despite claiming a distinction. Finally, the writing style is frequently imprecise and relies on hand-waving statements. For example, the benefit of “dense multidimensional feedback” is asserted without sufficient elaboration.

Further feedback to improve the paper:

*   **Clarify the theoretical underpinnings:**  Connect the proposed approach more explicitly to existing supervised learning theory. What are the conditions under which this reduction to supervised learning is valid?
*   **Provide a comprehensive literature review:**  The related work section is somewhat superficial. More in-depth discussion of relevant literature on prediction-based control, imitation learning, and meta-learning is needed.
*   **Improve reproducibility:**  Provide a detailed specification of all hyperparameters, network architectures, training procedures, and experimental settings. Release code.
*   **Strengthen the empirical evaluation:**  Perform a more thorough ablation study, analyze the sensitivity of results to different hyperparameters, and provide statistical significance tests. Report computational costs and model sizes for all compared methods.
*   **Address the ambiguity surrounding ‘measurements’:**  Provide a precise definition and justification for choosing specific measurements.
*   **Refine the writing style:**  Eliminate ambiguous language and provide more rigorous explanations.

Questions for the authors:

1.  What is the theoretical justification for choosing the specific temporal offsets (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32)? Did you explore other offset configurations?
2.  How were the weights (0.5, 0.5, 1) for the future measurements determined? Is there a principled way to optimize these weights?
3.  Can you provide a complete specification of the network architecture used, including the number of layers, filter sizes, and activation functions?
4.  What specific hyperparameter tuning strategies were employed for the baseline methods?
5.  What is the computational cost (training time, memory usage) of your approach compared to the baseline methods? What are the model sizes?
6.  Can you elaborate on the distribution used for generating “randomized goals” and provide a rationale for choosing this distribution?
