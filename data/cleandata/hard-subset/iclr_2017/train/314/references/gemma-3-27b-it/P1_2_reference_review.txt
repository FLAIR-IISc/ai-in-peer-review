## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper:** Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control

**1. Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control that reframes reinforcement learning as a supervised learning problem. Instead of relying on sparse scalar rewards, the authors propose leveraging the rich temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams to predict the effects of actions on future measurements. This “direct future prediction” (DFP) method aims to overcome challenges in complex, dynamic environments and enable learning of generalizable skills. The authors demonstrate superior performance to state-of-the-art deep RL methods on challenging Doom environments, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, and provide evidence for generalization and the benefits of vectorial measurements.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling and well-executed idea that addresses a significant challenge in reinforcement learning. The empirical results are strong, particularly the impressive performance in the Visual Doom AI Competition, and the analysis provides good support for the core claims.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty & Motivation:** The shift from reward-based RL to a direct prediction framework is a valuable contribution. The authors convincingly argue that dense, multi-dimensional feedback is more appropriate for complex sensorimotor tasks and better aligned with biological learning. The connection to earlier work (Jordan & Rumelhart, Sutton) is well-articulated and contextualizes the contribution.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The performance gains over established deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) are substantial, particularly in the more challenging Doom scenarios. Winning the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming a significantly more complex model *without* extra supervision, is a strong indicator of the method’s effectiveness.
* **Ablation Studies & Analysis:** The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the benefits of using vectorial measurements and predicting at multiple temporal offsets. The generalization experiments show that the learned representations are robust and transferable.  The analysis of goal-agnostic training is insightful, demonstrating the flexibility of the approach.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The architecture (Figure 1) is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

* **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are compelling, exploring a more formal theoretical justification for why DFP can be effective (beyond the motivation based on dense feedback) would strengthen the paper.  Is there a connection to information theory or optimal control that could be drawn?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the approach to key hyperparameters (e.g., the temporal offsets, network architecture details) would be useful.
* **Computational Cost:**  The paper mentions training speed. A more detailed comparison of the computational cost (training time, memory usage) of DFP versus the baseline methods would be valuable.  Is DFP more or less computationally expensive?
* **Failure Cases:** Discussing scenarios where DFP *fails* or underperforms would provide a more nuanced understanding of its limitations.
* **Future Work:** Expanding on the potential for combining DFP with other techniques like memory-based models or hierarchical RL would strengthen the conclusion and provide a clearer roadmap for future research.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the goal parameterization (Equation 1):**  Why was the linear combination chosen?  Were other functional forms explored, and if so, what were the results? Does the performance seem sensitive to this choice?
* **Regarding the normalization layer (Equation 6):** Could you elaborate on the motivation behind this normalization?  Does it significantly impact performance, and are there any potential downsides?
* **Regarding the architecture (Figure 1):**  Why was the split into expectation and action streams beneficial? What is the intuition behind this architectural choice?  Is there a connection to value decomposition networks?
* **Regarding the Visual Doom AI Competition:** Could you provide more details about the specific configuration and tuning used for the competition submission?  What were the most challenging aspects of adapting the method to that specific setting?
* **Regarding Goal-agnostic training:** Does performance improve significantly when training with multiple randomized goals, compared to a single randomized goal?  Is there a saturation point with respect to the number of different goals used during training?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning.  The authors have presented a compelling approach, backed by solid empirical evidence, and I believe it is well-deserving of acceptance to ICLR.
