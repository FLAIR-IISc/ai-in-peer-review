The paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem by predicting the effect of actions on future measurements, rather than relying on traditional reward-based reinforcement learning. This approach leverages the rich supervisory signal present in the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams, offering benefits in terms of training stability, speed, and generalization. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results in the challenging Doom environment, even winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Reject.** While the paper presents compelling results and a potentially valuable perspective, the experimental section lacks sufficient detail to fully assess the reproducibility and robustness of the findings. Specifically, the comparisons to baseline methods feel incomplete, and the ablation studies, while present, don't offer a sufficiently nuanced understanding of the individual contributions of different components.

The core idea of framing sensorimotor control as a supervised prediction problem is well motivated. The authors effectively build upon the existing literature in reinforcement learning, supervised learning, and predictive representations, clearly articulating the limitations of traditional RL in complex, immersive environments and positioning their approach as a viable alternative. The connection to previous work on temporal difference learning and Monte Carlo methods provides necessary context. The emphasis on dense, multivariate measurements as a more appropriate supervisory signal for learning in realistic environments is persuasive. 

The empirical validation is a significant strength. Achieving state-of-the-art performance in Doom, and winning the Visual Doom AI Competition, are impressive achievements. However, the paper lacks detailed implementation information regarding the baselines. The description of hyperparameter tuning for the baselines is limited, raising concerns about a fair comparison. For instance, stating that the "best results" were chosen without specifying the search space or process weakens the comparison. The ablation study is a good starting point, but the choices made regarding which components to ablate could be more targeted. Specifically, the impact of different temporal offsets in the prediction horizon could be explored more systematically. The detailed architecture section is helpful, but it’s hard to understand its complexity without implementation details.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Reproducibility:** Provide more detailed information about the hyperparameters used for all methods, including baseline models. Clearly describe the hyperparameter search process, if any. Releasing the code would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** Elaborate on the implementation details of the baseline models (DQN, A3C, DSR). Justify the choices made in their configuration. A more rigorous ablation of the baseline's hyperparameters would clarify their performance.
*   **Ablation Study:** Expand the ablation study to include other key components of the proposed architecture. For example, what is the effect of removing the expectation stream or the action stream?
*   **Statistical Significance:** Provide statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to support the claims of outperformance. Reporting only average performance metrics doesn't allow for assessing the reliability of the results.
*   **Generalization:** While the results on multiple Doom environments are promising, exploring generalization to different environments (beyond texture variations) would further strengthen the claims.
*   **Goal-Agnostic Learning:** Provide more examples of novel goals and demonstrate the model's ability to adapt to them in a more comprehensive manner.
*   **Clarification Questions:**
    *   What is the rationale behind choosing specific temporal offsets (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32) for future measurements? Was any experimentation done to optimize these offsets?
    *   How sensitive is the performance to the coefficients used in the regression loss function (Equation 4)?
    *   Can you provide more details on the training schedule, including the learning rate decay and the batch size?
    *   What specific modifications were made to the DSR baseline to ensure a fair comparison, given its slower training speed?
    *   Could you elaborate on the "higher-capacity model" used in the D4-tx scenario (D4-tx-L)? What specific changes were made to the architecture?
