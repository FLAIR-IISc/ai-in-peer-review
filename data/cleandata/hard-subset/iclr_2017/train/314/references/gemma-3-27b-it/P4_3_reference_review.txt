This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem of predicting future measurements given current sensory input and actions, instead of the traditional reward-based reinforcement learning paradigm. The core idea—leveraging the rich supervisory signal inherent in the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams—is well-motivated and intuitively appealing, particularly for environments offering dense, multidimensional feedback. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results in the Doom environment, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL methods and even winning a competition, showcasing the practical effectiveness of their approach.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a compelling idea and impressive results, the presentation lacks sufficient detail and justification in several key areas, hindering a confident assessment of the technical soundness and reproducibility of the work. Furthermore, while the novelty is present, its significance isn’t fully established due to the limited comparison with closely related work.

The primary reason for rejection is the insufficient detail regarding experimental setup and implementation. The description of the network architecture (Figure 1 and associated text) is superficial. Key details like the number of filters in convolutional layers, the size of fully connected layers, and the precise activation functions used are missing. This makes it difficult to understand the complexity of the model and to compare it fairly with other approaches. Similarly, the training procedure lacks crucial details. The mini-batch size, optimizer (beyond mentioning Adam), learning rate schedule, and other hyperparameters are not specified. Without these specifics, reproducing the results becomes challenging. The ablation study is a good start, but doesn't offer enough granularity to understand the contribution of individual components.

Secondly, the comparison with prior work, while mentioning several relevant papers, lacks depth. The authors claim to outperform state-of-the-art methods but don't provide a detailed analysis of *why* their approach excels. Are the improvements due to the richer supervisory signal, the specific network architecture, the training procedure, or a combination of factors? A more thorough ablation study and analysis are needed to isolate the key contributions. While DSR is mentioned, the reasons for its limited training time are not fully explored, and it's unclear how much further it could have been pushed with more resources. The significance of outperforming a method using “extra supervision” is undermined by not detailing exactly how extensive that supervision was.

Finally, while the authors highlight the generalization capabilities, the experimental setup for evaluating this aspect could be strengthened. Reporting performance on a wider range of unseen environments and goals would provide more convincing evidence of the approach's robustness.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the relationship to model-based RL:** The paper briefly touches upon model-based RL, but doesn't fully explore the connection. Is their approach a form of implicit model learning? Discussing this connection would provide valuable context.
*   **Theoretical justification:** While the empirical results are strong, a more formal analysis of why this supervised learning approach works well in the RL setting would be beneficial. Is there a theoretical guarantee of convergence or optimality under certain conditions?
*   **Sensitivity analysis:** Conduct a sensitivity analysis to assess the impact of different hyperparameters on performance. This will help to understand the robustness of the approach and provide guidance for practitioners.
*   **Visualizations:** Include visualizations of the learned representations, such as the activations of the convolutional layers or the output of the measurement prediction module. This would provide insights into how the model is processing the sensory input and making predictions.
*   **Detailed description of the goals:** More detail on how the goals `g` were selected during training and testing could be useful, particularly for the randomized goals regime.
*   **Reproducibility:**  Release the code and detailed experimental setup to facilitate reproducibility. This is particularly important given the impressive results.

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed description of the network architecture, including the number of filters in each convolutional layer and the size of each fully connected layer?
2.  What specific hyperparameters were used during training, including the learning rate, mini-batch size, optimizer, and learning rate schedule?
3.  Could you elaborate on the extent of the “extra supervision” used by the competing method in the Visual Doom AI Competition?
4.  What is the computational cost of training your model compared to the baseline methods?
5.  Were any techniques used to prevent overfitting, such as dropout or regularization? If so, what were the specific parameters used?
