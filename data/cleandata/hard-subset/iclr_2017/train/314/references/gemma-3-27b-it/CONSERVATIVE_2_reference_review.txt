This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem focused on predicting the effects of actions on future measurements rather than relying on traditional reward-based reinforcement learning. The core idea – leveraging the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams for richer, denser supervision – is intuitively appealing, particularly for complex environments. The authors demonstrate promising results in the Doom environment, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL methods and even winning a competition track.

**Reject.** While the empirical results are impressive, the paper presents a significant departure from established RL paradigms without sufficiently rigorous justification and exploration of potential limitations. The success in Doom, while notable, doesn’t automatically generalize, and the paper lacks a comprehensive analysis of *why* this approach works and under what conditions it might fail. The presentation feels more like a demonstration of success than a well-grounded scientific contribution.

The primary concern is the lack of extensive comparison to well-tuned, established RL baselines. While the authors compare to DQN, A3C, and DSR, the description of the baseline tuning is sparse. It's not clear if these baselines were optimized to the same degree as the proposed method, or if hyperparameters were selected specifically to showcase the superiority of the new approach. The claim of outperforming a submission to the Visual Doom AI Competition is strong, but details on the complexity and supervision level of that submission are crucial and should be explicitly detailed in the paper – simply stating it used “substantially more complex model and additional supervision” isn’t sufficient. 

Furthermore, the justification for the choice of temporal offsets (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, and 32) feels arbitrary. A more thorough ablation study varying these parameters, and analyzing their impact on performance, is needed. The ablation study included *does* demonstrate the value of multi-dimensional measurements and future prediction, but doesn't explore other critical design choices. The generalization experiments are encouraging but limited. Testing across more diverse environments and varying the degree of texture randomization would strengthen the claim of robustness.

The reliance on Doom as the sole experimental environment is a limitation. While Doom offers a complex and immersive setting, it is still a game with specific characteristics. Demonstrating the effectiveness of this approach on more diverse robotic or real-world tasks would significantly bolster the paper’s credibility. The authors acknowledge the need for adaptation to continuous action spaces, but don't outline a clear path forward.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper lacks a theoretical justification for why direct future prediction should outperform traditional RL methods in this setting. While empirical results are important, a theoretical analysis could provide valuable insights and strengthen the claims.
*   **Baseline Details:** Provide a detailed description of the hyperparameter tuning process for all baseline methods, including search spaces and selection criteria.
*   **Ablation Study Expansion:** Expand the ablation study to include variations in network architecture, the number of predicted time steps, and the loss function.
*   **Error Analysis:** Conduct an error analysis to identify cases where the proposed method fails and understand the underlying reasons.
*   **Computational Cost:** Provide a detailed comparison of the computational cost (training time, memory usage) of the proposed method and the baseline methods.
*   **Robustness to Noise:** Assess the robustness of the approach to noisy measurements and sensory input.
*   **Explain the Goal Vector Choice:** Why was a linear combination used for the goal? Were other representations considered?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the hyperparameter tuning process for the baseline methods? Specifically, what search strategies were employed, and how were the final hyperparameters selected?
2.  What is a more detailed breakdown of the complexities and supervision methods employed by the winning submission in the Visual Doom AI Competition?
3.  Why were the specific temporal offsets (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, and 32) chosen? Did you explore other combinations or ranges of offsets?
4.  What are the primary failure modes of the proposed approach? Are there specific scenarios or situations where it consistently underperforms?
5.  How sensitive is the performance to the quality and dimensionality of the measurement stream? What happens if the available measurements are limited or noisy?
