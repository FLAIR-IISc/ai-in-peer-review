## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper Title:** Learning Sensorimotor Control from Raw Experience via Direct Future Prediction

**Reviewer:** [Your Name/Anonymous Reviewer] – Expertise: Reinforcement Learning, Embodied AI, Deep Learning

**1. Summary of Claimed Contributions:**

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control that shifts away from the traditional reward-based reinforcement learning (RL) paradigm.  Instead of relying on a sparse scalar reward, the authors propose framing the problem as supervised learning – predicting the effect of actions on future *measurements* derived from the agent's sensory stream.  This offers several potential benefits: richer, temporally dense supervision; the ability to train without a fixed goal; and adaptability to dynamically specified goals at test time. The authors demonstrate impressive results in the challenging Doom environment, achieving state-of-the-art performance and winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.  They provide compelling empirical evidence that their approach outperforms existing deep RL methods and generalizes well across environments and goals.

**2. Decision & Key Reasons:**

**Accept** (with minor revisions). The paper tackles a significant challenge in RL – learning effective control policies from raw sensory input – with a conceptually simple yet surprisingly effective approach. The experimental results are strong, especially the winning performance in the Visual Doom AI Competition, convincingly demonstrating the superiority of the proposed method. The clear articulation of the problem and the thorough empirical evaluation are particularly commendable.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty & Motivation:**  The idea of reframing sensorimotor control as a supervised prediction problem, leveraging rich measurement streams, is a compelling alternative to standard RL, especially in environments where reward engineering is difficult or impossible. The authors convincingly argue why this approach is more aligned with how organisms learn in immersive environments.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The results in Doom are highly impressive. Outperforming state-of-the-art methods, especially on complex tasks (D3 and D4), and winning the competition provide strong evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The ablation studies further demonstrate the importance of vectorial feedback and multi-step prediction.
* **Generalization Capabilities:**  The experiments showing generalization across different environments (D3-tx, D4-tx) are a key strength. This addresses a major limitation of many RL algorithms, which struggle to transfer to unseen scenarios.  The ability to adapt to dynamically specified goals is also a significant contribution.
* **Clear & Well-Written:** The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow. The authors provide sufficient detail about the experimental setup and architecture, allowing for reproducibility.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Theoretical Analysis:** While the empirical results are strong, the paper would benefit from some theoretical discussion regarding the convergence properties of the proposed method.  Comparing and contrasting it with traditional RL algorithms from a theoretical perspective would add value.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While you did mention hyperparameter testing for the baselines, a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of your proposed method to hyperparameters would be beneficial.  This would strengthen the robustness of the findings.
* **Computational Cost:** A more detailed discussion of the computational resources required for training (e.g., training time, GPU memory) would be helpful, especially given the intensive nature of the experiments.
* **Measurement Selection:** The paper acknowledges the importance of selecting appropriate measurements.  A discussion of guidelines or heuristics for choosing effective measurements in different environments would be valuable.  Is there a principled way to determine which sensory inputs to designate as measurements?
* **Comparison to Imitation Learning:** The approach shares similarities with imitation learning.  A more explicit discussion of the relationship between the two paradigms, and the advantages/disadvantages of each, would be helpful.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding generalization:** In the D3-tx and D4-tx experiments, what proportion of the textures were completely novel (i.e., not seen at all during training) versus variations of textures seen during training?
* **Regarding the goal vector (g):** You mention that any goal can be expressed in terms of future measurements.  However, some goals may be difficult to encode in this way. Could you elaborate on potential limitations and strategies for handling more complex or abstract goals?
* **Regarding the action stream:**  The action stream architecture, normalizing the outputs of each action, seems crucial. Did you experiment with alternative normalization techniques or designs for this stream?  Why did you choose this particular approach?
* **Regarding future work:** You mention hierarchical RL as a potential extension.  How do you envision integrating a hierarchical structure with the proposed prediction-based approach? Would the prediction targets need to be redefined at different levels of the hierarchy?



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of reinforcement learning. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated, and the authors have convincingly demonstrated its potential for enabling more effective and adaptable sensorimotor control.  The suggested revisions are minor and intended to further strengthen the paper.
