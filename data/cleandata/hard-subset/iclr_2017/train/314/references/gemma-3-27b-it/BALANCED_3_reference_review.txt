This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem by predicting future measurements given current sensory input, actions, and goals. Instead of relying on sparse scalar rewards common in reinforcement learning, the approach leverages the rich, temporally dense information contained in a stream of measurements. The core idea is sound and well-motivated, addressing key challenges in RL such as learning from raw sensory input and acquiring generalizable skills. The experimental results, particularly the win in the Visual Doom AI Competition, are impressive and strongly suggest the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a compelling approach with strong empirical results, the presentation lacks sufficient detail regarding implementation and experimental setup to allow for thorough reproducibility and a complete assessment of the contributions. Furthermore, while the ablation studies are helpful, they don't fully dissect the relative contributions of the different components of the proposed framework.

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of detailed methodological description. The paper refers to “Additional details” in Appendices A and B, but the provided Appendices (which are not included in the submitted version) are crucial for understanding the specific architecture, hyperparameters, and training procedures. Without this information, it’s difficult to assess the novelty and robustness of the approach. The description of the baseline algorithms is also brief, making it hard to ascertain a fair comparison. For example, the specific hyperparameter search for A3C is mentioned but not fully detailed.

Secondly, the ablation study, while present, feels somewhat incomplete. It demonstrates the importance of vectorial feedback and multi-step prediction, but doesn’t, for example, explore the sensitivity to the choice of temporal offsets or the impact of the network architecture itself (beyond the broader comparison to DQN). A more thorough ablation study would help pinpoint the key factors driving the performance gains.

Despite these concerns, the underlying idea is valuable and the results are promising. The paper demonstrates a clear path toward solving challenging problems in sensorimotor control, and the success in the Visual Doom competition is a significant achievement.

Here are some suggestions for improvement:

*   **Provide complete Appendices:** Include Appendices A and B in the submitted version. These are essential for understanding the methodology.
*   **Detailed Architecture:**  Elaborate on the specific layers, sizes, and activation functions used in the predictor network *within* the main body of the paper. A diagram would be very helpful.
*   **Hyperparameter Details:** Provide a comprehensive list of all hyperparameters used for both the proposed approach and the baselines. Include the search space used for hyperparameter tuning.
*   **Baseline Implementation Details:** Provide more details on the implementation of the baseline algorithms. What versions were used? What were the key configuration settings?
*   **Extended Ablation Study:** Conduct a more in-depth ablation study. Investigate the sensitivity of the performance to the choice of temporal offsets, the dimensionality of the measurement vector, and the components of the network architecture (expectation vs. action streams).
*   **Error Analysis:** Include an analysis of failure cases. What types of scenarios does the proposed approach struggle with? This could provide insights into potential areas for improvement.
*   **Computational Resources:** Report the computational resources (e.g., number of GPUs, training time) required to train the models. This is important for assessing the practicality of the approach.
*   **Clarify Goal Representation:** The function *u(f;g)* for representing goals is described but could benefit from a more intuitive explanation. Providing examples of how different goal vectors *g* translate to specific behaviors would be helpful.



I have several questions for the authors:

1.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of temporal offsets (τ1, ..., τn)? Did you perform a hyperparameter search over these values?
2.  The paper mentions training and testing regimes with randomized goals. What distribution was used to sample the goal vectors, and how did this distribution affect performance?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources required for training the winning model in the Visual Doom AI Competition?
4.  The "experience replay memory" size (M) is mentioned, but not quantified. What value of M was used in the experiments?
5.  What were the specific settings for the ε-greedy exploration schedule (initial ε, decay rate)?
6.  The ablation study shows the importance of multiple measurements. Was any effort made to *learn* which measurements are most informative, rather than using all available measurements?
