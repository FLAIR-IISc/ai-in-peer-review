This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem of predicting future measurements given sensory input and actions, rather than relying on the traditional reward-based reinforcement learning paradigm. The key idea – leveraging the rich, temporally dense information contained in measurement streams – is compelling and offers a potentially significant shift in how we approach complex control tasks. The authors demonstrate strong results in Doom, even winning a competition against more complex, traditionally trained agents.

**Reject.** While the core idea is exceptionally promising and the empirical results are impressive, the paper feels somewhat incomplete and leaves too many questions unanswered for a confident acceptance. The novelty is high, but the validation, while strong *in the specific domain of Doom*, lacks breadth and a deeper analytical investigation.

The core strength lies in the shift in perspective: moving away from sparse rewards to dense, predictive supervision. This is a fundamentally interesting idea, aligning with intuitions about how biological systems learn. The results in Doom are compelling; outperforming state-of-the-art RL methods, especially with a simpler model and *without* reward shaping or additional supervision, is a strong signal. The generalization experiments, while limited, are encouraging. However, the paper relies heavily on Doom as the sole validation environment. While Doom is a complex environment, its specific characteristics (first-person perspective, discrete actions, relatively simple physics) might not generalize to other control domains. 

The ablation study is helpful, confirming the importance of both vectorial measurements and multi-step prediction, but it’s limited in scope. The discussion section acknowledges several avenues for future work (memory, hierarchical abstraction, continuous actions), but doesn't adequately address *why* these are necessary limitations of the current approach. For example, the authors mention the inability to predict full sensory streams, but don't explore the implications of focusing only on pre-defined "measurements"—could the agent learn useful measurements implicitly through this predictive framework?

The architectural details are presented, but lack a detailed justification for specific choices (e.g., the network architecture itself, the choice of temporal offsets). The training parameters also feel somewhat arbitrary; a more thorough hyperparameter search and sensitivity analysis would strengthen the claims. The comparison to baselines is good, but more detail on the specific configurations and training procedures of those baselines would be valuable.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Broader Evaluation:** Evaluate the approach in other simulation environments beyond Doom, preferably with different characteristics (e.g., continuous action spaces, different sensor modalities, more complex physics).
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** A more rigorous theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the proposed approach would be beneficial. How does it compare to traditional RL algorithms in terms of sample efficiency and stability?
*   **Measurement Selection:** Investigate methods for automatically learning which measurements are most informative for control, rather than relying on pre-defined measurements.
*   **Network Architecture Justification:** Provide a more detailed rationale for the chosen network architecture. What are the advantages of this specific structure?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Perform a more thorough hyperparameter search and report the results, demonstrating the robustness of the approach.
* **Explain the success in Doom:** What are the specific aspects of Doom that make it particularly well-suited to this approach? What challenges might arise in other domains that are not present in Doom?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  You mention the limitations of predicting full sensory streams. Have you experimented with learning latent representations of the sensory input and then predicting future values of those latent representations?
2.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of temporal offsets (τ1, ..., τn)? Is there an optimal range or distribution for these offsets?
3.  In the generalization experiments, did you observe any systematic differences in performance between models trained with different levels of texture randomization?
4.  Can you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the ranges of values explored and the criteria used for selection?
5.  What are the computational costs (training time, memory requirements) compared to the baseline RL methods?
