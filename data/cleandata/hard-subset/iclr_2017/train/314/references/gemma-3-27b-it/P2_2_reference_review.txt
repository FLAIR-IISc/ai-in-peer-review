## ICLR Review

**1. Summary of Claims:**

This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem rather than traditional reinforcement learning. The core idea is to leverage the rich temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams to predict the effect of actions on future measurements. By predicting these future measurements, the agent can effectively choose actions aligned with its goal, bypassing the need for sparse, scalar rewards. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach in the challenging Doom environment, achieving state-of-the-art performance and winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition. They also show generalization to new environments and goals, and highlight the benefits of vectorial measurements.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling and well-executed approach that addresses significant challenges in reinforcement learning, specifically sensorimotor control in complex environments. The experimental results are strong, and the winning performance in a competitive benchmark is a particularly impressive accomplishment.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The shift from reward-based RL to a prediction-based framework is a significant and well-motivated idea. The authors convincingly argue that dense, multi-dimensional measurements provide a richer supervisory signal than sparse rewards, especially in complex, immersive environments. This is particularly relevant given the limitations of traditional RL in these settings.
* **Strong Experimental Results:** The results in Doom are very strong, consistently outperforming existing state-of-the-art methods. Winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition is a substantial achievement and provides compelling evidence for the efficacy of the proposed approach. The ablation studies further solidify these findings, demonstrating the importance of both vectorial measurements and multi-step prediction.
* **Generalization Capabilities:** The demonstration of generalization across different environments and goals is a crucial contribution. This suggests that the learned policies are not merely memorizing solutions for specific scenarios but are acquiring more generalizable skills.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The technical details are explained clearly, and the experimental setup is described thoroughly.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **Discussion of Limitations:** While the results are impressive, a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach would be valuable. For instance, the reliance on well-defined measurements. Are there scenarios where identifying useful measurements is difficult? How does the approach perform when measurements are noisy or incomplete?
* **Computational Cost:** A more detailed comparison of the computational cost (training time, memory requirements) compared to the baseline RL methods would be useful.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Briefly discussing the sensitivity of the approach to different hyperparameters could provide valuable insights for future researchers.
* **Connection to Related Work:** The related work section is comprehensive but could benefit from a more focused discussion of how this work differs from and builds upon existing prediction-based RL methods. Specifically, highlighting the differences to model-based RL approaches.
* **Ablation Study Depth:** The ablation study is good, but perhaps exploring the effects of different measurement window sizes (number of future time steps predicted) could reveal additional insights.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* You mention that predicting full sensory streams is currently beyond your capabilities. Could you elaborate on the challenges associated with this and potential avenues for future research in this direction?
* The goal formulation (u(f;g) = g>f) seems somewhat restrictive. Have you explored other functional forms for u, and if so, did they yield any improvements?
* In the ablation study, you show that predicting multiple measurements is beneficial. Is there a point of diminishing returns as the number of measurements increases?
* The training regime with randomized goals seems particularly promising for generalization. What insights did you gain about the relationship between the range of goals sampled during training and the model’s ability to adapt to new goals at test time?



Overall, this is a high-quality paper with significant potential. Addressing the suggested feedback would further strengthen the manuscript and enhance its impact on the field.
