## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper Title:** Learning Sensorimotor Control from Raw Experience through Direct Future Prediction

**1. Summary of Claims:**

This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control that moves away from traditional reward-based reinforcement learning (RL) and instead frames the problem as a supervised learning task. The core idea is to leverage the rich temporal structure present in streams of sensory input and measurements to predict the effects of actions on future measurements. By predicting these future states, the agent can select actions that achieve its goals, effectively bypassing the need for a sparse, scalar reward signal.  The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach in challenging 3D environments (Doom), outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL methods, generalizing across environments and goals, and even winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling and well-supported argument for shifting the paradigm of sensorimotor control from RL to supervised learning, when dense, multi-dimensional feedback is available. The empirical results are strong, especially the performance in the Visual Doom AI Competition, which demonstrates the practical viability of the approach.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Novelty and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional RL in scenarios with rich sensory input and offers a well-motivated alternative. The framing of the problem as future prediction is insightful and elegant. The connection to prior work (Jordan & Rumelhart, Sutton) is well-established, and the paper effectively positions itself within the existing literature.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The results in the Doom environment are convincing. The consistent outperformance of state-of-the-art RL baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across multiple scenarios provides strong evidence for the efficacy of the proposed approach. The generalization experiments and the competition win further solidify these findings. The ablation study further strengthens the argument for the importance of vectorial feedback and multi-step prediction.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The explanation of the model architecture is clear, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail. The inclusion of a supplementary video is a helpful addition.
* **Practical Significance:**  Winning the Visual Doom AI Competition represents a significant achievement, demonstrating the approach’s ability to function in a complex, real-world-like environment without relying on engineered rewards or additional supervision.

**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

*   **Discussion of Limitations:** While the paper highlights the benefits of the approach, a more thorough discussion of its limitations would be valuable.  For instance, what types of environments or tasks might *not* be well-suited to this approach?  What are the potential challenges of defining appropriate “measurements” for different applications?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper briefly mentions hyperparameter tuning for the baselines but doesn't offer detailed insight into the sensitivity of *their* approach to hyperparameters. A discussion of how robust the performance is to changes in learning rate, network architecture, or other key parameters would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Cost:** Briefly address the computational demands of the method. Although achieving good results, a comparison with the computational resources needed to train comparable RL agents would be a valuable addition.
* **Clarify Goal Parameterization:** While the linear combination of future measurements is clear, a bit more intuition about how different goal parameterizations (vector *g*) translate to different behaviors would be helpful. Perhaps a simple example illustrating this mapping could be included.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Measurement Selection:** How does the choice of "measurements" affect performance?  Are there strategies for automatically identifying or learning relevant measurements from raw sensory data?
*   **Scalability to High-Dimensional State Spaces:**  The approach works well in Doom, where the dimensionality of the measurement vector is relatively low. How might the approach be scaled to environments with much higher-dimensional state spaces where identifying a concise set of measurements is challenging?
*   **Exploration Strategy:** The paper mentions an epsilon-greedy exploration strategy. Have you experimented with other exploration techniques (e.g., intrinsic motivation, curiosity-driven exploration) and how did they affect the performance of the model?
* **Comparison to Imitation Learning:** The described approach bears some resemblance to imitation learning. What differentiates this approach and how does it overcome the limitations of standard imitation learning, such as reliance on expert demonstrations?



In conclusion, this is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning.  The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and supported by compelling empirical results.  Addressing the minor feedback points outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
