This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem of predicting future measurements given sensory input and actions, rather than the traditional reward-based reinforcement learning paradigm. The core idea of leveraging the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams as a supervisory signal is compelling and intuitively sound. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results in the Doom environment, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL methods and even winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Reject.** While the approach shows promise and delivers impressive empirical results, the paper lacks sufficient clarity regarding crucial implementation details and the robustness of the method to hyperparameter tuning. The justifications for specific architectural choices and training procedures are not always well-explained, hindering reproducibility and a thorough understanding of the approach’s strengths and limitations.

The paper's strengths lie in its clear motivation and positioning within the existing literature. The authors effectively argue for the benefits of dense, multi-dimensional supervision compared to sparse rewards, especially in complex environments. The experimental setup, utilizing the Doom environment, is well-justified and allows for rigorous evaluation. The reported results are strong, with a clear demonstration of superior performance compared to existing methods, and the win in the Visual Doom AI Competition is a significant achievement. 

However, the presentation of the method itself is somewhat lacking in detail. The architecture (Figure 1) is described but critical aspects, such as the precise dimensions of layers and the rationale for specific choices within the expectation and action streams, are not fully elaborated. The ablation study, while present, feels incomplete – the reasoning behind choosing the specific ablations isn’t thoroughly explained. Furthermore, the hyperparameter search process isn’t detailed enough to assess the sensitivity of the results to parameter choices. The comparison to baselines is robust, but the details in Appendix B regarding baselines setup are brief. The discussion section is good, acknowledging future directions, but it could benefit from a more thorough discussion of potential drawbacks and limitations of the proposed method. The training setup is described, but it is unclear why the number of training steps for DSR was limited.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify architectural details:** Provide a more detailed description of the neural network architecture, including layer sizes, activation functions, and the rationale behind specific design choices.
*   **Explain hyperparameter tuning:**  Detail the hyperparameter search process used for both the proposed method and the baselines. Report the range of parameters explored and the criteria used for selection.
*   **Expand ablation study:** Justify the choices made in the ablation study and consider additional ablations to further isolate the contribution of different components.
*   **Address limitations:** Acknowledge and discuss the limitations of the approach. For example, how sensitive is the method to the choice of measurements? What are the challenges of applying this approach to environments with very high-dimensional sensory input?
*   **Provide more details on DSR training:** Explain why DSR training was limited to 10 days and how this might impact the comparison.
*   **Goal-agnostic training**: When discussing the results for goal-agnostic training, what does it mean for the “random goal vector” to be sampled uniformly from [-1, 1]? This needs further clarification.
*   **Experience Replay:** The authors briefly mention experience replay. Provide more details regarding the size of the experience memory and the sampling strategy employed.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the sensitivity of the model’s performance to the choice of temporal offsets (τ1, …, τn)? Did you experiment with different sets of offsets?
2.  How were the hyperparameters for the baseline methods selected, and how does their performance change with different hyperparameter settings?
3.  What considerations went into the choice of measurements (mt)? Are there specific criteria for selecting effective measurements in a given environment?
4.  Can you elaborate on the computational cost of the proposed method compared to the baseline methods?
5.  What are the primary challenges in scaling this approach to even more complex environments or to real-world robotic applications?
