This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem by predicting future measurements given sensory input and actions, rather than relying on the traditional reward-based reinforcement learning paradigm. The core idea—leveraging the rich, temporally dense information present in measurement streams—is compelling and well-motivated, particularly in complex, immersive environments. The experimental results, demonstrating superior performance in Doom compared to state-of-the-art RL methods and winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, are highly impressive and provide strong evidence for the efficacy of the proposed approach. The emphasis on learning from raw experience without explicit reward shaping is also a significant strength.

**Reject**

While the paper presents a strong technical contribution and compelling results, the presentation lacks sufficient clarity and depth in several key areas, hindering a complete assessment of the work's novelty and generalizability. Specifically, the connection to existing supervised learning literature is not fully explored, and certain experimental details are glossed over, making reproducibility difficult. 

The arguments supporting this decision are outlined below:

1. **Limited Novelty Discussion:** The paper positions its approach as framing RL as SL, but doesn't adequately discuss existing work that already makes this connection (e.g., imitation learning, model-based RL with learned models used for prediction). The authors acknowledge some related work (Jordan & Rumelhart, Levine et al.) but do not sufficiently differentiate their contribution. A more thorough discussion of *how* this approach differs from and improves upon existing techniques is crucial.  Simply stating that "formulation of sensorimotor learning from raw experience as supervised learning is rare" feels insufficient without elaborating on *why* this is the case and what challenges their approach overcomes.

2. **Insufficient Experimental Detail:** While the results are impressive, the experimental setup lacks crucial details.  The architecture of the predictor network (Figure 1) is referenced but not fully detailed; the number of layers, filter sizes, and other hyperparameters are not provided in the main text. This makes replication difficult. Similarly, the training procedure (e.g., optimizer, learning rate schedule) lacks sufficient detail. The ablation study, while present, could be more comprehensive; for example, why were only specific ablations tested?  More details on hyperparameter tuning are needed.  The claim that A3C training runs were performed with a search over learning rates is vague; how many runs were performed, and what criteria were used to select the best hyperparameters?

3. **Goal-Agnostic Training Results Interpretation:** The results of the goal-agnostic training are presented, but the interpretation is somewhat superficial. The performance is comparable to the fixed-goal training, but the *mechanism* by which the model achieves this generalization is not deeply explored. Is the model learning a more robust representation of the environment that facilitates transfer to new goals, or is it simply averaging over different policies?

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the Loss Function:** The loss function (Equation 4) is a simple mean squared error. Consider discussing whether other loss functions (e.g., Huber loss) might be more robust to outliers or offer improved performance.
*   **Discuss the Choice of Temporal Offsets:**  The selection of temporal offsets (τ1, ..., τn) seems somewhat arbitrary.  Is there a theoretical justification for these specific values, or were they determined empirically?  An ablation study varying these offsets could be insightful.
*   **Expand the Related Work:**  The related work section could be expanded to include more recent advances in visual prediction (e.g., generative adversarial networks for video prediction) and their potential relevance to this work.
*   **Address Limitations:** A dedicated section discussing the limitations of the approach (e.g., reliance on accurately defined measurements, potential for catastrophic forgetting in continuous goal spaces) would strengthen the paper.
*   **Code Availability:**  Making the code publicly available would greatly enhance the reproducibility and impact of this work.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific differences between your approach and existing methods that frame RL as SL, such as imitation learning or model-based RL? What unique challenges does your formulation address?
2.  Please provide a more detailed description of the network architecture used in your experiments, including the number of layers, filter sizes, and activation functions.
3.  Can you provide more specifics on the hyperparameter tuning process for all methods, including the search space and selection criteria?
4.  What is the computational cost of training your model compared to the baseline RL methods?
5.  What is the sensitivity of the results to the choice of temporal offsets (τ1, ..., τn)? Have you performed an ablation study to investigate this?
