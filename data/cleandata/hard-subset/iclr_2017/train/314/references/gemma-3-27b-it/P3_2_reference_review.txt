## ICLR 2024 Review: Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control

**1. Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem. Instead of relying on sparse, scalar rewards typical of reinforcement learning, the authors propose predicting future measurements from raw sensory input and actions. This “direct future prediction” (DFP) method leverages the rich, temporally dense information present in the interplay between sensory streams and internal state measurements, enabling learning without explicit reward engineering.  The authors demonstrate compelling results on the challenging Doom environment, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL methods, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.  The work highlights the benefits of dense, vectorial feedback and goal-agnostic training, showcasing the potential of a supervised learning perspective on complex control tasks.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning by offering a compelling alternative to reward-based methods. The results are strong, particularly the performance in the Visual Doom AI Competition, and the theoretical motivation is sound.  The clarity of the writing and thorough experimental evaluation further strengthen the paper.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The core idea of framing sensorimotor control as a supervised prediction problem is insightful and well-motivated.  The authors effectively articulate the limitations of sparse rewards and the advantages of utilizing the inherent structure in sensory and measurement data.  The connection to the existing SL vs. RL debate (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992) is well-placed.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The results in Doom are very convincing.  Consistently outperforming established deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across a range of scenarios demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach.  Winning the Full Deathmatch competition is particularly impressive, considering the complexity of the environment and the competitive landscape. The ablation studies convincingly demonstrate the benefit of multi-dimensional feedback and multi-step prediction.
* **Generalization Ability:** The experiments showing generalization to unseen environments (D3-tx, D4-tx) are a critical strength.  This demonstrates that the learned representations are not simply overfitting to the specific training environments, but are capturing generalizable control strategies. The ability to adapt to new goals without retraining is another significant advantage.
* **Clear and Thorough Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology, experimental setup, and results.  The architecture description (Figure 1) is helpful, and the inclusion of baseline details is valuable for reproducibility.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Theoretical Considerations:** While the empirical results are strong, further exploring the theoretical underpinnings of why DFP works so well would be valuable.  For instance, a discussion of the relationship between the prediction error and the Bellman equation could strengthen the theoretical grounding.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  A more in-depth analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity, especially the impact of different temporal offsets (τ1…τn) and the coefficients used in the loss function, would be helpful.
* **Computational Cost:** The paper mentions that DSR was limited by computational resources. Providing a more detailed comparison of the computational cost of DFP versus the baselines (training time, memory requirements) would be useful for assessing its practicality.
* **Error Analysis:** A qualitative error analysis of failure cases could offer insights into the limitations of the approach and suggest directions for future research.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the goal vector *g*:**  You mention that “any parametric function can be used” for *u(f; g)*.  Have you explored different functional forms for *u* beyond the linear combination, and if so, what were the results? 
* **Regarding the normalization layer:** The normalization layer in the action stream is an interesting idea. Did you experiment with other normalization techniques, and if so, what was the rationale for choosing this specific approach?
* **Regarding the choice of measurements:** How sensitive are the results to the specific measurements chosen (health, ammo, frags)?  Would the approach still work effectively with a different set of measurements, or even a single carefully chosen measurement?
* **Regarding scaling to more complex environments:** While Doom is a challenging environment, it’s still a relatively constrained domain. How do you anticipate the approach would scale to even more complex, real-world scenarios with high-dimensional sensory inputs and a larger action space?
