This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem rather than traditional reinforcement learning. The core idea is to predict future measurements given current sensory input, actions, and goals, thereby providing a dense supervisory signal. The authors demonstrate promising results in the Doom environment, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL models and winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful idea, the presentation lacks sufficient clarity and the experimental validation, while impressive, feels somewhat isolated and doesn't fully justify the claims of a broadly applicable improvement over established RL methods. The significant performance gain in the Doom competition is a strong point, but the paper doesn’t adequately contextualize this result.

The primary reason for rejection is a lack of clear justification for *why* this approach works so well, and a limited exploration of its generalizability beyond the Doom environment. The paper relies heavily on empirical results without a deep dive into the underlying mechanisms. For example, the benefit of vectorial measurements isn’t fully explored – is the improvement simply due to the increased dimensionality of the signal, or is there something fundamentally advantageous about predicting multiple, related quantities? The paper states that “a dense flow of multivariate measurements is a better training signal than a scalar reward,” but doesn't offer much theoretical or analytical backing for this claim beyond the experimental results. 

Secondly, the paper's presentation is dense and occasionally difficult to follow. The notation can be improved, and some concepts are introduced without sufficient explanation. The connection between the proposed approach and existing work in SL and RL feels somewhat superficial; the discussion lacks depth and fails to adequately position the contribution within the broader field. The architecture description, while present, could be more visually accessible and detailed.

However, the work is promising and has the potential to contribute significantly to the field. The experimental results are compelling, and the idea of leveraging dense measurements for control is worth further investigation.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Clarify the Motivation:** Expand on why traditional RL struggles with dense, multivariate feedback. What specific limitations are addressed by this approach?
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** Consider adding some theoretical justification for the effectiveness of the approach. Is there a connection to information theory, or can the advantages be explained in terms of the sample efficiency of supervised learning?
*   **Ablation Studies:** Expand the ablation studies to include a more systematic analysis of the importance of different components of the model (e.g., the expectation and action streams). What is the impact of the normalization layer?
*   **Generalization Experiments:** Investigate the generalization capabilities of the approach in more diverse environments beyond variations of Doom. Applying it to other simulated or real-world tasks would strengthen the claim of broader applicability.
*   **Comparison to Imitation Learning:** Explicitly compare and contrast the proposed approach with imitation learning. Although the authors mention SL, the distinctions and similarities deserve more attention.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discuss the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter settings.
*   **Code Release:** Releasing the code would greatly facilitate reproducibility and further research.
*   **Notation and Presentation:** Improve the clarity of the notation and streamline the presentation. Consider adding diagrams to illustrate the model architecture and training process.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper highlights the success in the Doom competition. Could you elaborate on the specifics of the competition setup, the characteristics of the unseen environments, and the advantages your approach had over other submissions? Specifically, what aspects of the Doom environment make it particularly well-suited for this approach?
2.  In the ablation study, the performance drops when predicting only at the farthest temporal offset. Could you explain why this is the case? Is it due to difficulties in long-term prediction, or is there another reason?
3.  The choice of linear combinations for goals seems simple. Have you explored more complex goal representations? If so, how did they affect performance and generalization?
4.  You mention that the approach “reduces sensorimotor control to supervised learning.” Does this imply that all the benefits of RL are lost? What are the trade-offs between this approach and traditional RL?
5.   How does the performance scale with the dimensionality of the measurement space? Are there any limitations or challenges associated with using a very high-dimensional measurement vector?
