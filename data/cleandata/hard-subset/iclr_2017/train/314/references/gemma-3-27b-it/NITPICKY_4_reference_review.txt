This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem by predicting future measurements given current sensory input, actions, and goals. The authors argue that this provides a richer, temporally dense supervisory signal compared to traditional reward-based reinforcement learning, and facilitates learning dynamically specified goals. They demonstrate promising results in the Doom environment, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL models and winning a competition track.

**Reject.** While the core idea is interesting and the results are impressive, the paper suffers from a concerning number of methodological shortcomings and a lack of rigorous analysis, overshadowing the potential contribution. The presentation feels rushed, lacking detail in crucial areas, and relies heavily on asserting advantages rather than demonstrating them systematically.

The most significant issue lies in the lack of thorough comparative analysis. While the authors claim to outperform other methods, the experimental setup and hyperparameter tuning for those methods are not clearly explained. The statement that DQN and A3C were tested with “the default” and “two alternatives” learning rates is insufficient; a proper grid search or optimization process is necessary to ensure a fair comparison. The significantly different training times (DSR's limited training) further weakens the validity of the comparisons. 

Furthermore, the ablation study, while present, is incomplete. Ablating only a subset of components (scalar reward vs. vector, single time step vs. multiple) doesn’t isolate the specific benefits of the proposed approach. For example, it’s unclear if the improvement from vector measurements stems from having *more* information, or from the *type* of information. A more granular ablation, examining individual measurements and their impact, is needed.

The architecture description, while providing a diagram, lacks sufficient detail.  The choice of network sizes, activation functions (beyond mentioning leaky ReLU), and optimization algorithms are not fully specified, hindering reproducibility. The explanation of the expectation and action streams feels intuitive but lacks a strong theoretical justification. Why this specific split, and how does it relate to the Bellman equation or other RL principles?

The claim of goal-agnostic training is compelling but requires stronger evidence. Table 3 demonstrates the model can generalize to new goals, but the performance drop for some goals is substantial. The authors don’t analyze why certain goals are more difficult, or if the representation learned during training inherently biases towards specific goal types. The training regimes (random goals between 0-1 or -1-1) are arbitrarily chosen, and the justification for these ranges is lacking.

Finally, the paper contains several minor but irritating inconsistencies. For example, “w” is used to denote both the number of actions and is part of a formula (w · dim(f)). The writing style, while generally clear, occasionally resorts to assertive statements without sufficient backing.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Detailed Baselines:** Provide a comprehensive description of the hyperparameters and training procedures for all baseline methods. Include a sensitivity analysis demonstrating the effect of key hyperparameters on performance.
*   **Complete Ablation Study:**  Conduct a more detailed ablation study, isolating the contribution of each component of the proposed approach.
*   **Architectural Details:**  Fully specify the network architecture, including layer sizes, activation functions, and optimization algorithms.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Provide a more rigorous theoretical justification for the proposed approach, relating it to existing RL frameworks.
*   **Goal Generalization Analysis:** Analyze the performance across different goal types, identifying potential biases and limitations.
*   **Reproducibility:** Release the code and trained models to facilitate reproducibility and further research.
*   **Clarify Notation:** Ensure consistent and unambiguous notation throughout the paper.

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  What specific hyperparameter tuning procedures were employed for DQN, A3C, and DSR? Can you provide a table summarizing the hyperparameter values used for each method?
2.  Why were the goal vectors sampled from [0, 1] and [-1, 1] in the goal-agnostic training experiments? What is the rationale behind these specific ranges?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the intuition behind splitting the prediction module into expectation and action streams?
4.  Could you provide an estimate of the computational cost (training time, GPU memory usage) of your approach compared to the baseline methods?
5.  Are there any theoretical guarantees regarding the convergence of the proposed learning algorithm?
