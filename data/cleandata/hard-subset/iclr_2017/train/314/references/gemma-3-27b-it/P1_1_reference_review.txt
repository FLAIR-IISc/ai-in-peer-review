## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper Title:** Learning Sensorimotor Control from Raw Experience as Supervised Learning

**1. Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control that reframes reinforcement learning as a supervised learning problem. Instead of relying on a scalar reward signal, the authors propose leveraging the rich, temporally dense information present in the interplay between raw sensory input and intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammunition). The core idea is to train an agent to predict the effect of actions on future measurements, effectively reducing the control problem to a prediction task. The paper demonstrates strong empirical results, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL methods, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition. The approach shows generalization across environments and goals, and the authors highlight the benefit of using vectorial measurements over scalar rewards.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** – This paper presents a compelling and well-supported approach to sensorimotor control. The results are impressive, particularly the performance in the challenging Doom environment and winning the Visual Doom AI Competition. The authors successfully demonstrate that a supervised learning formulation, leveraging richer feedback signals, can outperform traditional RL techniques in complex scenarios.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are robust and demonstrate clear improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods, particularly in the complex Doom environments (D3 and D4). Winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition is a significant achievement and strong evidence of the approach’s effectiveness.
* **Novel and Well-Motivated Approach:**  The paper offers a refreshing perspective on sensorimotor control, moving away from the traditional reward-based RL framework. The motivation is well-articulated, referencing the limitations of sparse rewards and the benefits of dense, multi-dimensional feedback found in natural systems.  The connection to prior work on SL vs. RL, and the argument for dense feedback, are convincingly presented.
* **Clear and Comprehensive Experiments:** The paper includes a series of well-designed experiments that investigate different aspects of the approach: comparison to baselines, generalization ability, the importance of vectorial feedback, and goal-agnostic training. The ablation studies provide valuable insights into the contribution of different components.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors provide sufficient details about the experimental setup and implementation, enabling reproducibility.



**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Discussion of Limitations:** While the results are impressive, a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach would strengthen the paper. For instance, the reliance on well-defined "measurements" could be a limitation in scenarios where identifying these measurements is difficult. Discussing the potential challenges of scaling this approach to even more complex environments would also be valuable.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** While the paper's strength lies in its empirical results, a more formal theoretical analysis could bolster the claims.  Exploring the conditions under which this supervised learning approach is guaranteed to converge, or a more rigorous analysis of its sample efficiency, could be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** While training speed is mentioned briefly, a more detailed comparison of the computational cost of this approach versus the baselines would be helpful.  Is the increased performance worth any additional computational overhead?

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Measurement Selection:** How sensitive is the performance to the choice of “measurements”? Do certain measurements provide more predictive power than others?  Could an automated process be used to identify relevant measurements from raw sensory data?
* **Generalization to Continuous Action Spaces:** The current work focuses on discrete action spaces.  What challenges are anticipated in extending this approach to continuous action spaces, and what modifications would be necessary?
* **Long-Horizon Prediction:** The paper uses a limited number of future time steps for prediction (up to 32).  Have you explored the effect of predicting further into the future, and what are the trade-offs between prediction horizon and performance?  Is there a point where predicting too far into the future becomes detrimental?
* **Reward Shaping vs. Measurement Prediction:** The authors state that the measurements act as a dense reward. Could you compare the performance to directly using reward shaping, with a carefully engineered reward function that provides dense feedback similar to the measurements? Would the measurement-based approach still outperform reward shaping, and if so, why?



Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. The approach is innovative, well-motivated, and supported by compelling empirical results.  I am confident that this work will inspire further research in this direction.
