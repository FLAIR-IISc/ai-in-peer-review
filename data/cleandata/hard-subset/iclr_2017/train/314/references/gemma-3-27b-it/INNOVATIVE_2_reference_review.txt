This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem of predicting future measurements given sensory input and actions, rather than relying on traditional reward-based reinforcement learning. The core idea—leveraging the rich temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams—is compelling and offers a potentially significant shift in how we approach RL, particularly in complex, immersive environments. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results in Doom, even winning a competition, outperforming methods with substantially more complex architectures and additional supervision.

**Reject.** While the idea is genuinely novel and the results are impressive, particularly the competition win, the paper feels somewhat under-developed for a definitive acceptance at ICLR. The core contribution, while strong, is presented with a lack of detailed mechanistic explanation and exhaustive ablation studies. The "why" behind the success isn’t fully explored, relying heavily on the empirical outperformance.

The strength of this work lies in its departure from standard RL. By reframing the problem as a prediction task based on intrinsic measurements, the authors sidestep the difficulties of reward shaping and sparse reward signals. The demonstration that a model trained this way can generalize across environments and goals is particularly exciting. The competition results provide strong evidence of the approach's practical effectiveness. However, the paper doesn’t fully dissect *why* this method works so well. Is it the dense supervision? The focus on prediction rather than reward? The specific network architecture? The ablation studies (Table 4) hint at the importance of both vectorial feedback *and* multi-step prediction, but more granular analysis would be beneficial. For instance, what happens with fewer measurements? What about different temporal offsets? A more thorough analysis of the learned representations (e.g., visualization of the predicted measurements, analysis of the learned action embeddings) would provide deeper insight. The success in Doom is impressive but limits generalizability claims; more diverse environments would strengthen the conclusions. The connections to existing work, while acknowledged, could be more thoroughly explored – particularly in light of recent advances in world models and predictive learning. 

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Mechanistic Explanation:** Elaborate on why predicting future measurements is an effective control strategy. Connect it more explicitly to the underlying dynamics of the environment and the agent’s ability to manipulate those dynamics.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Expand the ablation studies to include variations in the network architecture, loss function, and training procedure.
*   **Representation Analysis:** Visualize and analyze the learned representations within the network. What are the key features being predicted? How do they relate to the agent's actions and the environment?
*   **Generalization:** Test the approach in more diverse and challenging environments beyond Doom.
*   **Theoretical Considerations:** While a full theoretical analysis might be beyond the scope, a discussion of the convergence properties of this supervised learning-based approach would be valuable.
*   **Comparison to World Models:** Explicitly address how this work relates to the growing body of research on world models and predictive representations.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of *why* predicting future measurements leads to effective control, beyond simply stating that it provides dense supervision?
2.  What is the sensitivity of the performance to the choice of temporal offsets (τ1, …, τn)? Did you experiment with different offset distributions or adaptive offset selection?
3.  The architecture (Figure 1) seems quite specific. How sensitive are the results to variations in the network architecture, particularly the separation into expectation and action streams?
4.  You mention the advantage of using vectorial measurements. Could you quantify the performance degradation if you were to use a single scalar reward representing a combination of those measurements?
5.  What does the learned representation of the "goal" (g) look like? Is it interpretable, and does it change consistently with different goals?
