This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem by predicting future measurements instead of relying on traditional reward-based reinforcement learning. The core idea – leveraging the rich, temporally dense information within sensory and measurement streams – is compelling and represents a significant departure from standard RL formulations. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results in Doom, even winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming more complex methods.

**Reject.** While the approach is genuinely interesting and the results impressive, the paper feels incomplete as a scientific contribution. The success is largely presented as a "black box," lacking sufficient mechanistic insight into *why* this method works so well. The strong empirical performance, while valuable, doesn't automatically translate to a fundamental advancement without a deeper understanding. The paper heavily relies on empirical validation and, while successful, lacks the theoretical grounding needed to elevate it beyond a promising engineering result.

The primary argument supporting the rejection stems from the lack of detailed ablation studies exploring the critical design choices. While a small ablation is present (Table 4), it’s insufficient to understand the contribution of each component. For example, the impact of predicting multiple future time steps isn't thoroughly explored – is the performance gain linear with the number of steps predicted? What are the trade-offs? Furthermore, the superiority over A3C, a well-established method, isn’t fully dissected.  A more detailed comparison, beyond just final scores, analyzing learning curves, sample efficiency, and generalization behavior, is needed. The winning performance in the competition is highlighted, but details regarding the competitors’ approaches are sparse. The authors state the second-place model was “substantially more complex” and used “additional supervision,” but quantifying this difference is crucial for contextualizing the results. 

The paper's strength lies in its novelty. The shift from reward maximization to future prediction is a conceptually appealing idea, especially in environments with dense sensory feedback. However, the theoretical justification remains weak. Connecting this approach back to established RL principles – how does it relate to policy gradients or value iteration, even implicitly? – would strengthen the paper significantly. The claim that this approach *reduces* sensorimotor control to supervised learning is a bold one that needs more rigorous backing.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Theoretical Analysis:**  Investigate the theoretical properties of this approach. Can any convergence guarantees be provided? Does it align with existing RL theory under certain conditions?
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct a comprehensive series of ablation studies.  Systematically remove components (e.g., prediction of different measurements, varying the number of future time steps, different network architectures) and analyze the resulting performance.
*   **Comparison with A3C:** Provide a more detailed comparison with A3C, including learning curves, sample efficiency, and a discussion of the architectural differences and hyperparameter tuning.
*   **Error Analysis:**  Analyze failure cases. Where does the model struggle? What types of scenarios are most challenging?
*   **Generalization:**  The generalization experiments are a good start, but could be extended. Explore generalization to entirely different games or simulated environments.
*   **Clarify Goal Formulation:** The formulation of goals using linear combinations of future measurements is somewhat ad-hoc. A more principled discussion of goal representation and how it influences learning would be beneficial.
* **Code Release:** Releasing the code would dramatically improve the reproducibility and impact of this work.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the computational cost associated with predicting multiple future time steps? Is there a diminishing return in performance as the prediction horizon increases?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of your approach compared to A3C?
3.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of measurement vector? What principles guide the selection of measurements?
4.  What are the limitations of this approach? In what types of environments or tasks would you expect it to underperform?
5.  What connections, if any, can be drawn between this method and existing RL algorithms, particularly in terms of their underlying principles and assumptions?
