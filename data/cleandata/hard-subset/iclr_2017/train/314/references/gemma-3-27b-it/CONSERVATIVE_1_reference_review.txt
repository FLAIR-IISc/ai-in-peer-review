This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem focused on predicting the effects of actions on future measurements rather than relying on traditional reward-based reinforcement learning. The core idea – leveraging the rich supervisory signal present in the interlocked temporal structure of sensory input and measurements – is interesting and potentially valuable, particularly in environments where sparse rewards are problematic. The authors demonstrate promising results in Doom, even winning a competition track, outperforming more complex and heavily supervised baselines.

**Reject.** While the empirical results are compelling, the paper feels underdeveloped and relies too heavily on a single, albeit impressive, experimental validation. The leap from a successful Doom implementation to a broadly applicable control framework requires more rigorous justification and broader experimentation. The reliance on a carefully engineered environment (Doom, even with randomized textures) limits the generalizability claims.

The primary concern is the lack of thorough comparison with well-established RL algorithms across a wider range of tasks. While DQN, A3C and DSR are included, the experimental setup and hyperparameter tuning for these baselines feel somewhat cursory (stated as "best results we were able to obtain"). A more systematic and fair comparison, perhaps using standardized benchmarks, is crucial. The ablation study is helpful, but it doesn't fully address the question of why this approach *consistently* outperforms established RL methods. The superiority in the more complex Doom scenarios is attributed to richer measurements, but this feels more like an observation than a rigorous analysis. Is the benefit truly the multivariate nature of the feedback, or is it something else inherent to the Doom environment and measurement choices? 

Furthermore, the practical considerations of defining meaningful “measurements” in real-world robotic applications are glossed over. While the authors acknowledge this, they don’t offer sufficient guidance on *how* to select or engineer these measurements effectively. This is a critical practical hurdle for applying this approach outside of simulated gaming environments. The method’s sensitivity to measurement choice and the potential for suboptimal performance with poorly chosen measurements are not explored. The discussion around generalizing across goals feels promising but lacks sufficient detail to assess its robustness. A more comprehensive evaluation involving a diverse set of goal parameters and unseen goal combinations is needed.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Broader Benchmarking:**  Evaluate the approach on more standardized RL benchmarks (e.g., Mujoco, OpenAI Gym) alongside a wider range of algorithms (PPO, SAC).
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Provide a more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity, especially concerning the choice of temporal offsets and the weighting of future measurements.
*   **Measurement Selection:**  Discuss strategies for selecting informative measurements in real-world scenarios. Perhaps a pre-training phase to identify relevant features? Explore automated measurement discovery techniques.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** While empirical results are strong, a more formal analysis of why this supervised learning approach outperforms RL in certain scenarios would be beneficial.  Is there a theoretical advantage related to sample efficiency or gradient stability?
*   **Scalability:** How does the computational cost scale with the dimensionality of the sensory input and the number of actions?
*   **Comparison to Model-Based RL:**  A clearer distinction from model-based RL approaches would be helpful, as both involve predicting future states.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Beyond Doom, on what other environments have you tested this approach, and what were the results?
2.  Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process for the baseline RL algorithms? Were grid searches or more sophisticated optimization techniques employed?
3.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of temporal offsets (τ1, ..., τn)? Are there any guidelines for selecting optimal values for these offsets?
4.  In real-world applications, defining appropriate measurements is crucial. What strategies would you recommend for identifying and selecting informative measurements?
5.  What is the computational complexity of the training process, and how does it scale with the dimensionality of the input and the number of actions?
