## ICLR 2024 Review: Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control

**1. Summary of Claims:**

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control that reframes reinforcement learning as a supervised learning problem. Instead of relying on sparse scalar rewards, the authors propose predicting future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) given current sensory input, actions, and goals. This "direct future prediction" (DFP) leverages the rich temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams to provide dense, informative supervision. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance in the challenging Doom environment, outperforming established deep RL methods, generalizing across environments and goals, and even winning the Visual Doom AI Competition. The core claim is that dense, multi-dimensional feedback, readily available in many real-world scenarios, can significantly enhance learning and generalization compared to traditional reward-based RL.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling and well-supported approach that addresses important challenges in reinforcement learning, particularly in complex, visually-rich environments. The strong empirical results, including winning a competitive benchmark, convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. 

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The shift from reward-based RL to direct prediction is a genuinely novel idea, well-motivated by the limitations of sparse rewards in realistic environments. The paper effectively argues for the suitability of a supervised learning perspective when dense feedback is available, drawing on relevant literature.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The experimental results are impressive.  The consistent outperformance of DFP over strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across multiple Doom scenarios is a significant achievement.  The detailed ablation studies demonstrate the importance of both multi-dimensional measurements *and* predicting future values at multiple time steps, bolstering the claims. Winning the Visual Doom AI Competition provides external validation of the approach's effectiveness.
* **Generalization and Goal Flexibility:** The demonstration of generalization across different environments (textured mazes) and the ability to adapt to dynamically specified goals are important strengths. This highlights a key advantage of the proposed method over approaches tightly coupled to a specific reward function.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology, experimental setup, and results. The architecture diagram (Figure 1) is helpful. The comparison to prior work is thorough and accurately contextualizes the contribution.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are strong, a more formal theoretical justification for why DFP works so well would be valuable. Exploring connections to existing concepts like dynamic programming or information theory could strengthen the paper.  Is there a theoretical argument for why predicting *multiple* future time steps is beneficial?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  A discussion of the sensitivity of the method to key hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, network architecture, time horizon for prediction) would be helpful.  Were any significant hyperparameter tuning efforts required, and how robust is the performance to changes in these parameters?
* **Computational Cost:**  While training speed is mentioned, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of DFP compared to the baselines would be beneficial. Is there a trade-off between sample efficiency and computational resources?
* **Limitations:** A more explicit discussion of the limitations of the approach would be valuable. Are there scenarios where DFP might not perform well? (e.g., environments with very sparse or delayed feedback). 
* **Visualization of Predictions:** Including visualizations of the model’s predictions of future measurements would provide further insight into how the method works and potentially help diagnose any failure cases.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*  You state that predicting full sensory streams is currently beyond capabilities.  Are there specific challenges (e.g., high dimensionality, instability) that prevent this, and how might these be addressed in future work?  Could incorporating ideas from recent work on visual prediction (e.g., Kalchbrenner et al., 2016) improve the model?
* In the ablation study (Table 4), the performance degradation when predicting only the farthest time step is significant.  What is your intuition for why predicting *multiple* time steps is so crucial?
*  Could you elaborate on the details of the training procedure for the Visual Doom AI Competition? What specific hyperparameters and training strategies were used to achieve the winning performance?
* You mention the potential for extending this approach to continuous action spaces.  What specific challenges do you anticipate in adapting DFP to continuous control, and how might they be addressed?



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. The novel approach, compelling empirical results, and clear presentation warrant acceptance with minor revisions.
