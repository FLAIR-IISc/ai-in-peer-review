This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem focused on predicting the effects of actions on future measurements, rather than relying on the traditional reward-based reinforcement learning paradigm. The core idea – leveraging the rich supervisory signal inherent in the interlocked temporal structure of sensory input and measurements – is compelling and potentially significant. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results in the challenging Doom environment, even winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming a more complex model with additional supervision.

**Reject.** While the approach is highly innovative and the results impressive, the paper feels somewhat incomplete in its justification and analysis, relying heavily on empirical success without deeply exploring *why* this method works so well. The novelty is substantial enough to warrant consideration, but I'm not convinced the authors have fully articulated the theoretical underpinnings and scope of their contribution.

The central argument – that dense, multidimensional measurements offer a superior supervisory signal compared to sparse scalar rewards – is intuitively appealing and aligns with observations of biological learning systems. However, the paper doesn’t rigorously establish this claim beyond the empirical results. The comparison to existing RL methods feels somewhat cursory; a more in-depth ablation study probing the *specific* benefits of the measurement stream’s density and dimensionality would be valuable. Furthermore, while the authors acknowledge the connection to existing work on prediction-based control, they don’t fully address how their approach differs or improves upon these prior efforts beyond the specific implementation details. 

The experimental results are strong, particularly the performance in the Visual Doom AI Competition. However, the paper could benefit from a more systematic analysis of the learned representations. What do the learned predictors actually capture about the environment? Visualizing the activations of the network, or analyzing the learned goal embeddings, could provide valuable insights. The generalization results are promising, but further investigation into *which* aspects of the environment contribute to generalization (e.g., textures, object arrangements) would strengthen the claims.  The ablation study, while present, focuses primarily on the benefit of vectorial feedback and multi-step prediction, and doesn't explore alternative measurement representations or architectures in depth.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Theoretical grounding:** While a formal theoretical analysis might be beyond the scope of a single paper, a more detailed discussion of the theoretical implications of framing RL as a supervised prediction problem would be beneficial. For instance, how does this relate to concepts like representational learning or information bottleneck?
*   **Connection to related work:** The authors should more explicitly discuss how their approach relates to recent advances in world models and predictive coding.
*   **Robustness and limitations:**  The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the potential limitations of the approach. For example, how sensitive is the performance to the choice of measurements? Does it struggle in environments with noisy or ambiguous measurements?
*   **Goal representation:** The linear goal representation feels somewhat restrictive. Exploring alternative goal representations, such as hierarchical goals or learned goal embeddings, could lead to further improvements.
*   **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The paper should report the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters, particularly the learning rate and the weights assigned to different temporal offsets in the loss function.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Beyond the empirical demonstration, what theoretical justification supports the claim that a dense stream of measurements is fundamentally more efficient for learning sensorimotor control than a sparse scalar reward?
2.  How does this approach differ from, and potentially improve upon, existing methods that also focus on predicting future states (e.g., world models, successor representations)?
3.  The ablation study demonstrates the benefit of vectorial feedback. Could you provide more details about *which* measurements are most informative and how the model learns to utilize them?
4.  Can you elaborate on the choice of the linear goal representation? Have you explored alternative goal representations, and if so, what were the results?
5.  How robust is the approach to noisy or ambiguous measurements? Does the performance degrade significantly in such scenarios?
