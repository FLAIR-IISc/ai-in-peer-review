This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem focused on predicting future measurements given current sensory input, actions, and goals. This departs from traditional reward-based reinforcement learning, leveraging a dense, multi-dimensional measurement stream as a supervisory signal. The authors demonstrate strong performance in the Doom environment, exceeding state-of-the-art deep RL methods and winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Reject.** While the results are impressive and the approach conceptually interesting, the paper lacks sufficient clarity in several areas, particularly regarding the practical benefits over well-established RL methods and a deeper analysis of why this supervised learning formulation performs so well. The empirical justification feels somewhat like a “black box” success story without enough explanation of the underlying mechanisms.

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of a convincing argument for *why* this approach works so well, beyond simply stating that dense measurements provide better supervision. While intuitively appealing, this isn’t a sufficient justification, especially given the established success of methods built around sparse rewards and temporal difference learning. The paper doesn’t delve deeply into the connection between prediction accuracy of the measurements and successful control, or provide any theoretical insight into the advantages of this formulation. The comparison to existing RL methods feels somewhat superficial; a more thorough analysis of why these baselines struggle in the presented scenarios would strengthen the contribution.

A second, contributing reason for rejection is the presentation. While the paper is generally well-written, the details of the experimental setup and network architecture are somewhat buried in the appendices and can be difficult to follow. The ablation study is a good start, but more targeted ablations (e.g., varying the number of future time steps predicted, or the dimensionality of the measurement space) would provide more nuanced insights.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Lack of Theoretical Grounding:** The paper doesn't offer a strong theoretical justification for why directly predicting future measurements outperforms established RL techniques. It mentions Jordan & Rumelhart (1992), but doesn’t fully leverage that work to explain *when* this supervised learning approach should be preferred.
* **Limited Mechanistic Insight:** The impressive empirical results aren’t accompanied by a detailed analysis of *how* the model achieves its performance. What aspects of the learned prediction function are most crucial for successful control? Are specific measurements more important than others? Understanding these details is vital for advancing the field.
* **Superficial Baseline Comparison:** The comparisons to DQN, A3C, and DSR are presented primarily as performance numbers. A deeper dive into the specific challenges each algorithm faces in the Doom environment, and why the proposed approach circumvents these challenges, would be far more informative.
* **Presentation Issues:** Important details about the network architecture and experimental setup are relegated to the appendices, hindering clarity and accessibility. The description of the goal randomization regimes could be more precise.

**Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **Theoretical Analysis:** Explore the theoretical implications of formulating control as a prediction problem. Could this be connected to concepts like dynamic programming or optimal control?
* **Ablation Study Expansion:** Perform more targeted ablations. Investigate the impact of: (1) the number of future time steps predicted; (2) the dimensionality of the measurement space; (3) the specific architecture of the prediction network (e.g., removing the action stream); (4) the effect of different loss functions.
* **Visualization of Learned Representations:** Visualize the learned representations within the network to gain insights into what features the model is extracting and how they relate to successful control.
* **Error Analysis:** Analyze failure cases to understand the limitations of the approach and identify potential areas for improvement.
* **Clarify Goal Randomization:** Be more precise in describing how goals were randomized during training. Was there any constraint on the magnitude of the goal vector?
* **Reproducibility:** Provide more detailed information about the experimental setup, including hyperparameter settings and computational resources used, to facilitate reproducibility.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why you believe the dense, multi-dimensional measurement stream is inherently more informative than a sparse scalar reward, particularly in the context of environments where designing a good reward function is challenging?
2.  What specific aspects of the network architecture (e.g., the expectation/action stream split) do you believe are most critical for its success?
3.  Have you investigated the correlation between the accuracy of the future measurement predictions and the performance of the agent? Does a higher prediction accuracy directly translate to better control?
4.  Why did you choose to predict multiple future time steps (τ1, ..., τn)? Was there a diminishing return in performance as the prediction horizon increased?
5.  How does the performance of this approach scale with the complexity of the environment and the number of available actions? Would the advantage diminish in even more complex scenarios?
