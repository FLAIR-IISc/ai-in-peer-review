## ICLR 2024 Review: Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control

**1. Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control that departs from traditional reward-based reinforcement learning (RL). Instead of relying on sparse scalar rewards, the authors propose predicting future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) directly from raw sensory input and actions. This frames the problem as supervised learning, leveraging the rich temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams. The core idea is compelling: by learning to predict the consequences of actions, the agent can effectively act towards achieving goals defined in terms of future measurements. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results in the challenging Doom environment, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL methods and even winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition. They also demonstrate generalization across environments and goals, and provide a convincing argument for the benefits of vectorial feedback over scalar rewards.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of RL, offering a promising alternative to traditional methods, particularly in environments with dense, multi-dimensional feedback. The strong empirical results, including the competitive win, are very convincing.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The core idea of framing sensorimotor control as a supervised learning problem via future prediction is novel and well-motivated. The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional RL in complex environments and positions their approach as a potentially more efficient and stable alternative. The argument that dense, multi-dimensional feedback is more natural for learning in immersive environments is particularly persuasive.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling. The consistently superior performance compared to state-of-the-art deep RL methods across various Doom scenarios provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Winning the Visual Doom AI Competition is a particularly significant achievement, demonstrating the practicality and competitive edge of the method.
* **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies effectively isolate the contributions of different components of the model, demonstrating the importance of vectorial feedback and multi-step future prediction. This adds credibility to the findings and provides valuable insights into the underlying mechanisms.
* **Generalization Capabilities:** The demonstration of generalization to unseen environments and goals further strengthens the contribution, suggesting the approach is not simply overfitting to the training scenarios.  The ability to adapt to new goals without retraining is a significant advantage.
* **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the methodology and results. The presentation is logical and easy to follow.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

* **Theoretical Analysis:** While the empirical results are strong, a more rigorous theoretical analysis would further strengthen the paper.  Exploring the convergence properties of this Monte Carlo-like approach, especially in comparison to TD learning, would be valuable. The paper acknowledges this point but a brief discussion of potential theoretical challenges would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  A more detailed discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity would be helpful.  How robust is the performance to changes in learning rate, batch size, and network architecture?
* **Computational Cost:** While training speed is mentioned, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of training the model, particularly compared to the baseline methods, would be useful.
* **Discussion of Failure Cases:** While the paper focuses on successes, discussing any failure cases or limitations of the approach would enhance its credibility. Are there scenarios where this approach struggles?
* **Relationship to Model-Predictive Control:** The method has strong connections to model-predictive control (MPC). Explicitly acknowledging and discussing this relationship, and how this work differs from standard MPC approaches, would be valuable.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the goal vector *g*:** The paper states the goal can be expressed in terms of future measurements.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the goal vector *g*? Does the model learn a good internal representation of the goal, or is the performance highly dependent on a carefully engineered *g*?
* **Regarding temporal offsets τ:** How were the temporal offsets (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32) chosen? Was a systematic search performed, or were these values selected based on intuition?
* **Regarding computational complexity:**  Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources required for training (e.g., GPU hours)?
* **Regarding scalability to more complex environments:**  Do you anticipate any significant challenges in scaling this approach to even more complex, real-world environments with significantly higher dimensional state spaces?



Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field. The strong empirical results and compelling motivation justify acceptance with minor revisions. I believe this work has the potential to significantly impact the development of more robust and efficient sensorimotor control systems.
