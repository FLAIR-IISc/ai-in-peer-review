## Review of "Direct Future Prediction for Sensorimotor Control"

**1. Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem. Instead of relying on sparse scalar rewards common in reinforcement learning, the authors leverage the rich, temporally dense information present in streams of sensory input and measurements. The core idea is to train an agent to predict the effects of its actions on future measurements, effectively bypassing the need for a traditional reward function. The paper demonstrates strong performance in the Doom environment, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL models and even winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.  The authors provide a compelling argument for the benefits of this approach, particularly in complex, immersive environments.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning, demonstrating a promising alternative to traditional reward-based methods. The empirical results are strong, and the approach appears to offer advantages in terms of sample efficiency and generalization.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The approach of framing sensorimotor control as a direct prediction problem is a well-motivated and interesting departure from standard RL. The authors effectively argue that dense, multi-dimensional feedback is often more appropriate than sparse rewards, especially in realistic environments.  The connection to earlier work (Jordan & Rumelhart, Sutton) is clearly articulated, and the paper builds logically upon this foundation.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results in the Doom environment are impressive.  Outperforming state-of-the-art RL algorithms, particularly on more complex tasks, is a strong indicator of the method’s effectiveness. Winning the Visual Doom AI Competition is a particularly compelling demonstration of its practical applicability.  The ablation studies further support the claims, showing the benefit of vectorial feedback and multi-step prediction.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach.  The architecture is described in sufficient detail, and the experimental setup is well-defined.
* **Generalization:** The experiments demonstrating generalization across different Doom environments are crucial. This addresses a common criticism of RL algorithms, which often struggle to generalize beyond the specific training environment.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Theoretical Analysis:** While the empirical results are strong, a more detailed theoretical analysis of the proposed method would be beneficial.  For example, it would be interesting to explore the convergence properties of the direct prediction approach compared to traditional TD learning.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  A more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would strengthen the paper.  How robust is the approach to changes in learning rate, network architecture, and other key parameters?
* **Computational Cost:**  A discussion of the computational cost of the approach compared to other RL methods would be useful.  Is the increased sample efficiency offset by increased computational requirements?
* **Broader Applicability:**  While Doom is a compelling test environment, discussing the potential challenges and limitations of applying this approach to other types of tasks (e.g., robotics) would be valuable.
* **Goal Vector Selection:** The paper mentions different strategies for defining the goal vector. Further elaboration on the practical considerations involved in selecting an appropriate goal representation for different tasks would be beneficial.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the goal vector:** In the experiments, you use a linear combination of future measurements. Have you explored other functional forms for `u(f; g)` (e.g., neural networks)? If so, what were the results?
* **Regarding theoretical convergence:** Do you have any insights into the convergence properties of the proposed method, even if it's just a heuristic argument?
* **Regarding computational cost:** How does the training time of your approach compare to that of A3C and DSR, considering the same number of simulation steps?
* **Regarding the action stream in the network:** The normalization layer in the action stream is an interesting idea. Have you experimented with different normalization schemes or found this to be particularly sensitive to its specific implementation?
* **Regarding negative results:** Were there any scenarios or tasks where the direct future prediction approach performed poorly compared to traditional RL methods?  If so, what factors might have contributed to this?



Overall, this is a well-written and compelling paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning.  The strong empirical results and novel approach warrant acceptance. Addressing the minor feedback points would further strengthen the paper.
