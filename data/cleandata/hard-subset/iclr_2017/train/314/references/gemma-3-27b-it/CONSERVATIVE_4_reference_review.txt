This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem focused on predicting future measurements rather than relying on the traditional reward-based reinforcement learning paradigm. The core idea is that the temporal interplay between sensory input and measurements provides a rich supervisory signal, enabling learning from raw experience without explicit rewards. The authors demonstrate promising results in the Doom environment, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL methods and winning a competition track.

**Reject.** While the empirical results are encouraging, the paper presents a significant departure from established reinforcement learning principles without sufficient theoretical justification or ablation studies to fully understand *why* this approach works so well. The success in Doom, while impressive, doesn’t automatically generalize to other problem domains. The paper relies heavily on showcasing performance without fully dissecting the underlying mechanisms.

The primary concern lies with the lack of deeper analysis. The claim that dense, multi-dimensional feedback is inherently superior to sparse rewards is not novel (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992), but the paper doesn’t offer a compelling theoretical reason *why* this supervised learning formulation is effective in situations where traditional RL algorithms struggle. Is it simply the density of the signal, or are there other factors at play, such as reducing the credit assignment problem? The authors state this reduces sensorimotor control to supervised learning, but this glosses over the inherent challenges of prediction in a non-stationary environment. How does the method cope with distributional shift or unexpected events that deviate from the training data?

Furthermore, while the ablation studies are present, they feel incomplete. The ablation focusing on scalar vs. vectorial feedback is valuable, but the impact of the multiple temporal offsets isn’t thoroughly explored. Why were those specific offsets chosen? Does performance degrade significantly with fewer offsets, or with different offset patterns? The study comparing training regimes (fixed vs. randomized goals) is insightful, but doesn’t explore intermediate levels of goal randomization.

The connection to Monte Carlo methods is also somewhat superficial. The authors acknowledge the historical preference for TD learning, but don’t offer a detailed comparison of computational cost or sample efficiency. Simply stating that their Monte Carlo-type method "performs extremely well" isn’t sufficient; a quantitative comparison against TD-based methods would strengthen the argument. The improvement over the second-best submission in the Doom competition is highlighted, but the details of that submission's architecture and training procedure are crucial for understanding the significance of this win. A more detailed comparative analysis is needed.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Theoretical Analysis:** Provide a more rigorous theoretical justification for the proposed approach. Explore the relationship between the prediction task and the optimal control problem. Could this be framed within a model-predictive control framework?
*   **Detailed Ablation Studies:** Conduct more extensive ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component of the model. Specifically, investigate the impact of different temporal offsets, network architectures, and loss functions.
*   **Comparison to TD Learning:** Include a direct comparison against established TD-based RL algorithms (e.g., DQN, A3C) with carefully tuned hyperparameters.
*   **Generalization Analysis:** While the results on the textured environments are encouraging, a broader evaluation on more diverse tasks would strengthen the claims of generalization.
*   **Computational Cost:** Provide a detailed analysis of the computational resources required for training and inference. Compare the computational efficiency of this approach to traditional RL algorithms.
*   **Robustness to Noise:** Analyze the robustness of the model to noisy sensory input and measurements.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the theoretical justification for choosing to predict *future* measurements specifically? Why not predict the immediate effect of an action?
2.  How does the model handle situations where the environment dynamics are unpredictable or change significantly over time?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost and sample efficiency of this approach versus traditional RL algorithms?
4.  What is the sensitivity of the model’s performance to the choice of temporal offsets?
5.  Could you share the architectural details and training procedure of the second-best submission in the Visual Doom AI Competition to allow for a more informed comparison?
