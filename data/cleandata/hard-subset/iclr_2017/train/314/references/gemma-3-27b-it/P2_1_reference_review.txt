## ICLR Review

**1. Summary of Claims:**

This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control that frames the problem as supervised learning rather than reinforcement learning. The key idea is to leverage the rich supervisory signal present in the interlocked temporal structure of sensory input and intrinsic measurements. Instead of a sparse scalar reward, the agent learns to predict the effects of its actions on future measurements, effectively turning control into a prediction problem. The authors demonstrate superior performance compared to state-of-the-art deep RL methods in the challenging Doom environment, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition. They also show generalization across environments and goals, and highlight the benefits of using vectorial measurements.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling alternative to traditional RL, demonstrates strong empirical results on a challenging benchmark, and offers valuable insights into the benefits of dense, multi-dimensional supervision.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The paper addresses a core challenge in RL – learning from raw sensory input in complex environments. The re-framing of the problem as supervised prediction is insightful and well-motivated, drawing on existing literature that suggests SL can be advantageous with dense feedback. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of sparse reward signals in traditional RL and present a convincing argument for their approach.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are impressive. Outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL methods on Doom, particularly winning the Full Deathmatch competition, provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The ablation studies further solidify these findings, demonstrating the importance of both vectorial measurements and multi-step prediction.
* **Generalization and Goal Flexibility:** Demonstrating generalization across different Doom environments and the ability to adapt to dynamically specified goals are significant contributions. This suggests the learned representations are more robust and adaptable than those obtained through traditional RL.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method, experiments, and results. The figures and tables are helpful for understanding the key findings.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are strong, a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the convergence properties or sample complexity of the proposed method would strengthen the paper.  Specifically, how does this approach relate to existing theoretical results in supervised learning and how does it avoid potential pitfalls?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity. How sensitive are the results to the choice of network architecture, learning rate, and other hyperparameters?  A sensitivity analysis would increase confidence in the robustness of the findings.
* **Comparison to Imitation Learning:** The paper positions itself against traditional RL but spends less time discussing the relationship to imitation learning. While the authors mention imitation learning in the background, it would be valuable to explicitly compare and contrast the advantages and disadvantages of each approach, particularly in the context of the experiments presented.
* **Detailed Network Architecture:** While Appendix A provides some details, a more detailed description of the network architecture (e.g., number of layers, filter sizes, etc.) in the main body of the paper would be helpful for reproducibility.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  You mention that predicting full sensory streams is currently beyond your capabilities. What specific challenges prevent this, and what potential future developments could overcome them?
2.  The ablation study clearly shows the benefits of multi-step prediction. Is there an optimal number of future time steps to predict, or does performance plateau beyond a certain point?
3.  Regarding the Visual Doom AI Competition, could you provide more details on the specific challenges posed by the previously unseen environments in the Full Deathmatch track? What aspects of your approach contributed most to its success in this setting?
4.  The choice of goal function `u(f;g) = g>f` is relatively simple. Have you experimented with more complex goal functions, and if so, how did they affect performance and generalization?
