## Review of "Learning to Act from Raw Experience by Predicting Future Measurements"

**1. Summary of Claims:**

This paper proposes a novel approach to reinforcement learning (RL) that reframes sensorimotor control as a supervised learning problem. Instead of relying on a scalar reward signal, the authors leverage the rich temporal structure of concurrent sensory input and measurements (e.g., health, ammo) to predict the effects of actions on future measurements. This "direct future prediction" (DFP) approach aims to overcome the challenges of sparse rewards in complex environments and facilitate learning adaptable skills. The paper demonstrates superior performance to state-of-the-art deep RL methods in the Doom environment, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling and well-supported approach that addresses a significant challenge in RL – learning from sparse or delayed rewards. The strong empirical results, particularly the performance in the Visual Doom AI Competition, are highly convincing.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The core idea of framing sensorimotor control as a prediction problem is insightful. The authors effectively argue that dense, multivariate feedback from measurements provides a richer supervisory signal than traditional sparse rewards, particularly in immersive environments. The connection to earlier work on SL vs. RL (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992) is well-placed.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The results across the four Doom scenarios are consistently positive, demonstrating the effectiveness of DFP compared to strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR).  The win in the Visual Doom AI Competition is particularly significant, as it shows the method's ability to generalize to unseen environments.
* **Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies convincingly demonstrate the importance of both vectorial feedback and multi-step prediction. This strengthens the argument for the benefits of the proposed approach.
* **Generalization Ability:**  The experiments on randomly textured environments (D3-tx and D4-tx) reveal good generalization performance, particularly when the model is trained with a diverse range of goals.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach and experiments. The architectural diagram (Figure 1) is helpful.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are strong, a more detailed theoretical discussion of *why* DFP works well would be beneficial. Is there a formal connection to policy optimization or value function approximation? Elaborating on the theoretical underpinnings could significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning for baselines but provides limited details about the hyperparameters used for DFP. A more comprehensive discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity, perhaps with an ablation study focused on key parameters, would be helpful.
* **Computational Cost:** A comparison of the computational cost (training time, memory requirements) of DFP with the baselines would be valuable. Is the improved performance achieved at a significant computational cost?
* **Goal Representation:** The paper assumes a linear relationship between goal parameters and future measurements (Equation 1). Discussing the potential impact of using more complex goal representations (e.g., non-linear functions) could provide further insights.
* **Error Analysis:**  Analyzing the types of errors made by the DFP model could reveal potential areas for improvement. For example, are there specific situations where the prediction of future measurements fails?



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the theoretical motivations behind the effectiveness of DFP?  Is there a connection to existing RL algorithms or theoretical frameworks?
2.  What was the range of hyperparameters explored for DFP, and how sensitive was performance to different choices?
3.  How does the computational cost of training and running DFP compare to the baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR)?
4.  The paper focuses on linear goals (Equation 1). Have you explored more complex goal representations, and if so, what were the results?
5.  Can you provide examples of situations where the DFP model fails to accurately predict future measurements, and what are the potential causes of these failures?
