This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem by predicting future measurements from sensory input and actions, rather than relying on sparse scalar rewards typical in reinforcement learning. The core idea is that the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides a rich supervisory signal, enabling learning from raw experience without extraneous data. The authors demonstrate promising results in Doom, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL models and winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Reject.** While the core idea is interesting and the empirical results are compelling, particularly the competition win, the paper suffers from a multitude of inconsistencies, insufficiently detailed methodology, and a lack of rigorous analysis that prevents a positive recommendation. The presentation is often imprecise, making it difficult to ascertain the exact contributions and validate the claims.

The primary concerns stem from the following:

1. **Lack of Methodological Detail:** The architecture description (Section 3.2) is vague. The figure referenced is not informative enough to understand the network structure. What are the dimensions of each layer? How many filters are used in the convolutional network? The ablation study (Section 4.2) lacks crucial details regarding the hyperparameters used for each variant. It's unclear how the ablated models were trained and what exactly was modified. The choice of temporal offsets (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32) is arbitrary and lacks justification. Why these specific values? Were other configurations explored?

2. **Inconsistent Reporting & Missing Information:**  Table 1 reports average health *at the end of an episode* but doesn’t specify if these are averaged over successful (i.e., not terminated early by health reaching zero) or all episodes. This significantly impacts the interpretation of the results. The training regimes are poorly defined.  "Random goal vector with each value sampled uniformly from [−1, 1]" – does this apply to *every* time step within an episode or just at the episode start? How does this interact with the ε-greedy policy? The baseline implementation details are relegated to an appendix and are surprisingly brief. Were the hyperparameters of the baselines tuned extensively, or were default values used? 

3. **Overstated Claims & Lack of Rigorous Analysis:** The claim of "significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods" (Introduction) requires more rigorous statistical analysis than is presented. The error bars in Figure 3 are missing, making it impossible to assess the statistical significance of the performance differences.  The explanation for why the method generalizes well (Section 4.2) is speculative and lacks supporting evidence. The generalization experiments only test variations in texture; what about variations in environment layout or agent starting positions?  The claim that the method "outperformed the second best submission... by more than 50%" is also somewhat misleading, as details about the competitor model are scarce.

4. **Writing Quality & Precision:** The writing is often imprecise and lacks clarity. For instance, the statement "The measurement vector mt is distinguished from other sensations in two ways" is circular. Both st and mt *are* sensations. The distinction relies on how they are *used* by the model, not on inherent qualities. There are numerous instances of vague language and imprecise descriptions.



**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the relationship to imitation learning:** The paper briefly mentions imitation learning but doesn't fully explore the connections and differences. Is this approach a form of self-imitation? 
*   **Discuss the limitations of the approach:** What types of environments or tasks might be particularly challenging for this method?
*   **Provide pseudocode:** Including pseudocode for the training loop and the action selection process would greatly improve clarity.
*   **Ablate the goal representation:** How does the performance change when the goal is represented in different ways (e.g., using a simpler linear function)?
*   **Investigate the learned representations:** Visualize the learned features in the perception and measurement modules to gain insights into what the model is learning.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a detailed architectural diagram with layer dimensions and filter sizes?
*   What specific hyperparameters were used for each variant in the ablation study?
*   Were the performance differences between your method and the baselines statistically significant? Please provide p-values.
*   Can you elaborate on the details of the second-place submission in the Visual Doom AI Competition?
*   How sensitive is the performance to the choice of temporal offsets?
*   How does the method perform when the goal is changed *during* an episode?



In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting idea and demonstrates promising results, the lack of methodological detail, inconsistent reporting, overstated claims, and imprecise writing prevent a positive recommendation. Significant revisions are needed to address these concerns and establish the validity and significance of the proposed approach.
