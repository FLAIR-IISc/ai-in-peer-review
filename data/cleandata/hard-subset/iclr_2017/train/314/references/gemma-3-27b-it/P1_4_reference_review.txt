## Review for ICLR 2024

**1. Summary of Claims:**

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem rather than traditional reinforcement learning. The core idea is to leverage the rich temporal structure within streams of sensory input and measurements to predict the effect of actions on future measurements. By focusing on predicting these future measurements, the authors bypass the need for sparse scalar rewards, offering denser, more informative supervision. They demonstrate superior performance on visually challenging Doom environments, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL methods, including winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition. They further show generalization capabilities across environments and goals, and highlight the benefit of using vectorial measurements.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling and well-executed idea that addresses key challenges in reinforcement learning. The empirical results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach. The victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition is a significant achievement.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The shift from reward-based RL to a prediction-based framework is a significant departure from standard approaches and is well-motivated. The authors correctly identify the limitations of sparse rewards in complex environments and present a plausible solution. The connection to previous work on SL vs. RL and the discussion of temporal difference learning provide valuable context.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results in Doom are impressive. The consistent outperformance of state-of-the-art methods, particularly in the more challenging scenarios, is strong evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The ablation studies convincingly demonstrate the importance of vectorial feedback and multi-step prediction.  Winning the Visual Doom AI Competition further solidifies these findings.
* **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The authors provide sufficient detail regarding the model architecture, training procedure, and experimental setup, enabling reproducibility. The connection between the mathematical formulation and the practical implementation is well-explained.
* **Generalization Ability:** Demonstrating generalization across environments and goals is crucial for real-world applicability. The authors successfully show that their approach exhibits good generalization capabilities, making it a more practical solution than methods heavily reliant on environment-specific tuning.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

* **Computational Cost:** While the paper mentions training speed in comparison to baselines, a more detailed analysis of the computational resources required for the proposed method (e.g., GPU hours) would be valuable. This would help understand the practical feasibility of scaling the approach to even more complex environments.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the approach to hyperparameter settings would be beneficial. Are there specific hyperparameters that significantly impact performance, and if so, how were they tuned?
* **Error Analysis:** A qualitative analysis of the types of errors the model makes would provide further insight. Are there specific situations where the model struggles, and what might be the underlying reasons? This would help identify potential areas for future improvement.
* **Connections to World Models:** The work shares similarities with the burgeoning field of "world models," which also focus on learning predictive representations. Explicitly discussing these connections and highlighting the differences would strengthen the paper.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding generalization:** The results in Table 2 suggest that training in a more varied environment (D4-tx) significantly improves generalization. To what extent do you believe this is due to learning more robust representations versus simply overfitting to the larger set of training environments?
* **Regarding goal specification:**  You mention that goals can be expressed as linear combinations of future measurements. Have you explored more complex function forms for `u(f; g)`?  Could learning this function itself be part of the training process?
* **Regarding scalability:** The Doom environment, while complex, is still relatively constrained. How do you foresee this approach scaling to even more high-dimensional and unstructured environments (e.g., real-world robotics)?  What are the key challenges in adapting the method to such scenarios?
* **Regarding action selection:** Equation (3) describes a deterministic action selection. Have you explored stochastic action selection, and if so, what impact did it have on performance?



In conclusion, this is a well-written and compelling paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the feedback outlined above.
