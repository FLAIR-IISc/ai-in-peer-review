This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem by predicting future measurements given sensory input, actions, and goals. The key idea is that the interlocked temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides a rich supervisory signal, circumventing the need for sparse scalar rewards common in traditional reinforcement learning. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach in the challenging Doom environment, achieving state-of-the-art results and winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a compelling idea and strong empirical results, the novelty is not substantial enough to warrant acceptance, and the presentation lacks sufficient detail to fully assess the technical soundness. The core idea of using future prediction as a training signal is not entirely new (see related work), and the paper doesn't sufficiently highlight how their approach differs significantly from existing methods beyond its specific implementation and empirical performance.

The paper’s strength lies in its strong empirical results. The authors convincingly demonstrate superior performance compared to state-of-the-art deep RL methods in a complex environment, including winning a competition. The ablation studies further support the claim that vectorial measurements are beneficial. The generalization experiments are also encouraging, demonstrating the model’s ability to adapt to new environments and goals. However, the empirical sections lack detailed statistical analysis; error bars or significance testing would strengthen the conclusions.

The technical details regarding the network architecture (Figure 1) and training procedure are somewhat lacking. While an appendix is mentioned, key details are missing directly within the main text. For instance, the specific architecture used for the perception, measurement, and goal modules isn’t fully described, limiting reproducibility. The choice of temporal offsets (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32) and their weighting (0.5, 0.5, 1) feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks justification. Further, the description of the training regimes and the hyperparameter search for baselines could be more detailed. The reliance on a custom implementation of A3C raises concerns about fair comparison. The authors state the baselines were tested with various learning rates, but do not specify which hyperparameters performed best for each baseline.

Here are suggestions for improvement:

*   **Novelty:** Explicitly delineate the key differences between the proposed approach and related work, such as predictive state representations and imitation learning. Focus on what uniquely contributes to the observed performance.
*   **Technical Detail:** Provide a more comprehensive description of the network architecture, including the number of layers, filter sizes, and activation functions. Justify the choice of temporal offsets and weighting scheme.
*   **Statistical Rigor:** Include error bars or perform statistical significance testing on the empirical results to strengthen the claims of superiority.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** Use publicly available implementations of the baselines whenever possible to ensure a fair comparison. If custom implementations are used, provide detailed justification and validation.
*   **Goal Agnostic Training:** Provide more insight into *why* training with randomized goals leads to better generalization. Is it exploring a wider action space? Providing more diverse data?

I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  The paper states that "predicting multiple measurements significantly improves the performance." Can you quantify the impact of each additional measurement? Are some measurements more informative than others?
2.  The goal vector *g* is used to parameterize the goal. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of *g*? Is there a method for automatically learning or adapting *g*?
3.  What is the computational cost of the proposed approach compared to the baseline methods?
4.  How does the performance degrade as the dimensionality of the sensory input *s* increases?
5.  You mention limitations regarding predicting full sensory streams. Have you explored alternative methods for dimensionality reduction or feature extraction from the raw sensory input *s* to mitigate this issue?
