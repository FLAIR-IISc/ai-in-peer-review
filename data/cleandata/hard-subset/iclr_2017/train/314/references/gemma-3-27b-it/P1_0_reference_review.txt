## Review of “Learning Sensorimotor Control from Raw Experience as Supervised Learning”

**1. Summary**

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control that reframes the problem as supervised learning. Instead of relying on sparse scalar rewards typical in reinforcement learning (RL), the authors propose leveraging the rich, temporally dense supervisory signal present in the interlocked structure of sensory and measurement streams. The core idea is to predict the effect of actions on future measurements, given current sensory input and measurements, effectively transforming control into a prediction problem. The paper demonstrates strong empirical results in the challenging Doom environment, outperforming state-of-the-art deep RL models and winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition. The authors also show generalization across environments and goals, and highlight the benefit of vectorial measurements over scalar rewards.

**2. Decision**

**Accept** with minor revisions. This paper presents a compelling and well-supported alternative to traditional RL, particularly in scenarios with rich sensory feedback. The strong empirical results, especially the victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition, are highly convincing. The framing of sensorimotor control as a supervised learning problem is insightful and opens avenues for further research.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Novelty & Motivation:** The approach of directly predicting future measurements as a means of control is a novel and well-motivated departure from standard RL. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of sparse rewards in complex environments and provide a strong argument for leveraging dense, multi-dimensional feedback. The connection to previous work (Jordan & Rumelhart, Sutton) is well-established, positioning the work within a broader research context.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are very strong. The consistent outperformance of the proposed method compared to established deep RL algorithms (DQN, A3C, DSR) across multiple Doom scenarios is impressive. The success in the Visual Doom AI Competition, especially exceeding a strong baseline with more complex architecture and additional supervision, is a significant achievement. 
* **Ablation Studies & Generalization:** The ablation studies demonstrating the importance of vectorial measurements and multi-step prediction are valuable. Showing the ability to generalize across different textured environments and learn goal-agnostic policies further strengthens the paper's claims and demonstrates the robustness of the approach.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach, the experimental setup, and the results. The architecture diagram (Figure 1) is helpful, and the inclusion of a supplementary video is a nice touch.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement)**

* **Theoretical Analysis:** While the empirical results are strong, a more in-depth theoretical analysis would strengthen the paper. Could the convergence properties of this approach be analyzed, even if empirically demonstrated?  A discussion of the potential drawbacks or limitations of the approach would also be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning learning rates for the baselines. A discussion of the sensitivity of the *proposed* approach to key hyperparameters (e.g., network architecture, temporal offsets) would be beneficial.
* **Scalability Concerns:** The Doom environment, while challenging, is still a simulated environment. How might this approach scale to more complex, real-world scenarios with even higher-dimensional sensory inputs and more intricate dynamics?
* **Clarification on Goal Representation:** The formulation of the goal as a linear combination of future measurements is clear. However, expanding on the limitations of this representation and discussing potential alternatives (e.g., more complex function `u(f;g)`) might be insightful.
* **Experience Replay:** The paper mentions using an experience replay buffer.  More detail on the sampling strategy (e.g., uniform, prioritized) would be helpful.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

*   Regarding generalization, could you provide more examples of environments or goals where the model *fails* to generalize well? This would provide a more complete picture of the approach's limitations.
*   The architecture is described as being “as close as possible” to DQN. Could you provide a more detailed comparison, listing the specific architectural differences and the rationale behind those choices?
*   You mention that predicting full sensory streams is currently beyond your capabilities. Do you foresee any fundamental limitations preventing this in the future, or is it simply a matter of increased computational resources and improved architectures?
*   In the goal-agnostic training experiments, what specific randomization procedure was used to select the goal vector?  Was it truly random, or was there any bias introduced in the generation process?



In conclusion, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. The proposed approach is innovative, well-motivated, and empirically validated. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh its weaknesses, and I recommend it for acceptance.
