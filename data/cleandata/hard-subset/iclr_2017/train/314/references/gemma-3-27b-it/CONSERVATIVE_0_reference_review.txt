This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem by predicting future measurements given current sensory input and actions, rather than relying on traditional reward-based reinforcement learning. The core idea is that dense, multi-dimensional measurements provide a richer supervisory signal, potentially leading to more stable and efficient learning, particularly in complex environments. The authors demonstrate competitive performance in the Doom environment, even winning a competition against approaches employing more complex architectures and additional supervision.

**Reject.** While the results are promising, the paper presents a significant departure from established RL paradigms without sufficiently convincing evidence of its generalizability and robustness. The improvements over baselines, while impressive in the reported experiments, feel somewhat isolated to the specific Doom environment and task setup. The claim of outperforming a more complex model with additional supervision by 50% requires deeper scrutiny regarding the specifics of that model and the exact nature of the additional supervision.

The primary concern is the limited exploration of the parameter space and the potential sensitivity of the approach to the choice of measurements and temporal offsets. The ablation study is helpful but doesn’t fully address these concerns. The method’s reliance on accurately predicting future measurements seems potentially brittle; minor inaccuracies could compound over time, leading to unstable behavior. The paper lacks a thorough analysis of prediction error and its impact on control performance.

Furthermore, the comparison to other RL methods feels somewhat uneven. A3C, a strong baseline, is presented but the details of its implementation and hyperparameter tuning are not fully transparent. The claim of a "simpler" model compared to the winning competitor in the Doom AI competition downplays the significant architectural choices made in the proposed network (expectation/action streams), which introduce their own complexity. The rationale for these architectural decisions is not fully justified beyond empirical observation.

The paper also leans heavily on the specific characteristics of the Doom environment. The availability of clear, quantifiable measurements like health and ammunition is not representative of many real-world sensorimotor control problems where defining such measurements is challenging. The argument for the superiority of this approach over standard RL hinges on the availability of “dense multidimensional feedback,” but the authors don’t adequately address scenarios where such feedback is limited or noisy.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Detailed Baseline Comparison:** Provide a more thorough description of the A3C implementation, including hyperparameter search details and computational resources used. Include error bars in performance plots to demonstrate statistical significance.
*   **Robustness Analysis:** Investigate the sensitivity of the approach to variations in the choice of measurements and temporal offsets. Perform an ablation study specifically focusing on these parameters.
*   **Error Analysis:** Analyze the prediction error of the measurement prediction network. How does prediction error correlate with control performance? Visualize the predicted vs. actual measurements to identify potential failure modes.
*   **Generalization to Simpler Environments:** Demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach on simpler, well-understood control tasks where the benefits of dense measurement feedback are less pronounced. This will help establish a baseline for comparison with standard RL methods.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** While a full theoretical analysis may be beyond the scope of the paper, some discussion of the convergence properties of the Monte Carlo-type prediction would be beneficial.
*    **Explain architectural choices:** Provide a deeper justification for the expectation/action stream architecture beyond its empirical success. What is the theoretical motivation behind this design?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide more details on the architecture and training procedure of the competing model in the Visual Doom AI Competition, including the specific type and amount of additional supervision used?
*   How does the performance of your approach degrade when the measurement stream is incomplete or noisy?
*   What is the computational cost of training your model compared to the baseline RL methods?
*   Have you investigated alternative network architectures for the future predictor?
*   How do you choose the temporal offsets (τ1, …, τn) for future measurements? Is there a systematic way to determine optimal values for these offsets?
