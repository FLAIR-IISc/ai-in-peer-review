This paper frames the problem of sensorimotor control as a supervised learning task. Instead of maximizing a scalar reward, the proposed agent, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), learns to predict the future values of a vector of measurements (e.g., health, ammo) conditioned on the current sensory input, a potential action, and a goal vector. At test time, the agent selects the action that leads to the predicted future measurements most aligned with its current goal. The authors demonstrate that this approach outperforms strong deep reinforcement learning baselines (DQN, A3C) in complex 3D environments (ViZDoom). The paper also shows that the model can be trained without a fixed goal and can generalize to new goals and environments. The strength of the empirical results is highlighted by a win in the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Decision: Reject**

My recommendation is to reject this paper. While the core idea is appealing and the empirical results, particularly the competition win, are impressive, the manuscript is undermined by a significant lack of methodological clarity and a pervasive carelessness in its presentation. Crucial experimental details are either missing or deferred to an appendix that was not provided, making the results difficult to verify and the work impossible to reproduce. Furthermore, the paper contains notable technical and typographical errors that, in aggregate, erode confidence in the scientific rigor of the work.

**Supporting Arguments**

1.  **Critical Methodological Details are Missing:** The paper repeatedly omits key details required to understand and replicate the work, often referring to an appendix (which I assume was not submitted or was missing from the review materials).
    *   **Architecture:** Section 4.1 states the network was configured "to be as close as possible to the DQN model... to ensure a fair comparison," but defers specifics to Appendix A. Without this appendix, the claim of a fair comparison is unsubstantiated. The architecture of the larger D4-tx-L model is also referenced as being in the appendix.
    *   **Training Algorithm:** Section 3.1 states "The parameters of the predictor used by the agent are updated after every k new experiences." The value of `k`, a critical hyperparameter for this style of training, is never specified.
    *   **Loss Function:** The description of the loss weighting in Section 4.1 is ambiguous: "Only the latest three time steps contribute to the objective function, with coefficients (0.5, 0.5, 1)." It is unclear what "latest three time steps" refers to. Does it mean the predictions for the three largest temporal offsets (τ=8, 16, 32)? Or does it imply something about the agent's history? This is a crucial detail of the learning objective that is not adequately explained.
    *   **Goal-Agnostic Experiments:** The goals in Table 3 are described qualitatively (e.g., "pacifist," "aimlessly drain ammunition"). The actual goal vectors `g` used to produce these results are not provided, preventing a precise understanding of the tasks.

2.  **Technical and Notational Inaccuracies:** The paper contains several errors that suggest a lack of careful proofreading.
    *   **Incorrect Mathematical Notation:** In Equation 1 and 3, the utility function and action selection policy are defined using `g>f` and `g>F(...)`, respectively. This appears to be a typo for the dot product, which should be written as `g^T f`. This is a fundamental notational error in the core definition of the model.
    *   **Architectural Justification:** The splitting of the prediction module into "expectation" and "action" streams (Section 3.2) is inspired by the dueling network architecture. However, the paper provides only a vague, intuitive justification ("forcing the expectation stream E to compensate"). It fails to connect this choice back to the original, more formal motivation related to advantage functions, leaving the design feeling ad-hoc.
    *   **Typographical Errors:** There are numerous minor typos, such as "threedimensional" instead of "three-dimensional" (recurring) and an apparent missing number in Section 4.1: "resulting in possible actions" where `2^8=256` was likely intended.

3.  **Flawed Experimental Comparisons and Presentation:**
    *   The comparison against DSR is weak. The authors admit that DSR was trained for far fewer steps due to its computational cost. Including these underdeveloped results in the main comparison (Figure 3, Table 1) is misleading.
    *   The reference section is exceptionally poorly formatted. It contains inconsistent citation styles, missing information (e.g., journal/conference venues), and extraneous characters (e.g., `\Q2006\E`). This level of disorganization is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission and reflects a general lack of care.

**Additional Feedback**

While I cannot recommend acceptance in its current state, the core ideas are promising. To improve the paper, I suggest the following:
*   Integrate all critical experimental details from the appendices into the main paper. A reader should not need to hunt for the network architecture, key hyperparameters, or loss function details to understand the work.
*   Perform a thorough proofread of the entire manuscript to fix all notational errors (especially the dot product), typos, and grammatical mistakes.
*   Completely reformat the bibliography to a consistent and standard style (e.g., the ICLR style).
*   The justification for the `st`/`mt` split is framed as a practical limitation. It would strengthen the paper to discuss this more deeply. Is there a guiding principle for what constitutes a "measurement" versus "sensory input"? Could this distinction be learned?
*   The connection to model-based RL is understated. The predictor `F` is essentially learning a model of the environment's dynamics with respect to the measurement space. Explicitly positioning the work in this context could provide deeper insight.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the exact weighting scheme for the loss function based on the temporal offsets {1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32}?
2.  What was the update frequency `k` for the agent's policy network during training?
3.  Regarding Equations 1 and 3, is `g>f` intended to mean the dot product `g^T f`?
4.  Can you provide the precise network architectures (layer types, sizes, activations) for your DFP model and the baselines you implemented, as this is essential for reproducibility and for validating the fairness of the comparison?
