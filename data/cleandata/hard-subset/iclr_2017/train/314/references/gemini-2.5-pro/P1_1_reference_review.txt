### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel and effective approach to sensorimotor control, called Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which reframes the problem as a supervised learning task. Instead of relying on a scalar reward signal as in conventional reinforcement learning (RL), the agent learns to predict a vector of future "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input, current measurements, a potential action, and a goal representation. By acting greedily with respect to these predictions, the agent can learn complex behaviors from raw visual input in immersive 3D environments. The key contributions are: 1) A simple yet powerful formulation of sensorimotor control as a supervised regression problem, leveraging dense, multi-dimensional feedback streams. 2) A demonstration that this approach supports training without a fixed objective, allowing the agent to pursue dynamically specified goals at test time. 3) A comprehensive empirical evaluation in challenging Doom scenarios, showing that DFP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods like A3C and DQN. 4) Strong evidence of generalization across both unseen environments and novel goals, validated by winning the Visual Doom AI Competition.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Effectiveness and Simplicity:** The paper introduces a simple, elegant, and highly effective alternative to standard RL methods for an important class of problems. By directly predicting future outcomes instead of estimating value functions, the method leverages the rich supervisory signals available in many environments, leading to state-of-the-art performance on complex, dynamic tasks.
2.  **Exceptional Empirical Support:** The claims are backed by rigorous and extensive experiments. The comparisons to strong baselines (A3C, DQN) are thorough and reveal a dramatic performance advantage for DFP on challenging tasks. The generalization experiments (across textures and goals) and the ablation study convincingly support the core ideas, and the victory in a public competition provides powerful, objective validation of the method's real-world efficacy and robustness.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's core idea is to move away from the Bellman equation-based optimization common in RL and instead use supervised learning to directly predict the consequences of actions on a set of meaningful measurements. This is a compelling shift in perspective that is well-grounded in early literature (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992) but has been underexplored in the deep learning era. The authors convincingly argue that for environments with rich, multi-dimensional feedback (like most robotics or immersive simulation tasks), this direct prediction approach can be far more effective than trying to learn from a sparse, scalar reward.

The empirical results are outstanding. In the complex "Battle" scenarios, DFP outperforms A3C, a strong state-of-the-art baseline, by a factor of 2.5-6x in terms of frags. This is not an incremental improvement but a significant leap in performance. The ability to train a single "goal-agnostic" agent that can then be directed to perform various, even contradictory, tasks at test time (e.g., maximizing kills vs. being a pacifist) is a major step towards flexible and generalizable agents. This is a key advantage over standard RL agents that are typically trained for a single, fixed reward function.

Furthermore, the win in the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition is a particularly strong piece of evidence. This track explicitly tests for generalization to unseen environments. The fact that DFP, a conceptually simpler model, outperformed a more complex, specially-tuned recurrent model (Lample & Chaplot, 2016) that used additional privileged information during training, speaks volumes about the power and robustness of the proposed approach. The paper is also very well-written, clearly articulating the method, its motivation, and the experimental findings.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarifying the Sensory/Measurement Distinction:** The paper rightly notes that the distinction between sensory input (`s_t`) and measurements (`m_t`) is somewhat artificial. It might be clearer to frame this as a distinction between high-dimensional, unstructured sensory data (pixels) and a low-dimensional, structured state representation (game stats). It would be interesting to discuss the implications of this distinction. The current approach requires these measurements to be provided by the environment. A brief discussion on how one might learn or define these measurements in environments where they are not readily available would strengthen the paper's outlook on future work.
*   **Connection to Model-Based RL:** The approach has a strong model-based flavor, as it learns a predictive model of future outcomes (`m_{t+k}`). While the paper positions the work in contrast to TD and policy gradient methods, a more explicit discussion of its relationship to model-based RL could be beneficial. For instance, DFP learns a very specific type of transition model (mapping `(s, m, a) -> Δm`) rather than a full state-transition model. A comparison of the pros and cons of this design choice would be insightful.
*   **Justification of Loss Weighting:** In Section 4.1, it is mentioned that only the last three temporal offsets contribute to the loss function, with weights (0.5, 0.5, 1). This seems like a potentially important design choice. Could you provide some intuition or empirical justification for this specific weighting scheme? A small sensitivity analysis in the appendix would be helpful.

**Questions:**

1.  How does the Monte Carlo-style training of DFP (waiting for the ground-truth future measurements) compare to the bootstrapping approach of TD methods in terms of sample efficiency and variance in this high-dimensional visual setting? The paper references the historical debate, but a modern perspective in the context of these experiments would be valuable.
2.  The goal `g` is a linear weighting of future measurement changes. How would this framework accommodate more complex, non-linear, or state-dependent goals (e.g., "maintain health above 50," or "collect ammo only when it is below 20")?
3.  The Dueling Network-style architecture splits prediction into expectation and action streams. The ablation study focuses on measurements and temporal offsets. Did you perform any ablation on this architectural choice? How crucial is it for performance compared to a simpler monolithic prediction head?
