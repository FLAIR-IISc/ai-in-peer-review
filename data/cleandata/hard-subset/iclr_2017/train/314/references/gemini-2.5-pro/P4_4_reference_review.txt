### 1. Summary

This paper proposes an approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which frames the problem as a supervised learning task rather than a traditional reinforcement learning one. The core idea is to train a deep neural network to directly predict the future values of a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on the current sensory input, current measurements, a potential action, and a goal representation. The goal is defined as a desired linear combination of future measurement changes. At test time, the agent selects the action that is predicted to yield the best outcome according to the current goal. The authors argue that this formulation leverages the rich, temporally dense supervisory signal provided by a stream of measurements, in contrast to the often sparse scalar reward used in RL. Furthermore, it naturally supports generalization to dynamically specified goals not seen during training. The method is evaluated in a series of increasingly complex 3D visuomotor tasks in the Doom environment, where it is shown to significantly outperform state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods like A3C and DQN, particularly on tasks with richer, multi-dimensional feedback. The paper further substantiates its claims through ablation studies and by demonstrating successful generalization across both environments and goals. A model trained with this approach also won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Exceptional Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling and comprehensive empirical evidence that strongly supports its claims. The proposed method dramatically outperforms well-established and state-of-the-art deep RL baselines on challenging visuomotor control tasks. The rigor of the evaluation, which includes extensive comparisons, ablation studies, generalization tests, and a victory in a public competition, provides a high degree of confidence in the method's effectiveness.

2.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Formulation:** The work successfully reframes the sensorimotor control problem in a simple yet powerful way. While using supervised learning for control is not a new concept, this paper modernizes the idea and demonstrates its viability and superiority over dominant RL approaches in a complex, high-dimensional setting. The motivation to use dense, vectorial feedback instead of a scalar reward is clear, intuitive, and empirically validated by the ablation study.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem statement, proposed method, and experimental setup are described with precision and clarity. The motivation for moving away from the standard RL paradigm is articulated persuasively in the introduction and background sections. The architecture and training procedure are explained in sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is technically sound. It reduces the control problem to a standard regression problem, for which robust optimization techniques exist. The architectural design, which incorporates elements like a dueling-style prediction head, is well-reasoned and builds upon established practices in deep learning. The experimental methodology is rigorous; the authors compare against strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR), report conducting hyperparameter searches for them, and use appropriate metrics for evaluation. The ablation and generalization studies are well-designed to isolate the contributions of different components of the model and to test its robustness.

**Novelty and Significance:** The primary novelty lies in the successful application and demonstration of a direct prediction approach as a powerful alternative to value-function or policy-gradient-based methods for complex visuomotor control. The paper effectively argues, both conceptually and empirically, for a paradigm shift away from a monolithic scalar reward towards richer, multi-dimensional measurement streams when they are available. The significance of the work is underscored by the magnitude of the performance improvement over state-of-the-art RL methods and the success in the Doom AI competition. These results challenge the prevailing consensus and are likely to inspire further research into supervised learning-based approaches for control. The goal-conditioning aspect is also significant, as it provides a simple and effective mechanism for learning flexible, goal-directed behaviors.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended as constructive suggestions and are not critical for the decision.

*   **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of the potential limitations of the DFP approach. For example, in what kinds of environments might it struggle? One could imagine scenarios where the provided measurements are not sufficient to define the true task goal, or where the relationship between actions and long-term measurements is too complex for a direct predictor to capture. Acknowledging these boundaries would strengthen the paper.
*   **Connection to Model-Based RL:** The predictor `F` can be interpreted as a compact, goal-conditioned transition model of the environment that operates only on the measurement space. It would be insightful to discuss the relationship between DFP and more traditional model-based RL methods more directly. Is DFP a specific instance of model-based control, and what are the key differences (e.g., no planning, direct action selection)?
*   **Choice of Temporal Offsets:** The selection of future time steps {1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32} for prediction appears somewhat heuristic. While the ablation shows that using multiple offsets is beneficial, a brief discussion on the sensitivity to these specific values or the strategy for choosing them would be helpful.
*   **Sensory vs. Measurement Streams:** The paper notes that the distinction between sensory input `s_t` and measurements `m_t` is "somewhat artificial." This is a key point worth expanding upon. A discussion on the possibility of learning to identify salient measurements directly from the high-dimensional sensory stream as a future research direction could be a valuable addition to the conclusion.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   How does the deterministic predictor `F(o, a, g)` handle environmental stochasticity? The L2 regression loss would likely train the network to predict the expected future measurements. Was the impact of stochasticity observed in the environments, and have you considered modeling the predictive distribution over future measurements rather than just their expectation?
*   The paper contrasts the approach with RL, yet it shares several components with value-based methods (e.g., experience replay, ε-greedy exploration, dueling-style architecture). Could you elaborate further on what you believe is the fundamental reason for DFP's superior performance? Is it primarily the denser, multi-dimensional supervision signal, or does bypassing the Bellman equation and its associated bootstrapping issues play a more critical role?
*   How does the performance scale with the dimensionality of the measurement vector? While the benefit of three measurements over one is clearly shown, is there a point at which the prediction problem for a high-dimensional measurement vector becomes as challenging as value function approximation, potentially diminishing the advantages of the DFP approach?
