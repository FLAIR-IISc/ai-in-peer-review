### **Review of "Direct Future Prediction"**

**1. Summary of Contributions**
This paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), an approach to sensorimotor control that frames the problem as supervised learning rather than traditional reinforcement learning. The core idea is to train a deep neural network to predict a vector of future "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on the current sensory input, measurements, a proposed action, and a goal vector. At test time, the agent selects the action that leads to the best-predicted outcome according to its current goal. The authors argue that this formulation leverages the rich, temporally dense supervisory signal provided by a stream of measurements, as opposed to a sparse scalar reward. They evaluate DFP in a series of increasingly complex 3D environments based on the game Doom, demonstrating superior performance over state-of-the-art deep RL methods like A3C and DQN. The paper also shows that the learned models can generalize to new environments and dynamically specified goals not seen during training.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**
**Decision:** Reject

While the paper presents a compelling and empirically strong alternative to standard RL formulations, I am recommending rejection. My decision is based on two primary reasons:
1.  A significant lack of precision in the description of the methodology, including ambiguous notation and undefined hyperparameters, which severely hinders reproducibility and a clear understanding of the model.
2.  Substantial sloppiness in the experimental reporting and overall presentation, particularly concerning the baselines and the formatting of the manuscript, which falls short of the standards expected for ICLR.

Collectively, these numerous small-to-medium flaws undermine confidence in the scientific rigor of the work, despite its promising results.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

My concerns are detailed below, organized by section.

**Methodological Ambiguity (Section 3):**
*   **Notation:** The notation for the goal utility function in Equation (1), `u(f; g) = g>f`, and its use in action selection in Equation (3) is non-standard. The `>` symbol is ambiguous. I presume this is intended to be a dot product (`g^T f` or `g \cdot f`), but this should be stated explicitly using standard mathematical notation.
*   **Conflicting Variable Names:** In Section 3.1, the variable `N` is used to denote both the size of the mini-batch sampled from the experience memory and the total number of examples in the training set `D` in Equation (4). This is a confusing and avoidable error.
*   **Missing Hyperparameters:** The text states, "The parameters of the predictor used by the agent are updated after every k new experiences." The value of `k` is a critical hyperparameter that governs the stationarity of the agent's policy, yet its value is never specified anywhere in the paper or the appendix. This is a crucial missing detail.
*   **Architecture Definition:** In Section 3.2, the normalization layer in Equation (6) is written as `Ai(j) = Ai(j) - ...`. This notation typically implies an in-place update, which is confusing when defining a layer's function. It would be clearer to define the output with a new variable, e.g., `A'_i(j) = ...`.
*   **Objective Function:** The paper states that for the objective function, "Only the latest three time steps contribute... with coefficients (0.5, 0.5, 1)." This is a major detail that fundamentally alters the loss function defined in Equation (4). The formal definition of the final loss function is never provided. Is it a weighted L2 loss? Why were these specific coefficients and time steps chosen? This ad-hoc weighting scheme needs to be formally defined and justified.

**Experimental Concerns (Section 4 & Appendix B):**
*   **Baseline Fairness:** The comparison to DSR is particularly weak. The authors admit that DSR is much slower and was only trained for 10 days on the easiest scenario, while other methods trained for 50 million steps. This is not a fair or meaningful comparison. Including these partial results on a single task feels like an attempt to pad the comparison table.
*   **Baseline Implementations:** For A3C, an "independent implementation" was used. While this is common, it introduces a potential confounding variable. The performance of A3C can be sensitive to implementation details, and without validation against the original paper's benchmarks, it's hard to be certain that the baseline is as strong as it could be.
*   **Ablation Study:** In the ablation study (Table 4), the "scalar reward" variant only predicts frags. However, in scenarios D3 and D4, the RL baselines were trained on a composite reward of ammo, health, and frags. This is an inconsistent comparison. A true "scalar reward" ablation would have the model predict the same linear combination of measurements used to train the RL baselines, but collapsed into a single scalar value.

**Presentation and Formatting:**
*   **Reference Formatting:** The references section is exceptionally poorly formatted. There are numerous inconsistencies, typos, and outright errors. For example:
    *   Citations end with periods inconsistently (e.g., "Adolph and Berger.," vs "Blundell et al\.,").
    *   Bizarre artifacts like `? \Q2006\E` appear throughout.
    *   Journal names are abbreviated or incomplete (e.g., "IJRR, 32(11),").
    *   The citation for He et al. (2015) seems to be a garbled note: "He, He,? \Q2015\E, He,".
    *   The Silver et al. (2016) citation is truncated mid-sentence: "...and tree, search. Nature,,".
    This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a submission to a top-tier conference and reflects poorly on the overall diligence of the authors.
*   **Clarity:** The distinction between "sensory input" `s_t` and "measurements" `m_t` is admitted by the authors to be "somewhat artificial." While a pragmatic choice, this conceptual weakness warrants more discussion. Is the DFP framework fundamentally reliant on having a low-dimensional, human-curated set of state variables to predict? How would this scale to problems where such measurements are not readily available?

**4. Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

Despite my recommendation, the core idea is valuable and the results are impressive. To significantly improve this paper, I suggest the following:

*   **Improve Clarity and Precision:**
    *   Clean up all mathematical notation (`g>f`, `N`, Equation 6).
    *   Formally define the exact loss function used, including the weighting scheme for future time steps.
    *   Provide all critical hyperparameters (`k`, learning rates for DFP, `ε`-decay schedule, experience memory size `M`, etc.) in a comprehensive table in the appendix.
*   **Strengthen Experimental Rigor:**
    *   Either remove the DSR comparison or conduct a more thorough one.
    *   Provide more details on the A3C implementation and its performance on a standard benchmark (e.g., a few Atari games) to validate its strength.
    *   Re-run the scalar reward ablation study using the same reward composition as the RL baselines for a more direct comparison.
*   **Thoroughly Proofread:**
    *   Meticulously correct the entire manuscript, paying special attention to the reference list. The current state of the bibliography is disqualifying on its own.

**Questions:**
1.  What was the rationale behind choosing the temporal offsets {1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32} and then only using the predictions for the last three non-specified offsets with weights (0.5, 0.5, 1) in the loss? How sensitive is the model to these choices?
2.  In the goal-agnostic training experiments, how was the goal vector `g` passed to the predictor `F` during training if the loss function `L(θ)` in Equation (4) does not depend on `g`? The equation `‖F(oi, ai, gi; θ) - fi‖^2` seems to train the network to predict the future `fi` irrespective of the input goal `gi`, which feels counterintuitive. Please clarify how the goal conditioning is learned.
