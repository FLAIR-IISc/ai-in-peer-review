### Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes an alternative framework for sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which recasts the problem from reinforcement learning (RL) into supervised learning (SL). Instead of learning a policy to maximize a discounted sum of scalar rewards, the agent learns to predict the future values of a multi-dimensional "measurement" stream (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input, current measurements, a potential action, and a parameterized goal. The supervisory signal is derived directly from experience by observing the actual future measurements that result from taking actions. At test time, the agent selects the action that leads to the best-predicted outcome according to its current goal. The authors demonstrate this approach in the complex 3D environment of Doom, showing that it significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods like A3C and DQN, particularly on challenging combat scenarios. The paper further shows that this framework allows for training without a fixed goal and can generalize to new goals at test time, and that the use of a rich, vectorial measurement stream is superior to a single scalar reward. A model trained with this approach won the Visual Doom AI Competition, providing strong external validation.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

This is an excellent paper with a simple, powerful core idea that is supported by an exceptionally strong and comprehensive set of experiments. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Novel and Effective Reframing of the Problem:** The paper makes a compelling case for stepping outside the dominant Bellman-equation-based RL paradigm for sensorimotor control. By formulating the problem as a supervised prediction task, it replaces the often unstable and sample-inefficient process of value function estimation with a more direct and stable regression objective. The use of a dense, multi-dimensional measurement stream as a rich supervisory signal is a key insight that is well-motivated by the contrast between information-rich real-world environments and information-poor board games where sparse rewards are more natural.

2.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are outstanding. The proposed DFP method doesn't just edge out strong baselines like A3C; it outperforms them by a very large margin (e.g., a 6x improvement in frags on the "Battle" scenario). The authors go beyond a simple performance comparison by including rigorous experiments on generalization across both environments (unseen textures) and goals (training with random goals and testing on specific ones). The ablation study cleanly demonstrates the benefit of both multi-dimensional measurements and multi-step prediction. Finally, winning a competitive track in the Visual Doom AI competition against other specialized models is a powerful real-world validation of the approach's effectiveness and robustness.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's core strength lies in its elegant simplicity and the clarity with which it demonstrates the idea's power. The "guiding observation" that the temporal structure of sensory and measurement streams provides a rich supervisory signal is a departure from the mainstream focus on temporal-difference learning. The authors do an excellent job positioning this work in the literature, acknowledging early related ideas (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992) while demonstrating their applicability and superiority in the context of modern deep learning architectures and complex environments.

The experimental design is exemplary.
-   **Baselines:** The comparison against DQN, A3C, and DSR is appropriate. The authors report making a fair effort to tune the baselines, which adds credibility to the large performance gaps observed.
-   **Generalization:** The goal-agnostic training experiments (Table 3) are particularly compelling. Showing that an agent trained on randomized goals can perform nearly as well on a specific task as a specialist agent—while also being able to adopt completely new personas like "pacifist"—is a significant result. This directly supports the claim that the approach enables the acquisition of general skills that can be flexibly deployed.
-   **Ablation Study:** The ablation in Table 4 provides direct evidence for the central hypothesis of the paper: that a rich, vectorial measurement stream is a better training signal than a scalar reward, even when the final evaluation metric is scalar (frags). This is a crucial result that validates the core design choice of the DFP framework.

The architectural choices, such as the dueling-style split into an expectation and action stream, are well-reasoned and build upon relevant prior work to improve performance. The entire methodology is clearly explained and appears to be reproducible.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, the following points could be addressed to further improve it:

1.  **The Sensory/Measurement Distinction:** The paper correctly notes that the distinction between the sensory stream `s_t` (raw pixels) and the measurement stream `m_t` (game stats) is "somewhat artificial." This is a key assumption of the model. It would be beneficial to add a short discussion on the applicability of this method to domains where a clean, low-dimensional measurement stream is not readily available (e.g., real-world robotics). Could `m_t` be learned, for instance, via an autoencoder or other self-supervised objective on `s_t`? Acknowledging this limitation and suggesting future directions would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Long-Term Planning:** The model predicts measurements up to a fixed horizon (32 steps). This seems to encourage reactive, short-term behavior, which is highly effective in the fast-paced Doom scenarios. How might this approach handle tasks that require much longer-term strategic planning, where the consequences of an action are not apparent within the prediction window? A discussion contrasting this explicit, fixed horizon with the implicit, infinite horizon of a standard discounted value function would be insightful.

3.  **Relationship to Model-Based RL:** The approach can be viewed as learning a "model" of the environment, but one that predicts future *measurements* rather than future *raw states*. This is a very interesting and pragmatic form of model-based control. The paper could benefit from a slightly more detailed discussion of how DFP relates to and differs from other model-based RL approaches, such as those that learn a dynamics model in a latent space.

### Questions for Authors

1.  The success of the method seems to hinge on the availability of a meaningful set of measurements `m_t`. How sensitive is the approach to the choice of these measurements? For example, in the "Battle" scenario, what would happen if the agent was only given 'health' and 'frags' but not 'ammunition'?

2.  The goal `g` is used as an input to the network at every step. Have you experimented with architectures where the goal conditions the network's behavior at a later stage, or in a different manner (e.g., via FiLM layers or another form of multiplicative gating)?

3.  In the goal-agnostic training, the model performs remarkably well. Does the internal representation `j` learned by the network show any interesting structure? For instance, does it learn representations that are disentangled with respect to the different elements of the measurement vector, allowing for the flexible composition seen at test time?
