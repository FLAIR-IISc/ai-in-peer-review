### Review of "Learning to Act by Predicting the Future"

#### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes an alternative framework for learning sensorimotor control from raw experience, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP). Instead of relying on the conventional reinforcement learning (RL) paradigm of maximizing a discounted scalar reward, the authors frame the problem as a supervised learning task. The core idea is to train an agent to predict the future values of a multi-dimensional stream of "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input, current measurements, a potential action, and a specified goal. The goal itself is defined as a linear combination of desired future measurement changes. At test time, the agent selects the action that is predicted to yield the best outcome according to its current goal.

The key contributions are:
1.  A simple and effective supervised learning formulation for sensorimotor control that uses dense, multi-dimensional feedback from the environment as its primary supervisory signal.
2.  A goal-conditioned architecture that allows the agent to be trained without a fixed objective and to pursue dynamically specified goals at test time.
3.  Extensive empirical validation in complex 3D environments (Doom), demonstrating that DFP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods like A3C and DQN, particularly on challenging tasks.
4.  Strong evidence for the model's ability to generalize to unseen environments, culminating in a first-place finish in the Visual Doom AI Competition's Full Deathmatch track.

#### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

This is an excellent paper that presents a simple, elegant, and highly effective alternative to standard deep RL for a class of complex control problems. My decision is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Exceptional Empirical Results:** The paper provides compelling and rigorous evidence that the proposed DFP approach is not just a viable alternative but a superior one for the tested domains. The performance gains over strong baselines like A3C are not incremental but substantial (e.g., a 2.5x to 6x improvement in frag count on the "Battle" scenarios). This is further substantiated by the model's victory in a competitive AI competition against agents designed specifically for the task, which powerfully validates the method's effectiveness and generalization capabilities.
2.  **Strong Conceptual Contribution and Motivation:** The paper makes a convincing case for reconsidering the foundational assumptions of RL in environments rich with sensory feedback. The core hypothesis—that a dense, multi-dimensional measurement stream provides a richer and more stable training signal than a sparse scalar reward—is well-motivated by prior work and thoroughly validated by a clear ablation study. This reframing as supervised learning is not only effective but also elegantly incorporates goal conditioning, a significant challenge for traditional RL methods.

#### 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The claims made in the paper are exceptionally well-supported by the experimental design and results.

The comparison to baselines (Section 4.2) is fair and thorough. The authors use standard, strong baselines (DQN, A3C) and report an extensive hyperparameter search for A3C, ensuring the comparisons are robust. The results in Table 1 and Figure 3 clearly show DFP's superiority in terms of both final performance and training stability, especially as task complexity increases. The performance gap in scenarios D3 and D4 is particularly striking and highlights the limitations of scalar rewards in multi-faceted tasks.

The generalization experiments (Section 4.2) are a major strength. Proving generalization is critical for any learning-based approach. The authors test this in two convincing ways: generalization to new visual textures (Table 2) and generalization to unseen environments in the Doom AI Competition. Winning the competition, especially in the "Full Deathmatch" track held on unknown maps, is an undeniable demonstration of the robustness and practical utility of the learned policies. This goes far beyond typical academic benchmarks.

The goal-agnostic training results (Table 3) successfully demonstrate the flexibility of the DFP framework. The ability to train a single agent that can later adopt different personas (e.g., "aggressive" vs. "pacifist") simply by changing the goal vector `g` at test time is a powerful capability. The finding that a model trained on randomized goals performs nearly as well on a specific task as a specialist model is a significant result.

Finally, the ablation study (Table 4) provides the crucial scientific evidence for the paper's central thesis. It directly compares the full model (vectorial measurements, multiple time horizons) against variants using only a scalar reward (frags) and a single time horizon. The results clearly show that both vectorial feedback and predicting at multiple temporal offsets contribute significantly to performance, validating the core design principles of DFP.

#### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, the following points could help further improve its clarity and impact:

*   **The Sensory/Measurement Distinction:** The paper correctly notes that the distinction between sensory input `s_t` and measurements `m_t` is "somewhat artificial." The success of the method seems to depend on having access to a curated set of meaningful, low-dimensional measurements. It would be beneficial to include a brief discussion on the sensitivity of DFP to this choice. For instance, how would the system perform if some measurements were noisy, irrelevant, or not provided at all? How should one go about selecting the measurement vector in a new domain?
*   **Relationship to Model-Based RL:** The paper positions itself primarily as a supervised learning approach, distinct from RL. However, predicting the consequences of actions (`p_at = F(o_t, a, g)`) can be seen as learning a compact, task-focused world model (a model of environment dynamics projected onto the measurement space). A more detailed discussion of the relationship between DFP and model-based RL methods, especially those that learn latent dynamics models, would help place the work more precisely within the broader literature.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The temporal offsets (`τ_i`) and the goal coefficients for training are important hyperparameters. The paper fixes them across experiments, but a short discussion on the sensitivity to these choices would be welcome. For example, how does performance change with fewer or more distant prediction horizons?

#### 5. Questions for the Authors

1.  How crucial is the hand-picked nature of the measurement vector `m_t`? Have you experimented with providing the model with a larger set of available game variables, including potentially less informative ones, to see if it can learn to focus on the relevant measurements?
2.  Your action selection mechanism `argmax_a g^T F(o_t, a, g)` requires iterating through all possible actions to find the best one. While feasible for the discrete action spaces in Doom, how do you see this approach extending to environments with large or continuous action spaces?
3.  The DFP agent is purely reactive, as noted in the discussion. For tasks requiring memory (e.g., finding a key and later returning to a locked door), a simple recurrent extension seems plausible. Have you considered or experimented with integrating recurrence (e.g., an LSTM) into the perception or joint representation modules?
