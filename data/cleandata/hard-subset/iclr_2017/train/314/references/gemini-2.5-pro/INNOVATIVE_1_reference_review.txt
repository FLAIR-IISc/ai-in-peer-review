### **Review**

**Summary of Contributions:**

This paper introduces Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a novel and compelling approach for sensorimotor control in complex, immersive environments. The core idea is to reframe the control problem from the standard reinforcement learning paradigm into a supervised learning one. Instead of learning a value function or policy from a sparse scalar reward, the agent is trained to predict a vector of future "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input, a potential action, and a goal. The goal is parameterized as a linear combination of desired future measurements. At test time, the agent simply evaluates all possible actions and selects the one predicted to yield the best outcome according to its current goal.

The authors provide extensive empirical validation in the challenging 3D environment of Doom. They demonstrate that their relatively simple approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods like A3C, particularly on complex combat tasks. Furthermore, they show that the model can be trained without a fixed goal and can generalize effectively to new, dynamically specified goals at test time. The paper's claims are strongly supported by ablation studies and a first-place finish in the Visual Doom AI Competition, which tested generalization to unseen environments.

**Decision:**

**Accept.**

This paper presents a bold, elegant, and highly effective alternative to mainstream deep reinforcement learning for a significant class of problems. Its primary strengths are its conceptual novelty and the strength of its empirical results. The work challenges the community's prevailing focus on Bellman-equation-based methods and demonstrates that for environments with rich, dense feedback, a simpler supervised prediction framework can be substantially more effective.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1.  **A Bold and Refreshing Conceptual Shift:** The paper's main contribution is a paradigm shift. While the idea of using supervised learning for control is not new (as the authors acknowledge), this work successfully modernizes and scales it to complex, high-dimensional visual control tasks where deep RL has been considered the state of the art. The formulation is clean, intuitive, and sidesteps many of the notorious challenges in RL, such as credit assignment for sparse rewards and the instability of bootstrapping-based value estimation. By leveraging the rich, multivariate, and temporally dense signals available in many realistic environments, the paper makes a convincing case that the full machinery of RL may not always be necessary or even optimal. This is precisely the kind of thought-provoking and direction-setting work that ICLR should champion.

2.  **Exceptional Empirical Results:** The claims are not merely conceptual; they are backed by some of the most impressive empirical results I have seen in this domain. The performance gains over strong baselines like A3C are not minor—in the most complex "Battle" scenario, DFP outperforms A3C by a factor of six. This is a massive leap. The subsequent victory in the Visual Doom AI competition's "Full Deathmatch" track provides external, unbiased validation of the method's power and, crucially, its ability to generalize to completely unseen environments.

3.  **Demonstrated Flexibility and Generalization:** A key advantage highlighted by the authors is the ability to handle dynamic goals. The experiments in Table 3 are excellent, showing that an agent can be trained in a "goal-agnostic" manner and then effectively pursue diverse goals at test time, from aggressive combat to pacifism. This is a significant step towards more flexible and general-purpose agents, moving beyond the standard RL setup of training a specialized agent for a single, fixed reward function.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors:**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **The Sensory/Measurement Distinction:** The paper is candid that the distinction between raw sensory input `s_t` and measurements `m_t` is "somewhat artificial." In game environments, this is a natural and powerful abstraction (pulling from the game's UI). However, the paper's impact could be broadened by a brief discussion on how this framework might apply to domains like robotics, where such measurements are not pre-defined. Could these measurements be learned, for instance, as the latent variables of a VAE trained on the sensory stream? This could connect the work more directly to the literature on learning predictive state representations.

*   **Stability of Training:** One of the most appealing practical aspects of this work is its apparent training stability, stemming from a standard supervised regression loss. This is a significant advantage over many RL algorithms. It would be beneficial to explicitly comment on this. Did you observe the training to be substantially more stable and less sensitive to hyperparameters than the RL baselines?

*   **On the Action-Selection Mechanism:** Forgive me if I missed this, but how is the argmax in Equation (3) handled for the complex battle scenarios with 2^8 = 256 possible actions? Is the network's final layer structured to output a prediction for every one of these 256 actions simultaneously? If so, this is computationally impressive and a key architectural detail.

*   **Continuous Actions:** The current work is limited to discrete action spaces. What do you see as the primary obstacles to extending DFP to continuous action domains? Would this require learning a separate Q-like function `Q(s, m, g, a) = g^T F(s, m, g, a)` and then performing gradient ascent on `a`, or do you envision a different approach?
