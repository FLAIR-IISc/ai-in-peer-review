### Summary

This paper proposes a novel and compelling approach to sensorimotor control, reframing it as a supervised learning problem of direct future prediction (DFP). Instead of learning a value function from a scalar reward signal as is common in reinforcement learning, the proposed model learns to predict a vector of future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on the current sensory input, current measurements, a potential action, and a parameterized goal. The agent then acts by selecting the action that is predicted to yield the best outcome according to its current goal. The core idea is that the rich, temporally dense stream of measurements available in many immersive environments provides a powerful supervisory signal that is more effective than a sparse, scalar reward. The authors provide extensive empirical validation in challenging 3D environments (Doom), demonstrating that their approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods like A3C. Crucially, they show that the model can be trained without a fixed goal and can generalize to new goals and unseen environments at test time, a claim powerfully substantiated by winning the Visual Doom AI Competition.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is an excellent paper that I am enthusiastic to support. It presents a bold, simple, and highly effective alternative to the dominant paradigm in deep reinforcement learning for sensorimotor control.

1.  **Significant Conceptual Novelty:** The paper's primary strength is its elegant reconceptualization of the control problem. By moving from indirect value function approximation to direct prediction of meaningful, multi-dimensional outcomes, the authors sidestep many of the complexities associated with credit assignment and bootstrapping in traditional RL. This is a refreshing and thought-provoking direction that challenges the community's prevailing assumptions. The idea is not entirely new in principle, as the authors diligently review, but its modern implementation with deep networks and its compelling demonstration on complex tasks represent a significant advance.

2.  **Exceptionally Strong Empirical Results:** The bold claims are backed by thorough and convincing experiments. The performance gains over strong baselines like A3C are not incremental but substantial (e.g., a 6x improvement in frags on the "Battle" scenario). Furthermore, the paper goes beyond standard benchmark performance to demonstrate two crucial properties of intelligent agents: generalization to unseen environments (validated by the competition win) and generalization to novel goals (validated by the goal-agnostic training experiments). This level of empirical rigor and real-world validation is exemplary.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's argument is built on the simple yet powerful observation that many realistic environments provide more than just a sparse reward. They provide a rich stream of measurements that are intrinsically linked to the agent's state and actions. The DFP framework leverages this structure directly. The ablation study in Table 4 provides a clear "smoking gun" for the central hypothesis: predicting vectorial measurements is substantially better than predicting a scalar reward, and predicting at multiple future time steps is also beneficial. This directly supports the claim that the richness of the supervision signal is key to the method's success.

The ability to train a single, goal-agnostic agent that can later pursue a variety of goals (including contradictory ones like "pacifist" vs. "killer") is a major step towards more flexible and general AI. The results in Table 3 are particularly impressive, showing that the goal-conditioned model trained on random goals performs almost as well on a specific task as a specialized model, while retaining far greater flexibility. This elegantly separates the learning of the world's dynamics ("what happens if I do this?") from the execution of a specific policy ("what should I do to achieve this goal?").

Finally, the architectural choices, such as the dueling-style split into expectation and action streams, are well-motivated and contribute to the model's effectiveness. The fact that the model is architecturally simple and requires no complex additions like external memory or recurrence to achieve state-of-the-art results further highlights the power of the underlying formulation.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are meant to be constructive and do not detract from my strong recommendation to accept.

*   **The Sensory/Measurement Distinction:** The paper correctly notes that the distinction between the sensory stream `s_t` and the measurement stream `m_t` is "somewhat artificial." It would be beneficial to elaborate on the principles guiding this separation. Is it simply a matter of dimensionality reduction, or is there a semantic constraint that measurements must be quantities over which goals can be easily defined? A deeper discussion of this design choice would strengthen the paper's framework.
*   **Reactive Nature:** The current model is purely reactive, acting on the current observation `o_t`. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the discussion. It would be interesting to see a brief discussion on how this framework might be extended to handle partial observability or tasks requiring long-term memory. For instance, would a recurrent state simply replace the feed-forward perception module, or does the prediction target `f` itself need to be conditioned on a longer history?
*   **Goal Representation:** The linear goal formulation `g^T * f` is simple and effective. It would be interesting to speculate on the potential of more expressive, non-linear goal functions and how they might enable more complex behaviors.

### Questions for Authors

*   How sensitive is the model's performance to the specific choice of temporal offsets `τ` for future predictions? The set `{1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32}` seems reasonable, but was this chosen through systematic experimentation? What is the impact of using, for example, only long-term predictions or a much denser set of short-term ones?
*   The paper mentions that the competitor's model in the Doom competition used additional supervision from the game engine (e.g., ground-truth enemy presence). Your model achieved superior performance without this. Do you have any insights into why your simpler, end-to-end approach was more effective? Does it perhaps learn a more robust representation by not relying on these engineered features?
*   Regarding the goal-agnostic training, the model trained with goals in `[-1, 1]` seems to generalize slightly better than the one trained with goals in `[0, 1]`. Is the ability to learn to *avoid* certain outcomes (i.e., pursue goals with negative weights) critical for developing a more comprehensive understanding of the environment's dynamics?
