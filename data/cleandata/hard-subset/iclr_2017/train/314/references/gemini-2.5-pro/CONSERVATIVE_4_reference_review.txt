### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Direct Future Prediction (DFP), an approach to sensorimotor control that frames the problem as one of supervised learning rather than reinforcement learning. The core idea is to train a deep network to predict the future values of a vector of intrinsic "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on the current sensory input, current measurements, a specified goal, and a potential action. At test time, the agent selects the action that leads to the predicted future measurements most aligned with its current goal. The authors argue this approach is particularly well-suited for environments with dense, multi-dimensional feedback, in contrast to the sparse scalar rewards often assumed in traditional RL. The method is evaluated in a series of increasingly complex 3D environments based on the game Doom. The empirical results are strong, showing that DFP significantly outperforms established deep RL baselines like DQN and A3C, particularly on more challenging tasks. The paper also demonstrates the model's ability to generalize to unseen environments and to pursue dynamically specified goals not seen during training. A key piece of evidence for the method's effectiveness is its first-place finish in the Visual Doom AI Competition's Full Deathmatch track.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Weak Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on a set of challenging 3D visuomotor control tasks. The victory in the Visual Doom AI competition, in particular, provides strong external validation that is difficult to dismiss.
2.  However, the novelty and generality of the proposed method are questionable. The approach appears to be a modern implementation of a well-established concept (predictive control as supervised learning), and its success seems heavily contingent on the availability of a pre-defined, semantically meaningful measurement vector, which may limit its applicability compared to standard RL formulations.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The primary strength of this paper lies in its extensive and convincing experimental validation. The authors systematically compare DFP against strong baselines (DQN, A3C) across four scenarios of increasing difficulty. The reported performance gains, especially in the complex "Battle" scenarios (e.g., a 6x improvement in frags over A3C in D3), are significant. The ablation study effectively demonstrates the benefits of using vectorial feedback over a scalar reward and predicting at multiple temporal horizons. Furthermore, the generalization experiments—both to new environment textures and to novel goals—provide solid evidence for the flexibility of the learned representations. The success in the Doom AI competition against a strong field is perhaps the most persuasive result, suggesting the method is not only effective in a controlled research setting but also robust in a competitive one.

Despite these strong results, I have reservations about the paper's framing and the fundamental contribution. The core idea of reformulating control as a supervised prediction problem is not new, as the authors themselves acknowledge by citing work from the early 1990s (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992). The main contribution appears to be the successful application of this idea using modern deep learning architectures in a complex 3D domain. While this is a valuable engineering achievement, the work does not propose a fundamentally new learning paradigm.

My main concern is the method's reliance on a curated stream of "measurements." The distinction between high-dimensional sensory input `s_t` and a low-dimensional measurement vector `m_t` is critical to the method's success. This formulation works exceptionally well in domains like video games where the engine provides clean, noise-free, and highly relevant state information (health, ammo, kill count). However, the applicability to real-world robotics or problems where such measurements are not readily available is unclear. The standard RL framework, with its monolithic state and scalar reward, is arguably more general precisely because it makes fewer assumptions about the nature of the feedback signal. The paper claims its formulation "generalizes the standard reinforcement learning formulation," but it seems to be the opposite: it specializes it to a class of problems where dense, multi-objective feedback is available. This significantly curtails the claim that DFP is a broad alternative to RL. The method avoids the credit assignment problem by assuming the environment provides dense, per-timestep supervisory signals in the form of `m_t`. This is a strong assumption that is not met in many canonical RL problems.

Finally, while the comparison to DQN and A3C is well-executed, the comparison to DSR is noted as being limited due to computational constraints. This is a minor weakness but does slightly undermine the completeness of the comparison to related methods.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision:

1.  **Scope and Framing:** I would encourage the authors to be more precise in their framing. Rather than presenting this as a general alternative to RL, it might be more accurate to position it as a highly effective method for a specific, but important, class of problems where rich, multi-dimensional state information is available as a supervisory signal. Clarifying this scope would strengthen the paper by making its claims more defensible.

2.  **Architecture Justification:** The paper adopts a dueling network-style architecture, splitting the prediction into an expectation stream and an action stream. This structure was originally motivated for estimating state-value and advantage functions in Q-learning. Its theoretical justification in this direct prediction/regression setting is less clear. Could the authors provide more intuition or an ablation study showing the impact of this specific architectural choice compared to a simpler, unified prediction head?

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of temporal offsets `τ` and the weights used in the objective function `(0.5, 0.5, 1)` seem somewhat arbitrary. How sensitive is the model's performance to these choices? A brief sensitivity analysis would add to the paper's rigor.

4.  **Questions:**
    *   Could the authors elaborate on how this approach would be applied to classic RL benchmark problems that lack a rich measurement stream, such as many Atari games (e.g., Montezuma's Revenge), where the primary signal is a sparse reward? If the reward is the only measurement, does the method not effectively reduce to a Monte-Carlo policy evaluation method, which is known to have high variance?
    *   In the goal-agnostic training, the agent is trained on randomized goal vectors. Does this not implicitly assume that the underlying dynamics of how actions affect measurements are independent of the agent's high-level goal? Is this a reasonable assumption in general?
