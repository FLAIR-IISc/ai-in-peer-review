### Review

**Summary:**

This paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), an approach that frames sensorimotor control as a supervised learning problem. Instead of relying on a scalar reward signal, the agent learns to predict a vector of future "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input, a goal representation, and a potential action. By training a deep network to predict the outcome of its actions, the agent can, at test time, select the action that is predicted to best satisfy a dynamically specified goal. The authors argue that this formulation provides a richer, temporally denser supervisory signal than traditional RL, and enables generalization to novel goals not seen during training. The method is evaluated in a series of increasingly complex 3D navigation and combat scenarios within the ViZDoom environment. The empirical results are strong, showing that DFP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL baselines like A3C and DQN, and that the approach won a major competition, demonstrating its effectiveness and generalization capabilities.

**Decision:**

Reject.

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Critical Methodological Ambiguity:** The paper omits or describes with profound ambiguity several key details of the proposed method and experimental setup, making the work difficult to understand, reproduce, and critically evaluate. The precise mechanisms for defining goals, constructing the loss function, and handling the action space are particularly unclear.
2.  **Insufficient Rigor in Experimental Comparison:** While the results appear impressive, the fairness of the comparison to baseline methods is questionable. The hyperparameter tuning for baselines seems inconsistent and potentially insufficient, and the direct comparison with a method (DSR) trained for a fraction of the time is misleading. These issues undermine the confidence in the central claim of superior performance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of this paper is compelling and the headline results are impressive. However, the manuscript in its current form is plagued by a substantial number of minor flaws, omissions, and inconsistencies that, in aggregate, prevent a confident assessment of its contributions.

*   **Vagueness in Goal and Loss Formulation:** The description of how goals and temporal offsets are handled is critically underspecified.
    *   In Section 4.1, the goal for battle scenarios is a linear combination of three measurements with coefficients `(0.5, 0.5, 1)`. However, the future vector `f` is a concatenation of measurement differences across six temporal offsets (`τ` = 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32), making it an 18-dimensional vector. The paper fails to explain how the 3-element coefficient tuple is mapped to the 18-dimensional goal vector `g`.
    *   Section 4.1 states, "Only the latest three time steps contribute to the objective function, with coefficients (0.5, 0.5, 1)." This directly contradicts the loss function presented in Equation 4, which is an unweighted L2 norm over the entire prediction vector `fi`. This is a major inconsistency at the heart of the training procedure. It is impossible to know what was actually implemented.

*   **Missing Implementation Details:** The paper repeatedly defers crucial details to an "Appendix A" which has not been provided. This includes:
    *   The architecture of the perception, measurement, and goal modules (Section 3.2).
    *   The size of the experience memory `M` and the parameter update frequency `k` (Section 3.1).
    *   Details of goal randomization regimes (Section 4.2, Goal-agnostic training).
    Without these details, the work is entirely unreproducible. A claim like "we have not observed any adverse effect of using a small experience memory" is anecdotal without specifying what "small" means.

*   **Questionable Experimental Protocol:**
    *   **Action Space:** In scenarios D3 and D4, the agent has 8 binary sub-actions, resulting in `2^8 = 256` possible actions. It is highly unusual for a model to handle such a large discrete action space directly. The paper does not clarify whether all 256 actions were used or if the space was pruned. If the latter, the pruning method is a critical, missing experimental detail. This has significant implications for both the model architecture and the comparison to baselines.
    *   **Baseline Tuning:** The hyperparameter search detailed in Appendix B is concerning. For A3C, the search strategy changes between simple and complex tasks without justification. For DQN, only three learning rates are tested. Given the sensitivity of deep RL methods to hyperparameters, this search may not be sufficient to ensure the baselines were performing optimally, thereby inflating the perceived advantage of DFP.
    *   **DSR Comparison:** Including DSR in Figure 3 and Table 1 as a direct competitor is inappropriate when it was trained for only ~10M steps versus the 50M steps for other methods. This should have been presented with a much stronger caveat or omitted from direct comparison tables.
    *   **Normalization:** The paper mentions using "normalized measurements" to construct rewards for baselines and goals for DFP, but the normalization scheme is never described.

*   **Formatting and Presentation:** The manuscript is unpolished. The references section is improperly formatted, containing raw BibTeX-like markup (e.g., `? \Q2006\E,`). There is a stray sentence about network architecture appended to the end of the reference list. Citations are used inconsistently throughout the text. While these are minor points individually, their high frequency suggests a lack of care in the manuscript's preparation.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   The conceptual distinction between "sensory input" `s` and "measurements" `m` feels ad-hoc. The authors state it is "somewhat artificial." A more principled justification for this division would strengthen the paper's theoretical framing.
*   The work would benefit from a discussion of its limitations. How would DFP perform in environments with highly stochastic transitions or where meaningful measurements are not readily available? The L2 loss on a single trajectory's outcome is sensitive to stochasticity, which is a key reason for using expectation-based objectives in RL.
*   The architecture in Section 3.2 uses a dueling-style split into expectation and action streams. A more detailed ablation or analysis justifying this specific design choice over a simpler, monolithic prediction head would be valuable.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Please clarify the exact procedure for constructing the goal vector `g` from a set of high-level importance weights (e.g., (0.5, 0.5, 1)) across multiple measurements and temporal offsets.
2.  Please provide the exact mathematical formula for the loss function used in training, reconciling the description in Section 4.1 (weighted loss over 3 time steps) with Equation 4 (unweighted L2 norm).
3.  Did the model in the D3/D4 scenarios operate on the full action space of 256 actions? If not, please describe how the action space was constrained.
4.  How were the game measurements (ammo, health, frags) normalized before being used in the objective for DFP and the reward for the RL baselines?
