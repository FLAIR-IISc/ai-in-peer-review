### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a novel approach for learning sensorimotor control that reframes the problem as supervised learning rather than reinforcement learning. The core idea is to train an agent to predict the future values of a low-dimensional "measurement" stream (e.g., health, ammo) conditioned on the current high-dimensional sensory input (e.g., raw pixels), a proposed action, and a goal vector. The goal vector parameterizes a utility function over future measurements. At test time, the agent selects the action that is predicted to yield the best outcome according to its current goal.

The key claimed advantages are twofold:
1.  The rich, temporally dense, and multi-dimensional measurement stream provides a stronger supervisory signal than the sparse scalar rewards typical in RL, leading to more stable and faster training.
2.  The framework naturally supports goal-conditioning, allowing an agent to be trained without a fixed goal and then pursue dynamically specified goals at test time, including goals it has never seen during training.

The authors provide compelling empirical evidence in challenging 3D environments based on the game Doom. They demonstrate that DFP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods like A3C, particularly on complex tasks. The paper further validates its claims through experiments showing generalization across environments with different visual textures, successful goal-agnostic training, and an ablation study confirming the importance of vectorial feedback. A key piece of evidence is the model's victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition on previously unseen maps.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper with a simple, elegant idea that is executed exceptionally well and supported by a comprehensive and convincing set of experiments. The key reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **High Efficacy of a Simple Idea:** The paper presents a conceptually simple alternative to standard RL that proves remarkably effective. By replacing the complex machinery of value function estimation and policy gradients with a direct supervised prediction objective, the authors achieve state-of-the-art results on difficult visuomotor control tasks. This work challenges the default assumption that TD-learning or policy gradient methods are always superior for these problems and successfully revives an older perspective on control as prediction.

2.  **Exceptional and Thorough Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is a model of rigor. The authors compare against strong, relevant baselines (DQN, A3C) on a series of increasingly difficult tasks. The results are not just marginal improvements but show DFP outperforming A3C by a factor of 2.5-6 on the hardest scenarios. Crucially, the paper goes beyond standard comparisons to test the core claims of the framework, including generalization to unseen environments, the ability to pursue novel goals after goal-agnostic training, and an ablation study that confirms the utility of multi-dimensional, multi-temporal predictions. The win in an official competition provides powerful external validation.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's primary strength lies in its clear demonstration that in environments with rich, multi-faceted feedback, reframing control as supervised prediction is a highly effective strategy. The standard RL formulation often discards information by collapsing diverse environmental feedback into a single scalar reward. DFP leverages this richer signal directly. The regression loss (`‖F(o, a, g) - f‖^2`) is a stable, well-understood objective that sidesteps many of the instabilities associated with bootstrapping in Q-learning or high variance in policy gradients.

The experimental results are unambiguous. In Table 1, while A3C and DFP are comparable on the simple D1 task, DFP establishes a clear and growing advantage as task complexity increases, culminating in a massive performance gap in the D3 and D4 "Battle" scenarios. This strongly supports the hypothesis that the dense supervision from multiple measurements (health, ammo, frags) is particularly beneficial in complex, dynamic settings.

The goal-conditioning experiments in Table 3 are particularly novel and impactful. The ability to train a single agent on randomized goals and then have it successfully adopt different "personalities" at test time (e.g., aggressive fragger, pacifist, ammo-waster) is a significant step towards more flexible and general AI agents. The fact that the randomly trained agents perform nearly as well on the primary task as a specialist agent is a testament to the framework's representational power and generalization capability. This is a clear advantage over standard RL agents that are typically trained to optimize a single, fixed reward function.

Finally, the victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition's Full Deathmatch track is a powerful piece of evidence. Winning a tournament on unseen maps against other competitive agents demonstrates that the learned policies are not just good at optimizing a specific metric in a known environment but are robust and general. The authors note that the runner-up model was more complex and used additional supervision, making DFP's performance all the more impressive.

### 4. Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

These points are intended for improvement and clarification and do not detract from my strong recommendation to accept.

**Clarity on "Measurements" vs. "Sensory Input":** The authors correctly state that the distinction between sensory input `s_t` and measurements `m_t` is "somewhat artificial." This is a crucial design choice for the entire framework. Could you elaborate on the principles for selecting what constitutes a "measurement"? Does the approach depend on having access to privileged, human-meaningful state information (like health), or could measurements be, for example, the outputs of an unsupervised autoencoder trained on the raw sensory stream? How sensitive is performance to the specific set of measurements chosen?

**Relationship to Model-Based RL:** The approach feels closely related to model-based RL. You are learning a "model," `F`, that predicts the consequences of actions, but you predict consequences in a low-dimensional measurement space rather than the full state/pixel space. A more explicit discussion of this relationship would be valuable. Is DFP best understood as a specific, highly effective instance of model-based control where the model is defined over a task-relevant abstraction (`m_t`) of the state?

**Limitations of Linear Goals:** The utility function `u(f; g) = g^T f` is linear. While this is general and powerful, have you considered how the framework might handle more complex, non-linear goals? For instance, a goal like "keep health above 50 while minimizing ammunition consumption" is not easily expressed as a linear combination. A brief discussion of this potential limitation and avenues for future work would strengthen the paper.

**Potential Failure Modes:** The paper convincingly shows where DFP excels. Are there hypothetical scenarios where you would expect traditional RL (e.g., a TD-based method like A3C) to outperform DFP? For example, in an environment with extremely sparse measurements that only appear at the end of a long episode (thus resembling a traditional sparse reward), would the lack of dense supervision for the regression task become a disadvantage compared to TD's ability to bootstrap values? Understanding the boundary conditions of the method's effectiveness would be insightful.
