### **Summary of Contributions**

The paper proposes an approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which frames the learning problem as a supervised prediction task rather than a traditional reinforcement learning problem. The core idea is to train an agent to predict the change in future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input, measurements, goal, and a considered action. Instead of relying on a sparse, scalar reward, the model leverages a rich, temporally dense stream of vectorial measurements as a supervisory signal. A key advantage of this formulation is its ability to learn a goal-agnostic model that can be directed to pursue dynamically specified goals at test time by simply selecting the action that maximizes the predicted outcome according to the current goal. The method is evaluated in challenging, immersive 3D environments (ViZDoom) and demonstrates superior performance over state-of-the-art deep RL methods like A3C and DQN, particularly on complex tasks. The paper further supports its claims with strong results in generalization experiments and an ablation study, and notes the model's success in winning the Visual Doom AI Competition.

### **Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents a compelling and empirically successful alternative to standard RL formulations, the manuscript in its current form is marred by a significant number of errors, ranging from critical methodological ambiguities to severe formatting issues. These issues collectively undermine the paper's scientific credibility and prevent a confident assessment of the work. The lack of attention to detail is pervasive, and a thorough revision is required before the work can be considered for publication.

### **Supporting Arguments**

My decision is based on several major flaws that obstruct clarity, reproducibility, and a sense of scientific rigor.

1.  **Critical Methodological Ambiguity:** The experimental setup, a cornerstone of the paper's claims, is not described with sufficient clarity.
    *   In Section 4.1, the authors state they predict measurements at temporal offsets {1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32}, but that "Only the latest three time steps contribute to the objective function, with coefficients (0.5, 0.5, 1)." It is entirely unclear which of the six predicted offsets constitute the "latest three." Does this refer to the offsets of 8, 16, and 32 steps? This detail is crucial for understanding the training objective and for anyone attempting to reproduce the results.
    *   In Section 3.1, the authors state that the agent's predictor parameters are updated "after every k new experiences." The value of this critical hyperparameter, `k`, is never specified in the main text or the appendix description.
    *   In Section 4.1, the description of the action space for scenarios D3 and D4 is incomplete. It states there are eight sub-actions, "resulting in possible actions." The number of actions, presumably 2^8 = 256, is missing. This is a significant omission in the description of the experimental domain.

2.  **Confusing and Non-Standard Notation:** The mathematical formulation suffers from ambiguous notation.
    *   Equations (1) and (3) use the expression `g>f` to represent the utility of a predicted outcome. This appears to be a non-standard representation of the dot product, which should be written as `g^T f` or `g ⋅ f`. Using a greater-than symbol is highly confusing and suggests a comparison rather than an inner product.
    *   In Section 3.1, the variable `N` is overloaded, referring to both the total number of examples in the training set `D` and the size of a mini-batch sampled from it. This is poor notational practice.

3.  **Severe Formatting and Reference Errors:** The manuscript exhibits a profound lack of care in its preparation, particularly in the reference section.
    *   Nearly every entry in the reference list is corrupted with formatting artifacts, such as `,? \Q2006\E,`. This makes the bibliography difficult to read and reflects poorly on the manuscript's quality.
    *   A snippet of text, "The large network is similar, but all layers starting from the third are wider by a factor of two...", has been erroneously inserted into the reference list. This appears to be a fragment from the appendix that was misplaced during document preparation, and it is a major formatting error.
    *   "Kalchbrenner et al., 2016" is cited multiple times (end of Section 2, end of Section 5), but the corresponding entry is missing from the reference list, constituting a citation error.

These issues are not merely cosmetic; they obscure critical details of the methodology and demonstrate a lack of diligence that calls the reliability of the entire submission into question. While the results seem impressive, the work cannot be accepted without these fundamental problems being addressed.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The core concepts of this paper are genuinely interesting and the results are strong. The work has the potential to be an excellent contribution to the field. I strongly encourage the authors to undertake a thorough revision of the manuscript.

*   **Proofreading:** Please meticulously proofread the entire paper to correct all typos, grammatical errors, and formatting issues. The URL in Section 4.1 contains an errant space (`bit.ly/ 2f9tacZ`). Pay special attention to the reference section, which needs to be completely regenerated and checked for correctness.
*   **Clarify Methodology:** All methodological details must be stated clearly and unambiguously. This includes defining all hyperparameters (`k`), clarifying the loss function (which temporal offsets are used?), and precisely describing the experimental conditions (action space size).
*   **Standardize Notation:** Adopt standard mathematical notation throughout the paper (e.g., `g^T f` for the dot product). Ensure variables are not overloaded. The normalization in Equation (6) could be clarified by using distinct notation for pre- and post-normalization values (e.g., `A'_i(j) = A_i(j) - ...`).
*   **Appendices:** Ensure that all information promised in the appendices is present and clearly detailed. The architecture details, in particular, are vital for reproducibility.

### **Questions for the Authors**

To clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  Regarding the loss function in Section 4.1: Which three temporal offsets from the set {1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32} are considered the "latest three" and weighted by (0.5, 0.5, 1)?
2.  Regarding the training procedure in Section 3.1: What value was used for the hyperparameter `k`, which governs the frequency of the acting policy's updates?
3.  Regarding the utility function in Equation (1): Is the notation `g>f` intended to represent the dot product `g^T f`?
4.  Regarding the action space in Section 4.1: Was the total number of possible actions in scenarios D3 and D4, which appears to be 256, simply omitted from the text?
