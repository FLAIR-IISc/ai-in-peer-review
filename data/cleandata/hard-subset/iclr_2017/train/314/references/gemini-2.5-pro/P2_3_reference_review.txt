### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces an approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which frames the problem as a supervised learning task rather than a traditional reinforcement learning (RL) problem. The core idea is to train a deep network to predict the change in a vector of future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on the current sensory input (raw pixels), current measurements, a specified goal, and a candidate action. At execution time, the agent simply selects the action that is predicted to yield the most desirable future measurements according to its current goal.

The key contributions are:
1.  A simple and effective formulation for learning complex behaviors from raw experience by reducing control to a supervised prediction problem.
2.  Demonstration that this approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods (DQN, A3C) in complex, 3D first-person environments (Doom).
3.  Empirical evidence showing that using a dense, multidimensional measurement stream provides a richer and more effective supervisory signal than a conventional scalar reward.
4.  A demonstration of strong generalization, both to novel goals specified at test time (without retraining) and to previously unseen environments, as evidenced by winning the Visual Doom AI Competition.

### **Decision and Justification**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper with a clear and compelling contribution. The two key reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Exceptional Empirical Results and Rigorous Evaluation:** The paper presents not just an incremental improvement but a massive performance gain over strong, established baselines (A3C) on challenging tasks. The reported 6x performance increase in the "Battle" scenario is highly significant. The victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition provides a powerful, independent validation of the method's robustness and ability to generalize to unseen environments, which is a critical challenge in the field.
2.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Method:** The DFP approach is simple, intuitive, and effectively re-frames the control problem in a way that leverages the strengths of supervised learning. The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments, including crucial ablation studies (Table 4) that isolate the benefits of vectorial feedback and multi-step prediction, and experiments (Table 3) that validate the model's ability to handle dynamically specified goals.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper excels in its experimental design and the strength of its evidence. The authors compare DFP against appropriate and state-of-the-art deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C) on a series of four tasks with increasing difficulty. This allows for a clear demonstration of where DFP excels—namely, in the more complex scenarios with richer, multidimensional feedback. The performance gap shown in Figure 3 and Table 1 is substantial and convincing.

Furthermore, the paper successfully validates its core hypotheses. The central claim is that a dense stream of vectorial measurements is a better supervisory signal than a sparse scalar reward. The ablation study in Table 4 directly supports this by showing that performance drops significantly when using only a scalar measurement (frags) or predicting at only a single future time step. This provides strong evidence that the design choices are critical to the model's success.

The work on goal-agnostic training and generalization is another major strength. The ability to train a single agent that can later pursue various goals (e.g., pacifist, aggressive) without retraining is a significant step towards more flexible and general AI. Table 3 clearly shows that a model trained on randomized goals generalizes far better to novel objectives than a model trained on a single, fixed goal, while suffering only a minor performance drop on the original task.

Finally, the paper is well-written and situates the work effectively within the existing literature. It correctly identifies its connections to early work on supervised learning for control (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992), multi-objective RL, and universal value functions (Schaul et al., 2015), while clearly distinguishing its own contribution.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is an excellent paper, the following points could be addressed to further strengthen it:

*   **Relationship to Model-Based RL:** The approach has strong similarities to model-based RL, where the learned function `F` acts as a dynamics model over the "measurement" space. A more detailed discussion on the relationship between DFP and explicit model-based methods would be beneficial. For example, why predict future measurements directly instead of learning a full state-transition model and deriving measurements from predicted states? The paper implicitly argues for the former's tractability, but making this argument explicit would be valuable.
*   **The Nature of "Measurements":** The paper notes the distinction between sensory input `s_t` and measurements `m_t` is "somewhat artificial." This is a crucial design choice that seems to rely on the game engine providing privileged information (e.g., health, ammo count). It would be helpful to discuss the requirements for defining `m_t`. How would this approach apply to real-world scenarios, like robotics, where such clean, low-dimensional measurements may not be readily available and might need to be learned or estimated from raw sensory data?
*   **Exploration:** The paper relies on a standard ε-greedy exploration strategy. Given that the agent learns a predictive model of action outcomes, it seems well-suited for more sophisticated exploration strategies (e.g., based on prediction uncertainty or novelty). A brief discussion of this potential extension could be interesting.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of DFP to the specific choice of temporal offsets (`τ`) for future predictions? The paper uses a fixed set {1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32}. Have you experimented with other sets, and is there a principled way to choose them?
2.  The training is done using a small experience memory, which means the predictor is trained on off-policy data. Traditional off-policy RL methods can suffer from instabilities. Does this supervised regression objective provide inherent stability when training on data generated by a different policy? Have you observed any training instabilities, and if so, how large can the experience memory be before they arise?
3.  In the ablation study (Table 4), DFP with a scalar reward (predicting only frags) still outperforms the RL baselines (Table 1 reports A3C at 3.3 frags, while Table 4 shows scalar DFP at 10.4 frags). This suggests that the supervised prediction framework itself, even without vectorial feedback, offers a significant advantage. Could you elaborate on why you believe this is the case? Is it related to the stability of a regression loss versus optimizing a Bellman-derived objective?
