### Paper Summary

This paper introduces Direct Future Prediction (DFP), an approach to sensorimotor control that frames the problem as supervised learning rather than reinforcement learning. The core idea is to train a deep network to predict the change in a vector of future "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on the current sensory input, current measurements, a goal vector, and a potential action. At test time, the agent selects the action that is predicted to yield the most desirable future measurements according to its current goal. The authors claim that this method, by leveraging dense, multidimensional feedback from the environment, is more effective than traditional RL methods that rely on a scalar reward. They demonstrate the efficacy of DFP on a series of increasingly complex 3D navigation and combat tasks in the Doom environment. The empirical results show that DFP significantly outperforms established deep RL baselines like A3C and DQN. Furthermore, the paper shows that the DFP agent can generalize to unseen environments and can be trained in a "goal-agnostic" manner to pursue dynamically specified goals at test time. The approach also won the Visual Doom AI Competition, lending strong external validation to its effectiveness.

### Decision: Weak Accept

My decision is a weak accept. The primary reason for this is the strength of the empirical results. The presented method substantially outperforms well-established baselines like A3C on complex tasks, and the victory in the Visual Doom AI competition is a compelling piece of evidence. However, I have significant reservations about the paper's framing and the generality of the approach, which prevent a more enthusiastic recommendation. The central claim that this work departs from reinforcement learning in a fundamental way seems overstated, and the comparison to RL baselines may not be entirely equitable.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's main strength is its empirical evaluation. The performance gap between DFP and A3C in the "Battle" scenarios is substantial (a factor of 2.5-6x in frags), which is highly significant. The ablation study in Table 4 convincingly argues for the two core components of the method: the use of vectorial feedback over a scalar reward and the prediction at multiple temporal offsets. The goal-agnostic training experiments are also impressive, demonstrating a level of flexibility not easily achieved with standard value-based methods. These results are rigorous and address important challenges in the field.

However, my primary criticism lies with the conceptual framing. The authors position their work as an alternative to reinforcement learning, reducing control to a supervised learning problem. I find this distinction to be largely semantic. The agent still operates in a standard RL loop: it observes a state, takes an action, receives feedback, and updates its policy. The predictor network `F` and the argmax selection rule in Equation 3 together constitute a policy. The goal vector `g` functions as a dynamically weighted, linear reward function. The overall approach strongly resembles a simple, one-step model-based RL algorithm where the "model" directly predicts the expected (multi-objective) return for each action, rather than the next state. While this is a valid and interesting design choice, framing it as a departure from RL is misleading. The literature on model-based RL is extensive, and the paper should position itself more clearly within this context.

A second major concern is the fairness of the baseline comparisons. The DFP method is explicitly designed to leverage a rich, vector-valued measurement stream. The RL baselines, designed for scalar rewards, were adapted by providing them a reward signal formed by a linear combination of these same measurements. It is not clear that this is the optimal way to present this information to a standard RL agent. The performance of A3C or DQN might be substantially different with a more carefully shaped scalar reward or if they were adapted using techniques from multi-objective RL. The experimental setup gives an architectural advantage to the proposed method, as it is custom-built for the type of feedback signal used.

Finally, the reliance on a pre-defined, low-dimensional "measurement" vector seems to limit the method's generality. The distinction between raw sensory input `s_t` and measurements `m_t` requires domain knowledge to hand-engineer a suitable measurement vector. This undermines the goal of learning directly from raw experience and may not be straightforward in real-world applications where salient measurements are not as clearly defined as "health" or "ammo count." This is a form of supervision that is not required by standard RL agents that learn from a single reward signal. The paper does not sufficiently discuss this limitation or how one might define these measurements in more general settings.

### Additional Feedback and Questions

The following points are intended to help improve the paper but did not form the primary basis of my decision.
1.  **Relation to Model-Based RL:** I strongly suggest the authors revise the introduction and background sections to more accurately place their work in the context of model-based reinforcement learning. Explicitly discussing the similarities and differences with methods that learn a model of the environment's dynamics (or a model of expected returns) would strengthen the paper and clarify its contributions.
2.  **The Role of the Prediction Horizon:** The method relies on predicting measurements at a fixed set of future time steps (up to 32). This seems well-suited for a reactive, fast-paced game like Doom. How would you expect this approach to perform in environments that require long-term planning, where the critical consequences of an action might only become apparent hundreds of steps later? Standard RL methods handle this via Bellman bootstrapping; it is unclear how DFP would address such long-term credit assignment problems.
3.  **Architecture Details:** The dueling-style architecture for the prediction network is an interesting choice. Is there an intuition for why splitting the prediction into an "expectation stream" and an "action stream" is beneficial for predicting future measurements, similar to how it is for estimating state values and action advantages in Q-learning?
4.  **Questions for the Authors:**
    *   Could you comment on the sensitivity of the model's performance to the choice of temporal offsets `τ`? How were these values chosen?
    *   In the goal-agnostic training, the model seems to learn a good representation of the environment's dynamics. How does this learned model compare, in terms of performance on a fixed task, to a model trained with a single, fixed goal from the beginning (as in Table 3, column 'a')? The results are close, but is there any performance degradation from training with randomized goals?
    *   Given that the method relies on a hand-specified measurement vector, have you considered learning these measurements as well, for instance, by training an autoencoder on the raw sensory input and using parts of the latent code as measurements to be predicted? This could make the approach more general and less reliant on domain-specific feature engineering.
