### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which recasts the problem from a reinforcement learning framework into a supervised learning one. The core idea is to train an agent to predict the future values of a set of intrinsic "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input, a proposed action, and a goal. Goals are defined as a desired change in future measurements. By learning this predictive model from raw experience, the agent can select actions at test time by choosing the one that is predicted to best satisfy its current goal. The authors argue this formulation is particularly well-suited for immersive environments where a rich, dense stream of multidimensional feedback is available, in contrast to the sparse scalar rewards often assumed in traditional RL. Through a series of experiments in the challenging 3D environment of Doom, the paper demonstrates that this approach not only outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods like A3C and DQN, but also exhibits impressive generalization capabilities to new environments and dynamically specified goals not seen during training. A key empirical result is the model's victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition, providing strong external validation.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper with a clear and compelling contribution. My decision to accept is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Excellent Empirical Results:** The proposed DFP method achieves state-of-the-art performance, significantly outperforming strong deep RL baselines (A3C, DQN) on a suite of challenging 3D visuomotor control tasks. The performance gains are particularly pronounced in the most complex scenarios. The success in the Visual Doom AI Competition on unseen maps is a powerful and convincing demonstration of the method's effectiveness and generalization capabilities.
2.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Formulation:** The paper presents a simple yet powerful reformulation of the control problem. The motivation to leverage dense, multidimensional feedback as a rich supervisory signal is well-argued and provides a clear alternative to traditional reward-based RL. This framework naturally accommodates goal-conditioning, and the experiments convincingly show that this leads to flexible agents that can adapt to novel objectives at test time.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper is well-written, the method is clearly explained, and the claims are strongly supported by a thorough set of experiments.

*   **Strength of Experimental Evaluation:** The comparison to baselines is fair and rigorous. The authors report having performed hyperparameter searches for the baseline methods and use their publicly available implementations. The chosen scenarios in ViZDoom progressively increase in difficulty, allowing for a clear demonstration of where DFP's advantages lie. The large performance margins over A3C in the "Battle" scenarios (a factor of 6 and 2.5 in frags) are striking and highlight the real-world utility of the approach.
*   **Demonstrated Generalization:** A key challenge in modern AI is generalization. This paper tackles this head-on with two well-designed experiments. The results in Table 2 clearly show generalization across visual changes (random textures) when trained appropriately. More impressively, the goal-conditioning experiments in Table 3 demonstrate that a single DFP agent, trained on randomized goals, can learn a versatile policy that performs nearly as well as a specialized agent on a known task, while also being able to pursue entirely new objectives (e.g., acting as a "pacifist"). This is a significant step towards more flexible and general-purpose agents.
*   **Ablation Study:** The ablation study in Table 4 provides direct and convincing evidence for the paper's core hypotheses. It clearly shows that using a vector of measurements is superior to a single scalar reward (even when the evaluation metric is that scalar), and that predicting at multiple future time steps is beneficial. This strengthens the core claim that a dense, multidimensional feedback stream is a more effective training signal.
*   **Clarity and Simplicity:** The proposed method is refreshingly simple compared to many contemporary RL algorithms. The reduction to a supervised regression problem makes it easy to understand and implement, yet it proves to be highly effective. The architectural details, such as the dueling-style split into expectation and action streams, are well-motivated and build upon existing successful ideas.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to be constructive suggestions and do not detract from my positive assessment of the paper.

*   **Defining "Measurements":** The paper notes that the distinction between sensory input `s_t` and measurements `m_t` is "somewhat artificial." It would be beneficial to elaborate on the principles for making this distinction. Is the key criterion simply dimensionality? Or is it about semantics and domain knowledge (e.g., quantities directly related to game objectives)? A discussion of this design choice would add depth and help practitioners apply this method to new domains.
*   **Connection to Model-Based RL:** The predictor function `F` can be viewed as a form of dynamics model, albeit one that predicts a specific, low-dimensional aspect of the future state (the measurements) rather than the full next observation. Briefly discussing the work in this context and contrasting it with traditional model-based RL approaches (which often predict the full next state or use the model for planning) could provide additional insight and better situate the contribution.
*   **Continuous Actions:** The current work is limited to discrete action spaces. A brief discussion of potential avenues for extending the DFP framework to continuous action spaces would be a welcome addition. For instance, one might need to replace the `argmax` over actions with an optimization procedure, which could introduce new challenges.
*   **Temporal Offsets:** The choice of temporal offsets `τ` and their weighting in the loss function appears somewhat heuristic. While the chosen values work well, a short discussion on the sensitivity of the model to these hyperparameters could be useful for future work.

### **Questions for Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following questions in their rebuttal to help clarify my understanding.

1.  In the goal-agnostic training (Table 3, column 'c'), the agent is trained with goals sampled from `[-1, 1]`. This means it is sometimes explicitly trained to *minimize* quantities like health or ammo. Could you provide some intuition as to why this does not seem to harm, and in fact enables strong generalization for, tasks where these quantities should be maximized?
2.  The prediction target `f` is the *change* in future measurements (`m_{t+τ} - m_t`) rather than the absolute future measurements (`m_{t+τ}`). What was the motivation for this choice? Does predicting a delta provide better signal stability or training dynamics compared to predicting absolute values?
3.  The agent's policy is updated as the predictor `F` is trained, meaning the data distribution for the supervised learning problem is non-stationary. Did you observe any training instabilities arising from this feedback loop? How critical were the experience replay buffer and the periodic update of the agent's policy parameters in ensuring stable learning?
