### **Summary**

This paper proposes an alternative approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem rather than a traditional reinforcement learning problem. The core idea is to train an agent to predict future changes in a set of intrinsic "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input, a potential action, and a specified goal. The agent acts by selecting the action that is predicted to yield the most desirable future measurements according to its current goal. This method, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), leverages the temporally dense and multidimensional feedback available in many immersive environments as a rich supervisory signal, moving away from the sparse, scalar rewards common in RL. The authors demonstrate their approach in the challenging 3D environment of Doom, showing that it significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL baselines like A3C and DQN, particularly on complex tasks. The paper further provides evidence that the learned models can generalize to unseen environments and can be trained "goal-agnostically" to pursue dynamically specified goals at test time.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a comprehensive and convincing set of experiments that rigorously validate the proposed method. The DFP agent substantially outperforms well-established deep RL baselines (A3C, DQN) in complex, visually rich 3D environments. The external validation from winning the Visual Doom AI Competition further strengthens these claims.
2.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Formulation:** The work presents a simple yet powerful reformulation of the sensorimotor control problem. The motivation to use dense, multidimensional feedback as a direct supervisory signal is compelling and well-argued. This provides a valuable alternative perspective to the dominant temporal-difference learning paradigm, supported by strong empirical results.

### **Supporting Arguments**

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem statement is clear, the proposed method (DFP) is described precisely in Section 3, and the architecture is explained with sufficient detail. The experimental setup and results are presented logically and are well-supported by figures and tables, making the paper's claims easy to assess. The connection to and departure from prior work in RL and supervised learning for control is also clearly articulated.

**Technical Soundness:** The technical approach is sound. The formulation of control as a supervised prediction problem is coherent, and the use of a deep network as a function approximator is appropriate for the high-dimensional visual input. The experimental methodology is rigorous. The authors compare against relevant and strong baselines (A3C and DQN) and appear to have conducted a fair hyperparameter search for them, as detailed in the appendix. The claims are directly supported by the provided results:
*   **Performance:** Table 1 and Figure 3 clearly show DFP's superior performance, especially in the more complex D3 and D4 scenarios.
*   **Generalization:** Table 2 demonstrates successful generalization to novel environment textures, while Table 3 shows impressive flexibility in achieving various goals after goal-agnostic training.
*   **Ablation:** Table 4 provides a controlled study that validates the central hypothesis of the paper: that using a vector of measurements is more effective than a scalar reward, and that predicting at multiple temporal horizons is beneficial.

**Novelty:** While the idea of using supervised learning for control is not new (as acknowledged by the authors), its modern implementation in this specific context is novel and significant. The paper successfully combines several ideas—parameterized goals (similar to Universal Value Functions), a Monte Carlo-style direct prediction objective, and a dueling-like network architecture—into a simple, coherent framework that proves highly effective for visuomotor control from raw pixels. The demonstration that this approach can outperform sophisticated TD-based methods like A3C in complex domains provides a fresh and important data point for the research community, suggesting that direct prediction may be a more suitable paradigm for environments with rich, intrinsic feedback signals.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   The distinction between sensory input `s_t` and measurements `m_t` is a central design choice. While effective, the paper describes it as "somewhat artificial." A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the method to this choice would be valuable. For instance, what happens if a crucial measurement is not explicitly provided but is only observable as part of the visual input `s_t` (e.g., an ammo counter on the screen)? Exploring the boundary of what constitutes a "measurement" could provide deeper insight.
*   The choice of temporal offsets (`τ` values) and their weights in the loss function seems somewhat ad-hoc. While the chosen values clearly work well, the paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the sensitivity to these hyperparameters or the intuition behind this specific logarithmic spacing and weighting scheme.
*   The discussion could benefit from a deeper analysis of the failure modes of DFP compared to RL methods. Are there specific types of tasks (e.g., those requiring very long-term credit assignment with no intermediate measurement feedback) where value-based or policy-gradient methods would be expected to have a theoretical or practical advantage?

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  The model is trained to predict the *difference* in future measurements (`m_{t+τ} - m_t`). Was predicting the absolute future measurements (`m_{t+τ}`) also tested? What is the intuition for predicting the change, and is it critical to the model's performance?
2.  The relationship to Monte Carlo (MC) methods is mentioned. Standard MC methods in RL estimate a scalar state-action value by averaging discounted returns. Your method predicts a vector of future quantities directly. Could you elaborate on this connection? Does the DFP objective correspond to an MC-style estimation of a vector-valued Q-function, where the "return" is undiscounted and multidimensional?
3.  The training process uses an experience memory while the policy is continuously updated, creating a mix of on-policy and off-policy data. Could you comment on the stability of this training process? Did you observe any issues like catastrophic forgetting or oscillations that required careful tuning of the experience memory size (`M`) or the agent's parameter update frequency (`k`)?
