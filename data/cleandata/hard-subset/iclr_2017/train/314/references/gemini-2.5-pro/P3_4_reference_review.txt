### Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a novel approach for sensorimotor control that reframes the problem as supervised learning rather than reinforcement learning. The core idea is to train a deep network to predict the future values of a low-dimensional stream of "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on the current high-dimensional sensory input (raw pixels), a potential action, and a parameterized goal. At test time, the agent selects the action that leads to a predicted future that best aligns with its current goal. The key contributions are: 1) the formulation of sensorimotor control as a direct prediction problem, leveraging the dense supervisory signal from a vectorial measurement stream, 2) an effective deep network architecture for this task, and 3) extensive empirical validation in the challenging 3D environment of Doom. The results demonstrate that DFP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods (DQN, A3C), particularly on complex tasks. Furthermore, the paper shows the method's ability to generalize to unseen goals and environments, culminating in a winning performance at the Visual Doom AI Competition.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Strong Empirical Results and Rigorous Evaluation:** The paper presents compelling evidence that a conceptually simple supervised learning approach can outperform state-of-the-art, and often more complex, reinforcement learning algorithms in challenging, visually rich 3D environments. The performance gap, especially in the "Battle" scenarios, is substantial. The evaluation is thorough, including a well-designed curriculum of tasks, comprehensive comparisons, ablation studies, and crucially, external validation through a competition win in unseen environments.
2.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Formulation:** The DFP framework is an elegant departure from the conventional Bellman equation-based RL paradigm. The motivation—that dense, multi-dimensional feedback is a richer supervisory signal than a sparse, scalar reward—is well-argued and supported by both historical context (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992) and the paper's own ablation studies. This work provides a powerful modern demonstration of an alternative perspective on learning to act.

### Supporting Arguments

The strengths of this paper are numerous and well-supported. The central claim that direct prediction can be more effective than value-function-based RL is convincingly demonstrated. The experimental setup is a major strength. By progressing from simple navigation (D1, D2) to complex combat scenarios (D3, D4), the authors show that the performance advantage of DFP over A3C and DQN widens as task complexity and the richness of the measurement stream increase. This directly supports their core hypothesis.

The ablation study in Table 4 is particularly persuasive. It isolates the key components of the proposed method and shows that both using vectorial feedback (vs. a scalar reward) and predicting at multiple temporal offsets contribute significantly to performance. This provides strong evidence that the architecture and problem formulation are sound and that the gains are not merely due to implementation details or hyperparameter tuning.

Another significant result is the goal-agnostic training (Table 3). The ability to train a single policy that can later be directed to pursue novel, dynamically specified goals (e.g., switching from aggressive to "pacifist") is a key step towards more flexible and general AI agents. DFP achieves this in a natural way by making the goal an explicit input to the network. The finding that a model trained on randomized goals generalizes better to new goals than a model trained on a fixed goal is intuitive but powerful when demonstrated so clearly.

Finally, the victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition serves as unimpeachable evidence of the method's effectiveness and robustness. Outperforming a competitor that used a more complex recurrent architecture and additional supervised labels from the game engine, all while generalizing to unseen maps, underscores the practical power and data efficiency of the DFP approach.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, the following points could be addressed to further improve its clarity and impact:

1.  **Relationship to Model-Based RL:** The paper frames the work as an alternative to RL, positioning it as supervised learning. However, predicting the consequences of one's actions is the cornerstone of model-based RL. DFP is essentially learning a compact, abstract model of the world dynamics, not in the pixel space, but in the measurement space (`p(m_t+τ | s_t, m_t, a, g)`). A more detailed discussion of how DFP relates to and differs from classic model-based RL (e.g., learning a transition model `p(s_t+1 | s_t, a_t)` and a reward function `R(s_t)`) would better situate the work within the broader literature. Is the main advantage that DFP bypasses predicting the high-dimensional state, learning a more direct and task-relevant model instead?

2.  **Applicability and Limitations:** The success of DFP appears to hinge on the availability of a semantically rich, reasonably dense vector of measurements `m_t`. The authors should be more explicit about the problem classes where this approach is most suitable and where it might fail. For instance, in problems with truly sparse rewards and no obvious intermediate measurements (e.g., Montezuma's Revenge, Chess, Go), it is unclear how DFP would be applied without significant modification. A short discussion on this would temper the claims and provide a more complete picture.

3.  **The Role of the Goal Input `g`:** The goal `g` is an input to the predictor `F`. This implies the network learns to predict future measurements differently depending on the agent's current intention. This is a powerful idea. However, the physical dynamics of the world are independent of the agent's goals (e.g., shooting a monster will have the same effect on the frag count regardless of whether the agent wants to increase it or not). The model seems to be learning a representation that conflates world dynamics with goal-conditioned expectations. Could the authors elaborate on this design choice? An alternative might be to have a goal-independent predictor and use the goal only at test time to select actions, i.e., `a_t = argmax_a g > F(o_t, a)`. An ablation on this would be very insightful.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the relationship between DFP and model-based RL? Do you see DFP as a form of abstract model learning, and if so, what are the key advantages of predicting future measurements over predicting future latent states or raw pixels?
2.  The paper focuses on environments with rich, engineered measurement streams. How do you envision applying DFP to tasks where such measurements are not readily available? Could relevant measurements be learned in an unsupervised fashion, for example, via an autoencoder on the raw sensory stream?
3.  Regarding the goal-agnostic training: during experience collection with a random policy (or ε-greedy), is the random goal `g` for that episode passed as an input to the network to decide actions? Or is `g` only used as an input feature when training the regressor on the stored `(o_i, a_i, f_i)` tuples, where `g_i` is retroactively assigned?
4.  The dueling-style architecture for splitting the prediction into expectation and action streams is an interesting detail borrowed from Wang et al. (2016). How critical is this component to the overall performance? Have you performed an ablation study on a simpler architecture that directly predicts `p_at` for each action from the joint representation `j`?
