### Review of "Learning to Act by Predicting the Future"

**Summary of Contributions:**

This paper presents an alternative approach to sensorimotor control, framing it as a supervised learning problem rather than a traditional reinforcement learning problem. The proposed method, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), trains a deep network to predict future changes in a multi-dimensional stream of "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on the current sensory input, current measurements, a specified goal, and a potential action. At execution time, the agent selects the action that is predicted to yield the best outcome according to its current goal. The authors evaluate this approach in the complex 3D environment of Doom, demonstrating that DFP significantly outperforms established deep RL baselines like DQN and A3C, particularly in complex combat scenarios. The paper also shows that the model can be trained without a fixed goal and can generalize to new goals and unseen environments at test time, as evidenced by a winning performance in the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons for Decision:**

1.  **Exceptional Empirical Results:** The experimental evidence presented is compelling and robust. The proposed DFP method demonstrates a substantial performance improvement over state-of-the-art deep RL methods (A3C, DQN) in challenging, high-dimensional visuomotor control tasks. The performance gap in the more complex "Battle" scenarios (a factor of 2.5-6x over A3C) is too significant to ignore.
2.  **Thorough Validation and Generalization:** The claims of the paper are well-supported through extensive experiments, including ablation studies, generalization tests across different environments (textured mazes), and a demonstration of zero-shot adaptation to novel goals. The victory in the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition serves as a powerful, unbiased validation of the method's effectiveness and robustness in unseen environments against other competitive agents.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its rigorous empirical evaluation. While the conceptual framing of control as a supervised prediction problem is not entirely new (as the authors correctly acknowledge by citing work from the 1990s), the successful application and scaling of this idea to complex, 3D first-person environments with modern deep learning architectures is a noteworthy contribution.

The authors have performed a commendable comparison against strong and relevant baselines. The use of A3C, widely considered a state-of-the-art model-free RL algorithm, as the main point of comparison strengthens their claims. The details provided in Appendix B suggest a fair effort was made to tune the baselines, lending credibility to the reported performance differences.

The ablation study in Table 4 provides crucial support for the core tenets of the proposed method. It clearly demonstrates that both the use of vectorial, multi-dimensional feedback and the prediction of measurements at multiple future time steps are critical to the model's success. This directly supports the central argument that a dense, rich supervisory signal is more effective than a conventional scalar reward in these types of environments.

Furthermore, the experiments on goal-agnostic training are particularly interesting. The ability to train a single, flexible policy that can adapt to dynamically specified goals at test time (Table 3) addresses a significant limitation of standard RL approaches, which are typically trained to optimize a single, fixed reward function. The fact that a model trained on randomized goals performs nearly as well on a specific task as a specialist model is a very strong result.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors:**

While the results are impressive, I have some reservations and questions regarding the framing and the underlying reasons for the method's success. These points are intended to help improve the paper and clarify its contributions.

1.  **Novelty and Framing:** The paper's main conceptual contribution is reviving and successfully implementing an older idea. The manuscript would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of *why* this approach is successful now when it did not become the dominant paradigm in the past. Is the success primarily due to the expressive power of modern deep networks, the specific architectural choices (e.g., the dueling-style prediction head), the availability of simulators like ViZDoom that provide dense measurement streams, or a combination thereof? Clarifying the key enabling factors would provide a deeper insight than simply showing that "SL works for control."

2.  **The Nature of the Supervisory Signal:** A critical point that deserves more discussion is the fundamental difference in the information available to DFP versus the RL baselines. DFP is trained on a dense, multi-dimensional vector of ground-truth measurements (`health`, `ammo`, `frags`) at every step. The RL baselines are given a scalar reward, which, while derived from these measurements, represents a significant reduction in information. Could the performance gap be attributed less to the "SL vs. RL" formulation and more to the richer, more informative training signal available to DFP?
    *   For instance, in the Battle scenarios, DFP explicitly predicts future `health`, `ammo`, and `frags`. An RL agent receiving a composite scalar reward `r = 0.5*Δammo + 0.5*Δhealth + 1*Δfrags` faces a much harder credit assignment problem. It does not know which component contributed to the reward. It would be insightful to discuss whether this information disparity is the primary driver of the performance gains. Have the authors considered training an RL agent with a vector-valued reward (e.g., in a multi-objective RL framework) as a more direct comparison?

3.  **Scope and Limitations:** The success of DFP appears to be predicated on the availability of a set of meaningful, low-dimensional measurements that are correlated with the agent's objectives. How would this approach fare in environments where such measurements are not available, or where the goal is defined by a truly sparse event with no obvious intermediate proxies? For example, in tasks like Montezuma's Revenge, it is not clear what an analogous "measurement stream" would be beyond the global score. A discussion on the applicability and limitations of this method would make the paper more complete.

4.  **Goal Parameterization:** The goal `g` is provided as an input to the network. During training with randomized goals, how does the agent's behavior change within an episode if the goal `g` is fixed for that episode? Does the ε-greedy exploration strategy interact with the specified goal? For instance, if the goal is to *avoid* frags (pacifist), does the agent still explore the 'shoot' action due to ε-greedy, and how does the network learn from the resulting (undesirable) outcome? A little more detail on the dynamics of goal-conditioned exploration would be beneficial.

In summary, this is a strong paper with convincing results on a set of challenging tasks. It makes a clear case for an alternative, and seemingly simpler, approach to learning complex behaviors. Despite my skepticism towards new formulations that sidestep established RL theory, the empirical evidence is undeniable. The questions above are intended to encourage a deeper analysis of *why* the method works so well, which would further enhance the impact of this work.
