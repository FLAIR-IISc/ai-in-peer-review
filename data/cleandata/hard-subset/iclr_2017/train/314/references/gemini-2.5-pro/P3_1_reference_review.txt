This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which frames the problem as a supervised learning task rather than a traditional reinforcement learning one. The core idea is to train an agent to predict the future values of a multi-dimensional stream of "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input (raw pixels), current measurements, a potential action, and a specified goal. At test time, the agent selects the action that is predicted to yield the best outcome according to its current goal. This approach leverages the rich, dense supervisory signal provided by the measurement stream, in contrast to the often sparse scalar rewards used in RL. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments in the challenging 3D environment of Doom that their method significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL baselines like A3C and DQN, particularly on complex tasks. Furthermore, they show that the learned models can generalize to new goals not seen during training and that the use of multi-dimensional measurements is critical to the method's success.

**Decision: Accept**

This is an excellent paper presenting a simple, elegant, and highly effective method that challenges some of the core assumptions in modern deep reinforcement learning. The two key reasons for my decision are:

1.  **Exceptional Empirical Results:** The paper provides compelling and rigorous evidence that DFP is not just a viable alternative but a superior approach for the complex visuomotor tasks studied. The performance gap over strong baselines like A3C in the "Battle" scenarios is massive (e.g., a 6x improvement in frags in D3). This is further validated by the model's win in the Visual Doom AI Competition's Full Deathmatch track, which provides an unbiased, external benchmark of the method's generalization and robustness.
2.  **Strong Conceptual Contribution and Ablation:** The paper's core premise—that predicting dense, vectorial measurements is a better supervisory signal than a sparse scalar reward—is well-motivated and convincingly demonstrated. The ablation study in Table 4 is crucial, clearly showing that both using multiple measurements and predicting at multiple future time steps are beneficial. The experiments on goal-agnostic training further highlight the flexibility of the framework, a significant advantage over typical RL agents trained to optimize a fixed reward function.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The authors position their work very well within the existing literature, acknowledging the historical context of SL vs. RL for control (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992) and correctly relating their Monte Carlo-style prediction to the dominant TD learning paradigm. The connection to Universal Value Functions (Schaul et al., 2015) is also aptly made, framing their work as a practical and powerful instantiation of that more general framework.

The experimental setup is thorough and well-designed. The use of four Doom scenarios with increasing difficulty allows for a clear demonstration of where DFP excels. The comparison against strong, widely-used baselines (DQN, A3C) is fair, with the authors reporting a hyperparameter search for the baselines to ensure a robust comparison.

The ability to train on randomized goals and then execute policies for specific, novel goals at test time is a standout feature. Table 3 shows convincingly that the goal-conditioned models generalize far better than a model trained on a single, fixed goal. This demonstrates a level of flexibility that is often sought but rarely achieved in end-to-end RL systems.

The model architecture, which builds on the dueling network idea, is sensible and well-explained. While the architecture itself is not the primary contribution, its successful application shows how existing deep learning components can be effectively composed within this new framework.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

While this is a strong paper, the following points could be addressed to further improve it:

*   **The Nature of "Measurements":** The paper's main strength is also its main assumption: the existence of a low-dimensional vector of meaningful measurements provided by the environment. The authors rightly call the distinction between sensory input `s_t` and measurements `m_t` "somewhat artificial." However, this distinction is critical to the method's applicability. In many real-world robotics or control problems, such explicit measurements are not readily available. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of this limitation and potential future work to address it, such as learning the measurement function itself from raw sensory data via self-supervision.
*   **Relationship to Model-Based RL:** The approach can be viewed as a specific form of model-based RL. The agent learns a "model" that predicts the consequences of actions (`F(o_t, a, g)` predicts future `m_t`), and then uses this model for single-step planning (`argmax`). A deeper discussion comparing DFP to more traditional model-based approaches, which might learn a full state-transition model `p(s_{t+1} | s_t, a_t)`, would be illuminating. What are the trade-offs of only modeling the task-relevant measurements versus the full state?
*   **Architecture Ablation:** The authors note that the dueling-style split into Expectation and Action streams "enhances its ability to learn." An ablation study removing this component and using a single prediction head would provide direct evidence for this claim and help isolate the sources of DFP's performance gains.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of temporal offsets (`τ`) and their weighting seems somewhat heuristic. While the current scheme clearly works well, a brief discussion on the sensitivity to these parameters would add to the paper's completeness. For instance, how does performance change with fewer or more densely packed temporal offsets?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How do you see this framework being applied to problems where a clear, low-dimensional measurement vector is not provided by the environment (e.g., a real-world robot learning to stack blocks, where "success" is purely visual)? Would the first step be to train an auxiliary network to produce a suitable `m_t`?
2.  Could you elaborate on the connection to model-based RL? Do you view DFP as a type of model-based method, and if so, what do you see as its primary advantages over learning a full predictive model of the environment's visual states?
3.  In the goal-agnostic training (Table 3), the models trained on random goals perform nearly as well on the primary task as the specialized model. This is surprising and impressive. Do you have any intuition for why there is such a small performance drop? Does this suggest the agent learns a very general and robust underlying model of the environment dynamics?
