### Review

**1. Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a novel approach for sensorimotor control in complex, immersive environments. Instead of relying on the conventional reinforcement learning paradigm of maximizing a scalar reward, the proposed method reframes the control problem as a supervised learning task. The core idea is to train a deep network to directly predict the future values of a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on the current sensory input (raw pixels), current measurements, and a goal representation. By acting and observing the consequences, the agent generates its own supervisory signal. At test time, the agent selects the action that is predicted to yield the best outcome according to its current goal. The paper demonstrates that this simple and direct approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods like A3C and DQN on challenging 3D navigation and combat tasks in the VizDoom environment. Furthermore, the authors show that DFP can learn general policies that adapt to dynamically specified goals and generalize to unseen environments, a claim strongly supported by their model winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**2. Decision and key reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a simple, elegant, and highly effective method that provides a compelling alternative to standard value-based and policy-gradient RL. Reframing the problem as supervised prediction of rich, dense, multidimensional feedback is a powerful idea that is shown to be more sample-efficient and stable in the tested domains.
2.  The empirical evidence supporting the method's effectiveness is exceptionally strong and thorough. The work includes comprehensive comparisons against state-of-the-art baselines on complex tasks, showing not just marginal but massive performance gains. The claims of generalization are well-supported by dedicated experiments and validated by a victory in a competitive, public benchmark.

**3. Supporting arguments for the decision**

The paper's strengths are numerous.

*   **Clarity and Motivation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. It clearly motivates the departure from the standard scalar reward paradigm by highlighting the availability of dense, multidimensional feedback in many realistic settings. The introduction and background sections do an excellent job of situating the work within decades of research, connecting it to early debates on supervised vs. reinforcement learning and Monte Carlo vs. TD methods, while clearly distinguishing it from more recent related work like Universal Value Function Approximators.

*   **Methodological Simplicity and Power:** The proposed DFP method is refreshingly simple. By casting control as a supervised regression problem, it avoids many of the complexities and potential instabilities associated with bootstrapping in value-based RL or high variance in policy gradient methods. The architecture, while sophisticated, builds on well-understood components. This simplicity does not come at the cost of performance; on the contrary, the results suggest it is a major advantage.

*   **Experimental Rigor and Strength of Results:** The experimental evaluation is a model of thoroughness.
    *   **Strong Baselines:** The authors compare against formidable and relevant baselines, including DQN and A3C, which is widely considered the state-of-the-art for this type of task. The hyperparameter search for the baselines (detailed in Appendix B) suggests a fair and rigorous comparison.
    *   **Significant Performance Gains:** The results are not incremental. In the more complex "Battle" scenarios, DFP outperforms A3C by a factor of 2.5 to 6 in terms of frags (Table 1), a decisive margin. The learning curves (Figure 3) show that DFP learns faster and reaches a much higher level of final performance.
    *   **Convincing Generalization Studies:** The paper provides strong evidence for generalization. The experiments with randomized textures (Table 2) show the model can learn robust visual features that transfer to unseen environments. The goal-agnostic training experiments (Table 3) are particularly compelling, demonstrating that the agent can learn a general control policy that can be directed to pursue novel goals at test time, such as acting as a "pacifist," nearly as effectively as a specialist agent.
    *   **External Validation:** Winning the Visual Doom AI Competition in the "Full Deathmatch" track is a powerful, objective validation of the method's robustness and ability to generalize to completely new environments against other strong agents. This goes beyond typical academic benchmarking and provides strong evidence for the practical utility of the approach.
    *   **Ablation Study:** The ablation study (Table 4) clearly demonstrates the value of the core components of the DFP formulation. It shows that using vectorial feedback is significantly better than a scalar reward (even when the evaluation metric is that scalar) and that predicting at multiple temporal offsets is also beneficial, confirming the central hypotheses of the paper.

**4. Additional feedback for improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Discussing Limitations:** The paper would be more balanced if it included a discussion on the potential limitations of DFP. When would this approach be expected to fail? For example, in tasks with truly sparse feedback where no meaningful intermediate measurements can be defined (e.g., some Atari games like Montezuma's Revenge). It would also be interesting to discuss scenarios that require very long-term credit assignment, where the bootstrapping of TD methods might theoretically have an advantage.
*   **Connection to Model-Based RL:** The approach has strong parallels with model-based RL. It learns a "model," but one that predicts future measurements rather than future raw states. This is a very pragmatic choice given the difficulty of pixel prediction. It would be beneficial to explicitly discuss this connection. The current agent performs one-step lookahead (action selection via `argmax`). Could the learned predictor `F` be used for multi-step planning (e.g., via tree search)? A brief discussion of this possibility would be a valuable addition.
*   **The Role of Measurements:** The choice of which variables constitute the "measurement stream" seems crucial to the method's success. In these experiments, the measurements are human-designed game statistics provided by the engine. How might this approach be extended to settings where such measurements are not readily available? Could they be learned, for instance, via an autoencoder on the sensory stream or other self-supervised objectives? Acknowledging this dependency and speculating on future directions would strengthen the paper's contribution.

**5. Questions for the authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1.  In your training formulation, the goal vector `g` is an input to the predictor network `F`, but the loss function `L(θ)` (Eq. 4) is a simple MSE between the prediction and the true future measurement `f_i`, which does not depend on `g_i`. This implies the network must learn a conditional prediction, where the goal `g` modulates the output. Could you elaborate on the intuition behind this design? Why is it preferable to, for instance, learning a goal-agnostic predictor `F(o_i, a_i)` and then selecting actions via `argmax_a g^T F(o_i, a_i)`? Does conditioning the entire network on the goal help it learn a more powerful representation?
2.  Regarding the randomized goal training regime: is the training experience (the trajectory from which `f_i` is calculated) collected while the agent is pursuing the same randomized goal `g_i` that is fed into the network as input for that training sample?
3.  In the dueling-style architecture (Section 3.2), the action-specific stream `A(j)` is normalized to have zero mean across actions. The intuition is that the expectation stream `E(j)` learns the action-independent average outcome. Have you experimented with this component? How crucial is this architectural choice to the final performance, compared to a simpler architecture where the joint representation `j` is fed into separate heads for each action?
