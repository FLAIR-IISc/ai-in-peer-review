### Review of "Direct Future Prediction"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a novel and effective approach to sensorimotor control in complex, immersive 3D environments. The core idea is to reframe the control problem from a reinforcement learning perspective (maximizing a discounted scalar reward) to a supervised learning one. Instead of learning a value function or policy from a sparse reward, the agent learns to predict the future values of a multi-dimensional stream of "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input (raw pixels), current measurements, a goal vector, and a potential action. At test time, the agent simply selects the action that is predicted to yield the best future outcome according to its current goal.

The key claimed contributions are:
1.  A method that leverages the rich, dense supervisory signal inherent in multi-dimensional measurement streams, which is argued to be more suitable for complex environments than a sparse scalar reward.
2.  A flexible framework that naturally handles dynamically specified, multi-objective goals without retraining, by conditioning predictions on a goal vector.
3.  State-of-the-art empirical performance in the challenging VizDoom environment, significantly outperforming strong deep RL baselines like A3C, particularly on complex tasks. The method's strength is further validated by winning the Visual Doom AI Competition in unseen environments.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper with a clear, simple, and powerful core idea backed by exceptionally compelling empirical results. The two primary reasons for my decision are:

1.  **Elegant Re-framing with Strong Motivation:** The paper effectively argues that for many realistic agent-environment interactions, the standard sparse scalar reward formalism is an unnecessary and potentially detrimental information bottleneck. Recasting the problem as supervised prediction of rich, intrinsic environmental measurements is a well-motivated and elegant simplification that sidesteps difficult credit assignment problems in RL.
2.  **Overwhelming Empirical Evidence:** The experimental results are not just incremental improvements; they demonstrate a step-change in performance on complex tasks. The large margins over A3C, the successful generalization to new goals and environments, and the victory in a competitive tournament against other state-of-the-art systems provide robust and convincing evidence for the method's effectiveness and practical utility.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's strength lies in its simplicity and the clarity of its results. The central thesis—that predicting the future is a sufficient substrate for intelligent action—is an old idea, but this paper provides one of the most compelling modern instantiations of it for complex visuomotor control.

The motivation, grounded in the work of Jordan & Rumelhart (1992), is well-placed. The authors correctly identify that while TD learning is well-suited for sparse reward problems, the availability of dense, multi-dimensional feedback in many realistic settings opens the door for direct supervised approaches. The DFP method is a clean realization of this insight. The architecture, including the dueling-style split into expectation and action streams, is a thoughtful adaptation of existing network design principles to this new predictive task.

The experiments are thorough and well-designed. The comparison against strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) on a suite of tasks with increasing difficulty clearly demonstrates the superiority of DFP as task complexity and the richness of the measurement stream increase. The performance gap in the "Battle" scenarios (a factor of 2.5-6x over A3C) is particularly striking and highlights the benefit of using the vectorial feedback. The ablation study in Table 4 provides crucial support for this, showing that both predicting multiple measurements and predicting at multiple future timesteps contribute significantly to performance.

Perhaps the most impressive results are those concerning generalization. The ability to train in a "goal-agnostic" manner and then successfully pursue diverse, even conflicting, goals at test time (Table 3) is a significant step towards more flexible agency. This capability stems directly from the design choice to explicitly condition the model on a goal vector `g`. Furthermore, winning the Full Deathmatch track of the VizDoom competition provides an unbiased, external validation of the approach's robustness and ability to generalize to completely unseen environments, outperforming a competitor that used a more complex recurrent architecture and additional supervision.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is excellent, the following points could be addressed to further strengthen it:

*   **Relationship to Model-Based RL:** The paper frames its approach as "supervised learning vs. reinforcement learning." However, DFP can also be viewed as a specific form of model-based RL. It learns a model `p(m_{t+\tau} | s_t, m_t, a_t, g)` and uses it for one-step-ahead planning (`argmax`). Explicitly positioning the work within the model-based RL literature, and discussing how it differs from traditional approaches that predict full next states (`s_{t+1}`) or learn a separate value function on predicted rollouts, would provide a more nuanced and complete picture. This framing would not diminish the contribution but rather clarify its connection to an important subfield of RL.
*   **Limitations of Myopic Control:** The action selection policy is greedy with respect to a relatively short-term prediction horizon (up to 32 steps, or ~1 second). This is effective in a fast-paced environment like Doom, where immediate reactions are critical. However, the paper should more explicitly discuss the limitations of this myopic strategy. In problems requiring long-term strategic planning (e.g., sacrificing health now to secure a key item for a later boss fight), this greedy approach might fail. Acknowledging this would better define the scope of the method's applicability.
*   **The "Measurement" Oracle:** The success of the method seems to rely on access to a curated, low-dimensional set of measurements relevant to the task. The paper rightly calls the distinction between `s_t` and `m_t` "somewhat artificial." It would be beneficial to discuss the sensitivity of the approach to this choice. What if a crucial piece of information is missing from `m_t`? What if `m_t` is noisy? A brief discussion on what constitutes a "good" set of measurements and how they might be learned automatically (e.g., via representation learning) would be a valuable addition to the discussion.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Following up on the limitation of myopic control: Have you considered tasks where the optimal policy requires a long-term plan with short-term sacrifices in measurements? For example, a scenario where the agent must temporarily lower its health to pass an obstacle to reach an area with much greater resources. How do you anticipate DFP would perform, and could the framework be extended with a more sophisticated planning algorithm beyond the greedy `argmax`?
2.  The goal parameterization `g>f` is a linear combination of future measurement changes. Could you comment on the framework's ability to handle non-linear or state-dependent goals? For instance, a goal like "keep health above 50" or "engage enemies only if ammunition is above 20."
3.  The training is performed using an ε-greedy policy over the learned predictor. Have you explored more sophisticated exploration strategies? Given the rich predictive model, it seems plausible that one could use uncertainty in the predictions or other model-based exploration techniques to gather more informative experiences.
