### 1. Summary

This paper proposes an approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which frames the problem as a supervised learning task rather than a traditional reinforcement learning one. The core idea is to train an agent to predict future changes in a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input, current measurements, a specified goal, and a potential action. At test time, the agent selects the action that leads to the predicted future measurements most aligned with its current goal. This formulation leverages the rich, temporally dense signal provided by a multi-dimensional measurement stream, moving beyond the sparse scalar rewards common in RL. The authors demonstrate that this simple and direct approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C) on a series of challenging visuomotor control tasks in the 3D game environment of Doom. Furthermore, they show that the model can be trained without a fixed objective and can generalize effectively to new, dynamically specified goals and previously unseen environments.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed method demonstrates a substantial performance improvement over well-established and strong deep reinforcement learning baselines (notably A3C) on complex, high-dimensional sensorimotor control tasks. The reported results, including winning a competitive AI challenge, provide compelling evidence for the efficacy of the approach.
2.  **Novel and Effective Problem Formulation:** The paper successfully reframes sensorimotor learning as a direct prediction problem, which is a departure from the dominant value-function and policy-gradient paradigms. This supervised learning perspective is well-motivated, simple to implement, and shown to be highly effective, especially when rich, multi-dimensional feedback is available. The experimental results strongly support the hypothesis that this formulation is advantageous in such settings.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's claims are well-supported by a comprehensive and methodologically sound set of experiments.

First, the comparison to prior work is robust. The authors compare DFP against strong and relevant baselines, including DQN and A3C, which is widely considered a state-of-the-art method. The experiments are conducted across four scenarios of increasing difficulty. While all methods perform well on the simple "Basic" task, DFP shows a clear and growing advantage as task complexity increases, outperforming A3C by a factor of 6 in the "Battle" scenario and 2.5 in "Battle 2" (Table 1). This demonstrates the method's effectiveness in challenging domains where dense, multi-faceted feedback is crucial. The reported training speed advantage is also a significant practical contribution.

Second, the paper provides strong evidence for its central hypotheses through targeted experiments. The ablation study (Table 4) is particularly convincing. It clearly isolates the benefits of using vectorial feedback over a scalar reward and of predicting measurements at multiple future time steps. The results show that removing either of these components leads to a significant degradation in performance, directly validating the core design principles of the DFP approach.

Third, the experiments on generalization are compelling. The ability of a model trained on varied textures to generalize to unseen environments (Table 2) demonstrates that the learned representations are not merely memorizing specific visual patterns. More importantly, the goal-agnostic training experiments (Table 3) show that the model can learn a general predictive model of the environment's dynamics which can then be flexibly deployed to achieve diverse goals at test time. A model trained on randomized goals performs nearly as well on a specific task as a model trained exclusively for that task, while also generalizing much better to novel objectives (e.g., acting as a "pacifist"). This is a significant step towards creating more flexible and general-purpose agents.

Finally, the paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and well-placed in the context of existing literature. It correctly identifies its connections to early work on supervised learning for control, multi-objective RL, and universal value functions, while clearly articulating its unique contribution. The technical details of the model and training procedure are described with sufficient clarity for replication.

### 4. Additional Feedback

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   The distinction between "sensory input" `s_t` and "measurements" `m_t` is crucial to the method but is described as "somewhat artificial." It would strengthen the paper to elaborate on the principles for making this distinction. Is it primarily about dimensionality (high-dim pixels vs. low-dim scalars), semantic content (raw perception vs. known state variables), or a combination? Providing some guidance here would improve the general applicability of the framework.
*   The goal is formulated as a linear combination `g > f`. This is a powerful and general starting point, but it would be interesting to discuss the potential for more complex, non-linear goal functions `u(f; g)` and how the framework might accommodate them.
*   The comparison with DSR is weakened by the large discrepancy in total training steps. While the authors are transparent about this limitation, framing the superior training speed of DFP (in steps/day) more explicitly as a primary advantage could be a clearer way to present this result.
*   The choice of temporal offsets (`τ_i`) and the loss coefficients seems somewhat heuristic. While the current choices are shown to work well, a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the model to these hyperparameters, or the intuition behind their selection, would be beneficial for future work building on this method.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   Could you provide more detail on the computational cost of DFP compared to the A3C baseline? The paper notes DFP is faster in terms of "simulation steps per day," which is a valuable metric. A direct comparison of training time on identical hardware, or a metric like FLOPs per training step, would further clarify the computational trade-offs.
*   The agent uses an ε-greedy policy for exploration during training. Given that the model produces explicit predictions about future outcomes, have you considered alternative exploration strategies, for example, those based on prediction uncertainty or disagreement among an ensemble of predictors?
*   In the goal-agnostic training experiments (Section 4.2), the goal vector `g` is randomized for each episode. To confirm my understanding, is this same goal vector `g` then provided as input to the network `F(o_t, a, g; θ)` for every step within that episode?
