### Review Summary
This paper presents a compelling and elegant approach to sensorimotor control, called Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which reframes the problem as a supervised learning task. Instead of relying on a sparse, scalar reward signal, the agent learns to predict future changes in a vector of intrinsic "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input, measurements, action, and a parameterized goal. The key contributions are: (1) a simple and effective formulation for learning control from raw experience without traditional RL machinery; (2) a demonstration that this approach can handle dynamically specified goals not seen during training; and (3) a set of extensive experiments in complex 3D environments (Doom) showing that DFP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL baselines.

### Decision: Accept

This is a strong paper with a clear and well-motivated idea, backed by an exceptionally thorough and convincing empirical evaluation. The two primary reasons for my decision are:

1.  **Methodological Elegance and Effectiveness:** The DFP approach is refreshingly simple yet powerful. By leveraging the rich, temporally dense supervisory signal available from in-game measurements, it sidesteps many of the difficulties associated with traditional RL, such as credit assignment for sparse rewards. The formulation is clean and directly optimizes for what the agent needs to know to act: the near-future consequences of its actions.
2.  **Rigorous and Compelling Experimental Validation:** The empirical support for the paper's claims is outstanding. The authors not only show superior performance against strong baselines like A3C on a series of increasingly difficult tasks, but they also conduct insightful experiments on generalization across environments and goals, and a crucial ablation study that validates their core design choices. The win in the Visual Doom AI Competition on unseen maps serves as powerful, real-world validation of the method's robustness and generalization capabilities.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's core hypothesis is that for many complex, interactive tasks, a rich stream of measurements provides a better supervisory signal than a single scalar reward. The experiments convincingly prove this point. In the simpler scenarios (D1, D2), DFP is competitive with A3C. However, in the more complex "Battle" scenarios (D3, D4), which feature a richer, 3-dimensional measurement vector, DFP's performance advantage becomes dramatic, outperforming A3C by factors of 6 and 2.5, respectively (Table 1). This strongly supports the claim that leveraging vectorial feedback is beneficial.

The goal-conditioning aspect of the model is another significant strength. The experiments in Table 3 demonstrate that a single model trained on randomized goals can perform nearly as well on a specific, predefined task as a specialized model trained only for that task. More importantly, this goal-agnostic model generalizes far better to novel goals at test time (e.g., acting as a "pacifist"). This capability represents a significant step towards creating more flexible and general-purpose agents.

The authors' commitment to rigorous evaluation is commendable. They provide a fair comparison by using a similar network architecture to DQN and conducting a hyperparameter search for the A3C baseline. The ablation study (Table 4) is particularly valuable, as it systematically dismantles the model to show that both predicting multiple measurements (vectorial feedback) and predicting at multiple future time steps contribute significantly to performance. This level of analysis provides strong evidence that the performance gains are due to the proposed contributions, not incidental implementation details. Finally, the success in the Doom competition against a strong opponent that used a more complex recurrent model and additional supervision is a remarkable achievement that underscores the practical utility of this work.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

1.  **The Sensory vs. Measurement Distinction:** The paper notes that the distinction between sensory input `s_t` and measurements `m_t` is "somewhat artificial." This seems to be a key conceptual point. While `m_t` is currently hand-specified (game stats), a truly general agent might need to learn what measurements are important to predict. Could the authors comment on the sensitivity of the method to the choice of measurements? Have they considered scenarios where relevant measurements might need to be learned or inferred from the raw sensory stream `s_t` itself?

2.  **Greedy Action Selection and Long-Term Planning:** The action selection policy is greedy with respect to the one-step prediction `g > F(o_t, a, g; θ)`. This is effective in the tested environments, but could be myopic in tasks requiring more complex, long-term planning where a good immediate outcome might lead to a poor long-term state. How do the authors see this approach extending to environments with more pronounced delayed effects, where this greedy strategy might fail? Does predicting farther into the future (e.g., τ=32) sufficiently mitigate this?

3.  **Relationship to Model-Based RL:** The approach has strong parallels to model-based RL, where the predictor `F` acts as a compact world model that predicts future outcomes (measurements) rather than future raw states. It would be beneficial to expand the discussion on this relationship. What are the perceived advantages of this "measurement-prediction" model over a traditional "state-prediction" model, beyond the obvious computational feasibility?

Overall, this is an excellent paper that introduces a valuable and practical idea to the field. It is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and the results are impressive. It should be a valuable contribution to the ICLR community.
