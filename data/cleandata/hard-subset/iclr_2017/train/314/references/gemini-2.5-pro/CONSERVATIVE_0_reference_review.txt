### Review Summary

The paper proposes an approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which frames the problem as a supervised learning task. Instead of maximizing a discounted scalar reward as in standard reinforcement learning, the agent learns to predict future changes in a vector of game-related measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input, measurements, action, and a parameterized goal. At test time, the agent selects the action that leads to the best-predicted outcome according to its current goal. The method is evaluated in the visually complex 3D environment of Doom, where it is shown to outperform established deep RL baselines like DQN and A3C, particularly on more complex tasks. The paper also demonstrates the model's ability to generalize to new goals and environments.

### Decision: Reject

While the empirical results are impressive, particularly the winning performance in the Visual Doom AI Competition, I have significant reservations about the work in its current form. My primary concerns are:
1.  The methodological contribution is framed as a departure from reinforcement learning, but the approach is more accurately described as a specific, simplified form of model-based control. The conceptual novelty is overstated, and the relationship to established model-based RL literature is not sufficiently addressed.
2.  The experimental comparison, while thorough in execution, does not adequately isolate the core reason for the performance gains. The paper argues that the advantage stems from using a rich, vectorial measurement stream, but the RL baselines are also provided with a dense reward signal derived from the same measurements. This conflates the benefit of the signal with the benefit of the learning objective itself.

### Supporting Arguments

**1. Methodological Novelty and Positioning:**

The central claim of the paper is that it departs from the "reward-based formalization commonly used in RL" by reframing sensorimotor control as supervised learning. This idea, however, is not new. As the authors themselves note by citing Jordan & Rumelhart (1992), the SL-vs-RL trade-off has been discussed for decades. The proposed method can be viewed as a specific instance of model-based RL where the "model" is trained to predict a specific, low-dimensional subset of the next state (the measurements `m_t`) rather than the full sensory observation (`s_t`). The policy is then derived by a single-step lookahead using this learned dynamics model.

The paper would be significantly stronger if it were positioned within the model-based RL literature. It fails to discuss how DFP relates to other contemporary model-based methods that also learn a dynamics model (e.g., in a latent space) and use it for planning or control. The distinction between the sensory stream `s_t` and the measurement stream `m_t` is a practical and effective simplification, but it is an engineering choice rather than a fundamental paradigm shift. The success of the method seems highly dependent on the availability of a predefined, low-dimensional vector of measurements that is both highly relevant to the task and directly usable for defining goals. The paper does not discuss the applicability of the method to domains where such measurements are not readily available.

**2. Analysis of Empirical Results:**

The paper's narrative attributes its success to the use of a "rich and temporally dense" measurement stream in contrast to an "occasional scalar reward." However, the experimental setup for the baselines neutralizes this specific argument. For the complex "Battle" scenarios, the baselines (DQN, A3C) are not trained on a sparse reward but on a dense scalar reward which is a linear combination of the changes in the very same measurements used by DFP.

Therefore, the comparison is not between a dense, vectorial signal (for DFP) and a sparse, scalar signal (for RL). Instead, it is a comparison between two different ways of using a dense signal:
*   **DFP:** Uses the dense vectorial signal as a target for a supervised prediction task.
*   **A3C/DQN:** Uses a scalarized version of the same dense signal as a reward for a Bellman-style value-function estimation task.

The observed performance gap is therefore a result of the learning objective (L2 regression vs. Bellman error minimization), not the nature of the supervision signal. While it is plausible that direct prediction is more stable or sample-efficient than value iteration in this high-dimensional setting, this is the core scientific claim that should have been investigated. The current analysis is misleading, as it incorrectly attributes the performance gains to the availability of a dense signal, which the baselines also possessed. The strong results may simply indicate that for this specific task, a simple supervised objective is more effective than the more complex and often unstable Bellman-based updates, a point which deserves a more rigorous and focused analysis.

### Additional Feedback

*   **Strengths:** The paper is well-written and the experiments are extensive. The results on complex tasks, the generalization experiments (across textures and goals), and the victory in the competition are all very strong evidence for the practical effectiveness of the proposed system. The ablation study in Table 4 is a good step towards understanding the components of the method.
*   **Improving the Ablation:** To better support the paper's claims, an ablation study comparing DFP to a more direct RL counterpart would be beneficial. For example, one could train an agent using an approach like Generalized Value Functions (as cited from Sutton et al., 2011) or multi-objective RL to learn separate value functions for each measurement, using the same network architecture. This would provide a more direct comparison of the learning paradigms.
*   **Clarity on Architecture:** The paper states the DFP network was configured to be "as close as possible to the DQN model." However, the DFP architecture includes a "dueling"-style split into expectation and action streams. It is unclear if the baseline DQN and A3C implementations were also augmented with a dueling architecture. If not, this represents a potentially significant confounding factor in the performance comparison.

### Questions for Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the conceptual difference between DFP and a model-based RL agent that learns a dynamics model `p(m_{t+1} | s_t, m_t, a_t)` and uses one-step planning to select actions? Why should DFP be considered a distinct paradigm rather than a specific, effective implementation of this principle?
2.  Given that the RL baselines were also trained on a dense scalar reward derived from the same measurements, do you agree that the performance difference is attributable to the learning objective (prediction vs. value iteration) rather than the signal density? Could you provide more intuition or analysis as to why a supervised prediction objective might be superior to a Bellman objective when both are given access to the same rich information?
3.  How does the proposed method generalize to tasks where a clear, low-dimensional measurement vector is not available? For example, in a manipulation task, the "measurement" might be the distance to a target, which must be inferred from pixels. Does the DFP framework extend to learning these measurements as well?
