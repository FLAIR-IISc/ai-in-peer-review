**Summary**

This paper presents a novel and compelling approach to sensorimotor control, recasting the problem from one of reinforcement learning to one of supervised learning. The core idea, which the authors term Direct Future Prediction (DFP), is to train a model to predict a vector of future measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on the current sensory input, current measurements, a proposed action, and a parameterized goal. At test time, the agent simply selects the action predicted to yield the best outcome according to its current goal. This elegant formulation eschews the complexities of value function estimation and policy gradients, relying instead on a dense, rich supervisory signal naturally present in the environment's feedback streams. The authors demonstrate the power of this approach in the complex 3D environment of Doom, showing that DFP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods like A3C, particularly on challenging tasks. The paper further shows that the model can be trained without a fixed objective and can generalize to new goals and unseen environments, a claim powerfully substantiated by winning the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**Decision: Accept**

This is an excellent paper that I enthusiastically recommend for acceptance. My decision is based on two key factors:

1.  **Significant Conceptual Novelty:** The paper makes a bold and refreshing departure from the dominant reinforcement learning paradigm. By demonstrating that a simple supervised prediction objective can outperform complex value-based methods in certain domains, it challenges the community's prevailing assumptions and opens up a promising new direction for research in sensorimotor control.
2.  **Extremely Strong Empirical Validation:** The claims are not merely speculative; they are backed by comprehensive and convincing experiments. The performance gains over strong baselines like A3C are not incremental but substantial (e.g., a 6x improvement in frags on one task). The success in a competitive, blind-evaluation setting (the Doom competition) provides an unambiguous and powerful validation of the approach's effectiveness and generalization capabilities.

**Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this work lies in its conceptual simplicity and elegance. The standard RL formulation, built around maximizing a discounted scalar reward, can be difficult to apply in domains where the reward is sparse or difficult to engineer. This paper argues that in many realistic, immersive environments, an agent receives a rich, multidimensional stream of feedback. The insight to leverage this dense signal to directly predict future outcomes is both intuitive and, as shown, remarkably effective. This reframing feels less like an incremental improvement on an existing algorithm and more like a fundamental shift in perspective.

The empirical results are outstanding. The head-to-head comparisons in Figure 3 and Table 1 clearly show DFP's superiority in the more complex "Battle" scenarios. The ablation study in Table 4 provides crucial support for the paper's core theses: that vectorial feedback is superior to a scalar reward, and that predicting at multiple future time steps is beneficial. The experiments on goal-agnostic training (Table 3) are particularly exciting, demonstrating that a single model can learn a general understanding of the environment's dynamics and then flexibly apply that knowledge to pursue diverse goals, some of which were not explicitly optimized for during training. This is a significant step towards more general and flexible agents.

Finally, winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition is a capstone achievement that is hard to argue with. Performing best in unseen environments against a field of competitors—including a strong runner-up that used a more complex recurrent architecture and additional supervisory signals—is a testament to the robustness and generalizability of the DFP approach.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

While I am very positive about this paper, the following points could help improve it further and are meant as constructive suggestions, not as prerequisites for acceptance.

1.  **The Sensory/Measurement Distinction:** The paper rightly acknowledges that the distinction between raw sensory input `s` and engineered measurements `m` is "somewhat artificial." This is perhaps the most significant potential limitation of the approach, as it relies on having access to a meaningful, low-dimensional measurement vector. It would strengthen the paper to discuss the implications of this reliance more thoroughly. For example, how would one apply this framework to a real-world robotics task where such clean "measurements" are not provided by a game engine?
2.  **Goal Parameterization:** The linear goal formulation `u(f; g) = g>f` is simple and works well here. However, it would be interesting to hear the authors' thoughts on its limitations. Could this formulation express more complex, state-dependent, or non-linear goals? A brief comment on this could be valuable for future work.

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following questions in their response:

*   How sensitive is the DFP agent's performance to the specific choice of measurements? For instance, in the "Battle" scenario, what happens if you train the agent with only health and frags, omitting ammunition? Does the performance on the standard goal degrade gracefully?
*   The approach is purely reactive. While clearly powerful, have you considered how this predictive framework might be integrated with memory (e.g., an LSTM) to handle tasks requiring long-term planning or memory of past events? Does the direct prediction formulation offer any unique advantages or challenges in such a setting?
*   Could you elaborate on the path from this work to a system that learns its own measurements? It seems the logical next step is to learn a compact representation of the state from raw sensory input that is predictable and useful for control, thereby removing the need to hand-specify the measurement vector `m`. Do you see a way to integrate this into the DFP framework?
