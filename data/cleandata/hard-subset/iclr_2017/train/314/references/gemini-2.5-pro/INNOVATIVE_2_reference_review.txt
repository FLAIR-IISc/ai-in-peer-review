This paper presents Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a novel approach to sensorimotor control that recasts the problem from reinforcement learning to supervised learning. The core idea is to train an agent to predict a stream of future, multi-dimensional "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input, action, and a representation of its current goal. At test time, the agent simply selects the action that is predicted to yield the most desirable future measurements according to its goal. The authors demonstrate this approach in the challenging 3D environment of Doom, showing that it significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL baselines like A3C. They further show that the learned models can generalize to unseen environments and flexibly pursue new goals not seen during training.

**Decision: Accept**

This is an excellent paper that I am enthusiastic to support. The key reasons for my decision are:
1.  **High Conceptual Novelty:** The paper makes a bold and refreshing departure from the dominant paradigm of value-function-based deep RL. By framing sensorimotor learning as a direct prediction problem, it provides a simple, powerful, and highly effective alternative.
2.  **Exceptional Empirical Results:** The performance gains over strong, state-of-the-art baselines are not incremental but substantial, particularly in the most complex tasks. The victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition serves as a powerful, real-world validation of the method's strength and robustness.

**Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this work lies in its conceptual shift. While the RL community has largely focused on refining methods based on the Bellman equation, this paper convincingly argues that for many complex, interactive settings, a direct supervised prediction approach is not only viable but superior. The guiding insight—that the rich, dense stream of multi-dimensional measurements available in many environments provides a far stronger supervisory signal than a sparse, scalar reward—is compelling and well-articulated. This work effectively revives and modernizes an older line of thinking (SL for control) and demonstrates its power in a domain where deep RL methods have become standard. This is precisely the kind of work that can open up new and fruitful research directions.

The empirical validation is thorough and impressive. The comparisons to DQN and A3C are fair, and the results in Figure 3 and Table 1 are striking. The fact that DFP's advantage grows with task complexity (outperforming A3C by a factor of 6 in the "Battle" scenario) is a strong signal that the approach is capturing something fundamental that value-based methods struggle with. The ablation study in Table 4 provides a clear and direct confirmation of the paper's central hypotheses: that predicting vectorial feedback is superior to a scalar reward, and that predicting at multiple future time horizons is beneficial.

Furthermore, the experiments on generalization are a highlight. The ability to train a single model on randomized goals and then have it successfully pursue diverse, and even contradictory, goals at test time (Table 3) is a significant step towards creating more flexible agents. This goal-agnostic training capability is a natural consequence of the DFP formulation and represents a major advantage over traditional RL approaches that bake the reward function into a learned value or policy.

**Additional Feedback and Questions**

While I am very positive about this paper, the following points could help improve it further. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from my decision to accept.

*   **The Nature of Measurements:** The paper's formulation relies on a distinction between high-dimensional sensory input (`s_t`) and a lower-dimensional measurement vector (`m_t`). The authors rightly acknowledge this is "somewhat artificial." The work would be strengthened by a deeper discussion on the generality of this concept. What are the characteristics of a good measurement stream? How might one apply this framework to a real-world robotic task where clean measurements like "health" or "frag count" are not readily available? Could the measurement vectors themselves be learned via representation learning techniques?

*   **Goal Representation:** The use of a linear combination for goals (`g > f`) is simple and effective for the tasks presented. However, it would be interesting to hear the authors' thoughts on its limitations and potential extensions to more complex, non-linear, or logical goal structures.

*   **Architectural Contributions:** The dueling-style architecture for splitting the prediction into expectation and action streams is an interesting choice. While the motivation is provided, its specific contribution to the final performance is not isolated in the ablation study. While not essential, quantifying the impact of this architectural choice would be informative.

I have one main question for the authors to clarify my understanding:
*   The selection of temporal offsets (`τ`) for prediction seems like a critical hyperparameter. Could you provide some intuition or analysis on how this set was chosen? How sensitive is the agent's performance to different sets of offsets (e.g., more dense, more sparse, or a different final horizon)?
