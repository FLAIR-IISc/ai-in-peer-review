### Review of "Direct Future Prediction"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a novel and compelling approach to sensorimotor control in complex, immersive environments. The core contribution is the reframing of the control problem from a reinforcement learning (RL) paradigm to a supervised learning (SL) one. Instead of learning a policy or value function from a sparse, scalar reward, the proposed agent learns to predict the future values of a rich, multi-dimensional stream of "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition, kill count) conditioned on its current sensory input, current measurements, a potential action, and a parameterized goal.

This formulation provides two key advantages. First, the dense, vectorial measurement stream offers a much richer supervisory signal than a traditional scalar reward, which the authors argue stabilizes and accelerates learning. Second, by explicitly conditioning predictions on a goal vector, the agent can be trained in a "goal-agnostic" manner and then be deployed to pursue a wide variety of dynamically specified goals at test time, including those unseen during training. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of their approach in the challenging 3D environment of Doom, showing that DFP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods like A3C and DQN, particularly on complex tasks. The method's robustness and generalization capabilities are further validated by winning a major AI competition in previously unseen environments.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is an excellent paper with a simple, elegant idea that is executed effectively and validated with exceptionally strong empirical results. It makes a significant contribution to the field of sensorimotor learning.

1.  **Exceptional Empirical Performance:** The method drastically outperforms strong, widely-used baselines like A3C and DQN by a large margin (e.g., a 6x improvement in frags on the "Battle" scenario). Winning the Visual Doom AI Competition on unseen maps is a powerful, real-world validation of the method's effectiveness and generalization capabilities, lending significant credibility to the claims.
2.  **Novel and Effective Goal Conditioning:** The framework for incorporating dynamically specified goals is a major strength. The ability to train a single model that can later adopt different "personalities" (e.g., aggressive, pacifist) by simply changing an input goal vector is a significant step towards more flexible and general agents. The experiments in Table 3 convincingly demonstrate this capability.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper's central thesis, that dense, multi-dimensional feedback is a more effective supervisory signal than a sparse scalar reward for certain complex tasks, is well-motivated by prior work (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992) and convincingly demonstrated by the experiments. The ablation study in Table 4 provides clear evidence for this, showing that performance degrades significantly when using only a scalar "frag count" prediction or a single temporal offset. This directly supports the two main design choices of the DFP framework: using vectorial feedback and predicting at multiple time horizons.

The architectural design, while building on established components (CNNs, dueling architectures), is thoughtfully constructed. The separation of prediction into expectation and action streams, inspired by Wang et al. (2016), is a sensible choice for isolating action-independent and action-dependent dynamics.

The comparison to baselines is thorough. The authors have clearly put significant effort into tuning the competing methods (e.g., hyperparameter search for A3C) and reporting their best results, which makes the performance gap achieved by DFP all the more impressive. The paper is also well-situated in the literature, correctly identifying its relationship to Monte Carlo methods, universal value functions (Schaul et al., 2015), and predictive state representations.

Finally, the generalization experiments (Table 2) are another key strength. Showing that a model trained on varied textures can generalize not only to unseen textures but also to entirely different maze layouts is a strong result that speaks to the robustness of the learned representations.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper further and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **The "Measurement Stream" Assumption:** The paper's primary strength is also its central assumption: the availability of a meaningful, low-dimensional measurement vector `m_t`. In video games, these are readily available from the game engine. The authors should add a more explicit discussion on the limitations of this assumption. How would this approach be applied to real-world robotics, where such clean measurements might not exist or would need to be learned? This would help to clarify the scope and applicability of the proposed method.
*   **Relationship to Model-Based RL:** The paper frames the approach as SL vs. RL. However, learning a function `F(s_t, m_t, a)` that predicts future outcomes (`m_{t+\tau}`) can be seen as learning a compact, task-relevant dynamics model. The policy is then derived via single-step planning (`argmax_a`). This places the work squarely in the realm of model-based RL. While the authors mention model-based RL in passing, I believe the paper would be strengthened by explicitly discussing this connection. DFP can be viewed as an effective instance of model-based control where the model is learned in a semantically meaningful "measurement space" rather than raw sensory space.
*   **Choice of Temporal Offsets:** The choice of temporal offsets `τ = {1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32}` and the weighting of the last three in the loss function feels somewhat arbitrary. While the ablation shows that multiple offsets are better than one, a brief discussion on the sensitivity to these hyperparameters or the intuition behind this exponential spacing would be beneficial.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the practical requirements for defining the measurement vector `m_t`? How sensitive is the approach to the inclusion of less informative or redundant measurements, and how might one apply this framework to domains (e.g., real-world robotics) where salient measurements like "health" are not explicitly available?
2.  The paper contrasts DFP with model-free RL methods. Could you comment further on the relationship between DFP and model-based RL? Do you see DFP as a specific, effective implementation of a model-based approach where the "model" predicts outcomes in a specified feature space (the measurements) rather than in pixel space?
3.  Regarding the goal-agnostic training (Table 3), the performance of the models trained on random goals is remarkably close to the specialist model on the main task. This is an impressive result. Do you have any insights into why there is so little performance degradation? Does this suggest the agent is learning a very general internal model of the environment's dynamics that is easily repurposed?
