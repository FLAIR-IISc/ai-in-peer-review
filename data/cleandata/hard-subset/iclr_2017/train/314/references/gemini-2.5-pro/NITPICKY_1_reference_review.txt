### Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a novel approach for sensorimotor control in complex, three-dimensional environments. The core idea is to reframe the control problem as a supervised learning task. Instead of maximizing a scalar reward signal as in standard reinforcement learning, the agent learns to predict a vector of future "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input, measurements, goal, and a potential action. Action selection at test time involves choosing the action that leads to the best predicted outcome according to a dynamically specified goal. The authors evaluate this approach in several challenging Doom scenarios, demonstrating superior performance over state-of-the-art deep RL methods like A3C and DQN. Key contributions include the problem formulation itself, an effective deep network architecture, and strong empirical results that show robust generalization across environments and goals, culminating in a win at the Visual Doom AI Competition.

### Decision: Reject

While the paper presents a compelling and empirically successful alternative to standard reinforcement learning, my recommendation is to reject it in its current form. The decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  Several critical methodological details are either ambiguous or entirely missing, which hinders reproducibility and a rigorous assessment of the proposed method.
2.  The paper suffers from a significant number of presentation flaws, including a poorly formatted bibliography and inconsistencies in the experimental setup description, which undermine its overall quality and suggest a lack of careful preparation.

### Supporting Arguments

**1. Methodological Ambiguity and Missing Details:**

The paper's clarity is compromised by a lack of precision in several core components of the method.

*   **Distinction between Sensory Input (`s_t`) and Measurements (`m_t`):** The paper introduces this distinction by stating it is "somewhat artificial" and that `m_t` is the subset of sensations the agent aims to predict. The justification provided is that predicting the full sensory stream (`s_t`) is currently intractable. While pragmatic, this is not a principled distinction. It is unclear how one should decide what constitutes a "measurement" in a new domain, which is a crucial aspect for the general applicability of this method.
*   **Training Objective:** Section 4.1 states, "We set the temporal offsets τ1, . . . , τn of predicted future measurements to 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, and 32 steps in all experiments. Only the latest three time steps contribute to the objective function, with coefficients (0.5, 0.5, 1)." This description is critically ambiguous. Does "latest three" refer to the predictions for τ=8, 16, and 32? If so, why predict the other three offsets at all? Furthermore, the choice of coefficients (0.5, 0.5, 1) is not justified. These details are fundamental to the training procedure and their lack of clarity makes the work difficult to build upon or reproduce.
*   **Training Procedure:** Key details of the training loop are glossed over. The ε-greedy policy is mentioned, but the annealing schedule for ε is only described as a "fixed schedule." The agent's parameters are updated "after every k new experiences," but the value of `k` is not provided in the main text. While some details are deferred to an appendix, such fundamental parameters should be included in the main paper for a self-contained description.
*   **Architecture:** The architecture in Section 3.2 heavily borrows from the dueling network architecture of Wang et al. (2016), with its "expectation stream" (value stream) and "action stream" (advantage stream). While the prior work is cited in the paragraph, the description presents this structure as an integral part of the proposed model without making it sufficiently clear that this specific architectural innovation is not novel.

**2. Experimental and Presentation Flaws:**

The paper's claims of superiority are strong, but the presentation and certain experimental aspects are weak.

*   **Baseline Comparison:** The comparison with Deep Successor Representations (DSR) is incomplete. The authors state they were "only able to evaluate DSR on the Basic scenario" and could not perform "extensive hyperparameter tuning" due to its slow training speed. While the practical constraints are understandable, presenting a comparison based on a single, simple task with untuned hyperparameters does not constitute a fair or rigorous evaluation. The claims of outperforming DSR are therefore unsubstantiated.
*   **Bibliography and Citations:** The reference section is highly unprofessional and contains numerous errors. Multiple entries have extraneous question marks (e.g., Adolph and Berger.,? \Q2006\E). The citation for Silver et al. (2016) ends with "search. Nature," which appears to be an incomplete sentence. Most critically, the final entry in the bibliography is a stray, incomplete sentence: "The large network is similar, but all layers starting from the third are wider by a factor of two. In all networks we use the leaky ReLU nonlinearity LReLU(x) = max(x, 0.2x) after each nonterminal layer, He, He,? \Q2015\E, He,". This appears to be a misplaced note from the appendix. Such a high number of formatting errors in a critical section like the bibliography raises serious concerns about the diligence with which the manuscript was prepared and reviewed by the authors.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

*   Provide a clearer, more principled guideline for partitioning observations into sensory input `s_t` and measurements `m_t`.
*   The training objective for future predictions must be clarified. A precise mathematical formulation describing how the predictions for the different τ values are weighted and combined in the loss function is essential. Justify the choice of weights.
*   Please provide all necessary hyperparameters for reproducibility in the main paper (e.g., the ε-schedule, the update frequency `k`, mini-batch size `N`, memory size `M`).
*   The DSR baseline comparison should either be completed under fair conditions or removed.
*   The entire paper, and especially the reference section, requires meticulous proofreading and reformatting to adhere to conference standards. This is not merely a cosmetic issue; it reflects on the overall scientific rigor of the submission.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you please provide the exact formula for the loss function used in training? Specifically, how do the six predicted futures for τ ∈ {1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32} map to the three terms with coefficients (0.5, 0.5, 1) in the objective?
2.  The goal `g` is used both to parameterize the predictor network `F` and in the argmax operation for action selection. Did you investigate the effect of providing the goal to the network? Does the model learn different prediction strategies for different goals, or does it learn a single predictive model that is then modulated by the goal during action selection?
3.  The goal is defined as maximizing `g > f`, a linear combination of future measurement differences. Have you experimented with non-linear utility functions? How crucial is this linearity assumption to the success of the model?
