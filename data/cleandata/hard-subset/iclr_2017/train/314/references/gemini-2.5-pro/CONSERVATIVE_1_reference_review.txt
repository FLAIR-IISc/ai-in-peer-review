**Summary of Contributions**

The paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which frames the task as a supervised learning problem rather than a traditional reinforcement learning problem. The core idea is to train a deep network to predict future changes in a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on the current sensory input, current measurements, a specified goal, and a potential action. At test time, the agent selects the action that is predicted to yield the most desirable future measurements according to its current goal. The authors evaluate this approach in the complex 3D environment of the game Doom, demonstrating that DFP significantly outperforms established deep reinforcement learning baselines like DQN and A3C, particularly on more complex "battle" scenarios. The paper also shows that the learned models can generalize to new environments and can be trained "goal-agnostically" to pursue dynamically specified goals at test time.

**Decision: Reject**

While the empirical results are impressive, I am voting for rejection due to two primary concerns:

1.  The strength of the claims against established reinforcement learning methods is not fully supported by the experimental comparison. The extraordinary performance gap between the proposed method and state-of-the-art baselines like A3C raises questions about the fairness of the comparison, particularly regarding the translation of the DFP problem structure into an equivalent and optimally tuned reward signal for the RL agents.
2.  The paper frames the approach as a broad alternative to reinforcement learning, but its applicability seems contingent on the availability of a dense, multi-dimensional stream of "measurements." This dependency limits the generality of the claims and positions the method as a specialized solution for a particular class of problems, rather than a fundamental advancement over the RL paradigm, which is designed to handle more general cases like sparse rewards.

**Supporting Arguments**

The central claim of the paper is that DFP, a supervised learning approach, can "significantly outperform state-of-the-art deep RL methods." The results in Table 1 show DFP outperforming A3C by a factor of 6 in scenario D3 and 2.5 in D4. This is a remarkable result that demands extraordinary evidence and scrutiny. My main concern is that this gap may not stem from an inherent superiority of the DFP formulation, but rather from the difficulty of engineering an equivalent, effective reward function for the RL baselines.

The DFP model is trained to predict `f = <m_{t+τ1} - m_t, ...>`, a rich, multi-step, multi-variate target. The RL baselines, in contrast, were given a reward based on a linear combination of the changes in measurements at each step. While this seems like a fair translation, it is not clear that this is the optimal way to provide the same information to a value-based or policy-gradient method. Standard RL agents often struggle with dense, fluctuating reward signals, and their performance is highly sensitive to the reward shaping and discount factor. It is plausible that the stability of a direct regression objective (L2 loss) on a rich target vector provides a significant optimization advantage over the high-variance, bootstrapping-based updates of DQN or A3C, especially when the supervisory signal is so dense. The paper lacks a deeper analysis of *why* this performance gap exists. Is it a failure of credit assignment in the RL agents? A variance issue? Without this analysis, it is difficult to conclude that RL as a paradigm is inferior, rather than just the specific implementation of the baselines used.

Furthermore, the paper's framing downplays the method's reliance on a key assumption: the existence of a meaningful, low-dimensional measurement vector `m_t` that is distinct from the raw sensory stream `s_t` and in which goals can be expressed. While this is true for video games with on-screen displays (health, ammo), it is not true for many real-world problems or classic RL benchmarks where the only feedback is a sparse success/failure signal. The authors state the distinction is "somewhat artificial," but it is, in fact, the central element that enables their method. By assuming this measurement stream exists, the problem is converted from one of temporal credit assignment (the hard part of RL) into a multi-step prediction problem. This is a valid and interesting problem formulation, but it is a narrower one than what RL aims to solve. The paper should be more circumspect in its claims, positioning the work as a powerful technique for this specific, information-rich setting, rather than a general replacement for RL. The work of Jordan & Rumelhart (1992) is correctly cited, but their conclusion—that the choice between SL and RL is guided by the environment's characteristics—seems to be understated in the context of the paper's claims of general superiority.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The work is promising and the results are undeniably strong within the chosen domain. The paper could be significantly strengthened with the following additions:

*   **A More In-Depth Baseline Analysis:** A more compelling comparison would involve trying to augment the RL baselines to better utilize the available information. For instance, could the RL agent's value function also be conditioned on the goal vector `g`, similar to Universal Value Functions (Schaul et al., 2015)? Could the input to the RL agent include a history of measurements to approximate the multi-step information DFP uses? A controlled experiment where an RL agent predicts returns accumulated over the same temporal offsets (`τ_i`) used by DFP would help isolate the effect of the supervised regression loss versus the Bellman loss.
*   **Ablation on Measurements:** The ablation study convincingly shows that vectorial feedback is better than a scalar reward. It would be even more insightful to see how performance degrades as informative measurements are removed. For example, in the "Battle" scenario, what happens if the agent only has access to health and frags, but not ammunition? This would help clarify the sensitivity of the method to the quality of the measurement vector.
*   **Discussion on Limitations:** A more explicit discussion of the method's limitations would be welcome. The paper should clearly state that the approach is designed for environments with dense, multi-dimensional feedback and may not be applicable in sparse reward settings, which remain a key domain for traditional RL methods.
*   **Connection to Model-Based RL:** The approach is functionally a type of model-based control. The network `F` acts as a simplified world model that predicts the consequence of actions on a specific subset of the state (the measurements `m_t`). Framing the work in this context and comparing it to other model-based RL approaches would place it more firmly within the existing literature.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  For the RL baselines in scenarios D3 and D4, was the reward at each time step `t` simply `r_t = 0.5 * Δammo_t + 0.5 * Δhealth_t + 1.0 * Δfrags_t`? Was a discount factor `γ` applied? Could you clarify if you experimented with different reward shaping strategies to ensure the A3C baseline was as competitive as possible?
2.  The core of your method is the regression objective in Eq. (4). Do you have a hypothesis for why minimizing this direct prediction error is so much more effective and stable than minimizing a Bellman error, which is the cornerstone of many successful RL algorithms?
3.  How does one determine the appropriate set of measurements `m_t` for a new task? The success of the method seems to hinge on a human designer identifying the right variables to predict. How does this compare to an RL agent that could, in principle, learn to pay attention to these variables from a scalar reward alone?
4.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the choice of temporal offsets `τ`? The paper uses {1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32}. What happens if only short-term ({1, 2, 4}) or only long-term ({16, 32}) offsets are used?
