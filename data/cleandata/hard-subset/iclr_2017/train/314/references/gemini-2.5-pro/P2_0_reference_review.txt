### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes an alternative approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which frames the problem as a supervised learning task rather than a traditional reinforcement learning (RL) problem. The core idea is to train an agent to predict the future values of a multi-dimensional stream of "measurements" (e.g., health, ammo, score) conditioned on its current sensory input, current measurements, a potential action, and a goal vector. The goal vector specifies a desired linear combination of future measurement changes. At test time, the agent selects the action that is predicted to yield the best outcome according to its current goal.

The key claimed contributions are:
1.  A novel formulation that leverages dense, multi-dimensional feedback (measurements) as a rich supervisory signal, which is argued to be more effective than the sparse scalar rewards common in RL.
2.  A flexible framework that allows the agent to be trained without a fixed goal and to pursue dynamically specified goals at test time without retraining.
3.  State-of-the-art empirical results in complex, 3D first-person environments (VizDoom), demonstrating that this simpler, supervised approach significantly outperforms sophisticated deep RL baselines like A3C and DQN, particularly on challenging tasks. The authors also validate their approach by winning a public AI competition.

### 2. Decision: Accept

This is a strong paper that should be accepted. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Exceptional Empirical Results:** The proposed method demonstrates a massive performance improvement over state-of-the-art deep RL methods (A3C) on complex, visually rich tasks. The results are not marginal; outperforming a strong baseline by a factor of 2.5-6x is highly significant. The victory in the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition provides compelling, real-world validation of the method's effectiveness and generalization capabilities in unseen environments.
2.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Formulation:** The paper makes a convincing argument for reconsidering the supervised learning approach to control, especially in environments rich with intrinsic, measurable feedback. The formulation is simple, intuitive, and directly addresses the challenges of sparse rewards. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate that the core components of the approach—vectorial feedback and multi-step prediction—are critical to its success.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's claims are very well-supported by a comprehensive and rigorous set of experiments.

**On Empirical Strength:**
*   The comparison against baselines is fair and thorough. The authors use standard, strong baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) and, as detailed in the appendix, perform a hyperparameter search for A3C to ensure a strong comparison.
*   The progression of scenarios from "Basic" to "Battle 2" clearly illustrates the performance gap widening as task complexity and the richness of the measurement stream increase. This directly supports the central hypothesis that DFP excels where traditional RL struggles with sparse rewards.
*   The generalization experiments (across textures and goals) are crucial. The ability to train with randomized goals and then successfully pursue specific, even adversarial ("pacifist," "drain ammo") goals at test time is a powerful demonstration of the model's flexibility. This is a significant advantage over standard RL agents that are trained for a single, fixed reward function.
*   Winning the Visual Doom AI Competition is a standout result that is hard to argue against. It shows the method is not just effective in a curated set of experiments but is robust and generalizable enough to defeat other state-of-the-art systems in a competitive setting.

**On the Formulation and Analysis:**
*   The paper does an excellent job of positioning its work in the literature, acknowledging early ideas on supervised control (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992) and connections to universal value functions (Schaul et al., 2015). This grounding demonstrates a mature understanding of the field.
*   The model architecture is well-reasoned, borrowing the effective dueling network structure to separately model the expected outcome and the action-specific advantages.
*   The ablation study in Table 4 is a critical piece of evidence. It cleanly isolates the contributions of using vectorial feedback versus a scalar reward and predicting at multiple future time steps. The results unequivocally show that both elements are vital, providing strong support for the paper's core claims.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While this is an excellent paper, the following points could help further strengthen it for a final version. These are suggestions for improvement and do not change my positive assessment.

*   **Discussion on Measurement Selection:** The distinction between raw sensory input `s_t` and measurements `m_t` is a key design choice. The paper acknowledges this is "somewhat artificial." A deeper discussion on the principles guiding this selection would be beneficial. How would the system perform if some measurements were noisy or irrelevant? What if a crucial aspect of the state is not included in `m_t`? Exploring the sensitivity to this human-in-the-loop design decision would be valuable.
*   **Relation to Model-Based RL:** The approach involves predicting future outcomes, which has a strong connection to model-based RL. The paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of its relationship to model-based methods. For instance, one could view DFP as learning a specific type of "model" that directly predicts task-relevant measurements rather than the full next state (e.g., the next image frame). Clarifying this connection could help situate the work more precisely.
*   **Scope of Applicability:** The method's success seems tied to environments where a useful set of measurements is readily available (e.g., game HUDs, robot proprioception). It would be helpful to explicitly discuss the potential limitations or challenges in applying DFP to domains where such measurements are not easily defined or accessible, and perhaps speculate on how one might learn the measurements themselves.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of the model to the choice of temporal offsets `τ` for future prediction? The set {1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32} seems reasonable but is there a principled way to choose these, and how does performance change with, for example, a denser or sparser set of offsets?
2.  The goal `g` is used as an input to the network `F` during training and testing. Have you considered an alternative architecture where the network predicts a goal-agnostic future vector `f_a` for each action `a`, and action selection is then performed via `argmax_a g^T * f_a`? This would decouple the core prediction from the goal, which might improve generalization or training efficiency. Did you experiment with such a design?
3.  In the goal-agnostic training experiments (Table 3), the models trained with randomized goals perform remarkably well, nearly matching the specialist model on its own task. This is a very strong result. However, is there a risk of "averaging" behaviors, where the agent becomes competent at many things but excels at none? Do you foresee scenarios where this approach might fall short compared to a specialist agent trained for a very specific, narrow goal?
