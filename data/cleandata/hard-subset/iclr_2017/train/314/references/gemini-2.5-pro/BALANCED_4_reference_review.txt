### **1. Summary**

This paper presents an approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which reframes the problem as a supervised learning task. Instead of learning a policy or value function to maximize a scalar reward, the proposed model learns to directly predict the future values of a multi-dimensional stream of "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on the current sensory input (raw pixels), a candidate action, and a goal vector. The goal is defined as a desired linear combination of changes in future measurements. At test time, the agent selects the action that is predicted to yield the most desirable outcome according to the current goal. This formulation leverages the rich, temporally dense supervisory signal provided by the measurement stream, which the authors argue is more representative of real-world embodied agents than a sparse scalar reward. The method is evaluated in a series of increasingly complex 3D environments in the game Doom. The experimental results are comprehensive, showing that DFP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods like A3C and DQN, especially on challenging tasks. The paper further demonstrates the model's ability to generalize to unseen environments and to flexibly pursue novel goals not seen during training. A key piece of evidence for the method's effectiveness is its win in the Visual Doom AI Competition on previously unseen maps.

### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Well-motivated and elegant reformulation:** The paper provides a clear and compelling argument for shifting from the standard sparse-reward RL paradigm to a supervised prediction framework when dense, multi-dimensional feedback is available. This simple yet powerful idea is well-grounded in the literature and effectively tackles the dual challenges of credit assignment and goal generalization.
2.  **Exceptionally strong and thorough empirical validation:** The claims are supported by extensive experiments that are well-designed and convincing. The comparison against strong baselines (A3C, DQN) on a suite of challenging tasks, combined with insightful ablation studies, robust generalization experiments (across both environments and goals), and a decisive victory in a public competition, provides a powerful and multifaceted body of evidence for the method's effectiveness.

### **3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The primary strength of this work is the combination of a simple, well-motivated idea with rigorous and convincing empirical support.

**Conceptual Strength:** The authors situate their work within the historical context of supervised learning versus reinforcement learning for control (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992; Sutton, 1988). They convincingly argue that for many complex, immersive environments, the standard RL assumption of a sparse, scalar reward is an unnecessary constraint. The proposed formulation, which utilizes a rich stream of measurements (`m_t`) as a dense supervisory signal, is a natural and effective alternative. This approach elegantly sidesteps the temporal credit assignment problem inherent in sparse reward settings. Furthermore, by explicitly conditioning the prediction on a goal vector `g`, the model learns a goal-agnostic world model (in terms of measurements) that can be flexibly directed at test time, a significant advantage over models trained for a single, fixed objective. The architectural design, which cleverly adapts the dueling network architecture to separate action-independent and action-dependent predictions, is also a thoughtful implementation detail.

**Empirical Rigor:** The experimental section is exemplary.
*   **Strong Baselines:** The authors compare against A3C, widely considered the state-of-the-art at the time, and provide details (in Appendix B) of a fair hyperparameter search, lending credibility to the reported performance gaps.
*   **Compelling Results:** DFP doesn't just match but significantly outperforms A3C on the more complex "Battle" scenarios (by a factor of 2.5-6x in frags, Table 1). This demonstrates that the benefits of the approach scale with task complexity and the richness of the measurement stream.
*   **Insightful Ablation Study (Table 4):** The ablation study directly validates the core hypotheses of the paper. It shows that both using vectorial feedback (three measurements vs. only frags) and predicting at multiple future time steps are crucial for achieving top performance. This provides clear evidence that the rich, dense signal is indeed the key driver of success, not just an architectural quirk.
*   **Impressive Generalization:** The experiments on goal-agnostic training (Table 3) are particularly strong. A model trained on randomized goals performs nearly as well on a specific task as a model trained specialistically for it, while also gaining the ability to adapt to entirely new objectives (e.g., a "pacifist" goal). The generalization to unseen textures (Table 2) further demonstrates the robustness of the learned representations.
*   **External Validation:** The victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition on unseen maps is a powerful, unbiased testament to the method's real-world effectiveness and generalization capabilities, validating the approach outside the confines of the paper's own experimental setup.

### **4. Additional Feedback**

These points are intended to help improve the manuscript and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Clarifying the Sensory vs. Measurement Distinction:** The paper states the distinction between sensory input `s_t` and measurements `m_t` is "somewhat artificial." While this is true, the choice of what constitutes a "measurement" is fundamental to the method's success. It would be beneficial to provide more guidance or intuition on this choice. Are measurements defined as any non-visual, low-dimensional data provided by the environment? Could learned features from `s_t` also serve as `m_t`? A brief discussion on the principles governing this design choice would increase the paper's practical value.
*   **Sensitivity to Temporal Offsets:** The choice of temporal offsets (`τ`) and their weighting in the loss function feels somewhat arbitrary. While the ablation shows that multiple offsets are better than one, it would be interesting to know how sensitive the model is to the specific values chosen. A short discussion on this point, even if brief, would add to the completeness of the analysis.
*   **Boundary Conditions of the Approach:** The paper focuses on environments where rich measurements are available. It would be valuable to explicitly discuss the limitations or boundary conditions. For instance, how would this approach fare in a classic Atari game where the only signal is a sparse score? Acknowledging that the method is tailored for a specific, albeit important, class of problems would strengthen the paper's positioning.

### **5. Questions for the Authors**

*   **Nature of Goals:** The goal is formulated as a linear combination of future measurement changes (`g > f`). Could you comment on the potential limitations of this linear formulation? Have you considered or do you foresee challenges in extending this to more complex, non-linear goal functions `u(f; g)`?
*   **Exploration:** The agent uses a simple ε-greedy policy for exploration. Given that the model makes explicit predictions about future outcomes, it seems uniquely suited for more sophisticated exploration strategies (e.g., based on maximizing prediction uncertainty or information gain). Did you experiment with such strategies, and do you believe they could further improve sample efficiency or performance?
*   **Relation to Model-Based RL:** Your approach has similarities to model-based RL, but instead of predicting the full next state/observation, you predict a low-dimensional measurement vector. Could you further elaborate on how you see the trade-offs between DFP and more traditional model-based methods that learn a latent dynamics model and predict future latent states?
