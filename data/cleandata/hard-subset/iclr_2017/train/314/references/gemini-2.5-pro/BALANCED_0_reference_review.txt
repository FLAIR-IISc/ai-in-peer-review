### **1. Summary**

This paper proposes an alternative framework for sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), that recasts the problem from reinforcement learning to supervised learning. The core idea is that instead of relying on a sparse, scalar reward signal, an agent can leverage a rich, temporally dense stream of multi-dimensional "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) to learn control policies. The proposed model is trained to predict the change in future measurements for each possible action, conditioned on the current sensory input, current measurements, and a parameterized goal vector. At test time, the agent selects the action that is predicted to yield the best outcome according to its current goal.

The key contributions are: (1) A simple and effective supervised learning formulation for sensorimotor control that avoids many complexities of traditional RL methods. (2) A demonstration that this approach can be trained with randomized, unspecified goals, yet can generalize to pursue specific, dynamically provided goals at test time. (3) A comprehensive empirical evaluation in the challenging 3D environment of Doom, which shows that DFP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL baselines like A3C, particularly on complex tasks. The paper further substantiates its claims with strong generalization results, including winning the Visual Doom AI Competition on unseen environments, and a thorough ablation study validating the benefits of vectorial feedback and multi-step prediction.

### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Exceptional Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling and rigorous evidence that a simple supervised learning approach can significantly outperform state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods (like A3C) on complex visuomotor control tasks. The large performance gains on the "Battle" scenarios, coupled with the victory in an official AI competition, provide powerful validation of the method's effectiveness and generalization capabilities.
2.  **Elegant and Flexible Framework:** The DFP framework is conceptually simple yet powerful. By conditioning on a goal vector, it naturally supports training a single, goal-agnostic policy that can be flexibly directed to perform a variety of tasks at test time without retraining. This is a significant advantage over standard RL agents that are typically optimized for a single, fixed reward function.

### **3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper is well-written, well-motivated, and the claims are strongly supported by a thorough set of experiments.

*   **Motivation and Positioning:** The authors correctly identify that many realistic environments provide richer feedback than a single scalar reward. The motivation to leverage this dense, multi-dimensional signal by framing control as a prediction problem is clear and well-argued. The paper does an excellent job of placing this idea in historical context (e.g., citing Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992) and contrasting it with the dominant temporal-difference learning paradigm.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The evaluation is comprehensive. The progression of tasks from simple to complex (D1 to D4) clearly illustrates the widening performance gap between DFP and the baselines. The authors appear to have conducted a fair comparison, reporting extensive hyperparameter tuning for the A3C baseline. The ablation study (Table 4) convincingly isolates the contributions of the two main components of the method: using vectorial feedback over a scalar reward and predicting at multiple future time steps.
*   **Goal-Conditioned Generalization:** The experiments in Table 3 are a highlight, clearly demonstrating the framework's ability to learn general skills. The fact that a model trained on randomized goals performs nearly as well on a specific task as a specialist model is impressive. Furthermore, its ability to generalize to novel goals (e.g., "pacifist" or "drain ammo") showcases a level of behavioral flexibility not easily achieved with standard methods.
*   **Real-World Validation:** The success in the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition is a particularly strong piece of evidence. Winning a competition on unseen maps against other methods is an unbiased, external validation that demonstrates the robustness and practical utility of the proposed approach beyond a curated set of experiments.

### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Connection to Model-Based RL:** The paper frames the approach as a supervised learning alternative to RL. However, it bears a strong resemblance to model-based RL, where the agent learns a model of the environment's dynamics and uses it for control. DFP can be viewed as learning a partial dynamics model focused only on the measurement variables (`m_t`). Clarifying this connection and discussing how DFP relates to or differs from classic model-based approaches (e.g., those that perform multi-step planning using the learned model vs. DFP's single-step "planning") would strengthen the paper's positioning.
*   **Dependence on Measurements:** The success of DFP relies on the availability of a semantically meaningful measurement vector `m_t`. This is a reasonable assumption in many domains (e.g., games, robotics with explicit sensors) but is also a limitation. It would be beneficial to include a short discussion on the applicability of the method in scenarios where such measurements are not readily available. Could a method like this be extended to learn a useful measurement representation from raw sensations alone?
*   **Long-Term Planning Horizons:** The prediction horizons are relatively short-term (up to 32 steps, or ~1 second). This seems well-suited for the reactive, fast-paced combat scenarios in Doom. However, for tasks requiring more strategic, long-term planning, this greedy, short-horizon action selection might be insufficient. A brief discussion of this potential limitation and possible extensions (e.g., iterative prediction, hierarchical approaches) would be a valuable addition.
*   **Handling Stochasticity:** The model makes a deterministic prediction of future measurements. In highly stochastic environments, this might be problematic. Have the authors considered extensions that predict a distribution over future outcomes, which might lead to more robust, risk-aware policies?

### **5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  The choice of which future time steps to use in the loss function ({1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32}, with weights on the last three) feels somewhat heuristic. Could you provide intuition or ablation results on how performance is affected by the choice of these temporal offsets and their weighting? Is the system sensitive to these hyperparameters?
2.  In the randomized goal training experiments (Table 3), the generalist models perform almost as well as the specialist on the primary task, but there remains a small performance gap. What, in your view, is being lost during generalist training that prevents it from perfectly matching the specialist? Could this gap be closed, for example, by a short fine-tuning phase on the specific goal?
3.  Could you elaborate on the initial data collection process? Since the agent starts with a random policy, is there a "warm-up" period where it collects experience via random exploration before the predictor `F` is sufficiently trained to produce meaningful actions? If so, how much initial random data is needed?
