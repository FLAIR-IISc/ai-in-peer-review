### Paper Review

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes an approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which frames the problem as a supervised learning task rather than a traditional reinforcement learning one. The core idea is to train a deep network to predict the future values of a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on the current sensory input, measurements, a proposed action, and a goal vector. The goal vector specifies a linear combination of future measurements to be maximized. At test time, the agent selects the action that is predicted to yield the best outcome according to the current goal. This formulation provides a dense, multidimensional supervisory signal from the environment's feedback, in contrast to the often sparse scalar reward in RL.

The authors evaluate their approach in the challenging 3D environment of the game Doom. They demonstrate through extensive experiments that DFP significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods like A3C and DQN, particularly on complex tasks involving navigation and combat. Furthermore, they show that the model can be trained without a fixed goal and can generalize to novel goals and previously unseen environments at test time. The strength of this generalization was validated by their model winning the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper with a clear and compelling contribution. The two primary reasons for my decision are:
1.  **Exceptional Empirical Results:** The proposed method demonstrates a massive performance improvement over strong, widely-recognized baselines (A3C, DQN) on complex, visually rich tasks. The victory in an official competition on unseen maps provides powerful, independent evidence of the method's effectiveness and generalization capabilities.
2.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Formulation:** The paper convincingly argues for reframing sensorimotor control as a supervised prediction problem when dense, multidimensional feedback is available. This simple and elegant approach sidesteps many of the complexities and potential instabilities of value-based RL methods (e.g., bootstrapping) and leverages a richer learning signal, leading to superior performance and data efficiency.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's claims are strongly supported by a thorough and well-designed set of experiments.

*   **Rigorous Comparison to Baselines:** The authors compare DFP against multiple relevant and strong deep RL baselines. The performance gap shown in Figure 3 and Table 1, especially in the "Battle" and "Battle 2" scenarios, is not marginal but substantial (e.g., a 6x improvement in frags over A3C). This convincingly demonstrates the superiority of the DFP approach for this class of problems. The authors also appear to have made a good-faith effort to tune the baselines, strengthening the validity of the comparison.

*   **Validation of Core Hypotheses:** The ablation study in Table 4 directly supports the central claims of the paper. It shows that using vectorial feedback is significantly better than a scalar reward (even when the evaluation metric is that same scalar) and that predicting at multiple future time steps is beneficial. This provides clear evidence that the richness of the supervision signal is a key driver of the method's success.

*   **Demonstrated Generalization:** The experiments on goal-agnostic training (Table 3) are particularly compelling. The ability to train a single agent that can later adopt different "personalities" (e.g., aggressive, pacifist) simply by changing the goal vector `g` at test time is a significant step towards flexible and general-purpose agents. This capability is a direct benefit of the proposed formulation and is not easily achievable with standard RL agents trained on a single reward function. The generalization across visually distinct environments (Table 2) further underscores the robustness of the learned representations.

*   **Clarity and Simplicity:** The proposed method is conceptually simple and easy to understand. The reduction of a complex control problem to a standard supervised regression task is an elegant insight. The paper is well-written, and the model architecture and training procedure are described with sufficient detail to be reproducible.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is an excellent paper, the following points could be addressed to further strengthen it.

*   **Defining the Problem Class:** The paper's strength lies in environments with rich, engineered measurement streams (health, ammo, etc.). It would be beneficial to more explicitly discuss the limitations of the approach. How would DFP perform in an environment where the only available feedback is a single, very sparse reward (e.g., a single +1 for solving a puzzle)? The authors briefly touch on this by citing Jordan & Rumelhart (1992), but a more explicit discussion in the context of their results would help delineate the problem space where DFP is advantageous versus where traditional RL remains necessary.

*   **Clarifying the Role of the Goal Input `g`:** The goal vector `g` is used for action selection via the inner product `g > F(...)`, which is clear. However, `g` is also an input to the network `F` itself. The motivation for this is not fully explained. The prediction of future physical measurements (`f`) should depend on the state and action, but not necessarily on the agent's current *intentions* (`g`). Does including `g` as an input allow the network to learn a more flexible representation, perhaps by focusing its attention on goal-relevant features of the input? A sentence or two clarifying this architectural choice would be helpful.

*   **Relation to Model-Based RL:** The approach involves predicting future outcomes, which has a strong connection to model-based RL. However, instead of predicting the full future state `s_t+k`, it predicts a low-dimensional measurement vector `m_t+k`. This is a crucial and pragmatic distinction that likely makes the prediction problem much more tractable. The paper would benefit from a slightly more detailed discussion of where DFP sits in the spectrum between model-free and model-based methods. It could be seen as learning an "abstract" or "task-relevant" model of the world dynamics.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please elaborate on the motivation for conditioning the predictor `F` on the goal vector `g` (Equation 2)? Have you experimented with a variant where `F` only takes `(o_t, a)` as input and the goal `g` is only used for the final action selection?
2.  The distinction between sensory input `s_t` and measurements `m_t` is described as "somewhat artificial." Could you comment on the sensitivity of the method to the choice of measurements? For instance, in the "Battle" scenario, what would happen if the agent was only provided with "frags" as a measurement, effectively turning it into a scalar reward prediction problem (as in the ablation study), but still framed as supervised learning? Does the benefit come purely from the vector nature, or also from the specific semantics of "health" and "ammo" measurements?
