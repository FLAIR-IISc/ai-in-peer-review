### **1. Summary**

The paper proposes an approach to sensorimotor control, called Direct Future Prediction (DFP), that reframes the problem as a supervised learning task. Instead of learning a value function or policy to maximize a discounted scalar reward, the agent learns a function that directly predicts future values of a multi-dimensional measurement stream (e.g., health, ammunition, frag count) conditioned on its current sensory input, current measurements, a potential action, and a goal vector. The goal vector specifies the desired future state in terms of these measurements. At test time, the agent selects the action that is predicted to yield the most desirable future outcome according to its current goal.

The main contributions are:
1.  A model for sensorimotor control that leverages dense, multi-dimensional feedback from the environment as a rich supervisory signal, which is argued to be more effective than a sparse scalar reward.
2.  A framework that naturally supports goal-agnostic training and generalization to dynamically specified goals at test time, without needing to retrain the model.
3.  Strong empirical evidence in complex 3D environments (VizDoom) showing that this approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods (DQN, A3C) and can generalize effectively to unseen environments, as validated by winning an official competition.

### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Exceptional Empirical Performance:** The paper presents a simple yet highly effective method that achieves state-of-the-art results on a series of complex 3D visuomotor control tasks. The large performance gap over strong baselines like A3C, especially in the more challenging scenarios, provides compelling evidence for the method's efficacy.
2.  **Effective Goal Conditioning and Generalization:** The work successfully demonstrates a practical approach for training a single agent that can pursue diverse and dynamically specified goals. The experiments on goal-agnostic training and subsequent generalization to novel tasks (e.g., switching from aggressive to "pacifist" behavior) are well-executed and convincing, addressing a significant challenge in reinforcement learning.

### **3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's claims are strongly supported by a thorough and well-designed set of experiments.

*   **Technical Soundness and Clarity:** The method is clearly articulated in Section 3. The architecture, including the use of a dueling-style network to separate the prediction into expectation and action-specific streams, is well-motivated and sensible. The training procedure is straightforward and described with sufficient detail. The paper is well-written and places the work effectively within the context of existing literature, drawing connections to early supervised learning approaches for control, Monte Carlo methods, and universal value functions.

*   **Rigorous Empirical Evaluation:** The authors compare DFP against strong and relevant deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) across four scenarios of increasing complexity. The results in Table 1 and Figure 3 show that while all methods perform well on the simple task, DFP's advantage grows substantially with task complexity, outperforming A3C by a factor of 6 in the "Battle" scenario. This is a significant result. The external validation from winning the Visual Doom AI Competition on unseen maps further strengthens the claims of the model's robustness and generalization ability.

*   **Targeted Validation of Core Claims:** The paper includes specific experiments to validate its central hypotheses. The ablation study (Table 4) provides direct evidence that using vectorial feedback is superior to a scalar reward (17.0 vs 10.9 frags) and that predicting at multiple future time steps is beneficial. The goal-generalization experiments (Table 3) systematically demonstrate that training with randomized goals enables the agent to adapt to a variety of test-time objectives, performing nearly as well on a specific goal as an agent trained exclusively for it, while being far more flexible.

### **4. Additional Feedback**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   The distinction between "sensory input" `s_t` and "measurements" `m_t` is a core concept. While functionally clear in the paper (images vs. game stats), it would be beneficial to discuss the guiding principles for this separation more generally. Is it based on dimensionality, semantic interpretability, or something else? A brief discussion on how one might define or learn `m_t` in domains without explicit game-like stats would broaden the applicability of the work.
*   The choice of temporal offsets τ for future predictions is a key hyperparameter. The paper uses a fixed, exponentially spaced set. A sensitivity analysis or discussion on the impact of this choice (e.g., number of offsets, spacing) would add valuable insight into the model's behavior.
*   The paper notes a connection to Monte Carlo (MC) methods in the background section. This is an interesting link that could be explored further in the discussion. The DFP approach can be viewed as a form of goal-conditioned, multi-dimensional MC evaluation. Expanding on the relationship, including how DFP differs from traditional MC methods (e.g., through its direct prediction of state changes rather than value accumulation), could provide a richer theoretical framing.
*   A minor detail from the appendix is that only the last three time steps contribute to the loss function. This seems like a potentially important implementation detail that influences which predictions are prioritized during learning. A short justification for this choice in the main text could be helpful.

### **5. Questions for Authors**

Your response to these questions would help clarify my understanding of the work.

1.  The training loop involves an agent acting ε-greedily with respect to the current predictor `F`, while `F` is trained on mini-batches from an experience memory. This has characteristics of off-policy learning. Did you observe any training instabilities that are sometimes associated with off-policy function approximation, and does the supervised regression objective in Equation 4 mitigate these issues compared to the bootstrapping used in methods like DQN?
2.  In the goal-agnostic training regime, for a given training sample `⟨o_i, a_i, g_i, f_i⟩`, is the goal vector `g_i` the same one that the agent was using to collect that specific experience tuple? Or is it possible to resample goals for tuples in the replay buffer, similar to hindsight experience replay?
3.  The experiments are situated in domains where a low-dimensional, semantically meaningful measurement vector `m_t` is readily available. How do you see this approach extending to environments where such measurements are not provided a priori (e.g., real-world robotics)? Could an unsupervised representation learning objective be used to create a suitable measurement stream from the raw sensory input `s_t`?
