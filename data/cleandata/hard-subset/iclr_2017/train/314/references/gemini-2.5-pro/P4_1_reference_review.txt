### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which reframes the problem as a supervised learning task. Instead of relying on a scalar reward signal, the agent learns to predict future changes in a multi-dimensional stream of measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) based on its current sensory input (raw pixels), current measurements, a potential action, and a parameterized goal. The core contributions are:
1.  A formulation that uses the rich, temporally dense information from a vector of measurements as a supervisory signal, which contrasts with the often sparse scalar reward in traditional reinforcement learning.
2.  A goal-conditioned architecture that allows the agent to be trained without a fixed objective. This enables the learned model to generalize to dynamically specified goals at test time, including goals not seen during training.
3.  An extensive empirical evaluation in complex, 3D first-person environments (ViZDoom) demonstrating that this relatively simple supervised approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods (DQN, A3C) on challenging tasks. The paper further substantiates its claims with strong generalization results across different environments and goals, and a winning performance in the Visual Doom AI Competition.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a simple, elegant, and well-motivated alternative to standard value-based reinforcement learning. By reformulating sensorimotor control as a supervised prediction problem conditioned on goals, it effectively leverages dense, multi-dimensional feedback streams that are common in many complex environments.
2.  The empirical evidence supporting the method's effectiveness is exceptionally strong and comprehensive. The approach not only substantially outperforms well-regarded deep RL baselines in complex scenarios but also demonstrates impressive generalization capabilities to unseen environments and novel goals, validated by both controlled experiments and a victory in a public competition.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's primary strength lies in its clear and compelling argument, backed by rigorous experimentation.

**Technical Soundness and Motivation:** The core idea of using direct prediction of future outcomes is a departure from the dominant Bellman-equation-based methods in deep RL. The motivation is sound: in many real-world or immersive simulated environments, an agent has access to more than just a single reward signal. The paper argues convincingly that this multi-dimensional stream of "measurements" provides a richer, more stable training signal. The formulation is technically straightforward, treating the problem as a regression from state, action, and goal to future measurements, which avoids potential instabilities associated with bootstrapping in methods like Q-learning. The paper does an excellent job of placing this idea in the context of prior work, acknowledging its roots in early SL-for-control literature and its connections to Monte Carlo and universal value function methods, while clearly carving out its unique contribution.

**Empirical Rigor and Strength of Claims:** The experimental section is thorough and directly supports the paper's main claims.
*   **Performance Comparison:** The comparison against strong baselines like A3C and DQN in Section 4.2 is fair and the results are striking. While performance is comparable on simpler tasks, DFP shows a dramatic performance advantage in the more complex "Battle" scenarios (e.g., a 6x improvement in frags over A3C in D3). This supports the hypothesis that the benefit of dense, vectorial feedback becomes more pronounced as task complexity increases.
*   **Generalization:** The paper provides two powerful demonstrations of generalization. The results in Table 2 show that training on varied environments (D3-tx, D4-tx) produces models that generalize well to unseen environments. Even more impressively, the goal-conditioning experiments in Table 3 show that an agent trained on randomized goals can perform nearly as well on a specific task as a specialist agent trained only for that task, while also being able to adapt to novel, unseen goals (e.g., acting as a "pacifist"). This is a significant step towards creating more flexible agents.
*   **Ablation Study:** The ablation study in Table 4 provides a crucial piece of evidence. It clearly demonstrates that both key components of the proposed method—using vectorial feedback and predicting at multiple future time steps—are critical to its success. The drop in performance when using only a scalar "frag count" prediction is substantial, directly validating the paper's core premise.
*   **External Validation:** The victory in the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI Competition serves as strong, independent validation of the method's robustness and generalization capabilities in unseen environments against other competitive approaches.

The clarity of the writing and the logical flow of the arguments make the paper easy to follow and the contributions easy to appreciate.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended as constructive feedback and are not critical to the decision.
*   The distinction between sensory input `s_t` and measurements `m_t` is noted as "somewhat artificial." The paper could be strengthened by a brief discussion on the principles for selecting these measurements. For example, how would the system perform if a crucial aspect of the state (e.g., ammunition) was omitted from `m_t` but was still visually discernible in `s_t`? A discussion on the sensitivity to this design choice would be informative.
*   The paper relates the approach to Monte Carlo methods. This connection could be made more explicit. The learning target `f_i` (the observed future measurement differences) is effectively a Monte Carlo sample of the expected outcome. Framing the work as a goal-conditioned, multi-variate generalization of Monte Carlo policy evaluation for control could further clarify its theoretical grounding.
*   The choice of temporal offsets (`τ`) and their weighting in the objective function seems somewhat heuristic. While the appendix provides details, a short justification or a sensitivity analysis regarding these hyperparameters in the main text would add to the paper's completeness.

### 5. Questions for the Authors
*   In the comparison experiments (Section 4.2, Scenarios D3 & D4), the reward for the baseline RL agents was constructed as a linear combination of the changes in the same measurements used by your DFP agent, using the same weights. How sensitive were the baselines' performances to this specific reward shaping? Is it possible that a different reward formulation could have closed the performance gap?
*   The architecture incorporates a "dueling" style split into an expectation stream and an action stream. Given the simplicity and directness of the overall DFP approach, have you performed an ablation to quantify the performance gain from this specific architectural component compared to a simpler network that directly predicts the per-action outcomes from the joint representation `j`?
*   Table 3 demonstrates that goal-agnostic training leads to highly flexible agents. Was there any observed cost to this flexibility, for instance, in terms of sample efficiency? Did the randomly-trained models require more training steps to reach the same level of performance on the primary task compared to the model trained with a fixed goal?
