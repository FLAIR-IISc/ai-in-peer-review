### 1. Summary

This paper proposes a novel approach to sensorimotor control, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), which reframes the problem from one of reinforcement learning to supervised learning. Instead of learning a policy or value function from a scalar reward signal, the agent learns to predict the future values of a multichannel "measurement" stream (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input, current measurements, a potential action, and a goal vector. The goal is defined as a linear combination of these future measurements. By training this predictive model on its own experience, the agent can, at test time, simply select the action that is predicted to yield the most desirable future measurements according to its current goal.

The authors evaluate this approach in the challenging 3D environment of the game Doom. The experimental results are exceptionally strong, demonstrating that this simpler, supervised approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep reinforcement learning methods like A3C and DQN, especially on complex combat scenarios. The paper further shows that the learned models can generalize to new, unseen environments and can be trained in a "goal-agnostic" manner to flexibly pursue different objectives at test time. The strength of the approach is further validated by winning the Visual Doom AI Competition, outperforming a more complex, specially-supervised competitor.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Effectiveness and Simplicity:** The paper presents a simple and elegant method that is surprisingly effective. By reformulating the control problem as a supervised prediction task, it bypasses many of the complexities and instabilities associated with Bellman-based updates in deep RL. The strength of the results challenges the prevailing view that value-function-based RL is the default approach for such tasks.
2.  **Rigorous and Compelling Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare against strong, well-tuned baselines across a suite of tasks with increasing difficulty. The massive performance gains over A3C are significant. Crucially, the paper includes extensive generalization studies (across goals and environments) and an insightful ablation study that validates the core claims about the benefits of vectorial, multi-step predictions. The victory in an open competition serves as a powerful external validation of the method's robustness and state-of-the-art performance.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution is the demonstration that a direct, supervised prediction of future measurements is a powerful and viable alternative to traditional reinforcement learning for complex visuomotor control. The framing of this idea is well-motivated by early work in the field and is clearly positioned with respect to more recent work on universal value functions and predictive representations.

The empirical results are the main strength of this work.
*   **Performance:** In the simpler scenarios (D1, D2), DFP is competitive with or better than A3C. In the more complex combat scenarios (D3, D4), DFP is not just incrementally better; it outperforms the state-of-the-art A3C by a factor of 2.5-6x in terms of frags (Table 1). This is a substantial leap in performance.
*   **Generalization:** The experiments in Table 2 demonstrate impressive generalization to unseen environments (when trained on varied textures), a critical capability for real-world application. Furthermore, the goal-agnostic training experiments (Table 3) show that the agent can learn a general-purpose predictive model of the world and then use it to pursue novel goals (e.g., "pacifist") that it was not explicitly trained for. This flexibility is a significant advantage over methods that bake a fixed reward function into the policy.
*   **Ablation Study:** The ablation in Table 4 provides direct evidence for the paper's central claims. It clearly shows that using a vector of measurements is superior to a single scalar (even when the evaluation metric is that same scalar), and that predicting at multiple future time steps is also beneficial. This grounds the architectural and methodological choices in empirical evidence.

The clarity of the method and the writing is also commendable. The approach is described precisely, and the experimental setup is detailed, which should aid reproducibility.

### 4. Additional Feedback

While this is a strong paper, the following points could be addressed to further improve it. These are suggestions for refinement and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Relationship to Model-Based RL:** The paper frames the approach as a contrast between supervised learning (SL) and reinforcement learning (RL). However, DFP bears a strong resemblance to model-based RL, where the agent learns a model of the environment's dynamics (`s_t, a_t -> s_t+1`) and then uses it for planning. DFP could be interpreted as learning a partial, goal-relevant model that only predicts future measurements (`s_t, m_t, a_t -> m_t+τ`). A more detailed discussion of this connection would be valuable. Does the authors' view of SL vs. RL offer advantages over the model-free vs. model-based dichotomy?
*   **Dependence on Measurement Quality:** The success of the method seems to hinge on the availability of a well-chosen set of measurements `m_t` that are relevant to any potential goals. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the method's limitations. What happens if the measurements are noisy, high-dimensional, or do not fully capture the task-relevant aspects of the state? For instance, if "killing an enemy" only provided a frag count but no information about enemy locations or types, the agent might struggle.
*   **Exploration:** The paper states that an ε-greedy policy is used for exploration. In standard RL, exploration is often driven by uncertainty estimation or intrinsic motivation to ensure the agent learns about the consequences of all actions in all states. In DFP, exploration seems aimed only at collecting a diverse dataset to train the predictor `F`. Is this sufficient to avoid situations where the predictor is inaccurate for certain actions (because they were rarely tried), leading the agent to never select them? A brief discussion on the interplay between the prediction objective and the exploration strategy would be insightful.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points in their rebuttal:

1.  Could you elaborate on the relationship between DFP and model-based RL? Specifically, do you see DFP as a type of model-based approach, and if so, what are the key distinctions from traditional work in that area?
2.  How do you envision this approach being applied to domains where a low-dimensional, semantically meaningful measurement vector `m_t` is not readily available (e.g., a real-world robot learning to tidy a room)? Could the measurements themselves be learned from the raw sensory stream `s_t`?
3.  The goal-agnostic training protocol seems related to meta-RL, where an agent is trained on a distribution of tasks to enable fast adaptation. Could you comment on the connections and differences between your approach and the meta-RL framework?
