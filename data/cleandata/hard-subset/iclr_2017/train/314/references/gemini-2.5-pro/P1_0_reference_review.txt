### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a novel approach to sensorimotor control that frames the problem as supervised learning rather than traditional reinforcement learning. The core idea is to train a deep network to directly predict the future values of a vector of measurements (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on the current sensory input (raw pixels), current measurements, a specified goal, and a potential action. Instead of learning a value function or policy from a scalar reward, the agent learns a predictive model of its own state evolution. At test time, the agent selects the action that is predicted to yield the best outcome according to its current goal.

The key claimed contributions are:
1.  A method that leverages rich, temporally dense, multidimensional measurement streams as a powerful supervisory signal, overcoming the challenges of sparse rewards in traditional RL.
2.  A framework that naturally handles dynamically specified goals. The model is trained to be goal-conditioned, allowing it to pursue different objectives at test time without retraining.
3.  State-of-the-art empirical results in complex, 3D first-person environments (ViZDoom). The proposed DFP model is shown to significantly outperform strong deep RL baselines like A3C and DQN, particularly on more challenging tasks.
4.  Demonstrated generalization capabilities, both to unseen environment textures and to novel goals not encountered during training. The success of the model in the Visual Doom AI Competition further validates its robustness.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper with a clear and compelling contribution. My recommendation is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Elegant and Effective Formulation:** The paper presents a simple yet powerful alternative to standard RL for a significant class of problems. By reframing control as a supervised prediction problem, it bypasses many of the difficulties associated with value function estimation (e.g., credit assignment, bootstrapping errors) and instead learns from a much denser and more informative signal. This is a well-motivated idea that is executed effectively.
2.  **Exceptional Empirical Support:** The claims are backed by extensive and convincing experiments. The performance gap between DFP and state-of-the-art RL methods like A3C on the more complex "Battle" scenarios is massive (e.g., a 6x improvement in frags on D3). The paper goes beyond standard benchmarks with thorough analyses of generalization across environments and goals, and a well-designed ablation study that validates the core components of the approach. The victory in an official competition on unseen maps is a particularly strong piece of evidence.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's strength lies in its effective blend of a simple, intuitive idea with rigorous empirical validation.

**On the method's novelty and positioning:** The authors do an excellent job of placing their work in the historical context of the SL vs. RL debate (Jordan & Rumelhart, 1992). While the idea of using supervised learning for control is not new, this paper successfully modernizes it with deep learning architectures and applies it to a complex, high-dimensional visuomotor control setting where modern RL methods are considered the standard. The formulation is a specific, well-realized instantiation of more general ideas like Universal Value Function Approximators (Schaul et al., 2015), but its focus on predicting raw measurement differences makes it distinct and practical. The architectural choice to adapt a "dueling" style network for predicting action-specific outcomes is also a clever and well-justified detail.

**On the strength of the experiments:** The experimental section is a model of thoroughness.
*   **Baselines:** The comparison against DQN and especially A3C is crucial, as A3C is a very strong and widely used baseline. The authors report making a good-faith effort to tune the baselines, which lends credibility to the reported performance gap.
*   **Task Complexity:** The use of four scenarios with increasing difficulty clearly demonstrates where DFP's advantages become most pronounced—in the more complex "Battle" scenarios with richer measurement streams.
*   **Ablation Study:** The ablation study in Table 4 is critical. It convincingly shows that both key components of the proposal—using vectorial feedback (multiple measurements) and predicting at multiple future time steps—are essential for achieving the reported performance. This directly supports the central thesis that a dense, multivariate signal is superior to a scalar reward in this setting.
*   **Generalization:** The experiments on generalization to new textures and new goals are excellent. The ability to train a single "goal-agnostic" agent that performs nearly as well as a specialized one and generalizes far better (Table 3) is a significant practical advantage of this framework.
*   **Competition Win:** External validation via a competition win is one of the strongest possible forms of empirical support, as it provides an unbiased comparison against other state-of-the-art approaches in a challenging, zero-shot generalization setting.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While this is an excellent paper, the following points could help further strengthen it.

*   **Defining "Measurements":** The paper rightly admits the distinction between sensory input `s_t` and measurements `m_t` is "somewhat artificial." The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion on what constitutes a good measurement stream. Are they simply non-visual, low-dimensional game state variables? Are there principles for designing them? What happens if a crucial aspect of the state is not included in `m_t`? A discussion of these limitations would provide a more complete picture.
*   **Relationship to Model-Based RL:** The approach involves predicting future outcomes, which is conceptually related to model-based RL. The authors position it as distinct because it predicts low-dimensional measurements rather than the full sensory state. However, it could be viewed as learning a compact, task-relevant "world model" focused only on the measurement dynamics. A more explicit discussion of this relationship—highlighting DFP as perhaps a more practical and tractable form of model-based control—would be insightful.
*   **Clarity on Temporal Offsets:** The choice of temporal offsets (`τ_i`) and their weighting in the loss function feels somewhat ad-hoc. While the appendix provides details, a brief justification for this specific choice (e.g., a logarithmic scale to capture both immediate and long-term consequences) in the main text would be helpful. The ablation study shows that multiple offsets are better than one, but it's unclear how sensitive the method is to the specific values chosen.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

1.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the choice of temporal offsets `τ_i`? For instance, would a different set of offsets, or a denser/sparser set, significantly alter the results?
2.  The paper focuses on environments where meaningful measurements are readily available. How do you foresee this approach working in tasks with very sparse feedback, where the only available "measurement" is a game score that changes infrequently? Would the model effectively reduce to a Monte-Carlo policy gradient method in that scenario, and would you expect it to perform worse than TD-based methods like A3C?
3.  Regarding training stability: one of the major challenges in deep RL is the high variance and instability of the learning process. Did you observe any differences in training stability (e.g., variance across runs, sensitivity to random seeds or hyperparameters) between DFP and the RL baselines? Intuitively, a supervised regression objective should be more stable.
4.  In the goal-agnostic training experiments (Table 3), the agent trained with a fixed goal (0.5, 0.5, 1) performs poorly on the "pacifist" goal (-1, 1, 1). This is expected. However, it also does poorly on the "maximize health" goal (0, 1, 0), even though health was a component of its original training goal. Do you have any intuition for why it fails to isolate and maximize this component?
