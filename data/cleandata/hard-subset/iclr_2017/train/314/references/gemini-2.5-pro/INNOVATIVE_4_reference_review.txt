### Summary

This paper presents a novel and compelling approach to sensorimotor control, reframing the problem from one of reinforcement learning (RL) to one of supervised learning (SL). The core idea, termed Direct Future Prediction (DFP), is to train an agent to predict a vector of future "measurements" (e.g., health, ammunition) conditioned on its current sensory input, a potential action, and a parameterized goal. At test time, the agent simply selects the action predicted to yield the best outcome according to its current goal. This elegant formulation moves away from the conventional RL paradigm of value functions and policy gradients, instead leveraging the rich, temporally dense supervisory signal provided by the environment's intrinsic measurements. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach in the challenging 3D domain of Doom, showing that DFP not only significantly outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL methods like A3C but also enables remarkable generalization to unseen environments and dynamically specified goals.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Strong Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The paper makes a bold departure from the dominant deep RL paradigm. By reformulating control as a direct prediction problem, it provides a refreshingly simple yet powerful alternative to complex value-based or policy-gradient methods. This work challenges the community to reconsider the foundational assumptions of how to best frame learning for agents in environments with rich feedback.
2.  **Overwhelmingly Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evidence is exceptional. The DFP agent doesn't just incrementally improve upon baselines; it dominates them by a large margin (e.g., 6x more frags than A3C) in the most complex scenarios. The victory in the Visual Doom AI Competition on unseen maps provides a powerful, real-world validation of the method's robustness and generalization capabilities, outperforming a more complex, specially-supervised competitor.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this paper is its compelling central thesis: for many complex, interactive tasks, the traditional RL formulation of a sparse, scalar reward is an unnecessary and perhaps detrimental simplification. The real world provides a rich, multi-faceted stream of feedback, and this paper shows how to exploit that directly.

The approach is grounded in early ideas about SL for control but modernizes them with deep networks and demonstrates their power on a scale that was previously infeasible. The choice to predict a low-dimensional measurement vector, rather than the full future sensory stream, is a pragmatic and effective decision that makes the supervised learning task tractable while retaining the information necessary for control.

The experiments are thorough and convincingly support the paper's claims.
*   **Performance:** The performance gap between DFP and SOTA methods like A3C in the "Battle" scenarios is striking. This suggests that as task complexity and the richness of available feedback increase, the advantage of DFP's dense, multi-channel supervision becomes more pronounced.
*   **Generalization:** The goal-agnostic training experiments are particularly impressive. The ability to train a single agent that can later adopt a wide range of goals (including adversarial ones like "pacifist" or "drain ammo") without significant performance loss on the main task is a significant step towards flexible and general-purpose agents. This is a clear advantage over standard RL agents that are typically locked into optimizing a single, fixed reward function.
*   **Ablation Study:** The ablation study provides clean, conclusive evidence for the core hypotheses. It clearly demonstrates that both the vectorial nature of the feedback and the prediction of multiple future time steps are critical to the model's success. This vindicates the decision to move beyond a simple scalar reward.

While the model architecture itself is relatively standard, incorporating ideas like the dueling network structure, its successful application within this new framework is a valuable contribution. The simplicity of the overall approach, combined with its state-of-the-art performance, is a testament to the power of the underlying idea.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **The "Measurement Oracle":** The paper's main assumption is the availability of a meaningful, low-dimensional measurement vector `m_t`. This is readily available in video games but may not be in other domains like real-world robotics. I would be interested in the authors' thoughts on the applicability of this method in settings where such measurements are not provided. Could an auxiliary model be trained to *learn* a salient measurement vector from raw sensations, perhaps through self-supervision? How sensitive is the method to the specific choice of measurements?

*   **Relationship to Model-Based RL:** The approach has a strong flavor of model-based RL, but it learns a "model" of future measurements rather than a full state-transition model. This seems to be a key advantage, as predicting measurements is a much lower-dimensional and better-specified problem than predicting future image frames. It would be beneficial to explicitly discuss this connection and position DFP within the model-based vs. model-free spectrum. Is the primary advantage of DFP over traditional model-based approaches simply the tractability of the prediction task?

*   **Long-Term Planning:** The current model is purely reactive and predicts up to 32 steps into the future. How does this approach scale to tasks requiring much longer-term credit assignment, where the key measurements are only affected hundreds of steps after an action? Does the method implicitly rely on the measurement stream providing a continuous, shaped signal that correlates with long-term success? It would be interesting to see an analysis of a task where the link between action and measurement change is significantly delayed.
