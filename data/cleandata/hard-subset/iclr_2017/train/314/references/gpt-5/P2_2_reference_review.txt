1) Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning formulation of sensorimotor control. Instead of learning via scalar rewards, the agent predicts vectors of future intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) at multiple temporal offsets, conditioned on the current observation and a goal vector. Actions are chosen by maximizing the predicted goal-weighted utility. This reframes action selection as supervised multistep prediction, avoids bootstrapping targets, and supports goal-parameterization at test time. The authors introduce a simple architecture with perception, measurement, and goal modules, and a dueling-style prediction head (expectation + action-specific deviation). Experiments in 3D Doom tasks show:
- Strong empirical performance vs. DQN, A3C, and DSR, especially on harder scenarios with richer measurement streams.
- Generalization across textures/environments and to unseen goals when trained with randomized goals.
- Ablations indicating benefits from vector-valued measurements and multi-horizon prediction.
- A competition win in the Visual Doom Full Deathmatch track.

Overall, the paper claims that dense, multivariate measurement prediction is a powerful alternative to reward-centric RL, enabling stable training from raw experience and flexible goal pursuit.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Clear, simple, and well-motivated idea that is distinct from mainstream TD/policy-gradient RL and addresses sparse/bootstrapping issues by leveraging dense measurement streams.
- Strong empirical evidence: consistent and sizable gains over strong baselines on challenging 3D tasks, plus compelling generalization to new environments and goals.

3) Supporting arguments
Novelty and significance
- The core idea—predicting a vector of future intrinsic measurements for all actions, with goal-parameterized utilities—offers a pragmatic and conceptually clean alternative to value-based or policy-gradient methods. While related to UVFA and predictive representations, the direct multi-horizon measurement prediction and its use for action selection is a distinct, practical instantiation.
- Goal-conditioning and randomized-goal training enabling test-time goal changes is a valuable capability not commonly demonstrated with comparable breadth in 3D settings.

Technical soundness and correctness
- Training is straightforward supervised regression with multistep Monte Carlo targets, avoiding instability from bootstrapping. The dueling-style head is sensible and helps distinguish action-conditioned effects.
- The approach is well aligned with settings where dense, meaningful measurements are available and goals can be expressed as linear utilities over future measurement deltas.

Empirical evaluation
- Four scenarios of increasing difficulty; 50M steps for DQN/A3C; averages over three seeds; long test runs (1M steps/model). Results show parity on simple tasks and large gains (e.g., 6x frags in D3, 2.5x in D4) in difficult settings.
- Generalization experiments across textures/environments are thoughtfully designed (train/test texture splits; multiple regimes), showing that exposure to diversity plus higher capacity improves transfer.
- Goal-agnostic training shows that models trained with randomized goals approach the performance of goal-specific training on the main task and generalize better to new goals.
- Ablations support core claims: vector-valued measurements and multi-horizon prediction both improve performance.

Positioning and related work
- The paper situates itself well relative to TD/Monte Carlo debates, multi-objective RL, UVFA, predictive representations, and recent 3D visuomotor RL. The empirical comparisons to DQN/A3C are appropriate; DSR inclusion is limited by its computational cost but still informative.

4) Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not central to the decision)
- Fairness and clarity of baselines:
  - Explicitly state whether A3C/DQN receive the same observation stream (images plus measurements) as inputs. If they only receive images and a scalar reward, this is a potential confound; if they also receive measurements, say so clearly.
  - Report standard deviations or confidence intervals for the main results and ablations to quantify variability.
  - Expand on hyperparameter search breadth for A3C/DQN on the Doom tasks and include any sensitivity analyses.
- Ablations/extensions:
  - Include an ablation where DFP is trained to predict only a scalar reward target (keeping all else equal) while A3C/DQN receive the full measurement vector as input, isolating the benefit of multivariate prediction vs. input richness.
  - Show results with recurrence (e.g., LSTM) to address partial observability; even a brief appendix experiment would be valuable.
  - Provide training curves with exploration schedules and learning curves across seeds to further assess stability.
- Practical considerations:
  - Discuss computational cost per step and wall-clock efficiency vs. A3C more explicitly, given the claim of faster/more stable learning via dense supervision.
  - Clarify how action combinatorics (sub-action combinations) are handled in practice (e.g., total action count in D3/D4, any pruning).
- Broader scope/limitations:
  - More candid discussion of settings where meaningful measurement vectors are unavailable or expensive to define, and how one might learn suitable measurement features (e.g., via auxiliary tasks).

Questions for the authors
- Do the DQN/A3C baselines receive the same measurement vectors as part of their observations, or only the image and scalar reward? If not, can you provide results where they do, to equalize input information?
- How sensitive is DFP to the choice of temporal offsets and their aggregation weights? Have you tried learned weighting or discount-like schemes?
- How does performance change if the goal is non-linear in f (e.g., learned utility networks) rather than linear?
- What is the total number of discrete actions in D3/D4 (from sub-action combinations), and how does scaling to larger action spaces affect training stability and sample efficiency?
- Have you evaluated recurrent variants of DFP, and if so, do the gains persist under significant partial observability (e.g., longer maze corridors, occlusions)?
- Can you provide variance across seeds and statistical significance for the main tables/curves?

Overall, this paper presents a simple, well-motivated alternative to standard RL with strong empirical evidence and useful capabilities (goal parameterization, generalization). The contributions are clear and likely to be influential. I recommend acceptance.
