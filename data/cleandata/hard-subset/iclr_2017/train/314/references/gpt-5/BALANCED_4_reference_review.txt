1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning formulation of sensorimotor control that predicts goal-conditioned future “measurements” (e.g., health, ammo, frags) from raw sensory input and selects actions by maximizing the predicted goal utility. Key ideas: (i) exploit dense, multidimensional measurement streams as supervision instead of sparse scalar rewards; (ii) predict multi-step measurement deltas conditioned on candidate actions and a goal vector; (iii) at test time, combine predicted futures with a goal to choose actions, enabling dynamic goal specification and goal generalization. The approach uses a simple deep architecture with a perception module and a dueling-style predictor (expectation and action streams). On multiple Doom-based 3D control tasks, DFP trains efficiently and outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C), particularly on the harder combat scenarios, shows generalization across textures/environments and across goals, and won the Visual Doom AI Competition’s Full Deathmatch track.

2) Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Strong, consistent empirical performance and compelling simplicity: DFP substantially outperforms well-tuned A3C on the more complex tasks, generalizes to new environments and goal vectors, and trains fast and stably from raw pixels and intrinsic measurements.
- Clear, well-motivated formulation that connects to GVFs/UVFAs and multi-objective RL, but makes a crisp, practical choice (predict measurement vectors directly) that enables dynamic goal pursuit and effective learning from dense signals.

3) Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? Learning visuomotor control in complex 3D environments with potentially changing goals, from raw sensory streams and intrinsic measurements, while avoiding sparse-reward difficulties and enabling test-time goal specification.

Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The paper situates DFP among Monte Carlo methods, predictive representations (GVFs), multi-objective RL, universal value function approximators, and recent deep RL for 3D control. The departure from scalar rewards to dense, vectorial measurements is well argued, and the connection to goal-conditioning is natural. The architectural choice (expectation/action split) builds on dueling networks.

Do the results support the claims? Largely yes.
- Benchmarks: Four ViZDoom scenarios ranging from navigation to combat; many frames of experience; comparisons to DQN, A3C (with hyperparameter search), and DSR where feasible.
- Performance: DFP matches A3C on easy tasks and strongly outperforms on harder ones (notably 6× in Battle and 2.5× in Battle 2 in terms of frags), suggesting the benefit of dense, multidimensional supervision and goal conditioning.
- Generalization: Training across randomized textures leads to robust transfer to unseen environments; goal-agnostic training produces models that nearly match single-goal training on the main goal and generalize substantially better to new goals.
- Ablations: Predicting multiple measurements and multiple horizons improves performance even when evaluated on a single target metric. This supports the core hypothesis.
- External validation: Winning the Visual Doom AI Competition against strong baselines is a notable empirical endorsement.

Caveats: The evaluation is domain-limited (Doom), uses discrete actions, and lacks memory for partial observability. Some reporting details (confidence intervals, broader baselines) could be strengthened. The theoretical underpinnings are qualitative rather than formal.

4) Additional feedback and questions (to help improve the paper; not all impact my decision)
Clarifications and analyses requested:
- Baseline fairness and tuning: Please provide variance estimates (e.g., mean ± std across seeds) and statistical tests for the main comparisons. How many seeds per method? Clarify compute budgets per method. A3C performance can be sensitive; can you report learning curves for additional seeds and the final hyperparameters used?
- Relation to UVFA/GVFs and successor representations: The method effectively learns a vector of cumulants (future measurement deltas). Can you formalize when DFP is equivalent to learning a set of GVFs and why this might be advantageous empirically versus learning a single goal-conditioned value? Include a direct UVFA baseline if feasible.
- Delayed rewards and sparsity: How does DFP perform when relevant measurements are very sparse or delayed (e.g., only terminal events)? Any experiments where the goal depends on outcomes beyond 32 steps or is only revealed late?
- Off-policy aspects: You use a small replay memory and ε-greedy exploration. Did you observe any instability from the off-policy nature? How sensitive is performance to replay size, refresh frequency, and ε schedule?
- Architectural ablations: You adopt an expectation/action split akin to dueling networks. Please include an ablation comparing to a single-head predictor emitting per-action futures without the split, and quantify the contribution of the normalization layer.
- Memory and partial observability: Since Doom is partially observable, did you try frame stacking or recurrent variants of DFP? How does adding a simple LSTM affect performance?
- Measurements and practicality: In many real systems, only a subset of useful “measurements” may be available. How robust is DFP when some measurements are missing/noisy, or when you must learn useful measurement features from sensory data (e.g., learned cumulants)?
- Goal distribution: For randomized-goal training, please specify the sampling distributions and ranges, and analyze sensitivity. How does training with broader goal distributions trade off specialization vs generalization?
- Continuous actions and broader benchmarks: Any preliminary results on continuous control (e.g., MuJoCo/TORCS) or other 3D worlds (e.g., Habitat, Gibson)?
- Reporting: Please add confidence intervals in Tables 1–4 and in learning curves, and provide the exact architecture and optimization hyperparameters in the main text or a more accessible appendix. Some references appear incorrectly formatted; please clean up.

Overall, this is a clear, impactful paper. It puts forward a simple idea—predict dense, vectorial futures and choose actions by goal-conditioned evaluation—and shows it can outperform sophisticated RL on challenging 3D tasks while enabling goal generalization. With stronger statistical reporting, additional ablations (especially the dueling-style split), and discussion of limitations (sparse signals, memory, continuous actions), the work would be even more compelling. I recommend acceptance.
