1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning-based alternative to standard reward-driven reinforcement learning for sensorimotor control with raw sensory input. Instead of optimizing a scalar reward via Bellman-based targets, the agent predicts a vector of future “measurements” (e.g., health, ammo, frags) at multiple temporal offsets, conditioned on current observation, action, and a goal vector. Actions are chosen by maximizing the inner product between the predicted future measurements and a goal vector, enabling goal-parameterized control at test time. The approach uses a deep network with three input modules (perception, measurement, goal) and a prediction head split into an “expectation” stream and an “action” stream (akin to dueling architectures) to produce action-conditional predictions.

Empirically, on several ViZDoom scenarios of increasing difficulty, DFP trains from raw experience and outperforms deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C; DSR on simple scenarios), with especially large gains on the more complex tasks with richer measurement streams. The paper shows generalization across environment textures, adaptation to novel goals after goal-agnostic training, and ablations demonstrating benefits of vector-valued measurements and multi-horizon prediction. A variant of the method won the Visual Doom AI Competition (Full Deathmatch), outperforming a strong recurrent DQN baseline with additional supervision.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Strong empirical results with a simple, well-executed idea: recasting control as supervised multi-step prediction over rich measurement streams yields robust performance and generalization, surpassing widely used deep RL baselines on challenging 3D tasks.
- Sound and clear formulation that is well-motivated by dense feedback settings and connects to Monte Carlo prediction, universal value function approximators, and successor representations, while offering a practical, scalable architecture.

3. Supporting arguments
Clarity and positioning:
- The problem is clearly stated and the core idea is explained succinctly. The motivation—leveraging dense, multidimensional feedback instead of sparse scalar rewards—is compelling and well grounded in prior work (SL vs TD/MC, UVFA, multi-objective RL).
- The relation to UVFA and successor representations is acknowledged; the contribution lies in a concrete, goal-parameterized, action-conditional predictor trained with Monte Carlo targets and validated at scale in 3D visually rich tasks.

Technical soundness:
- Training targets are unbiased Monte Carlo estimates of future measurement differences for executed actions; choosing actions by argmax over g^T predictions is straightforward. The off-policy experience memory is acceptable for supervised regression to actual outcomes.
- The architectural choice (dueling-like expectation/action streams) for per-action predictions is sensible and empirically motivated.
- The multi-horizon prediction and vectorial measurement formulation are coherent and consistent with the claims.

Empirical support and rigor:
- Comparisons to DQN and A3C across multiple scenarios, three seeds, and large test budgets support the main claims. Ablations isolate the value of multiple measurements and multi-step targets. Generalization experiments across textures and goals strengthen the case that DFP learns more transferable knowledge.
- Winning the Visual Doom competition further validates practical effectiveness.

Caveats and limitations:
- Baseline coverage could be stronger. A3C variants with recurrence or auxiliary tasks (e.g., UNREAL-style) and deep successor feature methods at scale would provide a more stringent comparison. The paper acknowledges DSR speed limitations.
- While the method excels with dense, informative measurements, it is less clear how it would fare in domains where only sparse rewards exist or where salient measurements are not readily available.
- The role of goal conditioning inside the predictor is under-specified: targets do not depend on the goal, so the primary impact of randomized goals during training seems to be via the behavior policy and data distribution rather than through an explicit conditioning signal in the loss.

Overall, the paper is technically sound, clearly written, and delivers substantive empirical advances in a relevant domain.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not decisive)
- Clarify how the goal vector g is actually used during training. As written, F is trained to predict f, which is goal-independent, while g is provided as an input. Please explain whether g influences the predictor only indirectly via behavior policy (different data distributions per episode) or whether there are architectural/training mechanisms that leverage g directly (e.g., predicting u = g^T f, or gating outputs by g).
- Expand baseline comparisons if possible: (a) A3C-LSTM on D3/D4; (b) an auxiliary-task A3C (pixel control/feature control) to match the multi-signal advantages of DFP; (c) a deep successor-feature baseline on at least one mid-scale scenario to better contextualize the benefit of direct prediction.
- Report sensitivity analyses: experience memory size M, ε schedule, optimizer and learning rate(s), and the coefficients for multi-horizon losses. Include standard deviations or confidence intervals for final metrics.
- Provide an ablation of the expectation/action stream split to quantify its contribution.
- Discuss limits: behavior with sparser/less informative measurements, and whether learned measurement heads can be auxiliary predictions when explicit measurements are unavailable.
- Reference formatting needs cleanup (years, venues, duplicates).

Questions for the authors
- Training targets and goal conditioning: Do you ever train F to predict u = g^T f (scalar) in addition to or instead of f? If not, what mechanism encourages F to use g at all, given that f is goal-independent? Can you show an ablation of removing g from the predictor inputs?
- Data distribution: In the “randomized goals” regime, is the performance gain primarily due to diverse behavior policies induced by different goals? Can you quantify how training with multiple goals changes state-action coverage?
- Action supervision: The network outputs predictions for all actions; is the loss applied only to the executed action’s slice, or is there any use of counterfactual supervision (e.g., model-based rollouts, consistency losses)?
- Multi-horizon targets: How were the horizon offsets and the (0.5, 0.5, 1) weights chosen? How sensitive is performance to these choices?
- Generalization: In the texture-randomized settings, besides textures, were other factors (layout, spawn patterns) varied? Could you report performance with stronger domain randomization?
- Baselines: Did you evaluate A3C with an LSTM on D3/D4, and if so, what were the results? If not, what prevented a fair recurrent baseline?
- Measurement choice: How critical is the choice of measurements? Have you tried including additional measurements (e.g., velocity) or learned feature measurements as auxiliary prediction targets?
