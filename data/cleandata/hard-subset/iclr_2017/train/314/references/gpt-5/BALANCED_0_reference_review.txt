1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a goal-conditioned, supervised-learning formulation of sensorimotor control. Instead of optimizing a scalar reward via Bellman-based RL, the agent predicts action-conditional future changes of a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) at multiple time offsets, and selects the action that maximizes a goal-defined utility (typically a linear combination of predicted measurement changes). Key elements include:
- Casting control as supervised regression on dense, multivariate measurement streams, learned from raw sensory input without imitation or extraneous labels.
- Conditioning predictions on a goal vector, enabling training with varying goals and test-time goal changes.
- A simple network architecture with perception, measurement, and goal modules, and a prediction head split into expectation and action streams (similar in spirit to dueling networks).
- Strong empirical results on ViZDoom across four increasingly complex scenarios, demonstrating faster learning and higher final performance than DQN and A3C, particularly in complex settings and when multiple measurements are available.
- Generalization to unseen environments (randomized textures) and to novel goals, plus an ablation showing the benefits of multivariate, multi-horizon prediction.
- A win in the Visual Doom AI Competition (Full Deathmatch), outperforming a strong, more complex baseline trained with additional supervision.

2. Decision
Accept.
Key reasons: (1) A clean and well-motivated formulation that leverages dense measurement streams for goal-conditioned control, with practical simplicity and strong empirical benefits; (2) Convincing evidence of improved performance and generalization over strong deep RL baselines on challenging 3D visuomotor tasks.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: Learning sensorimotor control from raw pixels in dynamic 3D environments, with the ability to pursue different goals without retraining. This is an important and timely challenge.
- Motivation and placement: The work is well positioned relative to Monte Carlo methods, generalized value functions, UVFA, and successor representations. The central idea—predicting a vector of future measurement changes conditioned on goals—is a natural and compelling instantiation of goal-conditioned prediction with dense feedback. The argument that densely supervised measurement prediction can outperform TD-style updates in these settings is plausible and supported empirically.
- Evidence supporting claims:
  - Across Basic, Navigation, Battle, and Battle 2, DFP matches or exceeds DQN/A3C, with particularly large gains in the two more complex scenarios that expose richer measurement vectors. Training speeds are competitive, leveraging fast simulation.
  - Generalization results show meaningful transfer across texture-randomized environments, with further gains from training on diversity and with a larger-capacity model.
  - Goal-agnostic training achieves near-parity with fixed-goal training on the main task and superior adaptation to new goals, supporting the utility of goal conditioning.
  - Ablations indicate that multivariate measurements and multi-horizon prediction improve performance even when evaluation focuses on a single metric, aligning with the intuition that dense, structured supervision is beneficial.
- Rigor and correctness: The methodology is clear, the architecture is reasonable, and experimental procedures are described. Baselines are implemented with care and some hyperparameter search. Results are averaged over seeds and tested extensively. While theoretical analysis is limited, the empirical claims are well supported for the tested domains.

Caveats:
- Some baseline comparisons could be stronger. A3C is a fair reference, but comparisons to UVFA-like goal-conditioned RL or successor representations under comparable training budgets would better isolate the benefit of vector prediction versus scalar reward. The DSR comparison is limited by training speed and only reported on the simplest task.
- The approach’s success relies on the availability and choice of informative measurements; applicability to domains where meaningful measurement vectors are not readily available is not explored.
- No memory/partial observability handling is included (acknowledged by authors), which may limit performance in tasks requiring long-term information integration.
- Statistical reporting (error bars, significance) and architectural ablations (e.g., removing the expectation/action split) could be expanded.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Strengthen baselines: Add a UVFA-style goal-conditioned RL baseline and a successor-representation baseline trained to comparable step budgets on the harder tasks. Consider A3C with auxiliary prediction tasks (e.g., UNREAL-style) to probe whether auxiliary dense supervision narrows the gap.
- Architecture ablations: Report the effect of (i) removing the expectation/action split and normalization; (ii) different choices/weights of temporal offsets; (iii) horizon counts; and (iv) training with only the scalar reward vs the full vector under otherwise identical conditions.
- Calibration and analysis: Plot predicted vs realized measurement deltas per action to assess calibration and bias. Analyze how prediction error correlates with returns. Quantify sensitivity to measurement scaling and goal vector normalization.
- Exploration and off-policy aspects: Discuss the impact of ε-greedy, replay size M, and update lag k on stability and performance. Consider prioritized sampling for rare outcomes.
- Partial observability: A simple memory (LSTM) or frame stacking baseline would clarify how much headroom is left due to the current reactive design.
- Reporting: Include more seeds with error bars and, if possible, statistical tests. Clarify action space cardinalities per scenario and ensure exact parity across baselines. Clean up references and formatting glitches.

Questions for the authors
- Action space: In D3/D4, what is the exact number of discrete actions induced by sub-action combinations? Were the same action sets used for all baselines? Any pruning of unlikely combinations?
- Measurement design: How are measurements normalized and scaled? How sensitive is performance to scaling and to the choice of which measurements are included? What happens if you restrict to the scalar reward only—does DFP still match A3C/DQN?
- Goal conditioning: How are g vectors normalized at test time? In randomized-goal training, what distributions were used and how sensitive are results to them? Do models generalize to goals outside the training distribution (e.g., sparser or negative weights)?
- Temporal offsets: Why choose offsets {1,2,4,8,16,32} and weight only the last three? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- Prediction targets: Why predict differences (mt+τ − mt) rather than cumulative sums or discounted returns per measurement? Any experiments comparing these formulations?
- Calibration: Are predicted measurement changes well calibrated? Can you provide plots or metrics correlating prediction error with realized outcomes?
- Replay and updates: What are the values of M, N, and k? Did you observe instability with larger buffers or more off-policy data?
- DSR baseline: Given the limited training budget for DSR, can you add a small-scale DFP vs DSR comparison under matched step counts to make that comparison fairer?
- Memory: Did you attempt an LSTM/GRU extension of DFP? Any preliminary results on tasks with partial observability?

Overall, this is a simple, well-motivated, and empirically strong contribution that is likely to influence practice in goal-conditioned control with rich sensory input.
