1) Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning formulation of sensorimotor control that predicts future values of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) conditioned on current observations, action, and a goal vector. Control is achieved by selecting the action that maximizes the goal-weighted prediction, enabling goal-conditional behavior and generalization to novel test-time goals. The approach uses a deep network with separate perception, measurement, and goal heads, and a dueling-style prediction module (expectation + action-specific streams). Training is Monte-Carlo-style regression to future measurement differences at multiple horizons, using an experience memory and ε-greedy exploration.

Empirically, DFP outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C; DSR on the simplest task) on four ViZDoom scenarios of increasing difficulty, with larger gains on complex tasks with multiple measurements. The method generalizes across environments with different textures and to new goal vectors, including goal-agnostic training that adapts at test time. Ablations show that multi-dimensional measurement prediction and multi-horizon targets improve performance. A DFP variant won the Visual Doom AI Competition’s Full Deathmatch track.

2) Decision
Accept.
Key reasons: (i) A clear and well-motivated reformulation of control as goal-conditional supervised prediction of intrinsic measurements, positioned against TD-based RL and UVFA/successor literature; (ii) Strong empirical results on challenging 3D visuomotor tasks, including generalization across environments and goals, supported by informative ablations.

3) Supporting arguments
Clarity: The problem, model, and training are described clearly. The distinction between sensory input and measurement streams is well articulated. The action-selection criterion and multi-horizon targets are explicit. The architecture design (expectation/action streams) is well motivated by the need to capture fine action differences.

Positioning and motivation: The paper situates DFP with respect to SL vs RL (dense vector feedback vs sparse scalar reward), Monte Carlo vs TD, UVFA (goal-parameterized value functions), successor representations, and predictive state representations. The main novelty lies in directly predicting a vector of future intrinsic measurements conditioned on goal vectors and using these predictions for control, rather than learning scalar value functions or successor features. The architectural and training choices are coherent with the motivation (dense supervisory signal, goal-conditionality, stability).

Technical soundness: The approach is simple and internally consistent: regress to (multi-horizon) future measurement differences for each action, then select actions by inner product with goal vectors. While no convergence theory is provided, the algorithm is empirically stable, and modeling choices (multi-horizon targets, action/expectation decomposition, limited replay, ε-greedy) are standard and reasonable. The relation to UVFA and SR is discussed; DFP differs by avoiding TD bootstrapping and by predicting goal-independent measurement vectors.

Empirical support: 
- Benchmarks: Across Basic, Navigation, Battle, and Battle 2, DFP matches or exceeds A3C and DQN; gains are substantial in the multi-measurement scenarios (e.g., 6x and 2.5x in D3 and D4 on frags).
- Generalization: Training with diverse textures yields robust transfer to unseen textures and environments; higher model capacity further improves transfer.
- Goal-conditionality: Models trained with randomized goals perform nearly as well as goal-specific models on the main task and generalize better to novel goals (including “pacifist” configurations).
- Ablations: Predicting multiple measurements and multiple horizons both improve performance, even when evaluation focuses on a single metric (frags).
- Competition result: The win in a held-out, multi-map setting against a strong, more complex baseline further supports the method’s effectiveness.

Rigor and fairness: The paper uses three seeds, reports long testing episodes, and tunes baselines. A3C and DQN are trained to 50M steps. DSR could not be matched on steps, which is acknowledged. Reporting could be strengthened with variance/error bars and additional ablations against closely matched UVFA/SR implementations, but the overall evidence is substantial.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Statistical reporting: Add confidence intervals/standard deviations for key tables/curves; include significance tests where appropriate.
- Baselines: Provide a closely matched UVFA baseline (same architecture and training budget) and a successor features baseline, to isolate the effect of predicting measurement vectors vs scalar values. Clarify whether A3C/DQN used exactly the same reward (linear combination) in D3/D4 (Appendix B implies yes).
- Fairness and compute: Report wall-clock training time, GPU usage, and environment steps per second for all methods. Note that DSR received fewer steps; consider framing it as an incomplete baseline.
- Architectural ablations: Quantify the contribution of the action/expectation split, the normalization layer, and the effect of varying replay size and update frequency (k, M).
- Sensitivity: Analyze sensitivity to τ values, loss weights over horizons, goal scaling/normalization, and measurement normalization.
- Partial observability: Add experiments with explicit memory (e.g., recurrent or external memory) to assess benefits on these tasks.
- Broader applicability: Discuss how to define and obtain measurement streams in real-world settings; report a domain beyond ViZDoom if feasible.
- References: Clean up reference formatting and ensure complete bibliographic details.

Questions for the authors
- What are the exact values of experience memory size M and update frequency k? How sensitive is performance to these?
- How are measurements normalized, and is the goal vector normalized at train/test time? How sensitive is action selection to goal scaling?
- What are the weights used for multi-horizon losses beyond the last three steps; are near-term horizons ignored entirely? Did you test alternative horizon sets/weightings?
- Can you report variance across seeds for Tables 1–3 and add error bars to Figure 3?
- How much does the expectation/action split contribute vs a single fully connected head predicting per-action futures?
- Could you include a UVFA baseline that predicts scalar goal-conditioned values with the same architecture and training budget? Similarly, a successor-feature baseline trained end-to-end?
- How does performance change with sparser or lower-dimensional measurement streams, and with noisy measurements?
- Did you try recurrent architectures to mitigate partial observability? If so, what was the impact?
- What is the computational cost per environment step and per training update compared to A3C/DQN?
- In off-policy settings induced by replay, did you observe distribution shift issues when goals at test time lie outside the training distribution (especially with negative weights)?
