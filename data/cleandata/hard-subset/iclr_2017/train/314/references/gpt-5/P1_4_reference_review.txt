1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning alternative to reinforcement learning for visuomotor control. Instead of estimating value functions or policies from scalar rewards, the agent predicts a vector of future measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) at multiple temporal offsets, conditioned on the current observation, a candidate action, and a goal vector that linearly combines predicted measurement changes. Actions are selected greedily by scoring g^T F(o, a, g). The architecture shares perception across actions and uses a dueling-style split into an expectation stream and an action-difference stream to better capture action-specific effects.

Key claims:
- Replacing scalar reward with dense, multivariate measurement prediction stabilizes and accelerates learning from raw experience.
- Goal-parameterization enables training without a fixed goal and test-time adaptation to new goals.
- Empirically, DFP outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) on challenging ViZDoom scenarios, especially when multiple measurements are available.
- The learned models generalize across environments (texture randomization) and goals, and won the Visual Doom AI Competition’s Full Deathmatch track.

2. Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Strong, original formulation that reduces control to supervised prediction with goal-conditioning, clearly distinct from standard deep RL and well placed relative to UVFA/GVFs.
- Compelling empirical results on challenging 3D tasks with raw vision, including generalization across environments/goals and a competition win, supported by meaningful ablations.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses learning to act from raw perception in complex 3D environments with dynamically specified goals, arguing that dense, multivariate sensory/measurement feedback is available in many embodied settings. This is a well-motivated departure from sparse scalar rewards, and the framing is thoughtful.
- Novelty and relation to literature: The method is closely related to UVFA and GVFs but differs by directly predicting future intrinsic measurements as supervised targets and using goal vectors to select actions. The dueling-style decomposition for action-conditional prediction is a neat architectural contribution.
- Empirical rigor and breadth:
  - Benchmarks: Four Doom scenarios, increasing in difficulty; additional domain-randomized variants (D3-tx, D4-tx). The tasks require navigation, perception, and combat, all from pixels.
  - Baselines: DQN, A3C, and DSR with reasonable hyperparameter tuning; same measurement-based objective for RL baselines to ensure fairness.
  - Outcomes: DFP matches or exceeds A3C in simple tasks and dramatically surpasses A3C in the more complex Battle tasks (6x in D3, 2.5x in D4). The advantage grows with richer measurement vectors, aligning with the core hypothesis. Generalization across texture-randomized maps is demonstrated, and training with goal randomization yields robust goal-agnostic models that adapt well at test time.
  - Ablations: Show that (i) vectorial measurement prediction outperforms scalar reward prediction even when evaluated on a single metric, and (ii) multi-horizon prediction improves performance over a single long-horizon target.
  - External validation: Winning the Full Deathmatch track against a strong LSTM-based DQN that uses extra supervision strengthens the empirical case.
- Clarity and completeness: The exposition is clear; the formulation, architecture, and training are well explained; appendices reportedly provide implementation details.

4. Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all items affect the decision)
- Fairness and completeness of baselines:
  - Clarify whether the A3C baseline used frame stacking and/or LSTM variants; A3C-LSTM is a strong choice for partially observable 3D tasks. If not used, consider adding or discussing.
  - DSR comparison is limited by training speed; acknowledge implications and consider lighter successor-feature baselines or UVFA-style n-step Q for a more apples-to-apples comparison.
- Policy-consistency in long-horizon predictions: F(o, a, g) predicts measurement changes up to 32 steps, but only conditions on the first action and current observation. This yields predictions under the behavior policy used during data collection, not necessarily under the test-time greedy policy. Discuss this off-policy/policy-mismatch issue more explicitly, and whether your experience memory/evolving policy mitigates it (e.g., via continual re-collection).
- Sensitivity analyses:
  - Offsets and loss weights: Report sensitivity to the choice of temporal offsets and the weighting of the last three steps; show whether longer horizons or different weightings alter performance.
  - Goal distributions: Provide performance under out-of-distribution goals (e.g., extreme or sparse weight vectors, negative weights beyond those shown) and possibly non-linear utilities to test robustness of the parametric goal-conditioning.
- Architecture and inputs:
  - Specify whether frame stacking or recurrent state was used for DFP; given partial observability, adding memory (or stacking) could strengthen both method and comparison.
  - Provide architectural/optimizer details (e.g., Adam settings, normalization of measurements, input resolution), and, if possible, release code/seeds for reproducibility.
- Scope and limitations:
  - Discuss applicability when the measurement stream is sparse/noisy or incomplete, and how to select appropriate measurements in real-world settings.
  - Note current restriction to discrete actions and potential extension to continuous control (e.g., parameterizing F over continuous a or using policy gradients atop the predictive model).

5. Questions for the authors
- How do you reconcile the dependency of long-horizon predictions on future actions with conditioning only on the current action? Are you implicitly predicting under the current behavior policy? If so, why does this not bias control at test time when the policy changes?
- Did the A3C baseline include the LSTM variant or frame stacking? If not, can you comment on expected performance differences if these were included?
- How sensitive is DFP to the choice of temporal offsets and to the loss weighting scheme over horizons? Did you try larger offsets or learned weights?
- What exact observation stack was used (single frame vs. multiple frames)? If single-frame, did you try adding short-term memory, and how did that affect results?
- How are measurements normalized and scaled during training and across scenarios? Are the goal vectors normalized or constrained?
- Can the approach handle non-linear goal functions u(f; g) at test time if trained only with linear goals? Have you experimented with richer goal parameterizations?
- Could you provide training curves for goal-agnostic models under a variety of test-time goals, to better illustrate adaptation speed and stability?
- Do you foresee challenges when measurements are partially observed, delayed, or corrupted, and how would you adapt DFP in such settings (e.g., uncertainty-aware prediction)?

Overall, this paper offers a clean, well-motivated alternative to standard deep RL with strong empirical support and practical implications for goal-conditioned control from pixels. I recommend acceptance.
