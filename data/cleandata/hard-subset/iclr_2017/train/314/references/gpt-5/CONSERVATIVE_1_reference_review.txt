Summary
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): instead of optimizing a scalar reward via TD/policy gradients, the agent predicts, in a supervised fashion, future changes in a vector of “measurements” (e.g., health, ammo, frags) at multiple temporal offsets, conditioned on the current observation, candidate action, and a goal vector that linearly weights these future changes. At test time, the agent greedily selects the action maximizing the inner product of predicted future measurements with the goal. The authors argue this turns sensorimotor control into supervised learning, yields dense supervision, enables goal-parameterized behavior, and improves stability/training speed. On several ViZDoom scenarios, DFP reportedly outperforms DQN, A3C, and DSR, generalizes across textures/environments and goals, and won the Visual Doom AI Competition.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Empirical support is not sufficiently rigorous relative to established baselines; critical comparisons appear under-tuned or not like-for-like, and the most competitive baselines (e.g., memory-augmented/auxiliary-loss RL) are missing. (2) The method’s novelty is incremental relative to prior work on predictive representations, UVFAs, and successor representations; the paper lacks theoretical grounding and ablations needed to isolate why DFP works beyond leveraging dense, designed measurements.

Supporting arguments
- Baseline fairness and coverage: 
  - A3C is used via an independent implementation and appears non-recurrent, while these Doom tasks are partially observable. DFP is also reactive, but comparisons are not like-for-like with strong A3C variants that use LSTM and/or auxiliary losses (a standard choice on 3D visual tasks).
  - DSR is excluded from harder scenarios due to compute limitations; yet claims of outperforming “state-of-the-art deep RL” rely heavily on those scenarios.
  - Hyperparameter tuning effort differs across methods. It is unclear whether exploration strategies, frame stacking, entropy regularization, and network capacity are matched across baselines.
  - Missing baselines closely aligned with the proposal: UVFA (Schaul et al., 2015) variants that condition on goals; successor representations (Kulkarni et al., 2016b) with action selection under linear reward weights; Monte Carlo policy evaluation with strong function approximators; and A3C/UNREAL-style auxiliary prediction of the same measurements used by DFP. These would disentangle whether gains stem from the learning paradigm (supervised future prediction) or from the extra dense supervisory signal.

- Dependence on privileged measurement design:
  - The approach assumes access to a vector of informative, dense measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) and that goals are linear in future deltas of these measurements. This is a strong assumption and not available in many RL settings with sparse rewards or without such “scoreboard” signals. The paper demonstrates value only in domains where such measurements exist and are carefully chosen; general claims about “sensorimotor control” feel overstated.

- Limited analysis and theoretical grounding:
  - The method is essentially Monte Carlo prediction of handpicked future features with action-conditioning; the relation to UVFA and successor representations is acknowledged but not analyzed. A formal connection (e.g., when DFP recovers optimal policies under linear reward features, bias/variance tradeoffs vs TD, impact of multi-horizon targets) would strengthen novelty and justify design choices (temporal offsets and their weights).
  - No analysis of off-policy effects due to experience replay on a goal-conditioned predictor with ε-greedy behavior.

- Methodological clarity:
  - Several design choices are ad hoc and unexplored: the set of temporal offsets, using only the last three with fixed coefficients, normalization in the action stream, experience memory size, ε schedule. Sensitivity analyses are missing.
  - The ablation study is too limited to isolate key factors. For example, comparing against RL baselines augmented with the same multivariate measurement prediction as auxiliary losses would test whether vectorial supervision (rather than the DFP objective) is the driver.

- Evaluation scope and reproducibility:
  - The evaluation is confined to ViZDoom variants. While the reported competition win is impressive, broader benchmarks (e.g., other 3D environments; tasks with sparser feedback) are needed to substantiate generality.
  - Important practical details are deferred to appendices; references contain numerous formatting issues, which undermines confidence. Code availability is not stated.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Provide like-for-like baselines: recurrent A3C; UVFA; SR with linear goal weights; and A3C with auxiliary measurement prediction identical to DFP’s targets. Match architecture capacity, frame stacking, and training budgets.
- Include sensitivity studies: temporal offset set and weights; measurement set composition; experience memory size; ε schedule; optimizer and learning rates. Report variance over many seeds.
- Analyze theoretical connections to successor features and policy evaluation with linear rewards. Clarify when greedy action selection on short-horizon predictions can approximate long-horizon returns.
- Evaluate under sparse or delayed rewards and when measurements are noisy/incomplete to understand limits.
- Test generalization to truly unseen goals far outside the training distribution, and quantify interpolation vs extrapolation performance.
- Improve clarity and references; fix citation errors; release code and configs.

Questions for the authors
1. How were frame stacking and recurrence handled across all methods? Did any baseline use LSTM or memory, and if not, why not?
2. Can you provide results for A3C/UVFA/SR variants that condition on goals and/or predict the same measurements as auxiliary tasks? How do these compare to DFP under matched capacity and training?
3. How sensitive is performance to the choice of temporal offsets and their aggregation weights? What happens if you remove the “last three with coefficients (0.5, 0.5, 1)” heuristic?
4. How does DFP perform when only sparse scalar reward is available (no auxiliary measurements), or when the measurement vector is incomplete/noisy?
5. What is the effect of experience replay size and ε schedule on stability and final performance?
6. Can you report full training curves with shaded variance over ≥5 seeds, and per-method wall-clock budgets? 
7. Is the competition-trained model architecture identical to the one reported? Were any additional tricks used? Will code and trained models be released?

Overall, the paper presents an appealingly simple idea with promising results, but stronger and fairer baselines, broader evaluation, and deeper analysis are needed for a compelling case.
