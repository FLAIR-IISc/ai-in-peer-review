Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning formulation of sensorimotor control that predicts, conditional on current observation, candidate action, and a goal vector, the multi-step future of a low-dimensional measurement stream (e.g., health, ammo, frags). Acting reduces to choosing the action whose predicted future measurements best match the current goal (a linear utility over measurement differences). The approach removes Bellman bootstrapping, uses temporally dense and multivariate feedback as supervision, and supports goal-conditioning at train and test time. Architecturally, DFP processes image, measurement, and goal streams, and uses a dueling-style decomposition into an expectation stream and an action-advantage stream with zero-mean normalization. Empirically, on ViZDoom tasks of increasing complexity, DFP matches or outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C; DSR on the simpler scenario), exhibits generalization across textures/environments, adapts to unseen test-time goals after goal-agnostic training, and wins the Visual Doom AI Competition. Ablations indicate benefits of vectorial measurements and multi-offset prediction targets.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) A simple, well-motivated alternative to standard RL that leverages dense multivariate measurements to turn control into supervised prediction, with a clear goal-conditioned formulation; (2) Strong empirical evidence on challenging 3D tasks, including generalization and ablations, and an external competition win; (3) Clear positioning relative to UVFA, generalized value functions, Monte Carlo vs TD, and multi-objective RL.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles learning visually grounded control skills in complex 3D FPS settings, arguing that dense measurement streams provide richer supervision than sparse scalar rewards, and that many goals can be expressed as linear utilities over future measurements. This is a compelling and practical perspective for many embodied settings with telemetry-like signals.
- Relation to prior work: The formulation is closely related to UVFA (Schaul et al. 2015) and generalized value functions (Sutton et al. 2011), but differs by directly predicting future measurement vectors rather than a scalar value for a fixed reward, thereby enabling post-hoc goal selection without re-optimization. It also connects to Monte Carlo-style learning and predictive state representations. The paper appropriately discusses these lines and positions itself as a pragmatic instantiation for rich sensory input.
- Methodology: The learning objective is simple supervised regression to multi-step measurement deltas at multiple temporal offsets. Conditioning on action and goal is straightforward and makes action selection trivial. The architecture is sensible and grounded in prior work (dueling networks); the zero-mean normalization across actions is a nice stabilizing detail.
- Empirical support: 
  - Comparisons: On Basic and Navigation, DFP is competitive or better; on the more demanding Battle/Battle2 with multiple measurements and large discrete action sets, DFP substantially outperforms A3C in frags. Reporting three independent seeds and large test evaluations is good practice.
  - Generalization: Training across texture variations and testing on disjoint textures/environments is a meaningful generalization probe; results show expected trends and improvements with increased diversity/capacity.
  - Goal-agnostic training: Models trained with randomized goals nearly match task-specific training on the main goal and generalize much better to novel goals—strong evidence for the goal-conditioned formulation.
  - Ablations: Predicting multiple measurements and multiple future offsets both help, even when evaluated on a single measurement, supporting the central “dense multivariate supervision” thesis.
  - External validation: The competition win against a strong, more complex baseline that used additional supervision further corroborates effectiveness.
- Scope and limitations: The approach is reactive (no memory), myopic beyond the chosen offsets, and relies on access to informative measurement streams. There is no theoretical analysis of control optimality or error compounding. Nonetheless, the simplicity and strong empirical results justify acceptance.

Additional feedback and questions (to help improve the paper)
- Fairness and details of baselines:
  - Please specify the exact action-space cardinality in D3/D4 (combinatorial of eight binary sub-actions). Large action sets can differentially affect DQN/A3C; did you prune infeasible combinations or use frame-skip? 
  - For A3C, report number of workers, frame stacking, frame-skip, and observation preprocessing; these strongly affect performance. Clarify learning-rate schedules, entropy regularization, and whether recurrent variants were considered. 
  - DSR was severely under-trained relative to others; consider omitting it from the main comparison or clearly separating it as a throughput-limited reference.
- Targets and horizons:
  - You predict at offsets {1,2,4,8,16,32} and weight only the last three with coefficients (0.5, 0.5, 1). Provide a sensitivity study to offsets/weights and discuss mapping to an effective discount (how does behavior change with longer horizons or denser offsets?).
  - How does DFP handle tasks with delayed consequences beyond 32 steps or requiring long-term planning (e.g., exploration)? Any evidence on performance as horizons grow?
- Training signals and counterfactuals:
  - The loss supervises only the action actually taken at each step. Did you explore importance weighting, model-based rollouts, or other techniques to improve learning for unexecuted actions? How much does exploration rate (ε schedule) impact coverage and sample efficiency?
- Measurements and supervision:
  - You rely on numerical measurements (health, ammo, frags) exported by the environment. To what extent is this privileged vs what a human sees on the HUD? Could the method learn these measurements from pixels (e.g., via an auxiliary OCR head), and does that affect performance?
  - How robust is DFP when the measurement stream is noisy, partially observed, or sparser (e.g., removing ammo or frags)?
- Architecture:
  - Provide ablations of the expectation/action decomposition vs a single head; quantify the benefit of the zero-mean normalization.
  - Any gains from adding recurrence or memory (e.g., LSTM) to cope with partial observability in mazes?
- Connections to theory and related work:
  - Discuss more explicitly the relationship to successor representations/features and multi-objective RL: with linear utilities over measurement deltas, DFP approximates goal-conditional cumulants; are there transfer guarantees or error bounds analogous to those in successor-feature literature?
- Reproducibility:
  - Please release code/configs, and specify all training hyperparameters (ε schedule, replay size M, update lag k, optimizer settings) in the main text or a reproducibility appendix.

Overall, this is a clear, well-motivated paper with strong empirical evidence and a simple, practical alternative to standard deep RL in settings with rich measurement streams and dynamic goals. The above clarifications would further strengthen the work.
