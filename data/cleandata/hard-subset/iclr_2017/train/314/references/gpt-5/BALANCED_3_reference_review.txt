1. Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a goal-conditioned, supervised-learning formulation for sensorimotor control: instead of learning values or policies via TD learning, the agent predicts vectors of future measurement changes (e.g., health, ammo, frags) at multiple temporal offsets, conditioned on current observation, candidate action, and a goal vector g. At test time, the agent selects the action whose predicted future measurements best align with the goal (via g^T f). The approach is simple, leverages dense multivariate feedback rather than sparse scalar rewards, and supports changing goals at test time. The authors introduce a dueling-style architecture for per-action prediction, train with Monte Carlo targets from experience, and evaluate on several first-person ViZDoom scenarios of increasing complexity. Empirically, DFP matches or outperforms strong RL baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR), shows notable gains on the more complex tasks with richer measurements, generalizes across textures/environments and to new goals after goal-agnostic training, and won the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch track.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Clear, well-motivated formulation that reframes control as supervised multi-step, goal-conditioned measurement prediction, enabling goal flexibility and leveraging dense feedback.
- Strong empirical results on challenging 3D visual control benchmarks, with ablations supporting core claims (benefit of vectorial measurements and multi-step prediction), plus compelling evidence of generalization and a competitive win.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper squarely targets two persistent challenges—learning from raw high-dimensional sensory input and handling dynamic goals. The insight to use measurement streams (readily available in many embodied settings) as supervisory targets is intuitive and compelling, and the goal-conditioning neatly enables test-time goal changes. The Monte-Carlo, supervised framing is well-suited when dense measurements exist.
- Positioning in literature: The paper relates to UVFA (Schaul et al.) and successor representations (Kulkarni et al., Dayan), but differs in predicting future measurement vectors directly (not values), conditioning on goal, and using multi-offset Monte Carlo targets with a dueling-style architecture. The discussion of Monte Carlo vs TD and multi-objective RL is appropriate, though a deeper connection to successor features could further clarify theory.
- Empirical support: Results show consistent and sizable gains over A3C in Battle/Battle2, where richer measurements exist. The texture generalization experiments and goal-agnostic training convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the formulation. The ablation study directly supports the central hypothesis that multivariate and multi-horizon targets provide better supervision than a scalar reward. The experimental protocol (multiple seeds, long training, 1M-step evals) is reasonable, and the competition win adds credibility.
- Limitations: Some baselines and setup choices could bias in favor of DFP. A3C implementation details matter greatly; the paper uses an independent repo and no memory (LSTM) despite partial observability, while the top competition baseline uses LSTM and extra supervision. DSR was only evaluated on the simplest scenario and at a much lower step budget. The method supervises only the action that was actually taken (no counterfactual labeling), which could limit sample efficiency; however, practice shows strong performance here. The approach depends on selecting informative measurement vectors; in domains where such measurements are unavailable or incomplete, applicability is less clear. Finally, theory remains largely qualitative; a formal link to value approximation with linear reward over features (and to successor features) would strengthen the claims.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Strengthen theoretical framing: Make explicit the equivalence between maximizing g^T predicted measurement deltas and value estimation when rewards are linear in measurements. Relate this to successor features and generalized policy improvement, and discuss when DFP differs (multi-offset targets, finite horizons).
- Baseline fairness: Clarify A3C details (frame stacking, action repeat, entropy reg, LSTM vs feedforward). Consider adding a memory-based A3C or IMPALA/PPO-style baseline, or at least discuss expected impact. For DSR, acknowledge the step-budget mismatch more prominently.
- Reproducibility: Consolidate key hyperparameters in the main text (experience memory size, update frequency, ε schedule, Adam settings, frame skip/stacks, measurement normalization). Release code/scripts if possible.
- Analysis: Report data efficiency (performance vs environment steps and wall-clock), and calibration/MAE of future measurement predictions vs control performance. Include sensitivity to the set of temporal offsets and the choice to weight only the last three. Provide qualitative goal-switching examples within a single episode.
- Measurement design: Discuss how to select measurements in new domains, robustness to missing or noisy measurements, and limits when goals are not linear in measurements. If feasible, include a non-linear goal u(f;g) experiment or show g-generalization to out-of-distribution weights.
- Partial observability: Since Doom is POMDP-like, a brief experiment with a recurrent DFP variant (or frame stacking ablation) would be informative.

Questions for the authors
- Baseline details: Did A3C use frame stacking or recurrence? What action repeat/frame skip was used across all methods? Were observation normalizations identical? Can you report variance over seeds for all methods?
- Experience and updates: What are M (replay size), N (batch size), and k (update frequency)? Is the method sensitive to M? How many gradient steps per environment step?
- Targets/horizons: Why were only the last three offsets used in the loss? How sensitive are results to the set of τ and their weights? Did you try discounting or longer horizons?
- Goal-conditioning: How were random goals sampled and normalized? How does performance vary with the range ([0,1] vs [-1,1]) and with out-of-distribution g at test time?
- Measurement selection: What happens if you remove one measurement (e.g., ammo) or add noisy/irrelevant ones? How robust is control to measurement noise?
- Counterfactual actions: Since you only observe fi for the executed action, did you try techniques to improve per-action supervision (e.g., model-based counterfactuals, data augmentation)? Any signs of action imbalance harming learning?
- Generalization: In the texture experiments, do models trained on D4-tx also generalize to different enemy types or weapon dynamics if available?

Overall, this is a clear, impactful paper with a simple idea that yields strong results and opens a promising direction for goal-conditioned control with dense feedback.
