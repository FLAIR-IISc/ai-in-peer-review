Summary of contributions
- Problem and framing: The paper tackles sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments. It departs from standard reward-centric RL by separating observations into high-dimensional sensory input and low-dimensional “measurements” (e.g., health, ammo), and learns to predict future measurement changes conditioned on the current observation, action, and a goal vector.
- Method: A supervised learning approach (Direct Future Prediction, DFP) trains a goal-conditioned predictor F(o, a, g) to regress to future measurement differences at multiple temporal offsets, then acts by choosing the action that maximizes the dot product between the goal vector and the predicted future measurements. The architecture includes perception, measurement, and goal modules, and a dueling-style predictor with expectation and action streams.
- Claims: (1) Dense, multivariate measurement prediction provides a rich supervisory signal that stabilizes/accelerates learning compared to scalar reward. (2) Goal conditioning enables test-time adaptation to goals not seen during training. (3) In ViZDoom scenarios, DFP outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C), particularly on more complex tasks and when multiple measurements are available. (4) The learned behaviors generalize across visually diverse environments. (5) An instance of the method won the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch track.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Clear, well-motivated reformulation with a simple, effective architecture that is novel relative to conventional value-based/policy-gradient RL. (2) Strong empirical results, including generalization across environments/goals and a competition win, support the central claims.

Supporting arguments
- Clarity: The core idea, training protocol, and action-selection rule are clearly presented, with intuitive justification for measurement prediction and goal conditioning. The architecture choice (expectation/action streams) is sensible and tied to prior work (dueling networks). The training regimes (single vs randomized goals) are well explained. Some presentation gaps remain (see suggestions).
- Positioning and motivation: The work is well situated relative to TD/policy gradient RL, Monte Carlo methods, UVFA, successor representations, and multi-objective RL. The argument that dense, multidimensional measurement streams can make supervised prediction attractive is coherent and timely for visually rich domains. The formulation generalizes RL as a special case when measurements include reward.
- Technical soundness: The approach is straightforward and internally consistent. Targets are Monte Carlo-style future measurement differences at several horizons, minimizing squared error, with ε-greedy exploration and small replay buffers. The expectation/action stream decomposition is appropriate. No theoretical guarantees are provided, but the empirical focus is reasonable for this contribution.
- Empirical support: 
  - Benchmarks: Four ViZDoom scenarios ranging from simple navigation to combat. DFP matches or exceeds DQN/A3C, with large margins on the more complex scenarios (D3/D4), where multiple measurements are available.
  - Generalization: Training on texture-varied environments yields robust transfer to unseen textures and layouts; capacity scaling further helps.
  - Goal generalization: Models trained with randomized goals perform nearly as well as specialized models on a fixed goal and generalize substantially better to new goals.
  - Ablations: Predicting multiple measurements and multiple temporal horizons both improve performance, supporting the central premise that dense vectorial supervision is beneficial.
  - External validation: Competition performance strengthens the empirical case.
- Rigor and fairness: Baselines use public implementations with some hyperparameter tuning. Training budgets are comparable in steps for DQN/A3C/DFP. Reporting averages over seeds and substantial test budgets is positive, though confidence intervals are missing. One fairness caveat is that RL baselines receive only a scalar reward, while DFP leverages the full vector of measurements; although this asymmetry is central to the paper’s claim, an RL baseline augmented with multi-objective or auxiliary measurement prediction (e.g., UVFA, auxiliary tasks) would more fully isolate where the gains arise.

Additional feedback to help improve the paper
- Baselines and fairness:
  - Include a goal-conditioned value function baseline (UVFA) and/or RL agents with auxiliary measurement prediction losses (e.g., multi-head critics) to control for the benefit of vectorial supervision.
  - Report results with stronger, more recent RL baselines or variants with memory/auxiliary losses where applicable.
- Reporting and statistical rigor:
  - Add confidence intervals or standard deviations across seeds for all key plots/tables.
  - Clarify training wall-clock vs environment steps; detail the exact parallelism used for A3C and the optimizer settings for all methods.
- Clarity and missing details:
  - Specify the exact number of composite actions in D3/D4 (2^8 = 256 assumed, but not stated).
  - Provide full architecture details in the main text or a concise summary (layers/widths, normalization, activation functions), and the temporal weighting rationale (“only the latest three time steps”).
  - Clean up references (numerous formatting errors) and verify citations.
- Analysis extensions:
  - Sensitivity to temporal offsets and weights; how performance varies with horizon design.
  - Robustness to measurement noise and to sparser measurement streams (e.g., infrequent frag updates).
  - Diagnostics of prediction accuracy vs control performance to better understand error tolerance.
  - Ablate the expectation/action-stream split.

Questions for the authors
- Baseline parity: Can you provide results for a UVFA-style baseline conditioned on the same goal vector, and/or A3C/DQN augmented with auxiliary prediction of the measurement vector, to isolate the effect of vectorial supervision vs algorithmic differences?
- Action space: What is the exact number of actions in D3/D4 (all combinations of 8 binary sub-actions)? Were any combinations pruned?
- Temporal targets: Why select offsets {1,2,4,8,16,32} and weight only the last three? How sensitive is performance to these choices?
- Replay and updates: What are the replay memory size M and update frequency k? Did larger replay buffers or different k materially change results?
- Exploration: Beyond ε-greedy and A3C’s entropy regularization, were alternative exploration strategies considered? How does DFP perform with noisier/more stochastic environments?
- Prediction accuracy: What is the measured error of F’s predictions during training/testing, and how does this correlate with control performance?
- Generalization: How does DFP fare when the test-time goal lies far outside the training goal distribution (e.g., extreme weights), or when new measurements are introduced at test time?
- Computational cost: Provide wall-clock training times for DFP vs A3C/DQN under the reported configurations.
