1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning approach to sensorimotor control that predicts future changes in a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) conditioned on current observations and a goal vector g. Actions are selected by evaluating g^T times the predicted future measurement deltas across actions. The authors argue this reframing provides dense supervision, handles dynamically-specified goals, and avoids Bellman-style bootstrapping. They introduce an architecture that splits prediction into an expectation stream and an action-difference stream. Empirically, on several ViZDoom scenarios, DFP reportedly outperforms DQN, A3C, and DSR (on the simpler task), generalizes to unseen textures and goals, and won the Visual Doom AI Competition. Ablations suggest multi-dimensional measurements and multi-horizon prediction are beneficial.

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The empirical evidence is not sufficiently rigorous to support strong claims of superiority over established RL methods; several comparisons raise fairness and tuning concerns, and the evaluation scope is narrow (single domain, modest number of environments).
- The conceptual novelty is limited relative to established literature on generalized value functions, universal value function approximators, successor features, and Monte Carlo return prediction; the work lacks theoretical analysis or conditions under which the proposed reduction is preferable.

3. Supporting arguments
- Positioning and novelty: The core idea—predicting vector-valued future measurements conditioned on goals and acting by maximizing a goal-weighted utility—closely echoes GVFs and UVFA, as well as work on successor features and Monte Carlo prediction. The paper acknowledges related work but does not clearly articulate what is technically new beyond an application-driven instantiation (choice of “measurements,” multi-horizon regression, and the particular network split). Absent analysis, it is difficult to assess when and why DFP is expected to outperform TD or actor-critic methods.

- Fairness of baselines:
  - The DQN baseline appears to use the 2015 implementation without architectural improvements (e.g., dueling networks), whereas DFP includes a dueling-style split. This stacks the deck against DQN.
  - A3C: only an independent implementation was used; although some hyperparameter search is reported, details on parity in architecture (e.g., frame stacking, LSTM, auxiliary losses) and environment parallelism are sparse. A3C’s performance is sensitive to these choices.
  - DSR: comparison is truncated by computational constraints and lacks comparable training steps; thus inconclusive.
  - Reward shaping: DFP benefits from vector-valued measurement supervision, whereas baselines are trained on a scalar combination; this conflates algorithmic advantage with information advantage. A more appropriate control would add auxiliary measurement prediction tasks to A3C/DQN (akin to UNREAL-style auxiliary losses) or successor features/UVFA baselines.

- Scope and robustness:
  - All experiments are in ViZDoom. Claims about overcoming RL challenges (raw sensory input, complex 3D control, general skills) would be more compelling with additional benchmarks (e.g., DMLab, Procgen, Atari) or standardized Doom tasks with widely used evaluation protocols.
  - The training horizon uses short offsets (up to 32 steps); it is unclear how DFP scales to longer credit assignment horizons, delayed sparse rewards, or settings where desirable outcomes are not well-captured by the chosen measurements.
  - Off-policy aspects are under-explored. The method uses a small replay/experience memory with ε-greedy behavior. Sensitivity to memory size, ε schedule, and off-policy bias is not analyzed.

- Statistical rigor:
  - Results average over three seeds; given the high variance in deep RL, more seeds and statistical tests would increase confidence.
  - The competition result is encouraging but not a controlled scientific comparison; competing entries differed in algorithmic choices and training signal (e.g., privileged labels).

- Interpretability of claims:
  - The ablation shows benefit of multi-measurement, multi-horizon targets, but does not disentangle whether improvements stem from richer supervision per se (which could also be granted to baselines) versus the specific DFP objective. The conclusion that “vectorial measurements are beneficial” is likely correct, but it does not establish that DFP is the preferred mechanism to exploit them.

4. Feedback to improve the paper (meant to help; not all are decision-critical)
- Strengthen baselines:
  - Include dueling DQN and a strong A3C/IMPALA with auxiliary measurement prediction and/or successor features/UVFA. Ensure architecture parity (frame stacking, recurrent memory where applicable).
  - Provide wall-clock and sample efficiency comparisons with matched compute, parallelism, and data budgets.

- Expand evaluation:
  - Add benchmarks beyond ViZDoom and include tasks with long-horizon sparse rewards to probe when DFP’s Monte Carlo regression is advantageous or not.
  - Increase seeds and report confidence intervals; include ablations on the number and choice of temporal offsets, measurement subsets, and the size of the replay buffer.

- Analyze the method:
  - Provide a principled discussion connecting DFP to GVFs/UVFA and successor features, clarifying when u(f; g) with finite offsets approximates discounted returns and what biases arise.
  - Discuss limitations: continuous action spaces, partial observability/memory needs, and sensitivity to the choice of measurements.

- Clarify implementation details:
  - Whether the DFP architecture confers advantages over the baselines (e.g., dueling split, normalization layer); if so, give baselines comparable architectural capacity.
  - Report preprocessing, action repeat, frame skip, reward clipping, and training stability tricks for all methods.

Questions for the authors
- Did the DQN baseline include dueling and other modern enhancements? If not, can you add them to ensure parity with DFP’s split?
- Can you provide results for A3C with auxiliary measurement prediction (or successor features/UVFA) trained on the same vectorial signals?
- How does DFP perform with longer horizons (e.g., offsets up to 256) and on tasks with genuinely sparse rewards?
- What is the sensitivity to the choice and scale of measurements? If some measurements are noisy or weakly relevant, does performance degrade?
- How large is the experience memory M, and how sensitive are results to M and the update cadence k?
- Can you release code and environment configurations to facilitate reproducibility?
