Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning formulation of sensorimotor control. Instead of learning value functions via Bellman-style bootstrapping, the agent predicts action-conditional future measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) at multiple temporal offsets, conditioned on the current observation and a goal vector. Actions are selected by maximizing the predicted goal utility g^T f. This reframing leverages dense, multivariate supervision available in immersive environments.
- A simple deep architecture (convolutional perception plus fully connected measurement/goal encoders, and a dueling-style prediction head with an expectation and an action stream) is trained via Monte Carlo regression on experience.
- Extensive experiments in ViZDoom show: (i) DFP matches or exceeds strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C, and DSR where feasible), with especially large gains on more complex scenarios; (ii) models generalize to novel environments (textures) and to test-time goals not seen during training; (iii) multivariate measurements and multi-horizon prediction outperform scalar reward prediction; (iv) a DFP-based agent won the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch track without extra supervision.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) A simple, clear, and well-motivated formulation that replaces sparse scalar rewards with dense vector measurements and turns control into supervised prediction, enabling strong empirical performance; (2) thorough experimental evidence, including generalization across environments/goals and ablations, with sizable margins over state-of-the-art deep RL baselines on challenging 3D tasks.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem tackled: Learning visuomotor control with raw sensory input in complex 3D environments, while supporting dynamic goals and leveraging dense feedback. The paper asks whether we can avoid Bellman-style TD learning and instead use supervised prediction of meaningful future measurements to drive control.

2) Motivation and placement: The case for SL vs TD is well made: when dense, multidimensional feedback exists, direct prediction can be more stable/data-efficient. The paper situates itself relative to Monte Carlo methods, predictive representations, UVFAs, successor representations, and recent 3D RL work. The closest prior is UVFA (goal-conditioned value functions) and SR (decoupling dynamics and rewards). DFP’s novelty is the explicit prediction of vector measurement deltas at multiple horizons and action-conditional outputs, enabling goal-conditioned control without explicit bootstrapping. The literature discussion is appropriate and balanced.

3) Empirical support: Results are convincing. DFP matches A3C on simple tasks, strongly outperforms A3C and DQN on complex “Battle” scenarios, generalizes across textures, supports test-time goal changes, and benefits measurably from vector and multi-horizon targets. The competition win is a notable external validation. Training and testing protocols (millions of steps, multiple seeds, train/test splits for textures) are described; ablations isolate key design choices. While the method is largely empirical, this is consistent with the field norms for benchmarked control; claims are supported by results.

Caveats and potential weaknesses (not fatal)
- Baseline strength/fairness: A3C is widely used with LSTMs in partially observable 3D tasks; it is unclear if the baseline included recurrence. If not, this could understate A3C performance. Hyperparameter tuning for DSR was limited due to speed. Nonetheless, the reported margins (especially in D3/D4) are large.
- Off-policy target mismatch: Targets f are collected under an evolving ε-greedy policy and used to learn predictions for all actions; there is no off-policy correction. The paper demonstrates robust performance despite this, but some discussion of the induced bias would help.
- Horizon/credit assignment: Prediction horizons are short (up to 32 steps), and only the last three offsets are used in the loss. It would help to analyze sensitivity to τ and the weighting scheme, and whether tasks with long-horizon dependencies degrade performance.
- Limited goal class: Although the framework allows arbitrary u(f; g), experiments only use linear utilities. The generalization claims are therefore limited to linear goals over the measurement deltas.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Clarify baseline configurations: explicitly state whether the A3C baseline uses recurrence (e.g., LSTM) and frame stacking; report entropy regularization and learning-rate selections per scenario. If feasible, include an A3C-LSTM baseline; partial observability in FPS games often makes recurrence important.
- Report variability: add standard deviations or confidence intervals across seeds for the main metrics in Tables/Figures.
- Sensitivity analyses: (i) vary τ and the loss weights; (ii) report performance vs replay buffer size and update frequency k; (iii) show impact of goal-randomization regimes on generalization to unseen goals; (iv) ablate the dueling-style expectation/action streams to quantify their contribution.
- Policy interpretation: discuss the conditional distribution implicit in p_a predicting f given action a and subsequent behavior. Do you assume the rollouts follow the data-collection policy after the first action? How does changing g at test time affect the validity of predictions learned under a different continuation policy?
- Action space scalability: computing predictions for all actions is feasible here; discuss scaling to large or continuous action spaces (e.g., actor selecting actions using gradients of g^T F w.r.t. a, or learning a policy head on top of F).
- Long-horizon tasks: include a benchmark with more delayed outcomes to probe the limits of multi-horizon measurement prediction vs TD methods.
- Writing and references: fix several citation formatting issues and minor typos; ensure consistency in action counts (one place truncates “resulting in possible actions”).

Questions for the authors
1) After conditioning on action a at time t, what policy generates the future used to compute f for training targets? Is it the behavior policy at data collection time? Any attempts at off-policy correction or reweighting?
2) How sensitive is DFP to the choice of temporal offsets and loss weights (0.5, 0.5, 1)? Have you tried longer horizons or learned weights?
3) Did your A3C baseline include an LSTM/recurrence and frame stacking? If not, how does DFP compare to an A3C-LSTM baseline on D3/D4?
4) How robust are results across random seeds? Please provide variance for the main tables/figures.
5) Can you provide more details on the architecture and hyperparameters (Appendix A), including replay buffer size M, update frequency k, optimizer settings, input normalization, and action enumeration?
6) In goal-agnostic training, how often are goals switched and how are they sampled? Can the agent handle mid-episode goal switches at test time?
7) What is the computational overhead of predicting all actions vs a single action? Any tricks (e.g., shared computation) for larger action sets?
8) Have you tested nonlinear utilities u or learned goal embeddings beyond simple linear combinations?

Overall, this is a strong, clear, and impactful empirical paper. The idea is simple yet effective, and the experiments are compelling. With clarifications on baselines and some added analyses, it would make a valuable ICLR contribution.
