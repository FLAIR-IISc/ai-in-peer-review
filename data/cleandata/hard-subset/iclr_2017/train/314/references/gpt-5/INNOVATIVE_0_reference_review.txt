Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): a goal-conditioned, supervised-learning approach to sensorimotor control that predicts vectorial future measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) given current sensory input, measurements, an action, and a goal vector. Action selection maximizes the inner product of the goal with the predicted future measurements.
- This reframes control from reward-maximizing RL to supervised prediction on dense, intrinsic feedback streams, enabling training with raw experience and without extraneous supervision.
- The model supports dynamic, parameterized goals at test time and can be trained with randomized goals to encourage broad generalization.
- Architecture: a perception stream plus measurement and goal encoders, and a prediction head split into expectation and action streams (inspired by dueling networks) to better capture action differentials.
- Empirical results on ViZDoom across four scenarios show strong improvements over DQN and A3C in the more complex tasks, good generalization across textures/environments, effective goal-agnostic training, and ablations demonstrating benefits of vectorial feedback and multiple horizons. A competition win (Full Deathmatch) further validates the approach.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Conceptual novelty: a bold and clean reframing of control as supervised prediction of intrinsic future measurements, with explicit goal parameterization. This opens a promising direction beyond conventional scalar-reward, Bellman-based RL.
- Strong empirical support on challenging 3D tasks, including generalization and goal-agnostic capabilities, with competitive training speed and a public competition victory.

Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: how to leverage dense, multidimensional feedback to learn sensorimotor control from raw sensory input, while enabling flexible, dynamically specified goals. This is crisply addressed by directly predicting future measurements conditional on actions and goals.
- Motivation and placement: The paper situates DFP relative to Monte Carlo learning, UVFA, multi-objective RL, and predictive representations (GVFs), and contrasts to dominant TD/policy gradient methods under sparse scalar rewards. The argument for supervised prediction when dense feedback exists is compelling and timely for 3D embodied settings.
- Claims supported:
  - Performance: Clear outperformance over A3C/DQN on richer scenarios (D3/D4), with plausible parity on simpler tasks. The advantage grows with more measurements, consistent with the core hypothesis.
  - Generalization: Training across textures leads to robust transfer; larger-capacity models improve further.
  - Goal-agnostic training: Models trained with randomized goals nearly match single-goal models on the main task and generalize better to novel goals.
  - Ablations: Predicting multiple measurements and multiple horizons materially improves outcomes, even when evaluated on a single metric, supporting the “dense vectorial supervision” thesis.
  - Practicality: The approach is simple, stable (no bootstrapping), and fast enough to process tens of millions of steps/day.
- Novelty vs prior art: While related to UVFA and successor representations, the explicit measurement-prediction framing with goal-conditioned, action-conditional predictors and dense, supervised training on intrinsic signals is a notable conceptual departure that simplifies training and broadens applicability to arbitrary goal vectors.

Additional feedback (to strengthen the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Statistical rigor: Add confidence intervals/variance across seeds for main tables and generalization experiments; report sample efficiency and wall-clock comparably for all baselines.
- Baseline fairness: Clarify whether A3C used recurrent policies; add a recurrent A3C/DQN variant to test if memory narrows gaps on D3/D4. Discuss reward shaping used by baselines vs. your intrinsic measurements for parity.
- Connections and theory: Deepen discussion of relations to GVFs and successor representations; formalize when DFP is equivalent to optimizing a goal over finite-horizon returns and the effect of horizon choice on credit assignment.
- Measurement design: Discuss how to choose/learn measurements when intrinsic game stats are unavailable. Could learned latent measurements (e.g., via auxiliary tasks) substitute for hand-defined ones?
- Horizons: Analyze sensitivity to the chosen temporal offsets and the weighting scheme (only last three offsets used). What happens for sparser, long-horizon dependencies?
- Architecture ablations: Report the contribution of the expectation/action split, goal-conditioning in the encoder, and per-action vs. shared prediction heads.
- Exploration: ε-greedy may be suboptimal in sparse regimes—comment on integrating intrinsic motivation or uncertainty-aware exploration with DFP.
- Scope: Brief results or discussion on continuous control and partial observability with memory (even preliminary) would broaden impact.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive is performance to the set of temporal offsets and their weights? Have you tried longer horizons or learned weights over horizons?
2. To what extent is the advantage due to vectorial supervision vs. the supervised, Monte Carlo nature of training? If you train DFP only on a scalar reward (frags) with the same architecture, how close does it get?
3. Can you report variance over seeds for the main comparisons and generalization experiments, and include confidence intervals?
4. Did you evaluate recurrent baselines (e.g., LSTM-A3C) under the same training budget? If so, how do results change?
5. How would you apply DFP when explicit measurements like health/ammo are not available? Have you explored learning measurement vectors (e.g., via auxiliary predictive tasks) that still support goal parameterization?
6. What is the computational cost of predicting per-action measurement vectors compared to Q-value heads, and how does this scale with larger action spaces?
7. How robust is goal generalization to goal vectors far outside the training distribution? Could you visualize performance as a function of goal weights?
8. Does DFP degrade on tasks with truly sparse, delayed rewards (few/no intrinsic measurements changing frequently)? Any strategies to mitigate this (longer horizons, imagination rollouts, memory)?
9. Can you comment on off-policy stability with the small replay buffer and k-step delayed updates? Did you observe bias when the goal distribution at test time differs from training?

Overall, this paper introduces a fresh, impactful perspective with convincing empirical validation. Despite some scope and evaluation limitations, the novelty and promise of the approach justify acceptance.
