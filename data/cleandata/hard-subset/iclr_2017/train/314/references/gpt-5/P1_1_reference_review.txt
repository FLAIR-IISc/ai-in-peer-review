Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a goal-conditioned, supervised-learning formulation of sensorimotor control. Instead of learning a scalar value function or policy from rewards, the agent predicts, for each action, a vector of future intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) at multiple time offsets, conditioned on the current observation and a goal vector that linearly scores those futures. Action selection is greedy w.r.t. predicted goal-conditioned utility.
- Key ideas: (i) replace sparse scalar reward with dense, multivariate measurement streams as supervision; (ii) parameterize goals so a single model can pursue new goal combinations at test time; (iii) use a dueling-style prediction head (expectation + action-advantage) to sharpen action-specific differences.
- Empirical evaluation in ViZDoom shows strong performance against DQN and A3C, especially in complex scenarios (Battle, Battle 2) and in generalization (texture randomization, unseen maps). Ablations suggest vector-valued predictions and multi-horizon prediction are important. The model won the Visual Doom AI competition Full Deathmatch track without extra supervision.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Conceptual novelty and clarity: a simple, well-motivated reframing of control as supervised goal-conditioned future prediction with dense measurement supervision.
- Strong empirical evidence: substantial gains over strong deep RL baselines in visually rich 3D environments, generalization across textures/environments, and goal-agnostic training that adapts at test time.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets two core challenges—learning from raw pixels in complex 3D settings and handling dynamically specified goals. The use of dense measurement streams as supervision is well motivated, especially when such streams are natural and abundant, addressing stability and sample efficiency issues in sparse-reward RL.
- Relation to literature: The work is placed against TD/Monte Carlo debates, multi-objective/semi-parametric goals (UVFA), and predictive/state-representation literature. It is closely related to Schaul et al. (UVFA) and successor representations; the paper compares to DSR in Doom. The architecture borrowing from dueling networks is appropriate. Overall, the positioning is reasonable and timely; a deeper discussion of connections to successor features could further strengthen it.
- Methodological soundness: The learning objective is straightforward regression to multi-horizon future measurement differences, with off-policy updates via a small replay buffer and ε-greedy exploration. The architectural choice to separate expectation and action-specific deltas is sensible and demonstrated to help.
- Empirical rigor: Four scenarios with increasing difficulty; comparisons to DQN and A3C with some hyperparameter search; ablations on scalar vs vector feedback and multi-horizon vs single-horizon; generalization across 100 textured mazes; goal-agnostic training tests. The gains in the complex scenarios (6x and 2.5x over A3C) are large and consistent with the claimed advantages when multiple measurements are available. Competition results add external validation.

Caveats and limitations (do not outweigh the strengths but temper claims):
- Limited theoretical grounding: no guarantees about optimal control, bias from limited horizons (up to 32 steps) and choice of weighting of temporal offsets.
- Sensitivity and design choices: time offsets and weighting (only last three offsets contribute) are somewhat ad hoc; exploration relies on ε-greedy; experience memory size and off-policy effects are underexplored.
- Fairness and breadth of baselines: A3C tuning is described, but DSR results are undertrained due to computational constraints; no comparison to more recent goal-conditioned RL or model-based baselines. Results are averaged over only three seeds; no statistical tests.
- Scope: All experiments are in Doom; it would help to include a domain with sparser measurements to probe the boundary where the method is less effective.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Clarify the relationship to successor features/representations: F can be seen as predicting action-conditional feature expectations; connecting to (and contrasting with) GPI and SF-based transfer would sharpen the conceptual contribution.
- Analyze horizon sensitivity: vary τ sets and the weighting schedule; report how performance changes with longer horizons and sparser use of offsets.
- Report calibration/accuracy of future predictions: e.g., error vs horizon and its correlation with control performance.
- Expand ablations: effect of the action-stream normalization, goal-conditioning (train without g), measurement scaling/normalization, and memory/frame stacking vs purely reactive input.
- Fairness and reproducibility: detail compute budgets, update ratios, replay size, and provide code/configs; add more seeds and confidence intervals.
- Stress tests: evaluate in settings with sparser/partial measurement streams and in sparse-reward variants to delineate when DFP is preferable to TD-based methods.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of temporal offsets and to using only the last three offsets in the loss? Could longer horizons (beyond 32 steps) help in Battle/Battle 2?
- How are measurement scales handled? Are they normalized, and how robust is performance to mis-specified scaling or noisy measurements?
- Can you quantify the accuracy of the predicted futures across horizons and actions, and whether better predictive accuracy directly translates to higher returns?
- How critical is the action-stream normalization layer? What happens if you remove it or replace it with a standard fully connected head?
- Off-policy stability: given the small replay memory and ε-greedy behavior, did you observe drift or non-stationarity issues? Would larger replay or prioritized sampling help?
- How does DFP perform under sparser measurement feedback (e.g., only frags as the measurement), and under delayed effects (e.g., rewards/measurements materialize much later)?
- Could you compare to an SF/GPI baseline (or reinterpret DFP as learning successor features) to clarify similarities/differences and transfer properties?
- Does adding memory (e.g., LSTM) or stacking frames significantly change performance in Navigation/Battle tasks?
- How does goal-agnostic training handle distribution shift: if test-time goals place weight on measurements rarely emphasized during training, does performance degrade gracefully?

Overall, this is a clear, simple, and impactful contribution that opens a compelling alternative to standard RL in settings with dense, vector-valued feedback and variable goals. I recommend acceptance.
