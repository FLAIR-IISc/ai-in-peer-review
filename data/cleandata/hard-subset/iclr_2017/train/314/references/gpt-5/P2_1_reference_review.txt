Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): instead of optimizing a scalar reward via TD methods, the agent learns a supervised predictor of future changes in a vector of measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) conditioned on current sensory input, measurements, goal vector, and action. At test time it selects the action maximizing the goal-weighted predicted future measurements.
- Key contributions:
  1) A goal-conditioned predictive control formulation that reduces sensorimotor control to supervised learning using dense, multivariate feedback.
  2) A simple, dueling-style architecture that predicts action-conditional future measurements at multiple temporal offsets.
  3) Strong empirical results in 3D first-person environments (ViZDoom): outperforming DQN and A3C substantially on harder scenarios, better generalization across environments and test-time goals, and winning the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch track.
  4) Ablations indicating benefits of vector-valued measurements and multi-horizon prediction, and evidence that goal-agnostic training supports adaptation to new goals at test time.

Decision: Accept
- Reason 1: Novel and well-motivated formulation that is timely and impactful for RL in high-dimensional sensory domains, with a clean connection to dense multivariate supervision.
- Reason 2: Strong empirical support: large and consistent gains over strong deep RL baselines on challenging 3D tasks, convincing generalization across environments and goals, and competitive results in an external competition.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses sensorimotor control from raw sensory inputs with dense, meaningful auxiliary signals available to agents (measurements). The critique of sparse scalar reward-driven TD methods in such settings is reasonable; supervised prediction using rich temporal structure is a compelling alternative. The work is well positioned relative to Monte Carlo methods, predictive representations, multi-objective RL, and UVFA.
- Technical approach: Predicting future measurement differences at multiple horizons and conditioning on goal vectors is a clear, simple design that directly supports test-time goal changes. The expectation/action-stream decomposition adapts dueling networks to vector predictions and is sensible to improve discrimination among actions.
- Empirical evaluation:
  - Benchmarks: Four ViZDoom tasks of increasing difficulty (including adversarial combat), with both single-measurement (health) and multi-measurement settings. Using only raw images and measurements matches the problem setup.
  - Baselines: DQN, A3C, and DSR are reasonable choices. Reward scalarization for baselines matches the goal used by DFP, improving fairness. A3C tuning efforts are reported. DSR’s slower training is acknowledged.
  - Results: DFP is competitive on easy tasks and strongly superior on complex tasks (6x frags in D3, 2.5x in D4 vs. A3C). Gains are largest when multiple measurements are available, supporting the core hypothesis. Training speed (steps/day) and evaluation over 1M steps are reported. Curves aggregate over multiple runs.
  - Generalization: Training with texture randomization improves transfer to unseen textures; higher-capacity model further helps. This supports claims about robustness and invariance.
  - Goal generalization: Models trained with randomized goals perform nearly as well as task-specific models on the main goal and much better on novel goals, which is a key advantage of the formulation.
  - Ablations: Predicting multiple measurements and multiple horizons both improve performance; this isolates where the benefits likely come from (dense, multivariate, temporally-extended supervision).
  - External validation: Winning the Visual Doom competition against sophisticated baselines adds credibility.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The paper is clearly written, with a coherent narrative and empirical section. Appendices describe architecture and baselines. ViZDoom is public, which helps reproducibility.

Suggestions to improve (not central to decision)
- Baselines and fairness:
  - Include a UVFA-style A3C baseline conditioned on goal vectors, to disentangle gains from goal-conditioning vs. future-prediction training.
  - Consider a successor representation/factorization baseline (e.g., predicting cumulants/features with linear goal weights) for a closer architectural comparison.
  - Report standard deviations or confidence intervals across seeds for all figures/tables, not only averages, and ensure identical training budgets (frames, wall-clock, and compute) for baselines where feasible.
- Analysis:
  - Provide sensitivity to the set of measurements: What happens if some measurements are noisy, partially observable, or omitted? How crucial is access to ammo and frags for the large gains?
  - Explore the effect of horizon choices and coefficients; justify using only the last three horizons in the loss.
  - Ablate the expectation/action-stream decomposition to quantify its contribution.
  - Discuss failure modes when scalar rewards are truly sparse and no dense measurements are available.
- Broader evaluation:
  - Add a memory-based variant (e.g., conv-LSTM) to address partial observability, as acknowledged in the discussion.
  - Include an additional domain (e.g., another 3D platform or a subset of Atari) to test generality beyond Doom.
- Theory:
  - Provide a brief analysis connecting accurate future measurement prediction with policy improvement under linear goal weights, and discuss off-policy stability given small replay buffers.

Questions for the authors
1) UVFA baseline: Did you try an A3C/DQN variant that takes the goal vector as input and learns a goal-conditioned value/policy directly? How does it compare when trained on randomized goals?
2) Successor representations: Since DFP linearly combines predicted cumulants via g, how does it compare to learning successor features with linear reward weights, both in performance and sample efficiency?
3) Measurement choice: How sensitive is DFP to which measurements are provided? If you remove ammo or frags (keeping only health), does performance on D3/D4 drop to A3C levels?
4) Horizons: Why weight only the last three horizons in the loss? How does performance change with different horizon sets/weights or a learned weighting over horizons?
5) Partial observability: Did you experiment with recurrent models or frame stacks? How does memory affect performance and generalization?
6) Statistical robustness: Please report variance across seeds for the main results and ablations, and clarify whether hyperparameters were tuned per-task for DFP to the same extent as for A3C.
7) Compute and sample efficiency: Can you provide wall-clock training times and GPU/CPU usage for each method, and performance as a function of frames (not only final performance)?
8) Goal generalization: How far outside the training goal distribution can you generalize (e.g., extreme negative weights or sparse target vectors)? Does performance degrade gracefully?

Overall, this is a strong, well-motivated paper with clear empirical contributions and potential impact on how we formulate control with rich sensory feedback.
