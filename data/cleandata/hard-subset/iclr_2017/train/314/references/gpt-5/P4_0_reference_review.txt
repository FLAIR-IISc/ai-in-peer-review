1. Summary of the paper’s claimed contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning formulation of sensorimotor control that replaces scalar reward with a vector of intrinsic measurements and trains a goal-conditioned predictor to forecast future measurement changes under candidate actions. Action selection is done by choosing the action whose predicted future measurements best align with a (possibly changing) goal vector. The approach leverages dense, multivariate supervision derived directly from experience. The model architecture includes separate perception, measurement, and goal modules and a dueling-style prediction head that outputs per-action measurement forecasts. Empirically, DFP is evaluated on challenging 3D first-person Doom-based environments and is shown to outperform DQN and A3C, especially on more complex multi-measurement tasks, to generalize across environments with differing textures, and to adapt to goals not seen at training time. Ablations indicate benefits from vectorial measurements and multi-horizon prediction. A DFP-based agent won the Visual Doom AI Competition’s Full Deathmatch track.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical performance and convincing demonstrations of generalization across environments and goals, including a competitive benchmark (competition win).
- Clear, well-motivated formulation that is simple, practical, and well-placed in the literature (goal-conditioned prediction, Monte Carlo supervision, and multivariate feedback), with ablations supporting core design choices.

3. Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? The paper addresses learning goal-directed visuomotor control from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments, with the added challenge of dynamically specified goals. It reframes control as predicting actionable, intrinsic measurements rather than optimizing scalar reward.
- Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? The motivation is consistent with long-standing observations that dense, multidimensional feedback can make supervised learning attractive relative to TD methods. The work connects to predictive representations, universal value function approximators, and Monte Carlo methods, while diverging by directly predicting future intrinsic measurements and conditioning on a goal vector for action selection. Related work is appropriately cited; the positioning versus UVFA and predictive state representations is sound.
- Are claims supported with rigorous results? The empirical section is thorough within the chosen domain. Comparisons to DQN and A3C (and DSR on a simpler task) show substantial gains, notably in the more complex, multi-measurement scenarios. The generalization experiments across textures/environments and the goal-agnostic training demonstrate the claimed flexibility. The ablation isolating vector-valued targets and multi-horizon prediction is informative. The methodology (experience replay, ε-greedy) and architectural choices are standard and transparent. While the method lacks formal guarantees and theory, the empirical rigor on the chosen benchmarks is solid.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (constructive; not all are decision-critical)
- Fairness and strength of baselines: While A3C tuning is described, more details on hyperparameters and training budgets for all baselines would improve confidence. Consider comparing to strong auxiliary-task RL baselines (e.g., pixel control/UNREAL-style) that also benefit from dense supervision and to goal-conditioned RL baselines (e.g., UVFA) using the same measurement signals.
- Clarify supervision target and goal usage: You set τ = {1,2,4,8,16,32}, but “only the latest three time steps contribute to the objective.” Please clarify which components are in the loss, and whether the test-time goal vector g references only those horizons or all predicted horizons. Provide sensitivity to horizon set and weighting coefficients.
- Measurement normalization and scaling: Since action selection is g^T f, performance depends on relative scaling of measurement dimensions. Describe normalization procedures, potential rescaling at train/test, and sensitivity to these choices.
- Off-policy and data efficiency: Although the approach is supervised, the predictor depends on the policy-induced distribution. Provide a study of sample efficiency vs. A3C and DQN (e.g., learning curves vs. environment steps), and discuss stability as ε decays and the behavior distribution shifts.
- Architectural ablations: The dueling-like split (expectation/action streams) appears useful. Reporting an ablation without this split and/or without the normalization layer would strengthen the architectural justification.
- Uncertainty and robustness: Since action selection depends on point predictions, consider estimating predictive uncertainty (e.g., ensembles) and show whether that impacts exploration or robustness.
- References and presentation: Several references in the provided text are malformed or incomplete. Please fix the bibliography and ensure all citations are accurate and consistent.

Questions for the authors
- Target definition and loss: For τ = {1,2,4,8,16,32}, which τ contribute to the loss, with what weights, and which are used by the test-time goal? If only the last three contribute to training, why predict the earlier horizons at all?
- Goal conditioning: How are goal vectors chosen at test time? Are goals normalized to account for differing value ranges across f components and horizons? How sensitive is performance to goal misspecification?
- Measurement selection: How did you choose which measurements to include (e.g., ammo, health, frags)? Would adding more measurements (e.g., distances, velocities) further help, and do irrelevant measurements hurt?
- Baseline parity: Could A3C be augmented to receive the same multivariate measurement vector as auxiliary prediction targets (not just scalar reward) while still optimizing the same main objective? If so, how do results compare?
- Off-policy training: You maintain a small experience memory and update every k steps. What are M, N, k, and batch size; how sensitive are results to these values? Any issues observed with distributional shift?
- Action space combinatorics: In D3/D4, the text truncates the number of possible actions. Please specify w and discuss scalability when w is large. How would you extend DFP to continuous actions (e.g., via action-conditional regressors or policy gradient on g^T F)?
- Generalization scope: In the texture-randomization experiments, did you also randomize lighting or physics? How does performance change under more severe domain shifts (e.g., new enemy types, altered dynamics)?

Overall, the paper is clear, technically sound in its empirical methodology, and presents a novel and practically effective formulation with strong results and useful insights.
