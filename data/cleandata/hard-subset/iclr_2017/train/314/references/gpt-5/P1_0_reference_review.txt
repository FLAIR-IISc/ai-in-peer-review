Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a simple, goal-conditioned, supervised-learning formulation of sensorimotor control. Instead of learning value functions or policies from sparse scalar rewards, DFP predicts short-term future changes of a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) conditioned on current observation, a candidate action, and a goal vector. Acting reduces to choosing the action whose predicted measurements maximize the goal utility (linear in the paper).
- The model uses a perception CNN for raw RGB input, small MLPs for measurements and goals, and a dueling-style prediction head that outputs measurement-change predictions for all actions simultaneously, decomposed into expectation and action-advantage streams.
- Training is Monte-Carlo style regression on experience tuples with an experience buffer, both in fixed-goal and randomized-goal regimes. The goal is included because the policy used to collect future steps depends on it, so future outcomes are goal-dependent.
- Experiments on four first-person 3D Doom tasks show strong gains over DQN and A3C, particularly on harder combat tasks with multiple measurements; ablations indicate that vectorial measurements and multi-horizon prediction both help; training with randomized goals leads to good test-time generalization to new goals; the method also generalizes across environment textures and won the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch track.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- A clear, well-motivated idea that re-frames action selection as goal-conditioned future measurement prediction, capitalizing on dense intrinsic signals instead of sparse rewards.
- Strong empirical results on challenging 3D visuomotor tasks with careful ablations and compelling generalization (across environments and goals), plus an external competition win.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses two long-standing challenges—learning from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments and flexibly following changing goals. The motivation to exploit dense, multi-dimensional measurement streams is well articulated and ties to classic SL vs RL considerations and Monte Carlo vs TD debates.
- Positioning: The work is well placed with respect to Monte Carlo methods, multi-objective RL, universal value function approximators, and predictive representations. The goal-conditioning is closely related to UVFA; the measurement prediction perspective is a distinctive and pragmatic instantiation with raw pixels and multiple intrinsic signals. The dueling-style head is an appropriate architectural choice.
- Empirical support:
  - Benchmarks: Four Doom scenarios with increasing difficulty, moving from navigation to combat. The hardest two tasks (Battle/Battle2) are non-trivial, with partial observability, active adversaries, and richer action sets.
  - Baselines: DQN and A3C are strong and relevant; DSR is included where feasible. The authors use dense shaped rewards (aligned with measurement changes) for baselines, which is fair given DFP’s supervision.
  - Results: DFP matches A3C on the easiest task, is clearly superior on Navigation, and dominates on combat (up to 6x frags). The gap widens when multiple measurements are present, directly supporting the core claim that exploiting vectorial, dense signals is advantageous.
  - Generalization: Training on texture-randomized maps leads to robust cross-environment transfer; higher-capacity variants further improve. Randomized-goal training produces models that adapt to diverse test-time goals with little degradation relative to specialized training.
  - Ablations: Predicting multiple measurements and multiple horizons each improves performance; both together yield the best results. This gives credible causal evidence for the design choices.
  - Practicality: The method trains fast (millions of frames/day), is simple to implement, and performed strongly in an external competition, suggesting robustness.
- Rigor and clarity: The formulation is precise; the policy-dependence of future outcomes justifies conditioning on the goal. The loss and architecture are described clearly. Learning curves average across seeds; test evaluations use 1M frames. Some implementation details are deferred to appendices, but the main narrative is coherent.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Fairness and breadth of baselines:
  - Consider adding A3C variants with dueling heads or improved value/policy heads to match architecture capacity, and/or PPO if possible. Clarify whether image pre-processing, frame stacking, and action sets were identical across methods.
  - Include data-efficiency comparisons (frames to reach certain performance) in addition to end-of-training performance.
- Off-policy considerations: Clarify the extent of off-policy bias. The training target for a state-action pair uses Monte Carlo returns under a behavior policy that follows g thereafter. Since the network predicts for all actions but the loss is computed for the executed action only, discuss whether you explored any counterfactual training (e.g., importance weighting, model rollouts).
- Measurement design and scaling: Discuss sensitivity to which measurements are chosen and to their normalization. What happens when adding irrelevant or noisy measurements?
- Goal generalization:
  - Provide more systematic sweeps over goal vectors (e.g., random goals outside [−1,1], or out-of-distribution weightings) and report the resulting Pareto trade-offs among measurements.
  - Analyze behavior for conflicting goals (e.g., health vs ammo conservation) and how quickly the policy adapts within an episode when goals change.
- Partial observability and memory: The current controller is reactive. Including simple memory (frame stacking, LSTM) might substantially help; a brief experiment or discussion of preliminary results would strengthen the case.
- Horizons: Justify the choice of offsets and weighting; include a sensitivity analysis of horizons and weights vs performance/stability.
- Clarity and reproducibility: Please clean up reference formatting and typos; ensure Appendix A/B fully specify optimizer, LR schedules, replay size, epsilon schedule, normalization/statistics of measurements, and network sizes. Consider releasing code.

Questions for the authors
1) Training targets and off-policy effects: For each tuple (o, a, g), you regress to f computed under the subsequent policy conditioned on g. Do you ever train the outputs corresponding to non-executed actions at that state? If not, how critical is the shared action stream plus normalization to generalize to unseen actions?
2) Experience memory: What is the replay size M relative to episode length and horizon? Did you test larger replay buffers, prioritized replay, or target networks to stabilize longer-horizon prediction?
3) Horizon choice: Why 1,2,4,8,16,32 with the last three weighted (0.5,0.5,1)? How sensitive are results to this choice? Did you attempt exponentially discounted horizons to align with classic returns?
4) Measurement scaling: How are health, ammo, and frags normalized across scenarios? What happens if measurements vary in magnitude or if you include additional measurements (e.g., velocity) during training?
5) Goal conditioning: At test time, how robust is performance to goals outside the training distribution? Can you interpolate and extrapolate along the goal simplex reliably?
6) Baselines: Did you match the DFP architecture (perception backbone and dueling head) in the A3C/DQN baselines? If not, how much of the gain might be architectural?
7) Sample efficiency: How many frames to reach A3C’s final performance on Battle/Battle2? Any wall-clock comparisons?
8) Failure modes: Where does DFP fail (e.g., long-term planning, exploration-heavy tasks)? Any qualitative analyses of errors in measurement predictions and their impact on action selection?
