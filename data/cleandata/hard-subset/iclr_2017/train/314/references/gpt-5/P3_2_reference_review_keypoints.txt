- Proposal: DFP predicts short-horizon changes in a vector of intrinsic measurements conditioned on observation, action, and goal, then selects actions by maximizing goal-weighted predicted measurements.
- Architecture: Goal-conditioned network with perception/measurement/goal encoders and a dueling-style head that outputs future-measurement predictions for all actions in parallel.
- Training: Monte Carlo regression on observed multi-horizon measurement deltas using an experience buffer and ε-greedy data collection, enabling fixed or randomized goals for test-time re-goaling.
- Empirical result: DFP outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) on ViZDoom tasks, generalizes across texture-randomized environments and goals, and won the Visual Doom AI Competition (Full Deathmatch).
- Decision: Accept.

Key reasons for acceptance:
- Novel, practical reformulation of control as supervised goal-conditioned prediction with clear motivation.
- Strong empirical results on challenging 3D tasks, including generalization and external competition win.
- Careful, reproducible architectural/algorithmic design that avoids TD bootstrapping issues.

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity: dense multivariate measurements and g^T F(o,a,g) decision rule enable zero-shot re-goaling.
- Practicality/stability: pure supervised regression produces fast, stable training compared to bootstrapped RL.
- Architecture: goal-conditioning and dueling decomposition help learn fine action differences.
- Generalization: demonstrated environment (texture) and goal generalization, including randomized-goal training.
- Evidence: consistent outperformance of A3C in harder scenarios and competition win.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Baseline fairness: A3C used a scalar reward while DFP uses full measurement vectors; stronger baselines with auxiliary measurement prediction are needed.
- DSR comparison: sample-budget mismatch (DFP tens of millions vs ~10M for DSR) weakens the outperformance claim.
- A3C details missing: LSTM presence, actor count, and architecture specifics can materially affect results in partial observability.
- Statistical rigor: only three seeds reported, no confidence intervals, and many results lack variance reporting.
- Training/target clarity: unclear whether loss is applied only to the executed action’s prediction or to all action heads; masking should be explicit.
- Temporal-offset choices: rationale for using only the last three horizons and their weights is not justified or analyzed.
- Generalization scope limited: environment generalization tested mainly via texture randomization, not structural changes like new mazes.
- Privileged measurements: measurements come from the HUD and are privileged; clarify whether baselines had access to the same signals as auxiliaries.
- Related work positioning: relation to UVFAs and successor representations should be deepened and compared precisely.

Additional feedback to improve the paper:
- Report standard deviations/confidence intervals and significance tests across seeds for learning curves and final metrics.
- Add an A3C (or PPO/A2C) baseline augmented with auxiliary measurement prediction (and optionally depth/segmentation) to isolate benefits of dense supervision.
- Explicitly clarify training targets and masking: state whether only the chosen action’s head receives loss and whether unchosen actions are supervised.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for temporal offsets/weights, experience memory size, ε schedule, and multi-horizon vs single-horizon design.
- Report wall-clock runtimes and computational budget (GPUs/CPUs, actor count) to contextualize steps-per-day claims.
- Strengthen generalization experiments with unseen maze topologies, randomized spawn logic, and report in-distribution vs out-of-distribution gaps.
- Fix bibliography formatting and ensure all related works are correctly cited.

Questions for the authors:
1. Supervision masking: when predicting all actions, do you apply loss only to the executed action’s slice, and have you tried multi-action supervision via model-based rollouts or counterfactuals?
2. Goal distribution: how sensitive is performance to the training goal distribution in the randomized regime, and does training with negatively weighted goals help generalization?
3. Temporal offsets: why weight only the last three horizons (0.5, 0.5, 1) and how do different offsets/weights affect stability and performance?
4. Partial observability: did you evaluate a recurrent DFP (e.g., with LSTM) and how does it compare in battle/partially observable scenarios?
5. Baselines: did you try A3C with auxiliary measurement prediction or with n-step returns matching your horizons, and if not, can you provide those results?
6. Measurement robustness: how sensitive is DFP to scaling/normalization of measurements and to adding irrelevant measurements?
7. Sample efficiency: relative to A3C, how many environment steps does DFP need to reach equal performance; please provide learning curves with matched compute.
