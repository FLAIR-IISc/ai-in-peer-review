1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning formulation of sensorimotor control that predicts vector-valued future measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) at multiple time offsets conditioned on current sensory input, measurements, and a goal vector. Action selection at test time chooses the action whose predicted measurement vector best aligns with the current goal via a linear utility. The approach reframes control from learning value functions or policies to supervised regression on future measurement differences obtained from raw experience, without extraneous supervision. The authors introduce an architecture with separate perception, measurement, and goal modules, and an action/expectation split akin to dueling networks to sharpen action discrimination.

Empirically, DFP outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C; DSR on a simpler scenario) on multiple ViZDoom scenarios, with the largest gains on more complex tasks with richer measurements. The method exhibits generalization to unseen textures and to dynamically specified goals at test time, including models trained with randomized goals that remain competitive on a target task and adapt better to new goals. An ablation shows benefits of vectorial measurements and multi-horizon prediction over scalar reward and single-horizon targets. A variant of the method won the Visual Doom AI Competition’s Full Deathmatch track.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- A clear, well-motivated alternative to standard RL—casting control as supervised multi-horizon measurement prediction—that is simple to implement and achieves strong, reproducible empirical performance, especially on complex 3D tasks with dense measurements.
- Demonstrated generalization across environments and goals, with ablations that support the central claims about vectorial feedback and multi-horizon prediction.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: Learning robust visuomotor control from raw pixels in 3D environments and flexibly pursuing varying goals, avoiding sparse-reward instability and enabling test-time goal changes.
- Motivation and placement: The paper situates DFP within supervised-learning vs. RL trade-offs (dense multidimensional feedback vs. sparse scalar reward), connects to Monte Carlo, UVFA, and predictive state representations, and leverages dueling-network ideas. The choice to predict measurement vectors rather than full sensory streams is practical and justified.
- Technical soundness: The training objective is standard regression to Monte Carlo targets, avoiding bootstrapping and stability issues associated with TD. The architecture is reasonable and grounded in prior findings. The use of experience replay and ε-greedy data collection is conventional, and randomly varying goals during data collection diversifies experience. While the predictor conditions on a goal vector, the target does not depend on the goal; the primary operational role of goals appears in data collection (behavior policy) and in test-time action selection. This is consistent and logically coherent, even if the conditioning itself is not strictly necessary for correctness of prediction targets.
- Empirical rigor: Comparisons to DQN and A3C are conducted over tens of millions of steps with some hyperparameter search; DSR is included on a simpler task with the authors’ note on training speed limitations. Metrics and scenarios are sensible; multiple runs are averaged; results on texture generalization and goal adaptation are informative; an ablation isolates the contributions of multi-measurement and multi-horizon prediction. The competition result provides an additional external validation.

Limitations:
- The inclusion of goal conditioning in the predictor is not theoretically necessary given that the targets are goal-invariant; its empirical necessity is unclear from current ablations.
- Baselines are limited to standard implementations; stronger RL baselines with auxiliary predictive tasks or multi-head objectives could narrow the gap.
- The approach depends on the availability and sufficiency of informative measurements; discussion of cases where measurements are poor proxies for long-term objectives is brief.
- Theoretical analysis of when Monte Carlo prediction of measurements suffices for optimal control is limited.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect the acceptance decision)
- Clarify the role and benefit of conditioning F on the goal g when training targets are independent of g. An ablation that removes the goal input to F (while still using g at action selection) would help disentangle whether goal-conditioning improves learning beyond its effect on data collection.
- Provide more details on target computation for multi-horizon differences: how are terminal steps handled (padding, truncation), and are measurements normalized per-scenario? Report sensitivity to the chosen horizon set and the weighting of the last three steps.
- Discuss data efficiency: learning curves normalized by frames to reach a fixed performance would complement the final metrics. Include wall-clock times for all methods in a consistent hardware setting if possible.
- Expand on off-policy aspects: since only the executed action’s head is supervised, do you observe drift in unvisited actions? Any regularization beyond the action-stream normalization? Report sensitivity to replay buffer size and update cadence.
- Consider including an RL baseline that leverages dense auxiliary prediction tasks (e.g., predicting future measurements as auxiliary losses) to separate the benefit of dense supervision from the specific DFP control rule.
- Limitations and scope: add guidance on designing measurement sets in domains where explicit “game stats” are absent; discuss robustness when measurements are noisy or partially observed.

Questions for the authors
- Is F’s dependence on g empirically useful? Can you report performance when g is excluded from the predictor input, both with fixed and randomized goals during training?
- How exactly are multi-horizon targets fi computed at episode boundaries? Do you clip horizons that exceed the terminal time, and with what effect?
- Are losses applied only to the head corresponding to the executed action ai, or to all action heads (e.g., via counterfactuals when available)? If only to ai, how do you mitigate under-training of rarely chosen action heads?
- How sensitive are results to the replay memory size M and the update interval k? Any signs of bias due to the small memory?
- Do performance gains persist if A3C/DQN are trained with dense shaped rewards derived from each measurement separately or with multi-objective/auxiliary heads?
- How are measurements normalized/scaled across scenarios, especially when combining them linearly for baselines?
- Can you quantify sample efficiency (frames to threshold) and provide variance across seeds for all methods?
- In the competition setting, what differences (if any) existed between the submitted model and the paper’s D4-tx-L regime (e.g., curriculum, data augmentation, exploration schedule)?

Overall, the paper is clearly written, technically coherent, well-placed in the literature, and supported by strong empirical evidence on challenging tasks.
