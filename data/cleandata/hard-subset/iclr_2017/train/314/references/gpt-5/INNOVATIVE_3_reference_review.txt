Summary
This paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): a goal-conditioned, supervised-learning alternative to standard reward-based RL. Instead of learning value functions or policies via Bellman backups, the agent learns to predict the vector of future “measurements” (e.g., health, ammo, frags) at multiple temporal offsets as a function of current sensory input, current measurements, candidate action, and a goal vector g. At test time it chooses the action maximizing g^T predicted future measurements, allowing flexible goal specification without retraining. The model uses an architecture akin to dueling networks with separate expectation and action streams, trains from raw experience with a small replay buffer, and operates directly from pixels. Experiments on ViZDoom show strong performance vs. DQN, A3C, and DSR, including substantial gains on harder tasks, generalization across textures and goals, ablations showing benefits of multivariate/multi-horizon prediction, and a first-place result in the Visual Doom AI Competition.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Conceptual novelty and simplicity: reframing sensorimotor control as supervised, goal-conditioned future measurement prediction is a bold and impactful direction that challenges the standard reward-centric RL paradigm.
- Strong empirical evidence: consistent gains over strong baselines on challenging 3D tasks, convincing generalization to unseen environments and to new goals at test time, and a competition win without extra supervision.

Supporting arguments
- What is tackled: Learning to act from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments, while supporting dynamically specified goals and leveraging temporally dense feedback signals beyond sparse scalar rewards.
- Motivation and placement: Well grounded in literature on Monte Carlo methods, predictive representations, multi-objective/goal-conditioned RL (UVFA), and successor representations, while clearly articulating the novelty: directly predicting intrinsic measurements rather than discounted returns or successor features, enabling flexible goals and dense supervision.
- Empirical support: Careful comparisons to DQN/A3C (equal training steps), ablations (vector vs. scalar, multi-horizon), environment randomization (textures), and goal-agnostic training all support claims. The method’s advantage scales with richer measurement streams, aligning with the central premise. The competition result further corroborates effectiveness.
- Practicality: The approach is simple to implement (regression with experience memory), fast to train in ViZDoom, and does not rely on privileged labels or human demonstrations.

Caveats (not fatal for acceptance given novelty)
- Scope: Experiments are confined to Doom. It remains unclear how DFP fares under very sparse signals (e.g., Atari with sparse rewards) where the “dense measurement” assumption may not hold. The ablation indicates performance drops when reduced to scalar reward.
- Stationarity/credit assignment: Targets depend on the evolving behavior policy (one-step action followed by current policy), akin to Monte Carlo estimates with a nonstationary target. The paper could better analyze this dependence and why it remains stable.
- Memory and partial observability: The model is purely reactive; tasks that require long-term memory may challenge DFP without recurrence.
- Baselines and design choices: A3C variants with memory or auxiliary tasks might narrow the gap; fairness details are mostly addressed, but more transparency would help.

Suggestions to improve the paper (for authors)
- Clarify the training target: After choosing action a at time t, the future measurement vector fi is realized under an ε-greedy policy that evolves over training. Please formalize the induced target (on-policy vs. off-policy), discuss bias, and whether a target network or policy lag helps stability.
- Analyze horizon design: You use fixed offsets {1,2,4,8,16,32} and weight only the last three. Could the offsets/weights be learned? How sensitive is performance to this choice?
- Measurement dependence: Provide experiments where the measurement stream is noisy, partially observed, or sparser (e.g., only frags with occasional events). How robust is DFP? Can auxiliary learned measurements (features) complement sparse intrinsic ones?
- Baselines: Include a stronger goal-conditioned baseline, e.g., UVFA with A3C or successor features (closely related to DSR) tuned at scale, possibly with memory. Also consider an A3C-LSTM baseline on D3/D4.
- Uncertainty and calibration: Since decisions hinge on dot products with predicted futures, report calibration/uncertainty (ensembles or heteroscedastic regression) and whether uncertainty-aware action selection helps.
- Sample complexity: Report training wall-clock, frames per algorithm, and performance-vs-data curves to assess efficiency, not just final scores.
- Generalization details: Specify the exact texture randomization protocol and any data augmentation. The D4-tx-L capacity increase improves generalization—please quantify parameter counts and capacity-performance trade-offs.
- Reproducibility and clarity: Release code/configs. Clean up several reference formatting issues and missing numbers (e.g., action count typo in D3/D4).

Questions for the authors
- How does DFP behave when new test-time goals conflict sharply with behavior seen during training (e.g., “avoid frags” after training that prioritized frags)? Are there failure modes?
- Can you decouple prediction targets from the changing behavior policy (e.g., by using a frozen/lagged policy to generate targets) and does that improve stability?
- Did you try adding memory (e.g., LSTM) to the perception or prediction module? Does DFP benefit as much as A3C does in partially observed settings?
- How sensitive is performance to the size of the replay buffer and ε schedule?
- Could DFP be extended to continuous control by predicting future measurements under parameterized continuous actions (e.g., via actor sampling or conditional flows)?
- How would you integrate learned auxiliary “measurements” (latent features) to reduce reliance on hand-specified measurement streams?

Overall, this is a fresh, compelling direction with strong evidence on challenging 3D control and promising generalization properties. Despite some open questions and scope limitations, the conceptual contribution and empirical results merit acceptance.
