1) Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): reduce sensorimotor control to supervised learning by predicting action-conditional future measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) at multiple horizons, conditioned on a goal vector g. At test time, select the action maximizing g^T prediction. This reframes RL with vector-valued dense feedback and goal-conditioning, avoiding bootstrapping and Bellman errors.
- An architecture with separate perception, measurement, and goal modules, and a dueling-style decomposition into expectation and action streams is introduced.
- Empirical results in 3D ViZDoom scenarios show strong gains over DQN and A3C, especially on complex tasks with richer measurements. The approach generalizes across environments (domain randomization) and across goals (goal-agnostic training). It won the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch track.
- Ablations indicate benefits of vector measurements over a scalar reward proxy and of multi-horizon prediction.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, consistent empirical gains on challenging 3D tasks, with compelling generalization to unseen goals and environments.
- A simple, well-motivated reframing of control as supervised future-measurement prediction that leverages dense, vectorial feedback; clear architectural and training design that others can replicate and build on.

3) Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Learning visuomotor control directly from raw pixels in complex 3D environments, with the ability to pursue dynamically specified goals and to generalize across environments and goals.
- Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The paper connects to supervised “forward model” ideas (Jordan & Rumelhart), Monte Carlo and TD learning, GVFs and UVFAs (Sutton et al., Schaul et al.), and recent deep RL in 3D. The key motivation is that dense, multidimensional measurements offer a richer supervisory signal than sparse scalar rewards, making direct prediction attractive. The paper situates itself appropriately and articulates the empirical argument for Monte Carlo–style training in this regime.
- Do results support claims and are they rigorous? Largely yes. The method outperforms DQN/A3C on four Doom tasks, with larger margins when more measurements are available—consistent with the core hypothesis. Generalization experiments across textures and goals substantiate the goal-conditional formulation. An ablation shows multi-measurement and multi-horizon predictions materially improve performance. The competition win further validates effectiveness.
  Caveats: (i) Comparisons do not include strong auxiliary-task baselines (e.g., A3C/UNREAL) that are known to benefit from predicting auxiliary signals, which could reduce the reported gap. (ii) Results are averaged over 3 seeds—common but limited; confidence intervals are absent. (iii) The domain is limited to Doom with shaped measurement streams; sparse-reward or delayed-objective settings are not tested, where TD methods might be favored. Nonetheless, within the stated regime (dense vector feedback, dynamic goals), evidence is persuasive.

Additional positives:
- The approach is sample/time efficient (fast simulation throughput; simple supervised updates).
- Goal-agnostic training yielding strong performance on unseen goals addresses an important practical need.
- Architecture choices (dueling-style split, multi-horizon) are justified empirically.

4) Suggestions to improve the paper (to help, not required for decision)
- Fairness/baselines: Include comparisons to A3C with auxiliary prediction tasks (e.g., UNREAL-like) that also leverage dense measurements; such baselines would more directly test whether DFP’s advantage stems from richer supervision or from the SL framing.
- Analysis: Report variance/CI over more random seeds; add learning curves with shaded error bands.
- Sparse-reward regime: Include at least one task with sparse/delayed reward to delineate the boundary where DFP may underperform TD methods.
- Ablations:
  - Measure how performance scales with the number and choice of measurements; test if predicting a learned low-dimensional feature set (learned cumulants) works similarly.
  - Study sensitivity to horizon choices and loss weights; include a principled schedule or rationale.
  - Quantify effect of the action/expectation stream decomposition via ablations.
- Exploration: Discuss how ε-greedy interacts with goal randomization; consider intrinsic exploration bonuses or curiosity to reduce dependence on ε schedules.
- Theory/connection: Make the link to GVFs/UVFAs more explicit. Under linear u(f; g), g^T F can be viewed as a goal-conditioned value predictor; clarifying this helps position novelty.
- Reproducibility: Consolidate hyperparameters (normalization of measurements, replay size, update cadence) and release training code/checkpoints if possible.
- Clarity: Tighten notation between mt, f, predicted horizons, and loss weighting; specify normalization procedures for measurements in each scenario.

Questions for the authors
- How would DFP compare to A3C with auxiliary losses that predict the same measurement vector (and possibly pixel control)? Do you expect the gap to persist if A3C receives equivalent auxiliary supervision?
- How sensitive is performance to the selection and scaling of measurements? What happens if you remove one (e.g., ammo) or add noisy/irrelevant measurements?
- Horizon design: How were horizons and loss weights chosen, and how sensitive are results to these choices? Could learned discounting or exponential weighting match or exceed fixed schedules?
- Goal distribution: In goal-agnostic training, how does the distribution over g affect performance on downstream goals? Is there negative transfer when sampling g from [-1,1] vs [0,1]?
- Off-policy/stability: You mention small experience memory with no adverse effects. How does replay size and target staleness (update cadence k) affect stability and performance?
- Exploration and sparse rewards: How does DFP fare on tasks with truly sparse/delayed rewards where measurements don’t provide dense supervision? Can DFP incorporate bootstrapping or eligibility traces to extend effective planning horizon?
- Can DFP be combined with recurrent memory or hierarchical options (as suggested in the discussion), and do you anticipate additive gains particularly in partially observable settings?
- Continuous actions: Would you model F over a discretization, or learn a parametric action-conditional predictor amenable to continuous optimization? Any preliminary results?

Overall, the paper makes a clear, practical point: in settings with dense, meaningful measurements, direct supervised prediction of future measurement vectors is a powerful and simple alternative to conventional RL, with strong empirical support.
