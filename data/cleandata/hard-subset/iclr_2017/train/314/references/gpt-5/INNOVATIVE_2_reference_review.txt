Summary and contributions
This paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a bold reframing of sensorimotor control from reward-maximizing RL to supervised learning on temporally dense, vector-valued “measurements.” The agent predicts, for each action and goal vector g, future changes in a low-dimensional measurement stream (e.g., health, ammo, frags) at multiple temporal offsets, and then greedily selects the action that maximizes the predicted goal-weighted outcome. This supports: (i) learning from raw visual input with a rich supervisory signal (no bootstrapping), (ii) goal-conditioning that enables test-time adaptation to unseen goals, and (iii) a simple training procedure akin to Monte Carlo regression with experience replay. Architecturally, the predictor uses separate expectation and action streams (à la dueling networks). Empirically, DFP outperforms DQN and A3C on challenging ViZDoom scenarios, shows strong generalization across textures and goals, benefits significantly from vectorial measurements vs scalar rewards, and won the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch track.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel, conceptually clear reframing of control as supervised, goal-conditioned future-measurement prediction with dense feedback; enables training stability and goal flexibility. This is a substantial conceptual contribution beyond incremental tweaks.
- Compelling empirical evidence: consistent improvements over strong baselines (A3C) on complex 3D tasks; robust generalization to unseen environments/goals; ablations that substantiate the central hypothesis (vectorial, multi-horizon prediction matters); and an external competition win.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets two hard challenges—learning from raw sensory input in 3D environments and goal flexibility. Moving from sparse scalar rewards to dense multivariate measurements is well motivated and supported by classic arguments about when SL can dominate TD learning in dense-feedback settings. The goal-conditioning ties naturally to UVFA and multi-objective RL, but here it’s operationalized via direct multi-horizon prediction rather than value bootstrapping, which is both simpler and, in these settings, highly effective.
- Positioning in literature: The paper situates itself well relative to TD vs Monte Carlo, UVFA (Schaul et al.), successor representations, predictive state representations, and recent deep RL in 3D settings. The specific combination—predicting intrinsic measurement deltas at multiple horizons, conditioning on a goal vector, and using a dueling-style architecture—appears novel in this context.
- Claims vs evidence: Results are clear and, for the most demanding scenarios, large: DFP substantially outperforms A3C (up to 6x frags) where measurement streams are richer, consistent with the central thesis. The goal-agnostic training experiments convincingly show that DFP can learn without a fixed goal and later adapt. The ablation study shows both the importance of vectorial feedback and multi-horizon prediction. Generalization across textures/maps is solid, and the external competition win further validates practical utility.
- Methodological rigor: While not theoretically heavy, the empirical methodology is sensible: multiple seeds (3), strong baselines, reasonable hyperparameter searches (especially for A3C), and comprehensive scenario coverage. The architecture is kept comparable to DQN to mitigate capacity confounds.

Weaknesses and concerns (non-blocking)
- Privileged measurement stream: The approach assumes access to informative, low-dimensional measurements (health, ammo, frags). This is natural in many embodied settings but not always available; the paper could analyze sensitivity to missing/less informative measurements or learning such measurements from pixels.
- Myopia vs long-term credit: The policy is greedy w.r.t. predicted multi-horizon changes and uses finite offsets (up to 32 steps). It would help to discuss how this trades off with longer-horizon dependencies and delayed effects. Yet the strong results suggest the chosen horizons are sufficient for these tasks.
- Limited memory/formalism: The agent is reactive (no recurrence or memory) and uses discrete actions. Extensions to recurrent policies and continuous control are suggested but not explored.
- Baseline parity: A3C can benefit from recurrence in 3D navigation; it is unclear whether an LSTM-augmented A3C was attempted here (the competition baseline used one). The authors do tune A3C and report best results, but clearer accounting of architectural parity would strengthen the case.
- Statistical reporting: Most plots aggregate three runs; confidence intervals and significance tests would add rigor.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Clarify the training targets and horizon choice: Why predict differences mt+τ − mt rather than absolute mt+τ? How were the temporal offsets and weights chosen, and how sensitive are results to these choices?
- Calibrate prediction vs control: Report measurement prediction accuracy/calibration and how it correlates with control performance. Uncertainty-aware prediction (e.g., distributional or quantile regression) may further improve robustness.
- Relation to successor features/UVFA: Provide a more explicit theoretical link. DFP resembles learning goal-independent predictive features whose linear scalarization yields values; comparing to successor features empirically would be informative.
- Fairness details: Specify network capacities used for baselines vs DFP (e.g., dueling heads, input preprocessing) and whether an LSTM variant of A3C was tested.
- Ablations: Include ablations on measurement availability (e.g., drop frags or ammo) and test-time OOD goals (e.g., negative weights, extreme magnitudes).
- Reproducibility: Consider releasing code, maps, and training scripts.

Questions for the authors
1) Measurement design: How sensitive is DFP to the choice and dimensionality of the measurement stream? What happens if one removes frags or uses noisier/less informative measurements?
2) Horizons: How does performance vary with the set of τ and their weights? Could learned horizon weights or exponentially spaced offsets improve results?
3) Myopia: Are there scenarios where DFP’s greedy selection under multi-horizon predictions fails due to long-delayed effects? Any comparisons to n-step/MC returns in Q-learning on these tasks?
4) Goal distribution: In goal-agnostic training, how were goal vectors sampled, and how does the training goal distribution affect test-time generalization?
5) Uncertainty: Did you explore modeling uncertainty in future measurements (e.g., heteroskedastic losses) and does it affect action selection?
6) Memory and recurrence: Have you tested a recurrent DFP to cope with partial observability? If so, how does it compare?
7) Baseline parity: Did you try LSTM-A3C or dueling-DQN baselines under the same visual processing pipeline? Any changes in the relative ranking?
8) Replay and off-policy: How sensitive is performance to the replay buffer size and update cadence? Any stability issues when scaling the buffer?

Overall, this is a fresh, impactful direction with strong empirical support. Despite some limitations, the conceptual novelty and demonstrated benefits merit acceptance.
