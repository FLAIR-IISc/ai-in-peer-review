1) Summary of contributions
- Problem: Learn sensorimotor control directly from raw perception in complex 3D environments while supporting dynamically specified goals.
- Key idea: Replace sparse scalar reward with a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) and train a goal-conditioned predictor to forecast future changes of these measurements for each action over multiple horizons. Action selection is greedy w.r.t. the goal-weighted predicted outcomes.
- Formulation: Direct Future Prediction (DFP) reduces control to supervised learning on experience tuples by regressing future measurement differences. Goals are linear weights over these future measurements and can vary at test time.
- Architecture: A simple perception backbone plus measurement/goal encoders; a prediction head split into expectation and action streams (dueling-style).
- Empirical claims: (a) Strong performance on ViZDoom tasks vs. DQN/A3C/DSR, with especially large gains on harder, multi-measurement scenarios; (b) generalization to unseen textures and to novel goals; (c) ablations show benefits of vectorial feedback and multi-horizon prediction; (d) winning the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch track.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Conceptual novelty and clarity: a simple, well-motivated alternative to reward-centric deep RL using dense, vectorial self-supervision that is goal-conditioned.
- Strong empirical evidence: substantial improvements on challenging 3D control tasks, convincing demonstrations of generalization across environments and goals, and informative ablations.

3) Supporting arguments
- What question is tackled? Whether dense, multidimensional measurement streams can be leveraged via supervised prediction to produce effective control and improved sample efficiency in complex visual environments, while enabling goal flexibility.
- Motivation and placement: The paper positions itself against sparse-reward RL and relates to predictive representations (GVFs), UVFA, multi-objective RL, and Monte Carlo vs. TD tradeoffs. The framing—predicting future intrinsic measurements as supervised targets, parameterized by goals—is timely and well justified. The connection to dueling networks for action-conditional prediction is appropriate.
- Do results support claims?
  - Competitive baselines: DQN and A3C are strong and widely used; DSR is included where feasible. DFP matches A3C on simple tasks and strongly outperforms on “Battle” settings (factors 2.5–6). These gains are consistent with the hypothesis that richer measurement supervision improves learning in complex settings.
  - Generalization: Training with texture diversity and testing on disjoint textures yields meaningful transfer. Goal-agnostic training with randomized goal vectors generalizes well to unseen goals and nearly matches dedicated training on the primary goal—strong evidence for the goal-conditioned predictor’s flexibility.
  - Ablations: Demonstrate that predicting multiple measurements and multiple horizons improves performance even when evaluation is on a single metric, consistent with the claim that vectorial, temporally dense targets are beneficial.
  - Practicality: Simple ε-greedy exploration and small replay suffice; training speeds are high with ViZDoom. Winning the Full Deathmatch track against a stronger, more complex baseline (RNN + auxiliary supervision) is compelling.
- Rigor and limitations:
  - Fairness: DFP benefits from predicting all measurement components, while RL baselines optimize a scalarized reward; auxiliary multi-task/value heads for RL baselines are not explored. Nonetheless, ablations within DFP indicate that the vectorial signal itself is a key driver. The magnitude of improvements on harder tasks makes it unlikely that modest baseline tuning would close the entire gap, but a UVFA-style baseline would strengthen the case.
  - Scope: Discrete actions only; short prediction horizons (up to 32 steps) and a purely reactive policy may limit applicability in tasks with long-term credit assignment or partial observability. No theoretical guarantees.
  - Reporting: Means are presented; more systematic reporting of variance across seeds and sensitivity to hyperparameters would improve rigor. Reference formatting has many issues.

Overall, the contributions are clear, innovative, and empirically strong, with results that substantively support the main claims despite some fairness and scope caveats.

4) Suggestions for improvement (to help, not decisive for acceptance)
- Baselines:
  - Include a UVFA-style goal-conditioned value baseline and/or A3C with auxiliary prediction heads for the measurement vector, to better isolate the effect of vectorial supervision versus algorithmic differences.
  - Report variance/confidence intervals across more seeds; detail exploration schedules and replay size sensitivity.
- Analysis:
  - Study sensitivity to prediction horizons and inclusion/exclusion of specific measurements; characterize failure modes when important aspects are omitted.
  - Evaluate recurrent variants for partial observability and compare to LSTM-based baselines.
  - Provide results with longer horizons or temporally abstract actions to probe long-term credit assignment limits.
- Scope and practicality:
  - Discuss how to choose measurements in new domains; what happens if measurements are noisy/sparse; can they be learned automatically (e.g., via learned auxiliary features/successor features)?
  - Comment on scaling to large/continuous action spaces (e.g., actor-critic over goal-conditioned predictions).
- Reproducibility and presentation:
  - Release code/configs; include full architecture hyperparameters in the main text or clear appendix. Fix reference formatting.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is DFP to the choice and quality of the measurement vector? If some key measurements are omitted or corrupted, how does performance degrade?
- Can you provide results with a UVFA baseline trained on the same measurement vector (e.g., goal-conditioned value function) and/or A3C with auxiliary prediction losses for each measurement?
- What is the impact of prediction horizon selection and weighting? Does extending horizons beyond 32 steps help on Battle 2?
- Have you evaluated recurrent variants of DFP, and do they close any gaps on tasks requiring memory?
- How does performance vary with replay buffer size and ε schedule? Any signs of instability/off-policy bias?
- Could you comment on scaling to continuous actions (e.g., using the predicted utility as a critic for an actor) and to larger action spaces?
