Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): a goal-conditioned supervised learning formulation for sensorimotor control that predicts future intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) at multiple temporal offsets given current sensory input, measurements, and a goal vector. Action selection is by greedy maximization of the predicted goal utility g^T F(o,a,g).
- Key claims: (1) dense, vector-valued measurement streams provide a rich supervisory signal that stabilizes training relative to sparse scalar rewards; (2) conditioning on goals allows training without a fixed target and test-time adaptation to new goals; (3) a simple Monte-Carlo-style predictor (no bootstrapping) with an expectation/action-stream decomposition learns effective policies from raw pixels; (4) the approach outperforms strong deep RL baselines on ViZDoom tasks, generalizes across textures and goals, and won the Visual Doom AI competition.
- Contributions include an architectural decomposition inspired by dueling networks for measurement prediction, an empirical study across four 3D tasks, generalization across environments and goals, and ablations showing the benefit of multivariate, multi-horizon prediction.

Decision: Accept
Reasoning: The paper offers a simple, well-motivated re-framing with strong empirical evidence on challenging 3D visuomotor tasks. The goal-conditioned prediction of dense measurement vectors is an insightful and practically useful alternative to bootstrapped value learning, with convincing results and broad potential impact.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The work is well-placed within SL vs RL trade-offs (Jordan & Rumelhart; Sutton) and multi-objective/goal-conditioned RL (UVFA; predictive representations; multi-objective RL). The central insight—replace scalar sparse rewards with dense intrinsic measurements, predict their futures, and condition on goals—is clearly articulated and technically distinct from TD methods and from successor representations in that it directly models finite-horizon measurement deltas without bootstrapping.
- Methodology: The formulation is clean, and the training objective (L2 regression on future measurement deltas across multiple temporal offsets) is easy to optimize. The expectation/action-stream split is a sensible inductive bias mirroring dueling architectures. The goal-conditioning mechanism is straightforward and effective. The approach avoids instability from bootstrap targets and off-policy corrections common in deep RL.
- Empirical evidence: 
  - Benchmarks: On ViZDoom, DFP matches or outperforms DQN and A3C, with particularly large gains on the harder Battle scenarios where measurements are richer (3D navigation + combat). The results are qualitatively corroborated by a competition win.
  - Generalization: Systematic tests across unseen textures and increased capacity show meaningful generalization; training on varied textures improves invariance, as expected.
  - Goal generalization: Training with random goals yields policies that perform competitively on a held-out primary goal and adapt to new goals at test time—an important property not typically available in standard RL formulations.
  - Ablations: Predicting multiple measurements and multiple horizons improves downstream performance (even when evaluated on a single metric), empirically supporting the “dense vector supervision” hypothesis.
- Broader implications: The approach is attractive for domains where meaningful intrinsic measurements are available (robotics, embodied agents) and where goals change. The practical simplicity (purely supervised loss; no target networks/bootstrapping) is compelling.

Caveats and points for improvement (did not preclude acceptance)
- Baseline fairness and tuning:
  - A3C details matter (number of workers, recurrence, frame stacking, reward clipping). The paper uses a third-party implementation and hyperparameter sweeps, but fuller disclosure (and stronger baselines such as n-step or auxiliary-task A3C/UNREAL) would strengthen claims.
  - DSR is under-trained due to speed constraints; the comparison is thus limited.
- Statistical rigor: Most plots show means over three runs. Please include standard deviations or confidence intervals, and report statistical tests for the main claims.
- Scope/assumptions: The approach presumes access to low-dimensional intrinsic measurements and that goals can be expressed as linear functions of their finite-horizon deltas. This is reasonable in many settings but limits applicability where such measurements are unavailable or long-horizon credit assignment dominates. The current agent is reactive; tasks needing memory or partial observability may require recurrence.
- Learning signal coverage: The loss supervises only the executed action’s prediction while the network outputs for all actions. This raises potential concerns about accuracy for rarely executed actions. An analysis of per-action prediction errors and the effect of the normalization layer would be useful.
- Exploration: The method uses epsilon-greedy; no intrinsic exploration bonus or uncertainty is leveraged. This may limit performance in sparse-reward or hard-exploration tasks.

Questions for the authors
1. Baselines:
   - A3C configuration: number of parallel actors, frame stacking, use of LSTM, reward clipping, and entropy coefficients for the best models? Did you evaluate n-step A2C with tuned advantages?
   - Did you try auxiliary tasks (e.g., pixel control, reward prediction) to give A3C comparably dense supervision?
2. Supervision target:
   - You train only on the executed action’s target while predicting all actions. Have you measured calibration/accuracy of predictions for non-executed actions? Any techniques (e.g., counterfactual estimates, double-head consistency, conservative penalties) to mitigate hallucinated action advantages?
3. Horizons and discounting:
   - How sensitive is performance to the choice of temporal offsets and their weights? Can you emulate geometric discounting by appropriate g and offsets, and does that match discounted RL baselines?
4. Goal generalization:
   - Beyond linear utility, have you explored nonlinear goal parametrizations (e.g., a small goal MLP), and does this improve adaptation to more complex preferences?
5. Uncertainty and risk:
   - Have you tried predicting distributions over future measurements (e.g., quantiles) or ensembles to capture uncertainty and enable risk-sensitive control?
6. Memory and partial observability:
   - Any experiments with recurrent perception modules? How do results change on tasks requiring memory (e.g., long corridors, occlusions)?
7. Applicability without explicit measurements:
   - In settings without readily available intrinsic measurements, can learned latent measurements (self-supervised features) replace the hand-defined vector, and how does that affect performance?

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Clarify training details: size of experience memory M, update lag k, ε schedule, optimizer settings, input preprocessing (frame stacking, resolution, grayscale), and network capacity vs DQN/A3C to ensure apples-to-apples comparisons.
- Report sample efficiency (performance vs environment steps) and wall-clock time; include variance across seeds.
- Provide calibration/error plots for predicted vs realized measurement deltas, by horizon and by action, to support the learning signal’s fidelity.
- Analyze failure modes where long-term planning is required; discuss extending DFP with recurrence or hierarchical options.
- Discuss connections to successor representations and goal-conditioned value functions more explicitly, highlighting when DFP is preferable (no bootstrapping; dense multivariate feedback) and when SR/UVFA may have advantages (longer horizons, transfer).
