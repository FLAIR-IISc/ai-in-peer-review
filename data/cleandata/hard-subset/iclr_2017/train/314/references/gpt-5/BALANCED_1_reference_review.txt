1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a goal-conditioned, supervised-learning approach to sensorimotor control that predicts future values of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) from raw sensory input and uses these predictions to select actions. Key ideas:
- Replace scalar reward with a vector of measurements and define goals as functions (linear in experiments) over future measurement differences at multiple time offsets.
- Learn a deep network that, given current observation, a candidate action, and a goal vector, predicts the future measurement deltas for that action; choose the action with maximal predicted utility.
- A dueling-style architecture separates an action-independent expectation stream from an action-dependent stream to sharpen discrimination among actions.
- Extensive experiments in ViZDoom across four increasingly complex scenarios, including goal-agnostic training and cross-environment generalization via randomized textures.
- Empirically, DFP outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C; and DSR on the simplest task), with particularly large gains on the more complex 3D combat tasks; a DFP variant won the Visual Doom AI Competition’s Full Deathmatch track.

Overall, the paper is clear, the core idea is simple yet insightful, and the empirical results are strong and practically relevant.

2) Decision: Weak Accept
Primary reasons:
- Strong, consistent empirical gains on challenging 3D visuomotor tasks, coupled with a simple, well-motivated formulation that broadens the design space beyond scalar rewards.
- Convincing demonstrations of goal-conditioning and generalization across goals and visually varied environments, with ablations supporting the importance of multi-measurement and multi-horizon prediction.

3) Supporting arguments
What is the problem? Learning effective visuomotor control policies from raw pixels in complex 3D environments, with the ability to flexibly pursue different goals at test time.

Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The paper grounds its approach in supervised prediction and Monte Carlo-style targets, arguing that dense, multidimensional feedback is advantageous over sparse scalar rewards. It connects to UVFA (goal-parameterized value functions), multi-objective RL, predictive representations, and dueling networks. The specific focus on predicting intrinsic measurements rather than rewards, and using these predictions directly for control, is a clear and novel design choice.

Do results support the claims? Largely yes. The method substantially outperforms DQN and A3C in navigation and especially combat scenarios. Goal-agnostic training shows competitive performance on unseen goals; texture-randomized training yields meaningful cross-environment generalization. Ablations show both multi-measurement and multi-horizon prediction help. The competition result adds external validation.

Caveats: (i) Baseline fairness could be discussed more rigorously (e.g., memory/recurrent A3C, frame stacking, reward shaping parity, tuning procedures). (ii) The approach relies on hand-specified measurement vectors and linear utility; the scope of tasks for which such measurements suffice is not fully explored. (iii) The method is reactive (no memory), and the paper notes this limitation. (iv) Theoretical analysis is minimal, although the paper positions the approach empirically.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Baseline details/fairness: Please expand on architectural parity (e.g., frame history, recurrent baselines, action set handling) and hyperparameter tuning budgets across methods. Consider including a strong recurrent A3C/IMPALA-like baseline for partial observability, or justify exclusion.
- Measurement design: Discuss how one selects measurements in new domains and the sensitivity to their choice and scaling. Provide experiments where only reward is available, or where some measurements are noisy/sparse, to probe robustness.
- Multi-horizon objective: The choice to predict 6 horizons but weight only the last three with coefficients (0.5, 0.5, 1) seems ad hoc. Include sensitivity analyses over offsets and weights, and report whether using all horizons or learned weights changes outcomes.
- Off-policy aspects: Clarify the learning dynamics with a small replay buffer and epsilon-greedy exploration. Are targets unbiased Monte Carlo rollouts for the taken action only? Any tricks (e.g., target networks) used to stabilize training?
- Labeling and loss: Make explicit that loss is applied only to the component corresponding to the taken action, and clarify whether there is any auxiliary loss on other actions (e.g., consistency or regularization).
- Sample efficiency: Report learning curves versus environment steps (not just wall-clock speed) and data efficiency relative to A3C; include confidence intervals.
- Scaling with action space: In battle tasks, the number of composite actions appears large. Discuss computational complexity of predicting per-action futures and any action-factorization alternatives.
- Generalization scope: Texture randomization is a nice step. Consider also layout randomization or evaluation on different 3D suites (e.g., DM Lab) to test broader generalization.
- Theory/connection: Relate DFP more formally to successor representations/features and generalized value functions, and clarify similarities/differences (e.g., linear goal composition).
- Clarity: Clean up references (numerous placeholders/incomplete citations) and provide a code release to strengthen reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- Action space: In D3/D4, how many composite actions are enumerated? How does runtime/memory scale with action count, and did you consider factorized policies or sampling candidate actions instead of exhaustive prediction?
- Targets and terminals: How are future measurement deltas computed near episode end when horizons exceed remaining steps? Do you pad with terminal values, bootstrap, or truncate?
- Loss application: Is the regression loss applied only for the taken action’s prediction in each sample? Any loss for non-taken actions (e.g., via counterfactuals or imagination)?
- Measurement normalization: How are measurements normalized across tasks (per-episode, global)? How sensitive are results to normalization and the coefficients used in the goal utility?
- Exploration: Beyond epsilon-greedy, did you try entropy regularization or goal-randomization schedules to improve coverage? Any catastrophic behaviors observed?
- Baseline setup: Did baseline A3C include recurrence or frame stacking? If not, how might that affect results in partially observable mazes? Could you report A3C with LSTM?
- Goal generalization: How far can g at test time deviate from the training distribution (e.g., negative weights not seen during training)? Any calibration analysis of predicted vs realized measurements under novel g?
- Competition setup: What exact goal function and network variant were used? Any domain-specific tweaks (e.g., frame skip, reward shaping analogous to measurements)?
- Sensitivity: Please provide ablations on replay buffer size, update frequency k, epsilon schedule, and network capacity.
- Extensions: Have you tried continuous action tasks (e.g., TORCS) via action-conditional regression over sampled actions or learned policy over the predicted utility?

Overall, this is a timely and impactful paper. With strengthened baselines, expanded sensitivity analyses, and clarified training details, it would make a strong ICLR contribution.
