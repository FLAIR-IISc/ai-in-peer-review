1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a goal-conditioned, supervised-learning formulation of sensorimotor control. Instead of optimizing a scalar reward via temporal-difference learning, the agent predicts short- to mid-horizon changes in a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) conditioned on the current observation and a goal vector, and selects the action whose predicted future measurements best match the goal (via a dot product). This converts control to supervised regression on a dense, multivariate target stream collected from raw experience. The architecture includes separate modules for perception, measurements, and goals, and a dueling-style prediction head that decomposes action-averaged expectations from action-specific deltas. The paper evaluates DFP on multiple ViZDoom scenarios, showing strong gains over DQN and A3C in more complex settings, generalization to unseen textures, transfer across goals when trained with randomized goals, and an ablation demonstrating that vectorial, multi-horizon prediction improves performance over scalar reward prediction. A DFP variant won the Visual Doom AI Competition’s Full Deathmatch track.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Conceptual novelty and clarity: a simple, general re-framing of control as supervised prediction of goal-relevant future measurements, enabling goal-conditioned behavior and transfer without additional supervision.
- Strong empirical results and practical impact: consistent improvements over strong deep RL baselines on challenging 3D tasks, convincing generalization and goal-transfer, and a competition win.

3. Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The shift from sparse scalar reward to dense multivariate measurement streams is well motivated, especially in immersive, partially observed settings. The paper connects to Monte Carlo prediction, multi-objective RL, and UVFAs, and positions DFP as a practical instantiation that predicts intrinsic measurements instead of values. The relation to successor representations is implicit; predicting goal-independent future measurement increments resembles learning goal-independent dynamics/feature expectations and combining them with goal vectors at test time.
- Methodology: The formulation is straightforward: supervised regression on future measurement differences at multiple horizons, goal-conditioning, and greedy action selection. The architecture is reasonable for vision-based control and the dueling-style decomposition is apt for distinguishing action-averaged effects from discriminative action differences.
- Empirical support:
  - Benchmarks: On D1/D2 (health), DFP matches A3C on the simple task and clearly outperforms on navigation. On D3/D4 (health/ammo/frags), DFP substantially outperforms A3C (up to 6× frags), aligning with the claim that vectorial, dense supervision is advantageous as complexity increases.
  - Generalization: Training with texture randomization enables nontrivial transfer to unseen environments; a larger-capacity model further improves results.
  - Goal-agnostic training: Models trained with randomized goals perform nearly as well as goal-specific training on the main objective and generalize better to new goals (including “pacifist” or ammo-drain goals), supporting the claim of flexible goal specification at test time.
  - Ablations: Predicting multiple measurements and multiple horizons both help, even when evaluation is on a single measurement (frags), supporting the central hypothesis that multivariate, dense signals are beneficial.
  - Practicality: The method trains quickly in ViZDoom and does not require extra supervision (e.g., privileged enemy labels) used by a strong competing approach.
- Scientific rigor: Experiments use multiple seeds and clear protocols, and comparisons are done on the same platform with matched reward definitions where applicable. While not theoretically grounded, the empirical evidence is substantial for the claimed setting.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Baselines and fairness:
  - A3C without memory is a handicap in a partially observed 3D FPS. Including LSTM-based A3C (or IMPALA/UNREAL-style auxiliary tasks) would strengthen comparisons, especially because DFP benefits from dense targets akin to auxiliary losses.
  - Consider a UVFA baseline (goal-conditioned value function) and/or successor feature/value transfer baselines to clarify how much of the benefit stems from goal-conditioning versus the supervised prediction of measurements.
- Measurement design and applicability: The approach assumes access to meaningful intrinsic measurements beyond reward. Please discuss applicability when such signals are not readily available (e.g., real robots without “health/ammo”), and sensitivity to measurement choice, normalization, and scaling.
- Analysis and ablations:
  - Provide ablation of the dueling-style head (E/A split) and the effect of the action-normalization layer.
  - Report sample efficiency (performance vs environment steps) and training variance across more seeds; include standard deviations in plots.
  - Clarify how horizon weights and the choice of temporal offsets affect performance; explore longer horizons or learned weighting.
- Scope and scalability:
  - Discuss computational scaling to larger discrete action spaces and to continuous control (e.g., parameterizing a over a grid vs actor-based selection).
  - Consider combining DFP with memory or recurrence to address partial observability; even a simple conv-LSTM could change the competitive landscape.
- Evaluation metrics: In addition to final-episode metrics, report cumulative return and survival time; include per-episode distributions, not only means.

Questions for the authors
- Measurement stream:
  - How were measurements normalized and scaled during training? How sensitive is DFP to these choices?
  - In settings without explicit “HUD-like” stats, what constitutes a viable measurement set? Have you tried learned measurement features (e.g., autoencoder or predictive features) rather than hand-selected ones?
- Baselines:
  - Did you evaluate A3C with LSTM or auxiliary losses (e.g., pixel control/reward prediction) that might reduce the gap? If not, can you comment on expected performance?
  - Can DQN/A3C be augmented with the same vectorial measurement targets as auxiliary predictions, and how might that affect the comparison?
- Goal-conditioning:
  - How does performance vary with non-linear utilities u(f; g) (e.g., quadratic costs or constraints)? Can the network learn to use such goals if provided?
  - At test time, how robust is action selection to goal vectors outside the training distribution (especially for single-goal vs randomized-goal training)?
- Training details:
  - What is the replay buffer size M and update lag k? Any observed instability from off-policy sampling?
  - How were ε schedules chosen, and how sensitive is performance to exploration?
  - How are episodes that terminate early handled in computing future differences fi at longer horizons?
- Theory and connections:
  - Can you formalize the relation between DFP and successor features/representations? Under what assumptions does DFP recover optimal policies when rewards are linear in measurements?
  - Is there evidence that the multi-horizon targets improve credit assignment beyond serving as additional supervision?

Overall, this is a compelling, well-executed paper with clear practical impact. Addressing the baseline and analysis concerns would further strengthen it.
