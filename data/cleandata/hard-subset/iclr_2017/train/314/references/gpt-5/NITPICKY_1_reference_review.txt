Summary and contributions
- Problem: Learn sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments, with the ability to condition on dynamically specified goals.
- Proposal: Reduce control to supervised learning by predicting future changes in low-dimensional “measurements” (e.g., health, ammo, frags) conditioned on observation, action, and a goal vector g. At test time, select the action whose predicted future measurements maximize g^T f. The network predicts action-conditional future measurements using a perception module, measurement and goal modules, and a dueling-style expectation/action-stream head with a zero-mean constraint for the action stream.
- Claims: (i) denser vector feedback yields faster/ more stable training than sparse scalar rewards; (ii) model generalizes across environments and goals; (iii) competitive or superior to strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) in ViZDoom; (iv) winning result in the Visual Doom AI Competition.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Clarity and reproducibility are insufficient: critical methodological and experimental details are missing/ambiguous, and references/formatting are error-prone. (2) Empirical validation has fairness gaps and limited ablations relative to claims, making it hard to attribute gains to the proposed ideas.

Supporting arguments
- Missing/ambiguous core details:
  - Action space in D3/D4: the text introduces 8 binary sub-actions “any combination” but omits the resulting number of actions (“resulting in possible actions.”). If all combinations are allowed, this is 256 actions; outputting predictions for all is nontrivial. If a reduced set is used, it must be specified. This is central for both computational cost and comparability to baselines.
  - Temporal prediction targets: τ = {1,2,4,8,16,32}, yet “only the latest three time steps contribute to the objective with coefficients (0.5,0.5,1).” Why predict the first three at all if they do not enter the loss? How were the coefficients chosen? No sensitivity analysis is provided.
  - Experience memory: size M, batch size N, update cadence k, optimizer specifics (except the Adam citation), input preprocessing/normalization, and goal sampling distributions at train/test are not fully specified in the main text. Many essentials are deferred to Appendix A, but even here several items remain vague in the excerpt.
  - Notation/definition inconsistencies: s_t vs s, m_t vs m; the normalization layer uses Ai on both sides of Eq. (6), which is ambiguous. “Expectation stream” is a misnomer since it computes a mean across actions, not an expectation under any policy.
- Baseline fairness and scope:
  - DSR is only evaluated on the simplest scenario due to speed; this does not support strong conclusions against that class of methods.
  - A3C/DQN hyperparameter tuning is limited and not clearly matched in capacity to the proposed model (e.g., dueling architectures, prioritized replay, frame-skip choices, action set coverage). The authors state the predictor was configured “as close as possible to DQN” but also introduce a different head and conditioning on goals/measurements; this complicates direct fairness.
  - No comparison to other contemporary strong baselines that also benefit from dense feedback or multi-task conditioning (e.g., UVFA-style A3C/PPO, dueling DQN variants). Given the claims about vector feedback and goal conditioning, comparisons to UVFA/goal-conditioned policies and successor features would be particularly relevant beyond a citation.
- Evidence and generalization:
  - “Generalization across environments” is mostly texture randomization (D3-tx/D4-tx) with fixed underlying layouts. This tests robustness to appearance rather than broader environmental variation (layout, dynamics). The competition result is compelling but external; the paper does not report ablations establishing which components were necessary to win.
  - Goal generalization is demonstrated on a small set of manually chosen goals in one scenario; no systematic coverage analysis (e.g., interpolation/extrapolation in g-space) or robustness to re-scaling/normalization of measurements.
  - Ablations are incomplete: while the benefit of vector vs scalar targets and multi-τ is shown, there is no ablation of the dueling-style head, goal conditioning at train vs test, measurement vs sensory inputs, or horizon length. The claim that measurement streams provide denser supervision would be strengthened by a controlled shaping experiment for the RL baselines.
- Overstatement and conceptual issues:
  - “Reduces RL to supervised learning” is overstated: exploration and off-policy distribution shift remain, and the approach optimizes a finite-horizon proxy (up to 32 steps) rather than long-term return; no analysis of horizon effects or tasks requiring long-term credit assignment or memory.
  - The term “Monte Carlo” is used loosely; there is no convergence discussion, and the method is off-policy with a replay-like buffer.
- Presentation/formatting and references:
  - Numerous typographical/formatting errors (e.g., “threedimensional,” “ofthe-art,” broken lists, missing action count).
  - References are frequently malformed (missing years, duplicated author tokens, odd “? \Q2016\E” markers, malformed titles and venues), and some citations are ambiguous or duplicated. This is below ICLR standards.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide a complete, reproducible experimental specification: action sets per scenario, frame-skip, observation preprocessing, optimizer settings, memory/batch sizes, ε schedule, test-time ε, normalization of measurements, number of environment instances, seeds, and wall-clock compute.
- Clarify the head design: rewrite Eq. (6) to avoid notational reuse, justify the “expectation” terminology, and ablate the benefit over a single-head predictor.
- Justify the choice of τ and loss weights; add a horizon sensitivity study (including longer horizons).
- Expand goal-generalization experiments with systematic sweeps in g, scaling/normalization tests, and zero-shot transfer to goals outside the training distribution.
- Improve baseline coverage/fairness: include a UVFA-style A3C/PPO baseline with goal conditioning; consider dueling DQN variants; ensure action sets and network capacities match.
- Fix references and proofreading; this currently distracts from the content.

Questions for the authors
1. What is the exact number of actions used in D3/D4? Are all 2^8 combinations allowed? If not, which subset? How is action-conditional prediction scaled computationally?
2. What are M (replay size), N (batch size), k (update cadence), optimizer and learning rate schedules, and ε schedules? What is test-time ε?
3. Why predict τ = {1,2,4,8,16,32} but only train on the last three? Did you try training on all, or different weightings/horizons?
4. How are measurements normalized across scenarios, and how is g scaled? Does performance depend on the relative scales of measurements?
5. What is the effect of the dueling-style head? Please provide an ablation.
6. How sensitive are results to the size of the action set (especially if action combinations are pruned)?
7. Can you report wall-clock time and compute resources for all methods to contextualize “steps per day”?
8. Beyond texture changes, how does the method generalize to new layouts/dynamics? Any experiments with procedurally generated mazes?
9. Did you attempt a UVFA or successor-feature baseline conditioned on goals for a more apples-to-apples comparison?
10. Will you release code and exact configs to ensure reproducibility?
