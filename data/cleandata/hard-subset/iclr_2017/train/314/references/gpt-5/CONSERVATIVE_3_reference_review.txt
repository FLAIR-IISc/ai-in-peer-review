Summary
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): instead of optimizing a scalar reward via TD/policy-gradient methods, the agent predicts future changes in a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) conditioned on the current observation, a candidate action, and a goal vector. Action selection is then greedy with respect to the predicted utility g^T f. The model is trained via supervised regression on multi-horizon measurement deltas. The architecture is a goal-conditioned convnet with an expectation/action-stream decomposition. Empirically, on ViZDoom scenarios, the method reportedly outperforms DQN and A3C (often by large margins in the more complex tasks), generalizes to unseen textures and goals, benefits from multi-measurement and multi-horizon prediction, and won the Full Deathmatch track of the Visual Doom AI competition.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Methodological concerns and insufficiently controlled comparisons to established baselines; (2) Limited novelty relative to existing frameworks (e.g., GVFs/UVFAs/successor representations) and lack of analysis on settings where RL is traditionally needed (sparse/delayed reward), leaving central claims insufficiently substantiated.

Supporting arguments
- Placement and novelty. The formulation closely echoes generalized value functions and universal value function approximators, as well as Monte Carlo prediction of multi-step returns. The paper acknowledges these links but does not cleanly delineate what is fundamentally new beyond an empirical instantiation in ViZDoom and a particular network decomposition akin to dueling networks. Without theoretical insight or broader demonstrations, the novelty feels incremental relative to GVF/UVFA/SR lines.

- Assumptions and scope. The central assumption is access to a low-dimensional measurement stream that cleanly determines the agent’s goals and can be predicted reliably at short horizons. This is strong and arguably problem-specific. Many tasks lack such privileged, dense, vectorial measurements aligned with goals. The method also trains on a short finite horizon (1–32 steps) and emphasizes the last 3 offsets; tasks demanding long-horizon credit assignment or sparse rewards are not evaluated. This undermines the paper’s broader positioning as an alternative to TD methods.

- Baseline fairness and rigor. While the authors use public implementations and report “best” results, the hyperparameter search for A3C/DQN appears limited and potentially mismatched, particularly given sensitivity in 3D partial observability. It is unclear whether frame stacking, action repeat/frame-skip, input resolution, reward clipping, and recurrent memory were uniformly matched. The proposed model is purely reactive, but some baselines (e.g., A3C-LSTM) are standard in 3D navigation/shooting; excluding recurrent baselines may handicap them. DSR is not compared on the harder tasks and is given a much smaller training budget. Only 3 seeds are used, with no variance/error bars or statistical tests.

- Attribution of gains. The strongest gains appear when multiple measurements are available and used as dense supervisory targets. Although baselines were trained with shaped rewards formed from the same measurement deltas, the paper does not disentangle how much of DFP’s advantage stems from (a) multi-measurement prediction, (b) multi-horizon Monte Carlo targets, (c) architectural choices (dueling-like split and normalization), versus (d) differences in optimization details (replay, target refresh, loss scaling). Ablations are informative but incomplete for attributing gains relative to canonical RL baselines.

- Generalization claims. The texture generalization experiments are welcome, but all remain within Doom, and evaluation is a single metric (frags) with limited analysis. The goal-agnostic training shows reasonable adaptation to linear goal changes, but this class of goals is narrow (fixed offsets, linear utility). There is no evidence for handling goals requiring long-term planning, non-linear utilities, or constraints.

- Scientific presentation. Important training details are deferred to the appendix; some choices (only last 3 offsets weighted, small experience memory, off-policy updates without correction) warrant more justification. References are inconsistently formatted and incomplete; this raises concerns about polish and reproducibility.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Strengthen baseline comparisons: include A3C-LSTM and frame-stacked inputs; document exact preprocessing and action sets; provide thorough HP sweeps for DQN/A3C; report variance over more seeds and include confidence intervals. Consider IMPALA/PPO-style actors or comparable parallelization for a fair wall-clock/sample efficiency comparison.

- Evaluate on tasks with sparse/delayed rewards and without dense measurement vectors to probe when DFP breaks down versus TD methods. A controlled benchmark varying horizon and reward sparsity would substantiate claims about when SL-style direct prediction is preferable.

- Provide ablations aligning with baselines: (i) remove the dueling-like split, (ii) single-horizon targets that match N-step returns used in RL, (iii) single-measurement vs multi-measurement with identical shaping for baselines and DFP. Report how performance changes.

- Clarify algorithmic relations: formalize connections to GVFs/UVFAs, successor representations, and multi-step Monte Carlo policy evaluation. Discuss bias/variance trade-offs and potential failure modes (off-policy drift, compounding prediction errors).

- Improve reproducibility: release code, configs, and seeds; standardize references; add architectural and optimizer details in the main text.

Questions for the authors
1) Baseline parity: Did you use frame stacking, action repeats, and identical observation preprocessing across all methods? Why not include A3C-LSTM, which is commonly used in 3D partial observability?

2) Hyperparameters and sensitivity: How sensitive is DFP to the choice of temporal offsets and the specific loss weights on the last 3 offsets? What happens with longer horizons (e.g., up to 128 or 256)?

3) Off-policy training: You use a small replay and update off-policy without corrections. Did you observe instability or bias with larger buffers or different ε-schedules? How does performance compare to fully on-policy variants?

4) Attribution of gains: Can you quantify the contribution of (a) multi-measurement prediction, (b) multi-horizon training, and (c) the expectation/action stream, by re-running A3C/DQN with analogous components (e.g., multi-head reward prediction, dueling, N-step targets)?

5) Goal generalization: Beyond linear utilities over fixed offsets, can DFP handle non-linear goal functions or time-varying horizons? How would you incorporate constraints or safety-critical goals?

6) Sparse/delayed rewards: On tasks where only sparse scalar reward is available and no informative measurements exist, how does DFP perform relative to A3C/PPO/TD3? Can you provide evidence on such settings?

7) Measurement availability: In domains without natural measurement streams, what is your prescription for defining measurements, and how robust is performance to noisy or partially observed measurements?
