1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning formulation of sensorimotor control in which the agent predicts vector-valued future measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) at multiple temporal offsets conditioned on current sensory input and a goal vector. Actions are selected by scoring predicted measurements under the goal at test time. This reframes control as supervised prediction rather than value-function or policy optimization. The model includes an architecture that predicts action-conditional future measurement vectors via a dueling-style decomposition (expectation + action-specific deltas). Key claims are:
- Dense, multivariate measurement streams provide richer supervision than sparse scalar rewards, improving stability and performance.
- Goal conditioning enables training with randomized goals and test-time adaptation to unseen goals.
- Empirically, DFP outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C) on a suite of 3D visuomotor Doom tasks, especially the more complex ones, and generalizes across textures and goals.
- An ablation shows benefits from multi-measurement prediction and multi-horizon prediction. A DFP-trained agent won the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch track.

The paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and places the idea within related work on UVFA, predictive representations, Monte Carlo, and successor-like methods.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical evidence that a simple, well-motivated supervised prediction approach can outperform state-of-the-art deep RL baselines on challenging 3D control tasks and generalize across goals and environments.
- Clear methodological contribution: goal-parameterized future measurement prediction that decouples supervision from reward sparsity and supports test-time goal variation; plus an effective architecture and thorough ablations.

3. Supporting arguments
What is the problem tackled? Learning sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments with dynamically specified goals. The standard RL formulation struggles with sparse rewards and goal flexibility; this work asks whether dense, multivariate measurements can be directly predicted to drive action selection.

Is the approach well-motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The argument that dense multivariate feedback is a more natural supervision signal than occasional scalar reward is compelling for embodied settings. The paper connects to: Monte Carlo vs TD learning, predictive state representations, generalized value functions (Sutton et al.), and UVFA (Schaul et al.). The relationship to successor representations/parameters (Kulkarni et al. DSR) is acknowledged; DFP differs by directly predicting future measurement vectors at multiple horizons and combining them with goal weights at decision time, rather than learning a scalar value or successor map to a fixed reward.

Do the results support the claims?
- Comparative performance: On four Doom scenarios (Basic, Navigation, Battle, Battle 2), DFP matches A3C in the easy case and substantially outperforms A3C and DQN in the harder cases (up to 6x frags in D3). The baselines use the same constructed reward (linear combination of measurement deltas), making comparisons fair on the objective. Training curves average over multiple seeds; final evaluation uses 1M steps.
- Generalization: Training on texture-randomized environments improves cross-environment performance; higher-capacity model further improves. This supports the claim that the approach can generalize across visual variations.
- Goal-agnostic training: Randomized goal training yields performance close to fixed-goal training on the main task and better adaptation to new goals, supporting the goal-conditioning story.
- Ablations: Predicting multiple measurements and multiple temporal offsets both improve performance, even when evaluation is on a single target metric (frags), substantiating the “dense, multivariate supervision” advantage.
- External validation: Winning the Visual Doom AI Competition’s generalization track against a strong LSTM-based DQN with extra supervision is a notable empirical endorsement.

Scientific rigor: The experimental design is sensible, uses common baselines, and reports training speeds and seeds. While not theoretical, the empirical case is strong and the analysis is careful about design choices (matching architectures to DQN where possible, reporting ablations). Some statistical reporting (error bars/significance) is missing but the performance gaps are large on the harder tasks.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily part of the decision)
- Clarify the connections to successor features more explicitly. DFP can be viewed as learning a goal-independent prediction of feature outcomes with a linear goal, akin to UVFA and successor representations; positioning relative to these lines could sharpen the conceptual contribution.
- Sensitivity analysis: Report how choices of temporal offsets and weighting coefficients affect performance; discuss guidance for choosing them in new domains.
- Statistical reporting: Include variance/error bars across seeds for the main results.
- Baseline breadth: Consider A3C-LSTM or memory-augmented baselines, given partial observability in 3D mazes. This would help isolate the benefit of the learning signal versus memory.
- Compute/data parity: Clarify any differences in wall-clock or sample budgets between methods; you report steps/day, but a brief discussion on effective compute would strengthen fairness claims.
- Complexity and scalability: Discuss the O(|A|) inference cost of predicting per-action futures. Could you factor or share more across actions or handle large/continuous action spaces?
- Robustness to sparse rewards: Provide a small negative result or discussion on scenarios where meaningful measurement streams are not available or are very noisy.
- Reproducibility: Consider releasing code and environment configurations.

Questions for the authors
- Action space cardinality: In D3/D4 you mention 8 sub-actions with any combination allowed. How many composite actions are actually used in the network’s output layer (w = |A|)? If it’s the full combinatorial set, how does training scale with |A|, and did you try restricting combinations?
- Temporal offsets and weights: How were the six offsets and the (0.5, 0.5, 1) weights chosen? How sensitive is performance to these choices, and did you try discounting schemes?
- Measurement selection: How critical is the exact set of measurements (health, ammo, frags)? If you remove or corrupt some measurements, how does performance degrade? Could learned feature measurements (learned predictors) substitute for hand-chosen game stats?
- Goal distribution: For randomized-goal training, what distributions were used (exact ranges, normalization)? How does training with negative weights affect behavior and stability?
- Baselines: Which A3C variant was used (feedforward vs recurrent, frame stacking)? Did you explore n-step returns and entropy tuning to match the effective temporal horizons used by DFP?
- Off-policy effects: You use a small replay buffer and ε-greedy behavior. Did you observe any instability from off-policy training, and how does buffer size affect results?

Overall, this is a clear, impactful empirical paper with a simple idea that works surprisingly well, and it addresses an important practical pain point in RL. I recommend acceptance.
