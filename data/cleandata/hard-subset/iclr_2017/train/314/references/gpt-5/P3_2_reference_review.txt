Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): a supervised learning approach to sensorimotor control that predicts short-horizon changes in a vector of intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) conditioned on the current observation, a candidate action, and a goal vector. Action selection maximizes the goal-weighted predicted measurements.
- The model uses a goal-conditioned network with perception, measurement, and goal encoders and a dueling-style prediction head (expectation + action-specific deviations) that outputs future-measurement predictions for all actions in parallel.
- Training is Monte Carlo-style regression on observed future measurement deltas at multiple horizons, using an experience buffer and ε-greedy behavior. Goals can be fixed or randomized across episodes, enabling test-time re-goaling.
- Empirically, DFP outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C, and a DSR implementation) on visually rich ViZDoom scenarios, especially on the more complex battle tasks. It generalizes across texture-randomized environments and across goals, and an ablation suggests multi-measurement, multi-horizon prediction is beneficial. A DFP variant won the Visual Doom AI Competition (Full Deathmatch).

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Clear, simple, and well-motivated reformulation of control as supervised goal-conditioned prediction that is novel in its practicality and scope; (2) Strong empirical results on challenging 3D tasks, including generalization across goals and environments, and an external competition win; (3) Careful architectural/algorithmic design that is easy to reproduce and shows advantages over standard TD-based methods in this setting.

Supporting arguments
Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: The shift from sparse scalar rewards to dense vector measurements is compelling and well grounded in prior ideas (predictive representations, UVFAs). The decision rule g^T F(o,a,g) is elegant and supports zero-shot re-goaling.
- Practicality and stability: Pure supervised regression to multivariate, dense targets is simple, avoids bootstrapping instability, and appears to train fast and robustly.
- Architecture: The goal-conditioning and dueling-style decomposition are well-motivated and likely important for learning fine action differences.
- Generalization: Demonstrations of both environment (texture) generalization and goal generalization (including randomized-goal training) are valuable and relatively rare in 3D visuomotor work.
- Evidence: Consistent outperformance of A3C in harder scenarios, and a win in a competitive benchmark, support the method’s effectiveness.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Fairness of baselines: (i) A3C is evaluated with a scalar reward (linear combination) while DFP enjoys richer supervision via the full measurement vector. A stronger baseline would include A3C with auxiliary tasks predicting those measurements (e.g., UNREAL-style) or multi-task/value-decomposition variants; (ii) The DSR comparison is not matched in sample budget (tens of millions steps for DFP vs ~10M for DSR) due to implementation speed, weakening the claim that DFP “significantly outperforms” DSR; (iii) Details on A3C architecture (with/without LSTM) and number of parallel actors are crucial, as they substantially affect performance in partially observable 3D tasks.
- Statistical rigor: Only three seeds are reported and no confidence intervals are provided. Some results report means without variance; harder scenarios tend to be high-variance.
- Target definition and training clarity: The paper oscillates between “predict all actions at once” and a per-sample loss L(F(o,a,g), f). If the network outputs per-action predictions, is the loss masked to the executed action only? This should be explicit. Also, the choice of time offsets and the weighting of only the last three horizons need justification/sensitivity analysis.
- Scope of generalization: Environment generalization is primarily texture randomization on fixed layouts; it would be useful to separate visual vs structural generalization (new mazes).
- Use of privileged measurements: While the measurements are available on the game HUD (hence reasonable), they are still privileged structure. It is worth clarifying whether baselines had access to the same vector as auxiliary signals (not only via the scalar reward).
- Related work positioning: The relation to UVFAs and successor representations could be deepened. DFP can be viewed as learning action-conditional multi-step feature expectations, with linear evaluation under g; this is close in spirit to successor features. A more precise comparison would strengthen the paper.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide standard deviations or confidence intervals over seeds for learning curves and final metrics; include significance tests for key claims.
- Add a baseline with A3C (or PPO/A2C) augmented with auxiliary prediction of the measurement vector (and optionally depth/segmentation if available) to isolate the benefit of dense multivariate supervision.
- Clarify training targets: explicitly state whether only the chosen action’s head receives loss per sample, how masking is implemented, and whether unchosen actions are ever supervised (e.g., via off-policy counterfactuals, which seem unavailable).
- Report sensitivity to the choice of temporal offsets, their weights, experience memory size, ε schedule, and the multi-horizon vs single-horizon design.
- Report wall-clock times and computational budget (GPUs/CPUs, actor count) to contextualize “steps per day.”
- Strengthen generalization experiments by including unseen maze topologies and/or randomized spawn logic; report performance gaps relative to in-distribution.
- Clean up the bibliography formatting (many broken citations) and ensure all key related works are correctly cited.

Questions for the authors
1. Supervision masking: When predicting all actions, do you apply loss only to the executed action’s slice? If so, have you tried multi-action supervision via model-based rollouts or counterfactual estimates?
2. Goal distribution: How sensitive is performance to the training distribution over goals in the randomized regime? Does training with negatively weighted goals (penalizing frags) consistently help generalization?
3. Temporal offsets: Why only the last three horizons weighted (0.5, 0.5, 1)? How do different offsets/weights affect stability and performance?
4. Partial observability: Did you evaluate a recurrent DFP (e.g., with LSTM)? How does it compare, especially in the battle scenarios?
5. Baselines: Did you try A3C with auxiliary prediction of the measurement vector, or with n-step returns that match your horizons? If not, can you provide results?
6. Measurements: How robust is DFP to scaling/normalization of measurements and to adding irrelevant measurements? Does performance degrade gracefully?
7. Sample efficiency: For equal performance, how many steps does DFP need relative to A3C? Please include learning curves with matched compute.
