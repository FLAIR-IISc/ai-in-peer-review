Summary and contributions
- Problem: How to learn sensorimotor control from raw sensory input in complex 3D environments without relying on sparse rewards or fixed goals.
- Approach: Replace standard reward-centric RL with supervised learning that predicts future “measurements” (low-dimensional intrinsic signals such as health, ammo, frags) conditioned on the current observation, candidate action, and a goal vector. At test time, select the action that maximizes the predicted goal utility. The predictor is a deep network with an image perception module, measurement and goal modules, and a dueling-style prediction head that outputs per-action future measurement vectors at multiple horizons. Training is Monte Carlo (no bootstrapping), using a small replay buffer and ε-greedy exploration.
- Claims: (1) Dense, vectorial measurement prediction provides a rich supervisory signal that accelerates and stabilizes learning; (2) The method generalizes across environments and dynamically specified goals; (3) It outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) in ViZDoom scenarios; (4) Multiple measurements and multi-horizon prediction are beneficial; (5) The approach won the Visual Doom AI Competition (Full Deathmatch) without extra supervision.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: Strong empirical performance and compelling simplicity. The paper presents a clear, well-motivated alternative to reward-centric RL that enables goal-conditioned control and shows superior results on challenging 3D tasks, with solid ablations and generalization studies.

Supporting arguments
1) What problem is tackled? The paper asks whether sensorimotor control can be cast as supervised learning by predicting the consequences of actions on intrinsic low-dimensional measurements, and whether such predictions suffice to act effectively in complex partially observed 3D environments. This is clearly posed and practically important.

2) Motivation and placement in literature: The work is well motivated by the limitations of sparse scalar rewards and by the availability of dense intrinsic signals. It connects to:
- Monte Carlo vs TD learning (no bootstrapping, supervised regression on realized futures).
- Goal-conditioned value functions/UVFA and predictive representations, but here the target is a vector of future measurement deltas rather than a scalar value/reward.
- Multi-objective RL via linear utilities over measurement vectors.
The paper cites relevant antecedents (Sutton’s predictive representations, UVFA, successor features, multi-objective RL), and explains how its formulation differs (direct multivariate future prediction with parameterized goals and raw sensory input). The positioning is appropriate.

3) Do the results support the claims?
- Benchmarks: On four ViZDoom scenarios of increasing difficulty, DFP matches or exceeds DQN and A3C. Gains are substantial in the harder Battle/Battle2 tasks where measurement vectors are richer. Training curves are averaged over multiple seeds; final results are averaged over 1M steps; training speed is reported. While DSR comparisons are limited by speed, the main baselines are strong and properly tuned.
- Generalization: Training across many texture variants yields strong transfer to unseen textures; higher-capacity models further improve generalization. Goal-agnostic training (random goals per episode) produces models that perform almost as well on the “main” goal and generalize better to new goals, including antagonistic ones (e.g., “pacifist”).
- Ablations: Predicting multiple measurements and multiple horizons both improve frag performance even when evaluated by a single metric, supporting the claim that dense, vectorial supervision is beneficial.
- Competition result corroborates real-world generalization and robustness.
Overall, the empirical evidence is thorough and aligns with the claims.

Caveats and limitations (do not change my recommendation)
- Fairness of baselines: A3C is strong and tuned, but no explicit goal-conditioned RL baseline (e.g., UVFA-style conditioning on goal with value prediction) is included. Still, the primary claim is not only goal-conditioning but the direct measurement-prediction formulation, and the comparisons to strong non-goal-conditioned RL are informative.
- Dependence on available measurements: The method presumes access to informative, dense measurement vectors (e.g., health, ammo). This may be task-dependent; the paper acknowledges this design choice.
- Reactivity and partial observability: The model is feedforward on a single frame. Memory-free agents can be disadvantaged in 3D POMDPs; integrating recurrence or memory might further improve results (discussed as future work). Baselines appear similarly memory-light, which is fair, but this limits conclusions about long-horizon planning.
- No accuracy/ calibration analysis of prediction quality; the method optimizes action choice via predicted utilities without assessing prediction error distribution.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Add a UVFA-like baseline that predicts goal-conditioned scalar value (or successor features) from the same inputs to directly separate the benefits of vectorial measurement prediction from goal-conditioning.
- Report variance/error bars and number of seeds for all final test metrics; include statistical tests where feasible.
- Clarify label construction: for each time t, are targets f computed under the behavior policy after taking action a_t? Confirm that only the slot corresponding to the executed action receives loss.
- Analyze prediction quality: report calibration or correlation between predicted and realized measurement vectors per action; show whether improvements stem from better ranking or better absolute prediction.
- Sensitivity: provide ablations on choice of horizons, weights (0.5, 0.5, 1), and replay buffer size; discuss sample efficiency curves (performance vs. steps) explicitly.
- Partial observability: include a recurrent variant (and recurrent A3C) to assess how memory interacts with DFP.
- Discuss measurement selection: guidance for choosing measurement sets; robustness to noisy or partially observed measurements.

Questions for the authors
1) Targets and policy dependence: Are future measurement deltas f computed under the behavior policy after executing a_t at time t? How sensitive is DFP to off-policy data when goals change between episodes?
2) Supervision for non-executed actions: Since the network outputs per-action predictions, is the loss applied only to the executed action’s slot? Did you explore techniques to infer counterfactual labels (e.g., model-based rollouts) to enrich supervision?
3) Horizons and weights: How were τ and the loss weights chosen? Did you try longer horizons or learned weighting? How does performance change with different horizon sets?
4) Goal generalization: How far can the method extrapolate to goal vectors outside the training distribution (e.g., large magnitudes, opposing signs)? Any failure modes?
5) Measurement design: What principles guide which measurements to include? Have you tested with noisy or partially missing measurements, or with auto-learned latent measurements?
6) Calibration: Are predicted futures calibrated across actions/time? Does calibration correlate with control performance?
7) Comparison to UVFA/successor features: Can you comment on empirical differences if you implement a UVFA baseline with the same architecture and inputs?
