Summary
- This paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): a goal-conditioned, supervised-learning approach to sensorimotor control that predicts vector-valued future measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) at multiple horizons for each action, and chooses actions by maximizing goal-weighted predicted outcomes g^T F(o, a, g). This reframes control from reward maximization to supervised prediction with dense, multidimensional targets, enabling goal changes at test time.
- The model uses a perception/measurement/goal encoder and a dueling-style prediction head (expectation + per-action deltas). Training is simple regression with a small replay buffer and ε-greedy exploration.
- On multiple ViZDoom scenarios, DFP matches or exceeds DQN/A3C and strongly dominates on the harder, richer-measurement settings; shows generalization across textures and to unseen goals; ablations support benefits of vector feedback and multi-horizon prediction; and the method won the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch track against stronger, more supervised baselines.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Conceptually bold and novel reframing of control as goal-conditioned supervised future prediction, advancing a promising direction beyond standard TD/policy-gradient RL; (2) Strong empirical evidence, including state-of-the-art results on challenging 3D tasks, generalization to new goals/environments, and a competition win, despite a simple, scalable training procedure.

Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: How to learn sensorimotor control directly from raw observations in complex 3D environments without relying on sparse scalar rewards, and how to support dynamically specified goals at test time.
- Motivation and placement: Well grounded. The paper connects to Monte Carlo methods, generalized value functions (Horde), UVFA, multi-objective RL, and predictive models. The key insight—using temporally dense, vector measurements as supervision—addresses classic instability/sparsity issues in deep RL and enables goal-parameterization naturally.
- Empirical support:
  - Benchmarks: Four ViZDoom tasks from easy to hard; consistent and transparent training setups.
  - Results: DFP ≈ A3C on easy tasks; large margins on complex tasks with multiple measurements (6x and 2.5x in frags). This aligns with the claim that dense vector supervision helps most when the environment supplies multiple informative measurements.
  - Generalization: Training on texture variations yields robust transfer; goal-randomization training enables strong adaptation to new test-time goals, nearly matching dedicated training on the original goal.
  - Ablations: Multi-measurement and multi-horizon predictions both improve performance, even when evaluation is on a single metric, supporting the central thesis.
  - External validation: Competition win against a stronger, more supervised agent underscores practical impact.
- Rigor/limitations:
  - Baseline tuning is reasonable but not exhaustive; DSR comparisons are constrained by speed. Still, the core contributions (goal-conditioning, vector predictions, dense supervision) are validated by multiple angles and ablations.
  - The approach currently relies on hand-chosen measurements and action enumeration; theoretical analysis is limited; policy is reactive (no memory). The paper acknowledges these and outlines clear future directions.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Strengthen the connection to standard RL: give an explicit construction showing how discounted return is recovered via τ and g, and discuss approximation quality when τ is finite.
- Sample efficiency: report learning curves normalized per environment steps (e.g., frags per million steps) and final wall-clock throughput. Clarify inference/training fps and action-space size in D3/D4.
- Baselines: provide more details on A3C hyperparameter search (entropy, clipping, LR schedules) and, if feasible, include a stronger auxiliary-task baseline (e.g., UNREAL-style) or successor features with goal-conditioning.
- Architecture: add an ablation of the expectation/action-stream split and the action-stream normalization.
- Measurement design: analyze sensitivity to measurement selection. What happens with extraneous/noisy measurements, or if you remove health/ammo and train on frags only (very sparse)?
- Uncertainty: consider predicting distributions (quantiles or variance) over future measurements; greedy selection over means can be risk-seeking or brittle.
- Scalability: discuss computational cost when |A| is large or continuous; outline factorization or sampling strategies, and connections to continuous control.
- Partial observability: a recurrent or memory-augmented variant would likely improve performance; a brief experiment (even qualitative) would be informative.
- Reproducibility: releasing code and the texture-variant environments would amplify impact.

Questions for the authors
- Goal-conditioning: How exactly are g and measurements normalized during randomized-goal training? Do you rescale f online to keep g^T f comparable across episodes?
- Baseline fairness: How extensive was A3C tuning (entropy coefficient, gradient clipping, LR schedules, frame skip)? Can you provide a table of tried configs and the best achieved performance?
- DSR comparison: Given the step deficit, can you normalize by environment steps (not wall-clock) and report whenever DSR plateaus? Alternatively, include a note clarifying the ceiling it reached on Basic by steps, not days.
- Action space: What is the exact number of composite actions in D3/D4, and what is the per-step inference cost for predicting all actions? Any pruning or factorization?
- Sparse-only setting: If you restrict measurements to frags alone (removing health/ammo), how does DFP compare to A3C? This would isolate the benefit of vector densification.
- Robustness: How sensitive is performance to replay memory size and update lag k? Any instability observed with larger off-policy buffers?
- Horizons: Why weight only the last three τ in the loss? How sensitive are results to the τ set and weights?
- Failure modes: Any evidence of exploiting idiosyncrasies in measurements (e.g., ammo oscillations) rather than genuine skill? How did you check for this?
