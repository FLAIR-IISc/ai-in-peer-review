Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): instead of optimizing a scalar reward via TD methods, train a supervised predictor F(o, a, g) of future measurement differences f = [m_{t+τ} − m_t] at multiple horizons, conditioned on a goal vector g, then act by argmax_a g^T F. This reframes sensorimotor control as supervised learning using dense, multivariate, self-supervision from measurements.
- The architecture ingests raw pixels, current measurements, and goals; uses a dueling-style split into an expectation stream and an action-conditional stream that outputs predicted future measurements for all actions.
- Experiments in ViZDoom (four scenarios) show DFP matches or outperforms DQN/A3C, especially in complex settings with multiple measurements. The method appears to generalize across texture variations and across goals (goal-agnostic training). An entry based on this approach reportedly won the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch track.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Insufficient experimental rigor and fairness of baselines, with many crucial training details omitted, making it difficult to verify claims. (2) Presentation quality is well below venue standards: numerous reference errors, missing or inconsistent methodological details (e.g., action counts, normalization, horizon weighting), and ambiguity that materially affects reproducibility and interpretation.

Supporting arguments
- Clarity and completeness of method
  - The target construction f and the loss: τ = {1,2,4,8,16,32}, but “only the latest three time steps contribute to the objective with coefficients (0.5,0.5,1).” “Latest” is ambiguous (closest or farthest horizons?). This materially changes supervision. Also, how are episodes shorter than τ handled? Bootstrapping, truncation, or padding?
  - Measurement scaling: In D3/D4 the text mentions “normalized measurements,” but the normalization procedure (per-episode, global, per-dimension statistics, clipping) is not specified. Since the loss is MSE over heterogeneous dimensions, scaling critically affects training. Additionally, for D1/D2, training uses a single measurement (health). Were targets standardized there?
  - Optimization details are missing: optimizer (though Kingma & Ba is cited), learning rates, batch size N, replay size M, update period k, target networks, gradient clipping, and regularization are unspecified. Replay is described as “small”; exact M matters for stability and off-policy bias.
  - Perception pipeline: image preprocessing (grayscale vs RGB), resolution, normalization, frame stacking, action repeat/frame-skip, and decision rate are unspecified. You state “525 steps equals 1 minute of real time,” implying an 8.75 Hz decision rate (likely frame-skip 4 at 35 Hz), but this is not stated. These choices strongly affect difficulty and baseline performance.
  - Action space: For D3/D4 “Any combination of these sub-actions can be used, resulting in possible actions.” The number is missing. Earlier you list 8 sub-actions; thus 2^8=256 combinations if all simultaneous combos are allowed; this must be explicit because the network outputs for all actions.
  - Loss targets near terminals: how are death events handled when computing mt+τ − mt? Are negative health values meaningful? This is relevant because you report terminal health possibly negative.
- Baseline fairness and reporting
  - A3C: You used an “independent implementation.” It is unclear whether recurrence (LSTM) and/or frame stacking were allowed. The A3C paper demonstrates strong results with LSTM in 3D POMDPs; removing memory while your method uses dense measurement supervision may bias the comparison. Hyperparameter search is described but limited; there are no confidence intervals.
  - DQN: Was dueling architecture or prioritized replay used? You compare to DQN while your network includes dueling-style splitting; a non-dueling DQN is disadvantaged.
  - DSR: Only evaluated on Basic due to speed, with little tuning; yet the paper still cites the comparison when arguing superiority. This is not a fair test.
  - Metrics: In D3/D4 you optimize a linear combination (0.5 ammo, 0.5 health, 1 frags) but report frags only. This mismatch may bias perceived gains; please report the optimized utility and all component measurements with error bars.
  - Seeds and variability: Curves average 3 runs; tables have no standard errors. Given high variance in RL, this is insufficient.
- Claims versus evidence
  - “Outperforms state-of-the-art deep RL models” is overbroad given unclear fairness (architecture, recurrence, preprocessing) and limited baselines. The competition result is suggestive but confounded by different training signals among teams and extra supervision used by others; hardware/training budgets are unreported.
  - Goal generalization: encouraging but restricted to linear utility over intrinsic measurements seen during training. It is unclear how sensitive results are to goal distributions and scaling; no held-out goal distributions with different magnitude/scales are tested.
  - Ablations: You ablate (i) vector vs scalar targets and (ii) multi-horizon. Missing: ablation of the expectation/action-stream split (does it matter?), goal conditioning (what if g is removed?), and the contribution of measurement inputs m versus just pixels.
- Presentation/formatting
  - References contain numerous errors and placeholders (e.g., “Blundell et al.,? \Q2016\E,” “He, He,? \Q2015\E,” “search. Nature,” inconsistent author lists, missing venues/years). This is not acceptable for ICLR camera-ready or even submission quality.
  - Missing numbers and inconsistencies (e.g., “resulting in possible actions” without the count; ambiguous “latest three time steps”). Several sentences are grammatically awkward or incomplete.
  - Figures and appendices are heavily relied upon for details (“Appendix A/B”) but key elements are absent from the main text.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Provide a thorough, reproducible description: preprocessing, action repeat, observation stacking, optimizer and all hyperparameters, replay details, learning rate schedule, epsilon schedule, loss weighting across horizons and dimensions, target handling at episode ends.
- Expand and strengthen baselines: A3C with LSTM and proper preprocessing; DQN with dueling/prioritized replay; possibly PPO. Report all component metrics and utilities, with confidence intervals over ≥5 seeds.
- Add ablations: remove goal conditioning (compare to unconditional predictor + linear readout), remove measurement inputs m, remove expectation/action split, vary replay size M, and horizon sets τ.
- Clarify and justify normalization and the choice of linear goal; try nonlinear goal conditioning or test out-of-support goal magnitudes to probe generalization.
- Discuss computational costs: wall-clock training time, hardware, frames per second, training budgets per method.

Questions for the authors
1. Precisely which horizons are weighted in the loss, and how are terminal states handled for f at longer τ? Do you bootstrap, pad, or drop targets?
2. What exact preprocessing (resolution, color space, normalization), frame-skip/action repeat, and observation stacking were used? Is the policy strictly reactive to a single frame?
3. How are measurements normalized for training and for goal weighting? Are per-dimension loss weights used?
4. What is the exact number of actions in D3/D4, and are all sub-action combinations permitted? Any masking of invalid combos?
5. For A3C, did you include LSTM and frame stacking? If not, why, given POMDP nature and the original paper’s recommendations?
6. Can you report results with error bars across ≥5 seeds and include utility (not only frags) for D3/D4?
7. How sensitive is performance to the choice of τ and to the loss weights over horizons?
