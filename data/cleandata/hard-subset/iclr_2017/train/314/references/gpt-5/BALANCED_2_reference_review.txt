1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning formulation for sensorimotor control that replaces scalar rewards with a vector of intrinsic measurements and learns to predict future changes in these measurements conditioned on the current observation, candidate action, and a goal vector. Actions are chosen by maximizing the inner product between the predicted future measurement vector and the goal vector, enabling goal-parameterized control without explicit value iteration or policy learning. The model uses a perception module for raw visuals, simple MLPs for measurements and goal, and a dueling-style prediction head that decomposes predictions into expectation and action-specific streams. Training is via regression on Monte-Carlo targets over multiple temporal offsets.

Empirically, DFP outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C, and DSR where feasible) on four ViZDoom scenarios, with especially large gains on the more complex combat tasks. It shows appealing properties: (a) goal-agnostic training that generalizes to new test-time goals, (b) generalization across environments with randomized textures, and (c) ablations indicating the benefit of vectorial measurements and multi-horizon targets. The approach also won the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch track.

2) Decision
Accept.

Key reasons:
- Clear, simple idea that is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature (predictive representations, UVFA, Monte Carlo) and that enables goal-parameterized control with dense supervision.
- Strong empirical evidence with substantial margins over widely used baselines on challenging 3D visuomotor tasks, plus convincing ablations and generalization studies.

3) Supporting arguments
On problem framing and motivation: The paper addresses two persistent challenges in deep RL: sparse/unstable reward signals and lack of goal flexibility. Casting control as supervised prediction of dense, multivariate measurements is compelling when such measurements are available; it aligns with predictive state representations and UVFA, and the goal-parameterization is natural and practical. The dueling-style prediction head is a sensible architectural choice to tease apart average effects and action-conditioned differences.

On empirical support and rigor: The evaluation spans progressively harder scenarios, moving from simple health gathering to complex combat requiring navigation, object recognition, and shooting. DFP’s gains over A3C are especially pronounced in the richer measurement settings (D3/D4), consistent with the method’s reliance on dense multivariate feedback. The goal-agnostic training experiment demonstrates robust test-time goal adaptation without sacrificing performance on the original goal; the ablations indicate both components (vector feedback and multi-horizon prediction) matter; and the texture-randomization experiments test some degree of visual generalization. Reporting training speed further underscores practicality.

Caveats: Theoretical analysis is limited; the method is primarily empirical. Some comparison choices deserve more detail to fully establish fairness (e.g., whether A3C used recurrence or frame stacking, exact perception capacity matching). Statistical reporting (error bars, significance tests) is light, and only three seeds are mentioned. Generalization tests are mostly texture changes rather than structural variations in map topology. The approach assumes access to a meaningful measurement stream and goals that are linear in future measurement deltas; while reasonable in many settings, this is a restriction.

Overall, despite these limitations, the empirical evidence and simplicity/utility of the formulation make a strong case.

4) Additional feedback and questions for authors (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Clarify baselines:
  - Did A3C include an LSTM or frame stacking? If not, please justify, as partial observability is material here. If yes, detail how you matched network capacity to DFP.
  - Confirm visual preprocessing and perception module parity across methods (input resolution, normalization, data augmentation if any).
  - Specify the exact number of actions for D3/D4 (one line appears truncated: “resulting in possible actions”).
- Targets and horizons:
  - You predict mt+τ − mt for τ ∈ {1,2,4,8,16,32} but weigh only the last three offsets. Why this choice? How sensitive are results to these offsets and weights? How are targets handled near episode termination when τ exceeds remaining steps?
  - Discuss the relationship between your multi-horizon deltas and discounted returns; e.g., can a fixed discount be approximated by a suitable goal vector over horizons?
- Off-policy aspects and training details:
  - Provide the replay buffer size M, batch size N, update frequency k, optimizer and learning rate schedule, normalization of measurements, and handling of terminal transitions. Include standard deviations over seeds for all key results.
  - You state no adverse effect from small experience replay; could you quantify stability vs. buffer size?
- Connections to prior frameworks:
  - More explicitly relate DFP to UVFA and predictive state representations. With linear goals, DFP resembles learning expected future features (akin to successor features without a Bellman backup). A brief discussion would sharpen the conceptual contribution.
- Generalization:
  - Beyond texture randomization, can you test generalization to unseen map layouts or demonstrate transfer learning from D3/D4 to structurally different arenas? The competition result helps, but a controlled study in the paper would strengthen claims.
- Measurements and practicality:
  - Comment on requirements for the measurement stream (availability, noise, delays). How robust is DFP if some measurements are missing/noisy or if measurement semantics change (e.g., different ammo scales)?
  - Can the approach learn useful “measurements” from sensory streams (learned features) rather than hand-selected channels?
- Extensions:
  - Since the model is reactive, have you tried frame stacking or adding recurrence to S(s) for partial observability? A small study could be insightful.
  - Briefly discuss adaptation to continuous action spaces (e.g., sampling actions and selecting via u(f;g), or learning a policy that maximizes g^T F via gradient ascent).

Questions for authors
1) How are multi-horizon targets computed at episode ends, and how sensitive are results to the horizon set and the weighting scheme?
2) What exact A3C configuration was used (LSTM/no LSTM, frame stacking, entropy regularization, input resolution), and how was capacity matched to your network?
3) What is the replay buffer size, batch size, optimizer details, and measurement normalization? Please provide variance across seeds and, if possible, confidence intervals for Tables 1–4.
4) Does adding short frame histories or recurrence improve DFP materially on D3/D4?
5) How does performance degrade under noisy or delayed measurements, or when some measurement channels are removed?
6) Can DFP handle goal vectors outside the training distribution (e.g., extreme trade-offs), and how does performance vary as the test goal moves away from the training goal distribution?
7) Can you report results on unseen layouts (not just textures) to further support generalization claims?

Overall, this is a clear, impactful contribution with strong empirical results and a simple, useful idea. I recommend acceptance.
