Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a supervised-learning approach to sensorimotor control that predicts how intrinsic measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) will evolve under candidate actions at multiple short horizons, conditioned on a parameterized goal vector. At test time, the agent chooses the action maximizing the inner product between the predicted future measurement differences and the goal vector. Key claims:
- Dense, vector-valued “measurement” supervision yields faster, more stable learning than sparse scalar rewards.
- Conditioning on goals enables training without a fixed objective and test-time generalization to new goals.
- A simple, reactive architecture with an expectation/action-stream decomposition outperforms deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C, DSR) on challenging Doom tasks, and generalizes across varied environments; a DFP agent won the Visual Doom AI Competition’s Full Deathmatch track.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
1) Conceptual clarity and practicality: reframing control as supervised prediction over intrinsic measurements is simple, flexible, and well-motivated, bridging predictive representations and goal-conditioned control.
2) Strong empirical evidence: consistent and substantial improvements over solid baselines on visually rich 3D tasks, including generalization across textures and goals. The competition win adds credibility.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and literature placement: The work is positioned against TD/policy-gradient methods under sparse scalar rewards, and connects to Monte Carlo learning, multi-objective RL, UVFA, predictive representations (GVFs), successor representations, and recent deep visuomotor control. The distinction—predicting vector measurements at multiple horizons and conditioning on goals—is clear. While related to UVFA and GVFs, this instantiation with high-dimensional perception, direct multihorizon measurement prediction, and goal-parameterized action selection is novel and practically impactful.
- Methodological soundness: The objective is supervised regression on Monte Carlo targets for short horizons, avoiding bootstrapping instabilities. The architectural split into expectation and action streams (dueling-style normalization) is sensible given the multi-action, multi-output setting. The decision rule follows directly from the goal formulation.
- Empirical rigor: Four scenarios of increasing difficulty with clear metrics; comparisons to DQN, A3C, and DSR; ablations showing benefits of vectorial measurements and multihorizon prediction; generalization experiments with texture randomization; and goal-agnostic training demonstrating adaptation to novel goals. Reporting includes multiple seeds and training curves; they attempt to make baselines strong (learning-rate sweeps, reward shaping aligned with DFP’s goals).
- Significance: The approach demonstrates an alternative to RL that is both simpler and competitive/stronger on challenging 3D tasks, with an appealing story for goal flexibility and measurement-driven supervision.

Caveats and concerns (do not change my accept recommendation)
- Scope and fairness of baselines: A3C is a strong baseline, but recent improvements such as auxiliary prediction tasks (e.g., UNREAL) or recurrence could plausibly close gaps. DSR results are limited by training speed. While reward shaping matches DFP’s linear combination, DFP uniquely benefits from multi-task prediction targets; including an A3C baseline with similar auxiliary measurement-prediction losses would strengthen claims about the value of vector supervision per se.
- Assumptions and generality: The approach presumes access to useful, low-dimensional measurements aligned with goals; this is realistic in many domains but not universal. The linear goal parameterization is limiting; it would help to explore non-linear goal utilities or constraints.
- Horizon and credit assignment: Predicted horizons are short (up to 32 steps). The method may struggle with long-horizon credit assignment, partial observability, and exploration-heavy tasks. The model is reactive; no memory or planning is used.
- Off-policy aspects: Training uses a small replay buffer and ε-greedy exploration; while stable empirically, some discussion of off-policy bias for Monte Carlo targets with changing policies would be useful.

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Strengthen baselines: Add an A3C variant with auxiliary measurement prediction (multi-task) and/or with LSTM memory; consider comparing to UNREAL-style agents. Clarify whether the A3C baseline used frame stacking or recurrence for partial observability.
- Broaden analysis of horizons: Report sensitivity to the set of temporal offsets and weighting scheme; include longer horizons to test limits of the approach.
- Elaborate on measurement choice: Discuss how to select/design measurements in domains without obvious stats, and whether learned features (unsupervised or self-supervised) can serve as measurements.
- Theory and connections: Clarify the relation to successor representations (predicting cumulative feature expectations) and UVFA; explain when DFP approximates value functions under linear goals, and implications for policy improvement. Situate the method w.r.t. model-based RL (it is a partial predictive model over intrinsic measurements).
- Reproducibility and details: Bring key architectural and training details from the appendix into the main text; release code/models if possible. Clean up references (many entries have formatting errors).

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is DFP to the choice and scaling/normalization of measurements? Have you tried adding irrelevant/noisy measurements, and how does that affect learning and action selection?
2) Can you report results with longer prediction horizons or with learned horizon weighting? What breaks first as horizons increase: accuracy, stability, or policy quality?
3) How does performance change with replay buffer size and ε schedule? Any evidence of off-policy bias or benefit from larger buffers?
4) Could you include an A3C baseline augmented with auxiliary tasks that predict the same measurements (and possibly multihorizon), to isolate the benefit of vector supervision from the control algorithm?
5) How does DFP fare under stronger partial observability (e.g., mazes requiring memory)? Have you tried adding recurrence to the DFP architecture?
6) Can the goal parameterization be extended beyond linear utilities (e.g., constraints, non-linear trade-offs), and does the model generalize to such goals at test time?
7) In domains without explicit measurements, have you explored learning the measurement vector (e.g., via representation learning) jointly with DFP?

Overall, this is a clear, well-executed paper with a simple yet powerful idea, strong empirical results, and meaningful implications for goal-conditioned control and supervision from dense intrinsic feedback.
