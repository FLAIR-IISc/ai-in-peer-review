Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): instead of learning to maximize discounted scalar reward, the agent predicts changes in a low-dimensional measurement vector (e.g., health, ammo, frags) at multiple future horizons, conditioned on observation, candidate action, and a goal vector. Actions are chosen by maximizing the dot product of the predicted future measurement changes with the goal.
- The model uses a dueling-style architecture (expectation stream plus action-difference stream with zero-mean normalization across actions) to predict future measurements for all actions at once.
- The approach is trained via supervised regression on experience tuples collected with ε-greedy exploration, optionally with randomized goals per episode. Training uses a small replay memory and off-policy sampling.
- Experiments in ViZDoom across four scenarios show strong performance vs. DQN, A3C, and DSR, with particularly large gains on more complex tasks with multiple measurements. The model reportedly generalizes to unseen textures and goals, and won the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch track.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The paper, as written, has numerous technical omissions, typographical errors, and formatting issues that materially impede reproducibility and assessment (missing action-space size, unclear loss construction across horizons, undefined hyperparameters, broken Eq. (6), inconsistent baselines, severely broken references). (2) Baseline fairness and experimental rigor are insufficiently established (uneven hyperparameter search and training budgets, undertrained DSR, unclear observation parity, limited statistical reporting), making the magnitude of the claimed advantages difficult to trust.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem and motivation
- The idea of leveraging dense, vector-valued intrinsic measurements and parameterized goals to turn control into supervised prediction is timely and interesting, and the paper is well-positioned conceptually with respect to Monte Carlo methods, multi-objective RL, and UVFA. However, while the narrative motivation is clear, the practical setup lacks critical detail.

2) Methodological clarity and correctness
- Action space: For scenarios D3/D4, the text states “Any combination of these sub-actions can be used, resulting in possible actions.” The number is missing. If all 8 sub-actions can be combined, that implies 256 actions; predicting outputs for all actions simultaneously would considerably change model size and compute. This omission is material.
- Observation specification: The paper later claims the model is purely reactive (current frame only), but it does not state whether baseline DQN/A3C used the standard 4-frame stack. If DFP uses single-frame input while baselines use stacked frames (or vice versa), this significantly affects fairness and difficulty.
- Loss construction: f is defined as concatenated differences across τ ∈ {1,2,4,8,16,32}, yet training “only the latest three time steps contribute to the objective” with coefficients (0.5, 0.5, 1). It is unclear how this interacts with Eq. (4). Are earlier τ terms predicted but not penalized? Are targets normalized? How are measurement scales handled?
- Eq. (6): Ai(j) appears on both sides with the same symbol; presumably the right-hand side should be an unnormalized Ãi(j). As written, it is ill-defined.
- Replay details are incomplete: M (memory size), N (batch size), k (update frequency), optimizer, learning rates, gradient clipping, target normalization, and regularization are deferred to Appendix A (not provided here). Given the claims of stability and performance, these are essential.
- Goal randomization: The sampling distributions are briefly described, but the exact distribution over g (e.g., per-dimension independence, normalization) and curriculum, if any, are not specified.
- Measurement definition: Health “can be negative at the end of episode” is unusual; is this raw engine output? How are measurement ranges normalized across tasks for training stability?

3) Empirical support and rigor
- Baselines: DQN and DSR got minimal hyperparameter tuning; A3C received more. DSR was reportedly too slow to train and evaluated only on the simplest scenario. It is hard to claim state-of-the-art outperformance when one baseline is undertrained and tuned unevenly.
- Compute budgets: Training speeds differ widely (A3C faster; DSR slower). The paper reports training steps but not normalized compute budgets or wall-clock parity. Some comparisons might reflect engineering/training budget rather than algorithmic merit.
- Statistical reporting: Results are averaged over 3 seeds with data points computed from testing chunks; however, there are no confidence intervals or statistical tests. Given the claimed >6x improvements on some tasks, variance estimates are necessary.
- Generalization: Texture generalization is nice, but it remains within a single domain and engine. Claims of generalization would be more convincing with unseen layouts, enemy classes, or different measurement definitions. The number of training/test episodes in each setting is not fully specified.
- Goal generalization: The results for randomized training goals are compelling; however, the baselines do not include an explicit UVFA implementation or other multi-goal RL baselines, which are the most natural points of comparison.

4) Presentation quality and formatting
- References are severely malformed throughout (e.g., “? \Q2016\E”, duplicate authors, incomplete titles like “tree, search. Nature”), making it difficult to verify prior work and situate contributions.
- Multiple placeholders and missing numbers (action count; possibly others). This is beyond minor typos and obstructs evaluation.
- The paper repeatedly defers essential details to appendices; without them, the work is not reproducible.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Provide complete implementation details in the main text or a guaranteed, accessible appendix: exact architecture (layer sizes, strides, activations), observation pre-processing, frame stacking, action set enumeration, optimizer, learning rate schedule, batch size, replay memory size, update frequency, ε schedule, gradient norms/clipping, measurement normalization.
- Fix Eq. (6) and clarify the loss across horizons, including target normalization and the rationale for downweighting near-term predictions or early horizons.
- Ensure fairness with baselines: same observation history (frames), same action set, comparable training steps and compute budgets, and comparable hyperparameter tuning efforts. Include UVFA (Schaul et al.) as a multi-goal baseline.
- Report results with confidence intervals, learning curves with variance bands, and per-seed results in an appendix.
- Add ablations: (a) with/without the expectation/action stream split, (b) different measurement subsets, (c) varying numbers of temporal offsets, (d) on-policy vs small replay, (e) effect of goal sampling distributions.
- Improve generalization experiments: unseen map topologies, different enemy types, altered measurement definitions to test robustness of the measurement-prediction premise.
- Correct and complete the bibliography.

Questions for the authors
1) What is the exact number of actions in D3/D4? Are all 2^8 combinations allowed? If not, which combinations are excluded?
2) Did all methods (DFP, DQN, A3C) use the same observation history (single frame vs 4 stacked frames)? If not, why, and how does this affect results?
3) Please clarify Eq. (6): is the right-hand side Ãi(j)? How exactly is the zero-mean constraint implemented per measurement dimension?
4) How are measurement targets normalized across tasks and horizons? Are different measurements weighted to account for scale?
5) What are the precise values of M, N, k, optimizer type, learning rate schedule, gradient clipping, and ε schedule?
6) How are fi constructed when only the last three horizons are weighted? Are all six horizons predicted but only three contribute to the loss?
7) Did you control for compute/training budgets across baselines? Can you provide results under equal wall-clock time and equal number of environment steps?
8) How are terminal transitions handled when predicting future measurements at long horizons?
9) Could you include a UVFA baseline and/or a Monte-Carlo value regression baseline with multi-objective rewards to isolate the contribution of the measurement-prediction formulation?
10) Will you release code and environment configurations to enable reproducibility?

Despite promising ideas and results, the current submission’s omissions and presentation issues prevent a confident evaluation. Addressing the above would significantly strengthen the paper.
