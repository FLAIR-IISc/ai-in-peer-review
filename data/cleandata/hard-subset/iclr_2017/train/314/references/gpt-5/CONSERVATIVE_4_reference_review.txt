Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP): instead of optimizing a scalar reward via RL, the agent predicts future values of a low-dimensional measurement vector (e.g., health, ammo, frags) at multiple horizons given current sensory input, measurements, action, and a goal vector g. At test time, the action maximizing g^T predicted-future is chosen.
- This reframes sensorimotor control as supervised multi-step prediction, providing dense targets and enabling goal-parameterization (train with varying goals, act on new goals at test time).
- A dueling-style architecture predicts per-action deltas plus an expectation stream. Training uses ε-greedy exploration with a small replay buffer and Monte-Carlo-style targets for multiple temporal offsets.
- Empirically, on ViZDoom scenarios (navigation and combat), DFP is reported to outperform DQN and A3C, generalize to unseen textures and to new goal vectors, and won the Visual Doom AI Competition’s Full Deathmatch track.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) The empirical validation against established baselines is not sufficiently rigorous or fair to justify strong claims. The method benefits from richer supervision (vectorial measurements and multi-horizon targets) while the baselines are not given comparably strong auxiliary signals or competitive implementations (e.g., A3C-LSTM, dueling/double DQN, prioritized replay, UNREAL-style auxiliary tasks), and tuning appears limited.
2) Conceptual novelty is incremental relative to known frameworks (generalized value functions, UVFA, Monte-Carlo/episodic approaches, predictive representations). The paper lacks theoretical analysis or principled treatment of design choices (choice of measurements, horizons, regression targets) to ground the new formulation.

Supporting arguments
- Fairness of comparisons:
  - DFP leverages dense, vectorial supervision (full measurement vector across multiple horizons). The RL baselines optimize a scalarized reward and do not receive equivalent auxiliary objectives or measurement prediction losses. Recent RL results (e.g., auxiliary tasks) suggest such supervision itself can explain large gains.
  - A3C is evaluated via an independent implementation with limited hyperparameter search; it seemingly lacks LSTM/memory despite partial observability. Stronger baselines (A3C-LSTM, UNREAL, IMPALA-like auxiliary losses, dueling/double DQN with prioritized replay) are absent. DSR is severely undertrained and not comparable.
  - Capacity/architecture parity is unclear. DFP uses a dueling-like split; it is not stated that DQN/A3C baselines enjoy analogous architectural benefits.
- Statistical rigor:
  - Most plots average over three runs; confidence intervals or statistical tests are not reported. Claims like “6x better” are based on limited seeds. Variance in these environments is high; robustness is not established.
  - No sensitivity analysis for key hyperparameters (horizons τ, horizon weights, replay size, ε schedule).
- Scope and generality:
  - “Goal generalization” is limited to linear reweightings over the same measurement deltas; it does not test generalization to new measurement spaces or qualitatively different tasks.
  - The method assumes availability of meaningful, low-dimensional measurements that capture what should be optimized. In many domains such signals are unavailable or are the very quantities one seeks to learn to optimize; this limitation is acknowledged but not addressed empirically.
  - Exploration is ε-greedy; no evidence is provided that DFP addresses sparse-reward regimes where RL is typically required.
- Relation to prior work:
  - The idea closely resembles GVFs (Sutton et al.) and UVFA (Schaul et al.), and Monte-Carlo prediction with parametric goals. The contribution appears to be an instantiation with a particular architecture and training scheme rather than a substantial conceptual advance. The paper does not provide theoretical connections or guarantees, nor an analysis clarifying when/why MC prediction of vector measurements should outperform TD/policy gradient methods.

Constructive feedback to improve the paper
- Strengthen baselines and ensure parity:
  - Include A3C-LSTM and DQN variants with dueling, double Q-learning, prioritized replay; tune thoroughly. Include an auxiliary-task RL baseline (e.g., predicting the same measurement vector as auxiliary losses) to isolate the benefit of dense supervision from the specific DFP decision rule.
  - Report computational budgets, wall-clock, and environment steps matched across methods; provide standard errors and statistical tests over more seeds.
- Ablations and analysis:
  - Vary measurement sets (remove one, add noisy/irrelevant ones) to test robustness to measurement choice.
  - Compare MC-style DFP against n-step/A3C with equivalent n-step returns and against TD(λ) with similar horizons.
  - Study sensitivity to τ and horizon weights; study replay size and off-policy effects.
  - Add memory (LSTM) to DFP to address partial observability and compare to memory-augmented baselines.
- Generalization:
  - Evaluate “goal generalization” beyond linear weights over fixed measurements, e.g., new goals defined over transformed measurements, or compositional tasks.
  - Test on additional 3D suites (e.g., DM Lab) or standard benchmarks to assess breadth.
- Theory:
  - Clarify the connection to GVFs/UVFA formally; discuss conditions under which maximizing g^T predicted measurement deltas is equivalent to optimizing discounted returns. Analyze bias/variance trade-offs of MC targets versus TD for this setting.

Questions for the authors
1) What exact network capacities were used for DQN/A3C relative to DFP? Did baselines use dueling/double/priority or LSTM? Please report detailed hyperparameters and wall-clock compute.
2) Can you provide results with A3C-LSTM and with auxiliary measurement-prediction losses for RL baselines to control for the denser supervision?
3) How sensitive is performance to the choice of τ and the weighting of the three latest offsets? What happens with different horizon sets/weights?
4) How does DFP perform in sparser-reward settings without informative measurements, or when measurements are noisy/irrelevant?
5) Can you demonstrate robustness across more seeds with confidence intervals, and provide per-environment variance?
6) How critical is the expectation/action-stream split? Please include ablations removing it and/or giving the same split to the baselines.
7) How would you select/construct measurements in domains where game-style stats are not available? Can the approach learn useful measurement features end-to-end?

While the paper is clearly written and the empirical results are promising, the above issues prevent me from confidently endorsing the methodological claims at this time.
