Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Direct Future Prediction (DFP), a bold reframing of sensorimotor control as supervised learning: predict action-conditional future measurement vectors (not full sensory streams), then act by choosing the action whose predicted future best aligns with a goal vector. This decouples goal specification from learning and replaces TD-style targets with dense, temporally rich supervision derived directly from experience.
- Key ideas: (1) vector-valued measurements (e.g., health, ammo, frags) as “intrinsic signals” to predict; (2) goal-parameterization via linear weights over future measurement deltas, enabling goal changes at test time; (3) a simple architecture that predicts for all actions with an expectation/action stream decomposition (dueling-style) to sharpen action differences; (4) Monte Carlo-style supervised regression on multi-horizon targets rather than Bellman backups.
- Empirically, DFP outperforms strong deep RL baselines (DQN, A3C) in visually rich 3D Doom tasks, with especially large margins on the more complex battle scenarios; generalizes across textures/environments; supports goal-agnostic training that enables zero-shot goal changes; and benefits from vectorial and multi-horizon targets. A DFP variant also won the Visual Doom AI Competition Full Deathmatch track.

Decision: Accept
Reason 1: Conceptual novelty and clarity. Recasting control as supervised future-measurement prediction with goal conditioning is a clean, impactful shift away from TD-based RL and opens a promising direction for dense-feedback settings.
Reason 2: Strong empirical evidence. The method decisively outperforms competitive baselines on challenging 3D control tasks, shows generalization and goal flexibility, and includes ablations that support the central design choices.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty/significance: The paper goes beyond UVFA/goal-conditioned value functions by predicting measurement vectors themselves and deferring goal composition to test time. Compared to TD and policy gradients, the supervised Monte Carlo-style learning target is simple and stable, exploiting dense intrinsic feedback. This is a compelling alternative for immersive, richly instrumented environments.
- Positioning: Related work is well covered (UVFA, multi-objective RL, predictive representations, Monte Carlo vs TD). The paper clearly articulates when SL can be advantageous (dense, multivariate feedback) and connects to recent architectures (dueling). It would be valuable to tighten links to successor features/representations, but the distinction is meaningful.
- Empirical support: Experiments span increasing difficulty (Basic, Navigation, Battle, Battle2), include generalization to texture-randomized mazes and goal changes, and offer ablations (vector vs scalar, multi-horizon). The margins over A3C in complex scenarios are large. The competition result is an additional strong signal.
- Clarity/reproducibility: The formulation is clear; the architecture and training loop are straightforward. Many implementation details are deferred to appendices, but enough is present to follow the approach.
- Limitations (acknowledged or inferred): Requires access to meaningful, dense measurements and a goal expressible as a linear function of their future values; horizon is limited (up to 32 steps); not yet addressing memory/partial observability, temporal abstraction, or continuous actions. Baseline fairness has a few caveats (see below), but overall results are persuasive.

Additional comments to strengthen the paper (not gating the decision)
- Fair baselines and ablations:
  - Confirm whether baseline DQN/A3C variants include dueling heads or other architectural improvements comparable to the expectation/action split in DFP. If not, add a dueling DQN/A3C baseline or ablate the split in DFP to isolate its contribution.
  - Report sample efficiency (frames-to-target-performance) in addition to wall-clock steps/day. Ensure environment step throughput is comparable across methods when interpreting speed.
  - DSR evaluation is limited by training speed; please frame these results cautiously.
- Theoretical grounding:
  - Provide a clearer connection between maximizing g^T predicted future deltas and standard RL returns. Under what conditions (choice of τ and g encoding exponential decay) does DFP approximate discounted return? Discuss bias induced by finite horizons and multi-horizon weighting.
  - Relate DFP to successor features: if measurements equal features φ and rewards are w^Tφ, what is the relationship between DFP’s predictors and SFs? When does DFP subsume or differ from SF-based control?
- Diagnostics:
  - Include calibration/accuracy plots for future-measurement prediction versus control performance; learning curves of prediction loss; and sensitivity to τ values and the loss weights over horizons.
  - Analyze robustness to measurement scaling/noise and to mis-specified goals; e.g., how does performance degrade when measurement signals are delayed or partially missing?
- Scope and generality:
  - Discuss applicability to domains without readily available measurement vectors or with very sparse rewards. Could learned measurement proxies (unsupervised features) be used?
  - Explore longer horizons or curriculum over τ to handle longer-term credit assignment.
- Reproducibility:
  - Specify experience memory size M, batch size N, update frequency k, optimizer settings, and normalization of measurements. Consider releasing code/configs.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive is performance to the choice of temporal offsets τ and to the loss weights over horizons? Have you tried learning these weights or using an exponentially decayed objective to better approximate discounted returns?
2. Can you clarify whether A3C/DQN baselines used dueling architectures or comparable capacity to DFP’s expectation/action split? If not, what is the performance of DFP without the split?
3. How does sample efficiency (frames to reach a given score) compare to A3C when measured under identical environment throughput?
4. What is the relationship between DFP and successor features in the linear reward setting? Can DFP be seen as learning action-conditioned cumulants akin to generalized value functions?
5. How robust is DFP to noisy or partially observed measurements, and to misspecified goal vectors at test time?
6. Beyond Doom, have you explored Atari or continuous-control domains to assess generality, especially in sparser-reward settings?

Overall, this is a bold, well-executed idea with strong empirical traction. Despite some baseline and scope caveats, it opens a promising direction and merits acceptance.
