This paper presents a novel image compression method based on nonlinear transform coding, which is optimized end-to-end for rate-distortion performance. The approach uses a cascade of convolutional linear filters and nonlinear activation functions, inspired by models of biological neurons, to capture the features and attributes of images. The method is trained using a variant of stochastic gradient descent, with a continuous proxy for the discontinuous loss function arising from the quantizer.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it demonstrates that a nonlinear transform coding approach can outperform traditional linear transform coding methods, such as JPEG and JPEG 2000, in terms of rate-distortion performance. Second, it shows that the proposed method can achieve substantial improvements in visual quality, particularly at low bit rates, compared to traditional methods. Third, it provides a framework for optimizing the compression model for a specific rate-distortion trade-off, which is a key challenge in image compression.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its components. The experimental results are thorough and convincing, with comparisons to traditional methods on a variety of images and bit rates. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the performance of their method, including a discussion of the trade-offs between rate and distortion.

One potential limitation of the paper is that it focuses primarily on the performance of the proposed method in terms of rate-distortion, without providing a detailed analysis of its computational complexity or implementation challenges. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the relationship between the proposed method and other recent approaches to image compression, such as generative models and deep neural networks.

Overall, I believe that this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression and is worthy of publication in a top-tier conference. I recommend acceptance.
