Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a complete, end-to-end learned image compression system consisting of a convolutional analysis transform, a uniform scalar quantizer, and a convolutional synthesis transform. The key architectural choice is a three-stage cascade with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and its approximate inverse (IGDN), inspired by gain control in biological vision, which the authors argue is more efficient than standard pointwise nonlinearities. Because direct optimization is hindered by the non-differentiability of quantization, the paper introduces a continuous relaxation: replacing rounding with additive uniform noise and learning nonparametric marginal priors for the latents, enabling stochastic gradient descent on a rate–distortion Lagrangian. The authors also clarify the connection to variational autoencoders (VAEs), showing that under MSE this objective corresponds to an ELBO with a specific generative/approximate posterior pair, while emphasizing important differences from generative modeling. Importantly, they evaluate with an actual entropy coder (CABAC) and report real bitrates, comparing against JPEG and JPEG 2000 on unseen images. They find consistently better perceptual quality (MS-SSIM and visual inspection) and better PSNR for most images (especially at low rates).

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Clear, principled, and novel end-to-end formulation of transform coding that directly optimizes the rate–distortion tradeoff while handling quantization with a theoretically grounded relaxation; (2) Strong practical impact: a complete codec with real bitrates and broad qualitative and quantitative improvements over standard baselines.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles lossy image compression, targeting the fundamental rate–distortion objective. The approach is well-motivated: existing codecs optimize components separately and rely on linear transforms; here, the full pipeline is optimized jointly and nonlinearly, which is compelling given the known higher-order dependencies in natural images.
- Positioning in literature: The work is well-placed relative to transform coding, gain control models, and VAEs. The discussion in Section 3.1 carefully delineates similarities and differences with VAEs, clarifying why optimizing along a specified rate–distortion curve is different from maximizing likelihood. Comparisons to contemporary neural compression efforts (Gregor et al., Toderici et al.) are fair and identify why those methods are not immediately comparable in bitrate terms.
- Technical soundness: The additive uniform noise relaxation is a standard and appropriate proxy for quantization; the authors verify empirically that the relaxed distortion is nearly unbiased and the relaxed entropy becomes accurate at finer quantization. The nonparametric marginal modeling with splines is reasonable and differentiable. The use of GDN/IGDN is justified and supported by prior work on Gaussianization, and the architectural details (filter normalization, DCT parameterization, Adam, parameter constraints) demonstrate care for stability and practicality.
- Empirical support: The study trains on a large ImageNet subset and tests on standard benchmarks (Kodak and classic images). Crucially, it reports real bitrates via an implemented entropy coder and includes side information overhead. Visual results show consistent suppression of ringing/blocking and more natural degradation; MS-SSIM consistently improves; PSNR is improved in most cases, especially at low rates. The authors also provide evidence that the continuous relaxation predicts discrete performance reasonably well.
- Significance: The end-to-end, rate–distortion-optimized codec with real bitrates and superior visual quality is a substantial step forward. The GDN-based architecture appears efficient, suggesting practical deployment potential.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect my decision)
- Aggregated metrics: While the authors argue that summarizing R–D curves across images is tricky, including standard aggregate measures (e.g., average PSNR/MS-SSIM at matched bitrates, BD-rate/BD-PSNR over Kodak) would make the quantitative story crisper within the paper (without relying on the website).
- Runtime and complexity: Please report encoding/decoding speeds and model size (parameters, FLOPs) for typical image resolutions, and compare to JPEG 2000 runtimes. This will help readers assess practicality.
- Ablations: An ablation study would strengthen the case for GDN/IGDN: (i) replace GDN with ReLU or leaky-ReLU; (ii) remove joint normalization (use pointwise nonlinearity); (iii) vary the number of stages and channels. Reporting R–D deltas would quantify the claimed efficiency of GDN.
- Entropy model: The independence assumption plus simple raster-scan CABAC likely leaves bits on the table. An experiment with richer context models (e.g., conditioning on neighboring latents or simple autoregression) would clarify the headroom attributable to the transforms vs. the coder.
- Single model multi-rate: Currently, separate models are trained per λ. Consider conditioning the analysis/synthesis and/or prior on a rate parameter (or introducing learned per-channel scales) so a single model covers a range of rates. Even a preliminary result would be informative.
- Color handling: You train directly in RGB; many codecs operate in Y’CbCr. A brief comparison (or justification) would be useful, especially given MS-SSIM is computed on luma.
- Evaluation breadth: If feasible, include an additional test set beyond Kodak to confirm generalization (e.g., Tecnick or a withheld ImageNet validation subset).

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive is performance to the number of stages and channels? Is there a point of diminishing returns, and how does that trade off with runtime?
2. Can you quantify the contribution of GDN vs. the learned linear filters? For example, what is the R–D gap between your best model and an otherwise identical model with pointwise nonlinearity?
3. How much do adaptive updates in CABAC reduce rate versus a fixed, learned prior (no adaptation)? Do you observe notable mismatch between the learned splines and the empirical latent distributions at test time?
4. What are typical encoding/decoding times on CPU/GPU for 512×512 images? How does this scale with resolution?
5. Does the relaxation ever mislead optimization at very low rates (large quantization)? The entropy proxy shows some bias there—did you need any tricks to ensure convergence for small λ?
6. How robust are results across different training datasets or image pre-processing choices? Did you observe overfitting to ImageNet-like statistics?
7. Have you tried conditioning or sharing parameters across λ to obtain a single multi-rate model? If so, what failed or succeeded?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, carefully executed, and impactful paper that convincingly advances learned image compression as a rate–distortion optimization problem with demonstrated practical viability. I recommend acceptance.
