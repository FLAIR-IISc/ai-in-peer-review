1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression system built from cascaded convolutional transforms with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) in the analysis path and an approximate inverse (IGDN) in the synthesis path. The key claims are: (i) joint rate–distortion optimization of the full pipeline using a continuous relaxation of quantization (additive uniform noise) enables SGD-based training, with a principled link to VAEs; (ii) the learned nonlinear transform coding achieves better rate–distortion than JPEG/JPEG2000 on most images (especially at low rates) and markedly better perceptual quality (MS-SSIM and visual artifacts) across all tested images; (iii) evaluation is done with an implemented entropy coder (CABAC), reporting actual bit rates.

2. Decision: Accept
Reasons: (1) Strong, well-motivated novelty in end-to-end rate–distortion learning with GDN/IGDN and a practical relaxation of quantization; (2) Convincing empirical support with actual bitrates and broad qualitative/perceptual gains.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem/Approach: The paper targets optimal transform coding under a rate–distortion objective, replacing hand-designed pipelines with a learned nonlinear transform optimized end-to-end. The relaxation is justified and empirically validated (bias/variance analyses of relaxed vs. discrete RD).
- Literature placement: Prior work (VAE generative models, Toderici et al., Gregor et al.) is accurately positioned; differences in objective (generative vs. RD) and evaluation (entropy vs. real bits) are clearly articulated. The biological rationale for GDN is grounded in prior literature.
- Evidence: Results on Kodak and other images, with both PSNR and MS-SSIM, and visual comparisons, support claims. Reporting real bitrates via CABAC strengthens rigor over differential-entropy proxies.
- Usefulness: The decoder/encoder are simple at inference, suggesting practical applicability. The architecture and training details are sufficient for reproduction in principle.
- Limitations: Separate models per λ, independence assumptions in the entropy model, and relatively simple context modeling are acknowledged; yet gains persist, indicating robustness.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve; not decisive)
- Consider a single conditional model controllable by λ to cover the whole RD curve.
- Explore stronger entropy models (e.g., learned hyperpriors/context models) to further reduce rate.
- Include user studies or additional perceptual metrics; evaluate fairness in color subsampling across codecs.
- Report complexity (FLOPs, latency, model size) and ablations (GDN vs. ReLU, number of stages).
- Clarify reproducibility via code or detailed configs.

Questions for authors
- Can a single model conditioned on λ (or target rate) match separate-per-λ performance?
- How sensitive are results to the independence assumption in the latent prior; have you tried spatial/context priors?
- What are encoding/decoding runtimes and memory footprints on standard CPUs/GPUs?
- How does performance compare to more modern baselines (e.g., HEVC intra/BPG) and with 4:4:4 settings?
- Do results generalize across unseen content distributions (e.g., high-resolution, camera RAW)?
