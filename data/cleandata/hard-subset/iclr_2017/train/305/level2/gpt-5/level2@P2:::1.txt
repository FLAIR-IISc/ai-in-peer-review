1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes an end-to-end trainable image compression system built from three cascaded stages of convolutional analysis and synthesis transforms with a biologically inspired generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearity and its approximate inverse (IGDN). Quantization is handled during training via a continuous relaxation that replaces rounding with additive uniform noise, enabling stochastic gradient descent while explicitly optimizing a Lagrangian rate–distortion objective for a chosen trade-off parameter λ. The authors tie the relaxed objective to a variational autoencoder (VAE) view under certain conditions, but emphasize key differences rooted in discrete coding and operating across the entire R–D curve. Importantly, they model latent distributions non-parametrically per-channel, assume marginal independence, and implement a practical arithmetic coder (CABAC-like) to report actual bitrates, including side information. On standard test images (Kodak and others), their method improves visual quality markedly over JPEG and JPEG2000 and achieves better MS-SSIM consistently; PSNR is improved for most images, especially at low rates. They also empirically validate that the relaxed objective closely tracks the discrete R–D behavior.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The paper presents a principled, practical, and complete learned compression system with actual bitstreams, delivering consistent perceptual gains and competitive PSNR against strong baselines.
- The end-to-end R–D formulation with a quantization relaxation, combined with GDN/IGDN transforms and nonparametric latent modeling, is conceptually elegant, well-motivated, and likely to be influential.

3. Supporting arguments
- Soundness and rigor: The transition from discrete rate–distortion to a continuous proxy via additive uniform noise is standard but used here with care; the authors empirically verify bias/variance of both relaxed rate and distortion. The VAE correspondence is correctly derived for MSE (Gaussian slack) and appropriately caveated for general perceptual metrics. Units and assumptions are clear.
- Motivation and placement: The paper contextualizes the approach within transform coding, normalization models in vision, and recent generative/autoencoding work. It contrasts compression-specific constraints (discrete coding, operation across λ) with typical generative modeling goals. The biological motivation for GDN is relevant, and prior work from the authors (Gaussianization) provides rationale for the chosen nonlinearity.
- Empirical support: Results are strong and convincing qualitatively and quantitatively. Reporting actual bitrates with a working entropy coder is a major strength compared to prior learned codecs that report differential entropy. The consistent MS-SSIM advantage supports the perceptual claims; PSNR results are mixed at very high rates but favorable at low/medium rates. The analysis of the relaxation fidelity adds credibility.
- Practicality: Despite heavy training, encode/decode is efficient at test time. Implementation details (architecture, optimization, entropy coding, side information) are sufficiently described for reproduction.
- Limitations (non-fatal): Models are trained separately for each λ, which is less convenient than a single variable-rate codec. The entropy model assumes independent latents and a simple raster-scan context, leaving potential bitrate on the table. Comparisons omit recent learned compression baselines (e.g., Toderici et al., 2016) in RD terms. The evaluation dataset is relatively small (Kodak); nonetheless the authors provide extensive visual examples and a site with per-image curves.

4. Additional feedback for improvement (helpful suggestions, not central to the decision)
- Comparisons: Include RD comparisons to contemporary learned codecs (e.g., Toderici et al., 2016) and report both PSNR and MS-SSIM on the same test set to situate the contribution more clearly within learned compression.
- Variable-rate operation: Explore a single model that can traverse the R–D curve at test time (e.g., by conditioning on λ, scaling latents, or learning quantization step sizes), and analyze the rate control accuracy.
- Entropy modeling: Investigate spatial/context models or hierarchical priors over latents to reduce the independence gap; even simple autoregressive or hyperprior components could yield measurable bitrate gains.
- Ablations: Provide a systematic ablation on GDN vs pointwise nonlinearities (ReLU/LeakyReLU/sigmoid), number of stages, and the nonparametric vs parametric marginal models to quantify where gains originate.
- Fairness and metrics: Clarify JPEG/JPEG2000 settings (e.g., chroma subsampling, color transforms) and report BD-rate summaries (per metric) over Kodak for a succinct aggregate view, while retaining per-image plots.
- Runtime: Add encode/decode throughput on CPU and GPU to support claims of efficiency, and report memory usage.
- Perceptual training: Given the strong MS-SSIM outcomes, a small-scale experiment training directly for MS-SSIM (or with a perceptual transform) would be informative for readers considering deployment.

Questions for the authors
- Rate control and single-model operation: Can you adjust quantization step sizes or scale latents at test time to achieve intermediate rates without retraining for new λ? How does that perform relative to retrained models?
- Entropy coding details: How much bitrate benefit do you obtain from the adaptive CABAC vs a fixed non-adaptive code for your learned marginals? What is the measured gap (in bpp) to the empirical discrete entropy?
- Side information: Are the per-channel marginal models fixed per λ and known to the decoder, or are any parameters transmitted? Please quantify the side-information overhead in bits per image.
- Sensitivity to spline resolution: How sensitive are the RD results to the number of spline samples used for the marginal density approximation? Is there a noticeable trade-off between modeling accuracy and stability?
- GDN necessity: Beyond the preliminary note, can you provide quantitative RD deltas (e.g., BD-rate) when replacing GDN with ReLU or parametric pointwise nonlinearities at matched capacity?
- Generalization: Did you test on higher-resolution images beyond 256×256 crops? Does performance scale similarly in RD and perceptual quality?
- Computational cost: What are typical encode/decode times for a 512×512 RGB image on CPU and GPU for the reported models?
