1) Summary and contributions
The paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression system based on learned nonlinear analysis/synthesis transforms, a uniform scalar quantizer, and a learned entropy model. The transforms are cascades of convolutions and the generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearity, with an approximate inverse (IGDN) in the decoder. Training minimizes a rate–distortion Lagrangian by replacing quantization with additive uniform noise, enabling stochastic gradient descent and yielding a continuous proxy loss. The relaxed objective is shown to be equivalent to a variational autoencoder (VAE) under specific conditions, but the formulation explicitly targets a spectrum of rate–distortion operating points (via λ) and is evaluated with actual entropy coding (CABAC) and real bitrates. On Kodak and other standard images, the method achieves competitive or superior PSNR to JPEG/JPEG2000 for most cases, and markedly better perceptual quality (MS-SSIM and visual inspection) across all rates tested. The paper also reports empirical validation of the proxy loss as a reasonable approximation to discrete rate and distortion.

2) Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- A principled end-to-end framework for learned image compression with a practical entropy coder and actual bitrate evaluation, delivering consistent perceptual gains and competitive PSNR versus strong baselines (JPEG/JPEG 2000).
- Clear and rigorous treatment of the quantization challenge via additive-uniform relaxation, with empirical verification and a well-explained connection to VAEs, yet focused on true rate–distortion optimization.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The work addresses the classical difficulty of joint transform, quantizer, and entropy model design by optimizing a nonlinear transform coding pipeline directly for rate–distortion. This is well-motivated in the literature and positioned against established codecs and recent neural approaches (e.g., Toderici et al., Gregor et al.).
- Technical soundness: The relaxation of quantization using additive uniform noise is standard and appropriate; the paper goes further by checking the bias/variance of the relaxed rate/distortion against the discrete case, finding small distortion bias and diminishing entropy bias at finer quantization—sufficient for rate–distortion shaping via λ. The independence assumption for latents is stated explicitly, and marginals are modeled non-parametrically to reduce mismatch. The use of GDN/IGDN is justified by prior evidence on Gaussianization and is integrated coherently into the architecture.
- Novelty and significance: In contrast to contemporaneous generative approaches that report differential entropy or proxy measures, this work implements an entropy code and reports actual bit rates, making comparisons to standard codecs credible. Training separate models per λ yields a family of operating points along the convex hull, a direct embodiment of rate–distortion optimality for the given model class. Using GDN as the joint nonlinearity in a learned transform coder is a distinctive design choice with demonstrated efficiency.
- Empirical evaluation: Tests on Kodak and additional images (not in the training set) show strong perceptual improvements at all rates, supported by MS-SSIM. PSNR is better than JPEG and mostly better than JPEG 2000, particularly at lower rates. Visual artifacts are qualitatively different and less objectionable than ringing/blocking seen with linear transforms. Reporting real bitrates via CABAC and including side information in the bitstream strengthens claims of practical feasibility.

4) Additional feedback and questions (to improve the paper; not all items are decision-critical)
Essential clarifications
- Entropy model side information: The bitstream includes λ and uses fixed per-λ marginal models with online adaptation. Please clarify precisely what probability parameters are shared a priori versus adapted during coding, and whether any adaptation state must be transmitted or is decoder-synchronized deterministically.
- Computational complexity: Provide encode/decode runtime, memory footprint, and model parameter counts for the reported configurations (grayscale and RGB), ideally compared to JPEG 2000 on the same hardware. This will help assess practicality.
- Training/data details: More details on training/validation splits, number of updates, and early stopping criteria would improve reproducibility. Sensitivity to the training distribution (e.g., generalization to significantly different image statistics) would be useful.

Important but optional improvements
- Unified rate control: Currently, a separate model is trained per λ. Discuss or explore mechanisms for a single conditional model that can traverse the rate–distortion curve at test time (e.g., learned scale parameters, conditional normalization, or side-channel conditioning), and whether this compromises optimality relative to per-λ training.
- Ablations: While some observations appear in the discussion, ablation results in the main paper quantifying the impact of GDN vs. pointwise nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU) at matched parameter budgets would strengthen the architectural justification.
- Entropy modeling: The independent-latent assumption likely limits performance. Indicate whether simple spatial or channelwise context models (e.g., hierarchical priors or autoregressive conditioning) could be integrated while preserving differentiability during training, and speculate on expected gains.
- Baselines: If space permits, include comparisons to a more modern codec (e.g., intra HEVC/BPG) to contextualize performance beyond JPEG/JPEG 2000. A brief note on how far the current entropy code is from state-of-the-art context modeling would help readers calibrate results.
- Perceptual metrics: Given the strong MS-SSIM gains despite MSE training, a small experiment training directly for a perceptual metric (or a mixed objective) would be informative, even if preliminary.

Questions for discussion
- How sensitive is rate–distortion performance to the accuracy of the non-parametric marginal estimates? Did different spline resolutions or regularizations materially change outcomes?
- Did the optimization benefit from noise annealing (varying the additive-uniform noise scale) or alternative straight-through estimators, and were these explored?
- Are there observed failure modes (image types or content) where PSNR underperforms JPEG 2000 at high rates? Any qualitative patterns that suggest architectural remedies?

Overall, the paper presents a rigorous, impactful, and well-executed approach to learned image compression, with convincing evidence and clear potential for further advances.
