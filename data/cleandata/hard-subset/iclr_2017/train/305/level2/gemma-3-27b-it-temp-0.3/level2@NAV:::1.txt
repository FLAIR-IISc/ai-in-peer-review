## Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression method utilizing cascaded convolutional linear filters and nonlinear activation functions, inspired by biological neurons. The core contribution lies in jointly optimizing the entire compression pipeline – analysis transform, quantizer, and synthesis transform – for rate-distortion performance using stochastic gradient descent. A continuous proxy for the discontinuous quantization loss is introduced, cleverly linking the approach to variational autoencoders while maintaining the ability to operate at any point along the rate-distortion curve. The authors demonstrate improved rate-distortion performance and, crucially, significant visual quality improvements compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is innovative and addresses a long-standing challenge in image compression – the sub-optimal separate optimization of compression components. The results, particularly the observed improvements in visual quality, are compelling and represent a significant step forward.

The paper provides strong support for its claims. The authors demonstrate performance gains over established methods (JPEG and JPEG 2000) on a standard dataset (Kodak) and a variety of other images, using both objective metrics (MS-SSIM, PSNR) and visual inspection. The connection to variational autoencoders provides a theoretical grounding and justification for the optimization strategy. The empirical validation of the continuous relaxation of the quantization loss is also a positive aspect, demonstrating the reliability of the optimization process.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification. The choice of GDN nonlinearity, while biologically inspired, could be better motivated in terms of its specific advantages for compression beyond Gaussianization. A more detailed ablation study exploring the impact of different network architectures and hyperparameters would strengthen the findings. Additionally, while the authors acknowledge limitations, a discussion of the computational cost of training and the potential for real-time encoding/decoding would be valuable. 

Specifically, I would like to know: 
1. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the downsampling/upsampling factors in the analysis/synthesis transforms?
2. Could the authors provide more details on the implementation of the entropy code and its efficiency compared to more advanced entropy coding schemes?
3. What is the computational cost of training the model (e.g., training time, GPU resources)?
