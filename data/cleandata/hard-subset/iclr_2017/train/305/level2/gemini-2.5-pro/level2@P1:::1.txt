### Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

**Summary of Claims and Contributions**
This paper presents a novel, end-to-end learned approach to lossy image compression. The proposed model is framed as a nonlinear transform coding system, comprising an analysis transform (encoder), a uniform quantizer, and a synthesis transform (decoder). The core of the method lies in its use of highly flexible, nonlinear transforms built from cascaded convolutional layers and a biologically-inspired nonlinearity known as Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN).

A key technical contribution is the method for optimizing the entire system for a rate-distortion objective. To overcome the non-differentiability of the quantization step, the authors introduce a continuous proxy for the loss function by replacing the quantizer with additive uniform noise during training. This elegant relaxation enables optimization via standard stochastic gradient descent. The paper also draws an insightful connection between this relaxed optimization problem and the objective function of variational autoencoders (VAEs), clarifying both the similarities and the crucial differences between generative modeling and compression.

Empirically, the authors demonstrate that their method, optimized for mean squared error (MSE), outperforms standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of rate-distortion performance (measured in PSNR) on a standard test set. More strikingly, the paper shows a dramatic improvement in perceptual quality, supported by both visual examples and substantially better MS-SSIM scores across all tested bit rates.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Effective Framework:** The paper introduces a complete and well-motivated framework for end-to-end optimization of image compression. The proposed continuous relaxation for handling the non-differentiable quantizer is a clever and effective solution to a fundamental challenge in this domain. This work represents a significant step forward in applying deep learning to a classical signal processing problem.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Results and Superior Visual Quality:** The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate a clear advantage over established, highly engineered standards like JPEG 2000. The most compelling evidence is the remarkable improvement in visual quality; the resulting images lack the typical blocky or ringing artifacts of linear transform codes and instead exhibit much more natural-looking degradations. This qualitative leap is a major achievement.

**Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper is technically strong and the approach is well-grounded. The problem formulation as a rate-distortion optimization is clear and principled. By introducing a continuous and differentiable proxy for the loss function, the authors successfully bridge the gap between the discrete nature of compression and the continuous nature of gradient-based optimization. The validation in Figure 4, showing that the relaxed loss is a reasonable proxy for the true rate and distortion, adds confidence in the method.

The connection drawn to VAEs is insightful. It situates the work within the broader context of modern generative models while carefully articulating the specific requirements of compression (e.g., optimizing for a trade-off parameter λ rather than maximizing a lower bound). This discussion is valuable for both the compression and machine learning communities.

The experimental evaluation is thorough. The authors compare against strong and widely-used baselines on a standard dataset. Crucially, they go beyond reporting entropy estimates and implement a complete system with an arithmetic entropy coder to report actual bit rates. This makes the comparison to other codecs fair and demonstrates the practical feasibility of the approach. The consistent and significant gains in MS-SSIM, a well-regarded perceptual metric, strongly support the claims of improved visual quality.

**Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback for strengthening the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Ablation Study on GDN:** The paper attributes the model's efficiency and performance in part to the use of GDN/IGDN nonlinearities. While the discussion section mentions preliminary experiments with other activation functions, a more formal ablation study would be very valuable. Including rate-distortion curves for an identical architecture using, for example, ReLU activations would help to quantify the specific benefit of GDN over more standard choices. This would help clarify how much of the performance gain comes from the end-to-end optimization framework itself versus the specific choice of nonlinearity.
*   **Analysis of Computational Complexity:** For practical applications, the computational cost of encoding and decoding is a critical factor. The paper states that the process is efficient but does not provide quantitative figures. Including inference times (e.g., seconds per megapixel) on specified hardware (CPU/GPU) and comparing them to software implementations of JPEG 2000 would significantly enhance the paper's practical relevance.
*   **Rate Adaptability:** The current method requires training a separate model for each desired point on the rate-distortion curve. This is a potential drawback compared to codecs that can generate a scalable bitstream from a single encoding pass. A brief discussion of the challenges and potential future directions for creating a single, rate-adaptive model would be a welcome addition. For instance, could λ be provided as an input to the network?

**Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points during the discussion period:

*   The paper notes that the architecture choices were "somewhat ad-hoc". Have you performed any exploration of the architectural design space, such as varying the network depth, number of channels, or filter sizes? How sensitive is the performance to these hyperparameters?
*   In Figure 4, the relaxed entropy appears to be a biased estimator of the discrete entropy, particularly at lower rates. While the paper argues this simply re-scales λ, could this systematic bias in the gradient estimate for the rate term potentially hinder the optimization, especially in the low bit-rate regime where the approximation is less accurate?
*   The entropy model assumes independent marginals for the latent coefficients. This seems like a simplification, as one might expect spatial or cross-channel dependencies to remain. Have you considered incorporating a more sophisticated context model into the entropy coder? Do you believe there are significant further gains to be made by modeling these dependencies?
