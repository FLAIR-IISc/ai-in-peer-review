This paper presents a novel framework for lossy image compression based on a deep neural network optimized in an end-to-end fashion. The authors propose an autoencoder-like architecture featuring a non-linear analysis transform, a uniform quantizer, and a non-linear synthesis transform. The transforms are built from convolutional layers and a Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) non-linearity. A key contribution is the use of a continuous and differentiable proxy for the rate-distortion loss function, which allows the entire system to be optimized directly for compression performance using stochastic gradient descent.

I strongly recommend this paper for **Acceptance**. The work presents a significant advance in image compression for two main reasons. First, the proposed method achieves compelling results, outperforming standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000, especially at low bit rates. The improvement in perceptual quality, supported by both visual examples and MS-SSIM metrics, is particularly remarkable. Second, the methodology is sound, rigorous, and clearly presented, providing a principled and effective approach to a challenging optimization problem.

The paper’s strengths are manifold. The authors thoroughly motivate their approach, grounding their architectural choices in prior work on modeling natural image statistics. The technical solution for optimizing through the non-differentiable quantizer is clever and empirically validated. A significant strength is the rigorous evaluation; unlike much related work that only reports entropy estimates, this paper implements a full entropy coding scheme (CABAC) and reports actual bit rates, making the comparisons to established standards fair and convincing. The discussion thoughtfully differentiates the rate-distortion objective from the objectives of related generative models like VAEs, clarifying the paper's specific contributions.

For further improvement of the paper, I offer the following suggestions:
*   The discussion briefly mentions that GDN is more efficient than rectified linear units. A small ablation study comparing GDN against more standard activations would provide quantitative evidence for this claim and strengthen the motivation for this specific architectural choice.
*   The framework requires training a separate model for each point on the rate-distortion curve. It would be valuable for the authors to discuss the practical limitations of this approach and comment on the potential for extending the model to support variable bit rates within a single network.

I have a few questions for the authors to aid my understanding:
1.  The continuous approximation for rate appears biased in the low-rate regime (Figure 4). Could you elaborate on why this bias does not seem to negatively impact the optimization, particularly in this regime where the visual improvements are most significant?
2.  Could you provide more intuition on the sensitivity of the model’s performance to the network architecture (e.g., depth, number of filters)?
