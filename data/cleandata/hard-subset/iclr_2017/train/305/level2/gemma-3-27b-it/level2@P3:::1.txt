This paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression method based on nonlinear transforms, inspired by biological neurons. The core idea is to learn analysis and synthesis transforms, coupled with uniform quantization, to minimize rate-distortion performance. The work builds upon prior research by the authors on perceptual image modeling and extends it to a complete compression framework, including an entropy coding stage for reporting actual bit rates. The paper demonstrates improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of both rate-distortion performance and visual quality, as measured by MS-SSIM.

The decision is to **accept** with minor revisions. The work presents a novel approach to image compression, demonstrates compelling results, and is well-motivated within the broader context of probabilistic modeling and neural network architectures. The improvements in visual quality are particularly noteworthy.

The paper is generally well-written and clear. The motivation for using GDN and the connection to biological neurons is well-explained. The optimization framework is described in detail, and the experimental setup seems appropriate. The thorough comparison with JPEG and JPEG 2000, along with the inclusion of MS-SSIM metrics, strengthens the claims. The authors carefully address the challenges of optimizing with quantization by employing a continuous relaxation and establishing its connection to variational autoencoders, showcasing a strong theoretical understanding. 

However, several areas could benefit from further clarification or elaboration. First, while the ablation study of GDN versus other activation functions is mentioned, the results are only stated as preliminary. Providing more concrete details regarding the performance differences, even if in an appendix, would be valuable. Second, the choice of the specific network architecture (number of filters, stage configurations) appears somewhat ad-hoc. A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these architectural choices, or an attempt to systematically optimize them, could strengthen the work. Third, the connection between the continuous relaxation and the discrete quantization is a core aspect of the method. While the authors discuss this, providing a more detailed analysis of the approximation error and its impact on performance would be beneficial. Specifically, showing how the approximation accuracy changes with varying quantization levels, and relating this to the observed rate-distortion performance, could provide further insights. Lastly, the paper mentions the computational cost of training. Quantifying this cost (e.g., training time, hardware requirements) would provide a more complete picture of the practicality of the proposed method.

Additional feedback:

*   The paper would benefit from a diagram illustrating the flow of information through the entire compression/decompression pipeline, showing the analysis transform, quantizer, entropy coding, and corresponding inverse operations.
*   The authors mention an online repository with test images. Ensuring that the repository is easily accessible and well-documented would greatly enhance the reproducibility of the results.
*   It would be helpful to include a discussion of the limitations of the proposed method, such as its computational complexity or its performance on specific types of images (e.g., those with very high frequencies or complex textures).
*   Regarding the entropy coding stage, the authors state it only “crudely exploits spatial adaptation.” Expanding on the potential for improvements in this area and outlining future directions would be worthwhile.
*   Clarify how the initial probability models for the CABAC encoder were derived from the continuous densities and whether any adaptive updating occurred during the encoding process.
