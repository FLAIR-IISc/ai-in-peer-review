This paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression method based on nonlinear transforms, inspired by biological visual systems. The authors jointly optimize the analysis and synthesis transforms, along with an entropy coding scheme, for rate-distortion performance. A key innovation is the use of a generalized divisive normalization (GDN) as a joint nonlinearity and a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization. The paper demonstrates superior performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of both objective metrics (MS-SSIM) and visual quality.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a compelling approach and demonstrates promising results, the claims of substantial visual improvement, particularly given the complexity of the method, aren’t fully substantiated by the presented evidence. The experimental evaluation feels somewhat incomplete and relies heavily on subjective assessment of visual quality, supplemented by MS-SSIM, without a rigorous analysis of the statistical significance of these improvements. Furthermore, the paper lacks a sufficiently detailed comparison of computational costs beyond stating encoding/decoding efficiency.

The primary reason for rejection lies in the lack of rigorous statistical analysis supporting the subjective visual improvements. The authors state “remarkably, we find that the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested, and at all bit rates.” However, the presented figures (e.g., Figure 7) show MS-SSIM improvements, but without error bars or statistical tests to confirm these improvements are not due to chance. Simply showing improved MS-SSIM across a set of images doesn’t definitively prove a perceptually significant difference, particularly given the known limitations of MS-SSIM as a perfect proxy for human perception. The claim of “dramatic improvement in visual quality” feels overstated without stronger statistical backing.

Secondly, the detailed comparison with JPEG and JPEG 2000 is not completely fair. The authors acknowledge the potential for differing optimizations within the comparison codecs. While they attempt to mitigate this, a more controlled comparison with explicitly tuned parameters for JPEG and JPEG 2000, even if it requires significant effort, would strengthen their claims. The reliance on a specific OpenJPEG implementation introduces potential implementation-specific biases.

Finally, while the paper acknowledges computational complexity, it doesn’t provide concrete numbers for encoding/decoding time compared to the standard methods. This is a crucial factor for practical deployment, and its omission weakens the assessment of the method’s overall utility. The appendix details the optimization process, which appears computationally intensive, raising concerns about the feasibility of real-time compression/decompression.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Statistical Significance:** Conduct statistical tests (e.g., paired t-tests) on MS-SSIM scores to demonstrate the significance of the observed improvements. Provide confidence intervals.
*   **Ablation Study:** Investigate the contribution of the GDN nonlinearity. What performance is achieved with simpler activation functions (ReLU, sigmoid)? This would help justify the complexity of the proposed architecture.
*   **Computational Cost:** Provide detailed timing results for encoding and decoding, comparing the proposed method to JPEG and JPEG 2000. Analyze the computational complexity in terms of operations (FLOPs) as a function of image size and bit rate.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:**  Analyze the sensitivity of the method to the trade-off parameter λ.  How does the optimal value of λ vary across different images and bit rate requirements?
*   **Entropy Coding:** Elaborate on the entropy coding scheme.  While CABAC is mentioned, a more detailed explanation of the context modeling and arithmetic coding stages would be valuable.  How does the performance of the implemented entropy coder compare to optimized implementations?
*   **Training Data:** Discuss the potential impact of the ImageNet training set on the generalization performance of the method. Are there biases present in ImageNet that might affect the results on different types of images?
*   **Clarify the relaxed loss function:** Provide a clearer explanation of how the continuous relaxation of the quantizer impacts the training process. What is the expected error introduced by this approximation?
*   **Perceptual Metric:** While MS-SSIM is used, consider other perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) for a more comprehensive evaluation of visual quality.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide statistical evidence (e.g., p-values, confidence intervals) to support the claim of significant visual improvement over JPEG and JPEG 2000?
*   What are the encoding/decoding times for your method compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 for a typical image and bit rate?
*   How sensitive is the performance of your method to the choice of the GDN parameters (e.g., the exponents controlling the tail behavior)?
*   How was the value of λ selected for each image, and how does this selection process affect the results?
