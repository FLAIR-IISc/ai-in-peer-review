## Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression method based on cascaded nonlinear transformations, inspired by biological neurons. The core idea is to jointly optimize the analysis and synthesis transforms, along with a uniform quantizer, for rate-distortion performance using stochastic gradient descent. A continuous relaxation of the quantization step, interpreted as a log-likelihood of a variational autoencoder, enables this optimization. The authors demonstrate improved rate-distortion performance and, crucially, significant visual quality gains compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 across a diverse set of images.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is well-motivated and addresses a long-standing challenge in image compression: jointly optimizing all components of the pipeline. The observed improvements in visual quality are particularly compelling, suggesting a potentially significant advance over existing standards. 

The paper effectively builds upon prior work in nonlinear transforms and perceptual coding (specifically referencing their earlier work on MSE optimization and GDN). The use of a continuous relaxation of the quantization loss is a clever technique to enable gradient-based optimization, and the connection to variational autoencoders provides a valuable theoretical perspective. The experimental results are comprehensive, demonstrating performance gains on the Kodak dataset and a variety of other images. The extensive supplemental material and availability of test images are also commendable.

However, there are areas for improvement. The detailed explanation of the training procedure (e.g., parameter initialization, learning rate schedules) could be more thorough. While the authors acknowledge the computational cost of training, quantifying it would be useful. Additionally, a more in-depth analysis of the learned transforms would be insightful – what features are they learning, and how do they differ from those learned by standard convolutional neural networks? Finally, the discussion of the limitations of using MSE as a training metric, despite achieving superior visual quality, could be expanded.

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   Could you provide more details on the computational resources required to train the models, including training time and hardware specifications?
*   Can you visualize or characterize the learned filters in the analysis transform? Do they exhibit recognizable features or patterns?
*   You mention the potential for using perceptual metrics during training. Have you explored this, and if so, what challenges did you encounter?
*   How sensitive is the performance of the method to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the number of filters and the downsampling factors?
