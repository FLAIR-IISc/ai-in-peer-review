This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression method based on cascaded nonlinear transforms inspired by biological visual systems. The authors jointly optimize the analysis and synthesis transforms, along with an entropy coder, for rate-distortion performance using stochastic gradient descent. A key innovation is the use of a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization. The results demonstrate superior rate-distortion performance and improved visual quality compared to standard JPEG and JPEG 2000.

**Decision: Accept**

The primary reasons for acceptance are the novel approach to end-to-end compression optimization, the strong experimental results demonstrating improvements over existing standards, and the clear and well-structured presentation of the work. The authors effectively bridge concepts from neural network optimization, information theory, and computational neuroscience to create a compelling compression scheme.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Novelty and Approach:** The paper tackles the challenging problem of joint rate-distortion optimization in image compression. The use of cascaded nonlinear transforms, inspired by biological systems, is a novel departure from traditional linear transform coding approaches. The continuous relaxation of the quantization step is a clever solution to enable gradient-based optimization, a significant technical contribution. The connection to variational autoencoders is insightful and provides a useful perspective on the method.

2. **Strong Experimental Results:** The authors provide comprehensive experimental results on the Kodak dataset and other images, demonstrating consistently better performance than JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of both rate-distortion metrics (MSE) and perceptual quality (MS-SSIM). The inclusion of subjective visual comparisons further strengthens the claims. The availability of test images and results online is highly commendable.

3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the method. The organization is logical, and the figures are informative. The authors also acknowledge limitations and potential future work, which demonstrates a thoughtful and balanced perspective. The related work section is well-curated and positions the paper appropriately within the existing literature.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

1. **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention the high computational cost of training, a more detailed discussion of the training time and resource requirements would be valuable. A comparison of the computational cost of encoding/decoding with JPEG/JPEG 2000 would also be useful.

2. **Ablation Studies:** It would be beneficial to include ablation studies to investigate the contribution of different components of the model. For example, how much does the GDN nonlinearity contribute to the performance gain compared to simpler activation functions?  How does the number of stages in the cascade affect performance?

3. **Entropy Code Details:** The description of the entropy coding scheme is somewhat brief. More details about the context modeling and arithmetic coding implementation would be helpful. A comparison of the compression ratio achieved by the CABAC coder with a simpler entropy coder (e.g., Huffman coding) could further illustrate its effectiveness.

4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief analysis of the sensitivity of the performance to the choice of the trade-off parameter λ would be helpful. Are there certain ranges of λ where the performance is particularly sensitive, and what are the implications for practical use?

5. **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are strong, a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the benefits of the proposed approach would further enhance the paper.  Could some of the observed gains be explained by information-theoretic principles or properties of the nonlinear transforms?

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that the relaxed distortion term is mostly unbiased.  Could you provide more quantitative details on the bias and variance of the relaxed entropy estimation?  Specifically, how does the accuracy of the entropy approximation vary with the fineness of the sampling in the piecewise linear function representation?

2.  You state that the GDN/IGDN transforms are more efficient for compression than rectified linear units.  Have you investigated the computational cost difference between the two approaches? Is the higher parameter count in the ReLU-based models the primary reason for the performance difference, or are there other factors at play?

3.  The choice of the symmetric constraint on the weight matrix in GDN is mentioned. What motivated this choice, and did you experiment with alternative constraints or without any constraints at all?

4.  In section 6.3, you discuss averaging rate-distortion curves.  Are there any specific strategies you employed to address the variability in performance across different images when comparing against JPEG and JPEG 2000?

5.  Could you elaborate on how the parameters of the entropy code (beyond the initial models) are adapted during the encoding process?  Specifically, how is the context modeling updated based on the input image?
