

This paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression method using nonlinear transforms with a generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearity inspired by biological visual systems. The authors propose a framework that jointly optimizes the analysis transform, quantizer, and synthesis transform for rate-distortion performance using stochastic gradient descent with a continuous relaxation of the quantization step. They demonstrate that their method outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 in both rate-distortion performance and visual quality across multiple test images and bit rates, with consistent improvements in MS-SSIM metrics.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression by demonstrating that end-to-end optimization of the entire compression pipeline can yield substantial improvements over standard methods. The continuous relaxation approach for handling quantization is well-motivated and effectively enables gradient-based optimization. The experimental results are compelling, showing both quantitative improvements in PSNR and MS-SSIM, as well as clear qualitative improvements in visual quality that are supported by visual examples. The authors provide sufficient implementation details to enable reproducibility, including the entropy code and training methodology.

The approach is well-placed in the literature, with appropriate discussion of related work in transform coding, generative models, and biological visual systems. The distinction between their framework and variational autoencoders is particularly well-explained, addressing a potential point of confusion in the field.

Additional feedback:
1. While the paper claims "dramatic improvement in visual quality," it would be strengthened by including a brief user study or more formal perceptual evaluation beyond MS-SSIM to support this claim.
2. The comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 should clarify whether the standard implementations were tuned to their optimal performance for fair comparison.
3. The paper states "better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most images" but lacks statistical significance analysis for these claims. Providing such analysis would strengthen the empirical contribution.
4. The paper uses ImageNet for training but doesn't thoroughly discuss how this might affect performance on standard test sets like Kodak. A brief analysis of potential training set bias would be valuable.

Questions for authors:
1. How did you handle the fact that JPEG and JPEG 2000 are typically optimized for specific quality settings, while your method is optimized for specific λ values? Did you compare at equivalent bit rates or equivalent quality settings?
2. The paper states that "the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested, and at all bit rates." What specific perceptual metrics (beyond MS-SSIM) were used to support this claim, and how were they validated?
3. The appendix mentions testing simpler nonlinearities like ReLU. Could you elaborate on the trade-offs between model complexity and performance when using different nonlinearities, and why GDN appears to be more efficient for compression?
