

The paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression method using nonlinear transforms, specifically designed to jointly optimize the analysis transform, quantizer, and synthesis transform for rate-distortion performance. The authors introduce a generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearity inspired by biological visual systems, which they demonstrate is more effective than standard linear transforms for image compression. The key innovation is the use of a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization, allowing the entire model to be trained end-to-end. The method achieves better rate-distortion performance than JPEG and JPEG 2000 across multiple test images, with particularly notable improvements in visual quality as measured by MS-SSIM, even when trained with MSE as the distortion metric.

The paper should be accepted. The work addresses a significant problem in image compression with a well-motivated approach that makes a clear contribution to the field. The authors provide strong empirical evidence supporting their claims, and the method has clear potential for practical application.

The approach is well-placed in the literature, with a thorough comparison to existing methods and a clear explanation of how it differs from variational autoencoders and other generative models. The theoretical foundation for the continuous relaxation of the quantization step is sound, and the authors effectively demonstrate how their method avoids the limitations of using continuous-domain approximations for discrete compression problems. The experimental design is robust, with evaluations across multiple image datasets, bit rates, and quality metrics (PSNR and MS-SSIM), and the results consistently show improvements over standard compression methods.

The paper's key contribution lies in demonstrating that end-to-end optimization of a nonlinear transform coding model can achieve superior rate-distortion performance while also producing visually more natural images. The authors provide compelling evidence that the biologically-inspired GDN nonlinearity is more efficient than pointwise nonlinearities for compression tasks, requiring fewer parameters to achieve comparable performance. The visual quality improvements across all test images and bit rates are particularly noteworthy, as they address a common limitation of traditional transform coding methods.

The paper could be strengthened by providing more detailed information about the computational requirements of training and inference, as this is important for practical deployment. While the authors mention that encoding and decoding are efficient, a more quantitative comparison of inference speed with standard methods would be valuable. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more thorough exploration of the relationship between the number of GDN layers and compression performance, as the current implementation uses a fixed three-stage architecture.

The authors should clarify the exact relationship between their method and the recent work by Toderici et al. (2016) that also optimizes for multiple rate-distortion points. While the paper mentions this comparison, it would be helpful to explicitly state how the two approaches differ in terms of optimization guarantees and practical implementation.

The paper's claim that the method produces "dramatic improvement in visual quality" is well-supported by the visual examples and MS-SSIM results. However, the authors could strengthen this claim by including a user study to quantify the perceptual advantage, as this would provide additional evidence beyond objective metrics.

The paper's explanation of the continuous relaxation for the quantization step is technically sound and well-motivated. The authors correctly acknowledge the limitations of using continuous-domain approximations for discrete compression problems and demonstrate through experiments that their approach provides a good approximation to actual rate-distortion performance.

The authors should consider addressing the following questions to further strengthen the paper: How does the method scale to higher resolution images? What are the specific computational requirements for training and inference compared to standard compression methods? How does the performance compare to more recent state-of-the-art compression methods beyond JPEG and JPEG 2000?

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression by demonstrating the power of end-to-end optimization with nonlinear transforms. The results are compelling, the methodology is sound, and the work has clear potential for practical application in image compression systems. The paper successfully bridges the gap between theoretical rate-distortion optimization and practical image compression, offering a method that outperforms established standards in both objective and perceptual quality metrics.
