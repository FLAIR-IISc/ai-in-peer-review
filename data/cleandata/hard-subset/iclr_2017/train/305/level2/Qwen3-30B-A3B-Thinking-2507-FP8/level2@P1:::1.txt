

Summary: The paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression framework using nonlinear transformations inspired by biological visual systems. The authors propose a joint optimization of analysis and synthesis transforms along with quantization, using a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization. The method demonstrates superior rate-distortion performance and significantly improved visual quality compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 across various bit rates, as measured by both PSNR and MS-SSIM.

Decision: I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of image compression with a well-motivated approach that is theoretically sound and empirically validated.

Supporting arguments: The paper addresses a fundamental problem in image compression with a novel solution that effectively combines deep learning with rate-distortion theory. The theoretical foundation for the continuous relaxation of the quantization step is well-justified, and the experimental results are thorough and convincing. The authors provide a clear comparison to both traditional methods (JPEG, JPEG 2000) and related deep learning approaches (Gregor et al., Toderici et al.). The visual quality improvements are particularly notable and well-documented with objective metrics, not just subjective impressions. The paper also makes important distinctions between their approach and variational autoencoders, which helps clarify the unique contribution.

Additional feedback: The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison of computational complexity, particularly regarding the trade-off between training time and encoding/decoding efficiency. While the authors mention that "encoding or decoding an image with the trained models is efficient," providing concrete numbers would help readers assess the practicality of the method. Additionally, the discussion of GDN/IGDN versus simpler nonlinearities (ReLU, sigmoid) is somewhat brief; a more comprehensive analysis of the efficiency gains from using biologically-inspired nonlinearities would strengthen the paper. The authors should also clarify the exact relationship between their method and the work by Toderici et al. (2016), particularly regarding the single representation for all rate points.

Questions for authors: 
1. The paper states that "computational costs for training our models are quite high" but encoding/decoding is efficient. Could you provide specific timing comparisons (training time, encoding time, decoding time) for your method versus JPEG and JPEG 2000 on standard hardware?

2. You mention that "rectified linear units generally require a substantially larger number of model parameters/stages to achieve the same rate-distortion performance as the GDN/IGDN nonlinearities." Could you elaborate on the specific parameter counts or architectural differences that lead to this efficiency advantage, and whether this holds for both grayscale and RGB images?

3. Given that your method was optimized for MSE but achieves better perceptual quality, could you discuss whether the GDN nonlinearities might inherently capture some aspects of perceptual quality, or if this improvement is primarily due to the end-to-end optimization approach?
