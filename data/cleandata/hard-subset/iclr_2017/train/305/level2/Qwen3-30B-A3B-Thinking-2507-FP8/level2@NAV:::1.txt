

This paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression method using nonlinear transforms inspired by biological visual systems. The authors develop a framework that jointly optimizes analysis and synthesis transforms with a uniform quantizer using a continuous relaxation of the quantization step. The key claim is that this approach achieves better rate-distortion performance than standard methods (JPEG, JPEG 2000) while providing significantly improved visual quality across all bit rates, as evidenced by both PSNR and MS-SSIM metrics.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of image compression by demonstrating that end-to-end optimization of nonlinear transforms can substantially improve visual quality compared to conventional methods. The experimental results are compelling, with detailed comparisons across multiple datasets showing consistent improvements in visual quality and competitive rate-distortion performance. The authors provide sufficient architectural details and training procedures for reproducibility, and their theoretical connection to variational autoencoders (while highlighting key differences) is well-motivated.

The paper's strength lies in its practical demonstration of a complete compression pipeline that achieves both quantitative and perceptual improvements. The visual comparisons are particularly convincing, showing how their method avoids the blocking and ringing artifacts common in linear transform coding methods. The use of GDN as a nonlinear activation function is well-justified through references to prior work on image statistics.

To further strengthen the paper, I suggest:
1) Including a more detailed comparison of computational complexity between the proposed method and standard compression algorithms, particularly regarding encoding/decoding speed.
2) Providing more analysis on the specific image types where the method shows the most significant improvements versus JPEG/JPEG 2000.
3) Clarifying the relationship between the relaxed optimization approach and the final discrete implementation, especially regarding the entropy coding component.

I would appreciate clarification on how the authors selected the specific values for the downsampling factors and filter sizes across the three stages of their transform. Additionally, could the authors elaborate on the limitations of using MSE as the primary distortion metric, particularly in cases where perceptual quality might differ from PSNR trends?
