## Review of “END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION”

This paper presents a compelling end-to-end optimized image compression method based on nonlinear transforms, achieving impressive results and offering a fresh perspective on a well-established problem. The approach, leveraging cascaded convolutions, GDN nonlinearities, and a continuous relaxation of the quantization step for optimization, is both innovative and rigorously grounded in rate-distortion theory. The writing is exceptionally clear and the motivation is well-articulated, seamlessly integrating theoretical concepts with practical implementation details. The comparison to JPEG and, crucially, JPEG2000 is appropriate and demonstrates a clear advantage, particularly in visual quality. I anticipate this work will have a significant impact on the field of image compression and potentially inspire further research in learned image representations.

I recommend **Accept** with enthusiasm. The paper tackles a fundamental problem with a novel and effective approach, and the results are convincingly demonstrated. The clarity of the presentation and strong theoretical foundation further solidify its value.

The strength of this paper lies in its holistic approach to compression, optimizing the entire pipeline rather than individual components. The use of a continuous relaxation for optimization is a clever solution to the non-differentiability of the quantization step, and the connection to variational autoencoders provides valuable context. The empirical results, showing superior performance to JPEG and JPEG2000, are particularly noteworthy, especially the improvement in visual quality. The authors have clearly demonstrated a significant advancement in the field.

However, to further strengthen the paper, I suggest including results on standard, widely-used images like Lena, Barbara, and Baboon. This would facilitate direct comparison with a broader range of existing literature. Additionally, while the comparison to JPEG2000 is excellent, explicitly referencing and comparing against state-of-the-art *classical* compression methods (e.g., BPG, WebP) would provide a more comprehensive benchmark. Finally, while the paper acknowledges the differences with variational autoencoders, a clearer positioning of this work *relative* to other recent neural network-based compression methods (e.g., those based on autoencoders or GANs) with explicit performance comparisons would be beneficial. 

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors:

1.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, number of stages, filter sizes)?
2.  The paper mentions that the continuous relaxation provides a good approximation to the actual rate-distortion values. Could you provide more detail on the error bounds or variance of this approximation, perhaps with a more detailed analysis of Figure 4?
3.  Regarding computational cost, what is the approximate training time for a single model (given λ)? And what is the encoding/decoding speed compared to JPEG/JPEG2000?
