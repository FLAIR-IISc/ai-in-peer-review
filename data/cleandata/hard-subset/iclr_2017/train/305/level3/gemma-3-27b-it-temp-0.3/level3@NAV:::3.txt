## Review of “END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION”

This paper presents an end-to-end trained image compression system that demonstrably outperforms established methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of rate-distortion performance and, crucially, visual quality. The core technical contribution lies in a differentiable approximation of the rate function, enabling effective joint optimization of the entire compression pipeline – analysis transform, quantizer, and synthesis transform – across different rate-distortion trade-offs. This is a significant achievement, as traditional compression methods typically optimize these components separately. The authors’ thoughtful response to the pre-review question further demonstrates their engagement with the problem and the community.

I **accept** this paper. The approach is novel and well-motivated, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate its superiority over existing methods. The potential impact extends beyond compression, as the differentiable rate approximation could be valuable in other applications requiring differentiable approximations of similar functions.

The paper effectively tackles the problem of lossy image compression by moving away from hand-engineered components and towards a fully learned, optimized system. The motivation is strong, rooted in the limitations of traditional transform coding and the potential of deep learning to learn more effective representations. The literature review is appropriate, positioning the work within the context of both classical compression techniques and recent advances in generative modeling. The authors convincingly argue that enforcing a specific form on the compressor outputs (through the differentiable rate approximation) is crucial for achieving good compression performance, as a sufficiently complex network could otherwise trivially learn to maximize entropy within a fixed range. 

The results are well-supported by extensive experiments on standard datasets (Kodak, ImageNet subsets) and demonstrate consistent improvements in both objective (PSNR, MS-SSIM) and subjective visual quality. The inclusion of online access to the compressed images for direct comparison is a valuable contribution.

However, a few points could further strengthen the paper. While the authors acknowledge the computational cost of training, a more detailed discussion of the resources required (e.g., training time, GPU memory) would be helpful. Additionally, exploring the sensitivity of the method to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, network architecture) would provide further insight. 

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors:

1.  The paper mentions that the relaxed loss function can be interpreted as the log-likelihood of a generative model. Could you elaborate on the implications of this connection? Does this suggest potential avenues for leveraging generative modeling techniques to further improve compression performance?
2.  The choice of GDN as the nonlinear activation function seems crucial. What experiments were conducted to evaluate the performance of alternative activation functions (e.g., ReLU, sigmoid)?
3.  The entropy code implemented is described as a simple CABAC-based approach. How does the performance of this entropy code compare to more sophisticated entropy coding schemes commonly used in state-of-the-art image compression standards (e.g., arithmetic coding with optimized contexts)? Would incorporating a more advanced entropy coder further improve the compression ratio?
