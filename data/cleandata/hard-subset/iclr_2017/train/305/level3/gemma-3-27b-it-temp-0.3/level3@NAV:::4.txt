## Review of “END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION”

This paper presents a compelling end-to-end learning method for image compression, leveraging rate-distortion optimization with a clever continuous relaxation technique. The authors construct compression transforms using cascaded convolutional linear filters and nonlinear activations, inspired by biological neurons, and demonstrate superior performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 on standard datasets. The experimental results are thorough, and the analysis provides valuable insights into the learned representations.

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The approach is novel and addresses a fundamental problem in image processing with a well-motivated and effective solution. The improvements in visual quality, particularly the reduction of artifacts common in traditional methods, are significant and represent a substantial contribution.

My recommendation is supported by the following arguments. First, the paper tackles a well-defined and important problem – improving image compression performance while prioritizing perceptual quality. Second, the approach is well-motivated, building upon existing work in transform coding, generative models, and neuroscience. The authors clearly position their work within the literature and explain the rationale behind their design choices, such as the use of GDN nonlinearities. Finally, the paper convincingly supports its claims with comprehensive experimental results. The comparisons to JPEG and JPEG 2000 are rigorous, and the use of both objective (PSNR, MS-SSIM) and subjective evaluation (visual examples) strengthens the conclusions. The inclusion of code and compressed images online further enhances reproducibility and allows for detailed inspection of the results.

To further improve the paper, I suggest the authors consider the following. While the connection to variational autoencoders is insightful, a more detailed discussion of the differences in optimization objectives and constraints would be beneficial. Additionally, exploring the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to existing standards would provide a more complete picture of its practicality. 

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors: 
1.  The paper mentions that the relaxed loss function approximates the discrete problem “under certain conditions.” Could you elaborate on these conditions and provide a more formal analysis of the approximation error?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the method to the choice of the λ parameter? Is there a systematic way to select an appropriate value of λ for a given target bit rate or distortion level?
3.  The authors mention that preliminary results with simpler nonlinearities (ReLU, sigmoid) are promising. Could you provide a more quantitative comparison of the performance of these alternatives to the GDN nonlinearity, perhaps in an appendix?
