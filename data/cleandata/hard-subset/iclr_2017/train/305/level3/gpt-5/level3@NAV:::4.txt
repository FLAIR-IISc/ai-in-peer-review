Summary and contributions: The paper tackles the classical problem of lossy image compression by proposing an end-to-end learned transform coding system optimized directly for rate–distortion. The model comprises cascaded convolutional analysis/synthesis transforms with generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN), uniform scalar quantization, and a learned nonparametric marginal model for entropy coding. A key technical contribution is a principled relaxation that replaces quantization with additive uniform noise, enabling stochastic gradient descent while remaining faithful to the discrete objective; the authors also clarify its relationship to VAEs. The method is trained on a large natural image dataset and evaluated with an actual entropy coder (CABAC), reporting real bitrates. Experiments show compelling improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000 in MS-SSIM and visual quality across bitrates, with PSNR gains for most images.

Decision: Accept. The paper is well-motivated and well-situated in the literature, and it supports its claims with rigorous experiments and careful analysis of the relaxation, rate accounting, and visual quality.

Supporting arguments: 
- Problem clarity: The work squarely addresses end-to-end optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy model for image compression along the rate–distortion curve, a timely and impactful question. 
- Motivation and placement: The approach is grounded in transform coding and rate–distortion theory, connects cleanly to VAEs while highlighting crucial differences, and contextualizes against contemporary learned compression and generative models.
- Evidence and rigor: The authors validate the continuous relaxation versus discrete coding, use real bitstreams, compare on standard datasets, include both RGB and grayscale, and provide both objective (PSNR, MS-SSIM) and perceptual analyses. The ablations and architectural choices (GDN rationale) are discussed, and analysis is thorough.

Suggestions for improvement (non-blocking, to help strengthen the paper):
- Compare to stronger modern baselines (e.g., HEVC intra/BPG, WebP) and report BD-rate summaries where feasible.
- Report model size, encoding/decoding speed, and memory footprint; discuss deployment considerations.
- Explore a single model operating across rates (e.g., conditioning on λ) and stronger entropy models (spatial/context, hyperpriors) beyond independent marginals.
- Provide ablations quantifying GDN vs. ReLU/sigmoids and the impact of the marginal model resolution.
- Discuss generalization to higher resolutions and out-of-distribution content; consider releasing code/models.

Questions for the authors:
- How is λ selected in practice at encode time, and can a single conditional model cover multiple rate points without retraining?
- What are the parameter counts and runtimes for 1080p images on CPU/GPU?
- How much coding gain is left on the table due to the independent-marginal assumption; have you tried simple spatial/context models?
- How consistent is the relaxed vs. discrete rate/distortion gap across bitrates and images?
- Do the results hold without CABAC adaptation (i.e., with a fixed code), and how sensitive is performance to the learned marginal spline resolution?
- For RGB, any observations versus YCbCr coding or chroma subsampling?
