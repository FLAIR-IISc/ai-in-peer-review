Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes an end-to-end learned image compression framework that directly optimizes a nonlinear transform coder (analysis transform + quantizer + synthesis transform) for rate–distortion performance. The transforms are cascades of convolutions with a biologically inspired generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearity and its learned inverse (IGDN).
- A key technical contribution is a principled, practical relaxation of the discrete rate–distortion objective: the quantizer is replaced by additive uniform noise, enabling stochastic gradient descent while keeping a tight connection to the discrete problem. The relaxed objective is analyzed, related to VAEs, and used with nonparametric marginal models of the latent codes.
- The authors implement a full system with an actual entropy coder (CABAC) and report real bitrates. Experiments on independent test images (Kodak, classic test images, additional photos) show improved rate–distortion (especially at low rates) vs JPEG and JPEG 2000, and consistently better perceptual quality (MS-SSIM), with dramatically fewer artifacts. The analysis includes sanity checks on the relaxation bias and careful description of architecture and training.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
1) Strong, well-motivated approach that tackles compression as end-to-end rate–distortion optimization with a clever, effective relaxation bridging learning and coding. 
2) Compelling empirical results with real bitrates and broad qualitative gains, supported by rigorous analysis and linkage to the literature (transform coding, VAEs, normalization/GDN).

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? The paper addresses optimal lossy image compression: jointly shaping the analysis/synthesis transforms and the representation to optimize the rate–distortion tradeoff, rather than separately hand-designing transforms and quantizers. This is a central, important problem.
- Is the approach well placed and motivated? Yes. The method builds on transform coding and connects cleanly to probabilistic modeling and VAEs. The use of GDN is well grounded in statistical modeling of images and prior work. The relaxation with additive uniform noise is classical in quantization theory and is validated here for learning.
- Do results support claims? Largely yes. The paper:
  • Reports actual bitstreams via CABAC (not just entropy estimates), including side-information. 
  • Compares to JPEG and JPEG 2000 across images and rates, reporting PSNR and MS-SSIM, with image-by-image R–D curves available. 
  • Validates the relaxation empirically (approximation bias small; distortion unbiased; entropy bias diminishes at finer quantization). 
  • Demonstrates large and consistent perceptual improvements with clear qualitative examples. 
- Thoroughness and clarity: The architecture, training regimen, density modeling for latents, and entropy coding details are clearly described (with useful appendices). The relationship to VAEs is carefully articulated, including differences relevant for compression (discrete representations, operation across a range of λ).
- Practical relevance: The method is complete and feasible at inference time; encoding/decoding is efficient given trained models. Reporting real bitrates and including side information is a significant strength.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Comparisons: If feasible, include a comparison to a more modern baseline (e.g., HEVC/BPG intra coding) to strengthen the empirical case, even if only on a subset or without extensive tuning. At minimum, discuss expected relative performance.
- Ablations: Provide more systematic ablations to quantify the contribution of GDN/IGDN vs. pointwise nonlinearities, depth, and number of channels, beyond preliminary statements. Show R–D deltas and visual artifacts under alternative nonlinearities.
- Entropy model: The latent entropy model assumes independent marginals with limited adaptation. Discuss potential gains from lightweight spatial/context modeling (e.g., simple neighbors) and cross-channel coupling, and quantify the gap by measuring bits saved with your adaptive CABAC vs. a non-adaptive code (you partly do this; a clearer estimate of remaining dependency would be useful).
- Single model for multiple rates: Training a separate model per λ is practical but increases deployment complexity. Briefly discuss or experiment with conditioning (e.g., scale hyperparameters) to cover multiple rates with one model, or explain why you prioritize per-λ optimality.
- Evaluation protocol: You note challenges when averaging R–D across images. Consider adding a small aggregate summary using consistent target rates for all methods, alongside the per-image plots, to aid readers.
- Complexity: Include approximate encoding/decoding runtime and memory versus JPEG 2000, and parameter counts per λ, to contextualize deployment costs.
- Color handling: Since JPEG uses 4:2:0 and you train on RGB, clarify how color processing choices may affect fairness, and consider reporting luminance-only PSNR/MS-SSIM alongside RGB where applicable.

Questions for the authors
1) Entropy coding and adaptation:
   - How sensitive is bitrate to mismatches between the learned marginal models and test-image statistics? Did you measure coding loss when freezing vs. adapting CABAC contexts?
   - Do you share probability models across spatial positions only within a channel? Would simple spatial contexts (e.g., causal neighbors) provide significant additional savings?

2) Independent marginals:
   - What fraction of residual dependency remains in latents after GDN (e.g., average mutual information between nearby coefficients)? Any empirical estimate of “bits left on the table”?

3) Relaxation and gradients:
   - Did you compare the additive uniform noise relaxation to straight-through estimators or annealed noise? How sensitive are convergence and final R–D to the relaxation choice?

4) GDN/IGDN specifics:
   - Can you share quantitative ablations showing R–D differences when replacing GDN with ReLU or PReLU at similar capacity? How many more layers/parameters are needed to match performance?
   - How critical is the symmetric γ constraint, and did you test learned exponents vs fixed?

5) Multi-rate operation:
   - In practice, how is λ chosen at encode time? Do you envision deploying a small set of λ-specific models, or conditioning a single model? Any evidence that per-λ optimality materially outperforms a shared model?

6) Generalization and robustness:
   - How does performance vary across datasets with different statistics (e.g., high-ISO noise, graphics, medical images)? Any failure cases where JPEG 2000 still wins markedly in PSNR or MS-SSIM?

7) Visual quality:
   - You train with MSE but show strong MS-SSIM gains. Have you tried MS-SSIM-based training with the same framework, and if so, how do artifacts and R–D change?

Overall, this is a strong, timely paper that advances learned compression with a principled objective, careful engineering, and convincing results. I recommend acceptance.
