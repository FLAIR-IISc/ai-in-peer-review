Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes a complete, end-to-end image compression system built from learned nonlinear analysis/synthesis transforms (with GDN/IGDN), a uniform scalar quantizer, and a learned nonparametric entropy model. The entire pipeline is optimized directly for rate–distortion (R+λD) using stochastic gradient descent.
- The key technical contribution is a differentiable surrogate for the “rate” term: replacing quantization with additive uniform noise and learning marginal densities enables backpropagation through the codec, while remaining faithful to the discrete problem. The authors empirically verify that the relaxed loss tracks discrete rate and distortion well.
- The system achieves better rate–distortion than JPEG and JPEG2000 on the Kodak dataset (and additional images), particularly at low bitrates, while producing markedly better perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) and fewer typical linear-transform artifacts.
- The formulation is carefully connected to VAEs, clarifying similarities and differences, and the authors report actual bitrates using an implemented arithmetic coder, not just entropy estimates.
- The differentiable rate approach is of likely broader value for tasks that require differentiable approximations of discrete coding/quantization objectives.

Decision: Accept
- Reason 1: Strong empirical performance and clear practical relevance: real bitstreams, competitive or superior RD vs. JPEG/JPEG2000, and substantially improved visual quality.
- Reason 2: A principled, general differentiable rate relaxation that unlocks end-to-end training for compression and is likely impactful beyond this application.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Learning a transform coding system that is globally optimal for a chosen point on the rate–distortion curve, rather than optimizing transform, quantizer, and entropy model in isolation.
- Motivation and placement in literature: Excellent. The work is grounded in transform coding and rate–distortion theory, leverages insights from perceptual models (GDN), and explicitly relates to VAEs while emphasizing critical differences (discrete vs. continuous coding, operation along the entire RD curve, evaluation with actual bits).
- Evidence for claims: The authors (i) validate that the continuous relaxation approximates discrete rate and distortion; (ii) use a real entropy coder to report bits; (iii) provide comprehensive visual comparisons and MS-SSIM gains; and (iv) show PSNR improvements for most images/rates (with a candid discussion of exceptions).
- On the pre-review discussion: The authors provided a thoughtful response. I agree that, in principle, a sufficiently expressive network could learn to map codes into a fixed bounded range with near-maximal entropy (approaching an upper bound). However, I find the authors’ second argument convincing: enforcing a specific output form (e.g., fixed-range, fixed-marginal) would either force significantly greater network complexity to maintain RD optimality across operating points or necessitate a separate variable-rate encoder. The current design—learning transforms and marginal models per λ, with a differentiable rate surrogate—strikes a practical balance between optimality and complexity.
- Rigor and clarity: The architecture, training details, and evaluation protocol are described thoroughly (including code distribution of side information). The relationship to VAEs is insightful and technically careful. While ablations are limited, preliminary evidence suggests GDN/IGDN is parameter-efficient relative to common pointwise nonlinearities.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not central to my accept decision)
- Baselines: If space permits, include comparisons to a stronger contemporary baseline (e.g., HEVC-intra/BPG) to contextualize gains beyond JPEG2000, and/or strengthen the entropy model with more spatial/context modeling (the current raster-scan CABAC is intentionally simple).
- Variable-rate with a single model: Consider conditioning ga/gs on λ (or using learned per-channel scaling) to realize a single network that traces the RD curve, and compare against the per-λ models.
- Ablations: Expand analysis on the role of GDN vs. ReLU/other nonlinearities at matched parameter counts; quantify the performance/efficiency trade-offs.
- Entropy model: Discuss the impact of independence assumptions across channels/space; small-context autoregressive priors or hyperpriors could further tighten rates.
- Practical aspects: Report encode/decode runtimes, parameter counts, and memory footprint; quantify side-information overhead in bpp for the test set; and provide more details on color handling and any impact of chroma subsampling choices in baselines.

Questions for the authors
- Differentiable rate surrogate: How sensitive is training to the uniform noise width being exactly one? Did you try learning per-channel quantization step sizes or incorporating scale parameters into the relaxation?
- Independence assumption: What is the measured gap between differential entropy under the factorized model and the achieved arithmetic-coded rate? Have you tried lightweight spatial/context modeling to reduce this gap?
- Output distribution shape: Do you observe the encoder “filling” a bounded range uniformly (as per the upper-bound thought experiment) at any λ? Could you show representative histograms of latent coefficients across rates?
- Variable-rate encoder: Have you experimented with conditioning on λ (or a gain vector) to share one model across multiple RD points? How does performance compare to per-λ training?
- Stability and capacity: Did you observe optimization instabilities specific to GDN/IGDN (e.g., parameter blow-up), and how crucial were the parameterizations/normalizations you introduced? Any guidance on depth/width vs. RD performance?
- Generalization: How does performance scale to high-resolution images and to datasets with different statistics from ImageNet/Kodak? Any evidence of overfitting in the learned marginal models?
- Bitstream overhead: What is the average overhead for transmitting λ and model indices, and how does this scale if more operating points (λ values) are supported?
