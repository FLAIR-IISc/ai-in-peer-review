Summary and contributions
The paper introduces an end-to-end trainable image compression system that jointly optimizes analysis and synthesis transforms together with an entropy model for rate–distortion performance. The key technical idea is a continuous, differentiable relaxation of quantization and entropy: the quantizer is replaced by additive uniform noise, and the rate term is approximated via a nonparametric model of the relaxed latents’ marginal densities (yielding a surrogate differential entropy). The transforms are convolutional and include generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN), motivated by visual neuroscience and known to Gaussianize image statistics. The authors carefully relate their objective to the VAE ELBO under specific assumptions, but emphasize differences crucial for compression (discrete operation points along the R–D curve, actual bitrates). Importantly, evaluation uses a practical entropy coder (CABAC) and reports true bitrates. Empirically, the method outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 on most images in PSNR (especially at low rates) and shows substantial gains in MS-SSIM and visual quality across all rates. The differentiable rate approximation is a neat, broadly useful contribution for learning with quantized bottlenecks.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, well-motivated end-to-end formulation with a carefully engineered, differentiable proxy for rate that enables effective R–D optimization, and reporting of actual bitrates.
- Clear empirical improvements over JPEG/JPEG 2000 and consistent perceptual advantages, with convincing methodology and ablations validating the relaxed loss.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Learning an image codec directly from data that operates at chosen points on the rate–distortion curve, overcoming non-differentiability of quantization and coupling of transform, quantizer, and coder.
- Is the approach well motivated and well placed? Yes. The paper grounds the design in transform coding, information theory, and models of natural image statistics (GDN). The VAE connection is insightful and clarifies when the relaxed objective matches the ELBO. The work is positioned against recent learned compression/generative approaches and discusses practical differences (discrete coding, actual bitrates, operating across λ).
- Do results support claims? Largely yes. The authors:
  - Validate the continuous relaxation empirically: the distortion proxy is unbiased with low variance; the rate proxy’s bias shrinks with finer quantization and does not harm end performance since λ traces the convex hull.
  - Implement an entropy coder and report true bitrates including side information.
  - Compare on a standard test set (Kodak) and additional images, showing superior MS-SSIM at all rates and better PSNR for most images, with qualitative artifacts that are markedly more natural than block/ring/aliasing artifacts of linear codecs.
  - Provide architectural details and some preliminary evidence that GDN is parameter-efficient relative to standard pointwise nonlinearities.

On a potential concern that a sufficiently expressive network could “game” the rate proxy (e.g., drive latents to a fixed-range, high-entropy form), the paper’s design and experiments reduce this risk: the nonparametric marginal models adapt, the loss explicitly trades off rate with distortion under additive uniform noise, and, crucially, the actual evaluation uses quantization plus arithmetic coding. The authors’ reasoning that enforcing a specific form on codes would in practice require a more complex network or a separate variable-rate encoder to match the proposed system’s effectiveness is convincing, and their results corroborate it.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful; not required for acceptance)
- Variable-rate within a single model: Training a single model controllable by a λ-like parameter or a learned scale/importance map would improve practicality and fairness vs. variable-rate codecs. A brief exploration would strengthen the work.
- Ablations: Provide more systematic comparisons of GDN/IGDN vs. ReLU/parametric pointwise nonlinearities at matched parameter counts and depths to isolate the role of GDN in both RD performance and artifact characteristics.
- Entropy modeling: The independence assumption and relatively simple CABAC context limit potential gains; discussing learned context models or hyperpriors for latents would clarify headroom.
- Fairness and evaluation details: Summaries across images are tricky, but adding matched target-rate comparisons and reporting both Y (luma) and RGB metrics would help. Clarify chroma subsampling choices (e.g., JPEG 4:2:0 vs. your 4:4:4) and ensure fairness.
- Efficiency: Report encoder/decoder runtimes and memory on CPUs/GPUs, and compare to JPEG/JPEG 2000 at representative resolutions. Include side-information overhead breakdown.
- Generalization: Show results on higher-resolution and more diverse datasets; discuss how models trained on 256×256 patches scale to arbitrary sizes at test time.
- Reproducibility: Release code, trained models, and bitstreams for key examples to facilitate comparison.

Questions for the authors
- Entropy coder and model:
  - How sensitive are final bitrates to the independence assumption? Have you tried simple spatial/context models over latents, and how much does this close the gap to JPEG 2000’s coder?
  - What fraction of the total bitrate is side information (λ index, headers), and how does this scale?
- Relaxation fidelity:
  - Beyond Figure 4, can you quantify how the proxy’s bias varies with λ and architecture? Does training with the proxy ever lead to pathological latent distributions when evaluated with true quantization?
  - Did you try straight-through estimators or soft-to-hard annealing for quantization, and how do they compare?
- Variable-rate usage:
  - Can a single trained model be steered at test-time by scaling latents or altering quantization step sizes to cover a range of rates with limited loss vs. per-λ models?
- Architecture and efficiency:
  - Provide runtime/throughput numbers for encoding/decoding and FLOP counts for typical image sizes. Are there memory bottlenecks due to GDN/IGDN?
  - How do results change when replacing GDN with pointwise activations at matched compute/parameters?
- Comparisons and metrics:
  - Did you attempt MS-SSIM training to verify that further perceptual gains are achievable? If so, how did artifacts change?
  - Can you provide direct numerical comparisons to contemporaneous learned codecs (e.g., Toderici et al., Gregor et al.) on Kodak with actual bitrates where possible?

Overall, this is a well-executed and impactful paper. The differentiable rate formulation is elegant and broadly relevant, and the empirical evidence is compelling.
