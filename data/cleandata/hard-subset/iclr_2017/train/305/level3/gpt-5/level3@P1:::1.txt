Summary and contributions
The paper presents an end-to-end image compression system that jointly optimizes an analysis transform, a uniform scalar quantizer, an entropy model, and a synthesis transform for rate–distortion performance. The key technical ideas are: (i) using cascaded convolutional transforms with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and its inverse (IGDN) to Gaussianize and decorrelate features; (ii) replacing the non-differentiable quantizer with additive uniform noise to obtain a continuous, differentiable proxy objective; (iii) learning nonparametric marginal models for the code-space densities; and (iv) evaluating with a real entropy coder (CABAC) and reporting actual bitrates. The paper connects the relaxed objective to variational autoencoders, but emphasizes the distinct requirement of operating anywhere along the RD curve via a trade-off parameter λ. Empirically, the method yields strong rate–distortion results versus JPEG and, crucially, JPEG2000, with particularly large gains in perceived quality (MS-SSIM) and aesthetically superior artifacts across rates. The writing is clear, the integration of rate–distortion theory is careful and compelling, and the experiments are thorough relative to prior deep learning work in compression.

Decision: Accept
- The paper offers the most convincing deep-learning-based approach to image compression I have seen, with a principled RD objective, real bitrates, and strong perceptual quality.
- The comparison to JPEG2000 is appropriate and significantly stronger than comparing only to JPEG; the empirical evidence and analysis support the claims.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and positioning: It tackles lossy image compression with the explicit goal of optimizing the RD tradeoff end-to-end, bridging classical transform coding and modern deep learning. The method is well placed in the literature: it builds on GDN/IGDN for Gaussianization, relates the relaxed objective to VAEs while clearly distinguishing the goals, and discusses contemporary learned compression (e.g., Gregor et al., Toderici et al.).
- Motivation and methodology: The choice of GDN/IGDN is well motivated by image statistics and prior results showing improved efficiency over pointwise nonlinearities. The proxy loss using additive uniform noise is both theoretically sound (quantization-noise model) and empirically validated; the paper shows small distortion bias and acceptable entropy bias that diminishes at finer quantization.
- Scientific support: Claims are supported with:
  - Optimization per λ to trace the convex RD hull.
  - Actual entropy coding (CABAC) and reporting of real bitrates, avoiding pitfalls of differential entropy proxies alone.
  - Evaluations on Kodak (and some classic test images), showing consistent MS-SSIM gains at all bitrates and competitive or better PSNR, especially at low rates.
  - Clear qualitative improvements in artifacts (edge preservation, reduced ringing/blocking).
- Impact: The formulation is clean, general, and likely to influence subsequent work in learned compression and beyond. Even with MSE training, the perceptual gains are strong; the framework can readily accommodate other perceptual metrics.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not prerequisites for acceptance)
- Positioning vs best classical codecs:
  - Include comparisons to state-of-the-art classical still-image codecs circa 2016–2017, notably HEVC-Intra-based BPG (and optionally WebP or JPEG XR). BPG typically outperforms JPEG2000; showing results against it would solidify the claims.
  - Cite and discuss classical RD-optimized transform coding literature beyond JPEG2000 (e.g., HEVC intra, SPIHT/EBCOT families) to more explicitly position the method.
- Standard benchmarks:
  - Add results on standard images (Lena, Barbara, Baboon/Mandrill, Peppers) in the main text or appendix, with both PSNR and MS-SSIM. The paper mentions these were examined; making them easily findable and summarized would help readers cross-compare.
- Ablations and clarity:
  - Provide an ablation of GDN/IGDN versus ReLU/sigmoid architectures at matched parameter count/stages to quantify the efficiency advantage claimed in the discussion.
  - Report encoding/decoding speed and model size, and breakdown of side information in the bitstream. Practical complexity is increasingly scrutinized in this field.
  - Discuss the effect of the simple context model in CABAC; given that the spatial model is crude, your rates may be improvable with stronger context/adaptive models. This strengthens the claim that transform learning is the main driver of gains.
  - Clarify sensitivity to λ and whether a single model can cover multiple rates (e.g., via a learned scale parameter or conditional transforms), even if left for future work.
  - For color images, document the colorspace handling (e.g., RGB vs Y’CbCr), training differences, and any color artifacts.
- Reproducibility:
  - If possible, release pretrained models and evaluation scripts; at minimum, provide enough detail to reproduce CABAC settings and training schedules.

Questions for the authors
- Could you add direct comparisons to BPG (HEVC-Intra) at matched bitrates on Kodak and the classic set? If not feasible, can you comment on expected relative performance given your current entropy modeling?
- How does GDN/IGDN compare to pointwise nonlinearities when parameter budget and depth are matched? Can you provide quantitative ablations (RD curves and parameter counts)?
- Can a single conditional model (e.g., with a learned scale or rate parameter) approximate multiple points on the RD curve without retraining per λ? If you attempted this, what was the performance gap?
- What are typical encode/decode times on a CPU/GPU and the memory footprint for the RGB models? How does complexity scale with increased channels or stages?
- How much of the gain stems from the learned transforms versus the learned marginal models? Have you tested using stronger spatial context models in the entropy coder, and what headroom remains?
- For color, which colorspace is used internally, and do you observe any chroma-specific artifacts or rate allocation issues?
- Do you observe any systematic failure modes (e.g., highly periodic textures like Barbara) where PSNR lags JPEG2000 at high rates? Any insights on mitigating them?

Overall, this is an excellent, well-argued paper that cleanly integrates RD theory with deep convolutional transforms and sets a high standard for empirical evaluation in learned compression. I recommend acceptance.
