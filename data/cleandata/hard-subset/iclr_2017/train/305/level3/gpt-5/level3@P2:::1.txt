Summary and contributions
- This paper tackles end-to-end lossy image compression with an explicit rate–distortion objective. It proposes a nonlinear transform-coding architecture built from three cascaded stages of convolution and generalized divisive normalization (GDN) for analysis, paired with an approximate inverse (IGDN) for synthesis, and a uniform scalar quantizer with a practical arithmetic entropy code.
- The key technical device is a continuous relaxation of quantization via additive uniform noise, allowing joint optimization of transform and entropy model by stochastic gradient descent; the paper shows this relaxed loss closely tracks the discrete rate–distortion objective and clarifies its link to VAEs.
- The work reports actual bitrates via an implemented entropy coder (CABAC-style), not just entropy estimates, and demonstrates strong rate–distortion performance, with particularly large perceptual gains (MS-SSIM) and better PSNR than JPEG and JPEG 2000 on most images, especially at low rates.
- The paper is exceptionally well written, conceptually clean, and, in my view, the most convincing deep-learning-based image compression paper I have read to date. The integration of rate–distortion theory into the learning objective and the careful treatment of quantization stand out.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Principled, well-motivated approach that directly optimizes the classical rate–distortion trade-off end-to-end, with a clear and correct continuous proxy and a solid connection to variational inference.
- Strong empirical results with an actual bitstream, outperforming JPEG/JPEG 2000 in MS-SSIM across the board and in PSNR on most images, with visually much more natural artifacts.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses the core challenge in lossy compression—jointly optimizing transform, quantizer, and entropy model for a target rate–distortion point—by learning the transforms and the per-channel marginal distributions together. The use of GDN/IGDN is well motivated from prior work on Gaussianization and biologically inspired normalization, and the architecture choice is justified by efficiency and prior empirical evidence.
- Placement in literature: The paper situates itself relative to transform coding (JPEG, JPEG 2000), VAEs, and contemporary neural approaches (Gregor et al., Toderici et al.). The distinction between generative modeling objectives and rate–distortion optimization is clearly articulated and important.
- Scientific rigor: The authors validate the continuous relaxation by comparing differential entropy/distortion under noise to discrete entropy/quantization outcomes, showing small bias and variance where expected. They evaluate on Kodak and additional classic images, provide MS-SSIM and PSNR curves, and include an actual entropy coder and side-information accounting—crucial for credibility. The improvements are consistent and perceptually meaningful across rates, and the architecture/training details are sufficiently specified to enable reproduction.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not decision-critical)
- Stronger baselines: In addition to JPEG2000, please include comparisons to HEVC intra-based still image coding (e.g., BPG) and/or modern SPIHT/EBCOT variants. These are considered state-of-the-art classical methods and are commonly used baselines circa 2016–2017. Even if only on a subset, they will substantially strengthen the paper’s positioning.
- Standard images: You mention results for Lena, Barbara, Peppers, and Mandrill (Baboon). Please include representative quantitative curves (PSNR and MS-SSIM vs bpp) for these in the main paper or consolidated in an appendix table/figure, not only online.
- Positioning vs classical methods: Add a short subsection that explicitly contrasts your learned transforms with classical designs (DCT/wavelets, EBCOT, HEVC intra), and discuss where gains come from (nonlinear analysis/synthesis, learned marginals, overcompleteness, local gain control vs linear bases).
- Variable-rate operation: Currently each λ yields a separate model. Please discuss practical deployment implications and, if possible, show preliminary results with a single model supporting multiple rates (e.g., by scaling latents or using a learned rate-control parameter).
- Complexity and footprint: Report encoder/decoder runtime, FLOPs, and model size on a standard CPU/GPU, plus latency. This will help practitioners assess feasibility and compare to JPEG2000/HEVC intraframe coding complexity.
- Entropy modeling: Your CABAC-style coder uses shared per-channel marginals with raster scan. Consider a brief ablation (or discussion) quantifying how much you lose without spatial/context models, and whether simple context (e.g., causal neighbors) would narrow that gap.
- Fairness details: Since JPEG uses 4:2:0 chroma subsampling while your model operates on RGB, include a note on color handling and fairness to ensure PSNR/MS-SSIM comparisons are apples-to-apples (you partly address this; a concise summary in the main text would help).

Questions for the authors
- Baselines: Can you report results against HEVC intra coding (e.g., BPG) on Kodak and the classic images, at matched bpp, for both PSNR and MS-SSIM? If not feasible, please discuss expected gaps and cite best-known numbers.
- Generalization: How sensitive are your learned transforms to training data distribution? Have you tested on additional datasets (e.g., Tecnick, CLIC) and on high-resolution photos beyond Kodak?
- Model capacity vs GDN: You state preliminary findings that ReLU/sigmoid networks need more parameters/stages to match GDN. Can you provide a small ablation quantifying the trade-off (parameters, depth, bpp at fixed PSNR/MS-SSIM)?
- Variable rate: Is there a straightforward way in your framework to modulate λ at inference (e.g., scaling latents) without retraining? If not, what changes would be needed to enable a single model covering a rate range?
- Entropy code details: How many contexts are used in your CABAC setup, and how much adaptive gain do you observe over a non-adaptive code? Could simple spatial context models (e.g., dependence on a few neighbors) be integrated without retraining the transforms?
- Complexity: What are typical encode/decode times and memory footprints for 512×512 RGB images on a modern CPU/GPU? How do these compare to JPEG 2000 enc/dec under similar hardware?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, trained models for several λ values, and the entropy coder configuration? That would greatly aid adoption and impact.

Overall, this paper offers a principled and practically credible step forward in learned image compression, with compelling perceptual and quantitative gains and a clean theoretical foundation. With the suggested additions on baselines, positioning, and complexity, it will have strong impact.
