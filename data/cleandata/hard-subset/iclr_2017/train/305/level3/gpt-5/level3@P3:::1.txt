Summary and contributions
- This paper presents a complete, end-to-end learned image compression system that jointly optimizes an analysis transform, scalar quantizer, entropy model, and synthesis transform for rate–distortion (R–D) performance.
- A key technical contribution is the use of cascaded convolutional stages with generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN), along with a principled continuous relaxation of quantization using uniform noise, enabling stochastic gradient training directly on an R + λD objective. The paper carefully relates this relaxation to VAEs while highlighting crucial differences for compression.
- The evaluation uses an actual arithmetic entropy coder and reports real bitrates (not just differential entropy), compares against JPEG and JPEG 2000, and documents consistent perceptual quality gains (MS-SSIM) and strong PSNR–rate performance, especially at lower rates.
- The paper is exemplary in clarity: it integrates rate–distortion theory into the objective, explains the continuous relaxation, and positions the approach within transform coding and generative modeling. The comparison to JPEG 2000 is appropriate and much stronger than the commonly seen JPEG-only baseline.

Decision: Accept
- Reason 1: Most convincing learned image compression paper I have read: it optimizes the true R–D trade-off, deploys an actual coder, and demonstrates clear improvements over JPEG 2000 in both quantitative and visual terms.
- Reason 2: Methodological soundness and clarity: the paper ties the objective to rate–distortion theory, provides a careful relaxation and empirical validation, and offers a fully workable codec, suggesting strong impact.

Supporting arguments
Answering the three core questions:
1) What problem is tackled? Learn a nonlinear transform coding scheme that is jointly optimized end-to-end to minimize R + λD, producing competitive or superior image compression compared to standard codecs at various rates.
2) Is the approach well motivated and well placed? Yes. It is grounded in rate–distortion theory, leverages a continuous relaxation to enable SGD, and is carefully distinguished from VAEs and previous learned schemes. Positioning relative to classical transform coding (DCT/wavelets, scalar quantization, entropy coding) is apt; using JPEG 2000 as a baseline is appropriate.
3) Do the results support the claims? Largely yes. The paper uses real bitstreams, shows R–D curves on recognized datasets, demonstrates large and consistent MS-SSIM gains and strong PSNR competitiveness. The empirical check of the relaxation’s bias/variance is valuable and increases confidence.

Additional strengths
- Visual quality is markedly better at all rates; artifacts are more natural than blocking/ringing in linear transform codecs.
- The paper’s rigor and writing quality are high; the theoretical framing and implementation details are sufficient for reproduction.
- Anticipated impact is high: establishes a strong baseline for learned, end-to-end compression and articulates a path from theory to practical codec design.

Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not gating the decision)
- Include canonical “standard images” in the paper or appendix (Lena, Barbara, Baboon/Mandrill) in addition to the online repository, as these remain common reference points for the community. You already processed them; a compact figure would help readers.
- Strengthen positioning vs state-of-the-art classical methods beyond JPEG 2000:
  - Cite and, if feasible, compare against HEVC-intra/BPG and WebP (even if only in MS-SSIM/PSNR on a subset), and reference widely known classical coders such as SPIHT (Said–Pearlman) and EBCOT (within JPEG 2000), with a short discussion of where your method sits relative to their design choices.
  - Report BD-rate summaries (PSNR and MS-SSIM) over Kodak (and the “standard images”) versus JPEG 2000; if HEVC-intra/BPG comparisons are infeasible, at least discuss expected gaps or advantages.
- Ablations and clarity:
  - Provide an explicit ablation of GDN vs ReLU/sigmoid and number of stages, even if brief, to substantiate the efficiency claim (fewer parameters/stages for same R–D).
  - Quantify the impact of the independence assumption in the latent space and of the simple spatial adaptation in the entropy coder (CABAC): show a small study of context modeling or per-block/causal contexts to indicate headroom.
  - Add a compact complexity analysis: model size, encode/decode time on CPU/GPU, and memory footprint, to help readers assess deployability.
- Results presentation:
  - In the main paper, include at least one RGB example with per-method bitrates and both PSNR and MS-SSIM noted below the images for a fixed rate point, to make the perceptual gains immediately evident.
  - Report variability across images (e.g., median and interquartile ranges of PSNR/MS-SSIM at matched rates) and mention where JPEG 2000 still has an edge at high rates for certain images.

Questions for the authors
- Variable-rate usage: You train separate models per λ. Could you condition a single model on λ (or a learned scale) to support a continuous rate knob at test time with minimal loss? Any preliminary results?
- Entropy model: Have you quantified the gain from more expressive (e.g., context/hyperprior) models versus the current marginal independence? What BD-rate improvements do you anticipate?
- Relaxation fidelity: Do you have bounds or additional statistics on the bias of differential entropy vs discrete entropy beyond Figure 4, especially at the lowest rates?
- Runtime and footprint: What are encode/decode times and parameter counts for your small/large models on typical hardware? How do these compare to JPEG 2000 encoders/decoders?
- Perceptual optimization: Since you previously optimized for perceptual metrics, can you report a small head-to-head comparison of MSE-trained vs MS-SSIM-trained models at matched rates to quantify the perceptual trade-offs?

Overall, this is a well-written, rigorous, and impactful contribution that sets a high bar for learned image compression. I recommend acceptance.
