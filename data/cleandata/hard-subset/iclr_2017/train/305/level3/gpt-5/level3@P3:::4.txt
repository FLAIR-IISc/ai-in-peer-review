1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes a complete, end-to-end learned image compression system that jointly optimizes analysis and synthesis transforms, quantization, and entropy modeling for rate–distortion (R–D) performance. The core technical ideas are:
- Nonlinear transform coding with cascades of convolutions and a biologically inspired generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearity, and its learned inverse (IGDN).
- A principled relaxation of the discrete R–D objective: replace rounding with additive uniform noise, enabling stochastic gradient optimization while tightly approximating both rate (via differential entropy) and distortion.
- Nonparametric, per-channel marginal priors for the latent representation, trained jointly with the transforms, coupled with a practical CABAC-based entropy coder to report true bitrates (not just entropy estimates).
- A clear connection to variational autoencoders (VAE) that clarifies similarities and crucial differences (discrete coding, fixed R–D trade-off λ, evaluation in actual bits).
Empirically, the method outperforms JPEG/JPEG2000 on PSNR for most images, and achieves consistently higher MS-SSIM and superior visual quality at all bitrates on Kodak and other widely used images. The analysis includes validation of the relaxation (bias/variance of the surrogate rate and distortion), architectural/training details, and careful discussion of fairness in R–D averaging.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The paper establishes a clean, well-motivated end-to-end learning framework for lossy image compression with a clever and effective R–D relaxation, and demonstrates it works with real bitstreams.
- Compelling experimental results with thorough analysis show consistent perceptual gains and competitive or better PSNR versus strong classical baselines.

3. Supporting arguments
What specific problem is tackled?
- Learning an image compression model that jointly optimizes the analysis/synthesis transforms and the entropy model for any desired R–D operating point, avoiding hand-engineered linear transforms and manual component-wise tuning.

Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature?
- Yes. The paper is grounded in transform coding and rate–distortion theory, tackles the quantization non-differentiability with a principled additive-uniform relaxation, and situates itself relative to VAEs, generative compression (Gregor et al.), and prior learned compression (Toderici et al.). The choice of GDN is motivated by prior work showing strong Gaussianization of image statistics; the VAE connection is insightful and clarifies why compression requires different constraints than density modeling.

Does the paper support its claims with rigorous evidence?
- Yes. The surrogate loss is empirically validated against actual R–D with quantization; the method is evaluated with a real entropy coder, reporting bitrates including side information. Qualitative and quantitative results on standard images are extensive, with consistent MS-SSIM improvements and frequent PSNR gains (especially at lower rates). Architectural and training details are provided, including stability measures and prior modeling. The discussion of R–D averaging nuances shows care in evaluation.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not decision-critical)
- Single model across rates: Training separate models per λ is practical but increases storage/maintenance. Consider conditioning the synthesis/analysis or the prior on λ (or a learned gain/scale), or progressive coding, to cover multiple rates with one model.
- Stronger entropy modeling: Independent marginals limit coding efficiency. Explore spatially adaptive priors, hyperpriors, or autoregressive/context models to better capture dependencies, potentially reducing bitrate significantly.
- Comparisons: If feasible, add comparisons to BPG/HEVC-intra (widely used around 2016/2017) to strengthen claims against modern baselines.
- Complexity and latency: Report encoding/decoding runtime and memory on CPU/GPU, and model size. This will help assess deployment feasibility.
- Perceptual optimization: Since MS-SSIM benefits are strong, a version trained with MS-SSIM (or mixed losses) could be informative, especially for very low rates.
- Ablations: Provide more systematic ablations on GDN vs. standard activations, number of stages/filters, and prior parameterization to isolate contributors to performance.
- Color handling: Elaborate on color transforms, chroma subsampling choices, and their impact on R–D and perceptual quality.

Questions for the authors
- Prior independence: How sensitive is performance to the assumption of independent marginals? Did you evaluate simple spatial context models or hyperpriors, and what gains (in bits) did you observe?
- One model for many rates: Did you try conditioning on λ (or a scale parameter) so a single set of weights can operate across multiple R–D points? If so, how close did it come to per-λ optima?
- Quantization and relaxation: Beyond additive uniform noise, did you test straight-through estimators or temperature-annealed relaxations, and how did they affect convergence and final R–D?
- Density spline range: How is the support of the piecewise linear priors adapted during training, and how much coding loss arises from prior–data mismatch at the tails?
- GDN/IGDN stability: What constraints ensure invertibility/numerical stability of GDN/IGDN during training and inference? Any failure modes?
- RGB pipeline: Which color space is used internally? Any chroma subsampling? If not, do you anticipate extra gains from learned luma/chroma decoupling?
- Computational profile: What are typical encode/decode times and memory footprints for 512×512 and 2K images on CPU and GPU? Any fast approximations (e.g., depthwise separable convolutions) tested?
- Generalization: How does performance scale to high-resolution photographs beyond 256×256 crops? Any degradation in prior mismatch or entropy coding efficiency?
- Side information: What is the average proportion of side information in the bitstream, and does adaptive coding across channels/images significantly reduce it?
- Training set dependence: Did you assess robustness to the choice of training corpus or data augmentations (e.g., less constrained downsampling/cropping, noise levels)?

Overall, the paper presents a timely, principled, and well-executed step toward learned, end-to-end image compression with clear technical contributions and strong empirical support. I recommend acceptance.
