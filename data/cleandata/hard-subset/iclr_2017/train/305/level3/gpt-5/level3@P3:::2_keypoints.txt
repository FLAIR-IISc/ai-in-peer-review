- Extends classical rate–distortion optimization to a multi-stage deep nonlinear transform codec with GDN/IGDN, uniform scalar quantization, and adaptive entropy coding.  
- Introduces a smooth surrogate for the discrete RD objective by replacing quantization with additive uniform noise, enabling end-to-end SGD training.  
- Makes an explicit connection to VAEs, showing the relaxed objective corresponds to optimizing a generative model under certain conditions.  
- Evaluates with a real bitstream (CABAC-style) rather than entropy estimates, showing consistent visual and quantitative gains over JPEG and JPEG2000, especially at low rates.  
- Paper is exceptionally well written with clear methodology, architecture, and training details.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reasons for accept: strong empirical performance with a practical codec and a clear, principled end-to-end formulation that influenced later work.  
- Contribution is incremental on the core relax-quantization idea, but novelty comes from deeper networks, adaptive entropy coding instantiation, and bit-accurate evaluation.  
- Empirical rigor is strong: implemented adaptive arithmetic coder, reported actual bit rates, extensive RD curves and visual examples including MS-SSIM gains.  
- Main limitation: unclear how much gain derives from adaptive entropy coding versus increased model capacity/depth/GDN — an ablation is needed.  
- Limitation: switching to MSE-based training may regress perceptual quality compared to prior perceptual-metric training.  
- Limitation: the VAE equivalence is overstated; normalization constant Z may depend on gs(y;θ), invalidating the simple VAE mapping unless specific conditions hold.  
- Request: fully detail the adaptive entropy coder (contexts, update schedule, features, per-image overhead, initial priors).  
- Request: provide ablation comparing fixed coder, adaptive coder with identical transforms, and stronger context models; report Δbitrate at matched distortion.  
- Request: quantify how much raster-scan + shared-channel modeling loses vs. autoregressive/advanced context models (e.g., Kodak results).  
- Request: justify optimizing MSE versus perceptual metrics; provide MS-SSIM-trained results or explain constraints (stability, color images, comparability).  
- Request: include set-level MS-SSIM summary plots and representative perceptual crops to support perceptual-quality claims.  
- Request: correct and clarify the VAE derivation and Equation 10, specifying conditions where Z(λ,θ) reduces to Z(λ) and reconciling partition/normalization statements.  
- Request: ablations on architecture and capacity — GDN/IGDN vs ReLU/sigmoid with matched params, depth/width scaling, and effect of overcompleteness.  
- Request: report computational cost — encode/decode time and memory on CPU/GPU for typical images.  
- Request: provide quantitative plots of bias/variance of the relaxation across λ showing where approximation degrades (coarse quantization).  
- Request: position and, if possible, compare against contemporaneous learned codecs (e.g., Toderici et al.) on shared datasets.  
- Specific questions: percentage bitrate reduction from adaptive coding averaged over Kodak at matched MSE/MS-SSIM?  
- Specific questions: what CABAC contexts are used, how often are probabilities updated, and is there risk of overfitting adaptation to single images?  
- Specific questions: can you train with perceptual loss (MS-SSIM) for RGB and does the VAE correspondence and Z(λ,θ) behavior change?  
- Specific questions: precisely under what assumptions does the VAE equivalence hold; provide corrected derivation showing θ-dependence of Z if any.  
- Specific questions: sensitivity of results to replacing GDN with standard activations when matching param counts and depth?  
- Specific questions: can a single conditional model cover multiple rate points (varying λ) without retraining, and how does performance change?  
- Overall: despite limited core novelty, the paper delivers a convincing, end-to-end codec with strong results, careful engineering, and clear exposition—worthy of acceptance.
