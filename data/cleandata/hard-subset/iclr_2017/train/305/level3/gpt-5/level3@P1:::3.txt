Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes a full end-to-end image compression system that jointly optimizes an analysis transform, a uniform quantizer, a synthesis transform, and a learned entropy model for rate–distortion performance. The transforms are cascades of convolutions with generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN), a biologically inspired joint nonlinearity that effectively Gaussianizes local responses and improves coding efficiency.
- A key technical contribution is a principled and practical relaxation of the discrete, non-differentiable objective: the quantizer is replaced during training by additive uniform noise, and per-channel marginal densities are learned nonparametrically. This yields a differentiable surrogate for the rate and distortion terms and enables SGD-based optimization at arbitrary points along the rate–distortion curve by varying λ.
- The optimization can be interpreted under a variational autoencoder lens, clarifying the modeling assumptions while motivating differences (discrete coding targets, explicit λ-controlled trade-offs, and evaluation in actual bits).
- The authors implement an entropy coder (CABAC-style) and report measured bitrates, not just entropy estimates. Empirically, on Kodak and additional images (RGB and grayscale), the method outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 in MS-SSIM across all bitrates and often in PSNR, with markedly better visual artifacts (fewer ringing/blocking, more natural edge/contour behavior).
- The differentiable “rate” approximation is of broader interest for learning systems that must optimize discrete coding objectives.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong, well-motivated technical advance (continuous relaxation + nonparametric marginals) enabling true end-to-end rate–distortion optimization with actual entropy coding; (2) Compelling empirical evidence and careful comparisons demonstrating consistent perceptual gains and competitive-to-better PSNR over standard codecs.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Optimizing transform, quantizer, and entropy model jointly for rate–distortion is a classic but hard problem due to quantization non-differentiability and high-dimensional dependencies. The proposed relaxation (additive uniform noise) is standard in quantization theory and is here integrated into a modern convnet framework with GDN, yielding a tractable and principled objective.
- Positioning in literature: The connection to VAEs is accurate and illuminates similarities/differences (continuous vs discrete, explicit λ). Relative to Toderici et al. and Gregor et al., this work’s focus on measured bitrates via an implemented coder and on optimizing specific RD points is a notable strength.
- Scientific rigor: The authors check the fidelity of the relaxation by comparing relaxed differential entropy/distortion to realized discrete entropy/distortion, showing low bias/variance and the expected convergence at finer quantization. They carefully include side information in the bitstream and discuss coder adaptivity. Visual comparisons are consistent with MS-SSIM advantages.
- Scope and impact: The differentiable rate proxy and learned per-channel marginals are likely to be reusable beyond image compression (e.g., neural discrete bottlenecks, differentiable source coding components). The GDN/IGDN design is well justified and efficient.

Theoretical concerns and their handling
- In principle, a sufficiently expressive network might “game” a fixed-form entropy model by spreading codes to maximize entropy within a fixed range. The authors’ approach—learning per-channel marginals nonparametrically and verifying coder rates vs. entropy—mitigates this. I also found the argument convincing that enforcing a specific output form to match a hand-specified entropy model would either force a more complex network or require a separate variable-rate encoder, undermining the elegance and effectiveness of the current end-to-end solution.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decisive)
- Single-model variable rate: Currently, separate models are trained per λ. Discuss feasibility of conditioning the analysis/synthesis/entropy model on λ (or a learned “quality” input) to obtain a single, controllable model, or articulate why this is nontrivial within your framework.
- Entropy modeling: The independence assumption and simple raster-scan CABAC contexts are limiting. Even a lightweight spatial context (e.g., neighboring symbols, channel coupling) could further close the gap to optimal coding and strengthen results.
- Ablations: Provide quantitative ablations on GDN vs. ReLU/sigmoid at matched parameter budgets and depths, and on marginal model resolution (spline sample density). This would clarify how much each component contributes.
- Complexity: Report encoding/decoding runtime, model size, and memory footprint compared to JPEG 2000 on a standard CPU/GPU. This will help assess deployability.
- Datasets and metrics: Include additional modern datasets and summarize per-image RD variance. Consider reporting BD-rate/BD-PSNR summaries where appropriate. Since perceptual quality is a key claim, add qualitative comparisons at matched MS-SSIM as well as PSNR.
- VAE connection: Tighten the derivation and clearly state conditions under which the surrogate corresponds to a valid likelihood (with or without gp). This will aid readers applying the technique elsewhere.
- Reproducibility: If possible, release code/models or provide detailed hyperparameters, learning-rate schedules, and training compute budgets.

Questions for the authors
1. How close are the realized arithmetic coding bitrates to your relaxed entropy estimates across bitrates and images (e.g., average and worst-case gaps)? Do you observe systematic under/overestimation at low rates?
2. Did you observe any degenerate behaviors where the network concentrates outputs to exploit the nonparametric marginals (e.g., mode collapse or artificially uniform marginals)? How do you detect/prevent this?
3. How sensitive are results to the spline resolution and range adaptation of the marginal models? Is performance robust if you coarsen the marginal model?
4. Can your analysis/synthesis transforms be conditioned on λ to produce a single variable-rate model without significant RD loss?
5. What is the runtime and memory usage of encoding/decoding for typical Kodak-size images on CPU and GPU? How does this scale with the increased channel counts at higher rates?
6. For RGB, did you explore alternative color spaces or chroma subsampling? How does the method compare if the baselines are restricted to no chroma subsampling?
7. How stable is training with the additive-uniform relaxation at very low rates? Did you need special regularization to avoid exploding gradients or poor local minima?
8. Could you share more details on the IGDN approximation’s invertibility and its impact on reconstruction error, especially at low rates?

Overall, this is a timely, well-executed paper that advances end-to-end neural compression with clear technical and empirical contributions.
