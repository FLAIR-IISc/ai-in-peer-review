Summary of contributions
- The paper presents a complete end-to-end learned image codec built from cascaded convolutional analysis/synthesis transforms with GDN/IGDN nonlinearities, uniform scalar quantization, and an adaptive arithmetic coder. All components (analysis, synthesis, and marginal likelihoods for entropy coding) are trained jointly for rate–distortion using a continuous relaxation that replaces quantization with additive uniform noise.
- It extends prior work by the authors on nonlinear transform coding in several important ways: deeper convolutional transforms (three stages with subsampling/upsampling), nonparametric marginal models learned jointly, optimization over color images, and (crucially) reporting actual bit rates via an implemented entropy coder rather than differential entropy estimates.
- The paper carefully relates the relaxed objective to variational autoencoders, clarifying when the equivalence holds (Euclidean metric; more generally translation-invariant metrics with an affine perceptual transform).
- Empirically, it shows consistent perceptual improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000 at all bit rates, with substantial gains in MS-SSIM and generally competitive or better PSNR (especially at low rates). The paper is exceptionally clear and well written.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong and convincing empirical results with a complete, practical codec that reports true bitrates and clearly improves visual quality over established standards; (2) A principled, well-motivated formulation that advances end-to-end optimization for compression and clarifies its relationship to VAEs.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and motivation: Optimizing the full compression pipeline for rate–distortion is central and difficult. The paper is well-situated in both compression and generative modeling literatures, and the choice of GDN/IGDN is motivated by prior evidence on Gaussianization and efficiency.
- Novelty: While the rate–distortion relaxation and GDN-based transforms were explored in earlier work by the authors, this paper meaningfully advances the engineering of a practical codec: deeper conv cascades, joint training of nonparametric marginals, implementation of a context-adaptive arithmetic coder, and evaluation on actual bitstreams. The VAE connection is also usefully elaborated.
- Soundness: The continuous relaxation is appropriate and the paper provides empirical evidence that the relaxed loss closely tracks true RD, especially at finer quantization. The comparisons use a standard dataset and include both PSNR and MS-SSIM, with visual examples that align with the metrics.
- Scope and clarity: The method is clearly described, with architectural and training details and an informative appendix on the entropy coder. The study convincingly demonstrates consistent perceptual gains; PSNR is competitive to superior for most images, particularly at low rates.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all are decision-critical)
- Ablations on where the gains come from:
  - Adaptive entropy coding vs. fixed/non-adaptive coding: please quantify the bit savings from the context-adaptive arithmetic coder relative to (i) an idealized non-adaptive model matched to the learned marginals and (ii) a simple Huffman or arithmetic coder without context adaptation.
  - Transform capacity and nonlinearities: compare GDN/IGDN to ReLU/tanh networks of comparable parameter count and depth; vary number of stages/channels to show scaling.
  - Marginal model: parametric vs. spline-based nonparametric densities; effect of assuming independence across channels vs. modest context modeling across space/channels.
- Variable-rate operation: Currently a separate model is trained per λ. It would strengthen the paper to explore a single network that can traverse the RD curve (e.g., conditioning on λ or learned quantization step sizes), or at least to discuss this limitation and its practical implications.
- Choice of distortion: The paper trains for MSE but emphasizes perceptual quality. Given your prior perceptual-criterion results, please include a direct comparison of models trained for MSE vs. MS-SSIM (or your prior perceptual metric) on at least grayscale, to quantify the trade-offs in PSNR and MS-SSIM and the visual appearance.
- Evaluation breadth:
  - Report runtime (encode/decode), model size, and memory vs. JPEG 2000 on a standard CPU/GPU.
  - Clarify JPEG 2000 settings (rate control vs. quality) used for comparisons; consider also matching target bpp points across methods for per-image curves.
  - Include a small-scale human study or pairwise preference test to substantiate the perceptual claims.
- Presentation details:
  - Clarify the VAE equivalence conditions. In the general non-Euclidean case (Eq. 16), please justify when the normalizer Z depends only on λ (and not on θ or ỹ); if additional conditions are needed (e.g., translation-invariant metric in the perceptual space and affine invertible gp), state them explicitly.
  - Expand the entropy-coder description: how many contexts, how are modes estimated/updated, how large is side information, and how much does adaptation reduce bits empirically?

Questions for the authors
1. What is the measured bitrate gain from the adaptive arithmetic coder compared to (a) a non-adaptive coder using the learned marginals and (b) a fixed Laplacian/Gaussian parametric model? Please provide per-rate breakdowns.
2. How much do GDN/IGDN contribute over ReLU/tanh in equal-capacity networks? Can you show RD curves for these ablations?
3. Can you report encode/decode times and model size vs. JPEG 2000 on standard hardware?
4. How sensitive are results to the independence assumption across latents? Did you try simple spatial context models during coding beyond sharing marginals per channel?
5. Have you trained a single model that operates at multiple rates (e.g., by conditioning on λ or scaling latent noise/quantization)? If so, how close is it to the per-λ optimal frontier?
6. For the VAE connection, please clarify the precise assumptions under which Z(λ) in Eq. 16 is independent of θ and ỹ. If these assumptions are violated, how does that affect the equivalence?
7. Why prioritize MSE training given your demonstrated perceptual gains with learned metrics? Can you include a direct comparison of MSE-trained vs. perceptual-trained models on the same architecture?
8. What fraction of the performance gap to JPEG 2000 remains attributable to entropy coding versus transform quality? A table with true bitrates for the same quantized latents encoded by (i) your CABAC, (ii) an ideal entropy estimate, and (iii) a simpler coder would help.
9. How robust are the nonparametric marginals to overfitting the training distribution? Do you observe degradation on out-of-distribution textures or synthetic images?
10. Will you release code/models and bitstreams to facilitate reproducibility?
