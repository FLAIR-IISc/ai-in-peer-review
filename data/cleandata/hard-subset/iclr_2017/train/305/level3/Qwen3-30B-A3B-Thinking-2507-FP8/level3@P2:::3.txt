

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary
This paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression system that jointly optimizes the analysis transform, quantizer, and synthesis transform for rate-distortion performance. The authors introduce a novel differentiable approximation of the rate function that enables effective training across different rate-distortion trade-offs, which is a significant technical contribution. The approach uses a cascade of convolutional linear filters with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearities, inspired by biological visual systems. The paper demonstrates that this method achieves better rate-distortion performance than JPEG and JPEG 2000 across multiple test images, with particularly notable improvements in visual quality at all bit rates as measured by MS-SSIM.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant technical contribution with a differentiable approximation of the rate function that enables end-to-end optimization of the entire compression pipeline, and the empirical results provide strong evidence of the approach's effectiveness.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's primary contribution—the differentiable approximation of the rate function—is both technically sound and highly relevant to the field. The authors successfully address the fundamental challenge of optimizing a quantized system through a continuous relaxation, making the entire pipeline differentiable. This approach has clear potential for broader impact beyond image compression, as it provides a framework for differentiable approximations of similar problems in other domains.

The empirical results are compelling and well-supported. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements over established methods across multiple metrics and test images. The visual quality improvements are particularly noteworthy, as the compressed images maintain natural appearance with preserved contours and sharp edges, whereas JPEG and JPEG 2000 exhibit common artifacts like blocking and ringing. The paper also provides a thoughtful comparison to variational autoencoders, clarifying the important differences between compression and generative modeling objectives.

The paper is well-placed within the literature, with thorough discussion of related work and clear connections to prior research on nonlinear image representations and rate-distortion theory. The authors effectively address the challenge of optimizing a system with a discontinuous quantization step through their continuous relaxation approach.

## 4. Additional Feedback
While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance its impact:

- The paper could provide more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method for encoding and decoding, particularly for real-world deployment scenarios. This would help readers understand the practical implications of the approach.

- The discussion of the GDN/IGDN nonlinearities could be strengthened by providing more insight into why these specific nonlinearities outperform simpler alternatives like ReLU. A more detailed comparison of the efficiency gains would help readers understand the significance of this design choice.

- The paper mentions that the method was optimized for MSE but produces better visual quality. A brief analysis of why this occurs—perhaps related to the statistics of natural images captured by the GDN transforms—would add valuable insight.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that GDN/IGDN transforms require fewer parameters than simpler nonlinearities to achieve the same rate-distortion performance. Could the authors provide more specific numbers on this efficiency gain, and whether this advantage holds for higher-resolution images?

2. The paper demonstrates that the continuous relaxation of the rate function provides a good approximation to actual rate-distortion values. Could the authors share more details about the accuracy of this approximation across different quantization levels, particularly at very low bit rates?

3. The authors note that the method produces better visual quality despite being optimized for MSE. Could they speculate on why this occurs, and whether this phenomenon might be generalizable to other image compression tasks or even other domains where perceptual quality matters?
