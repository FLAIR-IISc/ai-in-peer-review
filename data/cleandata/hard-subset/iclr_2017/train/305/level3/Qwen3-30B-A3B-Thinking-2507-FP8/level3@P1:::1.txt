

This paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression framework using nonlinear transforms, addressing the long-standing challenge of lossy image compression through a novel integration of rate-distortion theory and deep learning. The authors propose a model consisting of nonlinear analysis and synthesis transforms with cascaded generalized divisive normalization (GDN) operations, jointly optimized for rate-distortion performance using a continuous relaxation of the quantization step. The approach effectively implements a form of vector quantization through nonlinear transformations, demonstrating superior rate-distortion performance compared to standard methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000 across multiple test images. Critically, the authors show not only better PSNR performance but also significantly improved visual quality at all bit rates, validated through MS-SSIM metrics, which is particularly noteworthy as the model was optimized using MSE rather than a perceptual metric.

I recommend acceptance with strong enthusiasm. The paper's most compelling contribution is its seamless integration of rate-distortion theory with deep learning, providing a theoretically grounded framework for end-to-end optimization of compression systems. The continuous relaxation of the quantization step enables effective gradient-based optimization, and the authors' careful analysis of the relationship to variational autoencoders demonstrates a deep understanding of both fields. The comparison to JPEG 2000 is particularly valuable, as it's a more appropriate benchmark than JPEG alone for modern compression research.

The paper excels in its technical execution and clarity. The motivation for GDN-based transforms is well-placed in the literature on biological visual systems and image statistics, and the authors convincingly demonstrate how these transforms better capture natural image statistics than linear alternatives. The implementation details, including the entropy code based on CABAC and the thorough evaluation methodology, lend credibility to the results. The visual quality improvements are striking and consistently observed across all tested images and bit rates, which is a significant advancement in the field.

I believe this paper will have substantial impact on both compression research and the broader deep learning community. It provides a compelling example of how end-to-end optimization can solve classical engineering problems with superior results, potentially inspiring similar approaches in other domains. The framework's flexibility in operating at any point along the rate-distortion curve makes it particularly valuable for practical applications with varying bandwidth constraints.

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend including results on standard test images (Lena, Barbara, Baboon) that are commonly used in the compression community. These would provide immediate comparability with previous work and help readers contextualize the improvements. Additionally, while the comparison to JPEG 2000 is appropriate, the paper would benefit from more explicit positioning against the best classical methods, including a discussion of how the proposed approach compares to state-of-the-art techniques like JPEG XR or WebP.

The authors should also clarify the relationship between their approach and the recent work by Toderici et al. (2016), which similarly targets end-to-end compression. While the paper briefly acknowledges this work, a more detailed comparison would help position their contribution more precisely within the landscape of neural network-based compression methods.

I have one key technical question: The paper states that GDN/IGDN transforms are more efficient for compression than alternatives like ReLU, requiring fewer parameters/stages. Could the authors provide more insight into why this efficiency advantage exists beyond the observation that GDN is a multivariate generalization of sigmoidal functions? A brief theoretical explanation would strengthen the argument.

Another question: The paper mentions that the relaxed loss function provides a good approximation to actual rate-distortion values, but the approximation errors (particularly for coarse quantization) are not fully characterized. Could the authors provide more detail on how these errors affect the final compression performance, especially at very low bit rates?

The paper is exceptionally well-written and thoroughly addresses the problem it sets out to solve. The integration of theoretical rate-distortion principles with practical implementation details is masterful, and the results are compelling. I have no concerns about the scientific rigor of the work, as the authors provide extensive empirical validation across multiple metrics and image sets. This represents a significant advance in image compression research and demonstrates the power of end-to-end optimization for classical problems.
