

# Review for "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary
This paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression method using nonlinear transforms, specifically a cascade of convolutional layers with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and inverse GDN (IGDN) operations. The authors jointly optimize the entire model for rate-distortion performance using a continuous relaxation of the quantization step, allowing gradient-based optimization. The approach is related to variational autoencoders but differs in its focus on operating at any point along the rate-distortion curve. The authors demonstrate that their method outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of rate-distortion performance, with particularly notable improvements in visual quality as measured by MS-SSIM. The paper is well-written and provides detailed experimental results.

## 2. Decision
Reject.

## 3. Supporting arguments
The paper's main contribution is an end-to-end optimization framework for image compression using deep nonlinear transforms. However, the novelty is limited as the core approach builds directly on the authors' prior work (Ballé et al., 2016), which already demonstrated end-to-end optimization for perceptual distortion. The paper does not clearly establish how this work advances beyond their previous publication.

The choice to optimize for MSE instead of a perceptual metric is particularly concerning given the authors' expertise in perceptual image quality. The paper does not adequately justify this choice or demonstrate that MSE optimization leads to better perceptual quality, which appears to be a step backward from their prior work. The authors should explain why they believe MSE optimization would lead to better visual quality, especially when they have previously shown that perceptual metric optimization leads to superior visual results.

The contribution of adaptive entropy coding is unclear. The paper does not provide an ablation study to determine how much of the performance gain comes from the adaptive entropy coder versus deeper network capacity. Without this, it's impossible to determine whether the gains are due to the novel optimization framework or simply increased model capacity.

Equation 10 contains a technical error that undermines the authors' connection to variational autoencoders. The partition function should depend on g_s(y; θ), which means the equivalence does not hold for non-Euclidean metrics. This weakens the theoretical foundation of their approach.

## 4. Additional feedback
- The paper would benefit from an ablation study comparing the performance of the adaptive entropy coder versus a simpler entropy coder, to clarify the contribution of this component.
- The justification for optimizing MSE instead of a perceptual metric needs to be strengthened. The authors should explain why they believe MSE optimization leads to better perceptual quality, especially given their prior work on perceptual metrics.
- The paper should clarify the exact contribution of the adaptive entropy coding versus deeper network capacity. An ablation study would help with this.
- The technical issue with Equation 10 should be addressed to strengthen the theoretical connection to VAEs.

## 5. Questions for authors
1. Why did you choose to optimize for MSE instead of a perceptual metric, given your prior work on perceptual image quality? Can you provide evidence that MSE optimization leads to better perceptual quality than perceptual metric optimization?
2. The paper claims that your method "introduces a continuous proxy for the discontinuous loss function arising from the quantizer" - can you clarify how this is novel compared to your previous work (Ballé et al., 2016)?
3. The adaptive entropy coding is presented as a key component, but without an ablation study, it's unclear how much of the performance gain comes from this versus deeper network architecture. Can you provide results comparing your method with and without adaptive entropy coding?
4. Equation 10 claims that the relaxed rate-distortion optimization problem is equivalent to VAE training, but the partition function should depend on g_s(y; θ), which means the equivalence doesn't hold for non-Euclidean metrics. How do you address this issue?
