

This paper presents a compelling end-to-end learning approach to image compression that significantly advances the field by optimizing the entire compression pipeline (analysis transform, quantizer, and synthesis transform) jointly for rate-distortion performance. The authors introduce a novel framework that leverages a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization, overcoming a fundamental challenge in traditional compression methods. By using generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearities inspired by biological visual systems and optimizing for mean squared error, the method achieves superior rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 across multiple test images and bit rates, with particularly impressive visual quality improvements.

I recommend acceptance with the following key reasons: (1) the paper provides a well-motivated and theoretically sound solution to a classical problem in image compression, with clear connections to information theory and biological vision models; (2) the experimental results are thorough, compelling, and well-supported by both objective metrics (PSNR, MS-SSIM) and qualitative visual comparisons.

The paper's key innovation is the continuous relaxation of quantization that enables end-to-end optimization. This is a significant technical contribution that addresses a longstanding challenge in compression: the quantizer typically breaks the gradient flow needed for optimization. The authors' relaxation using additive uniform noise is well-justified, and they provide a thorough theoretical analysis showing how their approach relates to variational autoencoders while highlighting critical differences. The empirical validation is particularly strong: they demonstrate consistent improvements in both rate-distortion performance and visual quality across multiple image categories and bit rates, with the visual improvements being especially striking. The paper also carefully addresses the limitations of using MSE as the distortion metric, noting that the learned representations capture aspects beneficial for perceptual quality even without explicit perceptual optimization.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive. The authors implement a complete compression system with actual entropy coding (CABAC), not just theoretical rate estimates, allowing for fair comparison to standard methods. They provide extensive visual examples showing how their method avoids the blocking, ringing, and aliasing artifacts common in linear transform-based compression methods. The MS-SSIM results are particularly convincing, showing substantial improvements across all test images and bit rates. The authors also thoughtfully address the challenges of comparing rate-distortion curves across multiple images and provide all test results online for full transparency.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, with a clear discussion of related work, including recent deep learning approaches to compression. The authors effectively differentiate their approach from variational autoencoders and other generative models, explaining why their framework is better suited for compression (particularly the need to operate at any point along the rate-distortion curve rather than focusing on a single optimal point).

For further improvement, the authors could strengthen the paper by providing a more detailed analysis of the entropy coding component, specifically how their simple CABAC implementation compares to more sophisticated entropy coding techniques used in JPEG 2000. Additionally, a brief discussion of the computational complexity during both training and inference would help readers understand the practical feasibility of the approach. Some ablation studies on the impact of different activation functions (beyond the GDN/IGDN comparison already mentioned) would further validate the importance of their specific nonlinearities.

The authors should also consider providing more specific statistics about the most dramatic improvements, such as which image categories or bit rate ranges show the largest gains in MS-SSIM or visual quality. This would help readers better understand the practical impact of their method.

Finally, the authors mention they're testing models with simpler activation functions to determine how much of the performance is due to the biologically-inspired nonlinearities. Sharing preliminary results on this analysis would be valuable for readers to understand the efficiency of their approach.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression, demonstrating that end-to-end learned compression can substantially outperform traditional hand-crafted methods. The method is theoretically sound, experimentally validated, and presents a compelling case for the value of deep learning in solving classical engineering problems. The visual quality improvements are particularly noteworthy and suggest the learned representations capture aspects of image structure that are beneficial for perceptual quality.
