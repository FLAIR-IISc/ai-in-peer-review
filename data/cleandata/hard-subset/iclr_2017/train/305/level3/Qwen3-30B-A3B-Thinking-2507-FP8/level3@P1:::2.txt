

The paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression framework using a cascade of nonlinear transforms (with GDN/IGDN) followed by uniform quantization. The authors jointly optimize the entire model for rate-distortion performance using a continuous relaxation of the quantization step, demonstrating improved rate-distortion performance over JPEG and JPEG 2000 with visually superior results across all bit rates. The approach is well-motivated by the limitations of standard transform coding methods, and the continuous relaxation technique for optimization is sound and well-explained.

Decision: Accept (with minor revisions)

Key reasons: The method demonstrates consistent and significant improvements in both objective (PSNR) and subjective (MS-SSIM, visual quality) metrics over standard compression methods. The experimental validation is thorough and well-executed across multiple test images.

Supporting arguments: The paper provides compelling evidence of the method's superiority through extensive comparisons with JPEG and JPEG 2000 across multiple image sets. The visual improvements are particularly striking, with the method effectively avoiding the blocking and ringing artifacts common to linear transform coding. The authors also provide a clear explanation of the relationship between their approach and variational autoencoders, while appropriately highlighting the key differences in optimization objectives. The implementation details are sufficiently thorough for reproducibility, including the entropy coding scheme and experimental setup.

Additional feedback: The paper would benefit from a more detailed clarification of the relationship between their method and VAEs, particularly addressing the issue with Equation 10 (the partition function should depend on g_s(y; θ), implying the method is not equivalent to a VAE for non-Euclidean metrics). An ablation study comparing the contribution of adaptive entropy coding versus simply increasing network capacity would strengthen the claims about the necessity of the adaptive entropy component. The rationale for using MSE instead of a perceptual metric deserves more explanation, especially considering the authors' prior work on perceptual metrics.

Questions for authors:
1. You previously demonstrated that optimizing for perceptual metrics yields visually superior results (Ballé et al., 2016). Why did you switch to MSE for this paper, and how does this choice affect the visual quality improvements you report?
2. Can you provide an ablation study showing the contribution of adaptive entropy coding versus simply increasing the network capacity (e.g., adding more channels or stages) to achieve similar rate-distortion performance?
3. The authors state that "the relaxed rate-distortion optimization problem bears some resemblance to those used to fit generative image models," but Equation 10 appears to incorrectly assume the partition function is independent of g_s(y; θ). Could you clarify this point and its implications for the relationship between your method and VAEs?

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression by demonstrating that deep end-to-end optimization can yield substantial improvements over established methods. The visual quality improvements at all bit rates are particularly impressive and suggest the approach could have practical value. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the technical content accessible to readers. The method's ability to operate at any point along the rate-distortion curve through a single parameter (λ) is a notable advantage over some competing approaches. While the novelty is somewhat limited by the prior work on rate-distortion optimization, the paper significantly advances the state of the art in practical image compression through its end-to-end approach, adaptive entropy coding, and superior visual results.
