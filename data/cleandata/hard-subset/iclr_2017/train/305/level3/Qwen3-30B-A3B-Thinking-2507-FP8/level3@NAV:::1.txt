

**Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"**

1. **Summary of Contribution:** This paper presents a compelling end-to-end optimized image compression framework using deep neural networks with nonlinear transformations. The authors introduce a novel approach that integrates rate-distortion theory seamlessly into the objective function, using generalized divisive normalization (GDN) inspired by biological visual systems. Their method outperforms standard JPEG and JPEG 2000 across multiple metrics, with particularly impressive visual quality improvements at all bit rates. The paper's elegant theoretical foundation, combined with strong empirical results, makes it a significant contribution to the field of image compression.

2. **Decision:** Accept. The paper makes a strong case for its approach, with clear theoretical grounding and robust empirical validation that addresses the fundamental problem of lossy image compression.

3. **Supporting Arguments:** 
   - The paper directly tackles the problem of optimizing rate-distortion performance in image compression through end-to-end neural network training.
   - The approach is exceptionally well-motivated, with thorough literature placement that clearly distinguishes their method from both classical transform coding and related deep learning approaches.
   - The paper provides rigorous empirical validation, including comparisons to JPEG 2000 (not just JPEG), demonstrating consistent improvements in both objective metrics (PSNR) and perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) across multiple image sets.

4. **Additional Feedback:** To strengthen the paper further, I recommend including results on standard test images (Lena, Barbara, Baboon) and providing explicit comparisons to state-of-the-art classical methods beyond JPEG 2000. A clearer positioning statement that explicitly contrasts neural-network methods with the best classical approaches would also enhance the paper's impact.

5. **Questions for Authors:**
   - Could you provide rate-distortion curves for the standard images (Lena, Barbara, Baboon) and compare your method's performance to the most recent classical compression techniques (e.g., BPG, HEIF)?
   - How do you position your method relative to the best non-neural network approaches in the literature, particularly those that might have been overlooked in your comparison?
