

This paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression system that achieves superior rate-distortion performance compared to standard methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The authors introduce a novel framework for jointly optimizing the entire compression pipeline (analysis transform, quantizer, synthesis transform) using stochastic gradient descent, overcoming the traditional challenge of optimizing lossy compression with discontinuous quantization steps. Their key technical contribution is a continuous relaxation of the quantization step that enables differentiable training, allowing the model to operate at any point along the rate-distortion curve.

The paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors clearly situate their work within the long-standing problem of lossy image compression, acknowledging the limitations of traditional transform coding methods and the recent advances in neural network-based approaches. They provide a thoughtful comparison with related work, particularly distinguishing their approach from variational autoencoders and other generative models. The use of generalized divisive normalization (GDN) as a biologically-inspired nonlinearity is well-justified through prior work on image statistics and density estimation.

The paper provides strong empirical evidence supporting its claims. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements in both PSNR and MS-SSIM across multiple test images at all bit rates, with particularly striking visual improvements that address the common artifacts of traditional compression methods (blocking, ringing, etc.). They carefully address the critical issue of rate estimation by implementing a proper entropy code and reporting actual bit rates rather than relying on entropy approximations. The ablation studies showing that GDN/IGDN nonlinearities outperform simpler activation functions with fewer parameters further strengthen their claims.

The paper's most significant contribution is the differentiable approximation of the rate function, which enables end-to-end training of the entire compression pipeline. This technique is not only valuable for image compression but also has broader implications for any problem requiring differentiable approximations of rate-distortion trade-offs. The authors thoughtfully address the relationship to variational autoencoders and explain why their approach is fundamentally different and better suited for compression.

Second, the work is well-motivated and properly situated within the literature, with thoughtful comparisons to relevant prior work.

The paper's contribution is significant because it demonstrates that end-to-end optimization of image compression can yield substantial improvements over traditional hand-crafted methods. The authors show that their neural network-based approach not only achieves better rate-distortion performance but also produces visually superior results that are consistent across all bit rates and image types. This is particularly impressive given that the model was trained using MSE as the distortion metric, yet the results show clear perceptual advantages.

The differentiable rate approximation is a technically elegant solution to a challenging problem. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their relaxed loss function provides a good approximation to actual rate-distortion values, and they carefully validate this through empirical evaluation. This approach enables the optimization of the entire compression pipeline at any desired point along the rate-distortion curve, which is a major advantage over previous methods.

Additional feedback: While the paper is strong overall, there are a few areas where the authors could further improve their work. First, it would be helpful to include more detailed comparisons with the state-of-the-art in neural network-based compression, such as the work by Toderici et al. (2016) and Gregor et al. (2016), which the authors mention but don't thoroughly compare against. Second, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method during encoding and decoding, particularly for deployment in resource-constrained environments.

Could you discuss the potential benefits of training with a perceptual distortion metric directly, and how this might affect the model architecture or training procedure?
