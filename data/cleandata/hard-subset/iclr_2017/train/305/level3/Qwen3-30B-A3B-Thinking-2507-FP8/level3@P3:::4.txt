

# Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper presents a groundbreaking end-to-end learning framework for image compression that jointly optimizes the analysis and synthesis transforms, quantizer, and entropy code for rate-distortion performance. The authors propose a novel nonlinear transform coding method using cascaded generalized divisive normalization (GDN) operations, inspired by biological visual processing, followed by uniform quantization. Crucially, they introduce a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization, effectively solving the intractable rate-distortion optimization problem. The method demonstrates superior rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 across multiple metrics, with particularly dramatic improvements in visual quality at all bit rates. The authors provide a thorough analysis of their approach's relationship to variational autoencoders and convincingly demonstrate that their continuous relaxation provides an accurate approximation of the discrete compression problem.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression by introducing a practical, end-to-end trainable compression framework that outperforms established methods with compelling evidence.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
**1. Problem Statement:** The paper clearly addresses the fundamental problem of lossy image compression as a rate-distortion optimization challenge, recognizing that traditional transform coding methods (like JPEG and JPEG 2000) optimize their components separately rather than jointly. The authors correctly identify that joint optimization is difficult due to the discontinuous nature of quantization, which the paper elegantly solves through a continuous relaxation.

**2. Motivation and Literature Placement:** The motivation is exceptionally strong, well-placed in the literature, and clearly articulated. The authors demonstrate a deep understanding of both classical image compression (transform coding, rate-distortion theory) and modern deep learning approaches. They properly contextualize their work relative to prior neural network-based compression methods (Gregor et al., 2016; Toderici et al., 2016) and clearly explain the critical differences between their approach and variational autoencoders. The use of biologically-inspired GDN operations is well-justified by prior work on image statistics and neural modeling.

**3. Claim Support:** The paper provides robust empirical evidence to support its claims. The experimental results are thorough and compelling, showing consistent improvements in both objective metrics (PSNR) and perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) across multiple test images and bit rates. The authors carefully analyze the relationship between their continuous relaxation and actual discrete compression performance, demonstrating that the relaxation provides an accurate approximation. The visual comparisons are particularly convincing, showing how their method avoids the blocking and ringing artifacts characteristic of linear transform coding methods.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:
- Including a more detailed comparison of computational complexity (training and inference) relative to JPEG and JPEG 2000.
- Providing a more systematic analysis of how the GDN/IGDN operations specifically contribute to the performance gains compared to simpler nonlinearities.
- Clarifying the relationship between the number of stages in the transform and the resulting rate-distortion performance, as this has implications for deployment in resource-constrained environments.
- Including a brief discussion of how the method might be extended to video compression.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that "rectified linear units generally require a substantially larger number of model parameters/stages to achieve the same rate-distortion performance as the GDN/IGDN nonlinearities." Could you provide more specific quantitative comparisons between GDN/IGDN and other activation functions in terms of parameter count versus performance?

2. In section 4, the authors mention that their method "exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images." Could you elaborate on the specific characteristics of the images where the method underperforms, and whether these characteristics might be related to the training dataset or the optimization process?

3. The paper states that "the relaxed (differential) entropy provides a somewhat positively biased estimate of the discrete entropy for the coarser quantization regime." Given this bias, how much does the actual bit rate differ from the relaxed rate estimate, and what implications does this have for the practical deployment of the method?
