

# Review for "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper presents a groundbreaking approach to image compression using deep neural networks, demonstrating a novel framework for end-to-end optimization of image compression models. The authors develop a complete compression pipeline consisting of a nonlinear analysis transformation, uniform quantizer, and nonlinear synthesis transformation, all jointly optimized for rate-distortion performance. The key innovation lies in their smooth integration of rate-distortion theory into the objective function through a continuous relaxation of the quantization step, enabling effective use of stochastic gradient descent. The method outperforms standard JPEG and JPEG 2000 compression methods across multiple metrics, with particularly impressive gains in visual quality as measured by MS-SSIM. The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the theoretical underpinnings and practical implementation details. The authors' use of biologically inspired nonlinear transformations (GDN/IGDN) proves highly effective, creating a model that not only achieves better rate-distortion performance but also produces visually superior results that avoid the common artifacts of traditional transform coding methods.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance of this paper. This is the most convincing paper on image compression with deep neural networks I have read, and I expect it to have strong impact on the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully addresses the three key questions for acceptance:

1. The paper clearly tackles the problem of end-to-end optimization of image compression models for rate-distortion performance, which is a fundamental challenge in the field.

2. The approach is exceptionally well-motivated, with thorough placement in the literature. The authors effectively contrast their method with both classical transform coding approaches (JPEG, JPEG 2000) and recent deep learning-based compression methods. The theoretical foundation in rate-distortion theory is solid, and the paper thoughtfully addresses the relationship to variational autoencoders while highlighting key differences that make their approach suitable for compression.

3. The paper strongly supports its claims through comprehensive experimental validation. The authors demonstrate superior performance across multiple metrics (PSNR, MS-SSIM) and across a variety of test images. The comparison to JPEG 2000 is particularly valuable, as it provides a more meaningful benchmark than the common comparison to JPEG alone. The paper includes detailed ablation studies, validation of the continuous relaxation approximation, and thorough analysis of the visual quality improvements. The results are scientifically rigorous and demonstrate clear improvements over standard methods.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. Including results on standard test images (Lena, Barbara, Baboon) that are commonly used in the image compression community. This would provide immediate context for readers familiar with these benchmarks.

2. Providing more explicit comparisons between neural-network methods and the best previous classical methods, including references to state-of-the-art classical compression techniques from the literature. A table comparing bit rates at specific PSNR values would be particularly helpful.

3. Adding a brief discussion of computational complexity and memory requirements for the proposed method, which is important for practical deployment considerations.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. Could you clarify how your method compares to other state-of-the-art classical compression methods beyond JPEG and JPEG 2000, such as the JPEG-XR standard or the newer BPG codec? A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the positioning of your work.

2. The paper demonstrates significant visual quality improvements with the MSE-optimized model. Could you provide more insight into why this occurs, particularly with respect to how the nonlinear transforms capture perceptually important features that MSE optimization alone might miss?

3. The paper mentions preliminary results with alternative nonlinearities (ReLU, sigmoidal), but the primary results use GDN/IGDN. Could you elaborate on the specific advantages of GDN/IGDN for compression that might not be captured by other nonlinearities, particularly in terms of model efficiency (number of parameters/stages needed)?
