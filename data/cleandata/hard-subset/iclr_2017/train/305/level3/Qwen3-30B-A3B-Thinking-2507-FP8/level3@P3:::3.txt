

# Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a groundbreaking end-to-end trained image compression system that significantly outperforms established methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000 in rate-distortion trade-offs. The authors propose a novel framework consisting of a nonlinear analysis transformation, uniform quantizer, and nonlinear synthesis transformation, all jointly optimized for rate-distortion performance. The key technical contribution is a differentiable approximation of the "rate" function that enables effective training through stochastic gradient descent, overcoming the fundamental challenge of quantization's non-differentiability. The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves superior objective metrics (PSNR) and, more importantly, substantially improved visual quality across all tested images and bit rates. The paper also thoughtfully addresses the relationship to variational autoencoders while highlighting important distinctions between generative modeling and compression objectives.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a significant technical contribution with a novel differentiable approximation of the rate function that enables end-to-end optimization of image compression systems, and the experimental results compellingly demonstrate improved performance.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution—the differentiable approximation of the rate function—addresses a fundamental limitation in training compression systems. The authors' relaxation of the quantization step through additive uniform noise provides a continuous proxy that enables effective gradient-based optimization, which is both elegant and highly practical. This approach has the potential to impact not only image compression but also other domains requiring differentiable approximations of similar discrete optimization problems.

The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare their method against JPEG and JPEG 2000 across multiple metrics (PSNR and MS-SSIM) and a diverse set of test images. Their results show consistent improvements in visual quality across all bit rates, with the most significant gains at lower bit rates where compression artifacts are most problematic. The paper's emphasis on actual bit rates (rather than entropy estimates) for evaluation is particularly noteworthy and aligns with practical compression needs.

The paper also makes a thoughtful contribution to the theoretical understanding of the relationship between compression and generative modeling. The authors clearly articulate the key differences between their approach and variational autoencoders, particularly regarding the discrete nature of compression versus continuous generative modeling, and the different optimization objectives. This theoretical grounding strengthens the paper's contribution and provides valuable context for the community.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, consider the following suggestions:

- The paper mentions that the GDN/IGDN nonlinearities are more parameter-efficient than alternatives, but provides limited quantitative comparison. Including a table or figure showing the parameter count versus performance trade-off would strengthen this claim.

- While the authors implemented a simple CABAC-based entropy code, exploring more sophisticated entropy coding techniques (such as those used in modern codecs) could provide additional insights into the achievable performance ceiling of their framework.

- The paper states that the method was optimized using MSE, yet demonstrates superior visual quality. A brief discussion of how the nonlinear transformations might inherently capture perceptual features would help explain this counterintuitive result.

- The computational complexity of training versus inference could be clarified. While the authors note that encoding/decoding is efficient, the substantial training costs might be relevant for certain applications.

## 5. Clarifying Questions

1. The paper mentions that "a sufficiently complex network could learn to produce codes within a fixed range that maximize entropy (meeting the upper bound)." How does your framework specifically address the practical challenge of achieving this without requiring an exponentially large network?

2. You argue that enforcing a specific form on compressor outputs would force a more complex network or require a separate variable-rate encoder. Could you elaborate on the specific trade-offs between network complexity and the need for an additional variable-rate encoder in your framework?

3. The paper notes that the GDN/IGDN nonlinearities are more efficient than pointwise nonlinearities. Could you provide more specific details about the parameter efficiency comparison (e.g., number of parameters required to achieve the same performance) across different image datasets?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression with a novel technical approach that is both theoretically sound and empirically validated. The differentiable approximation of the rate function represents a substantial advancement that could influence not only compression but also other areas requiring differentiable approximations of discrete processes. The results are compelling, the methodology is sound, and the paper is well-written and well-motivated.
