## Review of END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

This paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression method based on nonlinear transforms, building on prior work in rate-distortion optimization. The authors cleverly adapt this framework to deep encoders/decoders and incorporate adaptive entropy coding, drawing parallels to variational autoencoders (VAEs). The writing is exceptionally clear and the paper is well-structured, making it a pleasure to read. The results demonstrate visually compelling improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000, with strong performance compared to other learned codecs.

**Decision: Accept with Minor Revisions**

The primary reason for acceptance is the clear demonstration of improved visual quality and competitive rate-distortion performance compared to established codecs. Despite the incremental nature of the contribution (building on existing rate-distortion optimization), the practical results are substantial and represent a significant step forward in learned image compression. However, addressing the concerns regarding the metric choice and ablation studies would further strengthen the paper.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of end-to-end optimization for image compression is well-motivated. The paper effectively bridges theoretical rate-distortion optimization with practical deep learning techniques. The use of generalized divisive normalization (GDN) is a reasonable choice, leveraging prior work showing its effectiveness in image density estimation. The experimental results are persuasive, visually demonstrating the superiority of the proposed method. The inclusion of a functional entropy coder, reporting actual bitrates, is crucial for demonstrating a complete compression solution. 

However, the switch from perceptual metrics (used in prior work) to MSE for optimization is concerning. While the authors acknowledge this, the justification – enabling comparison and extending to color images – feels somewhat weak given their established expertise in perceptual modeling. Evaluating solely with PSNR feels inconsistent with the visually-focused improvements showcased. The relationship to VAEs, while insightful, is weakened by the observation (Equation 10) that the method is *not* equivalent to a VAE for non-Euclidean metrics, raising questions about the strength of the analogy.

A key weakness is the lack of ablation studies to dissect the contribution of the adaptive entropy coder versus simply increasing the capacity of the encoder/decoder network. Is the observed performance improvement primarily due to the more sophisticated entropy coding, or is it driven by the deeper network architecture? Without this, it’s difficult to assess the true novelty of the adaptive entropy coding component.  Furthermore, the paper lacks detailed information regarding the adaptive entropy coder itself. A more thorough explanation of its implementation and its impact on compression performance is needed.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not directly impacting the decision):**

*   **Equation 10:** The authors should revisit the derivation of Equation 10.  The partition function *should* depend on g_s(y; θ), which would invalidate the direct equivalence to a VAE when using non-Euclidean metrics. A clarification here is critical.
*   **Metric Choice:**  Please provide a more compelling justification for optimizing with MSE instead of a perceptual metric. If the goal is to demonstrate generality to color images, perhaps include a brief exploration of perceptual metrics for color images or acknowledge the limitations.  Why not include MS-SSIM *during* training?
*   **Ablation Study:**  Include an ablation study where the adaptive entropy coder is removed, and replaced with a simpler, fixed entropy coder (e.g., Huffman coding). This will isolate the effect of the adaptive coder and determine its true contribution.
*   **Entropy Coder Details:**  Expand on the description of the adaptive entropy coder. How is the context modeling implemented? What is the overhead associated with transmitting the context model? What are the limitations of the current implementation?  Quantify the improvement provided by the adaptive coder over a non-adaptive alternative.
*   **Training Details:** Clarify the criteria used for lowering the Adam learning rate. A more precise definition of “stagnation” would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Cost:** Provide more details regarding the computational cost of training the models. This is particularly relevant given the relatively high computational demands of end-to-end optimization.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding Equation 10, can you elaborate on the derivation and confirm whether the partition function indeed depends on g_s(y; θ)? If so, how does this affect the interpretation of the method as a VAE?
2.  What were the primary considerations that led you to choose MSE as the optimization metric, beyond enabling comparison and extending to color images? Were any perceptual metrics considered for color images, and if so, why were they ultimately discarded?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the adaptive entropy coder, including specifics on the context modeling and overhead?  What performance gain (in terms of bitrate) does the adaptive coder provide compared to a simpler, fixed entropy coder?
4.  What is the estimated computational cost (in terms of GPU hours) to train a model for a specific λ value? How does this scale with model capacity (e.g., number of channels)?
