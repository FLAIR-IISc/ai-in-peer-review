## Review of END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

This paper presents a novel end-to-end learning approach to image compression, offering a compelling alternative to traditional methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The core idea is to jointly optimize the analysis, quantization, and synthesis transforms for rate-distortion performance, leveraging a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization. The authors demonstrate impressive results, achieving improved rate-distortion performance and, crucially, significantly enhanced perceptual quality compared to existing standards.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper tackles a significant problem in a thoughtful and innovative way, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The clarity of the presentation and thoroughness of the analysis further strengthen the case for acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper successfully addresses a longstanding challenge in image compression – the joint optimization of all components of the compression pipeline. Existing methods typically optimize these components independently, often relying on hand-crafted transforms and heuristics. This work overcomes this limitation by formulating the problem as an end-to-end trainable model. The key innovation lies in the continuous relaxation of the quantization step, which allows the use of stochastic gradient descent for optimization. This is a non-trivial contribution, as quantization is inherently a discrete operation. The authors clearly articulate the connection between this relaxed optimization problem and variational autoencoders, providing valuable context and insight.

The experimental validation is robust. The authors compare their method to JPEG and JPEG 2000 on the Kodak image dataset, as well as several other standard and photographic images. The results consistently show improvements in both rate-distortion performance and perceptual quality, as measured by MS-SSIM. The authors also meticulously describe their experimental setup, including the training procedure and the implementation of the entropy code, enhancing the reproducibility of their results. The online availability of the test images and compressed results is also a major positive, allowing for independent verification of the findings. The ablation studies relating to the choice of nonlinearities, while not extensive, hint at the importance of the chosen GDN/IGDN transforms.

The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the existing literature, appropriately positioning their work within the context of transform coding, rate-distortion theory, and generative models. The discussion section thoughtfully addresses the limitations of the approach and suggests avenues for future research.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong overall, a few minor points could be addressed to further enhance its clarity and impact. 

*   **Computational Cost:** The paper mentions that training is computationally expensive. A more detailed discussion of the computational resources required (e.g., GPUs, training time) would be helpful for readers considering implementing or extending this work.
*   **Entropy Coding Details:** The description of the entropy coding scheme could be slightly more detailed. For example, discussing the context modeling employed in the CABAC framework would provide a better understanding of its performance.
*   **Generalization to Video:** The paper focuses solely on still image compression. Briefly discussing the potential challenges and opportunities for extending this approach to video compression would be valuable.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the ablation studies give some insight, a brief analysis of the sensitivity of the results to key hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, number of filters) would strengthen the robustness of the findings.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The choice of the GDN/IGDN nonlinearity appears crucial for performance. Could you elaborate on why you believe this nonlinearity is particularly well-suited for image compression compared to other options, such as ReLU or sigmoid?
2.  You mention that the relaxed loss function approximates the discrete problem well. Could you provide more intuition on the conditions under which this approximation holds, and how it might break down in certain scenarios?
3.  The paper compares performance to JPEG and JPEG 2000. Have you considered comparing your method to more recent learned compression techniques (e.g., those based on variational autoencoders or GANs)? If so, how does your approach compare?
4.  The optimization process involves multiple components (analysis transform, synthesis transform, probability models). Did you observe any significant interplay or dependencies between these components during training? Were there any specific challenges in coordinating the optimization of all these parameters simultaneously?
