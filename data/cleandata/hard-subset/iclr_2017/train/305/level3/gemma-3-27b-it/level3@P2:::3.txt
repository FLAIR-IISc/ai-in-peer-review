This paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression method based on nonlinear transforms, achieving improved rate-distortion performance and visual quality compared to standard techniques like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The core contribution lies in formulating a differentiable approximation of the compression rate, allowing for joint optimization of the entire model – analysis transform, quantizer, and synthesis transform – across a range of rate-distortion trade-offs. This is a compelling approach to a long-standing problem.

**Reject.** While the paper demonstrates promising results and a novel approach, the justification for certain design choices and the extent of the improvements aren’t entirely convincing. Specifically, the claim of substantial visual improvement, while supported by MS-SSIM, feels somewhat overstated given the complexity added to the system, and the explanation for *why* this improvement occurs beyond simply better parameter optimization isn’t fully explored. A clearer articulation of the limitations and potential future directions would strengthen the submission.

The paper tackles the fundamental problem of lossy image compression, aiming to jointly optimize rate and distortion – a classically difficult problem due to the non-differentiability of the quantization step. The authors address this by introducing a continuous proxy for quantization using additive uniform noise, effectively relaxing the loss function. This allows for end-to-end training using stochastic gradient descent. The choice of Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) as the core nonlinearity, inspired by biological neurons, is well-motivated by prior work demonstrating its effectiveness in Gaussianizing image densities. The approach is well-placed within the literature, building upon concepts from transform coding, variational autoencoders, and biological vision models. 

The paper supports its claims through empirical evaluation on the Kodak dataset and several standard images. The reported rate-distortion curves and MS-SSIM scores demonstrate consistent improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000. The authors thoughtfully address potential biases and limitations in the evaluation. However, the substantial visual improvements claimed require further scrutiny. While MS-SSIM supports the claim, a more detailed perceptual study with human subjects would provide stronger evidence. Furthermore, the relatively modest gains in PSNR, especially in some cases, raise questions about the practical impact of the method. The paper argues that the benefit isn't simply PSNR improvement, but a fundamentally different *type* of artifact, which is plausible but needs more in-depth investigation. The connection to variational autoencoders is well-explained, and the authors appropriately highlight the differences and nuances.

Here are some additional points to improve the paper:

* **Ablation studies:**  It would be valuable to include ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the architecture, particularly the GDN nonlinearity. How much performance is lost by replacing GDN with simpler activation functions like ReLU?  This would provide insight into the effectiveness of the biologically-inspired design choice.
* **Computational cost:** The paper mentions high training costs but doesn’t provide detailed information on the computational resources required. A more detailed discussion of the practical feasibility of training and deploying the model would be beneficial.
* **Entropy coding details:** While the authors implemented a CABAC-based entropy code, a more thorough comparison to the entropy coding capabilities of JPEG 2000 would be informative. How much of the gain is attributable to the transform itself versus the entropy coding?
* **Clarify the role of λ:** The paper correctly notes that λ doesn't have an intrinsic meaning. However, it might be helpful to briefly discuss strategies for selecting an appropriate value of λ for a given application.
* **Limitations:** Explicitly acknowledging limitations, such as the potential for sensitivity to the training dataset or the challenges of scaling to very high resolutions, would strengthen the paper.

I have a few questions for the authors:

1. The paper mentions that the continuous relaxation approximates quantization error well. What happens if the scale of the additive noise (representing quantization) is significantly increased or decreased during training? Does this affect the final performance, and if so, how?
2. You state that enforcing a specific form on compressor outputs would likely necessitate a more complex network. Could you elaborate on why this is the case? What inherent difficulty arises from trying to achieve the same performance with a simpler network, given the relaxed optimization approach?
3. Are there any observed correlations between the optimal values of λ and the characteristics of the input images (e.g., texture, complexity)? Could this information be used to dynamically adjust λ during encoding?
