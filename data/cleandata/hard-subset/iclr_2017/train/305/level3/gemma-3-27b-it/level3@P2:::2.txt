This paper presents a novel image compression method based on end-to-end optimized nonlinear transforms, coupled with an adaptive entropy coder. The authors construct analysis and synthesis transforms from cascaded convolutional linear filters and nonlinear activation functions, motivated by biological visual systems. They jointly optimize the entire model for rate-distortion performance using stochastic gradient descent and a continuous proxy for quantization, establishing connections to variational autoencoders. The core contribution lies in extending previous rate-distortion optimization frameworks to deep neural network architectures and incorporating an adaptive entropy coding stage, achieving compelling visual improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000.

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. The work presents a significant advance in image compression, demonstrating improved visual quality compared to standard methods, and a clear, well-motivated approach. While the core rate-distortion optimization isn't entirely novel, the successful application to deep architectures, coupled with the adaptive entropy coding, represents a substantial contribution.

My assessment is primarily driven by the strong empirical results and the clarity of the presentation. The improvements in visual quality, consistently observed across test images and bitrates, are remarkable and are well-supported by objective metrics like MS-SSIM. The connection to variational autoencoders provides a valuable theoretical grounding, even if the equivalence is limited by the choice of distortion metric. The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a complex topic accessible and easy to follow. The inclusion of extensive experimental details and online resources is commendable.

However, several points require clarification. Equation 10 appears incorrect; the probability density function of the relaxed model should depend on the output of the synthesis transform, *g<sub>s</sub>(y; θ)*, not simply on the quantized values. This discrepancy implies that the method is not strictly equivalent to a variational autoencoder when using non-Euclidean distortion metrics, as stated in Section 3.1.

The decision to optimize for MSE instead of a perceptual metric, given the authors’ expertise in perceptual image quality assessment, is somewhat puzzling. While the authors acknowledge this trade-off in the Discussion section, a stronger justification is needed, along with a clearer explanation of why PSNR was used for evaluation alongside MS-SSIM. A comparison against JPEG and JPEG2000 using a perceptual metric (even if not optimized for) would strengthen the claims.

Furthermore, the contribution of the adaptive entropy coding stage is not entirely clear. It is difficult to disentangle the improvements resulting from the deeper network capacity versus the benefits of adaptive entropy coding. An ablation study, removing the adaptive entropy coding and comparing performance to a fixed entropy coding scheme, would be extremely valuable. More details about the implementation of the CABAC-based entropy coder are also needed. Specifically, the choice of context modeling and how it interacts with the transform coefficients significantly influences compression performance. Detailing these choices and their impact would enhance the paper's reproducibility. 

Finally, a comment on the experimental setup. Although the authors state they have evaluated many images, the core results are shown with only a limited number of examples. The wide performance variations across images are highlighted, so presenting a more comprehensive set of results, perhaps summarized statistically, would be beneficial.

To improve the paper, I suggest the following:

*   **Correct Equation 10:** Ensure the equation accurately reflects the probability density of the relaxed model, incorporating the synthesis transform.
*   **Justify MSE Optimization:** Provide a more compelling rationale for choosing MSE over a perceptual metric, and consider including PSNR results alongside MS-SSIM.
*   **Ablation Study:** Conduct an ablation study to assess the independent contribution of adaptive entropy coding.
*   **Entropy Coding Details:** Provide a more detailed description of the CABAC implementation, including context modeling choices.
*   **Expanded Results:** Consider including a more comprehensive set of experimental results, potentially summarized statistically.



I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  Can you elaborate on the computational cost of training the models, particularly the memory requirements and training time, compared to training standard convolutional neural networks?
2.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the learning rate and the Adam optimizer parameters?
3.  What is the trade-off between the complexity of the GDN/IGDN nonlinearity and the performance of the compression method? Would simpler nonlinearities, such as ReLU, achieve comparable results with increased model capacity?
4.  Regarding the adaptive entropy coding, what is the overhead in terms of the number of bits required to transmit the context models to the decoder? Is this overhead significant, particularly at lower bitrates?
5.  How does the performance of the method scale with image resolution? Are there any limitations or challenges associated with compressing high-resolution images?
