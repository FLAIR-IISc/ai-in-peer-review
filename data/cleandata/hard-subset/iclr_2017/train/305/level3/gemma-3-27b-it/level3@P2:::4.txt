This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression method based on nonlinear transforms. The authors address the challenging problem of jointly optimizing rate and distortion in image compression, traditionally tackled with separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy coding stages. Their approach constructs an analysis and synthesis transform using cascaded convolutional linear filters and nonlinear activation functions, inspired by biological neurons, and learns these transforms directly from data using stochastic gradient descent. A continuous relaxation of the quantization step allows for optimization despite the discontinuity, and the method demonstrates superior performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, particularly in visual quality as assessed by MS-SSIM.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of image compression. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and supported by compelling experimental results. The improvements in visual quality over established methods are particularly noteworthy.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles a crucial problem in signal processing with a fresh perspective. The motivation for end-to-end optimization is clearly articulated, and the challenges of traditional transform coding are effectively highlighted. The authors successfully bridge the gap between rate-distortion theory and deep learning by formulating a tractable optimization problem and leveraging gradient descent. 

The technical details are well-explained, including the choice of generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and the reasoning behind it. The connection to variational autoencoders is insightful, providing a theoretical grounding for the approach and clarifying its relationship to other generative models. The authors are careful to differentiate their work from VAEs, pointing out the crucial constraint of operating at *any* point along the rate-distortion curve.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. Comparisons to JPEG and JPEG 2000 are well-documented, and the use of both MSE and MS-SSIM provides a comprehensive assessment of performance. The fact that the method outperforms existing approaches on both metrics, especially in terms of visual quality, is a strong indication of its effectiveness. The availability of test images and rate-distortion curves online is a valuable resource for the research community. The ablation study and discussion around the choice of GDN vs. ReLU are also beneficial.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is excellent overall, a few minor points could further enhance its clarity and impact.

*   **Computational Cost:** A more detailed discussion of the computational cost of training and compression/decompression would be helpful. While the authors mention that decoding is efficient, quantifying the training time and resource requirements would provide a more complete picture.
*   **Generalization to Video:** It would be interesting to briefly discuss the potential for extending this framework to video compression. Are there any specific challenges or modifications that would be necessary?
*   **Robustness to Adversarial Attacks:** Given the connection to generative models, it might be worth briefly commenting on the potential robustness (or lack thereof) of this method to adversarial attacks.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the performance to hyperparameter choices (e.g., learning rate, filter sizes) would provide useful insights.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The authors mention that the continuous relaxation of the quantization step introduces a bias in entropy estimation. How does this bias affect the choice of λ, and is there a systematic way to compensate for it?
2.  The use of ImageNet for training is mentioned. Have you explored the impact of the training dataset composition on the performance of the compression method? Specifically, how does performance vary when trained on datasets with different characteristics (e.g., medical images, satellite imagery)?
3.  The appendix details the parameterization and optimization techniques used. Could you elaborate on the reasons behind choosing DCT coefficients for the filters, and how this choice impacts convergence speed and performance compared to other parameterizations?
4.  In the discussion of RGB images, you mention increasing the network capacity (number of channels). Was this done systematically, or was it more of a heuristic approach? How does increasing the capacity impact the rate-distortion performance?
