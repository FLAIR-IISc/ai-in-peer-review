## Review of “END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION”

This paper presents a compelling end-to-end learning method for image compression, leveraging rate-distortion optimization with a cleverly designed relaxation technique. The core idea – constructing analysis and synthesis transforms using cascaded convolutional layers and nonlinearities, specifically a generalized divisive normalization (GDN) – is well-motivated by both engineering principles and neuroscientific insights into visual processing. The authors demonstrate that their optimized model surpasses the performance of standard JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of both rate-distortion metrics and, crucially, subjective visual quality. The thorough experimental analysis, including comparisons across a diverse set of images and detailed ablation studies, strengthens the validity of their claims.

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The approach is novel and impactful, offering a significant step forward in image compression technology. The observed improvements in visual quality, particularly, are striking and address a long-standing limitation of traditional compression methods.

My recommendation is based on the following arguments. First, the paper tackles a significant problem in a well-defined manner. The formulation of image compression as a rate-distortion optimization problem is standard, but the proposed end-to-end learning framework, coupled with the specific architectural choices (GDN and cascaded convolutions), represents a substantial contribution. Second, the approach is firmly grounded in existing literature, drawing connections to both classic transform coding and recent advances in generative modeling (specifically, variational autoencoders). The authors convincingly explain how their method relates to and differs from these prior works. Finally, the paper provides strong empirical evidence to support its claims. The consistent improvement in MS-SSIM scores, alongside the visual examples, convincingly demonstrates the superiority of their method. The inclusion of actual bit rates, rather than relying solely on entropy estimates, further validates the practical applicability of the proposed system.

To further strengthen the paper, I have a few suggestions. The authors mention the computational cost of training as a limitation. Providing more detailed information about the training time and resources required would be helpful. Additionally, exploring the sensitivity of the method to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the regularization parameter λ, could provide valuable insights. Finally, while the results demonstrate impressive performance on standard image datasets, it would be interesting to see how the method performs on images with specific characteristics (e.g., high dynamic range, medical images). 

Specifically, I would like the authors to clarify: 

*   How does the performance of the model scale with image size?
*   What is the impact of the downsampling factors within the analysis transform on compression performance and computational cost?
*   Could the authors elaborate on the choices made for the piecewise linear function representation of the marginals and the impact of the sampling interval on performance?
