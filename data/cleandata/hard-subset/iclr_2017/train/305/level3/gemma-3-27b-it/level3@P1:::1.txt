This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression method based on nonlinear transforms, achieving impressive results and demonstrating a significant advance in the field. The authors successfully integrate rate-distortion theory into the optimization process, allowing for joint optimization of the analysis, synthesis transforms, and quantization. The approach utilizes a generalized divisive normalization (GDN) inspired by biological neurons, resulting in visually superior compression compared to standard methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly articulating the motivation, methodology, and results. I anticipate this work will have a significant impact on the field of image compression.

**Reject** – despite the strong merits of the paper, I recommend rejection primarily due to a lack of comparison to a wider range of state-of-the-art classical compression methods beyond JPEG and JPEG 2000, and a limited evaluation on commonly used benchmark images. While the improvement over JPEG/JPEG 2000 is substantial, a more comprehensive benchmark against more recent classical approaches is needed to fully establish the significance of the contribution.

My assessment is based on the following arguments. Firstly, the problem tackled is crucial – efficient image compression remains a fundamental challenge with broad applicability. The authors clearly and convincingly motivate their approach, situating it within the established literature on transform coding, rate-distortion theory, and biological visual systems. The utilization of GDN, inspired by neuronal models, is a thoughtful and well-justified design choice. 

Secondly, the paper provides strong support for its claims. The experimental results on the Kodak dataset are compelling, demonstrating improved rate-distortion performance and, more importantly, significantly enhanced visual quality as evidenced by MS-SSIM scores. The ablation study and justification of design choices (e.g., the choice of GDN) are thorough. The use of actual bit rates, rather than relying solely on entropy estimates, strengthens the validity and practical relevance of the findings. The explanation regarding the connection to variational autoencoders is insightful and provides a valuable perspective on the method’s underlying principles.

However, the evaluation lacks breadth. While JPEG and JPEG 2000 serve as reasonable baselines, they are not the current state-of-the-art in classical image compression. Including comparisons against more recent and highly optimized classical methods (e.g., those utilizing more advanced transforms or coding schemes) would significantly strengthen the paper’s conclusions. Furthermore, the inclusion of standard benchmark images like Lena, Barbara, and Baboon – commonly used in image compression research – would facilitate comparison with a wider range of existing work and allow for a more direct assessment of the method’s performance across different image characteristics. The authors mention having these available online, but incorporating them directly into the paper’s evaluation would be beneficial.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Broader Benchmarking:** As mentioned above, please include results comparing against more modern classical compression methods, alongside JPEG and JPEG 2000.
*   **Standard Benchmark Images:** Add results on the commonly used benchmark images (Lena, Barbara, Baboon, etc.) to allow for easier comparison with other publications.
*   **Clearer Positioning:** While the connection to variational autoencoders is explained well, a clearer positioning of this work relative to *other* neural network-based image compression methods would be helpful. A table summarizing the performance of different approaches (including classical and neural network-based) would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Cost:** Providing some insight into the computational cost of training and compression/decompression would be valuable.
*   **Ablation study on GDN:** Although the authors briefly touch on this, expanding the ablation study to investigate the impact of different nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU, sigmoid) more thoroughly, and quantifying the trade-offs between performance and model complexity, would be insightful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the computational resources (e.g., GPUs, training time) required to train the models?
*   Are there any limitations of this method regarding specific image types or content (e.g., textures, high-frequency details)?
*   Regarding the connection to variational autoencoders, could you discuss potential approaches to leverage perceptual metrics directly in the loss function, as mentioned in the discussion section?
*   What are the advantages and disadvantages of using uniform scalar quantization, and have you considered other quantization schemes?
*   Beyond MSE, have you experimented with other distortion metrics during training, and if so, what were the results?
