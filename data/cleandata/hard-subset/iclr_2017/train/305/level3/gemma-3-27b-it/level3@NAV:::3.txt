## Review of “END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION”

This paper presents an end-to-end trained image compression system achieving improved rate-distortion performance compared to established methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The key technical contribution lies in a differentiable approximation of the rate function, enabling joint optimization across varying rate-distortion trade-offs. This is a significant accomplishment, and the authors have thoughtfully addressed a pre-review question, demonstrating engagement and responsiveness. The approach utilizes a cascade of convolutional linear filters and nonlinear activations, inspired by biological neurons, with a globally optimized generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearity.

I **accept** this paper. The core innovation – the differentiable rate approximation – is compelling and effectively allows for end-to-end training of a compression system. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate improvements over established benchmarks in both rate-distortion performance and, critically, visual quality, as evidenced by MS-SSIM scores. Furthermore, the authors successfully tie their work to the broader context of variational autoencoders, highlighting the potential broader impact of this differentiable approach.

The strength of this paper resides in the clever workaround for the non-differentiability of the quantization step. The authors convincingly argue, and demonstrate empirically, that a continuous relaxation of the rate function, using uniform noise, provides a viable and effective training signal. My initial concern—that a sufficiently complex network *could* learn to produce codes within a fixed range maximizing entropy—is addressed by the authors’ discussion regarding enforcing a specific form on the compressor outputs. They rightly point out that a more flexible network or a separate variable-rate encoder would be needed to match the current system's effective compression, bolstering the value of their approach.

To further strengthen the paper, I suggest the authors elaborate on the computational cost associated with training these models compared to traditional methods. While decoding is efficient, the training phase appears demanding. Additionally, exploring the sensitivity of the performance to different hyperparameter settings (e.g., the number of stages, filter sizes) could offer valuable insights. Finally, could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of the GDN nonlinearity? While its efficiency is alluded to, quantifying its contribution relative to other activation functions would be insightful.

Specifically, I'm curious about:

*   **Scaling:** How does the performance scale with image resolution? Are there limitations to the size of images that can be effectively compressed with this method?
*   **Generalization:** How well does the method generalize to image datasets significantly different from ImageNet?
*   **Perceptual Metric Selection:** If a more sophisticated perceptual metric beyond MS-SSIM were used during training, would the visual improvements be even more pronounced?
