## Review of END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression method leveraging deep convolutional networks with a biologically-inspired nonlinear transform. The core contribution lies in formulating a differentiable approximation to the rate function, allowing joint optimization of the analysis, synthesis transforms, and quantizer for desired rate-distortion trade-offs. The method demonstrably outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of both rate-distortion performance and, crucially, perceptual image quality.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper tackles a significant problem in a well-motivated manner and provides compelling evidence supporting its claims. The approach is innovative, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate its superiority over existing methods, particularly in visual quality.  The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the field and relate their work to existing literature in compression and generative modeling.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The problem tackled – optimizing image compression end-to-end – is a long-standing challenge. While transform coding is a well-established technique, traditionally the transform, quantizer, and entropy code are optimized separately. This paper elegantly addresses this limitation by formulating a differentiable proxy for the quantization step. This allows for joint optimization, leading to improved performance. The motivation behind utilizing generalized divisive normalization (GDN) inspired by biological visual systems is also well-justified, building on prior work showing its effectiveness in Gaussianizing image densities.

The paper provides strong empirical support for its claims.  Extensive experiments on the Kodak dataset, along with qualitative and quantitative comparisons to JPEG and JPEG 2000, consistently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method. The inclusion of MS-SSIM as a perceptual metric further strengthens the argument, highlighting the improved visual quality even when objective metrics like PSNR show only marginal gains. The authors also acknowledge the limitations of PSNR and provide a substantial visual comparison, allowing the reader to independently assess the quality of the compressed images.  The fact that they implemented a basic entropy code and report actual bitrates adds further credibility, demonstrating the feasibility of a complete compression system.  

Finally, the connection drawn between the relaxed loss function and variational autoencoders is insightful, placing the work within a broader context of generative modeling and suggesting potential avenues for future research.  While the authors acknowledge the distinctions, the connection illuminates the theoretical underpinnings of the method.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, several minor points could be addressed to further improve its clarity and impact. 

*   **Computational Cost:** A more detailed discussion of the computational cost of training and encoding/decoding would be beneficial. How does the method compare to JPEG/JPEG 2000 in terms of computational complexity?
*   **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The choice of network architecture and optimization parameters appears somewhat ad-hoc. A brief analysis of the sensitivity of the results to these choices would strengthen the argument.
*   **Generalization to Other Datasets:** While the results on the Kodak dataset are convincing, evaluating the method on a wider range of datasets, particularly those with different statistical properties, would broaden the scope of the study.
*   **Ablation Study:**  An ablation study evaluating the contribution of each component of the GDN nonlinearity (beta, gamma) would provide valuable insights into their individual impact on performance. 
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** A more thorough discussion of the potential limitations of the method, such as its performance on images with very high frequency content or specific texture patterns, would be appreciated.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that the continuous relaxation of the quantization step approximates quantization error. What are the implications of this approximation at very low bitrates, where the quantization error is significant?  How might this affect the optimization process and the final performance?
2.  Could you elaborate on the choice of using a piecewise linear function to approximate the marginal densities? What are the trade-offs between using a more complex parametric density model versus a non-parametric approach like the one described?
3.  You mention the potential for using this framework for other tasks beyond compression. Could you provide specific examples of how the differentiable rate approximation could be applied to problems like image denoising or super-resolution?
4.  The paper references previous work demonstrating benefits of batch normalization. Could you explain how GDN differs from batch normalization in this context and why GDN was chosen as more appropriate for the task?
