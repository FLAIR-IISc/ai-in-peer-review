This paper proposes an end-to-end learning method for image compression, leveraging rate-distortion optimization with a clever continuous relaxation to learn efficient compression transforms directly from image data. The approach utilizes cascaded convolutional linear filters and nonlinear activation functions (specifically, Generalized Divisive Normalization – GDN) for both analysis and synthesis, optimized jointly. The experimental results demonstrate compelling performance, surpassing JPEG and JPEG 2000 in both objective metrics (PSNR, MS-SSIM) and, importantly, subjective visual quality. The analysis provided is thorough and insightful, detailing the optimization process, architectural choices, and connections to related work like variational autoencoders. I recommend acceptance.

**Decision:** Accept, with high confidence. The paper presents a novel and effective approach to image compression, demonstrably outperforming existing standards in visual quality, and backed by a solid theoretical and empirical foundation.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Novelty and Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental problem in computer vision – image compression – with a fresh perspective.  Instead of hand-engineering transforms (like DCT in JPEG), the authors *learn* the optimal transforms directly from data using an end-to-end approach. This is a significant departure from traditional methods and holds substantial promise for future improvements. The continuous relaxation of the quantization step is a key innovation enabling gradient-based optimization.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on the Kodak dataset (and additional images) are convincing. The improvement in MS-SSIM, particularly, suggests a genuine advancement in perceptual quality, going beyond simply minimizing pixel-wise error (MSE). The inclusion of comparisons with JPEG and JPEG 2000 provides a necessary benchmark and highlights the benefits of the proposed approach. The availability of compressed images online for inspection is excellent.
3. **Thorough Analysis:** The paper doesn't simply present results; it provides a detailed explanation of the method, including the rationale behind architectural choices (GDN), the optimization procedure, and the connection to variational autoencoders. The discussion of the continuous relaxation and its relationship to entropy estimation is particularly insightful.  The authors acknowledge limitations and potential future directions, demonstrating a nuanced understanding of the work.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

* **Computational Cost:** While the paper mentions that encoding/decoding is efficient *after* training, the training process itself is likely computationally expensive. Providing some indication of the training time or resources required would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper states architectural choices were "somewhat ad-hoc." A brief exploration of the sensitivity of performance to different network architectures (number of layers, filter sizes, etc.) would strengthen the robustness of the findings.
* **Entropy Code Details:** While the CABAC implementation is described, a more quantitative assessment of its efficiency compared to state-of-the-art entropy coders would be helpful. How much overhead is introduced by the implementation?
* **Generalization to Other Datasets:** The experiments primarily focus on ImageNet and Kodak datasets.  Evaluating the method’s performance on other types of images (e.g., medical images, satellite imagery) would assess its broader applicability.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. **Regarding the continuous relaxation:** While the paper demonstrates that the relaxed entropy estimates are reasonably accurate, how sensitive is the performance of the trained model to imperfections in this approximation? Specifically, could a more accurate (but computationally expensive) relaxation scheme lead to further improvements?
2. **Scalability to High Resolution Images:** The reported results are on relatively small images. How does the performance of the method scale to very high-resolution images (e.g., those commonly found in modern digital cameras)? Are there any practical limitations or modifications needed to handle larger images efficiently?
3. **Choice of GDN:** The authors suggest GDN/IGDN are more efficient than other activation functions. Can you elaborate on the specific properties of GDN that contribute to this efficiency in the context of image compression? Are there any theoretical arguments supporting this claim?
4. **Future Work on Perceptual Metrics:** The paper acknowledges that optimizing using a perceptual metric could improve results. Are there specific perceptual metrics you are considering for future work, and what are the challenges of incorporating them into the optimization process?
