## Review of END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

This paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression method based on cascaded convolutional neural networks with a novel generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearity. The authors jointly optimize the analysis and synthesis transforms for rate-distortion performance, utilizing a continuous relaxation of the quantization process to enable gradient-based optimization. They demonstrate visually superior performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, supported by objective quality estimates using MS-SSIM, and draw connections to the framework of variational autoencoders.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a significant and well-executed contribution to the field of image compression. Despite some minor limitations, the substantial visual improvements over standard compression algorithms and the rigorous experimental validation justify acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles a fundamental problem in engineering – image compression – with a fresh perspective leveraging recent advances in deep learning. The approach is well-motivated, building on prior work in rate-distortion optimization and extending it to more complex, end-to-end trainable architectures. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of traditional transform coding methods and demonstrate how their approach overcomes these limitations by jointly optimizing all components of the compression pipeline.

The empirical results are compelling. The visual comparisons presented demonstrate a clear improvement in image quality over JPEG and JPEG 2000, even at lower bitrates. While the use of MSE as a distortion metric is debated (see below), the qualitative improvements are consistently observed across a diverse set of test images, and are corroborated by quantitative metrics like MS-SSIM. The inclusion of an adaptive entropy code further strengthens the practical relevance of the work.  The detailed discussion of the training process and the analysis of the relaxed loss function are particularly strong.

The authors have performed a comprehensive experimental evaluation, comparing their method to established standards on a standard dataset. They also make their test images and compressed results publicly available, promoting reproducibility and further research.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented, a few areas could benefit from further elaboration. The decision to optimize for MSE instead of a perceptual metric, especially given the authors’ previous work in this area, is a notable one.  A brief discussion of the trade-offs involved in this choice – perhaps relating to computational cost or ease of comparison to existing methods – would be valuable. Similarly, while PSNR is reported, a more thorough evaluation using a broader range of perceptual metrics would further strengthen the claim of superior visual quality.

Equation (10) appears to contain an error. The partition function should likely depend on *g<sub>s</sub>(y; θ)*, which would imply that the equivalence to a variational autoencoder only holds for Euclidean metrics. Clarifying this point would be beneficial.

Furthermore, the contribution of the adaptive entropy coding component is not entirely clear. An ablation study removing the adaptive component would help to isolate its impact and demonstrate its added value relative to simply increasing the capacity of the encoder/decoder networks. More details about the implementation of the entropy code, including the context modeling strategy, would also be useful. What is the overhead imposed by the adaptive entropy coder?

Finally, it would be useful to see a comparison against more recent learned codecs, such as those presented at recent conferences like NeurIPS and ICML. While JPEG and JPEG 2000 provide a solid baseline, assessing performance against state-of-the-art learned compression techniques would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the method’s capabilities.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. Can you elaborate on the rationale for choosing MSE as the distortion metric, particularly given your prior work using perceptual metrics?
2. Could you clarify the correct form of Equation (10), and discuss the implications of the correction for the relationship to variational autoencoders, especially when using non-Euclidean distortion metrics?
3. What is the impact of the adaptive entropy coding component on the compression performance? Have you conducted an ablation study to assess its contribution relative to increasing network capacity?
4. Could you provide more details about the implementation of the adaptive entropy coder, including the context modeling strategy and the computational overhead?
5. How does the proposed method compare to more recent learned image compression techniques, such as those published in NeurIPS and ICML?
