This paper presents a compelling end-to-end optimized image compression method based on nonlinear transforms. The authors tackle the fundamental problem of lossy image compression, framing it within a rate-distortion optimization framework and leveraging recent advances in deep learning to achieve state-of-the-art results. The approach, consisting of cascaded convolutional linear filters and nonlinear activation functions inspired by biological neurons, exhibits substantial improvements in visual quality compared to standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The novelty lies in the complete end-to-end optimization of the compression pipeline, a departure from traditional methods that typically optimize components separately. More importantly, the significant and consistently reported improvements in perceptual quality, supported by objective metrics like MS-SSIM, are highly convincing and demonstrate a substantial advancement in the field.

The motivation for this work is excellent. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of traditional transform coding, highlighting the challenges of joint rate-distortion optimization and the sub-optimality of manually tuned parameters. They skillfully connect the problem to probabilistic source modeling, referencing Shannon’s information theory and Gersho and Gray’s work on quantization. The choice of Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) is well-justified, drawing on previous research demonstrating its effectiveness in Gaussianizing image densities and its biological plausibility. The paper is exceptionally well-placed within the existing literature, appropriately citing relevant works on transform coding, variational autoencoders, and perceptual image quality assessment.

The paper provides strong support for its claims. The experimental results, presented on the Kodak image dataset and a collection of digital photographs, consistently demonstrate superior performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000. The authors acknowledge and address potential biases in the evaluation by reporting actual bit rates rather than relying solely on entropy estimates. The inclusion of a continuous relaxation of the quantization step for optimization and the subsequent validation of its accuracy are particularly noteworthy. The ablation study, though not explicitly labeled as such, comparing to alternative nonlinearities in the discussion section, further strengthens the findings. The online availability of test images and rate-distortion curves adds to the reproducibility and transparency of the work. 

However, a few points could further improve the paper. While the comparison to JPEG 2000 is well-justified, including results on standard, commonly used test images such as Lena, Barbara, and Baboon would provide a more direct comparison to a wider range of published results. Furthermore, explicitly positioning the method relative to the state-of-the-art in *classical* image compression techniques (beyond JPEG/JPEG2000) – referencing benchmarks and offering direct comparisons – would strengthen the presentation. The paper alludes to related work by Gregor et al. (2016) and Toderici et al. (2016), but a more thorough discussion of the differences and advantages of this method compared to those would be beneficial. Specifically, contrasting performance and algorithmic complexity would be useful.

I have a few questions for the authors:

1.  The paper mentions using a simple context-based arithmetic coding (CABAC) scheme. Could the authors elaborate on the specific implementation details of the CABAC encoder, including the context modeling strategy? How does this compare to the CABAC implementations used in JPEG 2000 and H.264/AVC in terms of complexity and compression efficiency?
2.  The choice of network architecture (number of filters, kernel sizes, downsampling factors) seems somewhat ad-hoc. Were any architectural search methods or hyperparameter optimization techniques employed to determine these values?
3.  The authors state that the relaxed distortion term is mostly unbiased. Could they provide a more quantitative assessment of the bias and variance of both the relaxed distortion and the relaxed entropy estimates? Specifically, how does the accuracy of the relaxation improve as the sampling interval used for the piecewise linear approximation of the density is decreased?
4.  The appendix provides extensive details on the optimization process. Would a publicly released implementation of the code be feasible, to further promote reproducibility and facilitate adoption by other researchers?
