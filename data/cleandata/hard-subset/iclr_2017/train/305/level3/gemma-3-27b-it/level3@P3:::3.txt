## Review: END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

**1. Summary**

This paper presents a novel end-to-end trainable image compression system that achieves competitive, and in many cases superior, rate-distortion performance compared to established standards like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The key contribution lies in formulating a differentiable approximation to the rate function, allowing for joint optimization of the analysis and synthesis transforms via stochastic gradient descent. The authors demonstrate a significant improvement in perceptual quality, supported by objective metrics like MS-SSIM, and provide a thorough comparison to existing methods. The work builds on prior research in nonlinear transforms and biologically inspired visual models, offering a compelling advance in the field of image compression. Furthermore, the authors thoughtfully addressed a pre-review question, demonstrating engagement and a clear understanding of their work’s implications.

**2. Decision**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a significant technical contribution with strong empirical results. The differentiable rate approximation is a clever solution to a challenging problem, and the demonstrated improvements in compression performance and perceptual quality are compelling.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The core innovation—the differentiable rate approximation—is exceptionally well-motivated. The intractability of directly optimizing rate and distortion jointly necessitates approximations, and the proposed continuous relaxation via additive noise provides a practical and effective solution. The reviewer’s point regarding the potential for a sufficiently complex network to learn entropy maximization is acknowledged and addressed effectively. The authors convincingly argue that imposing a specific form on the compressor outputs (via the quantization step) prevents the network from simply learning an arbitrary mapping and forces the development of a more efficient and structured representation. This addresses a crucial potential criticism.

The experimental results are robust and convincing. The comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 across a diverse image dataset demonstrates consistent improvements in both objective and subjective quality. The inclusion of actual bit rates, rather than solely relying on entropy estimates, strengthens the practical relevance of the work. The thorough ablation studies and analysis of the relaxation approximation (Figure 4) inspire confidence in the validity of the approach. The provided online resources—the complete set of test images and rate-distortion curves—further enhance the reproducibility and assessment of the results.

The authors also effectively situate their work within the broader context of variational autoencoders, highlighting both similarities and crucial differences. This clarifies the unique contributions of their approach and underscores its relevance to the ongoing development of generative models.

**4. Additional Feedback**

While the paper is strong, a few points could further improve clarity and impact:

*   **Computational Cost:** A more detailed discussion of the computational cost of training and encoding/decoding would be valuable. How does the complexity scale with image size and desired compression ratio? A comparison of training time to existing methods would also be helpful.
*   **Choice of GDN:** While justified, the reliance on GDN is somewhat strong. Briefly exploring the performance of alternative non-linearities (beyond the preliminary mention in the Discussion) could provide further insight into the importance of this specific architectural choice.
*   **Entropy Coding Details:** The description of the CABAC implementation could be expanded. Specifically, the trade-offs between computational complexity and compression gains from adaptive modeling would be useful to understand.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: Discuss the sensitivity of the results to the λ parameter and how it’s selected. What strategies are used to find a good value of λ for different images/datasets?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the training process, how sensitive are the results to the specific initialization of the network parameters? Were multiple random initializations explored?
2.  The paper mentions that the relaxed distortion term is mostly unbiased. Could you elaborate on the nature of any remaining bias, and how this might affect the optimization process or the final compression performance?
3.  The authors address the potential for arbitrarily complex networks to achieve high entropy. Given this, do you foresee any theoretical limitations on the achievable compression ratio with this approach, and how does this compare to the theoretical limits imposed by information theory?
4.  Could you provide some insights into the learned filters and their characteristics? Are there discernible patterns or features that emerge from the optimization process, offering a better understanding of the learned representation?
