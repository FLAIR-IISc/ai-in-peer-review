### Summary

The paper proposes a complete, end-to-end optimized framework for lossy image compression based on deep neural networks. The model comprises a nonlinear analysis transform (encoder), a uniform quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform (decoder). A key feature of the architecture is the use of a cascade of convolutional layers and Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN), a biologically-inspired nonlinearity. To overcome the non-differentiability of the quantizer during training, the authors introduce a continuous proxy by adding uniform noise, enabling joint optimization of the entire model for a rate-distortion objective via stochastic gradient descent. The authors provide an insightful connection between their formulation and variational autoencoders, while clearly distinguishing the requirements for a practical compression system. The contribution is a learned compression model that is shown to outperform standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of both PSNR and, most notably, perceptual quality as measured by MS-SSIM. The claims are supported by results based on actual bit rates from an implemented entropy coder, demonstrating a full and practical system.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is an excellent and highly impactful paper. It stands out as one of the most convincing demonstrations of learned image compression to date. My recommendation is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Principled and Sound Methodology:** The paper elegantly integrates classical rate-distortion theory into a modern deep learning framework. The approach for handling the non-differentiable quantization step is a clever and effective solution, allowing for true end-to-end optimization of a compression model.
2.  **Strong and Compelling Results:** The proposed method demonstrates significant improvements over strong, widely adopted baselines (JPEG 2000). The qualitative results are particularly impressive; the compressed images exhibit far more natural-looking artifacts than traditional methods, a claim strongly supported by objective perceptual metrics (MS-SSIM).

### Supporting Arguments

In line with the reviewing guidelines, my decision is based on the following assessment:

1.  **What is the specific question/problem tackled by the paper?**
    The paper tackles the classic problem of lossy image compression. The goal is to design a system that minimizes the number of bits (rate) required to represent an image while keeping the reconstruction error (distortion) as low as possible. The work specifically aims to build such a system where all components are learned jointly from data.

2.  **Is the approach well motivated, including being well-placed in the literature?**
    Yes, the approach is extremely well-motivated. The authors correctly identify the main limitation of traditional transform coding schemes like JPEG and JPEG 2000, where the transform, quantizer, and entropy code are designed and optimized in separate stages. The proposed end-to-end optimization is a powerful and natural alternative. The architecture, particularly the use of GDN transforms, is well-grounded in prior work on modeling natural image statistics and biological vision. Furthermore, the paper skillfully connects the proposed method to the literature on generative models, specifically variational autoencoders, clarifying both the similarities and the crucial differences needed to build an effective compression system that can operate across the entire rate-distortion curve.

3.  **Does the paper support the claims?**
    Yes, the claims are thoroughly supported by rigorous experimentation.
    *   The authors compare their method against strong and relevant baselines, including JPEG 2000, which is a much higher bar than comparing only to JPEG.
    *   A critical strength of the evaluation is the use of a complete entropy coder (CABAC) to report actual bit rates, rather than relying on entropy estimates, which can be misleading. This confirms the practical viability of the system.
    *   The paper provides both quantitative metrics (PSNR, MS-SSIM) and compelling visual examples. The visual results are striking, clearly demonstrating that the learned method avoids the blocking and ringing artifacts characteristic of linear transform codes. The significant gains in MS-SSIM provide objective backing for these qualitative observations.
    *   The authors also empirically validate their continuous relaxation of the loss function, showing it serves as a reliable proxy for the true discrete rate and distortion during optimization.

### Additional Feedback

The following points are intended as constructive suggestions to further strengthen this excellent work and are not part of my decision assessment.

*   It would be beneficial to place the results in the context of the absolute state-of-the-art in classical compression. While the comparison to JPEG 2000 is very strong, a brief mention or citation of more modern engineered standards like H.265/HEVC-Intra or VVC-Intra would help readers understand where this learned approach stands relative to the broader field. A quantitative comparison is not required, but acknowledging these methods would provide valuable context.
*   Your discussion of related neural network-based compression methods is very helpful. To further clarify your contribution, could you elaborate on which aspects of your model you believe are most critical for its success? For example, how crucial is the GDN nonlinearity compared to, say, standard ReLUs, in achieving this level of performance? The brief discussion in Section 5 is interesting, and any further insights would be appreciated.

### Questions for the Authors

*   The paper demonstrates that optimizing for MSE can lead to dramatic improvements in a perceptual metric like MS-SSIM. Have you experimented with directly optimizing for an MS-SSIM-based loss? Based on your previous work, I would expect this to yield even better visual results, and it would be interesting to know if this comes at a significant cost to PSNR.
