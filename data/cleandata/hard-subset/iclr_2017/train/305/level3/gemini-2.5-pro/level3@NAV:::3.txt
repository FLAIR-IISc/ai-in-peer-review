This paper presents a novel framework for end-to-end optimized image compression. The authors propose a system composed of a nonlinear encoder and decoder trained jointly to minimize a rate-distortion objective. The primary contribution is a clever solution to the problem of optimizing through the non-differentiable quantization step. By introducing a continuous proxy—replacing quantization with additive uniform noise during training—they enable the use of stochastic gradient descent to optimize the entire model. This approach allows the system to effectively learn transformations that outperform established codecs like JPEG 2000, particularly in terms of perceptual quality.

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The key reasons are twofold: 1) The technical contribution of a differentiable approximation for the rate-distortion loss is both novel and impactful, providing a viable path for end-to-end optimization in this domain. 2) The empirical results are excellent, demonstrating not only superior quantitative performance on metrics like MS-SSIM but also a remarkable improvement in the visual quality of compressed images.

The paper’s core strength lies in its effective training methodology. The continuous relaxation of the loss function is a principled approach that elegantly sidesteps the zero-gradient problem of quantization. As the authors convincingly argued in their response to my pre-review question, explicitly modeling and minimizing the rate term is more effective than hoping a complex network implicitly learns an efficient code. This method leads to a system that produces compressed images with impressively natural-looking artifacts, replacing the typical blocking and ringing of linear methods with smoother, more plausible simplifications. The results, validated on standard datasets, are compelling and clearly support the authors' claims.

For future improvement, the paper could be strengthened by providing a more direct ablation study on the choice of the Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) nonlinearity. While the discussion alludes to its efficiency advantages over simpler activations like ReLU, a quantitative comparison would offer more concrete evidence. Furthermore, a brief discussion on potential avenues for creating a single, rate-adaptive model—instead of one model per rate point—would be a valuable addition.

I have two questions for the authors to help clarify my understanding:
1. The current framework requires training a separate model for each λ value. Have you considered methods for training a single model that can operate across a continuous range of rate-distortion trade-offs, perhaps by conditioning the network on λ?
2. Could you elaborate further on the performance gap between the GDN-based architecture and one using more standard nonlinearities? Specifically, how much does network capacity need to increase with simpler activations to achieve comparable results?
