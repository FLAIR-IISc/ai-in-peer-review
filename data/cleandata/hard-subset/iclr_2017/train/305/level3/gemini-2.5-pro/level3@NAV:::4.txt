This paper presents a compelling end-to-end learning framework for lossy image compression. The proposed model consists of a nonlinear analysis transform, a quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform, which are jointly optimized to minimize a rate-distortion objective. The core of the contribution lies in a clever continuous relaxation of the loss function, which replaces the non-differentiable quantization step with additive uniform noise during training. This enables the entire system to be trained effectively using stochastic gradient descent. The experimental results demonstrate that this approach not only outperforms standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000 in rate-distortion performance but also produces compressed images with substantially better visual quality.

I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance. My decision is based on two key reasons: 1) the paper tackles a fundamental problem with a novel, well-motivated, and principled deep learning solution, and 2) the empirical results are thorough, convincing, and represent a significant advance over established and widely used compression standards.

The paper’s claims are well-supported and scientifically rigorous. The motivation for an end-to-end approach is clearly articulated by contrasting it with the separate optimization stages of traditional transform coding. The main technical hurdle—optimizing through a discrete quantization step—is overcome with an elegant and effective proxy loss function. The authors validate this approximation empirically, showing it is a reliable estimate for the true rate and distortion. The connection drawn to variational autoencoders is insightful, helping to place the work within the broader literature of generative models while also highlighting the key differences required for a practical compression system. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, using both quantitative metrics (PSNR, MS-SSIM) and qualitative examples on a standard test set to demonstrate superior performance and a remarkable reduction in visual artifacts.

The following points are intended to help improve the paper further and are not critical to my decision.
*   The paper notes that architectural choices were "somewhat ad-hoc." A brief ablation study or discussion on the sensitivity of performance to the number of channels or filter sizes would add further insight into the model's design.
*   Could you elaborate on the performance of models using simpler nonlinearities like ReLUs? Quantifying how many more parameters or stages are needed to achieve similar performance would strengthen the case for the efficiency of the proposed GDN/IGDN transforms.
*   The entropy coder uses a simple raster-scan context. Have you considered more sophisticated spatial context models in the latent space to potentially improve the rate, and if so, what were the results?
