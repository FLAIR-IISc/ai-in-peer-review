This paper presents an end-to-end framework for optimizing a lossy image compression system based on deep nonlinear transforms. The proposed model consists of three components: a nonlinear analysis transform (encoder), a uniform quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform (decoder). The transforms are built from a cascade of convolutional layers and a generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearity, a bio-inspired operation. To enable end-to-end training with stochastic gradient descent despite the non-differentiable nature of quantization, the authors introduce a continuous relaxation by replacing the quantizer with additive uniform noise. The paper provides an insightful connection between this relaxed rate-distortion objective and the loss function of a variational autoencoder (VAE). The entire system, including an adaptive arithmetic entropy coder, is trained to minimize a weighted sum of rate (actual bit rate) and distortion (MSE). The authors demonstrate that their method achieves superior rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, and more importantly, produces compressed images with substantially better perceptual quality, a claim supported by both visual examples and the MS-SSIM metric.

Decision: **Accept**

The primary reasons for this decision are:
1.  The paper presents a complete, practical, and well-engineered learned compression system that demonstrates state-of-the-art results, with particularly striking improvements in visual quality over widely-used standards like JPEG 2000.
2.  The methodology is sound and well-motivated. The continuous relaxation of the quantizer is an elegant solution to the optimization challenge, and the authors provide a valuable theoretical connection between their rate-distortion framework and variational autoencoders, helping to unify concepts from classical information theory and modern generative modeling.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The strength of this work lies in its comprehensive and rigorous approach to a classical problem. While building upon the authors' prior work, this paper represents a significant step forward by scaling the transforms to deeper, multi-stage architectures and, crucially, by integrating a practical entropy coding backend (CABAC). This allows for the reporting of actual bit rates rather than entropy estimates, making the system a complete codec and enabling fair, direct comparisons with established standards.

The empirical results are the most compelling argument for acceptance. The visual quality of the compressed images, as shown in Figures 5 and 6, is remarkable. The proposed method avoids the characteristic blocking and ringing artifacts of linear transform codes, instead producing images where detail is gracefully softened, preserving smooth contours and a natural appearance even at very low bit rates. This subjective improvement is quantitatively validated by the MS-SSIM results (Figure 7), which show a consistent and substantial advantage for the proposed method across all tested bit rates. The fact that these perceptual gains are achieved while optimizing for a simple MSE metric speaks to the power of the learned nonlinear representations.

The theoretical discussion in Section 3.1, which links the rate-distortion objective to the evidence lower bound (ELBO) in variational inference, is a strong contribution. It clearly explains the parallels and, more importantly, the key distinctions between training a VAE for generation and optimizing a transform code for compression (e.g., the role of the trade-off parameter λ). This connection provides a solid conceptual foundation for the work and will be valuable for researchers working at the intersection of these fields.

**Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper:**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback for strengthening the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

1.  **MSE vs. Perceptual Metrics:** The authors' previous work optimized a similar model for a perceptual metric, and they are recognized experts in this area. The switch to optimizing for MSE in this paper is therefore surprising, especially given that the primary advantage of the method appears to be perceptual. The justification—to allow a more interpretable comparison and to handle color images—is understandable, but it feels like a potential step backward. It would strengthen the paper to either include results from a model optimized for MS-SSIM or to provide a more detailed discussion on the challenges or trade-offs involved in using perceptual losses with this deeper architecture.

2.  **Ablation of Contributions:** The paper introduces several improvements over prior work, most notably the deeper encoder/decoder architecture and the context-adaptive entropy coder. However, the relative contribution of each component is unclear. An ablation study would be very insightful. For example, reporting the rate-distortion performance using the same trained transforms but with a simple, non-adaptive entropy code (based directly on the learned marginal probability models) would precisely quantify the gains from the context-adaptive coding scheme.

3.  **VAE Equivalence for Non-Euclidean Metrics:** There appears to be a subtle technical issue in the claim of VAE equivalence for general metrics in Section 3.1. In Equation 16, if the metric `d` is not Euclidean, the normalization constant `Z(λ)` will likely depend on the parameters of the generative distribution, specifically on the mean `gs(ỹ;θ)`. This would mean the log-likelihood `log px|ỹ` is not simply proportional to the distortion term `-λd(...)` but would include a `log Z(λ, gs(ỹ;θ))` term, complicating the equivalence. Clarifying this point would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

**Questions for the Authors:**

To help clarify my understanding and further strengthen the paper, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  Could you elaborate on the decision to optimize for MSE rather than a perceptual metric like MS-SSIM? Were there specific technical challenges in applying a perceptual loss to this deeper, end-to-end model for color images, or did you find that the powerful nonlinear model was sufficient to achieve excellent perceptual quality even when guided by a simple MSE loss?

2.  What is the specific performance gain (in terms of bits per pixel) attributable to the context-adaptive nature of your CABAC implementation? For instance, how do the results in Figure 4 or 7 change if you use a non-adaptive arithmetic coder whose probabilities are fixed from the trained marginal distributions `pỹi`?

3.  Regarding the VAE connection for non-Euclidean metrics (Equation 16), does the normalization constant `Z(λ)` depend on the synthesis network's output `gs(ỹ;θ)`? If so, how does this affect the equivalence between minimizing the rate-distortion loss and minimizing the KL divergence?
