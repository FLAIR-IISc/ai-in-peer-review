**Summary**

This paper presents an end-to-end trainable framework for lossy image compression using a deep neural network architecture. The model consists of a nonlinear analysis transform, a uniform quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform, which are jointly optimized to minimize a weighted rate-distortion loss. A key contribution is the introduction of a continuous and differentiable proxy for the quantization step, which enables optimization via stochastic gradient descent. The authors demonstrate that their method outperforms standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000, particularly at low bit rates. Most impressively, the resulting compressed images exhibit a substantial improvement in perceptual quality, replacing typical blocking and ringing artifacts with more natural-looking simplifications. This claim is well-supported by both visual examples and quantitative evaluation using the MS-SSIM metric.

**Decision: Accept**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. My decision is based on two key reasons: 1) The work provides a principled and elegant integration of classical rate-distortion theory into a modern, end-to-end deep learning framework. 2) The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating not just an incremental improvement in standard metrics but a significant leap in the perceptual quality of compressed images.

**Supporting Arguments**

This is the most convincing paper on neural network-based image compression that I have read. The paper is exceptionally well-written, and the theoretical motivation is clear and sound. The authors successfully bridge the gap between the discrete nature of compression and the continuous domain required for gradient-based optimization. Comparing against JPEG 2000, rather than just JPEG, provides a much stronger and more relevant baseline. The visual results are striking and effectively showcase the limitations of linear transform codes. I believe this work will have a significant impact and will inspire much future research in this area.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following suggestions are intended to help strengthen the paper further:
*   While results are shown for the Kodak dataset, formally including a table with PSNR and MS-SSIM values for widely-used standard images (e.g., Lena, Barbara, Baboon) would facilitate direct comparison with a broader range of literature.
*   The paper could be strengthened by referencing and positioning itself against state-of-the-art classical compression methods that outperform JPEG 2000, such as BPG (based on HEVC).
*   The discussion of related neural network-based methods is good, but a more direct and explicit comparison, perhaps quantitative, would better highlight the specific advantages of the proposed approach.

**Questions for Authors**
*   Could you comment on the computational complexity of the encoder and decoder relative to a software implementation of JPEG 2000?
*   Training a separate model for each rate-distortion tradeoff (λ) is a limitation. Have you considered approaches for training a single model that can operate across a range of bit rates?
