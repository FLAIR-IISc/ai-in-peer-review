### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a compelling and comprehensive framework for end-to-end optimized image compression using neural networks. The proposed model consists of a nonlinear analysis transform, a uniform quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform. The transforms are architecturally novel, composed of cascaded convolutional filters and a biologically-inspired Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) nonlinearity. The central contribution is the elegant integration of classical rate-distortion theory into a deep learning objective function, `R + λD`. To overcome the non-differentiability of quantization, the authors introduce a clever continuous relaxation by replacing quantization with additive uniform noise during training, which allows for stable optimization via stochastic gradient descent. The paper thoughtfully connects this relaxed objective to the framework of Variational Autoencoders, placing the work in a broader context of generative modeling. Crucially, the authors demonstrate a complete and practical compression system by implementing an entropy coder (CABAC) and reporting actual bit rates, a step often omitted in related work. The empirical results are strong, showing superior performance over JPEG and JPEG 2000, not only in terms of PSNR at lower bit rates but, more impressively, in terms of perceptual quality as measured by MS-SSIM and visual inspection across all rates.

### Decision and Key Reasons

My recommendation is to **Accept** this paper. This is one of the most convincing papers on learning-based image compression I have read.

1.  **Principled and Novel Framework:** The paper successfully marries classical rate-distortion optimization with modern deep learning techniques. The end-to-end optimization of a rate-distortion objective is well-motivated and cleanly executed. The use of a continuous proxy for quantization is an elegant solution to a fundamental problem in this domain.
2.  **Strong and Well-Supported Empirical Results:** The method is thoroughly evaluated against standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The demonstrated improvements in both objective metrics (PSNR, MS-SSIM) and, particularly, visual quality are significant. The artifacts produced by this method are far more natural than the blocking and ringing common to transform coding, which represents a substantial step forward for practical image compression.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's strength lies in its rigor and completeness. The problem is clearly defined as minimizing a weighted sum of rate and distortion. The authors' choice of architecture, particularly the GDN/IGDN nonlinearities, is well-justified by prior work on density modeling of natural images, suggesting that these transforms are particularly well-suited for creating compact, decorrelated representations.

The technical contribution of using additive uniform noise as a differentiable proxy for quantization is a key enabler for the entire framework. The authors provide an empirical validation (Figure 4) showing this is a reasonable approximation, which strengthens the credibility of the training procedure. Furthermore, by implementing a full entropy coding pipeline with CABAC and reporting actual compressed file sizes, the authors move beyond theoretical entropy estimates to demonstrate a practical, working codec. This makes the comparisons to established standards like JPEG 2000 fair and meaningful.

The results are impressive. The qualitative examples in Figures 5 and 6 clearly show a different and more perceptually pleasing kind of degradation at low bit rates. Instead of breaking down into basis functions, the images appear to simplify while retaining structural integrity, which is a highly desirable property. This visual superiority is backed quantitatively by the strong MS-SSIM results across all tested images and bit rates (Figure 7). The fact that this perceptual quality is achieved while optimizing for MSE is remarkable and speaks to the power of the learned nonlinear representations. The paper is very well-written, clear, and is likely to have a strong impact on the field.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper further and are not critical for my decision to accept.

1.  **Comparison to State-of-the-Art Classical Methods:** While the comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 is necessary and well-executed, JPEG 2000 is no longer the state-of-the-art in classical image compression. To firmly establish the performance of this method, a comparison against codecs based on modern video coding standards, such as BPG (which uses the intra-frame coding techniques of HEVC/H.265), would be highly valuable. This would provide a much stronger baseline and a clearer picture of where learning-based methods stand.
2.  **Inclusion of Standard Test Images:** The compression community has historically relied on a few standard images (e.g., "Lena," "Barbara," "Mandrill/Baboon") for evaluating algorithms. While the paper notes these were tested, including the rate-distortion curves for these specific images (perhaps in the appendix or on the supplementary website) would allow for more direct comparison with a vast body of prior work.
3.  **Positioning Relative to Other Learning-Based Methods:** The paper does a good job discussing related works by Gregor et al. (2016) and Toderici et al. (2016). This could be strengthened by providing a more explicit, side-by-side comparison. For instance, a small table summarizing the key differences in objectives (generative vs. rate-distortion), handling of rate (entropy vs. binary codes), and architecture would help readers quickly grasp the unique contributions of this work relative to its immediate deep learning predecessors.

### Questions for Authors

1.  Have you considered comparing your method against BPG (HEVC intra-frame)? Given BPG's strong performance, it would be insightful to see how your learned features compare against the highly-engineered prediction and transform modules in modern codecs, especially concerning perceptual quality.
2.  The paper suggests that GDN/IGDN transforms are more efficient than architectures with simpler nonlinearities like ReLUs. Could you provide a bit more quantitative detail on this? For example, roughly how many more parameters or stages would a ReLU-based network need to achieve comparable rate-distortion performance? This would help quantify the benefit of the specific GDN architecture.
3.  Your model is trained separately for each rate-distortion trade-off parameter λ. This is in contrast to the work of Toderici et al. (2016), which aims for a single model for all rates. Could you comment on the practical implications of this choice (e.g., storage of multiple models) and whether you see a path towards a single, rate-adaptive model within your framework?
