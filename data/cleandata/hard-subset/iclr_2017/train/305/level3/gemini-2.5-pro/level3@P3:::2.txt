### Summary

This paper presents an end-to-end optimized model for lossy image compression. The method consists of a deep, non-linear analysis transform (encoder), a uniform quantizer, and a non-linear synthesis transform (decoder). The transforms are built from cascades of convolutions and a biologically-inspired Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) non-linearity. The entire system is trained by minimizing a rate-distortion loss function, where a continuous relaxation (additive uniform noise) is used to overcome the non-differentiability of the quantizer, enabling stochastic gradient descent. The authors draw a formal connection between this rate-distortion objective and the loss function of a Variational Autoencoder (VAE). The framework is trained for different points on the rate-distortion curve and incorporates an adaptive arithmetic entropy coder to produce a final bitstream. The resulting method demonstrates significant performance gains over JPEG 2000 in terms of PSNR and, most notably, in perceptual quality as measured by MS-SSIM and visual inspection.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a complete, well-engineered, and highly effective learned image compression system. The empirical results are very strong, showing substantial improvements over standard codecs like JPEG 2000, particularly in terms of visual quality. The visual artifacts are far more natural and less distracting than the blocking and ringing common to transform coding methods.
2.  The paper is exceptionally well-written and provides a clear, principled framework for end-to-end rate-distortion optimization. The connection drawn to VAEs, while perhaps imperfect, provides valuable context and links the classical problem of compression to modern generative modeling.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this work lies in its impressive empirical results. The method consistently outperforms JPEG 2000, which is a strong baseline, across various bit rates on the standard Kodak dataset. More importantly, the qualitative results shown in Figures 5 and 6 are compelling; the compressed images exhibit a graceful degradation, preserving smooth contours and sharp edges while avoiding the typical blocky or ringing artifacts. This subjective improvement is quantitatively supported by a large margin in MS-SSIM (Figure 7), confirming that the learned representation captures perceptually relevant image structures.

The paper successfully extends the core rate-distortion optimization idea from the authors' prior work (Ballé et al., 2016) into a more complete and powerful system. The use of a deeper, multi-stage architecture and the integration of a practical, adaptive entropy coder (CABAC-based) are significant steps that move the concept from a research idea to a viable compression pipeline. This demonstrates feasibility and allows for a fair comparison based on actual compressed bit rates rather than entropy estimates, which can be unreliable.

The methodology is well-motivated and clearly explained. The continuous relaxation of the quantizer using additive noise is an elegant solution for end-to-end training. The detailed explanation of the relationship to VAEs is insightful, helping to position this work within the broader machine learning landscape and highlighting the key difference: compression requires optimizing for a trade-off (controlled by λ) rather than simply maximizing the model evidence (the λ → ∞ regime).

While the core idea of optimizing a transform for rate-distortion is not entirely new (building on the authors' previous publication), the extensions presented here—particularly the deeper architecture and the inclusion of a real entropy coding scheme—are substantial enough to warrant publication. However, the paper could be strengthened by addressing a few key points. The switch from a perceptual metric in prior work to MSE here feels like a step backward, especially given the authors' expertise and the final emphasis on visual quality. An ablation study is also missing, making it difficult to disentangle the performance gains from the deeper architecture versus the adaptive entropy coding.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

**Technical Correction:**
-   I believe the claimed equivalence to a VAE for non-Euclidean metrics is incorrect. In Equation 16, the normalization constant Z(λ) must depend on the output of the synthesis network, i.e., Z(λ, gs(ỹ;θ)), to ensure px|ỹ is a valid probability density over x. This means the log partition function `log Z` cannot be dropped as a constant during optimization with respect to the decoder parameters θ, breaking the direct correspondence between the negative log-likelihood and the distortion term `d(...)`. Could the authors clarify this point?

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
-   **Ablation Study:** The contribution of the adaptive entropy model is unclear. How much of the performance gain over a non-adaptive model is due to adaptation? An ablation experiment comparing the proposed adaptive CABAC scheme to a simpler, non-adaptive arithmetic coder using the learned marginals would be very informative. This would help isolate the gains from the learned transforms versus the entropy coding stage.
-   **Justification for MSE:** Given the authors' background and prior work on perceptual metrics, the decision to optimize for MSE is surprising, even if it allows for easier comparison. The paper argues for the superior visual quality of the results, which suggests a perceptual metric would be a more natural optimization target. Could you elaborate on the rationale for this choice and perhaps comment on the challenges of optimizing a perceptual metric for color images?
-   **Details on Entropy Coder:** The appendix provides some details on the entropy coder, but its effect could be better highlighted. For instance, showing the initial entropy estimate vs. the final bit rate achieved by the adaptive coder for a few examples would better illustrate its utility in correcting model error and exploiting spatial dependencies. Figure 10 is helpful but could be expanded upon in the main text.

I would be willing to raise my score if the authors can provide a convincing response to the technical point about the VAE equivalence and offer more insight (e.g., preliminary results from an ablation study) into the contribution of the adaptive entropy coder.
