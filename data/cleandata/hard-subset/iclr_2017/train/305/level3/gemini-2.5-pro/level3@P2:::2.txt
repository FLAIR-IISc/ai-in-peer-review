### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents an end-to-end framework for optimizing a lossy image compression system. The proposed model consists of a nonlinear analysis transform (encoder), a uniform scalar quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform (decoder). The encoder and decoder are built from a cascade of convolutional layers and a biologically-inspired nonlinear activation function, Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The entire system is trained by minimizing a rate-distortion objective function using stochastic gradient descent. A key technical contribution is a continuous relaxation of the non-differentiable quantization step, which is replaced by additive uniform noise during training, enabling gradient-based optimization. The authors draw an insightful connection between this relaxed objective and the loss function of a Variational Autoencoder (VAE). Empirically, the paper demonstrates that the resulting codec produces images that are visually superior to JPEG and JPEG 2000 at similar bit rates, a claim supported by significant gains in the MS-SSIM metric. The system reports actual bit rates from an implemented adaptive entropy coder, making it a complete and practical compression pipeline.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Weak Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a complete, end-to-end learned compression model that achieves compelling results, with clear qualitative and quantitative (MS-SSIM) improvements over strong baselines like JPEG 2000. The inclusion of a practical entropy coder and reporting of actual bit rates strengthens the contribution.
2.  However, the core optimization framework is an extension of the authors' prior work, and the paper lacks a crucial ablation study to isolate the performance gains of the new components (deeper architecture vs. adaptive entropy coding). This makes it difficult to fully assess the paper's novel contributions.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this work lies in its impressive empirical results. The authors demonstrate that an end-to-end optimized nonlinear transform can produce compressed images that are far more visually pleasing than those from traditional linear transform codecs. As shown in the figures, the proposed method avoids common artifacts like blocking and ringing, instead producing reconstructions that appear to gracefully simplify image content, preserving smooth contours and sharp edges. This is a significant achievement, especially given that the model was optimized for mean squared error (MSE), not a perceptual metric. The connection drawn between rate-distortion optimization and VAEs is also elegant and provides a valuable bridge between the information theory and generative modeling communities.

The main weaknesses relate to novelty and experimental analysis. The core concept of optimizing a rate-distortion objective with a continuous proxy for quantization was introduced in the authors' previous publication (Ballé et al., 2016). The main novelties in this paper appear to be the extension to a deeper, multi-stage architecture and the inclusion of a context-adaptive arithmetic coder. While both are valuable additions for building a high-performance system, their individual contributions are not disentangled. An ablation study comparing the performance of the deep architecture with a non-adaptive vs. adaptive entropy code is necessary to understand the source of the reported gains. Without this, it is unclear how much improvement comes from the more powerful transform versus more sophisticated entropy coding.

Additionally, the switch from a perceptual distortion metric in the prior work to MSE here feels like a step backward, particularly as the authors have a strong background in perceptual modeling and the results are ultimately evaluated with MS-SSIM. While using MSE allows for a more direct comparison with standard codecs, it weakens the argument for the superiority of the end-to-end learning paradigm, which should excel at optimizing for complex, non-standard objectives.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

This is a very well-written and clear paper. The following points are intended to help strengthen it further and are not critical to the decision.

*   The discussion on the VAE connection is very insightful. However, there seems to be a potential issue in Equation (16). For a general, non-Euclidean but translation-invariant metric `d`, the normalization constant `Z` would likely depend on the mean of the distribution, `gs(ỹ;θ)`, not just `λ`. If `Z` depends on the parameters `θ`, this would introduce an additional term in the gradient and complicate the equivalence to the VAE objective. Clarifying this point would strengthen the theoretical contribution.
*   The paper's discussion mentions preliminary results comparing GDN to other nonlinearities like ReLUs. While noted as provisional, even a brief quantitative summary of this comparison (e.g., in the appendix) would be very valuable, as it would help justify the use of the more complex GDN/IGDN architecture.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

Your response to these questions will help me better assess the paper's contributions.

1.  Given your prior work and the strong MS-SSIM results, why did you choose to optimize for MSE rather than a perceptual metric? Could you comment on the potential performance if the system were optimized directly for MS-SSIM?
2.  Could you please provide an ablation study that quantifies the contribution of the adaptive entropy coder? For instance, what is the rate-distortion performance of your system using the same trained transforms but with a simpler, non-adaptive arithmetic coder based on the marginal probabilities learned during training?
3.  Could you provide more implementation details about the context-adaptive binary arithmetic coder (CABAC)? How are the contexts defined for adapting the probability models? Figure 10 shows a small gain from adaptation; is this representative across different bit rates and images?
4.  Regarding the VAE formulation in Section 3.1: can you confirm if the normalization constant `Z(λ)` in Equation (16) is indeed independent of `gs(ỹ;θ)` for any translation-invariant metric `d`? If not, how does this affect the direct correspondence to the VAE objective?
