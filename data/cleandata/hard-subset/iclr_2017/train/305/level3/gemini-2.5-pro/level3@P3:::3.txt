### Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

**1. Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel, end-to-end optimized framework for lossy image compression. The system is composed of a nonlinear analysis transform, a uniform scalar quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform, which are jointly trained using stochastic gradient descent to minimize a rate-distortion objective. The transforms themselves are deep, using a cascade of convolutions and a biologically-inspired generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearity.

A key contribution is the method for overcoming the non-differentiability of the quantizer and the rate term. The authors introduce a continuous and differentiable proxy for the rate-distortion loss by replacing the quantization step with additive uniform noise during training. This allows the differential entropy of the continuous representation to approximate the discrete entropy of the quantized values, enabling effective gradient-based optimization. The authors thoughtfully connect their framework to variational autoencoders (VAEs), clarifying both the similarities and the crucial differences, particularly in the objective of optimizing for a rate-distortion trade-off rather than simply maximizing likelihood.

Empirically, the authors demonstrate that their system achieves superior rate-distortion performance compared to established standards like JPEG and JPEG 2000, as measured by both PSNR and, more significantly, MS-SSIM. The resulting compressed images exhibit a dramatic improvement in visual quality, avoiding the typical blocking and ringing artifacts of linear transform codecs and instead producing more natural-looking degradations.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper introduces a complete and highly effective end-to-end learned image compression system that convincingly outperforms strong, widely-used baselines like JPEG 2000 on standard benchmarks. The visual quality of the results is particularly impressive.
2.  The core technical contribution—a differentiable proxy for the rate term in the rate-distortion objective—is an elegant and powerful idea. This method directly addresses a fundamental challenge in training quantized systems and is likely to have a significant impact beyond image compression on other tasks requiring optimization of discrete representations.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The primary strength of this work is its successful formulation and execution of an end-to-end optimization strategy for a historically challenging problem. Traditional compression pipelines optimize their components (transform, quantizer, entropy coder) separately, which is inherently suboptimal. By creating a fully differentiable pipeline, the authors allow the analysis and synthesis transforms to be jointly optimized for the final objective, leading to state-of-the-art results.

The differentiable relaxation of the rate-distortion loss is the crucial innovation that enables this. Replacing the hard rounding operation with additive uniform noise is a simple yet brilliant move. It provides a continuous loss surface for gradient descent while remaining a principled approximation of both the quantization error (distortion) and the entropy of the quantized symbols (rate). The empirical validation in Figure 4, showing a close correspondence between the relaxed loss and the true discrete metrics, strongly supports the validity of this approach.

The authors also provide a thoughtful response to the (pre-review) question of why an explicit probability model for the latent codes is necessary. In principle, a sufficiently complex network could be trained to produce codes within a fixed range that implicitly maximize entropy (i.e., approach a uniform distribution), which would simplify the entropy coding stage. However, I find the authors' argument convincing: explicitly modeling the marginal distributions of the latent codes allows the network to focus on finding the most effective representation for minimizing distortion. Forcing the codes into a specific, simple distribution would impose a strong constraint, likely requiring a more complex network architecture or a separate, sophisticated variable-rate encoder to achieve the same level of compression efficiency. The chosen approach is more direct and flexible.

Finally, the results are excellent. The quantitative gains over JPEG 2000 are solid, but the qualitative improvements are remarkable. The model learns to discard information in a perceptually pleasing way, smoothing textures while preserving sharp contours, which aligns well with human visual perception. This is reflected in the very strong MS-SSIM results and suggests the learned nonlinear transforms are capturing important structures in natural images that linear transforms cannot.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended as constructive suggestions and do not detract from my positive assessment of the paper.

*   **Complexity Analysis:** The paper mentions that encoding and decoding are "efficient" but provides no quantitative analysis of computational complexity (e.g., MAC operations per pixel) or runtime comparisons against JPEG/JPEG 2000. For a compression algorithm, this is a critical aspect for practical adoption. Including such a comparison would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Architectural Exploration:** The authors note that the network architecture is "somewhat ad-hoc." While the chosen architecture clearly works well, the paper could be improved by including an ablation study. For example, how critical is the use of GDN versus more standard nonlinearities like ReLU? The discussion touches on this, but empirical results would be more definitive. Similarly, exploring the impact of network depth or the number of channels would provide valuable insights.
*   **Entropy Model:** The current entropy coder adapts marginal probabilities within each channel but does not explicitly model spatial dependencies between coefficients. Modern codecs gain significantly from more sophisticated context models. While the current simple model is sufficient to demonstrate the method's effectiveness, exploring a learned, spatially-aware entropy model as part of the end-to-end framework could unlock further performance gains.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

*   In your framework, you explicitly model the marginal distributions `p(y_i)` to estimate the rate. An alternative approach might be to add a regularization term to the loss that encourages the distribution of the latent codes `y` to match a simple, fixed prior (e.g., uniform or factorized Gaussian), for which entropy coding is trivial. Could you elaborate on the potential trade-offs between your approach of learning the distribution and an approach based on matching a fixed prior?
*   The continuous relaxation of the quantizer is a powerful idea. Have you considered its applicability to other domains where end-to-end training is hindered by discrete bottlenecks, such as training discrete latent variable models or quantization-aware training for model compression?
