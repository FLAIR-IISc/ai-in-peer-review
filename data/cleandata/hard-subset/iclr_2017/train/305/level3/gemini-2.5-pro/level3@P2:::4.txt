### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel end-to-end framework for lossy image compression that is optimized directly for rate-distortion performance. The proposed model consists of a nonlinear analysis transform, a uniform scalar quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform. The transforms are implemented as a three-stage convolutional network, innovatively using Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) as the nonlinearity, which is inspired by biological visual systems.

A key contribution is the method for overcoming the non-differentiable nature of quantization in a gradient-based optimization framework. The authors introduce an elegant continuous relaxation by replacing the hard quantization operation with additive uniform noise during training. This allows the entire system to be optimized jointly using stochastic gradient descent. The paper thoughtfully discusses the relationship between this relaxed rate-distortion objective and the objective functions used in Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), while clearly articulating the fundamental differences in their goals.

The authors demonstrate the efficacy of their approach through extensive experiments. They train separate models for different points on the rate-distortion curve and evaluate them on standard test datasets. The method is shown to outperform JPEG and JPEG 2000 on standard metrics like PSNR, and more substantially on the perceptual metric MS-SSIM. Perhaps most impressively, the resulting compressed images exhibit a dramatic improvement in visual quality, largely free of the blocking and ringing artifacts that plague traditional linear transform codecs.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that presents a complete, novel, and highly effective solution to the classical problem of image compression. The core reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Novel and Principled Framework:** The paper successfully formulates image compression as an end-to-end optimization problem and provides an elegant solution to the key technical challenge of training through a quantizer. The use of a continuous proxy for the rate-distortion loss is a clever and effective technique that enables the application of modern deep learning methods to this domain.
2.  **Compelling Empirical Results:** The proposed method demonstrates state-of-the-art performance, outperforming widely-used standards like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The quantitative improvements in PSNR are solid, but the qualitative results are truly remarkable. The compressed images are visually much more pleasing and natural, a claim strongly supported by superior MS-SSIM scores across all bit rates.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's primary strength lies in its thoughtful and rigorous approach. By replacing quantization with additive noise, the authors create a differentiable loss function that effectively approximates both the rate (via differential entropy) and the distortion. The validation of this approximation in Figure 4 adds confidence in the training methodology. Unlike some other learning-based compression works, this paper goes the crucial extra step of implementing a full entropy code (CABAC-based) and reporting actual bit rates, ensuring a fair and meaningful comparison with existing standards.

The architectural choices are well-motivated. The use of GDN connects this work to principles from computational neuroscience and prior work by the authors on density modeling, suggesting that these transforms are particularly well-suited for representing natural image statistics. The discussion situating the work relative to VAEs is insightful, clarifying that while the objectives are mathematically similar, the goal of compression (optimizing the R-D trade-off for a given λ) is distinct from that of generative modeling (where λ is effectively maximized).

The experimental results are thorough and convincing. The visual examples provided clearly illustrate the method's main advantage: it learns to discard information in a perceptually graceful way, simplifying textures and contours rather than introducing visually disturbing artifacts. This subjective improvement is successfully captured by the MS-SSIM metric, providing strong objective support for the visual evidence. The fact that these superior perceptual results are achieved while optimizing for simple MSE is a powerful testament to the representation capabilities of the learned nonlinear transforms.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions for strengthening the paper further and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Computational Complexity:** The paper mentions that encoding and decoding are "efficient" but does not provide a quantitative analysis. Including a comparison of the computational complexity (e.g., in terms of multiply-accumulate operations or execution time on a specific platform) against JPEG 2000 would be very valuable for assessing the practical viability of the proposed method.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The architecture is described as "somewhat ad-hoc". An ablation study exploring the impact of the number of stages, the number of channels, or the specific choice of nonlinearity (e.g., a more direct comparison with ReLU-based networks of similar capacity) would provide deeper insight into why the proposed architecture is so effective and how sensitive performance is to these design choices.
*   **Averaged R-D Curves:** The appendix rightly points out the difficulties in fairly averaging rate-distortion curves across a dataset. However, a summary plot (perhaps using the Bjontegaard-delta metric, common in the video coding community) would still be a useful, albeit imperfect, way to summarize the overall performance gain on a dataset like Kodak.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  The current framework requires training a separate, specialized model for each rate-distortion trade-off point (λ). Have you considered or explored architectures that can be controlled by a single parameter at test time to produce variable bit rates from a single model? How do you expect the performance of such a unified model to compare with the collection of specialized models presented here?
2.  The paper shows that the continuous relaxation of the rate is a biased estimator of the discrete entropy, particularly at low bit rates. While the argument that this merely results in a re-parameterization of λ is plausible, could this systematic bias negatively impact the optimization process, especially in the low-rate regime where the visual quality benefits of your method are most pronounced?
3.  The inverse transform uses IGDN, which is described as a one-iteration approximation of the inverse of GDN. What is the trade-off between reconstruction quality and complexity if more iterations were used? Is it possible that learning a different, non-symmetric decoder architecture could yield further improvements?
