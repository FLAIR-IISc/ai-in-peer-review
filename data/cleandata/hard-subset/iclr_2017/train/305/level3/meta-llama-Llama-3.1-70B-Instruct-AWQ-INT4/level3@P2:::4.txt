This paper proposes an end-to-end learning method for image compression, leveraging a rate-distortion optimization framework with a clever relaxation to learn efficient compression from a natural image database. The authors demonstrate compelling experimental results, showcasing the effectiveness of their approach in achieving improved rate-distortion performance and visual quality compared to standard methods such as JPEG and JPEG 2000.

I recommend accepting this paper.

The paper tackles the specific question of image compression, a fundamental problem in engineering, by formulating it as a rate-distortion optimization problem. The authors' approach is well-motivated, drawing inspiration from the literature on transform coding and generative models. The use of a generalized divisive normalization (GDN) joint nonlinearity, inspired by models of biological neurons, is a novel and interesting choice.

The paper supports its claims through a thorough analysis of the proposed method, including a detailed description of the optimization framework, the architecture of the analysis and synthesis transforms, and the entropy coding scheme. The experimental results are comprehensive, covering a range of bit rates and image datasets, and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in achieving improved rate-distortion performance and visual quality.

One of the strengths of the paper is its thorough analysis of the relationship between the proposed method and existing approaches, including generative models and transform coding methods. The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of the differences between these approaches and their own method, highlighting the advantages of their approach.

The paper could be improved by providing more insight into the optimization process, including the choice of hyperparameters and the convergence properties of the algorithm. Additionally, the authors could provide more analysis of the computational complexity of their method, including the time and memory requirements for training and testing.

Some specific questions I would like the authors to address:

* Can the authors provide more details on the choice of hyperparameters, including the number of filters, the downsampling factors, and the number of stages in the analysis and synthesis transforms?
* How do the authors ensure the convergence of the optimization algorithm, and what are the typical convergence properties of the algorithm?
* Can the authors provide more analysis of the computational complexity of their method, including the time and memory requirements for training and testing?
* How do the authors plan to extend their method to other types of data, such as video or audio?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression. With some additional analysis and insight into the optimization process and computational complexity, the paper could be even stronger.
