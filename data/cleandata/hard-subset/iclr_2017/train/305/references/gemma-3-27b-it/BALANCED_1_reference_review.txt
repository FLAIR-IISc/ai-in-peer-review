This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on cascaded nonlinear transforms, specifically utilizing Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The core idea is to jointly optimize the analysis and synthesis transforms for a given rate-distortion trade-off, circumventing the traditionally separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy code. The authors cleverly address the gradient issues arising from quantization by employing a continuous relaxation using additive noise, and importantly, evaluate performance using actual bit rates rather than entropy estimates. The connection to variational autoencoders is also thoughtfully discussed, highlighting both similarities and key differences.

**Decision: Reject**

Despite the interesting ideas and solid experimental results demonstrating improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000, the paper falls short of the rigor and clarity expected for ICLR publication. The primary reason for rejection is a lack of sufficient detail regarding the experimental setup and the justification for specific architectural choices, hindering reproducibility and preventing a thorough assessment of the method’s generalization capability. Additionally, the extensive reliance on ablation studies and comparisons presented *only* in an online supplement is problematic.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Insufficient Experimental Detail:** While the authors mention a subset of ImageNet being used for training, details like the exact number of images, data augmentation techniques (beyond downsampling/cropping), and hardware specifications are missing. The justification for the network architecture (number of filters, layer configurations) is described as "somewhat ad-hoc," raising concerns about whether these choices were systematically explored or simply arrived at through trial and error. The hyperparameters for the Adam optimizer aren't detailed enough to allow for straightforward replication.

2. **Over-Reliance on Supplemental Material:** A substantial portion of the evaluation – detailed comparisons across images and bitrates, additional examples – is relegated to an online supplement. While supplementary material is acceptable, key results crucial for understanding the method’s effectiveness *should* be presented within the main body of the paper. This significantly limits the ability of reviewers to fully assess the contribution without extensive effort. The claims of perceptual improvements are difficult to judge without direct access to a significant number of visual examples.

3. **Limited Ablation and Generalization Analysis:** The authors mention preliminary experiments with different nonlinearities (ReLU, sigmoid) but offer only a cursory discussion of the results. A more comprehensive ablation study investigating the impact of different architectural choices (e.g., number of stages, filter sizes, GDN parameters) would strengthen the paper. It is also unclear how well the method generalizes to image datasets significantly different from ImageNet. The Kodak dataset is used for evaluation, but deeper analysis of performance across various image types (natural images, synthetic images, etc.) is lacking.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Reproducibility:** The authors should prioritize providing sufficient detail to enable independent replication of their results. This includes detailed descriptions of the training process, architecture, hyperparameters, and data preprocessing steps.  Consider releasing the code alongside the paper.
* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct a more thorough ablation study to assess the impact of different architectural choices on performance. This would help justify the selected configuration and provide insights into the method’s sensitivity to various parameters.
* **Perceptual Evaluation:** Include representative visual examples of compressed images within the main paper, alongside quantitative results.
* **Theoretical Discussion:** While the connection to variational autoencoders is interesting, a more rigorous discussion of the theoretical underpinnings of the method and its relationship to rate-distortion theory would be beneficial. Explain why GDN is hypothesized to be particularly effective for compression.
* **Complexity Analysis:** Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method, both during training and inference, and compare it to existing compression algorithms.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources used for training (e.g., GPU type, training time)?
* What specific criteria were used to select the network architecture and hyperparameters? Were any systematic search techniques employed?
* Beyond the preliminary results mentioned, what are the performance differences observed when using ReLU or sigmoid nonlinearities compared to GDN?
*  How sensitive is the method to the choice of the initial probability models for the entropy code?
* What are the limitations of the proposed method? Are there specific types of images or scenarios where it is likely to perform poorly?
