This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework leveraging cascaded nonlinear transformations – specifically, Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) – and optimized for MSE rate-distortion trade-offs. The key contribution lies in jointly optimizing the analysis and synthesis transforms directly from data, bypassing the traditionally separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy code. The authors cleverly address the non-differentiability of the quantization step using a continuous relaxation with additive noise, drawing parallels to variational autoencoders, while maintaining a focus on discrete rate-distortion performance through actual bit rate evaluation.

**Reject.** While the work is conceptually intriguing and represents a bold departure from conventional image compression pipelines, the experimental validation, despite being comprehensive, doesn't convincingly demonstrate a substantial and *generalizable* improvement over established methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000 to warrant acceptance. The gains appear inconsistent across images, and the justification for the architectural choices remains somewhat superficial.

The motivation for end-to-end optimization is strong. The authors correctly identify the limitations of traditional transform coding, where components are optimized independently. The connection drawn to variational autoencoders is insightful, framing the problem within a well-established theoretical context. The use of GDN, inspired by biological visual systems, is a sensible choice given its established ability to Gaussianize image densities. The technique of relaxing the quantization step for gradient-based optimization is clever and effectively addresses a core challenge.

However, the empirical results, while demonstrating some improvements, are not consistently compelling. The figures showcasing visual comparisons are persuasive, highlighting fewer artifacts compared to JPEG/JPEG 2000. However, these are *qualitative* assessments. The quantitative comparison using PSNR and MS-SSIM is more nuanced – improvements are visible, but not dramatic or universally observed across the test set.  The authors acknowledge this variability and rightly point out the challenges in comparing rate-distortion curves across images. Crucially, the improvements don’t appear substantial enough to offset the increased computational complexity of training and encoding. The method relies on substantial training, making it less practical than readily available codecs. The extensive appendix detailing the optimization process highlights its complexity, implicitly acknowledging the engineering effort required for implementation.

Furthermore, the rationale behind certain architectural choices feels underdeveloped. The authors state the number of filters and downsampling factors are "somewhat ad-hoc," lacking a strong justification. A more systematic exploration of these hyperparameters would strengthen the findings. The claim that GDN is more efficient than other activation functions requires more rigorous evaluation. While preliminary results are mentioned, a more detailed comparative analysis would be valuable. 

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:** Conducting ablation studies to isolate the contribution of specific components (e.g., GDN vs. ReLU, different network depths) would provide valuable insights.
*   **Computational Cost:** A detailed breakdown of the training and encoding/decoding computational costs would be helpful.  How does this compare to JPEG/JPEG 2000 in terms of both time and memory requirements?
*   **Perceptual Metric Choice:** While MS-SSIM is used, exploring other perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) might offer further insights. The connection to human perception, hinted at by the biological inspiration of GDN, could be further investigated.
*    **Generalization to other datasets:** Evaluation on datasets beyond ImageNet and Kodak would be beneficial to assess the generalization capabilities of the method.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The results suggest variability in performance across different images. Can you identify any image characteristics (e.g., texture, complexity) that correlate with better or worse compression performance using your method?
2.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of λ? Was a systematic hyperparameter search performed to optimize λ for each image in the test set, or was a single value used?
3.  The paper mentions the potential for using perceptual metrics during training. Have you experimented with this approach, and if so, what were the results?
4.  Given the computational cost of training, are there strategies for transfer learning or fine-tuning that could reduce the training time for new datasets?
