This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on cascaded nonlinear transforms, specifically employing Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The authors aim to overcome limitations of traditional transform coding by jointly optimizing the analysis and synthesis transforms directly for rate-distortion performance, moving away from separate optimization of each component. They leverage a continuous relaxation of the quantization step for gradient-based optimization and demonstrate promising results, both quantitatively (in terms of rate-distortion curves) and qualitatively (visually more appealing reconstructions compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000).

Reject. While the presented work explores an interesting and potentially valuable direction in image compression, the sheer number of minor flaws and inconsistencies, coupled with a lack of rigorous comparison and insufficient clarity in several methodological choices, prevents a positive recommendation. The paper feels incomplete and, despite significant effort, fails to convincingly demonstrate a substantial advancement over existing state-of-the-art methods beyond visual appeal, which is subjective and not fully substantiated.

The core argument hinges on end-to-end optimization, yet the continuous relaxation of the quantization step, while necessary for gradient descent, is presented without a fully convincing justification of its faithfulness to the discrete compression problem. The authors acknowledge potential bias in the relaxed entropy estimate but dismiss its impact, which feels overly simplistic. The choice of MSE as the distortion metric, despite acknowledging the benefits of perceptual metrics in prior work, limits the broader impact of the demonstrated visual improvements. Moreover, the implementation details—particularly surrounding the entropy coding scheme—are described superficially and lack sufficient detail for reproducibility. The dependence on a proprietary implementation of CABAC without providing sufficient information about its configuration raises concerns about fairness in the comparison. The experimental section also suffers from inconsistencies: the authors acknowledge variations in performance across different images, but the analysis remains largely descriptive rather than providing a comprehensive statistical evaluation. The claim of better rate-distortion performance than JPEG and JPEG 2000 "for most (but not all) test images" is insufficiently supported by concrete data. The ablation studies, although mentioned, are lacking. A clear demonstration of the contribution of GDN versus simpler nonlinearities is promised but only briefly touched upon. Finally, the numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent equation numbering, referencing) and grammatical errors detract from the overall presentation and suggest a lack of careful attention to detail.

Additional Feedback (for potential revision):

*   **Quantization Relaxation:** Provide a more thorough theoretical analysis of the impact of the continuous relaxation on the rate-distortion trade-off. Explore alternative relaxation techniques. Justify the assumption of independence of marginals in the code space more rigorously.
*   **Entropy Coding:** Provide a detailed specification of the CABAC implementation used, including all relevant parameters and configurations. Consider using a standard, open-source entropy coding library. Compare performance against established entropy coding methods (e.g., arithmetic coding) with clear reporting of bit rates and computational complexity.
*   **Perceptual Metrics:** Re-evaluate the performance of the method using a robust perceptual metric (e.g., MS-SSIM, LPIPS) as the primary optimization target. Explore the trade-offs between MSE and perceptual distortion.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct comprehensive ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component of the framework (e.g., GDN, cascaded structure, specific parameter choices).
*   **Statistical Analysis:** Provide a rigorous statistical analysis of the experimental results, including confidence intervals and statistical significance tests.
*   **Computational Cost:** Analyze and report the computational cost of encoding and decoding images with the proposed method, comparing it to JPEG and JPEG 2000.
*   **Reproducibility:** Release the code and trained models to facilitate reproducibility and further research.
*   **Formatting and Clarity:** Carefully review the entire manuscript for formatting inconsistencies, grammatical errors, and unclear phrasing.

Questions for the authors:

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of MSE as the optimization metric, given your prior work highlighting the benefits of perceptual metrics?
2.  What specific parameters were used in the CABAC implementation, and why was this particular implementation chosen over readily available open-source alternatives?
3.  Can you provide a more quantitative comparison of the computational complexity of your method compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, particularly for encoding and decoding?
4.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the number of sampling points used in the piecewise linear approximation of the marginal densities?
5.  Were any attempts made to optimize the hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, batch size) using a validation set, and if so, what were the results?
