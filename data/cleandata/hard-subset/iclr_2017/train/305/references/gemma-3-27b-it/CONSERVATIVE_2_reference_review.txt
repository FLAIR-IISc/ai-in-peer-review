This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on cascaded nonlinear transformations, specifically utilizing Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The authors aim to jointly optimize the analysis and synthesis transforms for rate-distortion performance, moving away from the traditionally separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy coding stages. They utilize a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization and demonstrate competitive, and often superior, performance to JPEG and JPEG 2000, particularly in terms of perceptual quality.

Reject. While the idea of end-to-end optimization for image compression is appealing, the paper lacks sufficient evidence of substantial and consistent improvement over well-established methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000, particularly given the significant complexity introduced. The improvements observed seem contingent on the specific image dataset used and are not convincingly demonstrated across a broader range of content.

The core issue is the lack of a truly compelling advantage. The authors repeatedly state improvements in perceptual quality, but these are largely visually assessed and supported by MS-SSIM, a metric that, while useful, isn't a universally accepted ground truth for perceptual quality. The claim of significantly outperforming established codecs warrants more rigorous, statistically robust evaluation. The rate-distortion curves presented, while suggestive, are difficult to interpret without a more comprehensive comparison across a wider range of bitrates and image types. The paper relies heavily on comparisons to JPEG and JPEG 2000 with default settings; a thorough ablation study exploring parameter tuning for these codecs would be crucial. Furthermore, the computational cost of training these complex models is not sufficiently addressed. A practical compression scheme needs to be efficient not only in compression ratio but also in encoding/decoding speed and resource requirements. The presented method appears computationally demanding. 

The connection to variational autoencoders is interesting, but feels somewhat tenuously argued. While the mathematical similarity is noted, the authors acknowledge the key difference – the discrete nature of compression versus the continuous domain of VAEs. This difference, and its implications, could be explored more deeply. The extensive appendix, while thorough, further highlights the complexity of the approach and could benefit from greater conciseness and a clearer focus on the key contributions. The justification for architectural choices (number of filters, downsampling factors, etc.) feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks experimental validation. The claim of GDN's superior efficiency compared to other activation functions needs stronger backing than a preliminary finding.

Here are some suggestions for improvement:

* **More Rigorous Evaluation:** Perform a more thorough comparison with optimized versions of JPEG and JPEG 2000, reporting results with standard deviations. Include additional datasets beyond Kodak and the few example images.
* **Computational Cost Analysis:** Provide a detailed analysis of the encoding and decoding time for the proposed method, compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000.
* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the architecture, such as the GDN layers and the number of stages.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the results to the choice of λ and other hyperparameters.
* **Clarify VAE Connection:** Elaborate on the implications of the differences between the compression problem and the VAE framework.
* **Simplify and Focus:** Streamline the appendix and focus on the most essential details.

I have several questions for the authors:

1. What is the training time for a single λ value, and what hardware was used?
2. How does the performance of your method compare to JPEG and JPEG 2000 when using optimized parameter settings for those codecs?
3. Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational complexity of the analysis and synthesis transforms?
4. Why was MSE chosen as the distortion metric for training, given the emphasis on perceptual quality in the results? Would using a perceptual loss during training lead to further improvements?
5. Can you elaborate on the choice of the piecewise linear function to approximate the marginal densities, and how the number of sampling points affects the performance?
