This paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression framework leveraging cascaded nonlinear transforms built from convolutions and generalized divisive normalization (GDN). The core idea is to learn the analysis and synthesis transforms jointly, optimizing for rate-distortion trade-offs using stochastic gradient descent, cleverly addressing the non-differentiability of the quantization step with a continuous relaxation. The authors demonstrate visually superior performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, even when optimizing for MSE, a testament to the power of learning suitable nonlinear representations.

**Reject**

The paper presents a compelling idea and demonstrates promising results. However, the experimental validation lacks sufficient rigor and comparative analysis to justify acceptance into ICLR. While the authors *show* visually improved results, the quantitative comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 relies heavily on visual inspection of rate-distortion curves and lacks a systematic, statistically sound assessment of the improvements. Furthermore, the chosen experimental setup and the justification for specific design choices are not always clear.

The primary reason for rejection stems from the insufficient detail and justification concerning the experimental setup and evaluation metrics. While the authors acknowledge challenges in summarizing rate-distortion curves (Appendix 6.3), dismissing a more thorough quantitative analysis feels insufficient.  Simply stating “the progression from high to low bit rates is shown” doesn’t constitute evidence. Claims of "substantial improvements" require statistical backing, particularly when comparing against established compression standards. The reliance on a single dataset (a subset of ImageNet and Kodak) raises concerns about generalizability. The choice of parameters for JPEG and JPEG 2000 isn't fully explained. How sensitive are the results to these parameter choices?  A more systematic ablation study to understand the contribution of specific components of the architecture (e.g., GDN vs. ReLU) would also be valuable.

Secondly, the connection to variational autoencoders, while interesting, feels somewhat tangential. The authors spend a considerable amount of space establishing this link, but it doesn't fundamentally alter the core contribution – a learned, nonlinear image compression scheme. The argument that the approach differs from typical VAEs due to the discrete nature of compression feels somewhat understated; this is a critical distinction and deserves more thorough discussion.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** Provide a statistically robust comparison (e.g., paired t-tests, ANOVA) of PSNR and MS-SSIM across the entire test set for various bit rates. Report confidence intervals. Explore other perceptual metrics beyond MS-SSIM.
*   **Ablation Study:** Systematically evaluate the contribution of different components of the architecture (e.g., number of stages, filter sizes, GDN vs. ReLU).
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the results to key hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, λ).
*   **Computational Cost:**  Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of training and compression/decompression compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000.
*   **Dataset Diversity:** Evaluate the method on a more diverse set of image datasets to assess its generalizability.  Consider datasets beyond natural images.
*   **Clarify Experimental Setup:** Explicitly state the settings used for JPEG and JPEG 2000 (e.g., quantization tables, parameter settings). Justify these choices.
*   **Entropy Coding Details:** Elaborate on the limitations of the chosen CABAC implementation and how it might affect the comparison.
*   **Discretization Impact:** Provide more insight into the impact of the continuous relaxation of quantization on the actual discrete compression performance.  Is the bias in the entropy approximation significant in practice?
*   **Connection to VAEs:** If the connection to VAEs isn’t central, consider streamlining this section. If it *is* important, provide a more rigorous analysis of the similarities and differences.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a table showing the average PSNR and MS-SSIM values for your method, JPEG, and JPEG 2000 across the Kodak dataset (or a larger dataset) for a range of bitrates, *along with statistical significance tests*?
2.  How sensitive are your results to the choice of parameters in JPEG and JPEG 2000?  Did you perform any optimization of those parameters for a fair comparison?
3.  What is the computational cost (training time, encoding/decoding time) of your method compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000?
4.  The paper mentions a heuristic to adapt the range of the spline approximation for the marginal probability models. Could you provide more details about this heuristic?
5.  You state that the entropy code is a simplification compared to those used in JPEG 2000. What is the expected compression efficiency loss due to this simplification?
