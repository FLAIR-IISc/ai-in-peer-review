This paper introduces a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework, moving beyond traditional transform coding’s separate optimization of transforms, quantizers, and entropy codes. The core idea – using cascaded convolutions and generalized divisive normalization (GDN) jointly optimized with a continuous relaxation of the quantization step – is a compelling departure from established methods, with a clear connection to generative modeling and variational autoencoders. The authors demonstrate visually improved compression results compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, even when optimized for MSE, a noteworthy achievement.

**Reject.** While the approach is genuinely novel and potentially impactful, the paper feels incomplete in demonstrating a clear and consistent advantage over established methods beyond visual inspection. The reliance on somewhat hand-wavy arguments about the superiority of GDN and the lack of a fully convincing quantitative comparison, despite the extensive experimental setup, leads me to recommend rejection.

The primary strength of this work lies in its conceptual innovation. The formulation elegantly bridges the gap between rate-distortion optimization and generative modeling, and the use of a continuous relaxation to enable end-to-end training is clever. The authors appropriately highlight the limitations of linear transforms in capturing higher-order dependencies and position GDN as a solution, building on prior work. The experimental setup is commendable, including a substantial dataset and a custom entropy code implementation.  The visual results presented are striking and suggest a significant improvement in perceptual quality, especially regarding artifact reduction.

However, several concerns prevent a positive recommendation. First, the quantitative comparison, while showing improvements in some cases, is not consistently convincing. The variations in performance across different images (as acknowledged by the authors themselves in the appendix) raise questions about the robustness of the method.  Simply stating that “summaries of rate-distortion are of limited use” doesn’t excuse the lack of a more thorough statistical analysis. A more rigorous comparison, potentially using a wider range of metrics (beyond PSNR and MS-SSIM) or a more comprehensive statistical analysis of rate-distortion curves across the test set, is needed. 

Second, the justification for the architectural choices (number of filters, downsampling factors) feels ad-hoc. While the authors acknowledge this, a more systematic exploration of the design space could potentially reveal further improvements. 

Third, the relationship to variational autoencoders, while insightful, feels somewhat underdeveloped. The authors correctly point out differences, but a deeper exploration of how the constraints imposed on the optimization problem affect the learned representation and its generative capabilities would strengthen the connection.

Finally, the presentation could be improved. The paper is dense and requires significant effort to follow.  Streamlining the mathematical derivations and providing more intuitive explanations of the key concepts would enhance readability.



**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Quantitative Analysis:**  Provide a more robust quantitative comparison. Consider reporting confidence intervals or statistical significance tests to support the claims of improvement. Explore alternative metrics beyond PSNR and MS-SSIM.
*   **Ablation Studies:**  Perform ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the framework, such as the GDN nonlinearity or the cascaded structure.
*   **Architectural Exploration:** Investigate the sensitivity of the performance to different architectural choices.
*   **VAE Connection:** Expand on the connection to variational autoencoders. Can the framework be adapted to generate samples, or can insights from VAE training be used to improve compression performance?
*   **Computational Cost:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of training and compression/decompression.
*    **Clarify the role of λ**:  Explain more intuitively what different values of λ represent and how they influence the trade-off between rate and distortion.




**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed statistical analysis of the rate-distortion performance across the test set, beyond the individual image examples? Specifically, what is the median improvement in rate for a given distortion level across all images?
2.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the GDN parameters (e.g., the exponents controlling tail behavior)? Did you explore different parameter settings, and if so, what were the results?
3.  The paper mentions that the continuous relaxation of the quantization step provides a good approximation to the actual rate-distortion values. Could you quantify this approximation error more precisely (e.g., by reporting the difference between the relaxed entropy and the actual entropy for a sample of images)?
4.  You mention that the optimization process is computationally expensive. What is the approximate training time for a single model (given a specific λ) on the ImageNet subset?
5.  Could you elaborate on the practical considerations for deploying this method in resource-constrained environments (e.g., mobile devices)? What are the trade-offs between compression performance and computational complexity?
