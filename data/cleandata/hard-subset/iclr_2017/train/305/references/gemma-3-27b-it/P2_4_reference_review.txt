## Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

**1. Summary:**

This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on cascaded nonlinear transformations, specifically using Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The authors aim to jointly optimize the analysis and synthesis transforms directly for rate-distortion performance, overcoming the limitations of traditional transform coding methods where these components are optimized separately.  A key contribution is the use of a continuous relaxation of the quantization step during optimization, allowing gradient descent, and the subsequent evaluation of the compression performance using actual bitrates, demonstrating its practical viability. The paper demonstrates improvements in both rate-distortion performance and perceptual quality compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper tackles a significant problem in image compression and proposes a compelling solution that demonstrably outperforms established methods. The rigorous theoretical justification and thorough empirical evaluation strongly support the claims.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Problem & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional transform coding – namely, the separate optimization of components – and positions the proposed end-to-end approach as a logical and promising solution. The connection to probabilistic source modeling and the trade-off between rate and distortion are well explained.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed framework is well-motivated, drawing inspiration from biological vision systems and established techniques like GDN. The use of a continuous relaxation for optimization is a clever and effective approach to address the non-differentiability of the quantization step. The connection to variational autoencoders, while not central to the contribution, provides a useful theoretical context.
* **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experimental results are a major strength of the paper.  The comparisons to JPEG and JPEG 2000 are comprehensive, and the observation of improved perceptual quality is particularly convincing. The authors acknowledge the limitations of averaging rate-distortion curves and provide links to individual image results for detailed inspection, demonstrating a commitment to transparency. The fact that they report *actual* bitrates, and not just estimated entropy, further strengthens the credibility of the results. The ablation studies (implicit in the discussion of GDN vs. other non-linearities) are also well considered.
* **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details. The inclusion of the appendix with detailed architectural and optimization information is greatly appreciated.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Computational Cost:**  While the authors briefly mention the computational cost of training, a more detailed discussion of the resources required (e.g., training time, GPU memory) would be valuable. This is particularly important given the complexity of the model.
* **Generalization and Robustness:** It would be useful to briefly discuss the potential limitations of the approach, such as its sensitivity to the training data distribution or its performance on images significantly different from those in ImageNet.
* **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 is excellent, but a brief discussion of how this work compares to other recent learned image compression methods (e.g., those based on VQ-VAE) would strengthen the paper.
* **Figure Clarity:**  Some of the figures, particularly those in the appendix, are somewhat difficult to read. Increasing their resolution or simplifying their presentation would improve readability.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* You mention the possibility of optimizing with a perceptual metric instead of MSE. Have you experimented with this, and if so, what were the results?  What challenges did you encounter?
* The choice of 128 filters in the first stage seems somewhat arbitrary. Was this determined through experimentation, or is there a theoretical basis for this choice?
* Could you elaborate on the potential for hardware acceleration of the GDN/IGDN operations? This would be relevant to the practical deployment of this method.
* The code is not publicly available.  Could you provide a timeline for when the code might be released to the community? This would greatly facilitate reproducibility and further research.



In conclusion, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of image compression. The innovative approach, strong experimental results, and clear presentation make it a compelling and valuable piece of research. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
