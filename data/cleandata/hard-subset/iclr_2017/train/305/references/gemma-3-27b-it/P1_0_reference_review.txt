## Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

**1. Summary:**

This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework. Departing from traditional transform coding methods with separately optimized components, the authors propose a system where the analysis and synthesis transforms are jointly learned using stochastic gradient descent. The core innovation lies in utilizing cascaded convolutions and Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) to create flexible, non-linear transforms, followed by uniform quantization and an approximate inverse transform.  A crucial aspect of the work is the use of a continuous relaxation of the quantization step during training, enabling gradient-based optimization, and reporting actual bitrates for evaluation. The authors demonstrate, both qualitatively and quantitatively, that their method surpasses JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of perceptual image quality and rate-distortion performance.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of image compression, demonstrating a clear improvement over established methods. The approach is innovative, well-motivated, and supported by comprehensive experimental results.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty & Motivation:** The paper tackles a long-standing problem – jointly optimizing rate and distortion in image compression – with a fresh perspective. The move towards end-to-end learning, leveraging the power of deep learning-inspired architectures, is well-justified, considering the limitations of traditional, hand-engineered methods. The motivation, stemming from biological visual systems and prior work on Gaussianizing image densities, is clearly articulated.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed framework, utilizing GDN and a continuous relaxation of quantization, appears technically sound. The authors acknowledge the challenges of optimization with quantization and address them effectively.  The connection to variational autoencoders is insightful and helps position the work within a broader context of generative modeling.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are compelling. The authors rigorously compare their method to JPEG and JPEG 2000 on standard datasets, demonstrating consistently superior performance in terms of both PSNR/MS-SSIM and subjective visual quality (as evidenced by the provided example images).  The inclusion of actual bit rates and detailed evaluation setup adds to the credibility of the findings. The ablation studies in the appendix further strengthen the validation.
* **Clear Presentation:**  Despite the technical complexity, the paper is well-written and logically structured. The authors provide sufficient detail about the network architecture, optimization procedure, and experimental setup, allowing for reproducibility.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention the high computational cost of training, a more detailed discussion of the training time, hardware requirements, and potential optimizations (e.g., distributed training) would be beneficial.
* **Generalizability:**  The paper focuses on a specific network architecture (3 stages of convolutions, GDN, subsampling).  Exploring the sensitivity of the performance to variations in network depth, filter sizes, and other hyperparameters would be valuable. A brief discussion on the potential limitations regarding image types (e.g. medical images, satellite imagery) would also be helpful.
* **Entropy Coding Details:** The description of the entropy coding scheme, while present in the appendix, could be expanded upon.  More details about the context modeling and adaptive arithmetic coding implementation would improve the clarity and allow for a more complete comparison to other methods using advanced entropy coding.
* **Relation to Learned Image Compression:** While the paper draws connections to VAEs, briefly comparing and contrasting with other recent work explicitly focused on *learned* image compression (e.g., those based on autoencoders with learned quantization) would further contextualize the contribution.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the continuous relaxation:**  You mention that the bias in the relaxed entropy estimate disappears for finer quantization. Could you quantify this bias more precisely across different bitrates and for different image characteristics? Would a different relaxation technique (e.g. using a Gumbel-Softmax trick) have advantages or disadvantages?
* **Regarding GDN:** The authors note that while other nonlinearities might work, GDN seems to offer efficiency.  What specific properties of GDN contribute to this efficiency in the context of compression – is it the ability to Gaussianize the data, the nonlinearity, or something else?
* **Regarding perceptual metric:** You mention the possibility of optimizing directly with a perceptual metric. What are the major challenges involved in doing so, and do you have any preliminary results using such a metric?
* **Regarding future work:** Do you foresee opportunities to combine this method with other compression techniques, such as vector quantization or transform coding, to further improve performance?
