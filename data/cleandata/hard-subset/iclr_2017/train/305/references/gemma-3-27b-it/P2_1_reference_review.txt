## Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

**1. Summary:**

This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on cascaded nonlinear transformations, specifically utilizing Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The authors address the limitations of traditional transform coding by jointly optimizing the analysis and synthesis transforms, as well as the entropy coding stage, directly for rate-distortion performance.  They cleverly circumvent the gradient issues arising from the quantization step by employing a continuous relaxation using additive noise during training.  Importantly, they evaluate performance using actual bitrates, demonstrating a complete, practical compression solution and showing visually superior results compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000. The work draws strong connections to variational autoencoders, highlighting the theoretical underpinnings of their approach.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of image compression. The approach is innovative, well-motivated, and supported by compelling experimental results. The visual quality improvement over established codecs is particularly noteworthy.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty & Motivation:** The paper tackles a long-standing challenge – the suboptimal separate optimization of components in traditional image compression. The end-to-end approach, and the use of GDN, are well-motivated by the desire to capture higher-order dependencies in image data and approximate biological visual systems.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is technically sound. The use of a continuous relaxation to enable gradient-based optimization is a clever and effective solution to a core problem.  The connection to variational autoencoders provides a strong theoretical basis. The detailed explanation of the transforms (analysis/synthesis) and the entropy coding scheme enhances the credibility of the approach.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are convincing. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements in perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) and often in rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000. The inclusion of a large set of test images and the availability of the compressed images online for inspection are valuable assets. The thorough comparison to established codecs is well executed.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is, for the most part, well-written and clearly explains the complex concepts involved. The diagrams (especially Figure 1) are helpful in understanding the architecture.

**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **Computational Cost Discussion:** While the authors mention training is computationally expensive, a more detailed discussion of the computational requirements (e.g., training time, hardware used) would be helpful for reproducibility and understanding the practical limitations of the approach.
* **Ablation Study:** An ablation study investigating the contribution of each component (e.g., GDN, cascaded structure) to the overall performance would further strengthen the findings.  For example, what happens if you replace GDN with ReLU activations?
* **Comparison to Recent Learned Codecs:** The field of learned image compression has advanced rapidly. It would be beneficial to briefly discuss and compare the approach to more recent learned codecs, such as those based on VQ-VAE or other generative models, acknowledging any differences in methodology or objectives.
* **Further Analysis of Rate-Distortion Trade-offs:** While the results demonstrate improvements, a more detailed analysis of the rate-distortion curves and how the proposed method shapes this curve compared to JPEG/JPEG 2000 would be insightful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*  Could you elaborate on the choice of 10 sampling points for the piecewise linear function used to approximate the marginals? Was this number determined through experimentation, and how sensitive are the results to this parameter?
*  The paper mentions a slight positive bias in the relaxed entropy estimate for coarser quantization. How does this bias affect the choice of lambda during optimization, and is there a way to compensate for it?
*  You mention that the model parameters are fixed after optimization. Could you discuss potential benefits of *adaptive* parameter selection during encoding, potentially using some form of online learning?
*  Given the architectural similarities to VAEs, have you explored using a perceptual loss directly during training instead of MSE? You mention this as a possible future direction, but any preliminary results or insights would be appreciated.
