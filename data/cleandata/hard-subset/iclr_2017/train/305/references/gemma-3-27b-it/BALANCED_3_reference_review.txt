This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework leveraging cascaded nonlinear transformations inspired by biological visual systems. The authors build upon their previous work, moving from perceptual distortion optimization to MSE, and utilizing a generalized divisive normalization (GDN) combined with uniform scalar quantization. A key contribution is the use of a continuous relaxation of the quantization step during optimization, enabling stochastic gradient descent. The paper thoroughly demonstrates the method's performance against JPEG and JPEG 2000, showcasing improvements in both rate-distortion and perceptual quality.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound approach with promising results, the extensive supplementary material and the sheer length of the paper (including the appendix) make it challenging to assess the core contributions efficiently.  The arguments are buried within a lot of details, and the novelty, while present, isn't immediately clear from the main text. A more concise presentation focusing on the key innovations and experimental findings would significantly strengthen the submission. The core technique is a clever adaptation of ideas from generative modeling to compression, but the connection isn't highlighted strongly enough in the main body.

The approach is strongly motivated by both engineering principles (rate-distortion theory) and neuroscience (modeling the visual system). The authors effectively position their work within the broader context of image compression and generative models, drawing parallels to variational autoencoders. The literature review is comprehensive and well-integrated. The use of GDN, previously shown to be effective in Gaussianizing image densities, is a logical choice, and the authors build a strong case for its suitability in the compression context. The continuous relaxation technique to enable gradient-based optimization is a clever solution to a challenging problem. 

The paper supports its claims with a comprehensive set of experiments. The comparisons to JPEG and JPEG 2000 are thorough, and the presented rate-distortion curves and visual examples demonstrate clear improvements, particularly in perceptual quality. The empirical validation of the relaxed loss function, showing its approximation of the discrete rate-distortion values, adds credibility to the approach.  The availability of the compressed images and rate-distortion curves online is a significant positive aspect, enabling a detailed assessment of the results. However, the sheer volume of this data in the appendix makes it difficult to quickly grasp the key takeaways and impacts the overall readability.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Conciseness:** The paper is excessively long. The appendix contains a wealth of details, but much of it could be streamlined or moved to a supplemental website. The main paper should focus on the core contributions and experimental results, omitting many implementation details.
*   **Highlight Novelty:**  The connection to variational autoencoders and generative modeling should be made more explicit and central to the narrative.  Emphasize how this work *differs* from standard VAE approaches and *why* those differences are beneficial for compression.
*   **Ablation Studies:** While the authors perform comparisons against existing methods, some ablation studies exploring the impact of individual components (e.g., the GDN nonlinearity, the cascaded structure) would be valuable.
*   **Computational Cost:**  A more detailed discussion of the computational cost of training and compression/decompression would be helpful. While the authors mention that decoding is efficient, the training cost is significant and should be quantified.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:**  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., the number of stages, the number of filters)?
*   **Loss Function Justification:** Explain more clearly why MSE was chosen despite the previous work emphasizing perceptual metrics. Was this solely for ease of comparison? What are the potential drawbacks?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The continuous relaxation is a key aspect of the optimization. How was the number of sampling points for the piecewise linear approximation chosen, and how does this choice impact the accuracy of the approximation and the computational cost?
2.  The paper mentions that the optimization process involves renormalization of the linear filters. What impact does this renormalization have on the learning dynamics and the final performance?
3.  Can you quantify the computational cost of training a model for a given dataset size? What are the memory requirements?
4.  How does the performance of your method compare to more recent compression techniques, such as those based on deep learning (e.g., those using attention mechanisms or transformers)?
5.  You note that RGB models used a higher channel count. Was this determined empirically, or was it a heuristic based on the increased dimensionality?
6.  While the visual results are compelling, is there a more quantitative metric to evaluate the perceived smoothness of contours in the compressed images that you can present?
