This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework utilizing cascaded nonlinear transformations based on Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The authors aim to jointly optimize the analysis and synthesis transforms for a given rate-distortion trade-off, circumventing the traditionally separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy code. A continuous relaxation of the quantization step allows for gradient-based optimization, and the framework demonstrates improved perceptual quality compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, despite being trained with MSE as the distortion metric.

Reject. While the paper presents an interesting idea and demonstrates visually compelling results, the sheer number of minor methodological issues, inconsistencies, and a lack of rigorous justification for certain design choices outweigh the potential contributions. The paper feels incomplete and lacks the polish expected for ICLR.

The primary concerns stem from a lack of clarity and justification in several key areas. Firstly, the choice of hyperparameters, network architecture details (beyond a high-level description), and optimization procedures appear largely ad-hoc. The rationale behind the specific number of stages, filter sizes, and the choice of DCT parameterization isn’t convincingly explained. While the authors mention experimentation, the lack of ablation studies or sensitivity analysis leaves the reader questioning the robustness of the results. The claim of efficient Gaussianization with GDN, while cited, isn't directly demonstrated *within the context of compression* – it's a pre-existing result used as motivation.

Secondly, the entropy coding implementation details are insufficiently described. The authors mention a CABAC-based code but provide limited specifics on the context modeling and adaptation strategies. A crucial claim is made that the implemented entropy code results in rates “slightly larger” than the entropy estimate, but this is not substantiated with comparative figures against established entropy coders used in JPEG 2000. The assertion regarding negligible differences between differential and discrete entropy approximations feels unsubstantiated, especially in regimes where the relaxation may be poor.

Thirdly, the experimental evaluation, while extensive, lacks a standardized and rigorous comparison. The method of selecting comparison points on the rate-distortion curves feels subjective. Simply stating that the JPEG 2000 bit rate is "lowest possible" while being >= to the proposed method is vague and lacks reproducibility.  The claim that perceptual improvements are “substantial” relies heavily on visual inspection and MS-SSIM, and a more comprehensive perceptual study (e.g., subjective testing) would bolster this claim. The large number of figures feels excessive, and a more concise presentation of key findings would be preferred.

Finally, the connection drawn to variational autoencoders, while interesting, feels somewhat tenuous. The authors acknowledge the limitations of this connection when using a non-Euclidean perceptual metric, diminishing the strength of the analogy. The appendix contains a wealth of information, yet important details (e.g., precise GDN parameter bounds, details of piecewise linear marginal approximation) are buried within it, hindering readability. Numerous minor formatting issues (e.g., inconsistent equation numbering, hyphenation) further detract from the paper's overall presentation.



Additional feedback:

*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the architecture (e.g., GDN, number of stages, filter sizes).
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the results to changes in key hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, regularization parameters).
*   **Entropy Coding Details:** Provide a detailed description of the CABAC implementation, including context modeling strategies and adaptation mechanisms. Compare performance against a state-of-the-art entropy coder (e.g., used in JPEG 2000).
*   **Perceptual Evaluation:** Consider conducting a subjective perceptual study to validate the observed improvements in visual quality.
*   **Computational Complexity:** Analyze the computational complexity of the proposed method, both in terms of training and inference.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Provide a more rigorous theoretical justification for the design choices. For example, why is DCT parameterization preferred over DFT? Why this specific architecture for GDN?
*   **Clearer Connection to VAEs:**  If the connection to VAEs is central, strengthen the argument and address the limitations more directly. If not, consider downplaying it.
*   **Streamline Presentation:** Reduce the number of figures and focus on presenting the most important findings in a clear and concise manner.




Questions for the authors:

1.  What criteria were used to select the specific hyperparameters (e.g., filter sizes, number of stages) for the analysis and synthesis transforms? Was any hyperparameter optimization performed?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the context modeling strategy used in your CABAC-based entropy coder?
3.  How does the computational cost of encoding/decoding an image using your method compare to that of JPEG and JPEG 2000?
4.  How robust are the results to changes in the training dataset? Have you evaluated the performance on datasets other than ImageNet?
5.  What is the rationale behind using piecewise linear functions to approximate the marginal densities? Are there alternative approximations that were considered?
