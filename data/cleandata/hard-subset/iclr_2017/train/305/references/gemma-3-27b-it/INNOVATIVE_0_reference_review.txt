This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework, moving beyond traditional transform coding’s separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy code. The core idea is to learn a nonlinear transformation (built from cascaded convolutions and GDN layers) jointly with a quantization step, optimizing directly for rate-distortion using a continuous relaxation trick to enable gradient-based optimization. Importantly, the authors demonstrate performance using actual bit rates with an implemented entropy code, establishing feasibility beyond a theoretical framework. The connection to variational autoencoders is also thoughtfully discussed.

**Reject.** While the approach is undeniably innovative and ambitious, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating a substantial and consistent advantage over established methods, despite the visually appealing results. The experimental validation, while thorough, feels somewhat *post-hoc* in its justification of the method’s success. The gains are image-dependent, and the reasons for this variability aren't fully explored. 

The primary justification for rejection stems from the relatively incremental improvement observed, given the complexity of the proposed system. The core claim of superior performance is often qualified ("for most images," "especially at lower bitrates"), which weakens the overall impact. The qualitative improvement in visual appearance, while noted, isn’t rigorously quantified beyond MS-SSIM, and the dependence on the MSE optimization objective for training, despite aiming for perceptual quality, feels like a limitation not fully addressed. The visual examples provided are compelling, but a more systematic analysis across a larger, more diverse dataset is needed to solidify the claim of consistently better perceptual quality. The lack of a compelling explanation *why* this approach consistently outperforms JPEG and JPEG2000 beyond "nonlinear transformations capture image features" is concerning.

Supporting arguments:

* **Image Dependence:** Performance varies significantly across images (as admitted by the authors and observed in the supplemental material). This suggests the method is sensitive to image characteristics and might not generalize well. A deeper analysis of what image properties lead to better or worse compression is missing.
* **Limited Theoretical Justification:** While the connection to variational autoencoders is interesting, it doesn’t fully explain the success of the proposed method. The authors acknowledge that perceptual metrics break the direct correspondence to VAEs, leaving the theoretical underpinning somewhat unclear.
* **Complex Optimization:** The optimization process is complex, with numerous hyperparameters and potential failure modes. The robustness of the optimization procedure isn't adequately assessed.
* **Comparison to SOTA:** The comparison is primarily to JPEG and JPEG2000. It would be significantly more compelling to compare against more recent, state-of-the-art learned compression methods (e.g., those utilizing VQ-VAE or similar architectures) to better position the contribution.

Additional Feedback (to improve the paper):

* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component of the architecture (GDN, cascaded convolutions, specific downsampling factors). This would provide insights into the importance of different design choices.
* **Perceptual Metric Training:** Explore training the model directly with a perceptual loss function. This could potentially lead to even better visual quality and address the limitations of using MSE as a proxy.
* **Generalization Analysis:** Analyze the performance of the model on out-of-distribution datasets to assess its generalization ability.
* **Computational Cost:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of both training and inference, especially compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000.
* **Further Explore Rate-Distortion Tradeoffs:** Instead of primarily showing results at fixed bitrates, a more comprehensive exploration of the entire rate-distortion curve for various images would be helpful.

Questions for the Authors:

1.  The performance seems highly image-dependent. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the image characteristics that lead to better or worse compression performance? Specifically, how do texture, edge density, and color variance impact the compression ratio and quality?
2.  The optimization procedure appears complex. Were there any challenges with convergence or sensitivity to initialization? Did you explore different optimization strategies (e.g., learning rate schedules, different optimizers)?
3.  You mention that GDN is more efficient than ReLU in this setting. Can you provide more quantitative evidence to support this claim, perhaps through ablation studies or comparisons with models using ReLU?
4. You state that the use of MSE as an optimization target may limit perceptual quality.  If you were to retrain the model using a perceptual metric (like MS-SSIM directly), what improvements, if any, would you expect to see, and what challenges do you anticipate?
