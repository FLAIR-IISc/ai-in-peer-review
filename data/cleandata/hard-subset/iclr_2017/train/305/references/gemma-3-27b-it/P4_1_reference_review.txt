This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework leveraging cascaded nonlinear transformations inspired by biological visual systems, specifically Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The key contribution lies in jointly optimizing the analysis and synthesis transforms with respect to rate and distortion (MSE), using a continuous relaxation of the quantization step for gradient-based optimization. The authors demonstrate improved visual quality compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, particularly in reducing common artifacts associated with linear transform coding.

**Reject**

The paper, while presenting an interesting idea, lacks sufficient clarity in several aspects of its implementation and evaluation, making it difficult to fully assess the validity and significance of the claims. Furthermore, the empirical gains over established methods, while visually apparent, are not consistently demonstrated across a diverse set of images and require more rigorous statistical backing.

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of clear justification for several design choices and the insufficient detail in the experimental setup. The authors state a design choice based on Gaussianization performance from prior work but don't adequately explain *why* this translates to improved compression performance, especially given the MSE optimization target. The sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter choices (e.g., network architecture details beyond those presented) is unknown. While the appendix provides some information, it remains insufficient for reproducibility.  The number of images used for training (6507) feels small given the complexity of the model, and the impact of the image selection process is not discussed. A more comprehensive ablation study, investigating the contributions of different components (e.g., GDN vs. ReLU), would significantly strengthen the paper.

Secondly, the comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 relies on subjective visual assessment alongside PSNR and MS-SSIM. While the examples provided are compelling, a statistically significant comparison across a larger and more diverse dataset is needed to support the claim of consistently improved performance.  The chosen implementation of JPEG and JPEG 2000 (OpenJPEG with default settings) is not fully justified; the performance of these codecs can vary substantially based on configuration.  The authors also acknowledge inconsistencies in performance – outperforming JPEG 2000 on some images but not others – without a clear explanation.  The explanation of why the rate-distortion curve shape is important in this context is well made, but the presentation of those curves is limited.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the relationship to Variational Autoencoders (VAEs):** While the authors discuss the connection to VAEs, the implications of using MSE as a distortion metric (and deviation from the typical VAE setup) are not fully explored. How does this choice affect the learned representations?
*   **Explain the choice of the uniform quantizer:** Why was a uniform quantizer chosen over other quantization schemes? What is the impact of the quantization step size on performance?
*   **Provide more details on the entropy coding:** The description of the entropy coding scheme is brief. What are the limitations of this CABAC implementation, and how might it affect the reported bit rates? How does it compare to the entropy coding used in JPEG 2000?
*   **Address computational cost:** The authors mention high training costs but provide little information on inference costs. Is the proposed method practical for real-time compression/decompression?
*    **Regarding figures:** Figure 2 is somewhat abstract and could be clarified with a simpler illustration. Figure 8, illustrating the network architecture, is difficult to parse without a key describing the filter sizes and strides.
*   **The phrase "much more so than cascades of linear transforms followed by pointwise nonlinearities" lacks quantification.** How much more so?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What specific criteria were used for selecting the 6507 images for training? How sensitive are the results to the training set composition?
2.  How were the hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, batch size, number of filters) chosen? Was any hyperparameter optimization performed?
3.  Could the authors provide the computational time required for training and inference for a single image?
4.  What is the impact of the choice of MSE as the distortion metric on the learned representations compared to using a perceptual metric during training?
5.   Could the authors provide a quantitative comparison of the entropy coding performance of their scheme versus JPEG 2000’s?
