This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on cascaded nonlinear transformations, specifically employing generalized divisive normalization (GDN). The authors aim to jointly optimize the analysis and synthesis transforms, as well as the entropy coding, directly for rate-distortion performance, utilizing a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization. They demonstrate improved performance, particularly in perceptual quality, compared to traditional methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000.

**Reject.** While the approach is interesting, the paper relies heavily on empirical evidence without sufficient theoretical grounding or comparative robustness. The gains over established methods are presented without a thorough examination of hyperparameter sensitivity or a deeper analysis of why the proposed method performs better – beyond simply stating it *looks* better. The novelty isn’t substantial enough to justify the complexity without significantly stronger validation.

The core argument for improvement rests on the superior performance of the learned nonlinear transforms compared to hand-crafted linear transforms used in JPEG/JPEG 2000. However, the specific architecture choices (number of layers, filter sizes, etc.) appear somewhat arbitrary and lack justification. A more rigorous ablation study varying these hyperparameters would strengthen the claim that the GDN-based approach is fundamentally superior, rather than simply being a well-tuned instance of a complex model. The claim that GDN is more efficient than other activation functions (ReLU) is presented as preliminary, and requires further investigation. The connection drawn to variational autoencoders, while insightful, doesn’t fully justify the entire framework; the relaxation of quantization is presented as necessary for optimization, but its impact on the final compression performance (beyond the empirical validation) isn't thoroughly addressed.

Furthermore, the evaluation, while extensive in terms of the number of images tested, lacks sufficient detail regarding the specific settings used for JPEG and JPEG 2000. Were default parameters used? Were they tuned for optimal performance? The authors state the implementations were done “fairly,” but don’t provide enough information to substantiate this claim. The reliance on MS-SSIM as a primary metric is concerning, as perceptual metrics are subjective and can be easily manipulated. While visually appealing results are important, a robust compression algorithm must also demonstrate consistent gains in objective metrics like PSNR. The observation that performance varies across images is glossed over, implying the method is not universally superior. The statement regarding the benefit of not using a perceptual metric when generalizing to color images feels contradictory to the stated improvements in visual quality.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Theoretical Analysis:** Provide a more in-depth theoretical analysis of the continuous relaxation of quantization. How does this approximation impact the rate-distortion tradeoff? What are the limitations of this approach?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Conduct a thorough ablation study to assess the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters (number of layers, filter sizes, learning rate, etc.).
*   **Baseline Comparison:** Provide a more detailed description of the JPEG and JPEG 2000 implementations used for comparison. Demonstrate that these implementations were optimized for optimal performance.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests to demonstrate that the observed improvements are not due to chance.
*   **Computational Complexity:** Analyze the computational cost of encoding and decoding images using the proposed method.
*   **Generalization:** Investigate the generalization performance of the model on datasets other than ImageNet.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The choice of network architecture seems somewhat arbitrary. What principles guided the selection of the number of layers, filter sizes, and downsampling factors?
2.  The continuous relaxation of quantization is a key aspect of the approach. What is the theoretical justification for using additive uniform noise, and how does this choice affect the accuracy of the rate-distortion approximation?
3.  While visually improved results are presented, could the observed gains be attributed to a more sophisticated entropy coding scheme rather than the nonlinear transforms themselves? Have you compared the performance of the proposed entropy code to state-of-the-art alternatives?
4. The paper states “the model is trained for each lambda”. What is the computational overhead associated with training separate models for different rate-distortion trade-offs?
5. What is the memory footprint of the trained model and how does that compare to the memory requirements of JPEG and JPEG 2000 decoders?
