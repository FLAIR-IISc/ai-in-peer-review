This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on cascaded nonlinear transformations, specifically utilizing Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The core contribution lies in jointly optimizing the analysis and synthesis transforms, along with an entropy code, for a rate-distortion trade-off, addressing the limitations of traditional transform coding where these components are often optimized separately. The authors demonstrate improved performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, both in terms of MSE and perceptual quality.

**Reject**

The primary reason for rejection is a lack of clarity and thoroughness in the experimental validation, specifically concerning the comparison to existing standards. While the authors claim improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000, the details regarding implementation choices and evaluation protocols are insufficient to confidently assess these claims. The secondary reason relates to the significant reliance on prior work without fully justifying the design choices within the presented framework.

The paper’s claims of superiority are supported by experiments on the Kodak image dataset and a selection of standard test images. However, the details surrounding the entropy coding implementation are vague. Mentioning the use of CABAC is insufficient; a more detailed description of the context modeling and adaptation strategies used is needed. The sensitivity of the results to the specific CABAC parameters is not addressed. Furthermore, the authors acknowledge limitations in comparing their approach to JPEG 2000 due to differing optimization goals. The assertion that their continuous relaxation provides a "good approximation" to the actual rate-distortion is not fully substantiated with rigorous analysis beyond visual inspection of curves in Figure 4. While the authors discuss the potential bias in the entropy estimation, they do not quantify its impact on the reported rate-distortion performance. The presented results are heavily reliant on visual inspection, which, while compelling, is not a rigorous scientific assessment. The assertion that the improvement holds "for all images" requires stronger statistical validation.

Several aspects could improve the paper. First, a more detailed comparison to state-of-the-art image compression algorithms (beyond JPEG and JPEG 2000) such as VVC (Versatile Video Coding) or AV1 would be beneficial. Second, the authors should clarify the choice of network architecture and the rationale behind the specific hyperparameters used. A sensitivity analysis demonstrating the robustness of the results to variations in these parameters would strengthen the paper. Third, a more in-depth discussion of the computational cost of training and encoding/decoding would be valuable. Fourth, the connection to variational autoencoders, while discussed, could be explored further to identify potential benefits and drawbacks of the proposed approach. Finally, the appendix contains substantial information – integrating key aspects into the main text would improve readability.

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed description of the CABAC implementation used, including the context modeling strategy and any parameter tuning performed?
2.  How sensitive are the reported results to the choice of λ, and have you performed an ablation study to assess the contribution of different components of the network (e.g., the number of stages, the type of nonlinearity)?
3.  What is the computational cost (training time, encoding/decoding time) of the proposed method compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000?
4.  The paper states that “the relaxed distortion term appears to be mostly unbiased”. Can you provide quantitative evidence to support this claim, such as a statistical analysis of the error distribution?
5.  Why was MSE chosen as the distortion metric for training, given the reported improvements in perceptual quality? Would optimizing directly for a perceptual metric lead to further improvements?
