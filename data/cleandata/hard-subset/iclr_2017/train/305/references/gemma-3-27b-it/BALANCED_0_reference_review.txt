This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on cascaded nonlinear transformations, specifically using Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The authors demonstrate that optimizing for MSE, while employing these flexible transforms and a continuous relaxation for quantization, yields visually superior results compared to traditional transform coding methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000, and shows competitive rate-distortion performance. The connection to variational autoencoders is thoughtfully discussed, highlighting both similarities and crucial differences. The experimental validation, including a comprehensive online repository of compressed images, strengthens the claims.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a compelling approach and strong empirical results, the lack of a truly novel theoretical contribution and the heavy reliance on empirical demonstration, coupled with certain limitations in the experimental setup, prevent an acceptance at this time. The improvements over established codecs, while visually apparent and statistically supported, aren't overwhelmingly substantial to justify a paradigm shift.

The primary justification for rejection stems from the limited theoretical novelty. The core idea of end-to-end optimization of a compression pipeline isn’t entirely new.  While the application of GDN and the specific formulation of the relaxed optimization problem are valuable, they build upon existing work in variational autoencoders and nonlinear dimensionality reduction. The theoretical connection to VAEs, while clearly explained, doesn’t introduce a fundamentally new mathematical insight. The paper primarily demonstrates *how* to apply these concepts to compression, rather than offering a significant theoretical advance *in* compression itself.  A stronger theoretical justification for why this specific architecture performs so well (beyond the generally understood benefits of nonlinear transformations) would be beneficial.

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation, while extensive, could be more rigorous. The comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 is somewhat limited by the reliance on existing implementations (OpenJPEG) with default settings. A more thorough parameter sweep of JPEG and JPEG 2000 to achieve optimal performance for each image would provide a fairer comparison.  The claim of visual superiority is primarily based on subjective assessment and MS-SSIM, which, while useful, don't fully capture the nuances of perceptual quality.  A user study with human observers would significantly strengthen this claim. The selection of the ImageNet subset for training is also a potential limitation; its representativeness of typical image content should be discussed further. The ablation studies examining the contribution of GDN versus other nonlinearities, while mentioned, are not fully detailed in the main body, and their conclusive impact remains somewhat unclear.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Theoretical Analysis:** Elaborate on why the GDN architecture is particularly well-suited for compression. Is there a connection between the properties of GDN and the structure of natural image statistics that explains the performance gains? Can the rate-distortion trade-off be analyzed more formally?
*   **Experimental Rigor:** Conduct a more exhaustive parameter search for JPEG and JPEG 2000 to ensure a fair comparison. Consider including a user study to assess perceptual quality. Justify the choice of the ImageNet subset used for training. Provide more detailed ablation studies to quantify the contribution of each component of the proposed architecture (e.g., GDN vs. ReLU).
*   **Computational Complexity:** Discuss the computational cost of encoding and decoding compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000. While the authors mention efficiency, providing quantitative results would be valuable.
*   **Generalization:** Explore the performance of the method on different types of images beyond the Kodak dataset and those used in the main experiment. Consider medical images or satellite imagery.
*   **Entropy Coding:** Justify the choice of the specific entropy coding scheme (CABAC) and its implementation details. Are there alternative entropy coders that could further improve performance?
*   **Loss Function:** Explore other loss functions beyond MSE. A perceptual loss function, as suggested by the authors, could lead to even better visual quality.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the theoretical benefits of GDN for compression, beyond its ability to Gaussianize image densities?
2.  Have you performed a thorough parameter search for JPEG and JPEG 2000 to ensure a fair comparison? If not, how might this affect the reported results?
3.  What is the computational cost (encoding/decoding time, memory usage) of your method compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000?
4.  How sensitive is the performance of your method to the choice of the ImageNet subset used for training? Did you explore different subsets?
5.  Can you elaborate on the results of the ablation studies examining the contribution of GDN versus other nonlinearities? What specific configurations did you test, and what were the key findings?
6.  The paper mentions that using perceptual metrics for training might lead to further improvements. Have you experimented with perceptual loss functions during training, and if so, what were the results?
