## ICLR 2024 Review: END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

**1. Summary:**

This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework utilizing cascaded nonlinear transforms based on Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The authors move beyond traditional transform coding's separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy code, instead jointly optimizing these components using stochastic gradient descent. A key contribution is the use of a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization, and demonstrating feasibility with an actual entropy code, reporting achievable bitrates. The authors demonstrate improved visual quality compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, even when optimized for MSE, and provide thorough experimental analysis.  The work builds upon previous successes with GDN for density estimation and applies them to a practical, impactful problem.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions.  This paper presents a significant advance in image compression. The end-to-end optimization approach, coupled with the innovative use of GDN and the continuous relaxation for quantization, leads to demonstrable improvements in perceptual quality over established methods. The thorough experimental evaluation and clear presentation of results strongly support the claims made.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The end-to-end optimization of the entire compression pipeline is a significant departure from conventional methods. This holistic approach allows for interactions between components that were previously treated in isolation, leading to performance gains. The selection of GDN, motivated by its efficacy in Gaussianizing image densities, is well-justified and effective.
* **Technical Soundness:**  The mathematical formulation, particularly the continuous relaxation of quantization to enable gradient descent, is well-reasoned and addresses a critical challenge in optimizing discrete compression systems. The connection drawn to variational autoencoders is insightful, and the authors clearly articulate the differences.
* **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experimental results on the Kodak dataset and other image collections convincingly demonstrate the superior performance of the proposed method compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, particularly in terms of perceptual quality as measured by MS-SSIM. The authors’ commitment to reporting actual bitrates is commendable and crucial for evaluating practical applicability.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The authors effectively explain complex concepts and provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The inclusion of an extensive appendix with network architecture and optimization details strengthens the work.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Computational Cost:** While the paper acknowledges the high computational cost of training, a more detailed discussion of the training time, hardware used, and potential strategies for reducing computational burden would be valuable.  How does training scale with image resolution and dataset size?
* **Generalization to Other Datasets:** While the results on the test datasets are encouraging, exploring performance on more diverse image datasets (e.g., medical images, satellite imagery) would broaden the scope and demonstrate the robustness of the proposed method.
* **Complexity Analysis:**  A brief comparison of the computational complexity of the encoding and decoding processes of the proposed method with JPEG and JPEG 2000 would further strengthen the argument for its practical viability.
* **Ablation Study:** While the authors mention experimenting with simpler nonlinearities, a more systematic ablation study evaluating the contribution of each component (GDN, cascaded structure, end-to-end optimization) would provide valuable insights into the model's behavior.
* **Discussion on Choice of MSE:** The authors acknowledge that optimizing for perceptual metrics might yield further improvements. A more detailed discussion of the limitations of using MSE as a training objective, and potential challenges in implementing perceptual metrics for end-to-end optimization, would be beneficial.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Training Stability:** Did you encounter any challenges related to training stability (e.g., vanishing/exploding gradients) during the optimization process? If so, what techniques were used to mitigate these issues?
2.  **Entropy Coding Details:** Could you provide more details on the specific implementation of the CABAC entropy code, including the context modeling strategy employed and its impact on compression performance?  Specifically, how does it compare to the context modeling capabilities of JPEG2000?
3.  **Parameter Sensitivity:** How sensitive is the performance of the model to the choice of the regularization parameter λ?  Is there a 'sweet spot' for λ, or does performance degrade significantly outside a certain range?
4.  **Scalability:**  Considering the computational cost of training, how feasible would it be to scale this approach to larger image sizes (e.g., 4K or 8K resolution)?  Are there potential optimizations (e.g., distributed training, model parallelism) that could address this challenge?
5. **Future Work:** Beyond perceptual metrics, are there other loss functions or regularization techniques you plan to explore for further improving the performance of this compression scheme?



In conclusion, this is a well-executed and impactful work that addresses a fundamental problem in image processing. The authors have presented a compelling case for the benefits of end-to-end optimization and the effectiveness of their proposed framework. The minor suggestions above are intended to further strengthen the paper and enhance its contribution to the field.
