This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework leveraging cascaded nonlinear transformations built from convolutions and GDN layers, directly optimizing for rate-distortion trade-offs. The core contribution lies in moving beyond traditional, separately optimized transform coding pipelines to a fully learned, joint optimization approach, and demonstrating competitive, and often superior, performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, alongside significantly improved perceptual quality. The authors cleverly employ a continuous relaxation of the quantization step for optimization, drawing parallels to variational autoencoders, and importantly, evaluate performance using actual bit rates, solidifying its practical relevance.

**Reject.** While the approach is highly promising and introduces a genuinely novel direction in image compression, the experimental validation, while extensive, falls short of convincingly demonstrating a robust and generalizable advantage. The observed performance improvements are not consistently across all test images, and the dependence on the specific ImageNet pre-training and network architecture raises concerns about generalizability. The authors acknowledge this variation, but do not sufficiently address the underlying reasons or propose avenues for mitigation.

The core strength of the paper is its novelty. The idea of end-to-end optimization of the entire compression pipeline, including the nonlinear transform, is a significant departure from established methods and opens up exciting possibilities. The connection to variational autoencoders is insightful, and the use of GDN is well-motivated given its demonstrated effectiveness in density estimation.  The authors successfully navigate the challenge of optimizing through a quantization step by employing a continuous relaxation and demonstrate the feasibility of their approach with a working system. The presented results, especially the qualitative visual improvements, are compelling.  However, the reported quantitative improvements – while present – are not overwhelming. While MS-SSIM consistently shows gains, the PSNR results are more mixed. The claim of “substantial improvements” feels overstated given the variability.  

The experimental section feels somewhat descriptive rather than analytical. The authors present results across multiple images, but the analysis lacks depth. Why does the method perform better on some images than others? Are there image characteristics that correlate with the observed performance differences? A more thorough ablation study examining the impact of different architectural choices (e.g., number of layers, filter sizes) and regularization techniques could strengthen the findings. The sensitivity of the approach to the choice of λ, and how this relates to perceptual quality, deserves further investigation. The evaluation relies heavily on comparisons to JPEG and JPEG 2000; while valid, including comparisons to more recent learned compression techniques (e.g., those based on VQ-VAE) would provide a stronger benchmark. The limitations of the entropy code implemented are acknowledged, and the potential gains from a more advanced code are plausible, but this should be explicitly discussed as a future direction.

Here are some points that could improve the paper:

*   **Ablation Study:** Perform a more systematic ablation study to understand the contribution of different components of the architecture (e.g., GDN, number of stages).
*   **Generalization Analysis:** Investigate the robustness of the approach to different datasets and image types.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  Analyze the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly λ.
*   **Computational Cost:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of training and inference.
*   **Comparison to other learned compression methods:** Including comparisons to VQ-VAE and similar approaches would provide a more comprehensive benchmark.

I have several questions for the authors:

1.  You mention the choice of GDN being efficient. Was any attempt made to test other non-linearities besides ReLU? If so, were the results comparable?
2.  The training process relies heavily on the ImageNet dataset. How would you expect the performance to generalize to data significantly different from ImageNet (e.g., medical images, satellite imagery)?
3.  Could you elaborate on the choice of the specific hyperparameters used during training? Was any hyperparameter optimization performed?
4.  The rate-distortion curves show significant variation across images. Is there a way to predict, *a priori*, which images will benefit most from this approach?
5.  Regarding the continuous relaxation for optimization, could you quantify the error introduced by this approximation more precisely, beyond the observations in Section 4?
