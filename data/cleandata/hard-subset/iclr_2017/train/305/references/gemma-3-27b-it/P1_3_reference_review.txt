## Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

**1. Summary**

This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on cascaded nonlinear transforms, specifically employing Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The authors move beyond traditional transform coding's separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy code by jointly optimizing these components using stochastic gradient descent. A key innovation is using a continuous relaxation of the quantization step for optimization, allowing gradient flow. Importantly, the paper demonstrates performance using *actual* bitrates and an implemented entropy code, making it a complete compression method, and achieving visually improved results compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000. The connection to variational autoencoders is thoughtfully explored.

**2. Decision & Key Reasons**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of image compression. The core idea of end-to-end optimization of a nonlinear compression pipeline is compelling, and the experimental results demonstrate clear visual and quantitative advantages over established methods. The theoretical grounding connecting the approach to variational autoencoders further strengthens the work.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The end-to-end optimization approach is a substantial departure from traditional image compression pipelines. The use of GDN, motivated by neuroscience, is a clever choice, and the demonstrated improvements in visual quality are particularly noteworthy.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method against JPEG and JPEG 2000 on a standard dataset (Kodak) and additional images. The results consistently show improvements in perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) and competitive rate-distortion performance. The inclusion of actual bitrate performance and a functional entropy code is a major strength, moving beyond theoretical approximations.
*   **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The connection to variational autoencoders provides a solid theoretical grounding for the approach. The authors carefully address the challenges of optimization with quantization and propose a sensible continuous relaxation strategy. The discussion of the limitations and differences between the two approaches is also appreciated.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and the experimental setup. The inclusion of an appendix with detailed implementation details is helpful.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement)**

*   **Ablation Studies:** While the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the full pipeline, it would be valuable to include ablation studies to understand the contribution of individual components (e.g., GDN vs. other nonlinearities). This would help to clarify the importance of each design choice.
*   **Computational Cost:** The paper mentions the high computational cost of training. Providing some quantitative information about training time and resource requirements would be useful.
*   **Entropy Code Details:** While the entropy code is implemented and used, a more detailed description of its performance (e.g., compression ratio achieved) and comparison to state-of-the-art entropy coders (beyond CABAC) would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discussing the sensitivity of the method to hyperparameters (e.g., λ, network architecture) would provide further insights into its robustness and practical applicability.
*   **Explain the choice of uniform noise:** While explained well, highlighting the trade offs in using uniform noise versus other noise distributions (e.g. Gaussian) might be useful.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

*   The paper discusses the equivalence to variational autoencoders. Does the performance saturate at some point, as the λ approaches infinity, and is it possible to obtain lossless compression with this method?
*   The experiments primarily use MSE as the distortion metric during training. Do you anticipate that training with a perceptual metric (as in your previous work) would lead to further improvements in visual quality, and if so, are there any challenges associated with using such a metric for end-to-end optimization?
*   The appendix details the optimization process. Were any techniques used to prevent overfitting, beyond the regularization provided by the rate-distortion objective?
*   Could you elaborate on the choice of the piecewise linear function for approximating the marginals *pỹi*? What impact does the number of sampling points have on performance and computational cost?
*   The authors claim qualitatively superior results. While MS-SSIM supports this, is there a plan to conduct a user study to objectively measure perceived image quality?



In conclusion, this paper presents a well-motivated, technically sound, and experimentally validated contribution to the field of image compression. The minor revisions suggested above would further strengthen the paper and solidify its impact.
