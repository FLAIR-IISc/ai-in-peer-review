This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework utilizing cascaded nonlinear transformations, specifically Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN), coupled with uniform scalar quantization and an approximate inverse transform. The authors aim to overcome limitations of traditional transform coding by jointly optimizing all components – analysis transform, synthesis transform, and entropy coding – for a given rate-distortion trade-off. The key innovation lies in utilizing stochastic gradient descent to train these components, circumventing the non-differentiability of the quantization step with a continuous relaxation via additive uniform noise. They demonstrate improvements in visual quality and rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 on the Kodak dataset.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a technically sophisticated approach, the gains demonstrated over established methods (JPEG, JPEG 2000) are not sufficiently compelling, particularly given the increased complexity and computational cost. The empirical results, while promising, lack robustness across diverse datasets and the benefits aren’t clearly established as exceeding the engineering efforts needed to implement this significantly more complex system.

The primary concern revolves around the practical significance of the reported improvements. While the authors demonstrate visually improved results and some PSNR/MS-SSIM gains, these improvements are presented somewhat selectively. The authors acknowledge variability in performance across images, and the provided rate-distortion curves, even with the accompanying online resources, don’t consistently showcase substantial advantages. The claim of "substantial improvements" feels overstated given the observed data. Moreover, the computational cost of *training* the model is substantial, and while encoding/decoding is claimed to be efficient, the paper doesn’t provide concrete benchmarks quantifying this efficiency relative to highly optimized JPEG/JPEG 2000 encoders/decoders. This omission is critical, as practical compression solutions must be efficient in both training and inference.

A second significant issue lies in the heavy reliance on a continuous relaxation of the quantization process for optimization. While a standard technique, the paper's justification for its effectiveness relies on arguments about approximating quantization error with uniform noise, and the assumption that the relaxed entropy provides a good proxy for the actual entropy. While the authors attempt to validate this assumption empirically, the validation is limited. The bias observed in the relaxed entropy estimation, even if diminishing at finer quantization levels, introduces uncertainty about the true optimization target.

Finally, the connection drawn to Variational Autoencoders, while insightful, feels somewhat tangential and doesn't fundamentally strengthen the core contribution of the compression method. The authors themselves acknowledge the differences, and the analogy feels more like a framing device than a core methodological connection.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:** It would be beneficial to include ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component of the proposed framework. For instance, what performance is achieved by removing the GDN layers or using simpler nonlinearities?
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:**  Provide a detailed breakdown of the computational cost for training, encoding, and decoding. Compare this to the computational cost of JPEG/JPEG 2000.
*   **Robustness Assessment:** Evaluate the method on a wider range of datasets, including those with different characteristics (e.g., medical images, satellite imagery, scientific visualizations).
*   **Entropy Code Optimization:** Investigate more advanced entropy coding techniques beyond the described CABAC implementation to potentially further improve bit rates.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the model performance to different hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, number of stages, filter sizes).

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost associated with training, encoding, and decoding, and compare it directly to established methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the model to the choice of the continuous relaxation parameter (i.e., the width of the uniform noise distribution)?
3.  Have you explored alternative approaches to address the non-differentiability of the quantization step, such as straight-through estimators?
4.  Why was the MS-SSIM metric only applied to the luma component during evaluation, and how might including chroma information affect the reported results?
5.  Are there any known limitations or failure cases of the proposed method, and if so, what are they? Specifically, are there image types where performance degrades relative to JPEG/JPEG2000?
