- Presents an end-to-end optimized image compression framework using cascaded nonlinear transforms with GDN and joint analysis/synthesis optimization.  
- Introduces a continuous relaxation of quantization to enable gradient-based training.  
- Demonstrates superior perceptual quality and competitive or better MSE/PSNR rate-distortion performance vs. JPEG and JPEG 2000.  
- Establishes a theoretical connection between the framework and variational autoencoders.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Novelty: Moves beyond hand-engineered transforms to learned, jointly optimized transforms—significant advance.  
- Strong empirical results: Thorough evaluation (Kodak, standard images) with visual and MS-SSIM evidence, especially at low bitrates.  
- Theoretical grounding: Clear link to VAEs provides useful conceptual framing.  
- Practicality: Includes CABAC entropy coding and reports actual bitrates, showing end-to-end feasibility.  
- Request more details on computational cost (GPUs, training time) and strategies to reduce it (e.g., distillation/transfer learning).  
- Suggests a detailed ablation study to quantify contributions of GDN, stages, and architecture (e.g., replacing GDN with ReLU).  
- Recommends comparisons to other learned compression methods (e.g., VQ-VAE, autoregressive models) for a fuller benchmark.  
- Ask for sensitivity analysis of hyperparameters (learning rate, λ) and explanation of tuning procedures.  
- Appendix: optimization details should be clearer and more concise.  
- Questions: Have you tried optimizing with perceptual metrics and what challenges arose?  
- Questions: Is GDN’s efficiency consistent across datasets and architectures?  
- Questions: How sensitive is performance to the width of the uniform noise used in quantization relaxation?  
- Questions: Would using different distortion metrics (e.g., perceptual losses) change the model’s generative properties?  
- Questions: Provide more detail on CABAC limitations and potential gains from more sophisticated entropy coding.
