This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on cascaded nonlinear transformations, specifically utilizing Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The authors aim to overcome limitations of traditional transform coding by jointly optimizing the analysis and synthesis transforms directly for rate-distortion performance, using a continuous relaxation to enable gradient-based optimization in the presence of quantization. They demonstrate improved visual quality and rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 on standard image datasets.

**Reject.** While the idea of end-to-end optimized compression is appealing, the paper presents a substantial departure from established compression techniques without providing sufficiently compelling evidence of its practical advantages. The improvements over widely used standards like JPEG and JPEG 2000 are incremental and, crucially, appear highly dataset-dependent, as highlighted in the discussion section itself. The reliance on a continuous relaxation for optimization, while necessary, introduces approximations whose impact on the final compression performance is not fully justified, and the reported results do not overwhelmingly demonstrate the benefits outweigh the complexity.

The core issue lies with the lack of robust, comprehensive benchmarking. The authors primarily rely on visual inspection and comparisons on a limited set of datasets (Kodak, Lena, Barbara, etc.). The statement that performance “varies substantially across bit rates for different images” is concerning. While the online availability of test images is appreciated, a more systematic and statistically sound comparison is needed. The authors mention a varying performance advantage—sometimes better, sometimes worse than JPEG 2000—and do not thoroughly investigate *why* this occurs. The claims of substantial improvements in perceptual quality, supported by MS-SSIM, need further scrutiny. MS-SSIM, like other perceptual metrics, is known to have limitations and can be sensitive to specific image characteristics. A more rigorous perceptual evaluation, potentially including subjective testing, would strengthen the claims. 

Furthermore, the computational cost of training these models is likely to be significantly higher than the well-optimized implementations of JPEG and JPEG 2000. This aspect is only briefly acknowledged. A detailed analysis of the encoding/decoding speed and computational resources required for this method is essential to assess its practical viability. The authors mention efficient execution *after* training, but the cost to *get* to that point is a critical factor. The relationship to variational autoencoders is well-described, but the argument that the different optimization goals – rate distortion versus generative modeling – explain the performance differences feels somewhat hand-wavy and needs to be better substantiated.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to determine the contribution of each component of the proposed framework (e.g., GDN, cascaded structure). Is GDN truly essential, or would simpler nonlinearities suffice?
*   **Complexity Analysis:** Provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the encoding and decoding processes, as well as the training phase.
*   **Robustness Evaluation:** Evaluate the performance of the method on a wider range of image datasets, including those with different statistics and characteristics. Consider datasets beyond standard test images, like medical images or satellite imagery.
*   **Quantization Strategy:** Explore different quantization strategies beyond uniform scalar quantization. Could vector quantization or other techniques improve performance?
*   **Adaptive Entropy Coding:** Investigate more advanced entropy coding schemes than the basic CABAC implementation used in the experiments.
*   **Error Resilience:** Assess the robustness of the method to transmission errors or data corruption.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The performance seems highly variable across different image types. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of which image characteristics lead to better or worse compression performance with your method?
2.  What is the computational cost (in terms of training time and resources) to train a model for a specific rate-distortion trade-off?
3.  How does the performance of your method compare to state-of-the-art learned image compression methods (e.g., those based on VQ-VAE or other generative models)?
4.  You mention a relaxation of the quantization step using additive noise. How sensitive is the performance to the scale of this noise, and how was this scale chosen?
5.  Could you provide more details about the implementation of the entropy coding, specifically regarding the context modeling used in the CABAC scheme?
