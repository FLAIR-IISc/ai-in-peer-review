## ICLR 2017 Review: END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

**1. Summary of Claims:**

This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework. The authors move beyond traditional transform coding by learning a non-linear transform (built from cascaded convolutions and Generalized Divisive Normalization - GDN) jointly with quantization and entropy coding. By formulating the problem as a rate-distortion optimization, and employing a continuous relaxation to enable gradient-based training through the quantization step, they achieve visually superior compression performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, especially in reducing common artifacts. Crucially, the authors demonstrate practical bitrates using an implemented entropy code, showcasing a complete compression method.  The work also draws a connection between the proposed framework and Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), highlighting shared mathematical principles.

**2. Decision & Key Reasons:**

**Accept.** This paper presents a significant advancement in image compression. The approach is innovative, well-motivated, and supported by convincing experimental results demonstrating superior visual quality compared to established standards. The clarity of the paper, combined with the strong empirical evidence, makes it a valuable contribution to the field.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The paper tackles a challenging problem – optimizing the entire compression pipeline end-to-end.  The motivation stemming from limitations of traditional transform coding and inspiration from biological visual systems (GDN) is clearly articulated. The connection to VAEs provides a valuable theoretical framing.
* **Technical Soundness:** The use of GDN and the continuous relaxation trick to address the non-differentiability of the quantization step are clever and well-explained. The authors acknowledge the limitations of the relaxation and empirically demonstrate its validity. The details of the network architecture and optimization process are thoroughly described in the appendix.
* **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experimental results, comparing the proposed method to JPEG and JPEG 2000 on the Kodak dataset and other standard images, are compelling. The visual comparisons convincingly demonstrate the reduction of artifacts. Reporting actual bitrates, rather than relying solely on entropy estimates, strengthens the practical impact of the work.  The provided link to the test images is extremely valuable.
* **Thoroughness:** The authors address potential issues, like the bias in the relaxed entropy estimation and the relationship to generative models, with thoughtful discussion and analysis.  The inclusion of detailed implementation notes in the appendix is excellent.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention the high training cost, a more detailed discussion of the inference (encoding/decoding) computational complexity compared to JPEG/JPEG 2000 would be beneficial.  Specifically, how do the forward and inverse transforms scale with image size?
* **Ablation Studies:**  While the paper validates the complete framework, some ablation studies focusing on the contribution of specific components (e.g., GDN versus ReLU activations) would further strengthen the conclusions.
* **Perceptual Metric Justification:**  The authors acknowledge that MSE is not ideal.  While they opted for MSE for comparability, briefly discussing the challenges of a robust perceptual metric for color images is helpful.
* **Generalization to other data types**: The paper focuses primarily on images. Is the framework potentially applicable to other data types like video or audio? Any discussion of these potential extensions would be welcome.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the optimization:**  What sensitivity was observed to the initial values of the network parameters? Were different initializations explored, and if so, what was the effect on the final performance?
* **Regarding the continuous relaxation:**  How was the number of sampling points in the piecewise linear approximation of the relaxed density determined?  Was a sensitivity analysis performed to assess the impact of this parameter on the accuracy of the rate-distortion approximation?
* **Regarding computational cost:** Could you provide a rough estimate of the encoding/decoding time for a 512x512 image using your method compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 on a standard CPU?
* **Regarding future work:** The authors mention GDN/IGDN being more efficient. Can you elaborate on whether preliminary results with other activation functions have shown the need for a significantly larger network to achieve comparable performance?
