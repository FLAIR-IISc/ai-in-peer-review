This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on cascaded nonlinear transformations, specifically utilizing Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The approach aims to jointly optimize analysis and synthesis transforms for a desired rate-distortion trade-off, addressing limitations of traditional transform coding methods. The core innovation lies in using stochastic gradient descent with a continuous relaxation of the quantization step, and reporting performance using actual bit rates and extensive empirical comparisons against JPEG and JPEG 2000.

**Reject.** While the paper demonstrates promising results and introduces a conceptually interesting approach, the extensive experimental section doesn’t convincingly establish a consistent advantage over established methods, and the presentation lacks sufficient clarity in key aspects, hindering a full evaluation of the technical soundness.

The primary reason for rejection stems from the inconsistency in performance gains across different images, as acknowledged by the authors themselves. While qualitative examples show improvements, the summary of quantitative results (rate-distortion curves) is vague and relies heavily on directing the reader to an online resource. The dependence on an external resource for a comprehensive performance assessment is a significant weakness. Furthermore, the explanation for *why* the proposed method achieves visual improvements (smoother contours, less blocking) relies on qualitative descriptions of the transformations, lacking a deeper analysis of the learned representations. 

Secondly, the connection to variational autoencoders (VAEs), while discussed extensively, feels tenuous. The authors highlight differences, but the framing as an instantiation of a VAE doesn’t add substantial insight, and the benefits of the continuous relaxation are not clearly delineated beyond enabling gradient-based optimization. The discussion of the relationship to VAEs seems to primarily serve as justification for the chosen optimization strategy, rather than providing a novel perspective on either compression or generative modeling.

Supporting arguments:

*   **Inconsistent Performance:** The performance improvements aren’t universally observed, as the authors admit. The reliance on visual inspection of numerous images online, while helpful, doesn’t substitute for rigorous statistical analysis of the rate-distortion performance across a standardized dataset.
*   **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The contribution of GDN specifically isn't thoroughly investigated. While the authors mention preliminary results with other nonlinearities, a more detailed ablation study comparing different components of the architecture (e.g., different numbers of stages, filter sizes) would strengthen the argument for the proposed design choices.
*   **Clarity Issues:** The mathematical formulation, particularly the optimization section, is dense and difficult to follow. The notation is complex, and the flow of equations could be improved for better readability.
*   **Limited Novelty:** While end-to-end learning for compression isn’t entirely new, the specific application of GDN within this framework is a valid contribution. However, the paper doesn’t sufficiently highlight how this approach differs from and builds upon existing work in learned image compression.

Additional feedback:

*   **Quantitative Analysis:** Provide a more comprehensive quantitative analysis of the rate-distortion performance. Include average PSNR and MS-SSIM values across the test set for different bit rates, along with standard deviations.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to assess the impact of different components of the architecture (e.g., number of stages, filter sizes, nonlinearity choices).
*   **Representation Analysis:** Investigate the learned representations. Visualize the filter weights or activation maps to gain insights into what features the model is learning.
*   **Entropy Coding Details:** Provide more details about the entropy coding scheme, including the context modeling strategy and the performance gains achieved compared to a simpler entropy coder.
*   **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational cost of training and inference in more detail.
*   **Clarify VAE connection:** Refine the discussion regarding the VAE connection. If it doesn't offer substantial insights, consider removing or significantly streamlining it.

Questions for the authors:

*   Can you provide a more detailed statistical analysis of the rate-distortion performance, including average values and standard deviations across the test set?
*   How sensitive is the performance to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the learning rate and the number of training iterations?
*   What is the computational cost of training and inference, and how does it compare to other image compression methods?
*   Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of why the proposed method achieves visual improvements, beyond simply stating that it "smoothes contours"? Is there a quantifiable measure of smoothness or edge preservation that correlates with the perceptual quality?
*   Regarding the statement that the continuous relaxation provides a good approximation to the actual rate-distortion values, how was this empirically verified and are there scenarios where this approximation breaks down?
