## ICLR 2024 Review: END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

**1. Summary:**

This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on cascaded nonlinear transformations (Generalized Divisive Normalization - GDN) and uniform scalar quantization. The authors move beyond traditional transform coding’s separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy code, aiming for joint optimization via stochastic gradient descent.  A key innovation is using a continuous relaxation of the quantization step with additive uniform noise to enable gradient-based optimization, coupled with an actual entropy coding implementation for performance evaluation. The work demonstrates visually appealing results, exceeding JPEG and JPEG 2000 in perceptual quality, and achieving competitive or better rate-distortion performance, especially at lower bitrates. The authors also draw compelling parallels between their approach and variational autoencoders, highlighting the underlying principles of density estimation.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** The paper tackles a fundamental problem with a well-motivated and technically sound approach. The results are compelling, demonstrating clear improvements in perceptual quality over established methods and competitive rate-distortion performance.  The connection to variational autoencoders adds valuable theoretical context.  While the experimental evaluation is thorough, some clarity regarding implementation details and statistical significance would strengthen the work.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:**  The joint optimization of the entire compression pipeline is a significant departure from traditional methods and addresses a long-standing challenge in image compression. The biological inspiration from visual systems and the use of GDN are well-justified based on prior work.
* **Technical Soundness:** The continuous relaxation technique for handling quantization is a clever solution to enable gradient-based optimization.  The detailed description of the analysis and synthesis transforms, including the GDN and IGDN operations, is clear and allows for replication.  The explanation of the connection to variational autoencoders provides valuable insight.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The visual comparisons provided in the paper demonstrate a clear qualitative advantage over JPEG and JPEG 2000, particularly in terms of artifact reduction.  The rate-distortion curves and MS-SSIM results further support the claim of superior performance. The authors’ effort to evaluate with actual bitrates, not just entropy estimations, is commendable.
* **Thorough Comparison:** The comparison against JPEG and JPEG 2000 is well-executed, and the authors acknowledge the complexities of fair comparison (e.g., different implementations, chroma subsampling).

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Statistical Significance:** While the visual examples and rate-distortion curves are convincing, it would be beneficial to include some statistical analysis to confirm the significance of the improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000.  Reporting p-values or confidence intervals would strengthen the claims.
* **Implementation Details:**  More detail on the entropy coding implementation (specifically, the context modeling used in CABAC) would be helpful.  Was the context modeling specifically tailored for the output of the GDN transform?  How does it compare to the context modeling in state-of-the-art JPEG 2000 implementations?
* **Computational Cost:**  A discussion of the computational cost of training and encoding/decoding would be valuable.  While the authors mention efficiency of encoding/decoding *after* training, the training cost is substantial.  A comparison of training time and resource requirements with other deep learning-based compression methods would be useful.
* **Ablation Studies:**  While the authors discuss the benefits of GDN, an ablation study comparing the performance of the model with simpler nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU) or without any nonlinearity would provide further insight into the contribution of each component.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to the choice of the regularization parameter λ would be beneficial.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1. **Statistical Significance:**  Could you provide statistical analysis (e.g., p-values) demonstrating the significance of the performance improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000 across the tested image datasets?
2. **Entropy Coding Details:** Could you elaborate on the specific context modeling strategy employed in your CABAC implementation?  How was this strategy chosen and how does it compare to the context modeling approaches used in optimized JPEG 2000 encoders?
3. **Training Cost:** What were the computational resources (e.g., GPUs, training time) required to train the models presented in the paper?  How does this compare to training costs for other recent deep learning-based image compression methods?
4. **λ Sensitivity:**  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the regularization parameter λ? Did you perform a hyperparameter search to optimize λ for each image, or was a fixed value used? If a fixed value was used, how was it chosen?
5. **Generalization to other datasets:** Have you tested the generalization performance of the trained models on image datasets significantly different from ImageNet (e.g. medical images, satellite images)?



Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression. Addressing the suggested feedback would further strengthen the work and solidify its impact.
