## Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

**1. Summary:**

This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on cascaded nonlinear transformations, specifically utilizing Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The authors aim to overcome limitations of traditional transform coding by jointly optimizing the analysis/synthesis transforms directly for rate-distortion performance. They cleverly address the non-differentiability of the quantization step by employing a continuous relaxation using additive noise, and demonstrate competitive results against standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000, with visually superior image quality. The paper also establishes a connection between their approach and variational autoencoders, providing further theoretical grounding.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper addresses a challenging problem in a novel and theoretically sound manner. The empirical results demonstrate a clear advantage in perceptual quality compared to established codecs, and the connection to variational autoencoders provides valuable context.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The approach of end-to-end optimization of nonlinear transforms for image compression is a significant departure from traditional methods. The motivation – overcoming the limitations of separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy coder – is well articulated and grounded in the theoretical limitations of optimal quantization.
* **Technical Soundness:** The use of GDN, inspired by biological vision models, and the continuous relaxation via additive noise are well-justified and contribute to the technical strength of the paper. The connection to variational autoencoders, while not entirely novel, provides a useful framing and highlights the theoretical underpinnings of the method.
* **Empirical Results:** The comparison with JPEG and JPEG 2000, backed by visual examples and quantitative metrics (MSE, MS-SSIM, PSNR), convincingly demonstrates the improved perceptual quality of the proposed method.  The authors acknowledge limitations and provide comprehensive experimental details.  The availability of test images and code online is a significant positive aspect.
* **Clear Presentation:** Despite the technical complexity, the paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The explanation of the optimization procedure and the connection to variational autoencoders is relatively clear.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the paper mentions the high computational cost of training, a more detailed discussion of the training time and hardware requirements would be beneficial.  A brief comparison of the encoding/decoding speed with existing codecs would also be valuable.
* **Generalization & Robustness:** The paper focuses on the ImageNet dataset for training.  Exploring the performance on other datasets, potentially with different image statistics, could help assess the generalization ability of the method.
* **Ablation Studies:**  While the paper mentions preliminary results with alternative nonlinearities (ReLU, sigmoid), a more thorough ablation study exploring the impact of different components (e.g., number of stages, filter sizes, GDN parameters) on performance would strengthen the analysis.
* **Entropy Coding:** The description of the entropy coding scheme is somewhat brief. More details on its implementation and performance compared to state-of-the-art entropy coders would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Training Details:** Could you provide more specific details regarding the training time (e.g., hours/days on a specific hardware configuration) and the memory requirements for training the model?
2.  **Scaling with Resolution:** How well does the method scale to higher resolution images? Do the computational costs become prohibitive, and are there any strategies to mitigate this (e.g., patch-based processing)?
3. **Parameter Sensitivity**: What is the sensitivity of the method to the λ parameter? Does the optimal λ value vary significantly across different images or datasets? Is there a principled way to select λ automatically?
4. **Lossy Compression Variants**: The paper focuses on MSE as a distortion metric for training. Given the visual quality improvements, have you explored training with perceptual loss functions beyond MS-SSIM, and how do those impact performance?



In conclusion, this is a well-executed and impactful paper that presents a compelling approach to image compression.  Addressing the minor points raised above would further strengthen the contribution and make the paper even more valuable to the research community.
