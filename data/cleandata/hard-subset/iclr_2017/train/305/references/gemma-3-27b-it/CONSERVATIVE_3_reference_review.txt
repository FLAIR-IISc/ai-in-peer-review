This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on cascaded nonlinear transformations, specifically utilizing Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The authors aim to jointly optimize the analysis and synthesis transforms for a given rate-distortion trade-off, employing a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization. They demonstrate improved visual quality and competitive rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, despite optimizing for MSE rather than perceptual metrics.

**Reject.** While the paper tackles an important problem and proposes an interesting approach, the improvements over established methods are not convincingly demonstrated, and the methodology relies heavily on unproven assumptions and lacks sufficient rigor in its evaluation. The complexity of the proposed system is significant, and the demonstrated gains do not seem to justify this complexity.

The core issue lies in the extensive reliance on approximations and the limited validation against strong baselines. The use of a continuous relaxation of quantization, while necessary for optimization, introduces potential biases in the rate and distortion estimates. The authors acknowledge this but provide only limited empirical validation of the accuracy of these approximations.  The claim of visual improvement, though supported by examples, lacks a robust perceptual evaluation beyond MS-SSIM – a metric known to have limitations. More comprehensive perceptual studies, potentially involving human observers, are necessary to substantiate this claim.

Furthermore, the comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 feels somewhat superficial. The authors acknowledge the potential for further optimization within these established methods, but do not present a thorough comparison against highly tuned implementations. The choice of the OpenJPEG implementation and the specific settings used require justification – a more comprehensive evaluation against state-of-the-art encoders would be essential. The results shown in Figure 11 regarding rate distortion curves are confusing, and it is unclear which curves are being compared and how they were derived.

The paper also lacks clarity regarding computational cost. While the authors mention efficient encoding/decoding, the training costs are substantial. A detailed analysis of the computational resources required for training and the encoding/decoding speed would provide a more complete picture of the practical feasibility of this approach.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Quantify Approximation Error:** A more thorough analysis of the error introduced by the continuous relaxation of quantization is needed. How does this error vary with bit rate and image content?
*   **Perceptual Evaluation:** Supplement MS-SSIM with other perceptual metrics and consider conducting subjective visual quality assessments with human observers.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** Perform a more rigorous comparison against optimized JPEG and JPEG 2000 encoders. Explore different settings and parameter tuning to ensure a fair comparison.
*   **Computational Complexity:** Provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of training and encoding/decoding.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Investigate the contribution of different components of the model (e.g., GDN, cascaded structure) through ablation studies.
*   **Generalization:** Demonstrate the performance of the model on a wider range of datasets beyond the ImageNet subset.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of the method to the choice of the number of sampling points used in the piecewise linear approximation of the marginal distributions?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational resources required to train the model, including training time, memory usage, and GPU requirements?
3.  Have you explored alternative perceptual metrics beyond MS-SSIM, and how do those metrics compare to the subjective visual quality of the compressed images?
4.  What is the rationale behind fixing the exponents in the GDN transform? Did you explore optimizing these parameters as well?
5.  How does the performance of the method degrade when applied to images with significantly different characteristics than those present in the training set?
