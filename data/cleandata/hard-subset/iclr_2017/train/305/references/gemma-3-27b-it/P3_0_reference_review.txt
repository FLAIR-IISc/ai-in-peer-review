## ICLR 2024 Review: END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

**1. Summary:**

This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on cascaded nonlinear transforms, specifically employing Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN).  The authors move beyond traditional transform coding’s separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy code by jointly optimizing these components directly for rate-distortion performance using stochastic gradient descent. Crucially, they address the non-differentiability of the quantization step with a continuous relaxation using additive noise and demonstrate feasibility with a practical entropy coding implementation. The results show visually superior performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, particularly in terms of artifact reduction, while achieving competitive or better rate-distortion performance.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a significant advancement in image compression by demonstrating the power of end-to-end optimization of nonlinear transforms. The results are compelling, and the approach is well-motivated and clearly explained.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Importance:** The end-to-end optimization approach is a substantial departure from traditional image compression pipelines.  Addressing the quantization bottleneck with a clever continuous relaxation is a key contribution. The focus on visually plausible compression – mitigating artifacts – is particularly important.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The quantitative comparisons against JPEG and JPEG 2000, especially the visual examples and MS-SSIM scores, are convincing. Demonstrating performance *with* a practical entropy coding scheme solidifies the contribution. The thorough evaluation across diverse images strengthens the claims.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The connection drawn to variational autoencoders provides a valuable theoretical perspective and helps contextualize the work within the broader landscape of generative modeling.  The discussion of the differences and similarities is insightful.
* **Clear and Well-Written:**  The paper is remarkably well-written and clearly explains complex concepts. The detailed explanation of the network architecture, optimization procedure, and entropy coding scheme is commendable. The appendix provides necessary details without cluttering the main text.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

* **Computational Cost Discussion:** While the authors briefly mention training costs, a more thorough discussion of the computational resources required for training (GPU hours, memory requirements) would be valuable for reproducibility and to understand the practicality of scaling this approach to larger datasets or more complex architectures.
* **Ablation Studies:**  While the paper argues for the benefit of GDN, a more formal ablation study comparing GDN to simpler nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU) with comparable model capacity would further strengthen the argument. Showing the trade-off between model complexity and performance would be beneficial.
* **Adaptive Entropy Coding:** The authors acknowledge limitations in their entropy coding. Discussing potential improvements – for example, exploring more sophisticated context modeling within the CABAC framework – could highlight avenues for future work.
* **Rate-Distortion Curves Analysis:** While individual image rate-distortion curves are available online, a more comprehensive statistical analysis across the image set (e.g., average curves, confidence intervals) would provide a more robust summary of the performance gains.
* **Generalizability to Video:** Briefly discuss the potential and challenges of extending this framework to video compression.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the continuous relaxation:**  You mention the bias in the relaxed entropy estimation diminishes with finer quantization.  Could you quantify this bias at different bit rates for a representative image, and perhaps explore strategies to correct for it during training?
* **Regarding network architecture choices:** What criteria were used to select the number of filters, layer depths, and downsampling factors? Did you perform any hyperparameter optimization beyond the adaptation of the learning rate, and if so, what were the results?
* **Regarding perceptual metrics:** The use of MSE for training is somewhat at odds with the emphasis on visual quality. Have you experimented with using a perceptual metric (like MS-SSIM or LPIPS) directly in the loss function, and what were the results?  Would this require a larger model capacity?
* **Regarding computational resources:**  Can you provide more specific details about the hardware used for training and the approximate training time for a given λ value?  This will help others assess the feasibility of replicating your results.
* **Regarding the IGDN:** The “approximate inverse” is a key element. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of parameters within the IGDN, and could this component be further optimized?



In conclusion, this is a high-quality paper that presents a compelling and innovative approach to image compression. The results are strong, the explanation is clear, and the potential impact is significant.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points outlined above.
