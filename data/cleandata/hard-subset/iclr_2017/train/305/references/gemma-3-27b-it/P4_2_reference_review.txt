This paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on cascaded nonlinear transformations, specifically utilizing Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The core contribution lies in jointly optimizing the analysis and synthesis transforms, alongside an entropy coding stage, directly for rate-distortion performance, using a continuous relaxation to enable gradient-based optimization through the quantization step. The method demonstrates promising results, achieving visually improved image quality compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 at comparable bitrates, and offers a compelling connection to variational autoencoders.

**Reject**

The paper, while presenting a conceptually interesting approach, suffers from a lack of clear comparative advantage demonstrated through rigorous and standardized evaluation. The extensive empirical analysis is hampered by an overly complex presentation of results and insufficient justification for design choices, making it difficult to assess the true novelty and impact of the proposed method.

The arguments for rejection are threefold: 1) The evaluation focuses heavily on visual assessment, without a clear, statistically significant, and standardized quantitative comparison. While perceptual quality is crucial, it needs to be backed up by objective metrics consistently across all methods. The presented rate-distortion curves across various images are presented in a way that is difficult to synthesize into a clear overall conclusion. 2) The paper extensively benchmarks against JPEG and JPEG 2000, but uses a relatively basic entropy coding scheme (CABAC) and lacks comparison to more recent and advanced compression algorithms (e.g. VVC, AV1). This limits the scope of the demonstrated improvement. 3) The architectural choices (number of layers, filter sizes, etc.) appear somewhat arbitrary and lack a thorough ablation study to justify their selection. The claims regarding the efficiency of GDN over other nonlinearities are asserted but not fully substantiated with sufficient empirical evidence beyond preliminary results.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Evaluation:** The authors should prioritize a clearer and more statistically sound evaluation. Consider using a standardized image dataset (beyond Kodak and the few other examples provided) and reporting average rate-distortion performance metrics with confidence intervals. A thorough comparison to state-of-the-art image compression algorithms is crucial. The current presentation of results relies heavily on visual comparisons, which are subjective and insufficient.
*   **Ablation Studies:**  Conduct more comprehensive ablation studies to assess the contribution of individual components, particularly the GDN layers and the specific architectural choices. How does performance change with different numbers of layers, filter sizes, or different nonlinear activation functions?
*   **Entropy Coding:** The performance of the entropy coding stage is a critical component of any compression algorithm. Investigating more sophisticated entropy coding techniques (beyond the relatively basic CABAC implementation) could lead to significant improvements.
*   **Theoretical Justification:**  While the connection to variational autoencoders is discussed, a more detailed theoretical analysis could further strengthen the paper. For example, can the rate-distortion trade-off be characterized more precisely?
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the results to the regularization parameter λ. How does performance vary with different values of λ?
*   **Computational Cost:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of training and encoding/decoding images. While the paper mentions efficiency at inference, the high training costs should also be acknowledged.

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  Why was CABAC chosen as the entropy coding scheme, and were other, potentially more efficient, schemes considered? If so, why were they not implemented?
2.  The paper mentions optimizing for MSE and achieving improved visual quality. Can the authors quantify the perceptual improvement (e.g., using metrics like LPIPS) and present a statistical analysis to support the claim of superior perceptual quality?
3.  The architectural choices appear somewhat ad-hoc. What systematic exploration of different network architectures was conducted, and what motivated the final design?
4.  How does the performance of the proposed method compare to more recent image compression standards like VVC or AV1?
5.  Can you elaborate on the specific implementation details of the continuous relaxation used for optimization, and how this approximation impacts the accuracy of the rate-distortion optimization? What steps were taken to minimize the error introduced by this approximation?
6. What are the limitations of the proposed approach, and what directions for future research could address these limitations?
