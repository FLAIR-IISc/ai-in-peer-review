

# Review: END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression framework that jointly optimizes the analysis transform, synthesis transform, and entropy code using stochastic gradient descent. The authors build upon their previous work using Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) as a nonlinear transform, extending it to a cascade of linear convolutions and nonlinearities. The key innovation is a continuous relaxation of the quantization step that enables gradient-based optimization, followed by implementation of a practical entropy code to report actual bit rates rather than entropy estimates. The paper claims their method achieves better rate-distortion performance than JPEG and JPEG 2000 while producing visually superior images with fewer artifacts.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The primary reason for rejection is the flawed evaluation methodology that fails to support the paper's strong claims about performance across all images and bit rates. While the approach is interesting and the technical development is sound, the evaluation does not provide sufficient evidence for the paper's main claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes several strong claims that are not fully supported by the evidence:

1. The paper claims "substantial improvements across all test images and bit rates" (abstract, conclusion), but Figure 15 in the appendix clearly shows their method underperforms JPEG 2000 at high bit rates for one image. The authors acknowledge this in the discussion but continue to make broad claims about universal improvement.

2. The evaluation methodology is problematic. The authors note that "rate-distortion behavior varies substantially across bit rates for different images" and that "there is no obvious or agreed-upon method for combining rate-distortion curves across images." Despite this, they present a single aggregated rate-distortion curve (Figure 7) that they claim represents "substantial improvement across all test images and bit rates." This aggregation is methodologically questionable because:
   - They average MSE and entropy values across images without justification
   - They don't explain how they selected points on the rate-distortion curve for aggregation
   - They acknowledge that different methods (JPEG vs JPEG 2000) use different ways to select points on the rate-distortion curve, but don't address this in their aggregation

3. The paper lacks a meaningful comparison with recent deep learning-based compression methods. While they briefly mention works by Gregor et al. (2016) and Toderici et al. (2016), they don't provide a detailed comparison that would help position their contribution relative to the current state of the art.

4. The paper claims to optimize for MSE but demonstrates perceptual improvements. While this is an interesting observation, the paper doesn't sufficiently explain why optimizing for MSE would lead to perceptual improvements, especially since the authors acknowledge in the introduction that perceptual metrics would be better for optimization.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

- Provide a more nuanced presentation of results that acknowledges cases where their method does not outperform JPEG 2000, rather than claiming universal improvement.
- Replace the single aggregated rate-distortion curve with either detailed per-image curves (as they've made available online) or a more standard evaluation approach like the one used in the VVC standard.
- Include a direct comparison with recent deep learning-based compression methods, particularly those that report actual bit rates (not just entropy estimates).
- Clarify the relationship between the continuous relaxation used for optimization and the actual discrete compression performance. The paper mentions this in section 3, but could provide more empirical evidence on the gap between the relaxed loss function and the actual bit rates achieved.
- Consider using a more standardized perceptual metric (like LPIPS) that is more aligned with human perception than MS-SSIM for their perceptual quality claims.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. How do you reconcile the claim that your method provides "substantial improvements across all test images and bit rates" with Figure 15 in the appendix, which shows underperformance at high bit rates for one image?

2. The paper acknowledges that "rate-distortion behavior varies substantially across bit rates for different images" and that "there is no obvious or agreed-upon method for combining rate-distortion curves across images." Given these challenges, why did you choose to aggregate results in the way you did?

3. How does your method compare to more recent deep learning-based compression methods like the one by Ballé et al. (2018) or the recent work by Toderici et al. (2016) that directly outputs binary representations?

The paper presents an interesting technical approach to image compression with end-to-end optimization, but the evaluation methodology undermines the strength of the claims. With improved evaluation and more careful presentation of results, this work could make a valuable contribution to the field.
