

# Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary
The paper introduces an end-to-end optimized image compression framework that jointly optimizes analysis and synthesis transforms, quantization, and entropy coding using a nonlinear transform architecture. The authors propose using cascades of linear convolutions followed by generalized divisive normalization (GDN) operations, which are inspired by biological visual systems and have proven effective in Gaussianizing image densities. They address the challenge of optimizing with quantization (which has zero gradients) through a continuous relaxation using additive uniform noise, and implement a proper entropy code to report actual bit rates rather than just entropy estimates. The method achieves better rate-distortion performance than JPEG and JPEG 2000, particularly at lower bit rates, and produces visually superior images with fewer artifacts. The paper is well-positioned within the literature on transform coding and variational autoencoders, with clear explanations of how their approach differs from previous work.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to image compression by demonstrating the effectiveness of end-to-end optimization with nonlinear transforms, with rigorous empirical validation and clear theoretical justification.

## 3. Supporting arguments
The paper's key contribution is well-motivated and addresses a fundamental limitation in traditional image compression: the separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy code components. The authors provide a compelling rationale for using nonlinear transforms (GDN) instead of traditional linear transforms, citing their effectiveness in Gaussianizing image statistics and reducing higher-order dependencies. The technical approach is sound, with a clear explanation of the continuous relaxation technique to handle quantization during optimization, which is a standard but well-executed technique in this domain.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and rigorous. The authors compare their method against JPEG and JPEG 2000 on standard datasets (Kodak, Lena, Barbara, etc.), reporting both quantitative metrics (PSNR, MS-SSIM) and qualitative visual results. Crucially, they implement a proper entropy code to report actual bit rates rather than relying on entropy estimates, which is essential for meaningful comparison to existing compression methods. The results consistently show improved rate-distortion performance at lower bit rates and superior visual quality across all tested images.

The paper also provides valuable insights into the relationship between their framework and variational autoencoders, clearly explaining the important differences that make their approach more suitable for compression than standard generative models. The discussion of limitations and future work is thoughtful and grounded in the results.

## 4. Additional feedback
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of computational complexity (both training and inference) compared to JPEG/JPEG 2000, which is important for practical deployment considerations.
- While the authors mention testing simpler nonlinearities (ReLU, sigmoid), they could provide more specific details about these ablation studies, particularly regarding the parameter efficiency of different activation functions.
- A clearer explanation of how the choice of λ (the rate-distortion trade-off parameter) affects performance across different images would be helpful for practical implementation.
- The paper could provide a more concise summary of the network architecture in the main text, with detailed specifications moved to the appendix.

## 5. Questions for authors
- The paper claims "substantial improvements" in MS-SSIM across all tested images and bit rates. Could the authors provide statistical significance testing to confirm these improvements are not due to chance?
- The authors mention increasing network capacity for larger λ values and RGB models. Could they clarify why these specific capacity increases were necessary for those particular cases?
- The paper states that "the perceptual advantages hold for all images tested." Could the authors identify which specific image features or content types benefit most from their approach, and whether there are any image types where the improvements are minimal?
