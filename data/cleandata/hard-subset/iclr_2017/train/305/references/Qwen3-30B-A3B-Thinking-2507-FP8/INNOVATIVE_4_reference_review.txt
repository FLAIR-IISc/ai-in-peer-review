

# Review for "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper presents a fundamentally new approach to image compression by introducing an end-to-end optimization framework that jointly optimizes the analysis transform, synthesis transform, and entropy coding. The authors propose a nonlinear transform architecture inspired by biological visual systems (using cascaded Generalized Divisive Normalization - GDN), which they optimize using a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable stochastic gradient descent. Their method achieves superior rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, with particularly striking improvements in perceptual quality as measured by MS-SSIM. The paper makes a compelling case that this approach not only outperforms existing methods but also produces visually more natural images, avoiding the blocking and ringing artifacts characteristic of linear transform coding.

## 2. Decision
Accept

Key reasons: (1) The paper introduces a truly novel approach to image compression that optimizes all components end-to-end, breaking from the traditional separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy code; (2) The biological inspiration behind the nonlinear transforms and the resulting visual improvements represent a significant conceptual advance in the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's core contribution is the end-to-end optimization framework, which is a bold departure from the standard practice in image compression. While previous work has explored nonlinear transforms (Ballé et al., 2015, 2016), this paper is the first to demonstrate that a complete compression pipeline can be optimized end-to-end using stochastic gradient descent, with the continuous relaxation of quantization being the key technical innovation.

The novelty extends beyond the technical implementation to the conceptual framework. The authors successfully bridge the gap between rate-distortion theory and deep learning, demonstrating that a model trained with MSE can still produce perceptually superior results—a counterintuitive finding that challenges conventional wisdom in the field. The visual examples in Figure 5 and Figure 6 are particularly compelling, showing how their method preserves contours and edges while eliminating artifacts common in JPEG and JPEG 2000.

The paper's claims are well-supported by empirical evidence. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements across multiple datasets and metrics (PSNR and MS-SSIM), and they provide a thorough comparison to standard baselines. The theoretical justification for their continuous relaxation approach is sound and well-motivated, with clear connections to variational autoencoders while highlighting important differences.

The paper's contribution is significant not just as a technical improvement to image compression, but as a new paradigm that could influence other areas of signal processing and representation learning. The authors' insight that "the cascade of biologically-inspired nonlinear transformations" captures the statistics of natural images in a way that benefits compression is particularly profound.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, consider the following:

1. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how the GDN parameters differ from standard neural network activation functions. While the paper mentions GDN is "more efficient for compression," a more detailed comparison with standard ReLU or sigmoidal nonlinearities would strengthen the argument for the biological inspiration.

2. The authors mention that "preliminary results indicate that qualitatively similar results are achievable with other activation functions," but this is presented as a minor point. A brief ablation study comparing GDN with standard nonlinearities would provide stronger evidence for the claimed efficiency advantage.

3. The paper could better clarify the computational cost of the proposed method. While the authors mention that "encoding or decoding an image with the trained models is efficient," a more precise comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of encoding/decoding time would be valuable for practical adoption.

4. The discussion of the relationship to variational autoencoders (Section 3.1) is excellent, but it might be helpful to explicitly state how this work could influence future generative modeling approaches, particularly in terms of handling discrete representations.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that "the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested, and at all bit rates." Could you elaborate on whether there are specific image categories or features where the advantage is particularly pronounced, or if there are any image types where the method performs less well than JPEG 2000?

2. The authors mention that "GDN represents a multivariate generalization of a particular type of sigmoidal function." Could you provide a more formal connection to universal approximation theorems for neural networks, particularly regarding why this multivariate generalization might lead to better compression efficiency?

3. The paper notes that "the performance gains compared to a well-designed non-adaptive entropy code are relatively small." Given that the authors implemented a simple CABAC-based entropy code, how much of the rate-distortion improvement would be lost if a more sophisticated entropy code (like those used in JPEG 2000) were employed?
