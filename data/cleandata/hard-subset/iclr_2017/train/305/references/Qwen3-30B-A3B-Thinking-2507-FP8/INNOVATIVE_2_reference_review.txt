

# Review for "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel framework for end-to-end optimization of image compression using nonlinear transforms. The authors introduce a complete compression pipeline that jointly optimizes the analysis transform, synthesis transform, and entropy coding through a continuous relaxation of the quantization step. Their key innovation is using generalized divisive normalization (GDN) with cascaded nonlinearities instead of traditional linear transforms, which enables better representation of image features and leads to visually superior results compared to standard methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The approach is grounded in biological visual processing models and provides a principled way to optimize the entire compression pipeline rather than its components separately. The authors demonstrate both theoretical justification for their continuous relaxation approach and strong empirical results showing improved rate-distortion performance and perceptual quality across multiple datasets.

## 2. Decision

Accept. This paper presents a significant conceptual advance in image compression that moves beyond traditional transform coding approaches. The end-to-end optimization framework with nonlinear transforms represents a bold new direction that is both theoretically grounded and empirically validated.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's contribution is genuinely novel in the context of image compression. While prior work has explored nonlinear image representations (Ballé et al., 2015, 2016), this paper is the first to demonstrate how to optimize such representations end-to-end for rate-distortion performance, with a continuous relaxation enabling gradient-based optimization. This represents a fundamental shift from the standard practice of separately optimizing transform, quantizer, and entropy code components.

The empirical results are compelling: the method achieves better PSNR than JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most images and bit rates, and crucially, demonstrates substantially improved visual quality across the board. The authors provide a clear explanation for why their approach produces better visual results (avoiding the blocking and ringing artifacts common in linear transform coding) and validate this with perceptual metrics like MS-SSIM. The paper's key insight—using nonlinear transformations to better capture the statistical structure of natural images—resonates with recent advances in deep learning for representation learning.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, clearly distinguishing its approach from variational autoencoders and other generative models, while acknowledging the relationship to prior work on nonlinear image representations. The theoretical justification for the continuous relaxation of quantization is sound, and the authors provide empirical evidence that the approximation is valid.

While the paper has some minor limitations (e.g., the entropy coding is relatively simple, and the authors note they're exploring simpler nonlinearities), these are minor compared to the significance of the core contribution. The work represents a major step forward in image compression, not just an incremental improvement.

## 4. Additional Feedback

The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of computational complexity during encoding/decoding compared to standard methods. While the authors mention that "encoding or decoding an image with the trained models is efficient," a brief comparison of inference times would strengthen the practical relevance.

The authors should consider addressing whether their approach could be extended to video compression, which would significantly broaden its impact. The current focus on still images is understandable, but the framework might naturally extend to video.

The discussion of the relationship to biological visual systems could be slightly more detailed to better connect the model's structure to the underlying neuroscience.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper mentions that "rectified linear units generally require a substantially larger number of model parameters/stages to achieve the same rate-distortion performance as the GDN/IGDN nonlinearities." Could you provide more quantitative comparison of the parameter efficiency between GDN and ReLU-based approaches?

2. The authors state that their method "exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images." Could you elaborate on the specific characteristics of the images where their method underperforms, and whether these are related to particular image content or compression challenges?

3. Given the strong visual improvements observed, could you discuss potential extensions to optimize for perceptual metrics directly (rather than MSE), and how this might further improve results?
