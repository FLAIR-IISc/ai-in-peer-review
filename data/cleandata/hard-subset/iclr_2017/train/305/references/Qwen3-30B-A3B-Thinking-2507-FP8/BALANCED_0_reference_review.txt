

# Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper presents a framework for end-to-end optimization of image compression using nonlinear transforms. The authors build upon their prior work (Ballé et al., 2015, 2016) by introducing a cascaded transformation architecture using generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and its inverse (IGDN), followed by uniform scalar quantization. The key contribution is a method to jointly optimize analysis and synthesis transforms along with quantization parameters through a continuous relaxation of the quantization step, enabling the use of stochastic gradient descent. The authors demonstrate improved rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, with visually superior compressed images that exhibit fewer blocking artifacts and better preservation of contours.

## 2. Decision
Reject. While the paper presents a reasonable extension of prior work and demonstrates some improvements over traditional compression methods, the novelty is limited and the evaluation methodology has significant shortcomings that undermine the main claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main weaknesses are:

First, the novelty is incremental rather than transformative. The authors build directly on their own previous work (Ballé et al., 2015, 2016), and the improvements over their prior methods are marginal. The paper fails to sufficiently distinguish itself from recent deep learning-based compression approaches, such as those by Toderici et al. (2016) and Agustsson et al. (2017), which also optimize for rate-distortion with end-to-end learning. The connection to variational autoencoders (Section 3.1) is interesting but doesn't provide a fundamentally new approach.

Second, the evaluation methodology is problematic. The authors acknowledge in the appendix that their entropy coding implementation (CABAC with limited spatial adaptation) is significantly less sophisticated than JPEG 2000's entropy coding, which "likely smaller than those achieved by the entropy code in JPEG 2000." This means the reported bit rate improvements (20-30% at low bit rates) are likely overestimated compared to a fair comparison. The paper claims to use "actual bit rates" but doesn't provide a fair comparison to the most sophisticated entropy coding methods used in modern compression standards.

Third, the paper doesn't adequately compare to the most recent state-of-the-art. The comparison is limited to JPEG and JPEG 2000, but recent works (e.g., BPG, WebP, and the 2017 ICLR paper by Agustsson et al.) have already achieved substantially better compression than JPEG 2000. Without comparison to these, the claimed improvements appear modest in the context of current research.

Fourth, the paper lacks sufficient analysis of why the GDN/IGDN transformations are necessary. The authors note that "preliminary results indicate that qualitatively similar results are achievable with other activation functions" but require "a substantially larger number of model parameters/stages." This suggests the GDN/IGDN may not be the most efficient choice, but the paper doesn't provide a thorough ablation study to quantify this.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen this paper for future publication, I recommend:

1. Conducting a more comprehensive comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art image compression methods (e.g., BPG, WebP, and Agustsson et al.'s "Learned Image Compression" from ICLR 2017). This is essential to demonstrate whether the proposed method represents a meaningful advancement over current research.

2. Implementing a more sophisticated entropy coding scheme that matches or exceeds the sophistication of JPEG 2000's coding. The authors acknowledge their entropy code is limited (Appendix 6.2), so improving this would significantly strengthen the evaluation.

3. Performing more rigorous ablation studies to determine how much of the performance gain comes from the specific GDN/IGDN transformations versus the general flexibility of the nonlinear transform architecture. This would help clarify whether the biological inspiration is actually beneficial or if simpler nonlinearities would suffice.

4. Providing a clearer explanation of how this work differs from and improves upon the recent work by Toderici et al. (2016) and Agustsson et al. (2017), which also optimize for rate-distortion with end-to-end learning.

## Questions for Authors
1. How does your entropy coding implementation compare to the entropy coding used in JPEG 2000 (e.g., in OpenJPEG)? What specific limitations of your implementation are causing the relatively small bit rate gains compared to JPEG 2000?

2. Can you provide a more rigorous comparison to recent deep learning-based compression methods (e.g., Agustsson et al., "Learned Image Compression," ICLR 2017), which have already demonstrated significant improvements over JPEG 2000?

3. What evidence do you have that the specific GDN/IGDN nonlinearities are necessary for the performance gains, rather than just using any nonlinear activation function (e.g., ReLU) with more parameters? How much more capacity would be required for ReLU to achieve similar performance?
