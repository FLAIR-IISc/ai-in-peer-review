

# Peer Review: END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel framework for end-to-end optimized image compression based on nonlinear transforms. The authors develop a model that jointly optimizes the analysis transform, synthesis transform, and entropy code to minimize the rate-distortion tradeoff, rather than optimizing these components separately as is common in traditional methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The key innovation is their use of a generalized divisive normalization (GDN) as the nonlinear transformation, which they have previously shown to be highly effective in Gaussianizing image densities. To enable gradient-based optimization despite the non-differentiable quantization step, they introduce a continuous relaxation using additive uniform noise, which they empirically validate in Figure 4. The authors demonstrate that their method achieves better rate-distortion performance than JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most images and bit rates, with significantly improved visual quality that avoids the blocking and ringing artifacts common in linear transform coding methods. The paper provides a complete solution by implementing an actual entropy code and reporting performance using actual bit rates, making it directly comparable to existing image coding methods.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper addresses a fundamental problem in image compression with a well-motivated, scientifically rigorous approach that represents a significant advance over traditional methods.
2. The empirical results are comprehensive, well-validated, and provide clear evidence of both quantitative and qualitative improvements over established baselines.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of image compression by providing a complete, end-to-end optimized solution that addresses a long-standing limitation of traditional transform coding methods. The authors have clearly articulated the problem: traditional methods optimize transform, quantizer, and entropy code separately, which is suboptimal. They then present a well-motivated solution using nonlinear transforms with GDN, which they have previously shown to be effective in Gaussianizing image densities.

The technical approach is sound and well-explained. The use of continuous relaxation for optimization (replacing quantization with additive uniform noise) is a clever solution to the non-differentiable quantization problem, and the authors provide empirical validation of this approximation in Figure 4. The implementation details, including the entropy coding using CABAC, are thorough and demonstrate a complete solution rather than just a partial component.

The empirical results are particularly compelling. The paper provides both quantitative metrics (PSNR and MS-SSIM) and qualitative visual examples (Figures 5-7) showing substantial improvements across all tested images and bit rates. The comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 is fair and thorough, with the authors implementing a simple entropy code (CABAC) for fair comparison rather than just comparing to entropy estimates. The paper also provides a thoughtful discussion of how their approach differs from related work on variational autoencoders and generative models, which helps position their contribution clearly in the literature.

The paper's contributions are significant and well-supported, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at ICLR.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend the following improvements:

1. **Clarify the relationship between GDN and compression performance**: While the paper mentions that GDN is more efficient than other nonlinearities, it would benefit from a more detailed explanation of why GDN specifically provides better compression performance. A brief theoretical discussion of how the GDN parameters relate to the rate-distortion tradeoff would be valuable.

2. **Include comparative results with recent methods**: The paper compares to JPEG and JPEG 2000, but does not include comparisons to more recent compression methods like WebP, BPG, or the work by Toderici et al. (2016). Including these comparisons would strengthen the paper's position in the current landscape of image compression.

3. **Provide more details about the training process**: The paper mentions they used ImageNet for training, but doesn't provide specifics about the training time, computational resources required, or how many iterations were needed for convergence. This information would help readers understand the practicality of the approach.

4. **Clarify the single representation vs. multiple λ points**: The paper states "a single representation is sought for all rate points" in the discussion, but earlier mentions optimizing separately for each λ. A clearer explanation of how they handle different rate points would improve the paper.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper claims that GDN is more efficient than other nonlinearities (like ReLU), but doesn't provide quantitative comparisons. Could you include a table comparing GDN with ReLU or sigmoidal nonlinearities on the same dataset, showing both rate-distortion performance and visual quality?

2. The paper states that the method was optimized for MSE, yet achieves better perceptual quality. Could you provide more insight into why optimizing for MSE leads to better perceptual quality, rather than the other way around?

3. The paper mentions that they've tested models with simpler nonlinearities but doesn't provide results. Could you share these results in the supplementary materials to demonstrate how much of the performance gain is due to GDN specifically?

4. The paper states that their method "exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images." Could you provide more detail about which images underperform compared to JPEG 2000, and why?

5. The paper uses a simple CABAC implementation, but acknowledges that it doesn't fully exploit spatial adaptation. How much of the performance gap between the authors' method and JPEG 2000 would be closed by implementing a more sophisticated entropy code?
