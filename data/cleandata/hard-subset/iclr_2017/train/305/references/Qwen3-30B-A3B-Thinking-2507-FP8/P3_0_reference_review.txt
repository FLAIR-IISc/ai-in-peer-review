

# Peer Review: "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a significant advancement in the field of image compression by introducing an end-to-end optimized framework that jointly optimizes the analysis transform, synthesis transform, and quantization for rate-distortion performance. The authors propose a nonlinear transform coding approach using cascaded generalized divisive normalization (GDN) operations inspired by biological visual systems, which they demonstrate is more effective at modeling natural image statistics than traditional linear transform methods. The key innovation lies in their approach to handling quantization during optimization through a continuous relaxation (adding uniform noise), which enables the use of stochastic gradient descent. The paper provides a thorough theoretical foundation connecting their approach to rate-distortion theory and variational autoencoders, while clearly distinguishing their method from generative modeling approaches. The experimental results demonstrate substantial improvements in both rate-distortion performance (measured by PSNR) and perceptual quality (measured by MS-SSIM) over JPEG and JPEG 2000 across a variety of images and bit rates, with visually superior compression artifacts.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**. This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of image compression with a well-motivated approach that addresses a fundamental limitation in traditional compression methods. The key reasons for acceptance are:

1. The paper presents a novel end-to-end optimization framework for image compression that successfully addresses the longstanding challenge of jointly optimizing all components (transform, quantizer, entropy code) rather than optimizing them separately.
2. The experimental results are robust, well-designed, and demonstrate clear improvements over standard compression methods across multiple metrics and image datasets, with particularly compelling perceptual improvements that go beyond traditional PSNR metrics.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a compelling theoretical foundation for their approach, clearly explaining how they handle the intractability of quantization during optimization through a continuous relaxation. The authors make a careful distinction between their method and variational autoencoders, addressing potential misunderstandings in the literature. Their explanation of why the continuous relaxation works (both in terms of entropy approximation and distortion modeling) is well-supported by their empirical results in Section 4. This theoretical grounding is particularly strong for a paper that makes a practical contribution to image compression.

**Technical Innovation**: The use of GDN as a nonlinear transform is well-justified by previous work on Gaussianizing image densities, and the authors clearly demonstrate its advantages over linear transforms. The cascade of GDN operations followed by uniform scalar quantization effectively implements a parametric vector quantizer, which is a clever approach to handling the high-dimensional quantization problem. The paper's approach to optimizing the entire compression pipeline end-to-end is technically sophisticated and represents a significant step forward from traditional transform coding methods.

**Experimental Rigor**: The evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors:
- Compare to standard baselines (JPEG, JPEG 2000) using standard test sets (Kodak, Lena, etc.)
- Report both PSNR and MS-SSIM metrics to capture both objective and perceptual quality
- Provide extensive visual examples that clearly demonstrate the qualitative improvements
- Implement a proper entropy code (CABAC) rather than relying on entropy estimates
- Make all test images and results available online for independent verification

The results are particularly impressive because the authors optimize for MSE (a traditional metric), yet achieve substantially better perceptual quality than JPEG and JPEG 2000. This suggests their method has learned to better capture image statistics that align with human perception, which is a significant insight.

**Novelty and Impact**: While the idea of using deep learning for image compression has been explored previously (e.g., by Toderici et al., 2016), this paper makes a distinct contribution by focusing on end-to-end optimization of the entire compression pipeline rather than using a generative model approach. The authors clearly articulate how their method differs from related work and why their approach is better suited for compression. The potential for deployment in embedded systems (due to the efficiency of GDN/IGDN transforms) is an important practical consideration that adds to the paper's impact.

## 4. Additional Feedback

**Strengths to Highlight**:
- The paper's careful distinction between rate-distortion optimization and generative modeling is crucial for the field and well-executed.
- The visual examples in the paper (Figures 5-7) are exceptionally strong evidence for the perceptual improvements.
- The authors' transparency in making all test images and results publicly available is commendable and increases the paper's credibility.

**Areas for Improvement**:
1. **Theoretical Justification for GDN**: While the paper cites previous work showing GDN's effectiveness for density estimation, a more detailed explanation of why GDN specifically is advantageous for compression (beyond just being good at Gaussianizing densities) would strengthen the theoretical foundation. Why does GDN outperform other nonlinearities like ReLU or sigmoid for compression?

2. **Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper references Toderici et al. (2016) but could more fully compare with other recent deep learning-based compression methods (e.g., the work by Agustsson et al. at ICCV 2017, or the work by Minnen et al. at ICML 2018). A brief comparison would help situate the contribution more precisely in the current landscape.

3. **Computational Complexity**: While the paper mentions that encoding/decoding is efficient, a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 (including inference time and memory requirements) would be valuable for assessing practical deployment potential.

4. **Ablation Study on GDN**: The paper mentions that preliminary experiments with other nonlinearities (ReLU, sigmoid) were conducted, but a more thorough ablation study comparing different nonlinearities would help clarify how much of the performance gain is specifically due to GDN versus the use of nonlinear transformations in general.

5. **Rate-Distortion Curve Interpretation**: The paper wisely notes that summary rate-distortion curves across images can be misleading, but the discussion could be strengthened by providing a more systematic way to present the results that captures the image-dependent nature of compression performance.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. In Figure 6, you show the progression of compression artifacts at different bit rates. Could you elaborate on why your method appears to "progressively simplify contours" rather than introducing artifacts that reflect the underlying basis functions (as in JPEG/JPEG 2000)? Is this related to the specific properties of the GDN transform?

2. The paper states that "the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested, and at all bit rates." Could you clarify whether this includes very high bit rates where JPEG 2000 typically performs well? Are there any images or bit rate ranges where the improvement is particularly pronounced or less pronounced?

3. Regarding the continuous relaxation approach, could you provide more details about how the bias in the entropy approximation (mentioned in Section 4) might affect the final compression performance? Would the bias be different for different quantization levels?

4. The paper mentions that "GDN represents a multivariate generalization of a particular type of sigmoidal function." Could you elaborate on how this multivariate property contributes to the efficiency of the compression, beyond what would be achieved with a simple pointwise nonlinearity?

5. The authors note that "the visual improvement arises because the cascade of biologically-inspired nonlinear transformations in the model have been optimized to capture the features and attributes of images that are represented in the statistics of the data." Could you provide a more specific explanation of how the GDN layers learn to capture these statistics, perhaps through visualizing the filters or activation patterns?

This paper represents a significant advance in image compression research, with clear theoretical grounding, strong empirical results, and potential for real-world impact. The authors have made an important contribution to the field by demonstrating that end-to-end optimization of the entire compression pipeline can yield substantial improvements over traditional methods, and their approach has clear potential for further development and application.
