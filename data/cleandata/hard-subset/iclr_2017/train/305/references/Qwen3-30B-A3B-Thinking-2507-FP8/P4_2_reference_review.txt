

# Review for "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contribution
The paper presents a novel framework for end-to-end optimization of image compression using nonlinear transforms. The authors propose a model consisting of cascaded linear convolutions followed by generalized divisive normalization (GDN) operations for the analysis transform, and an inverse GDN (IGDN) for the synthesis transform. The approach jointly optimizes the transforms, quantization, and entropy coding parameters to minimize a rate-distortion objective. The key contribution is demonstrating that an end-to-end trainable compression model can outperform traditional methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000 in both rate-distortion performance (measured by PSNR) and perceptual quality (measured by MS-SSIM), while maintaining the feasibility of a complete lossy compression method with actual bit rates.

## 2. Decision
Accept.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision
The paper makes a strong case for its approach based on three key aspects:

First, the problem addressed is well-defined and significant. The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional transform coding methods (like JPEG and JPEG 2000) which separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy code components. The authors demonstrate how their end-to-end framework overcomes these limitations by jointly optimizing all components.

Second, the approach is well-motivated and properly situated in the literature. The paper provides a thorough review of related work on transform coding, neural network-based image compression, and connections to variational autoencoders. Crucially, the authors clearly distinguish their approach from generative models, explaining how their rate-distortion optimization differs from typical variational inference objectives. The theoretical foundation for their continuous relaxation of quantization is sound and well-justified.

Third, the claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence. The paper provides detailed experimental results comparing against JPEG and JPEG 2000 across multiple metrics (PSNR and MS-SSIM) on standard image datasets. The visual examples demonstrate consistent perceptual improvements, and the quantitative results show significant gains, particularly at lower bit rates. The paper also carefully addresses the relationship between their continuous relaxation and actual discrete compression performance, showing that the approximation is valid for practical purposes.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
The paper is well-written and technically sound, but could benefit from:

1. More explicit discussion of computational efficiency: While the paper mentions that encoding/decoding is efficient after training, a more detailed comparison of computational requirements (both training and inference) with JPEG and JPEG 2000 would strengthen the practical relevance of the approach.

2. Clarification on the scope of the "end-to-end" claim: The paper states that their method is end-to-end optimized, but the entropy coding is implemented as a simple CABAC-based approach rather than being fully integrated into the optimization. A more precise discussion of what constitutes "end-to-end" in this context would be helpful.

3. Further exploration of the relationship between GDN and other nonlinearities: While the paper mentions preliminary results with other activation functions, a more systematic comparison of GDN against alternatives (e.g., ReLU, sigmoid) would provide stronger evidence for the claimed efficiency advantage.

## 5. Clarifying Questions
1. The paper states that the relaxed rate-distortion optimization problem "bears some resemblance" to variational autoencoders but differs in key constraints. Could the authors elaborate on which specific constraints are most critical for the rate-distortion optimization that prevent direct application of variational autoencoder techniques?

2. In Figure 4, the paper shows that the relaxed entropy provides a positively biased estimate of the discrete entropy for coarser quantization. How does this bias affect the practical implementation of the method, and what measures were taken to ensure that the bias does not accumulate across the rate-distortion curve?

3. The paper mentions that the method was optimized using MSE as the distortion metric, but achieved better perceptual quality. Could the authors clarify whether they believe the GDN/IGDN transforms inherently capture perceptual features, or whether the optimization process itself (even with MSE) leads to representations that better align with human perception?
