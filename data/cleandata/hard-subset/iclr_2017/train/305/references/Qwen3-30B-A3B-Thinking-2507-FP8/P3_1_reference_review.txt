

# Peer Review: "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel framework for end-to-end optimization of image compression using nonlinear transforms, addressing a long-standing challenge in lossy compression where traditional methods optimize the transform, quantizer, and entropy code components separately. The authors introduce a cascaded nonlinear transformation architecture inspired by biological visual systems (using Generalized Divisive Normalization or GDN), which is jointly optimized with the quantization and entropy coding components using stochastic gradient descent. The key innovation lies in their continuous relaxation of the quantization step (replacing it with additive uniform noise) to enable gradient-based optimization. The authors demonstrate that their method achieves superior rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, with substantially improved perceptual quality that is consistent across all tested images and bit rates. The paper is well-motivated, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression by bridging traditional rate-distortion theory with modern deep learning techniques.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of image compression with a well-motivated approach that is rigorously evaluated. The key reasons for acceptance are:

1. The paper successfully demonstrates that end-to-end optimization of the entire compression pipeline (including nonlinear transforms, quantization, and entropy coding) leads to significant improvements in both rate-distortion performance and perceptual quality compared to established methods.

2. The authors provide a theoretically sound continuous relaxation technique for optimization that is well-justified and empirically validated, addressing a fundamental challenge in optimizing lossy compression systems.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's contributions are compelling for several reasons:

First, the authors successfully address a fundamental limitation in traditional image compression: the separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy code components. By framing the problem as a rate-distortion optimization with a continuous relaxation of quantization, they enable end-to-end training of a complete compression system. This is a significant methodological advancement that builds upon and extends previous work in the field (e.g., Ballé et al., 2016), while providing a more principled approach than recent deep learning-based compression methods (e.g., Toderici et al., 2016).

Second, the empirical results are convincing and well-documented. The paper provides clear evidence that their method outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 on both PSNR (a standard metric) and MS-SSIM (a perceptual metric), with improvements consistent across all tested images and bit rates. The visual examples (Figures 5 and 6) compellingly demonstrate the qualitative improvements in compression artifacts, which align with the quantitative metrics. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of their continuous relaxation technique (Figure 4), showing that it effectively approximates the true rate-distortion performance.

Third, the paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding why nonlinear transforms outperform linear transforms in compression. The authors correctly identify that the cascade of biologically-inspired nonlinear transformations better captures the statistics of natural images, particularly for representing features with continuously varying orientation, position, and scale. This insight provides a theoretical foundation for their empirical results that goes beyond what is typically offered in deep learning-based compression papers.

The paper also thoughtfully addresses the relationship between their approach and variational autoencoders (Section 3.1), clarifying important conceptual distinctions that prevent the method from being conflated with generative modeling approaches. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the field and helps position the work within the broader context of machine learning.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, the following suggestions would further strengthen it:

1. **Quantify "most images"**: The paper states that their method "exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images." It would be helpful to provide a specific percentage or count of images where the method outperforms the baselines to better quantify this claim.

2. **Compare with more recent compression methods**: While the comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 is appropriate as a baseline, the paper would benefit from a comparison with more recent deep learning-based compression methods (e.g., Ballé et al., 2018; Toderici et al., 2017) to better position their work in the current landscape.

3. **Clarify the entropy coding comparison**: The authors use a simple CABAC-based entropy code, while JPEG 2000 uses a more sophisticated entropy coding scheme. It would be valuable to explicitly discuss how the performance gap between their method and JPEG 2000 might change if they used a more advanced entropy code. The paper mentions this in the appendix but doesn't discuss it in the main text.

4. **Explain the perceptual quality improvement more clearly**: The paper states that the method was optimized using MSE but produces better perceptual quality. It would be helpful to include a brief discussion of why MSE optimization leads to better perceptual quality, perhaps by analyzing the statistics of the compressed representations or by comparing the learned transforms to known perceptual models.

## 5. Questions for Authors

To help clarify some aspects of the work and better understand the results, I would like to ask the authors the following questions:

1. The paper states that "the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested, and at all bit rates." However, Figure 15 shows an image where their method underperforms JPEG 2000 at high bit rates (based on PSNR). Could the authors clarify why this particular image behaves differently than the others, and whether this trend holds for other similar images?

2. The authors mention in the appendix that they are testing models with simpler nonlinearities (ReLU, sigmoid) to determine how much of the performance is due to the GDN/IGDN transforms. Could they share preliminary results on this comparison, particularly regarding the number of parameters required to achieve similar performance with different activation functions?

3. The paper uses a simple raster-scan ordering for the CABAC entropy code, which doesn't fully exploit spatial dependencies. Would using a more sophisticated ordering (like that used in JPEG 2000) substantially improve the bit rates, and how would this affect the comparison to JPEG 2000?

4. The authors note that their method was optimized for MSE but produces better perceptual quality. Could they share an analysis of the statistical properties of the learned representations that might explain this disconnect between the optimization metric and perceptual quality?

5. The paper mentions that their model for RGB images uses a first stage with filters operating across three color channels. How does this compare to the approach used in JPEG 2000 (which typically processes color channels separately)? Would a more sophisticated color transform (like Y'CbCr) further improve performance?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression and represents an important step toward end-to-end optimized compression systems. The authors have provided a well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated approach that is likely to influence future work in this area. I recommend acceptance.
