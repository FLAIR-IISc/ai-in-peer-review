

# Peer Review: END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper presents a novel framework for end-to-end optimization of image compression models using nonlinear transforms. The authors address a fundamental limitation in traditional compression methods (like JPEG and JPEG 2000) where transform, quantizer, and entropy code components are separately optimized. Their approach uses cascaded linear convolutions with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and its inverse (IGDN) as nonlinear transforms, followed by uniform scalar quantization. The key innovation is a continuous relaxation of the quantization step that enables gradient-based optimization of the entire pipeline. The authors demonstrate that their method achieves superior rate-distortion performance over JPEG and JPEG 2000, with substantially improved perceptual quality across all tested images and bit rates. The paper also provides a detailed theoretical analysis of the relationship between their approach and variational autoencoders, clearly articulating the key differences that make their method better suited for compression.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression with a well-motivated approach that is clearly positioned within the literature. The empirical results are strong, scientifically rigorous, and demonstrate clear advantages over established methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

The paper tackles a well-known problem in image compression with a novel solution that addresses a fundamental limitation of existing methods. The approach is well-motivated: the authors clearly explain why joint optimization is difficult (the intractability of high-dimensional quantization) and why their continuous relaxation approach is valid and effective. The paper is well-placed in the literature, with careful distinction from related work on variational autoencoders (section 3.1) and other compression approaches.

The results are scientifically rigorous and well-supported. The authors provide extensive empirical evidence across multiple images and bit rates, using both standard metrics (PSNR) and perceptual metrics (MS-SSIM). The visual quality improvements are not just quantitative but also qualitatively apparent in the figures. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., the relatively small gains from entropy coding improvements) and provide detailed implementation details that enable reproducibility. The continuous relaxation approach is properly validated, with the authors showing that the relaxed loss function provides a good approximation to actual rate-distortion values.

The paper's contribution is significant because it demonstrates that end-to-end optimization of the entire compression pipeline can yield substantial improvements over traditional methods that optimize components separately. The visual improvements are particularly notable, as the paper shows that optimizing for MSE can still lead to better perceptual quality than traditional methods optimized for MSE.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

To further strengthen the paper, I would recommend:

1. A more detailed analysis of why GDN/IGDN specifically leads to better compression efficiency compared to other nonlinearities. While the paper mentions that "rectified linear units generally require a substantially larger number of model parameters/stages," it would be helpful to include some quantitative comparisons of the parameter efficiency between different nonlinearities.

2. A discussion of the computational complexity and memory requirements of the proposed method, particularly for real-world deployment. While the paper mentions that encoding/decoding is efficient, a more detailed analysis would be valuable for practitioners.

3. Additional details on the entropy coding approach. The paper states that "the performance gains compared to a well-designed non-adaptive entropy code are relatively small," but it would be helpful to know how much improvement could be achieved with a more sophisticated entropy coding scheme that better exploits spatial dependencies.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper mentions "preliminary results indicate that qualitatively similar results are achievable with other activation functions." Could you provide some specific examples or data points to support this claim? How much larger would the network need to be to achieve comparable performance with, say, ReLU activations?

2. The authors note that "the continuous relaxation using additive noise was used only for training purposes." How sensitive is the performance to the choice of noise distribution and width? Would a different relaxation approach yield similar results?

3. The paper states that their method "exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images." Can you provide more details about the specific images where their method underperforms, and why this might occur? This could help identify potential limitations of the approach.

4. The paper mentions "we are currently testing models that use simpler rectified-linear or sigmoidal nonlinearities." Could you share any insights about the trade-offs between model size, training time, and performance for these alternative approaches?

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of image compression with a well-executed approach that demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods. The results are compelling, the methodology is sound, and the paper is well-written and clearly organized. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the ICLR proceedings.
