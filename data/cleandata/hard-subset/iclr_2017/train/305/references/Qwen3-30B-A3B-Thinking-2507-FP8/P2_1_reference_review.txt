

# Review for ICLR: "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper presents a novel framework for end-to-end optimized image compression using deep learning techniques. The authors propose a nonlinear transform coding model that jointly optimizes the analysis and synthesis transforms, quantization, and entropy coding using gradient-based optimization. Their key contribution is the use of cascaded generalized divisive normalization (GDN) operations, inspired by biological visual systems, which they demonstrate to be more effective for image compression than traditional linear transforms.

The paper makes several important contributions:
- A continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization
- A complete compression framework that reports actual bit rates rather than just entropy estimates
- Demonstrated improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000 in both rate-distortion performance (measured by PSNR) and perceptual quality (measured by MS-SSIM)
- Visual evidence that their method produces fewer artifacts than traditional transform coding methods

The approach is well-motivated by the limitations of existing image compression methods, which optimize transform, quantizer, and entropy code components separately. The authors clearly articulate the theoretical foundation and provide empirical validation of their claims.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression by demonstrating the power of end-to-end deep learning optimization for this classical problem. The theoretical framework is sound, the implementation is detailed, and the empirical results are compelling.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

### Strong Theoretical Foundation
The paper provides a clear theoretical justification for their continuous relaxation of the quantization step, which is critical for enabling gradient-based optimization. They carefully explain how this relaxation approximates the discrete optimization problem while being differentiable. The comparison to variational autoencoders (Section 3.1) is particularly strong, as it clearly articulates both the similarities and key differences between their approach and generative modeling.

### Rigorous Empirical Evaluation
The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-executed:
- They use a standard benchmark (Kodak image dataset) for comparison
- They provide visual comparisons showing the qualitative improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000
- They report both PSNR (standard metric) and MS-SSIM (perceptual metric) results
- They make all test images and code available online for reproducibility
- They demonstrate that their method works across a diverse set of test images

The results are compelling: their method outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 in rate-distortion performance for most images and bit rates, and the perceptual quality improvements are consistent across all test cases.

### Clear Distinction from Prior Work
The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work (Gregor et al., Toderici et al.) by emphasizing that their approach optimizes directly for rate-distortion performance rather than using a generative model that is then adapted for compression. This is a crucial distinction that the authors effectively articulate in Section 5.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong overall, the following points could further improve its quality:

- **Computational Complexity**: The paper mentions that "encoding or decoding an image with the trained models is efficient," but doesn't provide specific computational metrics (e.g., time per image, memory requirements) compared to JPEG/JPEG 2000. Adding this would strengthen the practical relevance of the work.

- **Comparison to Other Deep Learning Methods**: While the paper mentions Toderici et al. (2016), it could provide a more detailed comparison of their approach with this and other recent deep learning-based compression methods. Specifically, how does their end-to-end optimization approach differ from Toderici's method that "directly output a binary representation"?

- **Scalability Discussion**: The paper focuses on 256x256 images, but doesn't discuss how the method would scale to higher resolutions (e.g., 1080p, 4K) which are common in real-world applications.

- **Why MSE Optimization Leads to Better Perceptual Quality**: The paper notes that "although the method was optimized using mean squared error as a distortion metric, the compressed images are much more natural in appearance." This is a fascinating observation that deserves more detailed discussion. The authors could explore whether the GDN-based transforms inherently capture perceptually important features, or if there's something about the end-to-end optimization process that leads to this outcome.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To further clarify and strengthen my assessment, I would like to ask the following questions:

1. Could you provide specific computational metrics (encoding/decoding time, memory requirements) for your method compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000? How does this impact the practical deployment of your method?

2. In Section 5, you state that "the model developed by Toderici et al. (2016) is optimized to provide various rate-distortion trade-offs and directly output a binary representation." How does your method's approach to achieving different rate-distortion points (through different λ values) compare to Toderici's single representation for all rate points?

3. You mention that "the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested, and at all bit rates." Could you provide more details about the diversity of the test images used? Specifically, how many different image categories were included in your test set beyond the standard Kodak images?

4. In Section 6.1, you state that "rectified linear units generally require a substantially larger number of model parameters/stages to achieve the same rate-distortion performance as the GDN/IGDN nonlinearities." Could you provide specific numbers or figures to support this claim (e.g., number of parameters, stages, or PSNR improvements for different activation functions)?

These questions would help clarify the practical implications of your work and strengthen the paper's contribution to the field.
