

# Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions
The paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on nonlinear transforms (GDN/IGDN) that jointly optimizes analysis and synthesis transforms for rate-distortion performance. The authors claim their method outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 in both rate-distortion performance (measured by PSNR) and visual quality (measured by MS-SSIM), with compressed images exhibiting fewer artifacts than standard methods. They use a continuous relaxation of quantization to enable gradient-based optimization, implement an entropy code, and report actual bit rates rather than just entropy estimates. The paper also discusses connections to variational autoencoders while highlighting key differences in the optimization objectives.

## 2. Decision
Reject.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper presents a technically interesting approach to image compression but fails to provide sufficient evidence to justify its key claims of substantial improvements over established methods. As a reviewer who prefers established methods and requires rigorous validation before endorsing novel approaches, I have several concerns:

First, the paper's claim that the method "offers improvements in rate-distortion performance over JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most images and bit rates" lacks statistical rigor. While they present example images and MS-SSIM results, they do not provide a comprehensive statistical analysis across their test set. The paper states improvements occur "for most images" but does not quantify this (e.g., percentage of images improved, statistical significance). The test set appears limited (Kodak images, Lena, Barbara, etc.), and they acknowledge that for some images, their method underperforms JPEG 2000 at high bit rates (figure 15). Without clear statistical evidence across a representative test set, the claim of "most images" remains unsubstantiated.

Second, the paper's claim that the continuous relaxation of quantization provides a good approximation to actual rate-distortion performance is insufficiently supported. They show figure 4 suggesting the relaxation is reasonable, but this single figure does not provide statistical evidence of the approximation's accuracy across different bit rates or image types. The paper states "a constant bias in either of the terms would simply alter the effective value of λ," but this doesn't address whether the bias is consistent and small enough to produce reliable rate-distortion curves.

Third, the paper omits critical comparisons to other end-to-end compression methods. Toderici et al. (2016) presented a similar end-to-end approach that directly outputs binary representations and has been published in a top conference (ICLR 2016). The authors briefly mention this work but fail to provide a thorough comparison that would contextualize their contribution. Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine whether their approach offers meaningful advantages over other recent methods.

Fourth, the paper claims "substantial improvements" in perceptual quality but provides only qualitative visual comparisons and MS-SSIM values without statistical validation. The paper states "the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested, and at all bit rates," but the evidence consists of a few example images and MS-SSIM values. A more rigorous assessment would include statistical analysis of the perceptual quality improvements across the entire test set.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide a comprehensive statistical analysis of their rate-distortion performance across the entire test set, including measures of significance and variability. This should include a clear statement about how many images showed improvement over JPEG 2000 versus those that didn't.

2. Include a thorough comparison with Toderici et al. (2016) and other recent end-to-end compression methods, explaining how their approach differs and why it might be superior. This is critical for placing their work in the context of current research.

3. Provide more evidence for the accuracy of their continuous relaxation approach. A more detailed analysis of the approximation error across different bit rates and image types would be valuable.

4. Consider a more rigorous perceptual quality analysis beyond MS-SSIM, such as a user study or comparison with other perceptual metrics.

5. Address the claim that "GDN/IGDN transforms are more efficient for compression" with more concrete evidence. The paper mentions that "rectified linear units generally require a substantially larger number of model parameters/stages," but this is based on "preliminary results" and should be expanded with more thorough experiments.

6. Clarify the relationship between their approach and existing work on transform coding, particularly how their nonlinear approach differs from previous attempts to incorporate nonlinearity (e.g., Malo et al., 2006).

**Questions for Authors:**
1. Can you provide statistical significance testing for the PSNR and MS-SSIM improvements across your test set? What percentage of images showed statistically significant improvements over JPEG 2000?
2. How does your method compare to Toderici et al. (2016) in terms of rate-distortion performance, computational complexity, and visual quality? Why is your approach superior to their method?
3. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the approximation error between the continuous relaxation and actual bit rates across different bit rates and image types?
4. The paper states "for all images tested, and at all bit rates" the perceptual quality is better. Can you provide evidence that this holds across the entire test set, not just the examples shown?
