

# Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of paper's claims and contribution

The paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression framework using nonlinear transforms (specifically, cascades of linear convolutions followed by generalized divisive normalization [GDN] operations) instead of traditional linear transform coding methods. The authors claim their method achieves better rate-distortion performance than JPEG and JPEG2000, particularly at lower bit rates, and produces visually superior images with fewer blocking and ringing artifacts. They argue that their approach, which jointly optimizes analysis and synthesis transforms using stochastic gradient descent with a continuous relaxation of the quantization step, provides a more natural representation of image features that better aligns with human visual perception.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper fails to provide sufficient empirical validation against established baselines and does not demonstrate significant enough improvements over existing methods to justify the complexity of their proposed approach.

## 3. Supporting arguments for the decision

The paper's main claim is that their method "exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images, especially at the lower bit rates." However, this claim is not sufficiently supported for several reasons:

First, the experimental validation is limited to a small, non-representative set of images (Kodak dataset, Lena, Barbara, Peppers, Mandrill, and a few personal photographs) with no analysis of how performance varies across different image types (e.g., natural scenes, textures, text, medical images). The paper does not provide statistical significance testing across the image set, making it difficult to determine if the claimed improvements are consistent or merely coincidental for specific images.

Second, the comparison to JPEG and JPEG2000 is not fair. The authors use a simple entropy code (CABAC) while JPEG2000 employs a more sophisticated entropy coding scheme. The paper acknowledges that their entropy code "likely smaller than those achieved by the entropy code in JPEG 2000," which undermines their claimed performance advantage.

Third, the paper claims perceptual improvements (MS-SSIM) despite being trained on MSE. The authors acknowledge that "the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested," but provide no theoretical or empirical explanation for this phenomenon. This is a significant claim that requires more rigorous justification, as the paper fails to demonstrate why MSE-optimized models would inherently produce better perceptual results.

Fourth, the paper does not compare against more recent state-of-the-art methods. For instance, the authors cite Toderici et al. (2016) but do not directly compare against this work, which was already published in 2016 and represents a more modern approach to end-to-end image compression. The field has advanced significantly since 2016, and the paper's claims about "superior performance" lack context against contemporary methods.

## 4. Additional feedback to improve the paper

The paper would benefit from the following improvements to strengthen its claims:

- Provide a more comprehensive comparison against recent state-of-the-art image compression methods (e.g., those from 2017-2023 conferences), not just JPEG and JPEG2000.

- Include a detailed analysis of the specific visual artifacts avoided by the proposed method compared to traditional methods, with quantitative measures of artifact reduction.

- Demonstrate the robustness of the claimed improvements across a larger, more diverse image dataset (e.g., the full ImageNet validation set or standard compression benchmarks like CLIC).

- Provide a more detailed explanation of the relationship between MSE optimization and perceptual quality improvements, including ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the GDN/IGDN transforms.

- Include a thorough computational complexity analysis showing how the proposed method compares to existing methods in terms of training and inference time, memory usage, and hardware requirements.

- Address the limitations of the continuous relaxation approach and provide more empirical evidence that the rate and distortion approximations used during training accurately reflect the actual performance of the discrete compression system.

## Questions for authors

1. The paper claims perceptual improvements without training on a perceptual metric. What specific properties of the GDN/IGDN transforms lead to this improvement, and how can this be explained theoretically or empirically?

2. How does your method compare against more recent end-to-end image compression approaches (e.g., those using neural networks for entropy coding or more sophisticated transform architectures) that have been published since 2016?

3. Why did you choose a simple CABAC entropy code instead of implementing a more sophisticated entropy coding scheme that would better match JPEG2000's capabilities, and how would this affect your comparison?

4. The paper states that "rectified linear units generally require a substantially larger number of model parameters/stages to achieve the same rate-distortion performance as the GDN/IGDN nonlinearities." Can you provide more evidence for this claim, including quantitative comparisons of model size and performance?

5. How do you explain the fact that your method, optimized for MSE, consistently outperforms JPEG and JPEG2000 on MS-SSIM across all bit rates and images? This is a significant claim that requires stronger justification.
