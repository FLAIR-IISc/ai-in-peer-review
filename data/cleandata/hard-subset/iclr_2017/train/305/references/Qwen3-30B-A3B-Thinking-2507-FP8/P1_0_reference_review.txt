

# Peer Review: END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel framework for end-to-end optimized image compression using nonlinear transforms. The authors address the long-standing challenge of jointly optimizing the three key components of transform coding (transform, quantizer, and entropy code) by introducing a cascaded nonlinear transform architecture based on Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The key innovation is the use of a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization, allowing the entire compression pipeline to be trained end-to-end for optimal rate-distortion performance.

The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms traditional methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000 in both rate-distortion performance (measured by PSNR) and perceptual quality (measured by MS-SSIM), with visually superior results that avoid the common blocking and ringing artifacts of linear transform coding. The paper provides a thorough theoretical foundation, detailed implementation, and extensive empirical validation across multiple image datasets. The work is well-positioned in the literature, clearly distinguishing its approach from related work on generative models and variational autoencoders.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper presents a significant methodological advance in image compression by demonstrating the effectiveness of end-to-end optimization with nonlinear transforms, which has been largely unexplored in the literature.
2. The results are scientifically rigorous, with careful empirical evaluation across multiple metrics (PSNR, MS-SSIM) and visual comparisons that clearly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of image compression for several compelling reasons:

First, the problem statement is well-motivated and clearly articulated. The authors effectively explain the limitations of traditional transform coding approaches that optimize transform, quantizer, and entropy code separately, and why end-to-end optimization is both necessary and feasible with modern deep learning techniques.

Second, the technical approach is carefully developed and justified. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of the relaxed rate-distortion optimization problem, and they demonstrate how it relates to (but differs from) variational autoencoders. The distinction between generative modeling and compression is particularly well-articulated, which is crucial for establishing the paper's contribution.

Third, the empirical results are compelling and thoroughly presented. The paper includes visual comparisons that clearly show the qualitative advantages of the proposed method over JPEG and JPEG 2000, which is particularly important since the method was trained with MSE (a metric that doesn't always correlate with perceptual quality). The MS-SSIM results across multiple images and bit rates provide strong evidence that the method improves perceptual quality consistently, not just in terms of PSNR.

Fourth, the paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The figures (particularly Figures 1, 2, 4, 5, 6, and 7) effectively illustrate the key concepts and results. The authors provide sufficient detail in the appendix for reproducibility, including network architecture, optimization details, and entropy coding implementation.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, the following suggestions would help further improve it:

1. **Clarify the practical deployment considerations:** The paper mentions that "computational costs for training are quite high, but encoding or decoding is efficient." It would be helpful to provide some quantitative estimates of the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to JPEG/JPEG 2000, especially for real-world deployment scenarios (e.g., mobile devices).

2. **Expand the comparison to more recent methods:** While JPEG and JPEG 2000 are standard baselines, it would be valuable to compare against more recent compression methods like BPG (Blind Pixel Grouping) or other deep learning-based compression approaches to better position the contribution in the current landscape.

3. **Address the limitations more explicitly:** The paper mentions that the method "exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images." It would be helpful to discuss why this is the case—what characteristics of images make the method less effective, and whether these can be addressed in future work.

4. **Provide more insight into the relationship between GDN and the compression performance:** While the paper mentions that GDN was previously shown to be efficient in Gaussianizing image densities, it would be valuable to have a more direct analysis of how the specific properties of GDN contribute to the compression performance, perhaps through ablation studies comparing GDN to other nonlinearities.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help clarify and strengthen the paper, I'd appreciate answers to the following questions:

1. The paper states that "the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested, and at all bit rates." Could you provide more details about the specific images where the method underperforms in terms of PSNR? What characteristics of those images might explain the poorer performance?

2. In section 6.3, the authors note that "summary plots can still over- or underemphasize high rate vs. low rate performance." Given this challenge, what specific metrics or approaches do you recommend for fairly comparing compression methods across different image sets?

3. The authors mention that "rectified linear units generally require a substantially larger number of model parameters/stages to achieve the same rate-distortion performance as the GDN/IGDN nonlinearities." Could you provide some quantitative comparison of the number of parameters or computational complexity between the GDN-based model and a comparable model using ReLU?

4. The paper states that "our method exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images, especially at the lower bit rates." Could you clarify whether this is consistent across both grayscale and RGB images? The paper mentions separate models for RGB, but doesn't explicitly state whether the performance advantage is consistent across both.

Thank you for this excellent contribution to the field of image compression. The work demonstrates the power of end-to-end optimization in a classical problem domain and provides valuable insights that will likely influence future research in this area.
