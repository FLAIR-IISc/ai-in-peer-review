

# Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Claims
The paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression framework using nonlinear transforms (specifically, cascades of linear convolutions with GDN nonlinearities) to replace traditional linear transform coding methods. The authors claim that their approach, which optimizes the rate-distortion trade-off through a continuous relaxation of quantization, achieves better rate-distortion performance than JPEG and JPEG 2000, particularly at lower bit rates. They also claim that the compressed images exhibit "visually superior" quality compared to traditional methods, with reduced blocking, ringing, and aliasing artifacts. The method is trained end-to-end using stochastic gradient descent with a relaxed loss function that approximates quantization through additive uniform noise.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper makes significant claims about performance improvements over established compression methods but fails to provide sufficient empirical evidence to support these claims with the rigor expected for a high-impact publication.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's key weaknesses fall into three categories:

First, the empirical validation is insufficiently rigorous. While the authors compare their method to JPEG and JPEG 2000 on standard test sets (Kodak, Lena, etc.), they do not provide statistical significance testing for their claims of "better rate-distortion performance" or "substantial improvements" in perceptual quality. The paper states their method outperforms competitors "for most (but not all) test images," yet fails to quantify this claim or demonstrate that the improvements are consistent across a sufficiently large and diverse image set. The visual comparisons provided are subjective and lack objective user studies to validate the "visual superiority" claim.

Second, the paper makes a fundamental methodological error in its evaluation framework. They claim to report "actual bit rates" but use a simple CABAC entropy code that is explicitly acknowledged to be suboptimal compared to JPEG 2000's entropy coding. The paper admits their entropy code "only crudely exploits spatial adaptation of the probability model compared to existing coding methods such as JPEG 2000," which undermines their comparison. Without a fair comparison using equivalent entropy coding techniques, their claimed performance gains cannot be trusted.

Third, the paper's novelty is overstated. The continuous relaxation technique for quantization (using additive noise) has been widely used in the literature for training quantized models (e.g., in quantized neural networks), and the authors acknowledge this in section 3.1. The paper's main contribution is applying this known technique to image compression, but without sufficient evidence that this application yields meaningful improvements over established methods.

The paper's claim that their method "exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most test images" is particularly concerning given that the authors themselves acknowledge their method underperforms JPEG 2000 on some images (e.g., "for the image in figure 15, our method only slightly outperforms JPEG 2000 at low rates, and under-performs it at high rates"). This inconsistency suggests the method's performance is highly image-dependent, which is not a desirable characteristic for a general-purpose compression algorithm.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide statistical significance testing for all performance claims across the entire test set, not just specific examples.

2. Compare their method to the latest state-of-the-art deep learning compression methods (e.g., Toderici et al., 2016), not just traditional methods. The paper briefly mentions Toderici et al. but fails to provide a direct comparison.

3. Use a more sophisticated entropy coding scheme that matches the complexity of JPEG 2000's entropy coding for a fair comparison.

4. Include a user study to objectively validate their "visual superiority" claim, rather than relying solely on MS-SSIM and subjective visual comparisons.

5. Address the computational complexity of their method compared to JPEG 2000, including training time, encoding time, and decoding time.

6. Clarify the relationship between their continuous relaxation approach and the actual discrete compression problem, particularly regarding the claimed "end-to-end" optimization.

Questions for authors:
- How much of the performance gain is due to the nonlinear transforms versus the entropy coding approach?
- Why does the method underperform JPEG 2000 on some images (e.g., figure 15), and what are the characteristics of these images that make them challenging for the method?
- Can you provide a detailed analysis of the computational costs (training, encoding, decoding) compared to JPEG 2000?
- How would the results change if you used a more sophisticated entropy code matching JPEG 2000's implementation?

The paper presents an interesting application of continuous relaxation techniques to image compression, but without the rigor and thorough validation required to support its significant claims, it does not meet ICLR's standards for publication.
