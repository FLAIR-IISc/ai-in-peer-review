

# Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression framework that uses cascaded nonlinear transforms (Generalized Divisive Normalization, GDN) instead of traditional linear transform coding. The authors propose a method to jointly optimize the analysis and synthesis transforms along with the entropy code for rate-distortion performance. Their approach replaces the intractable quantization step with a continuous relaxation using additive uniform noise, enabling gradient-based optimization. The paper demonstrates improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of both PSNR and perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) across various bit rates. The theoretical foundation is well-developed, with careful distinctions made between their approach and variational autoencoders, and the paper provides a clear explanation of the relationship between their method and rate-distortion theory.

## 2. Decision

I recommend rejection. The key reasons are: (1) The comparison to state-of-the-art compression methods is outdated and incomplete, and (2) The experimental evaluation lacks sufficient rigor to support the claimed advantages.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a meaningful contribution by proposing an end-to-end optimization approach for image compression using nonlinear transforms. The theoretical foundation is solid, and the method's connection to rate-distortion theory is well-established. However, the paper fails to properly position itself against more recent state-of-the-art methods, which is critical for a paper in this domain.

First, the comparison to existing methods is severely limited. The authors only compare to JPEG and JPEG 2000, which are older standards. Since the time of the cited work, multiple newer compression methods have emerged, including BPG (based on HEVC), WebP, and neural network-based approaches like Toderici et al. (2016). The paper does not compare to these methods, making it impossible to determine whether the proposed approach represents a meaningful advance over the current state-of-the-art. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the discussion but do not address it adequately.

Second, the experimental evaluation has significant shortcomings. The paper claims "substantial improvements" across all test images, but the appendix shows that for some images (like the one in Figure 15), their method underperforms JPEG 2000 at high bit rates. The paper does not provide a thorough analysis of these cases. Additionally, the evaluation uses a limited test set (Kodak dataset, Lena, Barbara, etc.) that does not represent the diversity of modern images. The entropy coding implementation is described as a "simple entropy code" that does not fully exploit spatial dependencies, which likely underestimates the potential performance of their method.

The paper also fails to provide a comprehensive analysis of computational complexity. While the authors note that "encoding or decoding an image with the trained models is efficient," they do not quantify this efficiency or compare it to existing methods. This is particularly important for a compression method, as computational efficiency is a critical factor in real-world applications.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. Comparing to more recent state-of-the-art methods, including BPG, WebP, and Toderici et al. (2016), to properly position the contribution. This would provide a more accurate assessment of the method's advantages.

2. Expanding the test set to include a more diverse collection of modern images, particularly high-resolution images that better represent contemporary image content.

3. Providing a more detailed analysis of the method's limitations, including which types of images benefit most from the approach and which types do not.

4. Including a thorough computational complexity analysis, particularly for real-time applications, and comparing it to existing methods.

5. Improving the entropy coding implementation to better exploit spatial dependencies, which would provide a more accurate assessment of the method's potential performance.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. Why did you choose to compare only to JPEG and JPEG 2000, given the existence of more recent compression standards like BPG and WebP? How would your method perform against these newer approaches?

2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the cases where your method underperforms JPEG 2000 (e.g., the image in Figure 15)? What factors contribute to this underperformance?

3. How would your method perform on a more diverse test set, such as the one used in recent compression benchmarks (e.g., the KODAK dataset with additional modern high-resolution images)?

4. What is the computational complexity of your method compared to JPEG 2000, particularly for real-time encoding/decoding on standard hardware?

5. How does your entropy coding implementation compare to the one used in JPEG 2000 in terms of exploiting spatial dependencies, and how does this affect the final bit rates?
