

# Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contribution
The paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on nonlinear transforms, specifically using cascades of linear convolutions followed by generalized divisive normalization (GDN) operations. The authors claim that their method achieves better rate-distortion performance than JPEG and JPEG 2000, with visually superior results that reduce common artifacts of linear transform coding. They introduce a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization of the entire compression pipeline, and demonstrate the feasibility of their approach by implementing a complete compression system with actual bit rate measurements.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper's claims are not sufficiently supported by rigorous methodology and evaluation, and the presentation contains several technical inconsistencies that undermine confidence in the results.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's primary claim is that an MSE-optimized model produces perceptually superior results compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000. However, the paper fails to adequately explain this phenomenon. The authors state: "the appearance of compressed images is both qualitatively different and substantially improved, compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000" without providing a compelling theoretical or empirical justification for why MSE-optimization would lead to better perceptual quality. This is a critical gap in the paper's argumentation.

The evaluation methodology contains significant inconsistencies. The paper claims to report "actual bit rates" but doesn't provide sufficient detail about how the sideband information (image size, λ value, etc.) is accounted for in the bit rate calculation. This omission is particularly problematic because sideband information can constitute a significant portion of the total bit rate in image compression systems. The paper states "all sideband information needed by the decoder (size of images, value of λ, etc.) was included in the bit stream," but without quantifying the exact contribution to the bit rate, the comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 is misleading.

The paper also lacks statistical significance testing for its claims of improved performance. While the authors state that "our method exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images," they don't provide statistical evidence to support this claim across the entire test set. The paper mentions "a set of relatively standard (if outdated) images" but doesn't explain why these particular images were chosen or how they represent the broader population of images.

Additionally, there are several technical inconsistencies:
- The paper states they use "uniform scalar quantization" but never specifies the quantization step size, which is critical information for reproducibility.
- The paper claims "for the two largest values of λ, and for RGB models, we increased the network capacity," but doesn't explain why this was necessary or how it affects the comparison to other methods.
- The paper mentions using "a subset of the ImageNet database consisting of 6507 images" but doesn't specify which subset or how it was selected, making reproducibility difficult.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Clarify the perceptual quality claim**: The paper needs to provide a more rigorous explanation for why an MSE-optimized model would produce better perceptual results. Consider adding a theoretical analysis or additional experiments showing the relationship between MSE optimization and perceptual quality.

2. **Detail bit rate calculation**: Provide a precise breakdown of the bit rate calculation, including the exact contribution of sideband information. This is crucial for fair comparison with other compression methods.

3. **Improve evaluation methodology**: Add statistical significance testing to the performance claims. Provide a more comprehensive evaluation across a larger and more diverse image set.

4. **Address network capacity scaling**: Explain why increased network capacity was necessary for larger λ values and RGB models, and how this affects the comparison to other methods.

5. **Fix formatting inconsistencies**: The paper contains several formatting issues, including inconsistent reference styles, incomplete figure references, and typographical errors (e.g., "transforma-" with a line break). These should be corrected for professional presentation.

6. **Quantify quantization step size**: Clearly state the quantization step size used in the experiments, as this is critical information for reproducibility.

## Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide the exact quantization step size used in the scalar quantization, and how it relates to the bit rate?
2. What percentage of the total bit rate is attributable to sideband information (image size, λ value, etc.), and how does this compare to the bit rate used by JPEG and JPEG 2000 for similar settings?
3. Can you provide statistical significance testing for the claim that your method "exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images"?
4. Why was increased network capacity necessary for larger λ values and RGB models, and how does this affect the comparison to other methods?
