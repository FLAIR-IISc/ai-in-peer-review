

# Peer Review: End-to-End Optimized Image Compression

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper presents a novel framework for end-to-end optimized image compression based on nonlinear transform coding. The authors propose a cascaded transformation architecture using Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) as a biologically-inspired nonlinear transform, followed by uniform scalar quantization. Their key contribution is a method to jointly optimize the analysis and synthesis transforms, along with the entropy code, for rate-distortion performance using stochastic gradient descent. The authors address the challenge of quantization (which produces zero gradients) by introducing a continuous relaxation using additive uniform noise. They demonstrate that their approach achieves superior rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, with the surprising benefit that the compressed images exhibit substantially better visual quality than those produced by traditional methods, despite being optimized for MSE rather than perceptual quality.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by bridging the gap between theoretical rate-distortion optimization and practical implementation, while providing a clear explanation of how their method differs from variational autoencoders (which are generative models). The authors provide thorough empirical evidence, including rate-distortion curves, visual comparisons, and perceptual quality metrics (MS-SSIM), demonstrating consistent improvements across diverse test images.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression with strong empirical results and a well-justified technical approach. The key reasons for acceptance are:

1. The paper successfully demonstrates a method for end-to-end optimization of image compression that outperforms standard methods (JPEG/JPEG 2000) in both rate-distortion performance and visual quality, with a clear and rigorous experimental evaluation.

2. The authors provide a sophisticated theoretical foundation for their approach, carefully distinguishing their method from related work on variational autoencoders and generative models, while clearly explaining the practical implications of these differences.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's strength lies in its combination of theoretical insight and practical implementation. The authors have thoroughly addressed the challenges of optimizing a lossy compression system end-to-end, particularly the intractability of quantization in gradient-based optimization. Their continuous relaxation using additive uniform noise is well-justified and supported by empirical evidence showing that the approximation works well in practice (Figure 4).

The experimental results are compelling and well-designed. The authors compare their method to standard benchmarks (JPEG and JPEG 2000) using both PSNR (as a traditional metric) and MS-SSIM (as a perceptual metric), demonstrating improvements across both. Their visual examples (Figures 5-7) clearly illustrate the qualitative advantages of their method, particularly in reducing blocking and ringing artifacts common in linear transform coding.

The paper also makes a strong theoretical contribution by clarifying the relationship between their rate-distortion optimization framework and variational autoencoders. The authors correctly identify key differences between compression (which requires discrete representation) and generative modeling (which operates in continuous space), which is crucial for avoiding misleading comparisons between these two approaches.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong overall, I suggest the following improvements to strengthen it further:

1. **Clarify computational complexity**: The paper mentions that encoding/decoding is efficient, but doesn't provide concrete metrics on inference speed compared to JPEG/JPEG 2000. For practical adoption, it's important to show that the computational cost of encoding/decoding is reasonable (e.g., how many times slower than JPEG is it).

2. **Expand on the visual quality analysis**: While MS-SSIM is a good metric, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of specific visual artifacts that their method avoids. A short qualitative analysis of why the GDN-based transforms produce better visual results (e.g., better preservation of edges) would strengthen the visual quality claims.

3. **Address potential limitations**: The paper notes that their method was optimized for MSE, but doesn't fully explore the potential benefits of optimizing directly for perceptual quality. A brief discussion of how the method might be adapted for perceptual optimization (e.g., using MS-SSIM as the distortion metric) would be valuable.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that the visual quality improvements are "remarkable" despite optimizing for MSE. Could the authors provide more insight into why the GDN-based transforms inherently produce better visual results for MSE optimization, beyond just the statistical properties of natural images?

2. The authors mention that GDN/IGDN transforms are more efficient than pointwise nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU). Could they provide specific numbers on how much smaller the model needs to be (in terms of parameters or layers) to achieve the same performance, and how this impacts practical deployment considerations?

3. The paper compares to JPEG and JPEG 2000 implementations, but doesn't specify which exact implementations were used. For reproducibility, could the authors clarify which JPEG and JPEG 2000 libraries were used (e.g., libjpeg, OpenJPEG), and whether any specific settings were used (e.g., chroma subsampling, quantization tables)?

4. The authors state that the continuous relaxation works well for optimization, but the paper doesn't discuss how sensitive the final results are to the choice of noise distribution. Would a different noise distribution (e.g., Laplacian instead of uniform) produce similar results?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression. The authors have addressed a fundamental problem in compression with a novel approach that combines theoretical insight with practical implementation, and the results are compelling. The paper should be accepted for publication at ICLR.
