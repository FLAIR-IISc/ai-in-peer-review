

# Peer Review: END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel framework for end-to-end optimized image compression using nonlinear transforms. The authors propose a cascaded architecture with Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) nonlinearities inspired by biological visual systems, which they jointly optimize with the analysis/synthesis transforms and entropy code for rate-distortion trade-off. Their key contributions include: (1) a continuous relaxation technique to handle quantization during optimization; (2) an implementation of a complete compression system with actual bit rates (not just entropy estimates); and (3) compelling evidence that their method outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 in both rate-distortion performance and perceptual quality, despite being trained with MSE as the distortion metric.

The paper is exceptionally well-motivated, clearly positioning its approach within the theoretical framework of rate-distortion optimization while making a strong case for why traditional separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy code is suboptimal. The authors carefully distinguish their work from related generative modeling approaches, addressing important theoretical differences in section 3.1.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression by demonstrating a practical, end-to-end optimized solution that outperforms established standards. The method is theoretically sound, well-executed, and provides both quantitative and qualitative evidence of improvement.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution is methodologically sound. The continuous relaxation approach to handle quantization (section 3) is well-justified and carefully validated in figure 4. The authors demonstrate that the relaxed loss function provides a good approximation to actual rate-distortion values, addressing a critical concern in differentiable optimization of discrete compression systems.

The experimental results are scientifically rigorous. The authors provide comprehensive comparisons across multiple metrics (PSNR, MS-SSIM), multiple image sets (Kodak, standard test images, personal photos), and multiple bit rates. The visual comparisons (figures 5-7) are particularly compelling, showing how their method avoids the blocking artifacts common in JPEG/JPEG 2000. The fact that their method, trained with MSE, yields superior perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) is a strong point that challenges conventional wisdom in the field.

The paper also makes important theoretical connections to variational autoencoders (section 3.1) while clearly distinguishing their compression-focused approach from generative modeling. This nuanced discussion elevates the paper beyond a simple engineering contribution to a theoretically grounded method.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance its impact:

1. **Evaluation Methodology**: The paper acknowledges the difficulty of fairly comparing rate-distortion curves across images. The authors' solution of providing individual curve visualizations online is excellent, but they should include a brief discussion of the challenges in their main text to help readers interpret the results.

2. **Computational Cost**: The paper mentions high computational costs for training but doesn't quantify the inference speed. For a practical compression method, it would be valuable to report encoding/decoding times compared to JPEG/JPEG 2000, especially for embedded systems as mentioned in the discussion.

3. **Ablation Study**: While the paper mentions testing simpler nonlinearities (rectified linear units), a more systematic ablation study comparing different activation functions and network architectures would strengthen the claim that GDN/IGDN provides efficiency advantages.

4. **Perceptual Metric**: The authors note that their method was trained with MSE but yields better perceptual quality. A brief discussion of why MSE training leads to perceptual improvements (e.g., through the learned representations capturing perceptually relevant features) would add theoretical depth.

## 5. Questions for Authors

To help clarify my understanding and ensure confidence in the assessment:

1. The paper states that "the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested, and at all bit rates." Given that figure 15 shows their method under-performing JPEG 2000 at high rates for one image, could you clarify whether this is an exception or whether the statement should be qualified?

2. The authors mention that "GDN represents a multivariate generalization of a particular type of sigmoidal function." Could you elaborate on how the specific functional form of GDN relates to the optimization of rate-distortion performance, beyond just its efficiency for density estimation?

3. Regarding the entropy coding (section 6.2), the authors state that their CABAC implementation "only crudely exploits spatial adaptation of the probability model." How much of the performance gap between their method and JPEG 2000 might be attributed to this limitation versus the actual compression model?

This paper represents an important advance in image compression that successfully bridges theory and practice. The authors have demonstrated a compelling end-to-end approach that challenges the conventional wisdom of separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy code. The method is well-motivated, rigorously evaluated, and delivers tangible improvements over established standards. I believe this work would be of significant interest to the ICLR community and will likely inspire further research in the intersection of deep learning and signal processing.
