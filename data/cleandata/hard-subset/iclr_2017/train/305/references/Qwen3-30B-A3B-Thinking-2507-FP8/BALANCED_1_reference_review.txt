

# Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary
The paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression framework using nonlinear transforms based on cascades of linear convolutions and generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearities. The authors develop a continuous relaxation to handle quantization during optimization, enabling joint optimization of analysis and synthesis transforms, as well as the entropy code, using stochastic gradient descent. They demonstrate improvements in both PSNR and perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 across multiple test images, with visually superior results that avoid common artifacts of linear transform coding methods.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper makes an incremental contribution to the field of learned image compression with limited novelty and insufficient experimental validation of key claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contributions are not sufficiently novel or clearly articulated. While the authors build upon Ballé et al. (2016) and Gregor et al. (2016), they don't clearly establish how their approach advances beyond these works. The core idea of using a continuous relaxation for optimization is similar to Toderici et al. (2016), and the GDN nonlinearities were previously explored in Ballé et al. (2015). The paper fails to convincingly demonstrate why this specific combination of techniques represents a significant advancement.

The experimental evaluation lacks rigor. The authors compare to JPEG and JPEG 2000 but omit comparisons to more recent state-of-the-art deep learning-based compression methods (e.g., Minnen et al. 2018, Toderici et al. 2017), which is a significant omission given the paper's claimed contributions to the field. The test set is limited (Kodak images, Lena, Barbara, etc.), and the paper itself admits that rate-distortion performance varies substantially across images. The paper claims "substantial improvements" in perceptual quality but only provides MS-SSIM results for a small subset of images (Figure 7), without statistical significance testing or a comprehensive analysis.

Most critically, the paper does not sufficiently validate that the continuous relaxation accurately approximates the discrete optimization problem. While Figure 4 shows some correlation between relaxed and actual rate-distortion values, it doesn't demonstrate that the continuous optimization finds solutions that are optimal or near-optimal in the discrete domain. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide evidence that the relaxation leads to practical improvements in discrete compression performance.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen this work, the authors should:

1. Provide a more thorough comparison to recent state-of-the-art deep learning-based image compression methods, including Toderici et al. (2017), Minnen et al. (2018), and others. The paper should clearly position itself within the current landscape of learned image compression.

2. Conduct a more comprehensive experimental evaluation with a larger, more diverse test set and include statistical analysis of performance differences.

3. Provide a more rigorous theoretical justification for why the continuous relaxation is sufficient for optimizing the discrete problem. The paper should include ablation studies showing how different aspects of the relaxation affect the final compression performance.

4. Include a detailed analysis of computational complexity and runtime performance compared to traditional compression methods, which is critical for practical deployment.

5. Provide more details about the ablation studies on different nonlinearities mentioned in Section 5, which would help clarify the specific contribution of GDN.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How does your continuous relaxation approach compare to the straight-through estimator used in other learned compression methods (e.g., Toderici et al. 2017)? What are the specific advantages of your relaxation method?

2. Could you provide more details about the comparison to recent state-of-the-art deep learning-based image compression methods? How does your method compare in terms of rate-distortion performance, computational complexity, and visual quality?

3. The paper claims "substantial improvements" in perceptual quality across all test images and bit rates. Could you provide statistical evidence (e.g., p-values) supporting this claim, and explain how the improvements vary across different image categories?

4. The paper states that "the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested, and at all bit rates." Could you provide more detailed analysis of how the visual quality improvements manifest at different bit rates, and whether there are any specific image characteristics where the method performs particularly well or poorly?
