

# Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper presents a novel end-to-end approach to image compression that jointly optimizes all components of the compression pipeline (analysis transform, quantizer, synthesis transform, and entropy code) for optimal rate-distortion performance. The authors propose a nonlinear transform architecture based on cascaded Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) operations, followed by uniform scalar quantization, and an approximate inverse transform. The key innovation lies in their continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization, allowing the entire system to be trained end-to-end. The method demonstrates significant improvements in both rate-distortion performance (measured by PSNR) and visual quality compared to standard methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000, with the surprising benefit that optimizing for MSE leads to substantially better perceptual quality.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance**. This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression by providing a theoretically sound and empirically validated end-to-end optimization framework that outperforms traditional methods. The key reasons for acceptance are the strong theoretical motivation for the approach and the compelling empirical evidence supporting the claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of traditional transform coding methods (separately optimizing transform, quantizer, and entropy code) and position their work within the relevant literature, citing key prior work on nonlinear transforms and their connection to biological visual systems. They carefully distinguish their approach from related work in variational autoencoders (section 3.1), highlighting important differences in objectives and implementation that justify their methodology.

The theoretical foundation is robust. The continuous relaxation of quantization through additive uniform noise is well-justified and carefully explained, with empirical evidence (figure 4) showing that the approximation is effective. The authors demonstrate that their approach can be framed as a rate-distortion optimization problem, with the continuous relaxation enabling practical optimization.

The empirical results are thorough and convincing. The paper compares against two standard benchmarks (JPEG and JPEG 2000) on the Kodak dataset and other standard test images, showing consistent improvements in PSNR and visual quality. The qualitative results (figures 5 and 6) are particularly compelling, demonstrating that the method avoids the blocking and ringing artifacts common in linear transform coding. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the rate-distortion trade-offs across different images (section 4.3), acknowledging that performance varies by image but showing that the method consistently provides visual improvements.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. Adding a comparative analysis of different nonlinearities (ReLU, sigmoid vs. GDN) in terms of parameters, rate-distortion, and visual quality. The paper mentions that GDN is more efficient but doesn't provide concrete comparisons.

2. Including a more detailed analysis of cases where the method underperforms (e.g., figure 15), explaining what characteristics of these images lead to poorer performance compared to JPEG 2000.

3. Providing a brief discussion of how the method would scale to video compression or other multimedia applications, as this would help readers understand the broader applicability of the approach.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "our method exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images." Can you provide more details about the specific images where the method underperforms, and what characteristics of those images might explain the poorer performance?

2. The paper mentions that "the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested, and at all bit rates." However, figure 7 shows that for some images (like "Lena"), the MS-SSIM improvement is more pronounced at lower bit rates. Can you elaborate on why this might be the case?

3. The paper states that the computational costs for training are high. Could you provide more specific details about the training time and computational resources required, and how this compares to other image compression methods?

4. The authors mention they used a subset of ImageNet (6507 images) for training. How would the performance change with a larger training set, and what is the point of diminishing returns for training set size?

5. The paper notes that "the continuous relaxation using additive noise was used only for training purposes." How sensitive is the final performance to the choice of relaxation method (e.g., using a different noise distribution instead of uniform)?

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field with a well-motivated approach, rigorous theoretical foundation, and compelling empirical results. The end-to-end optimization framework represents a significant step forward in image compression, and the visual improvements over standard methods are particularly impressive. I believe this work would be a valuable addition to ICLR.
