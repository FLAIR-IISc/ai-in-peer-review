

# Peer Review: "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel end-to-end approach for image compression that jointly optimizes the entire compression pipeline (analysis transform, quantizer, synthesis transform, and entropy coding) for rate-distortion performance. The authors propose a framework that replaces traditional linear transform coding with a cascade of nonlinear transformations inspired by biological visual systems (Generalized Divisive Normalization or GDN), followed by uniform scalar quantization and approximate inverse transforms. Key innovations include:

- A continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization
- A framework that directly optimizes the rate-distortion trade-off rather than optimizing components separately
- Demonstrated improvements in both objective metrics (PSNR) and perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000
- Theoretical connections to variational autoencoders while highlighting critical differences between compression and generative modeling

The authors provide a thorough theoretical foundation, detailed experimental validation, and compelling visual evidence of the superiority of their approach. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of image compression by demonstrating the power of end-to-end optimization for a classical engineering problem.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper presents a significant methodological advance in image compression by enabling end-to-end optimization of the entire pipeline, which has been a longstanding challenge in the field.
2. The empirical results are compelling, showing both objective rate-distortion improvements and substantial perceptual quality gains across multiple test images and bit rates.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

### Strengths

**Methodological Innovation**: The paper makes a clear and well-motivated contribution to the field. The use of GDN cascades for nonlinear transform coding represents a significant departure from traditional linear transform approaches. The continuous relaxation of quantization to enable gradient-based optimization is a standard technique in this area (Gray & Neuhoff, 1998), but the authors properly contextualize it within the specific problem of rate-distortion optimization rather than simply borrowing it from generative modeling.

**Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for their approach, carefully distinguishing their framework from variational autoencoders (section 3.1). This is crucial because many recent works have conflated generative modeling with compression, and the authors clearly articulate why their approach is better suited for compression.

**Empirical Validation**: The experiments are thorough and well-designed. The authors compare against established baselines (JPEG, JPEG 2000) using standard datasets (Kodak, Lena, etc.) and provide both objective metrics (PSNR) and perceptual metrics (MS-SSIM). The visual comparisons in figures 5-7 are particularly compelling, showing how their method avoids the blocking artifacts common in linear transform coding.

**Technical Rigor**: The implementation details are well-described (appendix), including the entropy coding framework (CABAC) and the specific parameterization of the transforms. The authors also acknowledge limitations in their entropy coding implementation (section 6.2), showing awareness of potential weaknesses in their approach.

### Minor Concerns

While the paper is strong overall, there are a few minor concerns that could be addressed to improve clarity and scientific rigor:

1. **Statistical Significance**: The paper claims "better rate-distortion performance" but doesn't provide statistical tests to confirm that the improvements are significant across the dataset. It would strengthen the paper to include statistical comparisons (e.g., paired t-tests) across the image set.

2. **JPEG 2000 Implementation**: The comparison to JPEG 2000 uses OpenJPEG with "default multiple component transform" but doesn't specify whether the comparison is fair across all bit rates. The paper acknowledges this in section 4 ("For the two largest values of λ, and for RGB models, we increased the network capacity..."), but a more detailed description of the exact settings used for JPEG 2000 would be helpful.

3. **Perceptual Metric Justification**: While the paper shows MS-SSIM improvements, it doesn't deeply analyze why the MSE-optimized model improves perceptual quality. A brief discussion of how the nonlinear transforms better align with human visual perception would strengthen the paper.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Clarify the Relationship to Previous Work**: While the paper mentions related work (Gregor et al., 2016; Toderici et al., 2016), it could better distinguish their approach from these methods. Specifically, the paper should clarify why their end-to-end optimization framework is superior to the "hierarchical representation" approach of Gregor et al. (2016) or the direct binary output approach of Toderici et al. (2016).

2. **Discuss Computational Costs**: The paper mentions that "computational costs for training our models are quite high" but doesn't provide specific numbers. Including training times and resource requirements would help readers understand the practical feasibility of the approach.

3. **Expand on GDN Efficiency**: The paper mentions that "GDN/IGDN transforms are more efficient for compression" but only provides limited evidence (section 5). A more detailed comparison of the number of parameters required for equivalent performance would strengthen this claim.

4. **Address Limitations**: The paper acknowledges limitations in the entropy coding implementation but doesn't discuss other potential limitations (e.g., performance on video vs. still images, or performance on non-natural images).

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims that their method "exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images." Could the authors provide more details on which images are exceptions and why their method underperforms on those specific images?

2. In section 4, the authors note that "for the image in figure 15, our method only slightly outperforms JPEG 2000 at low rates, and under-performs it at high rates." Could you elaborate on the characteristics of this image that make it particularly challenging for your method?

3. The paper mentions that "we are currently testing models that use simpler rectified-linear or sigmoidal nonlinearities." What were the specific quantitative results comparing these simpler nonlinearities to GDN/IGDN, and how did the visual quality compare?

4. The authors state that "the additive noise used in the objective function as a relaxation of quantization might also serve the purpose of making supervised classification networks more robust to small perturbations." Could you elaborate on this potential application and provide preliminary evidence supporting this claim?

5. The paper uses a continuous relaxation of quantization for optimization but reports actual bit rates for evaluation. How sensitive is the performance to the specific choice of relaxation (e.g., uniform noise vs. other noise distributions)?

---

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of image compression by demonstrating the power of end-to-end optimization for a classical engineering problem. The methodology is well-motivated, the theoretical foundation is sound, and the empirical results are compelling. With the minor revisions suggested above, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
