

# Peer Review: END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims/Contributions

This paper presents a novel framework for end-to-end optimized image compression that addresses a fundamental limitation in traditional compression methods: the separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy code components. The authors introduce a model based on nonlinear transforms using cascades of linear convolutions and generalized divisive normalization (GDN), followed by uniform scalar quantization and approximate inverse transforms. The key contributions include:

- A continuous relaxation technique using additive uniform noise to enable gradient-based optimization through the quantization step
- A complete compression pipeline with joint optimization of analysis/synthesis transforms, quantization, and entropy coding
- Demonstrated improvements in both rate-distortion performance (measured by PSNR) and perceptual quality (measured by MS-SSIM) compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000
- A compelling explanation for the visual quality improvements, linking the nonlinear transforms to biological visual processing mechanisms
- A careful distinction between their approach and related work on variational autoencoders, highlighting the critical differences in optimization goals

The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical foundation, detailed implementation, and thorough empirical evaluation across multiple image datasets, making a strong case for the effectiveness of their approach.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The key reasons for this decision are:
1. The paper presents a significant and well-executed contribution to the image compression field by enabling true end-to-end optimization of the entire compression pipeline, which has been a longstanding challenge.
2. The approach is well-motivated, rigorously evaluated, and provides both theoretical and empirical evidence supporting its claims, with results that clearly outperform standard methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's contributions are substantial and address a long-standing problem in image compression. Traditional methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000 optimize components separately, which limits their performance. The authors' approach of jointly optimizing the entire pipeline using gradient-based methods is both novel and practical.

The continuous relaxation technique they introduce to handle the non-differentiable quantization step is clever and well-justified. The paper carefully demonstrates that this relaxation provides a good approximation to the actual rate-distortion values (Figure 4), and they report actual bit rates rather than theoretical entropy estimates, which is crucial for a practical compression method.

The empirical results are compelling: the method shows superior rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most images and bit rates, with particularly striking improvements in visual quality. The explanation for the visual improvements—that the nonlinear transforms better capture the statistics of natural images, analogous to biological visual processing—is well-supported by the results and the visual examples provided.

The paper also makes a crucial distinction between their framework and variational autoencoders, which is important for the field. The authors clearly explain why their optimization objective is fundamentally different (focusing on the convex hull of achievable rate-distortion points rather than minimizing differential entropy), and why this matters for compression performance.

The implementation details are thorough, including the entropy coding method (based on CABAC), training procedure, and evaluation protocol. The authors provide a thoughtful analysis of the limitations of summary rate-distortion curves across images and wisely direct readers to their online repository for individual image results.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong as is, these suggestions could further improve it:

1. **Clarify the connection between the continuous relaxation and actual bit rates**: The paper mentions that the continuous relaxation is used only for optimization and that actual bit rates are measured with the entropy code. However, it would be helpful to explicitly state how close the continuous relaxation is to the actual discrete optimization, perhaps with a brief analysis of the gap between the relaxed rate and the actual bit rate.

2. **Expand on the visual quality paradox**: The paper notes that their method optimized for MSE yields better visual quality than JPEG/JPEG 2000 (also optimized for MSE). A more detailed explanation of why this occurs would strengthen the paper. Is it primarily due to the nonlinear transforms better capturing image statistics, or are there other factors at play?

3. **Add more analysis of the "failure cases"**: The paper states "for most (but not all) test images" their method outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 in PSNR. It would be valuable to provide a brief analysis of the specific cases where their method underperforms, including examples and possible explanations.

4. **Include a comparison to more recent deep learning compression methods**: While the paper mentions Toderici et al. (2016), a more detailed comparison to other recent deep learning-based compression methods would provide better context for the contribution.

5. **Discuss potential computational trade-offs**: The paper mentions that training costs are high, but encoding/decoding is efficient. It would be helpful to provide a brief comparison of the computational complexity of the proposed method versus JPEG/JPEG 2000 for both training and inference.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of λ (the rate-distortion trade-off parameter)? Are there specific values of λ that work better for certain image types or characteristics?

2. The paper states that "GDN represents a multivariate generalization of a particular type of sigmoidal function." Could you elaborate on how this relates to the observed efficiency advantage over pointwise nonlinearities, and whether this advantage would generalize to other types of nonlinearities?

3. In the appendix, the authors mention they're using a raster-scan ordering for entropy coding. How does this affect the final bit rate compared to more sophisticated ordering schemes used in JPEG 2000 or H.264? Would a more sophisticated ordering scheme significantly improve the results?

4. The paper mentions that they're testing models with simpler rectified-linear or sigmoidal nonlinearities. Could you share preliminary findings on how much of the performance advantage is due to the specific GDN/IGDN nonlinearities versus just having a nonlinear transform architecture?

5. The paper uses a subset of ImageNet for training. How much does the performance depend on the specific training data? Would using a larger or more diverse training set (e.g., a larger subset of ImageNet or a dataset specifically curated for compression) lead to further improvements?

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of image compression with significant theoretical and practical implications. The paper is well-written, well-executed, and provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. I recommend acceptance.
