

# Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper presents a novel framework for image compression that optimizes the entire compression pipeline end-to-end, rather than optimizing individual components (transform, quantizer, entropy code) separately as is standard practice in traditional image compression methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The authors propose a cascade of linear convolutions followed by generalized divisive normalization (GDN) as the analysis transform, followed by uniform scalar quantization, and a corresponding synthesis transform with inverse GDN (IGDN). The key innovation is the use of a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization of the entire system. The paper demonstrates that this approach achieves superior rate-distortion performance compared to standard methods, with substantially improved visual quality that avoids the common artifacts (blocking, ringing, aliasing) associated with linear transform coding. The authors provide a thorough theoretical justification for their approach, including a careful comparison to variational autoencoders, and present comprehensive empirical results on standard image datasets.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression by demonstrating that end-to-end optimization of the entire pipeline can yield substantial improvements over standard methods.
2. The approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and well-placed in the literature with clear distinctions from related work.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Theoretical Contribution and Motivation (Strengths):**
- The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for using nonlinear transforms in compression, correctly identifying the limitation of linear transforms (residual higher-order dependencies) and justifying the use of GDN as a solution.
- The distinction between their approach and variational autoencoders (Section 3.1) is particularly insightful and well-executed, addressing a critical point of confusion in the literature.
- The continuous relaxation of quantization to enable gradient-based optimization is elegantly implemented and thoroughly validated in Figure 4.

**Empirical Evidence (Strengths):**
- The results are comprehensive, comparing against two standard methods (JPEG and JPEG 2000) on multiple datasets, including Kodak, standard test images (Lena, Barbara, etc.), and the authors' own photographs.
- The paper provides both standard PSNR metrics and perceptual MS-SSIM metrics, showing that their method improves in both quantitative and qualitative aspects.
- The visual comparisons in Figures 5-7 are compelling and clearly demonstrate the advantages of their approach over standard methods, particularly in avoiding the common artifacts of linear transform coding.
- The paper acknowledges limitations of their entropy coding implementation (CABAC) and provides a clear explanation of how it affects the results.

**Overall Scientific Rigor:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of both the theoretical framework and empirical results.
- The authors provide sufficient implementation details for reproducibility, including network architecture, training procedures, and entropy coding details.
- The paper makes a clear and defensible case for the superiority of their approach without overclaiming.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong overall, these minor improvements would enhance it further:

1. **Comparison to Recent Work:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison to recent deep learning-based compression methods (e.g., Toderici et al., 2016; Gregor et al., 2016). Specifically, it would be valuable to understand how their end-to-end optimization approach differs from and improves upon these methods.

2. **Computational Complexity Analysis:** The paper mentions that encoding/decoding with the trained models is efficient, but it would be helpful to include a more detailed comparison of computational complexity (both training and inference) relative to JPEG and JPEG 2000, especially for real-world applications.

3. **GDN Efficiency Claim:** The paper claims that "GDN/IGDN transforms are more efficient for compression, producing better models with fewer stages of processing," but the evidence for this is somewhat limited. A more thorough comparison of model size, number of parameters, and rate-distortion performance across different activation functions would strengthen this claim.

4. **Quantification of Perceptual Improvements:** While the paper shows MS-SSIM improvements, it would be helpful to quantify how much the perceptual improvements vary across different image categories or bit rates.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested, and at all bit rates." Could you provide more specific statistics on how much the MS-SSIM improvements vary across different image categories or bit rate ranges?

2. How does your method compare to the work by Toderici et al. (2016) in terms of rate-distortion performance and computational complexity, particularly for the same target bit rates? The authors mention that Toderici et al. "do not necessarily lead to rate-distortion optimality" - could you elaborate on why you believe your approach achieves better optimality?

3. The paper mentions that "preliminary results indicate that qualitatively similar results are achievable with other activation functions," but the evidence is limited. Could you provide more details about this comparison, including specific numbers for different activation functions?

4. The paper uses CABAC for entropy coding, which is a relatively simple scheme. How would the performance change if a more sophisticated entropy coding method (like those used in JPEG 2000) were employed, and how does this affect the comparison to standard methods?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong contribution to the field of image compression, with a well-motivated approach, sound technical execution, and compelling empirical results. The paper makes a clear case for the benefits of end-to-end optimization of the entire compression pipeline, and the results demonstrate significant improvements over standard methods in both rate-distortion performance and visual quality. The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field that is worthy of publication at ICLR. The suggested improvements would enhance the paper further but are not required for acceptance.
