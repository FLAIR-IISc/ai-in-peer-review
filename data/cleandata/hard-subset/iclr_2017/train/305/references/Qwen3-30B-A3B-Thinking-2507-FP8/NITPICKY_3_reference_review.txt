

# Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## Summary of Claims
The paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression framework using nonlinear transforms (specifically Generalized Divisive Normalization or GDN) that jointly optimizes the analysis transform, synthesis transform, and entropy code. The authors claim their method achieves better rate-distortion performance than JPEG and JPEG 2000, with visually superior images that exhibit fewer artifacts. The approach uses a continuous relaxation of the quantization process during training, followed by actual quantization during inference, and reports performance using actual bit rates rather than entropy estimates.

## Decision
Reject

## Key Reasons for Rejection
1. **Critical Lack of Technical Detail on Quantization Process**: The paper states "the continuous relaxation using additive noise was used only for training purposes" but fails to provide a clear, complete description of how quantization is performed during inference (actual compression). This is a fundamental omission that prevents reproducibility and undermines the paper's core claim about practical implementation. Without knowing the quantization step size, the actual compression process, or how the discrete representation is generated, the claimed bit rates and performance cannot be verified.

2. **Insufficient Experimental Rigor**: The paper claims "better rate–distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images" without providing statistical significance testing, standard deviations, or confidence intervals. The lack of proper statistical analysis makes it impossible to determine whether the reported improvements are meaningful or merely due to random variation across the test set. Additionally, the paper doesn't compare against more recent compression standards (e.g., WebP, BPG, JPEG-XL), which are now standard benchmarks in the field.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper contains several technical inconsistencies that compound the issues with the experimental evaluation. For instance, the paper states "the net output thus has half the dimensionality of the input" but doesn't clarify how this dimensionality reduction affects the compression ratio or quality. The relationship between the continuous relaxation during training and the actual quantization during inference is not sufficiently explained, creating a gap between the proposed method and its implementation.

The paper's comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 is also problematic. The authors state they use "JPEG with 4:2:0 chroma subsampling" but don't specify whether they're using the standard JPEG implementation or a custom one. Similarly, for JPEG 2000, they reference "OpenJPEG implementation" but don't provide details about the specific parameters used (e.g., wavelet transform type, rate-distortion optimization method). Without these details, the comparison is not reproducible.

The paper also contains formatting inconsistencies, such as inconsistent use of "λ" vs. "lambda" for the trade-off parameter, and unclear references to "the two largest values of λ" without specifying what these values are. These minor issues accumulate and undermine the paper's credibility.

## Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen this paper, the authors should:
1. Provide a complete, step-by-step description of the quantization process during inference, including how the quantization step size is determined and applied.
2. Include statistical significance testing for all performance claims, reporting standard deviations and confidence intervals for PSNR and MS-SSIM results.
3. Compare against more recent compression standards (WebP, BPG, JPEG-XL) to provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
4. Clarify the relationship between the continuous relaxation during training and the actual discrete quantization during compression.
5. Provide more details about the ImageNet subset used for training to ensure it's representative of the test images.

## Questions for Authors
1. Can you provide a detailed, step-by-step explanation of how quantization is performed during inference (actual compression), including the quantization step size and how it's determined?
2. Why did you choose to optimize separately for each λ value rather than using a single model for all rate points, as is done in Toderici et al. (2016)?
3. How did you ensure the ImageNet subset used for training is representative of the Kodak and other test images used for evaluation?
4. What specific parameters did you use for the OpenJPEG implementation of JPEG 2000, and why were these chosen?
5. How did you determine the "two largest values of λ" for which you increased network capacity, and why was this necessary for those specific values?
