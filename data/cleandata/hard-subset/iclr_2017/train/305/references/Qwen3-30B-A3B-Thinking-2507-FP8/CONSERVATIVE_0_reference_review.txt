

# Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel image compression framework that uses end-to-end optimization of nonlinear transforms to achieve better rate-distortion performance than traditional methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The authors introduce a cascade of generalized divisive normalization (GDN) transforms followed by uniform scalar quantization, with parameters optimized using a continuous relaxation of the quantization process. They claim that their method not only achieves better PSNR performance but also produces visually superior images with fewer artifacts than conventional transform coding methods. The paper provides theoretical justification for their approach by relating it to variational autoencoders, while emphasizing key differences in the rate-distortion optimization context.

## 2. Decision: Reject

My decision to reject is based on two key reasons:
1. The paper lacks sufficient empirical evidence to support its claims of substantial performance improvements over established methods, particularly regarding the practical implementation and deployment of the proposed method.
2. The comparison to standard compression methods is not sufficiently rigorous or fair, as it does not employ the optimized entropy coding frameworks used in JPEG and JPEG 2000, which significantly impacts the measured bit rates.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unconventional approaches without extensive validation, I have several concerns about this paper:

First, the paper's claim of "substantial improvements" across all test images and bit rates is not sufficiently supported by statistical evidence. The results section shows some improvements in PSNR and MS-SSIM, but the paper fails to provide statistical significance testing or analysis of the variability in results across different images. Without this, it's impossible to determine whether the observed improvements are meaningful or merely due to random variation in the test set.

Second, the comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 is fundamentally flawed. The paper states they "implemented a simple entropy code based on the context-based adaptive binary arithmetic coding framework (CABAC)," but they don't use the optimized entropy coding that is standard in these established methods. The paper acknowledges this limitation in section 6.2, noting "the performance gains compared to a well-designed non-adaptive entropy code are relatively small (figure 10), and likely smaller than those achieved by the entropy code in JPEG 2000." This admission undermines their main claim of superior performance, as they're comparing against a suboptimal implementation of their own method while using the standard (optimized) entropy coding for JPEG 2000.

Third, the paper's approach to "end-to-end optimization" relies on a continuous relaxation of quantization, which is mathematically convenient but may not translate well to practical implementation. The authors acknowledge this in section 4: "the continuously-relaxed loss function" provides "a good approximation" to actual rate-distortion values, but they don't provide sufficient evidence that this approximation is reliable across a wide range of bit rates and image content.

Fourth, the paper lacks a thorough discussion of computational complexity and practical implementation challenges. While they note that "encoding or decoding an image with the trained models is efficient," they don't provide concrete comparisons of encoding/decoding times or memory requirements to standard methods. This is particularly important for a compression method, as practical utility depends heavily on implementation efficiency.

Finally, the paper's claim that their method "offers improvements in rate-distortion performance over JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most images and bit rates" is not sufficiently substantiated. The paper shows results for a limited set of test images (Kodak, Lena, Barbara, etc.), but doesn't demonstrate consistency across a broader range of image content or modern image compression benchmarks. The authors acknowledge this limitation in section 6.3: "summarizations of rate-distortion are of limited use" and direct readers to their website for individual image comparisons, which is not sufficient for a conference paper.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen this work for future publication, I recommend the authors:

1. Conduct a more rigorous comparison with standard compression methods by implementing the same entropy coding frameworks used in JPEG and JPEG 2000 (e.g., using the same CABAC implementation as in H.264). This would provide a fair comparison of the core compression approach, independent of the entropy coding implementation.

2. Provide statistical significance testing for their performance claims across multiple test images. Without this, it's impossible to determine whether the observed improvements are meaningful.

3. Include a thorough analysis of computational complexity, including encoding/decoding times and memory requirements, compared to standard methods. This is critical for assessing the practical utility of the proposed method.

4. Expand the evaluation to include more modern image compression benchmarks and a wider variety of image content to demonstrate consistent performance improvements across diverse scenarios.

5. Provide a more detailed discussion of the limitations of their continuous relaxation approach and how it affects practical implementation. The paper acknowledges this as a limitation but doesn't fully address how it might impact real-world deployment.

## Questions for Authors

1. How do you reconcile your claim of "substantial improvements" with your own admission that "the performance gains compared to a well-designed non-adaptive entropy code are relatively small" (section 6.2)? Would the observed improvements remain significant if you used the optimized entropy coding frameworks of JPEG and JPEG 2000?

2. The paper states that "our method exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images, especially at the lower bit rates." What is the statistical significance of this claim across the full set of test images? Are the differences large enough to be practically meaningful?

3. How do you plan to address the computational cost of training the model (which you note is "quite high") in practical applications? Would the high training cost be prohibitive for real-world deployment?

4. The paper acknowledges that "the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested, and at all bit rates." Given that the method was optimized using MSE (not a perceptual metric), how do you explain this consistent perceptual improvement? Is there a fundamental reason why MSE optimization would produce perceptually better results in this specific case?
