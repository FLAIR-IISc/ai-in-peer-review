

# Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression framework using nonlinear transforms (GDN/IGDN cascades) that jointly optimizes rate and distortion. The authors claim their method outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 in both rate-distortion performance (PSNR) and visual quality (MS-SSIM), with a more natural appearance of compressed images. The approach uses a continuous relaxation of the quantization process to enable gradient-based optimization, followed by an entropy code to report actual bit rates. The paper provides a thorough comparison to existing methods and demonstrates qualitative improvements in image quality.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper contains multiple technical inconsistencies and methodological ambiguities that undermine the validity of the claimed results. While the core idea is interesting, the presentation fails to provide sufficient mathematical rigor to support the claims, and the evaluation methodology contains critical flaws.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Rejection

### Mathematical Inconsistencies
The paper's mathematical formulation contains several critical inconsistencies that render the relaxation approach questionable:

1. In equation (9), the authors define "Pqi(n) = ∫_{n-1/2}^{n+1/2} pyi(t) dt" but later claim in equation (10) that "pỹ(n) = Pq(n)". This is mathematically incorrect: pỹ is a continuous density function while Pq(n) is a discrete probability mass function. The paper fails to distinguish between these fundamentally different mathematical objects.

2. Equation (3) for GDN operation is ambiguously written: "u^(k+1)_i (m,n) = w^(k)_i (m,n)( βk,i + ∑j γk,ij ( w^(k)_j (m,n) )^2 )^(1/2)". The square root should apply to the entire expression, not just the sum of squares. The notation is ambiguous and could lead to misinterpretation.

3. The paper claims the continuous relaxation "approximates quantization error in terms of its marginal moments" but fails to provide any mathematical justification for why the uniform noise approximation is valid for the joint distribution, not just marginal moments.

### Evaluation Methodology Flaws
The evaluation methodology contains critical flaws that undermine the claimed performance improvements:

1. The paper states "we implemented a simple entropy code based on the context-based adaptive binary arithmetic coding framework (CABAC)" and later admits "the performance gains compared to a well-designed non-adaptive entropy code are relatively small (figure 10), and likely smaller than those achieved by the entropy code in JPEG 2000." This directly contradicts the paper's claim of reporting "actual bit rates" comparable to standard methods. The entropy coding is a critical component of compression, and using a suboptimal entropy code undermines the comparison.

2. The paper claims "our method exhibits better rate–distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images, especially at the lower bit rates" but provides no quantitative definition of "most" or statistical evidence to support this claim. This is a significant understatement of the results and fails to provide sufficient evidence for the claimed superiority.

3. The paper uses "Kodak image dataset" without specifying the exact number of images or their characteristics, making it impossible to assess the representativeness of the evaluation.

### Formatting and Presentation Issues
Multiple formatting inconsistencies undermine the paper's credibility:

1. References are inconsistently formatted (e.g., "Ballé et al., 2015" vs. "Ballé, Johannes, Valero Laparra, Eero P. Simoncelli, In: arXiv e-prints. Presented at the 4th Int. Conf. for Learning Representations, 2016." with inconsistent use of "et al." and "and").

2. The paper mentions "Figure 1" but the provided text does not include any figure, making it impossible to assess the proposed architecture.

3. The paper uses inconsistent spacing before parentheses (e.g., "d(z, ẑ) = ‖z − ẑ‖22" has inconsistent spacing around the comma and minus sign).

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Mathematical Rigor**: The authors should provide a clear mathematical justification for the continuous relaxation, explicitly showing why the uniform noise approximation is valid for the joint distribution, not just marginal moments. The relationship between continuous and discrete formulations needs to be rigorously established.

2. **Entropy Coding**: The authors should implement a more sophisticated entropy code comparable to those used in JPEG 2000 (e.g., using the same context models and adaptive coding techniques) to ensure a fair comparison. The current CABAC implementation is clearly suboptimal.

3. **Quantitative Claims**: The paper should provide statistical evidence to support the claim that their method "outperforms for most test images" rather than making vague qualitative statements. A clear definition of "most" (e.g., "80% of images") would strengthen the claim.

4. **Reference Formatting**: The references should be consistently formatted according to ICLR standards (e.g., using "et al." consistently, proper use of "and" in author lists, and standard citation styles).

5. **Figure Inclusion**: The paper should include all figures referenced in the text to allow readers to properly understand the proposed architecture and experimental results.

## Key Questions for Authors

1. Could you provide a mathematical proof showing that the continuous relaxation (with uniform noise) accurately approximates the discrete rate-distortion optimization problem for the joint distribution, not just marginal distributions?

2. Given that your entropy code is explicitly stated to be less efficient than JPEG 2000's entropy code, how do you account for this in your comparison, and what would the performance be with a more optimal entropy code?

3. Could you provide the exact number of images in the Kodak dataset used for evaluation, and explain how representative it is of the broader image compression problem?

4. Why were the specific numbers 256 and 192 chosen for the increased network capacity in the largest λ and RGB models, and how does this affect the fairness of the comparison with JPEG and JPEG 2000?
