

# Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper presents an end-to-end optimized framework for image compression using nonlinear transforms, addressing the long-standing challenge of jointly optimizing the three components of transform coding (transform, quantizer, and entropy code). The authors propose a model consisting of cascaded linear convolutions followed by Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN), which is optimized using stochastic gradient descent. They overcome the non-differentiability of quantization through a continuous relaxation with additive uniform noise, allowing the entire compression pipeline to be trained end-to-end. The approach achieves both improved rate-distortion performance (measured by PSNR) and superior perceptual quality (measured by MS-SSIM) compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, with the added benefit of using actual bit rates rather than entropy estimates.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression with a technically sound approach that provides strong empirical evidence for its claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper effectively addresses the three key questions for assessment:

1. The specific problem tackled is the joint optimization of the entire lossy image compression pipeline, which has traditionally been optimized in separate components. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches (transform coding methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000) and the intractability of optimal quantization in high-dimensional spaces.

2. The approach is well motivated and well-placed in the literature. The paper provides a thorough literature review that positions their work relative to transform coding, variational autoencoders, and previous attempts at optimizing image representations for compression. The distinction between their approach and generative models is particularly well-explained, addressing a key concern in the field.

3. The paper strongly supports its claims with both theoretical justification and comprehensive empirical evaluation. The continuous relaxation technique is rigorously derived and validated through experiments showing that the relaxed loss function provides a good approximation to the actual rate-distortion values. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements across multiple metrics (PSNR, MS-SSIM) and test images, with detailed visual comparisons that clearly show the qualitative advantages of their method over traditional approaches. The authors provide sufficient implementation details to allow reproducibility, including the use of actual bit rates rather than entropy estimates.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, the authors could:

- Provide a more detailed ablation study on the impact of different activation functions (e.g., ReLU vs. GDN) on compression performance and parameter efficiency. While the paper mentions that GDN/IGDN transforms are more efficient, a quantitative comparison would be valuable.

- Include a more thorough analysis of the entropy coding component. The paper uses a relatively simple CABAC-based entropy code, which may not fully leverage the statistical properties of the quantized representation. A comparison with more sophisticated entropy coding techniques would provide clearer insight into the relative contributions of the transform and entropy coding.

- Clarify the relationship between the continuous relaxation and the final discrete compression performance. While the paper demonstrates that the relaxation provides a good approximation, a more detailed analysis of how the approximation error propagates to the final compression performance would strengthen the theoretical justification.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- The paper states that GDN/IGDN transforms are more efficient than pointwise nonlinearities, requiring fewer parameters/stages. Could you provide specific numbers on the parameter counts and the exact performance comparisons for different activation functions?

- The paper mentions a positive bias in the entropy approximation for coarser quantization regimes. How does this bias affect the final compression performance, and how did the authors address it during optimization?

- The paper claims "substantial improvements across all test images and bit rates" for MS-SSIM. Could you provide specific quantitative metrics (e.g., average percentage improvement) across the entire test set to better quantify the magnitude of these improvements?
