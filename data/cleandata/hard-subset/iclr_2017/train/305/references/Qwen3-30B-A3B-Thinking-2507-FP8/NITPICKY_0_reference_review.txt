

# Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on nonlinear transforms, specifically using cascaded linear convolutions with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and inverse GDN (IGDN) operations. The authors claim to achieve superior rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, with visually more natural compressed images despite being optimized for MSE rather than perceptual metrics. They introduce a continuous relaxation of quantization to enable gradient-based optimization and implement a practical entropy code for evaluation.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper contains significant technical inconsistencies and contradictory claims that undermine its core contributions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Rejection

The most critical issue is a fundamental contradiction between the paper's main claim and its own evidence. In the abstract and conclusion, the authors state: "the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested, and at all bit rates." However, in section 6.3, they explicitly state: "For the image in figure 15, our method only slightly outperforms JPEG 2000 at low rates, and under-performs it at high rates." This is a clear contradiction that cannot be reconciled. The claim of universal perceptual advantages is directly contradicted by their own results, which severely undermines the paper's central contribution.

A second critical issue is a significant technical typo that appears to be a major error in the mathematical formulation. In equation (3), the GDN operation is written as:

u^(k+1)_i(m,n) = w^(k)_i(m,n)( βk,i + ∑_j γk,ij ( w^(k)_j(m,n) )^2) 12

The exponent "12" is clearly a typo for "1/2" (as used consistently elsewhere in the paper). This is not a minor formatting issue but a critical mathematical error that would mislead readers about the actual operation. The exponent determines the mathematical properties of the normalization, and the incorrect value would change the behavior of the model.

The paper also makes an unjustified claim about using CABAC for image compression. CABAC is a video compression standard (H.264/AVC), not an image compression standard. The authors state "We implemented an entropy code based on the context-adaptive binary arithmetic coding (CABAC) framework," but they fail to justify why CABAC is appropriate for image compression or explain how it differs from implementations in standard image compression methods. This lack of justification is particularly concerning since entropy coding is a critical component of compression performance.

Additionally, the paper contains inconsistent notation. In equation (8), it states "ŷi = qi = round(yi)" but round(yi) is a discrete operation, while qi is supposed to represent the quantized value. The notation is confusing and could lead to misinterpretation.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Clarify the perceptual advantage claim**: The paper must either retract the claim that "perceptual advantages hold for all images" or provide a more nuanced analysis explaining why the method performs better on most images but not all, and why the counterexample (figure 15) is not representative.

2. **Correct the mathematical error**: The exponent in equation (3) must be corrected from "12" to "1/2" throughout the paper. This is a critical error that needs to be addressed immediately.

3. **Justify entropy coding choice**: Provide a clear explanation of why CABAC is appropriate for image compression, including a comparison to standard image compression entropy coding methods (e.g., Huffman coding, arithmetic coding used in JPEG).

4. **Improve consistency in notation**: Standardize the notation for quantized values (qi) and rounding operations to avoid confusion.

5. **Clarify dimensionality statement**: The claim that "the net output thus has half the dimensionality of the input" is vague. Specify whether this refers to spatial dimension, channel dimension, or both.

6. **Provide statistical evidence**: The claim that the method "exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images" lacks statistical support. Provide quantitative evidence about what "most" means (e.g., percentage of images).

7. **Address reference formatting inconsistencies**: The references contain numerous formatting inconsistencies (e.g., "Ballé et al.,? 2015" should be "Ballé et al., 2015"), which detract from the paper's professionalism.

8. **Clarify the relationship between perceptual metrics and MSE optimization**: The paper claims "the compressed images are much more natural in appearance than those compressed with JPEG or JPEG 2000" despite being optimized for MSE. This is a key claim that needs a more rigorous explanation.

The paper presents an interesting approach to image compression with potential value, but the fundamental contradiction in claims and the critical mathematical error significantly undermine its credibility. These issues are not minor but directly challenge the paper's main contributions. The authors should address these issues before the paper can be considered for publication.
