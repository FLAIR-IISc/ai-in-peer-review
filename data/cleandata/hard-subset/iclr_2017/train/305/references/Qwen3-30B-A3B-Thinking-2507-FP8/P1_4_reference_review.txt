

# Peer Review: END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper presents a novel framework for end-to-end optimization of image compression based on nonlinear transforms. The authors propose a model using cascades of linear convolutions and generalized divisive normalization (GDN) as the analysis transform, followed by uniform scalar quantization and an approximate inverse transform for reconstruction. The key innovation is the joint optimization of the entire compression pipeline (analysis/synthesis transforms, quantization, and entropy coding) using stochastic gradient descent, with a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization. The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves better rate-distortion performance than JPEG and JPEG 2000 across most images and bit rates, while producing visually superior images with fewer artifacts. The paper also provides a thorough analysis of the relationship between their approach and variational autoencoders, highlighting fundamental differences between compression and generative modeling.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance of this paper.

**Key reasons for acceptance:**
1. The paper addresses a fundamental problem in image compression with a well-motivated and novel approach that has been rigorously validated.
2. The results are impressive, showing both quantitative improvements in rate-distortion performance and qualitative improvements in visual quality that are clearly evident in the provided examples.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression. Traditional methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000 have dominated the field for decades but suffer from artifacts due to their linear transform-based approaches. The authors' end-to-end optimized nonlinear transform approach demonstrates clear advantages over these established methods.

The paper is well-written and thoroughly motivated. The literature review is comprehensive, placing their work in context with both classical transform coding methods and recent deep learning approaches. The explanation of the continuous relaxation of quantization (a critical technical contribution) is clear and well-justified, with empirical evidence showing the relaxation provides a good approximation to the discrete problem.

The experimental evaluation is robust and comprehensive. The authors compare their method to standard benchmarks (JPEG, JPEG 2000) using both PSNR and MS-SSIM metrics across multiple datasets (Kodak, standard test images, and their own photographs). The visual comparisons in Figures 5-7 are particularly compelling, showing that their method produces images with fewer artifacts and more natural appearance. The paper also provides a thoughtful discussion of the relationship between their approach and variational autoencoders, explaining why their framework differs fundamentally from generative modeling approaches.

The paper's contribution is especially significant because it demonstrates that end-to-end optimization of the entire compression pipeline can lead to substantial improvements over traditional methods that optimize components separately. This is a powerful demonstration of the potential of deep learning for classical signal processing problems.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- While the paper provides impressive results, it would strengthen the claims to include a comparison to more recent compression methods like those proposed by Toderici et al. (2016) or Gregor et al. (2016), to better position the work in the current landscape of image compression research.

- The paper mentions that "for the image in figure 15, our method only slightly outperforms JPEG 2000 at low rates, and under-performs it at high rates." It would be helpful to provide a more detailed analysis of why this specific image behaves differently, which could inform future work.

- The claim that "the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested, and at all bit rates" would be strengthened by a statistical analysis of the results across the entire test set (e.g., paired t-tests comparing PSNR and MS-SSIM values).

- The paper states that GDN/IGDN transforms are more efficient than pointwise nonlinearities, requiring fewer model parameters/stages. Including specific numbers about the parameter count differences for equivalent performance would provide concrete evidence for this claim.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper mentions that "for RGB images, we trained a separate set of models, with the first stage augmented to operate across three (color) input channels." How does this approach compare to a joint optimization of all color channels in the first stage, and why was this architecture chosen?

2. The authors state that "the visual improvement arises because the cascade of biologically-inspired nonlinear transformations in the model have been optimized to capture the features and attributes of images that are represented in the statistics of the data." Could you provide more specific analysis of which features or attributes are better captured by this approach compared to traditional methods?

3. The paper mentions that "GDN represents a multivariate generalization of a particular type of sigmoidal function." Could you elaborate on how this mathematical property relates to the observed efficiency advantage in compression?

4. The authors note that "the additive noise used in the objective function as a relaxation of quantization might also serve the purpose of making supervised classification networks more robust to small perturbations." Have you tested this hypothesis on standard classification benchmarks to validate this claim?

5. The paper claims that "the rate–distortion objective can be seen as a particular instantiation of the general unsupervised learning or density estimation problems." How might this insight be leveraged to improve other unsupervised learning tasks beyond image compression?

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field with a well-executed study that demonstrates significant improvements over established methods. The authors have provided a compelling case for the value of end-to-end optimization in image compression, and the results are both scientifically rigorous and practically relevant. I believe this work will be of significant interest to the ICLR community.
