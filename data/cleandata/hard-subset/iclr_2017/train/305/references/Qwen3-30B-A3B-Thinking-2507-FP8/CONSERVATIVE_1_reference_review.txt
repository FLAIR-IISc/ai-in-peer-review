

# Review for "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

1. **Summary of Claims and Contribution**: The paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression framework using nonlinear transforms (specifically, cascades of linear convolutions with generalized divisive normalization - GDN) instead of traditional linear transform coding (like DCT in JPEG or wavelets in JPEG 2000). The authors claim their method achieves better rate-distortion performance than JPEG and JPEG 2000, with significantly improved perceptual quality (as measured by MS-SSIM), despite being trained with MSE as the distortion metric. They argue that their approach provides a more natural appearance of compressed images by better preserving contours and edges while eliminating common artifacts of linear transform coding.

2. **Decision**: Reject. The paper lacks sufficient evidence to support its claims of substantial improvements over established methods, and the approach does not demonstrate sufficient rigor to justify its novelty.

3. **Supporting Arguments**:
   - The paper's primary claim of "substantial improvements" across all test images and bit rates is not adequately supported. The authors provide qualitative examples and MS-SSIM results, but fail to demonstrate statistical significance or provide comprehensive comparisons beyond JPEG and JPEG 2000. The Kodak dataset is small and not representative of modern image compression challenges.
   - The paper does not sufficiently address whether the specific GDN nonlinearities are necessary for the claimed improvements. The authors mention "preliminary results indicate that qualitatively similar results are achievable with other activation functions," but this is presented as a footnote without proper ablation studies. Without demonstrating that GDN is fundamentally better than simpler nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU), the methodological contribution appears overstated.
   - The comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 is incomplete. The paper does not compare against more recent compression methods (e.g., WebP, BPG, or other deep learning-based approaches), which have been developed specifically to address the limitations of traditional methods. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their comparison in Section 6.3 but do not adequately address them.
   - The paper's claim that "the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested, and at all bit rates" is not sufficiently verified. The authors present a few examples but do not provide statistical evidence that the improvements are consistent across a large, diverse image set.
   - The paper's theoretical justification for the approach is weak. While they draw connections to variational autoencoders, they acknowledge significant differences between their rate-distortion optimization problem and generative modeling. The paper does not provide a rigorous theoretical foundation for why their approach would outperform established methods.

4. **Additional Feedback for Improvement**:
   - The authors should conduct comprehensive comparisons against a wider range of modern image compression methods, including both traditional and deep learning-based approaches. This would provide a more meaningful context for their claims.
   - The paper should include proper ablation studies to determine the necessity of GDN versus simpler nonlinearities. They should quantify how much of the performance gain is due to the specific nonlinearities versus the end-to-end optimization framework itself.
   - The authors should provide more rigorous statistical analysis of their results, including confidence intervals and significance testing, to demonstrate that the claimed improvements are not merely due to random variation in the test set.
   - The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the computational complexity and practical deployment considerations. While they mention that encoding/decoding is efficient, they do not provide concrete metrics (e.g., time per image, memory requirements) that would be relevant for real-world applications.
   - The authors should clarify whether the "substantial improvements" in perceptual quality are consistent across different types of images (e.g., natural scenes vs. synthetic images) and whether the improvements hold for a wider range of distortion metrics.

**Questions for Authors**:
1. Can you provide statistical evidence that the rate-distortion improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000 are significant across the entire Kodak dataset, rather than just for a few representative images?
2. How do you justify the claim that GDN is necessary for the improvements, given your preliminary results showing similar performance with other nonlinearities? What specific properties of GDN contribute to the performance gains?
3. Why did you choose to compare only against JPEG and JPEG 2000, rather than more recent compression methods that have been specifically designed to outperform these baselines?
4. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and resource requirements of your method, particularly for deployment in real-world applications with constrained resources?

The paper presents an interesting approach that builds on previous work in nonlinear image representations, but it falls short of the rigorous validation required to support its claims of substantial improvements over established methods. Without more comprehensive evidence and a better contextualization within the broader literature on image compression, the contribution does not meet ICLR's standards for acceptance.
