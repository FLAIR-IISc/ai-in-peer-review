

# Review for "End-to-End Optimized Image Compression"

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper presents a fundamentally new approach to image compression by introducing an end-to-end optimized framework that jointly optimizes the entire compression pipeline (analysis transform, quantizer, synthesis transform, and entropy code) using deep learning techniques. The authors propose a novel nonlinear transform architecture inspired by biological visual systems (using Generalized Divisive Normalization or GDN), which they optimize directly for rate-distortion performance using stochastic gradient descent. The key innovation is the continuous relaxation of quantization during optimization, allowing the entire pipeline to be trained end-to-end without the need for separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy code components. The authors demonstrate that their method significantly outperforms traditional approaches (JPEG, JPEG 2000) in both rate-distortion performance and perceptual quality across a range of images.

## 2. Decision

Accept

## 3. Supporting Arguments

This paper makes a bold and novel contribution to a classic problem that has been studied for decades. The authors successfully shift the paradigm from traditional separate optimization of compression components to a unified end-to-end learning approach, which is a significant conceptual advance. This is not merely an incremental improvement but a new direction in image compression research.

The novelty of the approach is compelling: using GDN-based nonlinear transforms optimized end-to-end for rate-distortion performance, rather than using hand-crafted linear transforms. The paper's key insight that the continuous relaxation of quantization allows for gradient-based optimization of the entire pipeline is elegant and well-justified. The authors carefully distinguish their approach from variational autoencoders (which they correctly note are fundamentally different in purpose and optimization regime), and their empirical results convincingly demonstrate that this approach leads to superior image quality with fewer artifacts compared to traditional methods.

The paper's most impressive contribution is the visual quality improvement: the compressed images have a more natural appearance with fewer blocking artifacts, even though the method was optimized using MSE (a metric that doesn't directly correlate with perceptual quality). This suggests the nonlinear transforms have learned to better represent image features that align with human visual perception, which is a significant insight. The authors demonstrate this across multiple image datasets and bit rates, with both quantitative (MS-SSIM) and qualitative evidence.

While the paper has some minor limitations (e.g., the entropy code implementation could be improved), these don't detract from the fundamental novelty and significance of the approach. The authors have made a clear, bold contribution that challenges the established engineering approach to image compression and opens a new research direction.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I would recommend:

1. A more detailed comparison of computational complexity between the proposed method and traditional compression methods, especially regarding the training vs. inference costs. While the paper notes that encoding/decoding is efficient, the computational requirements for training the model could be a barrier to adoption.

2. A brief exploration of how the method might generalize to video compression, which would help contextualize the potential impact of this work beyond static images.

3. A more thorough discussion of the limitations of the current approach. For example, how does the method perform on images with extreme content (e.g., text, logos, or highly structured patterns) that might not align well with the statistics the model was trained on?

4. A clearer explanation of why GDN specifically works better than other nonlinearities (beyond the brief mention in section 5), as this could provide deeper theoretical insights into why the approach succeeds.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper mentions that "GDN represents a multivariate generalization of a particular type of sigmoidal function." Could you elaborate on how this specific form of nonlinearity relates to the rate-distortion optimization problem, and why other nonlinearities might not be as effective?

2. The authors state that their method "exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images, especially at the lower bit rates." Could you provide more insight into why the method underperforms at higher bit rates for some images? Is this a limitation of the current model architecture, or does it reflect a fundamental trade-off?

3. In section 3.1, the authors note that "the discretization of luminance values is usually considered a nuisance" for generative models. Could you elaborate on how your approach to handling quantization (via continuous relaxation) might inform other areas of machine learning that deal with discrete representations?
