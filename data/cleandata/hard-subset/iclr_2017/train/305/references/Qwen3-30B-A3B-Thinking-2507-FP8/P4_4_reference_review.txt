

# Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression framework using nonlinear transforms (cascades of linear convolutions and generalized divisive normalization [GDN] nonlinearities) followed by uniform scalar quantization and entropy coding. The authors develop a relaxed optimization approach that enables joint training of the analysis/synthesis transforms and entropy code via stochastic gradient descent, addressing the intractability of direct rate-distortion optimization. The method achieves superior rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, with visually superior results that lack the blocking and ringing artifacts characteristic of traditional transform coding methods.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper presents a well-motivated, technically sound contribution that makes a significant advancement in image compression. The key reasons for acceptance are the paper's rigorous theoretical foundation for end-to-end optimization and compelling empirical results demonstrating superior performance across multiple metrics.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper addresses the fundamental problem of image compression with a clear, well-motivated approach. The authors effectively position their work within the literature, building upon prior research on transform coding (JPEG, JPEG 2000), Gaussianizing image densities (Ballé et al., 2015), and variational autoencoders. They clearly articulate the limitations of traditional approaches and justify their use of cascaded nonlinear transforms with GDN.

The theoretical foundation is strong and well-explained. The paper derives a continuous relaxation of the rate-distortion optimization problem that enables end-to-end training using stochastic gradient descent. This relaxation carefully addresses the intractability of quantization in the optimization process while maintaining a connection to the discrete problem. The relationship to variational autoencoders is thoroughly examined, with clear explanations of both similarities and critical differences (particularly regarding discrete vs. continuous domains and optimization objectives).

The empirical results are compelling and scientifically rigorous. The authors provide comprehensive comparisons to JPEG and JPEG 2000 across multiple metrics (PSNR, MS-SSIM) and image sets. The visual comparisons in figures 5-7 clearly demonstrate the qualitative improvements in reconstructed images, with reduced blocking artifacts and preserved edge sharpness. The paper includes detailed experimental setup, ablation studies, and thorough analysis of the rate-distortion trade-off. The authors also address the important distinction between continuous relaxation for optimization and actual bit rate evaluation, which strengthens their empirical claims.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
- The paper could include a more detailed analysis of computational complexity (both training and inference) compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, which would help assess practical deployability.
- A more comprehensive comparison with other deep learning-based compression methods (e.g., Toderici et al., 2016) would strengthen the positioning of the work within the broader landscape of neural image compression.
- The discussion of architectural choices (e.g., number of stages, filter sizes) could be expanded with quantitative analysis of how these choices impact performance and model size.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that "our method exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images, especially at lower bit rates." Could the authors provide more specific details about which images performed worse and the conditions under which this occurs?

2. The authors mention that "GDN/IGDN transforms are more efficient for compression, producing better models with fewer stages of processing." Could they provide quantitative evidence for this claim, such as comparing the number of parameters required for similar performance?

3. The paper uses MSE as the distortion metric during training but shows improvements in perceptual quality (MS-SSIM). Could the authors discuss how their method might be adapted to directly optimize for perceptual metrics, potentially leading to even better visual quality?

4. The paper states that "the relaxed (differential) entropy provides a somewhat positively biased estimate of the discrete entropy for the coarser quantization regime, but the bias disappears for finer quantization." Could the authors provide more details about how this bias affects the practical implementation of their method, particularly at low bit rates?
