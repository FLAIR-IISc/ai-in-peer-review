

# Review for "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper introduces a novel end-to-end framework for image compression that fundamentally challenges traditional transform coding approaches. The authors propose optimizing the entire compression pipeline—including analysis transform, quantization, and synthesis transform—jointly using gradient-based methods, rather than optimizing these components separately as is standard practice (e.g., in JPEG and JPEG 2000). Their key innovation is using cascaded nonlinear transforms with Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN), inspired by biological visual systems, and implementing a continuous relaxation of quantization to enable gradient-based optimization. The method achieves significant improvements in both rate-distortion performance and visual quality compared to existing methods, with compressed images exhibiting substantially fewer artifacts and more natural appearance. Crucially, the authors demonstrate that optimizing for MSE yields perceptual improvements that are not captured by traditional metrics alone.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a bold, novel contribution to a well-studied problem by introducing a fundamentally new approach to image compression. The end-to-end optimization framework with nonlinear transforms represents a significant departure from conventional wisdom in the field, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate both quantitative and qualitative improvements.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's novelty is its most compelling aspect. While prior work has explored some aspects of nonlinear transforms for compression, this paper presents the first complete framework for end-to-end optimization of the entire compression pipeline using gradient-based methods. The authors' use of GDN/IGDN transforms—inspired by biological visual systems and previously shown to be effective in Gaussianizing image densities—provides a theoretically grounded approach to capturing the complex statistics of natural images.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly placing its work in the context of existing literature while highlighting the limitations of current approaches. The distinction between their method and variational autoencoders (section 3.1) is particularly insightful and well-justified. The authors carefully address the key challenge of optimizing with quantization (a non-differentiable operation) through a continuous relaxation that they empirically verify is effective.

The results are compelling: the method demonstrates superior rate-distortion performance across multiple standard datasets, with substantial improvements in both PSNR (a traditional metric) and MS-SSIM (a perceptual metric). The visual examples provided (figures 5 and 6) clearly demonstrate the qualitative improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000, with the proposed method producing images that preserve contours and edges more naturally while avoiding the blocking and ringing artifacts characteristic of linear transform coding.

This paper exemplifies the type of bold, novel approach that ICLR values most highly. While there are minor methodological aspects (like the continuous relaxation of quantization), the authors acknowledge these limitations and provide careful empirical validation. The significance of the contribution outweighs these minor concerns.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper:

- Include a brief comparison of computational complexity between your method and JPEG/JPEG 2000. While the paper mentions that encoding/decoding is efficient, a quantitative comparison would help assess practical adoption potential.

- Expand on the discussion of GDN's efficiency advantage over simpler nonlinearities (ReLU, sigmoid). Your preliminary results in section 5 suggest GDN/IGDN are more efficient, but a more detailed comparison would strengthen your argument.

- Consider adding a brief discussion of how your method might scale to video compression, which would naturally extend your image compression framework.

- Clarify the relationship between the continuous relaxation used for optimization and the actual bit rates achieved. While you note the relaxation is well-approximated, a more detailed analysis of the gap between relaxed and actual rates would be valuable.

## Questions for Authors

1. How does your method's computational complexity compare to JPEG and JPEG 2000 in practice? Is it feasible for real-time applications on mobile or embedded devices?

2. You mention testing simpler nonlinearities but only provide preliminary results. Could you share more details on how the performance degrades with ReLU or sigmoid activations compared to GDN?

3. Have you considered how your approach might be adapted for video compression, where temporal coherence could provide additional optimization opportunities?

4. The paper mentions that the continuous relaxation might lead to misleading results, but you verify it empirically. Could you elaborate on the specific conditions where this approximation might be problematic?

5. How does the method perform at extremely low bit rates (e.g., below 0.1 bpp)? The paper shows improvements across most bit rates, but the behavior at the lowest rates might be particularly interesting.
