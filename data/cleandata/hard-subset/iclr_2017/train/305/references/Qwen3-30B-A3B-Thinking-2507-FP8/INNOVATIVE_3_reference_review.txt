

# Review for "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims/contribution
This paper presents a groundbreaking approach to image compression by introducing a complete end-to-end optimized framework that jointly optimizes the entire compression pipeline (analysis transform, synthesis transform, and entropy coding) using nonlinear transforms. The authors build upon their previous work with Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) to create a cascade of nonlinear transformations that effectively learn to represent image statistics in a way that optimizes the rate-distortion trade-off. Their key insight is using a continuous relaxation of quantization to enable gradient-based optimization, allowing them to train a model that significantly outperforms traditional methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000 in both rate-distortion performance and perceptual quality. The paper demonstrates that their method produces visually superior images with fewer artifacts, even when optimized using MSE as the distortion metric.

## 2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) The paper introduces a fundamentally novel approach to image compression that challenges the established paradigm of separately optimizing transform, quantizer, and entropy code components; (2) The authors demonstrate significant improvements in both rate-distortion performance and perceptual quality across multiple datasets and bit rates, with visual results that are consistently superior to established methods.

## 3. Supporting arguments
This paper stands out for its bold, novel approach to a classical problem. The authors successfully address the long-standing challenge of end-to-end optimization for lossy compression by introducing a continuous relaxation technique that effectively handles quantization. This is a significant methodological leap from traditional transform coding methods.

The experimental results are compelling and well-documented. The authors show consistent improvements in both PSNR (for most images at lower bit rates) and MS-SSIM (across all images and bit rates), with visual comparisons that clearly demonstrate the absence of blocking and ringing artifacts that plague JPEG and JPEG 2000. The authors effectively show that their model learns to represent images in a way that aligns with human perception, even when trained with MSE.

What makes this paper particularly strong is the clear demonstration that their end-to-end optimization approach yields both better numerical performance and better visual quality. The authors also provide a thoughtful comparison to related work, including a detailed analysis of how their approach differs from variational autoencoders (a key strength, as they acknowledge the relationship but clarify critical distinctions).

The paper's contribution is not merely incremental—it establishes a new direction for image compression that leverages deep learning to optimize the entire pipeline. This aligns perfectly with ICLR's preference for novel concepts over methodological perfection.

## 4. Additional feedback
While the paper is strong, these points could further enhance it:

1. The authors mention that they are testing models with simpler nonlinearities (ReLU, sigmoid) but only provide preliminary results. A more detailed comparison of different activation functions and their impact on model efficiency would strengthen the analysis of why GDN/IGDN is particularly effective for compression.

2. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational costs of training versus inference. While they note encoding/decoding is efficient, the high computational cost of training might be a barrier for some applications.

3. The authors could explore how their approach might be extended to video compression or other multimedia formats, as this would demonstrate the broader applicability of their framework.

## Questions for authors
1. The paper mentions that "GDN/IGDN transforms are more efficient for compression, producing better models with fewer stages of processing." Could you provide more specific evidence for this claim, perhaps in terms of model parameters or inference speed?

2. In the appendix, the authors note that the entropy code they implemented is relatively simple (CABAC with limited spatial adaptation). How might more sophisticated entropy coding techniques impact the rate-distortion performance, and would this change the relative comparison to JPEG 2000?

3. The paper states that "the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested, and at all bit rates." Given the demonstrated improvements in MS-SSIM across all images, could you elaborate on why this perceptual improvement occurs, particularly with MSE as the training metric?
