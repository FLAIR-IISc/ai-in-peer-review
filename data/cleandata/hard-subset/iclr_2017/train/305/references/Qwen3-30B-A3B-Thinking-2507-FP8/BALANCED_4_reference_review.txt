

# Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on nonlinear transforms, which significantly improves upon traditional transform coding methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The authors develop a cascaded nonlinear transformation architecture using Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) that is jointly optimized with quantization and entropy coding for rate-distortion performance. The key innovation lies in using a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable end-to-end training via stochastic gradient descent, while still achieving actual bit rates through a proper entropy code implementation. The approach demonstrates both superior rate-distortion performance (measured by PSNR) and substantially improved perceptual quality (measured by MS-SSIM) compared to traditional methods.

## 2. Decision

Accept.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of image compression by demonstrating a practical end-to-end optimization framework that achieves state-of-the-art performance. The key reasons for acceptance are the paper's clear theoretical motivation, rigorous empirical validation, and the practical implementation of a complete compression system with actual bit rates.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses a fundamental problem in image compression with a well-motivated approach. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of traditional transform coding (orthogonal linear transforms, separate optimization of components) and position their work as a solution that jointly optimizes the entire compression pipeline. Their use of GDN as a biologically-inspired nonlinear transform is well-justified by prior work (Ballé et al., 2015) and shown to be effective in Gaussianizing image statistics.

The technical approach is scientifically rigorous. The authors develop a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization, which they carefully validate through experiments (Figure 4). They explicitly distinguish their approach from variational autoencoders (Section 3.1), addressing potential confusions in the literature. The evaluation methodology is sound, with proper implementation of entropy coding (CABAC) and fair comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 across multiple metrics (PSNR, MS-SSIM) and image sets.

The empirical results are compelling: the method achieves better rate-distortion performance than JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most images and bit rates, and demonstrates substantially improved perceptual quality. The qualitative analysis (Figures 5-7) convincingly shows how the method avoids the blocking artifacts common in linear transform coding. The authors also provide a thoughtful discussion of limitations and connections to related work (Section 5).

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few points could enhance its impact:

1. The paper mentions that "the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested, and at all bit rates" (Section 4). It would be helpful to quantify this more precisely - for example, what percentage of images show improvement in MS-SSIM across all bit rates? This would strengthen the claim.

2. The paper states that "the method was optimized using mean squared error as a distortion metric, but the compressed images are much more natural in appearance." It would be valuable to include a qualitative comparison between MSE-optimized and MS-SSIM-optimized versions of their method to directly demonstrate the benefit of optimizing for perceptual quality.

3. The appendix mentions that "a more thorough exploration of alternative architectures could potentially lead to significant performance improvements." It would strengthen the paper to include at least one ablation study showing how different architectural choices (e.g., number of stages, filter sizes) affect performance.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "for most (but not all) test images, we find that our method exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images." Can you provide more detail on the images where the method underperforms, and what characteristics of these images might explain the performance difference?

2. In Section 4, the authors note that "the progression from high to low bit rates is shown for an example image in figure 6." Given that the paper claims improvements across all bit rates, it would be helpful to see a more systematic comparison of performance across the entire rate-distortion curve for multiple representative images.

3. The paper mentions that "GDN represents a multivariate generalization of a particular type of sigmoidal function." Could you provide more theoretical justification for why this specific form of nonlinear transformation is particularly effective for compression, beyond the empirical results presented?
