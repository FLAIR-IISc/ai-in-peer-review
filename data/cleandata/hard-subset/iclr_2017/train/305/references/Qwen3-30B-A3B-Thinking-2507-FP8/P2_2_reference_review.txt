

# Review for "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

This paper presents a novel framework for end-to-end optimization of image compression using nonlinear transforms. The authors propose a model that consists of cascaded linear convolutions and nonlinearities (using Generalized Divisive Normalization - GDN), followed by uniform scalar quantization, and then an approximate inverse transform for reconstruction. The key contribution is the ability to jointly optimize all components of the compression pipeline (analysis transform, synthesis transform, and entropy code) using stochastic gradient descent, which has not been done before in the context of image compression. 

The authors demonstrate that their method outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of both rate-distortion performance (measured by PSNR) and perceptual quality (measured by MS-SSIM), with visually superior results that lack the blocking and ringing artifacts common in traditional transform coding methods. The paper provides a thorough theoretical justification for their approach, including a careful explanation of how they overcome the non-differentiability of the quantization step using a continuous relaxation with additive uniform noise.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper makes a significant technical contribution to a well-established problem (image compression) by enabling end-to-end optimization of all components of the compression pipeline.
2. The experimental results are comprehensive, rigorous, and clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over established baselines.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in several key areas:

**Problem significance and motivation**: The paper correctly identifies the limitations of current image compression methods (which optimize transform, quantizer, and entropy code separately) and provides a strong theoretical foundation for why end-to-end optimization would be beneficial. The authors clearly situate their work within the literature, explaining both the relationship to and differences from variational autoencoders (a key connection that's important to establish).

**Technical contribution**: The continuous relaxation of the quantization step using additive uniform noise is a clever and well-justified solution to the non-differentiability problem. The paper provides clear theoretical justification for why this relaxation is appropriate (the density function of the relaxed variable is a continuous approximation of the discrete probability mass function, and the noise approximates quantization error in terms of marginal moments).

**Experimental rigor**: The evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors:
- Compare against two standard baselines (JPEG and JPEG 2000) across multiple images
- Use both PSNR (standard for compression) and MS-SSIM (for perceptual quality)
- Provide visual examples that clearly demonstrate the qualitative improvements
- Include ablation studies and comparisons with related work (Gregor et al., 2016; Toderici et al., 2016)
- Implement a proper entropy code and report actual bit rates (not just entropy estimates)

The results are convincing: the proposed method achieves better rate-distortion performance than JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most images and bit rates, and the perceptual quality improvements are substantial and consistent across all test images.

**Clarity and writing**: The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details. The figures (particularly Figures 1, 2, 5, and 6) effectively illustrate the key concepts and results.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong as is, these suggestions could further improve it:

- **Computational complexity**: The paper mentions that encoding/decoding is efficient, but it would be helpful to provide specific numbers on the time complexity or memory requirements compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000. This is important for assessing practical applicability.

- **Scalability to higher resolutions**: The experiments are conducted on 256x256 images. It would be valuable to discuss or demonstrate how the method scales to higher resolutions (e.g., 1080p or 4K), which are common in real-world applications.

- **More detailed ablation studies**: The paper mentions that they're testing simpler nonlinearities, but it would strengthen the paper to include a more detailed comparison between GDN/IGDN and simpler nonlinearities (ReLU, sigmoid) in terms of both performance and model size.

- **Training with perceptual metrics**: The paper states that they use MSE as the distortion metric, but they also measure perceptual quality with MS-SSIM. It would be interesting to see how the method performs if trained with MS-SSIM instead of MSE, as this would directly address the claim that the method's visual quality is superior.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help clarify some aspects of the paper and strengthen the authors' claims, I would appreciate answers to these questions:

1. Could you provide more details about the computational complexity of your method compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000? Specifically, what are the encoding/decoding times on standard hardware for images of different sizes?

2. The paper mentions that your method works better with MSE as a distortion metric, but you also measure perceptual quality with MS-SSIM. How would the results change if the model were trained with MS-SSIM as the distortion metric instead of MSE? Would the visual improvements be even more pronounced?

3. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the ablation study comparing GDN/IGDN with simpler nonlinearities (ReLU, sigmoid)? Specifically, how much of the performance gain can be attributed to the specific choice of nonlinearity versus other aspects of the model architecture?

4. The paper states that "our method exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images, especially at the lower bit rates." Could you provide a more detailed analysis of which types of images (e.g., natural scenes vs. textures) benefit the most from your method?

5. The paper mentions that they are currently testing models with simpler nonlinearities. Could you share preliminary results on how much of the performance gain comes from the specific choice of GDN/IGDN versus other aspects of the model?
