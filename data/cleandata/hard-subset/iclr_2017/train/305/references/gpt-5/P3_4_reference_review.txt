1) Summary and contributions
The paper tackles lossy image compression by jointly optimizing analysis/synthesis transforms, quantization, and entropy modeling end-to-end for a given rate–distortion trade-off. The core ideas are:
- Nonlinear transform coding with cascaded convolutional layers and generalized divisive normalization (GDN), and a parametric inverse (IGDN) for synthesis.
- A continuous relaxation of quantization via additive unit uniform noise that simultaneously enables: (i) differentiable rate estimation by matching the relaxed differential entropy to the discrete entropy of the quantized latents; and (ii) differentiable distortion estimation under MSE.
- Nonparametric, factorized marginal models (piecewise linear splines) for latent densities, optimized jointly with the transforms.
- Practicality: trained per λ, but evaluated with real bitstreams using a CABAC-style entropy coder; rate–distortion is reported in actual bits, not just entropy estimates.
- Strong empirical results on Kodak (and additional images), with notably improved perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) and competitive-to-better PSNR versus JPEG and JPEG2000, especially at low bitrates. A thoughtful discussion of the connection to VAEs clarifies similarities and key differences.

Overall, the paper presents a clear, principled, and practical framework for learned compression, with careful treatment of the quantization barrier and end-to-end optimization.

2) Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Novel, well-motivated methodology that bridges classic rate–distortion optimization and modern deep learning, with a sound continuous relaxation and a plausible probabilistic interpretation.
- Compelling empirical evidence with actual bitstreams, improved perceptual quality, and competitive PSNR versus strong baselines (JPEG/JP2K). The qualitative improvements are consistent and convincing.

3) Supporting arguments
Strengths
- Methodological clarity: The quantization relaxation via additive uniform noise is elegant, properly justified (both rate and distortion), and empirically validated (bias analysis of relaxed vs. discrete metrics).
- End-to-end training for rate–distortion: Optimizing over transforms and marginal models per λ exposes the convex hull of achievable points and ties directly to classic transform coding intuitions.
- Transform design: The GDN/IGDN nonlinearity is well-motivated from vision and prior work on Gaussianization, and the architecture is simple but effective. The paper also anticipates and discusses alternatives (ReLU, sigmoid) and efficiency trade-offs.
- Evaluation rigor: Reporting actual bitrates with an implemented entropy coder avoids the common pitfall of comparing differential entropies to bitrates. The qualitative artifact analysis is thorough and aligns with MS-SSIM gains.
- Contextualization: The relationship to VAEs is accurately described, with an important emphasis on discrete coding, true bitrate evaluation, and the different optimization regime (fixed λ vs. inferred variance).

Concerns and limitations
- Independence assumption in the latent space: The factorized entropy model limits achievable rates; JP2K and modern learned codecs reduce rate further with spatial/context/hyperpriors. The authors partially offset this with CABAC adaptation, but the context is rudimentary (raster scan, per-channel sharing). This likely leaves bits on the table.
- Per-λ models: Training a separate model per operating point is less practical than a single model supporting multiple rates (e.g., via learned quantizer scaling or conditional transforms). This is acknowledged but remains a usability limitation.
- Baselines: While JPEG/JP2K are standard, by 2016/2017 BPG (HEVC intra) and contemporary learned codecs (e.g., Toderici et al.) constitute stronger rate–distortion baselines, at least for PSNR. A direct comparison, even if imperfect, would strengthen claims.
- Dataset and metrics: Kodak is relatively small; results would be stronger with larger test suites and more comprehensive PSNR/MS-SSIM summaries. The paper argues against pooled summaries (fair point), but some standardized aggregation (e.g., BD-rate over matched rates) would aid interpretability.
- Ablations: Limited quantitative ablations on architectural choices (GDN vs. ReLU, number of stages, overcompleteness), entropy model resolution, and the effect of CABAC adaptation. These would clarify where the gains arise and the efficiency of GDN.
- Computational aspects: No encoding/decoding runtime, memory footprint, or model size analysis. For adoption, these are important.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Provide a stronger comparison set: Include BPG/HEVC intra and at least one contemporary learned codec. Even if your method focuses on perceptual quality, PSNR comparisons against BPG would calibrate performance.
- Add a compact ablation table: GDN vs. ReLU/ELU; number of channels/stages; factorized vs. lightweight context models; spline resolution for marginal estimation; effect of CABAC online adaptation. Show both bitrate and MS-SSIM/PSNR deltas.
- Multi-rate support: Explore a single model trained across λ via a conditioning variable, learned quantizer step size, or a Lagrange-augmented training schedule. This would significantly improve practicality.
- Entropy modeling: Even minimal local spatial context or an auxiliary hyperprior (e.g., scale model) could close the gap to more advanced coders without heavy complexity. Consider reporting potential gains from a simple context window.
- Fairness and reproducibility: Detail JPEG/JP2K settings, quantization tables/transforms, and color space handling. Release code, trained models, and the entropy coder to facilitate reproducibility.
- Complexity: Report FLOPs, latency, and memory for encoding/decoding on standard hardware. Discuss trade-offs of increased channel counts used for high-rate models.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the factorized latent assumption? Have you measured the mutual information remaining between latents after GDN? What bitrate reduction would a simple autoregressive or hyperprior provide?
- How robust is the additive uniform noise relaxation across rates? Beyond the bias plot, does training with noise ever lead to suboptimal discretized operating points (e.g., mode collapse of y distributions)?
- Can a single conditional model handle a range of λ at inference time with small losses? Have you tried training with λ sampled per batch, or with a learned scale parameter for the latents?
- What are the encoding/decoding runtimes and model sizes for the 128/192/256-channel variants? How do they compare to JPEG/JP2K for common image sizes?
- For RGB, what color space is used internally? Could a learned color transform improve RD?
- How much of the MS-SSIM gain comes from the transform vs. entropy coding details? If you replace CABAC with a non-adaptive coder matched to the learned marginals, how large is the RD delta?

In summary, this is a strong, well-argued paper that advances learned image compression with a principled and practical framework. Addressing context modeling and multi-rate usability would further elevate its impact.
