1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on cascaded nonlinear analysis/synthesis transforms with generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN), uniform scalar quantization, and an entropy coder. The central technical contribution is a continuous relaxation of the rate–distortion objective by replacing quantization with additive i.i.d. uniform noise, enabling stochastic gradient descent to jointly optimize the analysis transform, synthesis transform, and marginal entropy model. The method is carefully related to variational autoencoders, while emphasizing crucial differences arising from discrete coding and rate–distortion trade-offs. The authors implement a practical entropy coder (CABAC), include side information in the bitstream, and report actual bit rates. Empirically, on the Kodak dataset (and additional images), the method demonstrates improved perceptual quality over JPEG and JPEG 2000 at comparable rates, markedly better MS-SSIM across all tested images/bit rates, and better PSNR for most images, especially at low rates. The paper also validates the relaxation by comparing relaxed quantities to actual quantized rate/distortion.

2. Decision
Accept, due to:
- Strong technical soundness and clear, well-motivated formulation that unifies transform design, quantization, and entropy modeling under a single, optimizable objective.
- Compelling empirical evidence with real bitstreams, showing consistent perceptual gains and competitive PSNR against established baselines.

3. Supporting arguments
On problem scope: The paper tackles the classic lossy image compression problem by directly optimizing the rate–distortion objective, rather than optimizing transform, quantization, and entropy coding separately. This addresses a well-defined, high-impact problem.

On motivation and placement: The work is well-situated in the compression literature (transform coding and quantization) and in modern deep learning. The adoption of GDN/IGDN is motivated by prior work on Gaussianizing image statistics. The link to variational inference is insightful and helps clarify the role of the relaxation and the distinct optimization regimes of compression vs. generative modeling.

On technical soundness: 
- The relaxation via additive uniform noise is standard in quantization theory and is justified here both theoretically (continuous relaxation approximates discrete entropy and distortion) and empirically (bias/variance characterization).
- The assumption of factorized latents is explicit, with nonparametric marginal models, and the entropy coder is implemented with adaptive contexts. This yields a coherent end-to-end pipeline.
- The architecture and training details, including parameterizations and stabilization strategies (e.g., DCT parameterization, re-scaling, Adam), are clearly described.

On empirical support:
- Reporting actual bit rates with a practical entropy code substantiates real-world viability.
- Comparisons to JPEG and JPEG 2000 on a standard dataset (Kodak) with both PSNR and MS-SSIM are appropriate and transparent; side information is included.
- The relaxation quality is evaluated, showing small distortional bias and manageable entropy approximation bias (diminishing at finer quantization).
- The method’s qualitative behavior (edge/contour preservation, reduced ringing/blocking) is consistent with the hypothesis that optimized nonlinear transforms better match image statistics.

Limitations (not fatal):
- The entropy model is factorized, and the entropy coder uses simple raster-scan contexts; stronger context models could further improve rates.
- A separate model is trained per λ; a single model covering multiple rates is not demonstrated.
- Evaluation is focused on Kodak and selected images; broader benchmarks and runtime/memory measurements would strengthen claims of practicality.
- Ablations isolating GDN/IGDN benefits, number of stages, and prior modeling strength are limited (some preliminary statements are in discussion).

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Provide ablations: (a) GDN/IGDN vs pointwise nonlinearities at comparable parameter counts; (b) factorized prior vs simple autoregressive/context models; (c) number of stages and channel counts. This would clarify where the gains originate.
- Report computational costs: encoding/decoding time, model size, and memory, to contextualize deployment feasibility.
- Expand evaluation: include larger and more diverse datasets and high-resolution images; provide per-image PSNR/MS-SSIM scatter plots vs. JPEG/JPEG 2000 to complement curves.
- Analyze latent dependencies: quantify residual correlations to motivate future context models or hyperpriors.
- Consider a single model covering multiple rates (e.g., conditioning on λ or employing gain-control layers) and compare its RD performance to per-λ models.
- Discuss color handling in more detail and report full-reference metrics on RGB, not only luma for MS-SSIM.
- Release code/models and the entropy coder to facilitate reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- Relaxation fidelity: Can you provide per-image statistics quantifying the gap between relaxed differential entropy and actual coded bit rate across rates, and how this gap scales with λ?
- Factorized prior: What is the empirical magnitude of cross-channel/spatial correlations in the latent y? Have you tested simple spatial/context models or hyperpriors, and what is their RD impact?
- Model capacity and ablations: How sensitive are results to the number of stages and channels? Can you quantify the incremental gain of GDN/IGDN vs ReLU/sigmoid at fixed capacity?
- Single model for multiple rates: Did you attempt conditioning on λ or feature gating to produce a unified model covering a range of rates? How does its convex hull compare to per-λ training?
- Complexity: What are typical encode/decode runtimes and memory footprints for 512×512 or higher resolutions on CPU/GPU? How do they compare to JPEG 2000 under similar settings?
- Entropy coding: How much additional gain is achievable with stronger contexts (e.g., neighborhood- or channel-aware) beyond the current raster-scan approach?
- Color treatment: For RGB models, do you observe different RD behavior than grayscale beyond capacity increases? Have you evaluated MS-SSIM on RGB or multi-channel perceptual metrics?
- Generalization: How does performance vary across image domains not represented in ImageNet (e.g., medical, line art, graphics)?

Overall, the paper addresses the three key questions positively: it tackles a specific and important problem, is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature, and supports its claims with technically sound methodology and compelling empirical evidence using real bitstreams.
