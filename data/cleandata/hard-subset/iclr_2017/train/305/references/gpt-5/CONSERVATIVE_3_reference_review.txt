1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes an end-to-end learned image compression system that jointly optimizes the analysis transform, synthesis transform, and entropy model for rate–distortion performance. The core technical ingredients are: (i) a cascaded convolutional architecture with generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN) nonlinearities; (ii) a continuous relaxation of quantization via additive uniform noise that enables SGD-based optimization; (iii) nonparametric independent marginal models in the latent space; and (iv) evaluation using an implemented entropy coder (CABAC-like), reporting actual bit rates rather than differential entropy. The method is trained for multiple λ values (one model per rate point) and is shown to outperform JPEG and JPEG 2000 in MS-SSIM consistently and in PSNR for most images/rates, with qualitatively fewer artifacts at low bit rates.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Methodological soundness and clarity: The rate–distortion formulation, the quantization relaxation, and the relationship to VAEs are carefully developed, with attention to discrete vs. differential entropy and practical coding.
- Empirical strength against established baselines: The method is compared against widely used codecs (JPEG, JPEG 2000) with actual bitstreams and demonstrates consistent perceptual gains and frequent PSNR gains, particularly at low rates.

3) Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The paper is well positioned within transform coding, vector quantization, and recent learned generative approaches. The authors articulate differences from VAEs and justify the choice of MSE for comparability. The use of GDN is motivated by prior work showing effective Gaussianization.
- Technical correctness: The relaxation via additive uniform noise is standard and justified; the link between the relaxed objective and a VAE objective under MSE is correctly derived. The entropy model is explicitly instantiated and used in a working arithmetic coder, avoiding the common pitfall of reporting only differential entropy.
- Evidence: The authors provide bit-accurate evaluations, ablate the approximation quality of the relaxed loss versus the discrete setting, and demonstrate robustness of the conclusions across multiple images. Qualitative examples align with the MS-SSIM improvements (reduced ringing/blocking).
- Practical feasibility: Encoding/decoding cost is modest at test time (convs + scalar quantization + arithmetic coding), making the approach implementable.

4) Concerns and limitations (impact on decision mitigated but important)
- Baselines: Comparisons are limited to JPEG and JPEG 2000. More modern codecs (e.g., HEVC intra/BPG, WebP) are missing. Given the paper’s claims, stronger baselines are important to establish state-of-the-art standing.
- Aggregation and statistical rigor: The paper largely avoids aggregate RD summaries, citing difficulties. While per-image curves are informative, standard aggregate metrics (e.g., averaged RD curves, BD-rate/BD-PSNR vs baselines) would strengthen the empirical claims and make results easier to interpret.
- Model per rate point: Training a separate model per λ reduces practicality and complicates deployment. Competing learned methods target a single model with a controllable rate (e.g., via recurrent or conditional modules). The paper discusses this trade-off but does not provide a unifying model.
- Entropy modeling: The latent prior assumes independent marginals with limited spatial adaptation through a simple raster scan/context. This likely leaves coding gains on the table compared to more structured priors or richer context models and may understate the method’s full potential, but it also weakens direct comparisons if JPEG 2000’s coder exploits stronger contexts.
- Evaluation breadth: The test set is primarily Kodak (and some classic images). A larger, modern, out-of-distribution test set would better support generalization claims. No subjective user study is presented, despite strong perceptual claims.
- Efficiency reporting: There is no systematic runtime/memory/latency analysis compared to JPEG 2000 or modern codecs.

5) Suggestions to improve the paper (for the camera-ready; not all are decision-critical)
- Add modern codec baselines (e.g., BPG/HEVC intra, WebP). If unavailable, provide a clear rationale and at least an estimated comparison or discussion.
- Provide aggregate metrics (e.g., BD-rate) over Kodak and an additional test set (e.g., CLIC, Tecnick), and report confidence intervals or at least variance across images.
- Include ablations: GDN vs ReLU/sigmoids under matched parameter counts; number of stages; effect of different down/up-sampling; sensitivity to the nonparametric density resolution.
- Report encoding/decoding speed and model size, and compare to JPEG 2000 to contextualize practicality.
- Clarify fairness of color handling: JPEG uses 4:2:0; your model appears to compress RGB without subsampling. Provide matched comparisons (e.g., with/without chroma subsampling) and report metrics on RGB and luma consistently.
- Discuss the overhead and adaptation of the entropy coder in more depth, and quantify how far the achieved bit rates are from the measured discrete entropy.
- Consider a variant that enables a single model to operate across rates (e.g., a learned scale hyperparameter or conditional transforms), even as a preliminary result.

6) Questions for the authors
- Baselines: Can you include comparisons to a stronger modern codec (HEVC intra/BPG) on Kodak and one larger test set? If not, can you provide evidence that your method is competitive via indirect comparisons?
- Aggregation: Can you report BD-rate numbers versus JPEG 2000 for PSNR and MS-SSIM across Kodak? Even with your caveats, this would greatly aid interpretability.
- Fairness in color handling: Are your RGB models compressing full-resolution chroma, while JPEG uses 4:2:0? How do results change if you match subsampling, or evaluate purely on luma?
- Entropy coding gap: What is the average gap between the measured discrete entropy (from histograms) and the actual arithmetic-coded bitstreams? How much does your minimal context modeling leave on the table relative to JPEG 2000’s contexts?
- Generalization and robustness: How does performance vary on larger, more diverse test sets beyond Kodak? Any signs of overfitting to ImageNet crops?
- Efficiency: What are encoding/decoding times on a CPU/GPU for 512×512 and 1080p images? Model sizes per λ? Any shared-weights variant across λ?
- Single-model multi-rate: Have you tried learning a rate-conditional model (e.g., conditional GDN parameters or learned scale parameters) to avoid training per-λ models? How does its RD curve compare?

Overall, this is a careful, well-motivated, and practically grounded contribution that advances learned image compression beyond prior generative modeling work by optimizing the true rate–distortion tradeoff and reporting actual bit rates. Despite the noted shortcomings in baselines and aggregation, the technical soundness and the clear empirical benefits over established codecs merit acceptance.
