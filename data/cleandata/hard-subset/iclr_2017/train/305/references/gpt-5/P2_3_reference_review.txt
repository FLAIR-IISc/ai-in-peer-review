1) Summary and contributions
The paper tackles lossy image compression by jointly optimizing the analysis transform, quantizer surrogate, synthesis transform, and entropy model end-to-end for a rate–distortion objective. The core ideas are:
- Replace non-differentiable quantization with additive i.i.d. uniform noise to obtain a continuous, differentiable surrogate objective whose rate term is the differential entropy of the relaxed latents and whose distortion term approximates MSE after quantization.
- Use cascaded convolutional layers with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and its learned inverse (IGDN) for analysis/synthesis transforms; learn nonparametric (spline) marginal priors for the relaxed latents.
- Train separate models per Lagrange multiplier λ via SGD on a large image subset; at test time, perform actual scalar quantization and entropy coding (CABAC-like) and report true bit rates.
- Empirically, achieve consistent perceptual improvements and competitive or better PSNR vs JPEG and JPEG 2000 on Kodak and additional images; show large MS-SSIM gains; and validate that the continuous relaxation tracks discrete rate/distortion well, especially at finer quantization.
The paper also articulates a clear connection to variational autoencoders (VAEs), clarifying similarities and key differences when optimizing for compression (finite-entropy discrete codes and trade-offs along the rate–distortion curve).

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) Strong, principled formulation with an effective optimization surrogate and a biologically inspired transform that yields clear empirical gains over strong baselines, measured in actual bit rates. (2) The work closes an important gap between learned generative modeling and practical compression, providing an end-to-end codec with demonstrated superiority in perceptual quality and competitive PSNR.

3) Supporting arguments
- Novelty and significance: While additive uniform noise as a quantization surrogate is known, the paper’s integration—GDN-based nonlinear analysis/synthesis, learned spline marginals, per-λ end-to-end training, and an implemented entropy coder—amounts to a compelling, complete codec. The work is among the first to show learned transforms outperform JPEG 2000 across a range of rates in both objective and subjective terms with actual bitstreams.
- Technical soundness: The relaxation is well motivated: (i) the relaxed entropy equals the discrete mass at integer points (p_y convolved with U(0,1)), making it a faithful rate proxy; (ii) uniform noise mimics scalar quantization error in moments; (iii) empirical checks demonstrate small bias/variance for both rate and distortion surrogates, with diminishing bias at finer quantization. The VAE equivalence is clearly derived for Gaussian likelihood (MSE) and the distinctions (discrete codes, λ trade-off, practical entropy coding) are explained convincingly.
- Empirical rigor: Training is described in detail (architectures, optimization, priors), and test sets are standard. Reporting actual bit rates via CABAC avoids pitfalls of comparing differential entropy to bit rates. Results include PSNR and MS-SSIM; qualitative comparisons show suppression of ringing/blocking versus transform coders, with graceful degradation at low bit rates. The observed MS-SSIM gains despite MSE training are notable and consistent with the smooth-edge behavior induced by GDN.
- Clarity and placement: The paper is clearly written, well motivated, and positioned relative to transform coding, VAEs, and contemporary learned compression (e.g., Toderici et al., Gregor et al.). The appendices contain practical details that support reproduction.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Single model across rates: Training one model per λ is operationally heavy. Consider mechanisms to control rate at test time (e.g., a learned global scale for latents/quantization step, or a conditional “gain” input), enabling a single model to traverse the RD curve.
- Entropy model: The assumption of independent marginals in the latent space likely leaves bits on the table. Context models or learned hyperpriors could capture spatial/channel dependencies and improve rate further. Even with CABAC, the raster order is simplistic; richer context (e.g., autoregressive over latents) could be explored.
- Ablations: Provide ablations on (i) GDN/IGDN vs ReLU/batch norm at matched parameter counts and depth, (ii) number of stages/channels, and (iii) parametric vs nonparametric marginals. This will better isolate where the gains come from.
- Complexity: Add encoding/decoding runtime and memory comparisons versus JPEG 2000 on CPU/GPU, and model size, to assess deployability.
- Dataset generalization: While Kodak is standard, add results on larger modern benchmarks or cross-dataset tests to address potential dataset bias. Report variance across images and statistical significance where possible.
- Fairness/details: Clarify color pipeline (chroma subsampling, color space) used for your method vs baselines to ensure comparability. Some readers may prefer 4:4:4 comparisons as well.
- Reproducibility: Consider releasing code/models and bitstreams to foster adoption and confirmability.

Questions for the authors
- Rate control: How do you select λ per image at test time to meet a target bit rate, and what overhead results from storing λ and maintaining multiple decoders? Could a single model with a learned latent scaling or adaptive quantization approximate the entire RD frontier?
- Entropy coding: How close is your CABAC implementation to the empirical entropy of the quantized latents? Do you observe consistent gaps, and if so, are they attributable more to independence assumptions or to coder/context limitations?
- Independence assumption: Can you quantify the residual dependency structure among latents (e.g., mutual information across channels/spatial neighbors) after training? How much additional rate could be recovered with a simple context model?
- GDN contribution: Beyond preliminary notes, can you provide controlled experiments showing parameter efficiency of GDN vs common activations at equal RD performance?
- Runtime: What are typical encode/decode times for 512x512 RGB images on CPU and GPU? Any bottlenecks from GDN/IGDN numerics?
- Overcompleteness: You state the representation is overcomplete, yet the net output has half the dimensionality of the input. Can you reconcile this, or clarify in what sense the first-stage filters confer overcompleteness?
- Robustness: How sensitive are results to the number of spline bins or the support chosen for the marginal priors? Does the relaxed training ever produce degenerate priors or instability at extreme λ?

Overall, this is a strong, well-executed paper that advances learned image compression both methodologically and practically.
