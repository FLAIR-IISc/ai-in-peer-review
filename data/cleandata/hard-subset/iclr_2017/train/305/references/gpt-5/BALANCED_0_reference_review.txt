Summary of contributions
The paper proposes an end-to-end trainable image compression framework that jointly optimizes analysis and synthesis nonlinear transforms, the quantizer, and the (factorized) latent prior to achieve a desired rate–distortion trade-off. Key elements include:
- A cascaded convolutional architecture with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and its learned inverse (IGDN), motivated by biological vision and prior work on Gaussianization of image statistics.
- A principled continuous relaxation of the discrete rate–distortion objective by replacing quantization with additive uniform noise, enabling stochastic gradient descent. The relaxed objective is shown to correspond closely to the discrete problem and is related to variational autoencoders (VAEs), with clear discussion of similarities and differences.
- Nonparametric, per-latent marginal models (learned splines) and an implemented context-adaptive arithmetic coder (CABAC-like), with reporting of actual bit rates (not just entropy estimates).
- Empirical validation on Kodak (and additional images), for both grayscale and RGB, showing consistent perceptual gains (MS-SSIM) over JPEG and JPEG 2000 at all bit rates, with PSNR gains on most images, especially at low rates. Visual artifacts are more natural (edge-preserving, less ringing/blocking).
- An analysis showing the continuous relaxation provides a good approximation to discrete rate and distortion across bit rates.

Decision: Accept
Reasons:
- The paper offers a principled, practical, and effective end-to-end solution to image compression, bridging classical rate–distortion theory and modern deep learning, and demonstrating clear empirical advantages over standard codecs with actual bitstreams.
- The formulation and technical insights (GDN/IGDN transforms, additive-uniform relaxation, nonparametric priors, explicit relation to VAEs) are novel, well-motivated, and likely to have significant impact.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem tackled: Design of lossy image compression systems that jointly optimize transform, quantization, and entropy coding to achieve optimal rate–distortion trade-offs. The work addresses the classic difficulty of optimizing quantization in high-dimensional spaces by learning nonlinear transforms and a latent prior end-to-end.

2) Motivation and placement in literature: The paper is well positioned relative to classical transform coding (JPEG/2000), normalization-based models in vision, and recent learned generative/compression models. The GDN motivation is clear and backed by prior work; the discussion distinguishing this work from VAEs and generative compression (Gregor et al., Toderici et al.) is thoughtful and technically sound. The choice to evaluate with actual bit rates via an implemented entropy coder strengthens the practical relevance.

3) Support for claims/rigor: 
- The continuous relaxation is validated empirically: the distortion approximation is largely unbiased and the (differential vs discrete) entropy gap shrinks at finer quantization. This is a key contribution enabling end-to-end training.
- The experimental setup (training on ImageNet subset, testing on Kodak and other images not seen during training) is reasonable. The paper reports both PSNR and MS-SSIM and provides extensive qualitative examples; improvements in MS-SSIM and artifacts are compelling and consistent.
- The codec is complete (analysis, quantization, entropy coding, synthesis) and rates include side information.
- Some limitations exist: the latent prior assumes independence across latents, the entropy coder uses simple contexts (raster scan), and comparisons are mainly against JPEG and JPEG 2000 rather than contemporary learned codecs. Aggregate quantitative summaries could be stronger. Nonetheless, the evidence is sufficient to support the core claims.

Additional feedback and suggestions (to help improve the paper; not all central to the accept decision)
- Baselines: If feasible, add quantitative comparisons to Toderici et al. (recurrent learned codec) on Kodak, reporting actual bit rates. Even a partial comparison would strengthen the empirical case.
- Aggregate metrics: Consider BD-rate summaries over Kodak for PSNR and MS-SSIM, or standardized rate points, to complement per-image plots.
- Ablations: Include controlled studies on (i) replacing GDN/IGDN with ReLU/sigmoid and varying depth/width; (ii) effect of stages/channels; (iii) fixed parametric priors vs learned splines. This would clarify where the gains originate.
- Entropy model: The factorized prior and simple adaptation limit coding efficiency. Discuss potential extensions (e.g., hyperpriors, spatial/auto-regressive context) and estimate their expected gains.
- Single model across bit rates: Training separate models per λ is pragmatic but less convenient. Explore conditioning (e.g., scale hyperprior or λ-conditional transforms) to cover a range of rates with one model.
- Perceptual evaluation: Given the strong visual claims, a small-scale user study would be valuable. Alternatively, report additional perceptual metrics beyond MS-SSIM.
- Reproducibility: Consider releasing code/models, details of CABAC contexts, and training scripts.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the independence assumption in the latent prior? Have you tried simple spatial/neighbor contexts or hyperpriors, and what gains (in bits) did you observe?
- How do results change when replacing GDN/IGDN with ReLU or leaky-ReLU at the same parameter budget? Can you quantify the depth/width needed to match GDN performance?
- What is the parameter count and runtime (encode/decode) for typical image sizes on CPU/GPU? Any memory/latency constraints for practical deployment?
- How robust are the models across datasets (e.g., camera noise, high-ISO images, graphics)? Any observed domain shift issues?
- Can a single conditioned model achieve comparable R–D performance across a range of λ, and how would you expose λ at inference time to hit target rates reliably?
- The CABAC scheme shares a per-channel marginal across spatial positions. Did you experiment with simple block-based or autoregressive context signals (e.g., previously decoded neighbors)? How much extra gain is available?
- Do you observe cases where the relaxed entropy is a poor proxy for the discrete rate during training (e.g., at very low rates)? If so, how do you mitigate training instability?
- IGDN is described as a one-iteration approximate inverse with separate parameters. Did tying parameters between GDN/IGDN or using more iterations change performance?

Overall, this is a strong, well-motivated paper that advances learned image compression with a complete, principled, and effective system.
