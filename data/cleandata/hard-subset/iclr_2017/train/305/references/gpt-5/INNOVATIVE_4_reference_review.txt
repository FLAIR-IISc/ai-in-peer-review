Summary
This paper proposes a complete, end-to-end learned image compression system that directly optimizes a nonlinear analysis/synthesis transform pair for rate–distortion performance. The key ideas are: (i) using cascaded convolutional layers with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and its learned inverse (IGDN) to form a highly flexible nonlinear transform; (ii) replacing quantization during training with additive uniform noise to obtain a continuous, differentiable surrogate objective and learning nonparametric marginal density models; (iii) training separate models along the rate–distortion curve by varying λ; and (iv) evaluating using an actual entropy coder (CABAC), reporting real bit rates. The authors relate their relaxation to VAEs while emphasizing differences relevant to lossy compression. Empirically, the method achieves substantially improved perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) and often better PSNR than JPEG and JPEG 2000, especially at low bitrates, with visually compelling artifact profiles.

Decision: Accept
Reason: The paper introduces a bold and genuinely novel direction: fully end-to-end rate–distortion optimization of a nonlinear transform with a principled quantization/entropy relaxation and validation via real bitstreams. The technical design (GDN/IGDN, noise-based surrogate, nonparametric marginals) is original and impactful, and the empirical results are strong and qualitatively distinctive. While some components (independent marginals, per-λ training, simple entropy model) are not yet optimal, they do not detract from the significance of the concept and its demonstrated feasibility.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and significance: Moving from hand-crafted linear transform coding to learned nonlinear transforms optimized end-to-end for rate–distortion is a substantial conceptual shift. The uniform-noise relaxation that simultaneously approximates quantization and discrete entropy is elegant, effective, and pragmatically important.
- Methodological soundness: The objective is well-motivated from classical rate–distortion theory. The connection to VAEs clarifies the role of λ and the difference between generative modeling and compression. The use of nonparametric marginal models is sensible and helps reduce model mismatch.
- Completeness: Unlike many contemporaneous works, the paper implements an actual entropy coder and reports real bit rates, making comparisons meaningful. The system is a working codec, not just an analysis tool.
- Empirical quality: Consistent perceptual improvements across many images and bitrates, with substantial MS-SSIM gains and competitive PSNR vs. JPEG/JPEG2000. The artifact profile (edge-preserving simplification rather than blocking/ringing) is compelling and aligns with the modeling choices.
- Clarity and positioning: The paper situates itself well relative to transform coding, normalization in deep nets, and related neural compression/generative approaches (e.g., Toderici et al., Gregor et al.).

Caveats (not decision-blocking)
- Independence assumption and entropy model: The codec assumes independent marginals in the latent space and uses a relatively simple context model; stronger spatial/hierarchical priors (e.g., hyperpriors or autoregressive models) could further reduce rate. The authors acknowledge this and show only modest gains from CABAC adaptation.
- Per-λ models: Training a separate model per rate increases engineering complexity and storage; a single model controllable at test time would be attractive (the authors discuss Toderici et al.’s alternative and its trade-offs).
- Ablations and architecture: The network architecture is somewhat ad hoc; ablations isolating the contribution of GDN vs. standard activations are only briefly reported (preliminary). More systematic exploration would be useful.
- Dataset/scope: Training on a filtered subset of ImageNet and testing on Kodak and a few classic images is reasonable, but broader evaluations (e.g., camera pipelines, other datasets, color spaces) and runtime/resource profiling would strengthen the case.

Suggestions to improve (helpful, not required for acceptance)
- Provide a more thorough ablation: GDN vs. pointwise nonlinearities at matched parameter counts; effect of number of stages; effect of the nonparametric marginal vs. parametric alternatives.
- Analyze residual dependencies: Quantify inter-channel/spatial correlations remaining after analysis transform and estimate the headroom for better entropy models.
- Single model across bitrates: Explore mechanisms (e.g., learned gain/noise schedules, conditional normalization) to support continuous rate control without retraining per λ.
- Runtime and memory: Report encoding/decoding speed, model size, and compute requirements to inform practical deployment.
- Broader baselines: If feasible, include additional modern baselines (e.g., WebP) or discuss why JPEG/JPEG2000 suffice for positioning at this stage.

Questions for the authors
1) How much correlation remains among latents after training? Could you report cross-covariance/MI across channels and space to bound potential gains from better entropy models?
2) How sensitive is performance to the uniform-noise relaxation (e.g., width ≠ 1, different noise distributions) and to the nonparametric marginal resolution?
3) Can you share results from training a single model with a rate-control input (e.g., scaling of latents or noise) to cover multiple points on the R–D curve? How close does it come to per-λ optima?
4) What are the typical encode/decode times and model sizes for your reported configurations? Any issues on high-resolution images beyond 256×256 crops?
5) How does GDN compare to well-tuned alternatives (e.g., PReLU + local response norm) under a fixed parameter budget? Any insights into why GDN gives better perceptual artifacts?
6) Do you observe a systematic gap between the relaxed differential entropy and realized bit rates across bitrates/datasets? If so, can the CABAC adaptation close it further?
7) For RGB, did you explore alternative color spaces (e.g., YCbCr) or learned color transforms in the first layer, and how did that affect R–D performance?

Overall, this is a bold, well-motivated, and empirically convincing paper that opens a promising direction in neural compression. I recommend acceptance.
