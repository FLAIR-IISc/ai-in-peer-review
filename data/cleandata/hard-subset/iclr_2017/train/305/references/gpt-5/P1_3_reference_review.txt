1. Summary of contributions
The paper tackles end-to-end optimization of lossy image compression, a classical rate–distortion problem that is usually addressed piecemeal (transform, quantization, entropy coding). The authors propose a nonlinear transform coding framework with jointly trained analysis and synthesis transforms built from convolutional layers and a biologically inspired generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearity, with an IGDN approximate inverse. To make the non-differentiable quantization step trainable, they replace rounding by additive uniform noise and optimize a Lagrangian R + λD directly, while learning a non-parametric factorized prior over latents for rate estimation. Importantly, they implement a context-adaptive arithmetic coder and report actual bit rates, not just entropy estimates. They demonstrate improved perceptual quality and, for most images and bitrates, better PSNR than JPEG and JPEG 2000 on the Kodak dataset, with especially strong gains in MS-SSIM. They also clarify the connection to variational autoencoders (VAEs), and why compression requires different design choices.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (i) Strong, well-motivated end-to-end formulation that integrates learned analysis/synthesis transforms, a tractable relaxation for quantization, and an actual entropy coder; (ii) Convincing empirical evidence showing consistent perceptual improvements and competitive or better rate–distortion performance than widely used baselines; (iii) Clarity and careful placement in the literature, with a useful discussion of the VAE connection and constraints unique to compression.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and approach: The paper targets the core difficulty in learned compression—jointly optimizing a rate–distortion objective in the presence of quantization. The additive uniform noise relaxation is appropriate and principled for both rate and distortion; the authors empirically validate the bias/variance of the relaxation versus discrete outcomes (Fig. 4), an important check often omitted in related work.

- Model design: The cascade of conv + (I)GDN is both biologically motivated and practically effective. Prior work from the authors showed GDN’s Gaussianization properties; here its utility is demonstrated for compression, enabling efficient models with relatively few stages.

- Rate modeling and coding: Learning non-parametric, factorized marginals for y, combined with a CABAC-like entropy coder, enables reporting true bit rates. This elevates the work beyond comparisons using only entropy or differential entropy, making results directly comparable to deployed codecs.

- Empirical results: On Kodak (and additional samples), images compressed by the proposed method exhibit markedly reduced linear-basis artifacts (blocking/ringing) and more natural contour preservation, especially at low rates. MS-SSIM gains are substantial; PSNR is generally better than JPEG/J2K, particularly at low rates. The qualitative results are compelling.

- Relation to VAEs: The paper carefully articulates the equivalence of the relaxed objective to a VAE under Euclidean distortion and an additive uniform posterior, and then justifies the divergences required for compression (discrete coding, choice of λ, and evaluation via bitstreams). This helps position the contribution within the broader generative modeling literature.

4. Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Baselines: Consider adding comparisons to stronger modern baselines (e.g., BPG/HEVC intra or modern JPEG 2000 variants) to contextualize gains beyond JPEG/J2K. Even if direct integration is nontrivial, reporting PSNR/MS-SSIM at matched rates for a subset would strengthen claims.

- Ablations: Provide ablations isolating the contributions of (i) GDN vs ReLU/sigmoids; (ii) number of stages/channels; (iii) factorized vs simple context-conditioned priors; and (iv) entropy coder adaptivity. Some preliminary notes are in the discussion; quantitative plots would be valuable.

- Complexity: Report encoding/decoding runtimes and memory on a standard GPU/CPU versus JPEG/J2K. This would help assess deployability.

- Single model for variable rates: The current approach trains separate models per λ. While this is defensible for mapping the convex hull, it is less convenient in practice. Discuss or experiment with mechanisms to obtain multiple rates from a single model (e.g., learned quantization step scaling, latent-channel pruning, or λ-conditioned transforms).

- Dataset and generalization: Kodak is small; include an additional test set (e.g., Tecnick, CLIC, or high-resolution photos) and report aggregate metrics with CIs to assess robustness. Clarify color space handling and any chroma subsampling for fair PSNR evaluation.

- Bitstream analysis: Provide a breakdown of bitstream components (headers, side info, latents) and the effect of CABAC adaptivity versus a static arithmetic coder.

- Perceptual evaluation: Since MS-SSIM is highlighted, a small-scale user study (even as supplemental material) would strengthen perceptual quality claims, particularly at low rates.

5. Questions for the authors
- Relaxation fidelity: How sensitive are final RD results to the exact width and distribution of the training noise? Did you try annealing the noise or mismatching the noise at train/test?

- Prior independence: Factorized priors leave spatial/channel dependencies unmodeled. How much headroom remains if you add simple context models or a hyperprior? Can you quantify the gap between differential entropy and achieved bit rate per channel?

- Variable-rate coding: Can your trained transforms be used with a learned per-channel scaling (effectively changing the quantization step) to traverse a rate range without retraining?

- Runtime: What are the encoding/decoding times for 512×512 RGB images on CPU and GPU? How do they compare to JPEG 2000?

- Training stability: Did you observe instability with GDN/IGDN (e.g., exploding gains)? How critical are the parameterizations and normalizations described in the appendix?

- Generalization: How does performance change for high-resolution images (e.g., 2–12 MP) and for content outside ImageNet (medical, graphics, text-overlaid images)?

- Color handling: What color space is used internally? Are MS-SSIM/PSNR computed on luma only or RGB, and is the coding symmetric across channels?

- Reproducibility: Will code/models and the entropy coder be released? If not, can you provide sufficient details to reproduce the bitstreams?

Overall, this is a significant and well-executed contribution that demonstrates the practicality and benefits of end-to-end learned transform coding with real bit rates.
