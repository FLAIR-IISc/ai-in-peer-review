1. Summary of contributions and claims
The paper tackles end-to-end optimization of lossy image compression. It proposes a nonlinear transform coding framework in which both the analysis and synthesis transforms are learned jointly for each rate–distortion operating point, using a cascade of convolutions and generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN). The quantization obstacle is addressed by a principled continuous relaxation: replacing rounding with additive uniform noise, enabling stochastic gradient descent. The rate term is modeled with nonparametric marginal densities on the relaxed latents and evaluated at test time using an implemented arithmetic coder (CABAC-like), so results are reported as actual bit rates, not just entropy estimates. The paper also establishes a clear relationship to variational autoencoders, clarifying similarities and key differences. Empirically, on Kodak (and other examples), the method delivers significantly better perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) and generally better PSNR than JPEG and JPEG2000, especially at low bit rates, with artifacts that are visually more natural. It further validates that the continuous relaxation provides a good proxy for discrete rate and distortion during training.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Clear and well-motivated end-to-end rate–distortion optimization with a sound relaxation for quantization, paired with an actual entropy coder, constitutes a substantive and timely contribution.
- Strong empirical evidence of perceptual quality gains and competitive or superior PSNR against widely used standards, together with explicit analysis validating the optimization surrogate.

3. Supporting arguments
Problem and motivation:
- The paper addresses a central problem (learning transforms jointly with quantization and entropy models for optimized rate–distortion), well framed within classical transform coding and modern representation learning. The rationale for nonlinear, jointly optimized transforms (GDN-based) is compelling and well grounded in prior statistical and biological vision literature.
- The connection to VAEs is insightful and carefully drawn, yet the authors correctly highlight why compression differs (discrete coding, operating at finite λ, and the focus on actual bits).

Methodological soundness:
- The additive uniform noise relaxation is standard in quantization theory and used here with care. The authors empirically verify that the relaxed differential entropy and MSE track the discrete entropy and squared error, providing evidence the surrogate is appropriate across the rate range.
- The entropy model uses independent marginals with nonparametric piecewise linear densities, reducing model mismatch; rates are realized via an actual arithmetic coder. This avoids a common pitfall in learned compression papers of reporting only entropies.
- Architecture and training details are thorough (staged convolutions, GDN/IGDN, parameterization tricks for stability, optimization schedule), improving reproducibility.

Empirical support:
- Comparisons on the Kodak set and diverse images show consistent perceptual improvements (MS-SSIM) and often PSNR gains, most pronounced at low bitrates. Visual examples compellingly illustrate reduced blocking/ringing vs. linear transform codecs. The authors’ caution about averaging RD curves is appreciated; they provide per-image curves and images for inspection.

Scope and novelty:
- At the time, deep compression results either lacked proper rate accounting, did not compare to real codecs, or aimed at generative modeling. This work fills that gap by addressing RD optimization with realistic evaluation. The use of GDN/IGDN and nonparametric latent models is a solid architectural contribution.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not decisive)
- Baselines: Consider including a modern baseline such as HEVC intra (BPG) for a more challenging comparison, or at least discuss expected gaps. This would strengthen claims of practical competitiveness.
- Summary metrics: Provide standard RD summaries (e.g., Bjøntegaard delta-rate) per image and averaged, to complement the qualitative cautionary notes. Even with caveats, BD-rate helps readers quantify gains.
- Ablations: Add ablations on (i) GDN/IGDN vs. ReLU/other nonlinearities with similar parameter counts, (ii) effect of the nonparametric density vs. parametric alternatives, and (iii) the impact of independence assumptions vs. simple spatial context models. Some of this is discussed, but quantitative ablations would clarify where gains originate.
- Single model for multiple rates: Training a separate model per λ may limit practical deployment. Discuss strategies for a single conditional model (e.g., scale hyperprior, λ-conditioning) and trade-offs, even if left for future work.
- Complexity: Include encoder/decoder runtime and parameter count, memory, and throughput on common hardware, to assess deployability.
- Dataset and generalization: Although Kodak is common, report results on a larger, more diverse benchmark and higher resolutions to strengthen generalization claims.
- Color handling and chroma: Clarify color space choices and whether performance is sensitive to chroma subsampling choices, since JPEG uses 4:2:0 in the comparisons.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the independence assumption in the latent entropy model? Would lightweight context (e.g., masked autoregression across spatial locations within channels) substantially improve rate without harming complexity?
- Can you report BD-rate numbers vs. JPEG/JPEG2000 (per-image and average) for PSNR and MS-SSIM?
- What are the encoder/decoder runtimes and memory footprints for 512×512 and 1080p images on CPU and GPU? How do they compare to JPEG2000?
- How stable is training across random seeds and dataset splits? Any evidence of overfitting to ImageNet crops when evaluating on Kodak?
- Could you quantify the contribution of GDN/IGDN by matching depth/width when replacing them with pointwise nonlinearities?
- How well does the relaxation hold at very low rates (e.g., <0.1 bpp)? Does the bias between differential and discrete entropy affect optimization in that regime?
- Do you foresee a path to a single model covering a continuum of rates (e.g., via learned scale parameters or hyperpriors) within your framework?

Overall, the paper is well positioned, methodologically sound, and empirically persuasive, and it advances learned image compression in a way the community can build upon.
