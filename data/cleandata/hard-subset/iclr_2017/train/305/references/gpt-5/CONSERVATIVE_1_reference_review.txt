Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression framework that jointly learns nonlinear analysis/synthesis transforms (with GDN/IGDN) and marginal entropy models, trained for rate–distortion (R + λD) using a continuous relaxation that replaces quantization with additive uniform noise. This enables SGD-based optimization despite quantization.
- The objective is connected to variational autoencoders, clarifying similarities and differences (e.g., discrete vs differential entropy; compression’s RD trade-off vs generative modeling’s likelihood).
- An actual entropy coder (CABAC-style) is implemented and reported bitrates are from a real code, not just entropy estimates. Experiments compare against JPEG and JPEG 2000 on Kodak and several classic images, showing improved perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) and PSNR gains for most images, especially at low rates.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons:
1) Empirical validation is too narrow for the strength of the claims: baselines, datasets, and summary metrics are limited and omit stronger established codecs (e.g., HEVC intra/BPG). 
2) Practical and methodological questions remain insufficiently addressed (independent latents, per-λ models, limited context modeling in the coder), which undermines claims of end-to-end compression competitiveness.

Supporting arguments
- What is the specific problem? Optimizing image compression end-to-end (transforms, quantization surrogate, entropy model) for RD performance. This is important and timely.
- Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Generally yes: the link to transform coding, quantization theory, and VAEs is clearly articulated, and the motivation for GDN is grounded in previous work. However, related compression systems closer to state-of-practice (e.g., HEVC/BPG) are not used as baselines despite being widely recognized at the time; Toderici et al. are discussed but not compared quantitatively.
- Do the results support the claims rigorously? Partially. Strengths include:
  - Training with a differentiable RD surrogate and then reporting actual bitrates from a real coder is a high bar and commendable.
  - Visual results and MS-SSIM improvements are compelling across examples, and PSNR is better for most images at low rates.
  - The paper examines the bias/variance of the relaxation (Fig. 4), which is good practice.
  Limitations that reduce confidence:
  - Baselines: JPEG and JPEG2000 are relatively weak by current standards; no comparisons to BPG/HEVC-intra or WebP. This makes it hard to assess true competitiveness.
  - Datasets: Evaluations rely on Kodak (24 images) and a few classics; this is too small to substantiate broad claims. Arguments against summarizing RD across images are not sufficient—standardized aggregate metrics (e.g., BD-rate/BD-PSNR) over larger test sets are the norm.
  - Entropy model and coder: The latent model assumes independent marginals and the arithmetic coder uses simple raster-scan contexts. This is a limiting and somewhat unrealistic assumption compared to modern coders’ strong context models; the gap to a well-optimized entropy model is not quantified. Further, the training uses nonparametric marginals potentially per element (ψ(i)), while the coder shares models across space per channel—this mismatch risks overstating performance of the relaxed objective vs the implemented bitstream.
  - Practicality: Training a separate model per λ is inconvenient for deployment and evaluation; the paper notes this but does not quantify the practical cost or investigate variable-rate alternatives. Computational complexity (encode/decode time, memory) is not reported.
  - Validation of the relaxation: While Fig. 4 is helpful, there is no thorough ablation showing that optimizing with additive noise reliably leads to the same operating points as optimizing the true discrete problem (e.g., compare models trained with the surrogate vs trained/fine-tuned with straight-through estimators or STE variants).
  - Ablations: The role of GDN vs simpler nonlinearities, number of stages, overcompleteness, and capacity scaling is not systematically studied; statements about GDN efficiency are anecdotal.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not necessarily part of the accept/reject decision)
- Include strong, established baselines: BPG (HEVC intra), WebP, and, if possible, a modern learned baseline (e.g., recurrent or variational models) with actual bitstreams. Report BD-rate/BD-PSNR/BD-MS-SSIM across a larger and more diverse test suite (e.g., larger subsets of ImageNet, CLIC, Tecnick).
- Provide complexity metrics (runtime on CPU/GPU, memory footprint) and compare to JPEG2000/BPG for practical relevance.
- Clarify and rationalize the entropy model: are ψ(i) parameters shared across spatial positions or learned per-position? Ensure alignment between trained distributions and what the coder actually uses; quantify the rate gap from model mismatch and from limited contexts.
- Consider a variable-rate model (single set of transforms with a learned rate control) or at least quantify overheads of per-λ models in practice (model storage, switching).
- Expand ablations: GDN vs ReLU/parametric activations; number of stages; impact of overcompleteness; effect of nonparametric vs parametric marginal models; sensitivity to training crop sizes.
- Report color metrics (e.g., MS-SSIM on RGB) and perceptual metrics beyond MS-SSIM if feasible.

Questions for the authors
1) Baselines: Why not include BPG/HEVC-intra and WebP? Can you provide quantitative comparisons (PSNR and MS-SSIM) and BD-rate summaries on a larger dataset?
2) Entropy modeling: During training, are pỹi learned per spatial location or shared per channel? At decoding, you state models are shared across space within channels. Please quantify the rate penalty due to this mismatch and due to limited context modeling (vs an oracle entropy of the latents).
3) Rate control: How many distinct λ models are needed to cover the shown RD range? What is the practical overhead of storing and deploying multiple models? Could you train a single model that supports variable rates?
4) Relaxation fidelity: Beyond Fig. 4, can you show that optimizing with additive uniform noise reaches similar discrete RD points as directly optimizing quantized models (e.g., STE), across λ values?
5) Complexity: What are the encoding/decoding times and memory usage for typical image sizes compared to JPEG2000 and BPG? Any constraints on image size due to down/upsampling factors?
6) Color/perceptual evaluation: MS-SSIM is computed on luma only; how do results change for full-color MS-SSIM or other perceptual metrics?
7) Generalization: How does performance vary on more diverse content (synthetic graphics, text, saturated colors) beyond Kodak/natural photos?

Overall, the paper is an interesting and promising direction, but I require stronger empirical evidence against established codecs and clearer answers on modeling/practicality before recommending acceptance.
