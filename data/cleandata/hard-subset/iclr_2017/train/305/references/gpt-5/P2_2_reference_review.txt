Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles lossy image compression by jointly optimizing analysis/synthesis transforms and the entropy model end-to-end for the rate–distortion objective. The key technical device is a principled continuous relaxation of quantization (replacing rounding with additive uniform noise), enabling stochastic gradient training while remaining faithful to discrete coding at evaluation time.
- The transform architecture is a cascade of convolutions, down/up-sampling, and generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN), with parameters learned per rate point. The entropy model assumes independent marginals in the latent space, learned nonparametrically (piecewise linear), and the authors implement a practical arithmetic coder (CABAC-style) to report actual bitrates including side information.
- They show: (i) the relaxed objective is a good proxy for the discrete rate–distortion (quantified bias/variance), (ii) consistent perceptual gains (MS-SSIM) over JPEG/JPEG2000 across images and bitrates, and (iii) PSNR gains on most images, notably at lower rates. They also situate the method relative to VAEs, clarifying similarities and differences.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong, well-motivated end-to-end formulation that directly optimizes the compression objective with a sound relaxation and evaluates with real bitrates; (2) Convincing empirical improvements in perceptual quality and competitive PSNR against mature baselines, with transparent methodology and a complete coding pipeline.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses the core challenge of jointly optimizing rate and distortion, which classical transform coders approach via separate design of transform, quantization, and entropy coding. The motivation to move to nonlinear, learned transforms that adapt to natural image statistics is compelling and well grounded in prior literature (GDN, visual coding, transform coding).
- Placement in literature: Related work is carefully positioned. The connection to VAEs is informative and technically precise: the relaxed RD objective equals a VAE ELBO under specific choices of prior, approximate posterior, and likelihood; but the goals differ (rate–distortion trade-off with discrete coding vs generative modeling). Comparisons to contemporary neural compression (e.g., Toderici et al.) are fair, noting trade-offs (single model for multiple rates vs RD optimality and actual bitrates).
- Technical soundness: The continuous relaxation via additive uniform noise is a standard and appropriate surrogate for scalar quantization; the paper checks empirically that (a) differential entropy of the noisy latents approximates discrete entropy, and (b) the distortion surrogate is unbiased with modest variance. The architecture and optimization details are clear (filter parametrization, constraints on GDN/IGDN, normalization, learning rates). The nonparametric marginal modeling is reasonable and differentiable.
- Empirical rigor: Using a CABAC-style coder to report bitrates is a major strength; it avoids pitfalls of comparing differential entropy to real bitrates. Training and test sets are disjoint (ImageNet subset vs Kodak and classic images). Results include both objective measures (PSNR, MS-SSIM) and qualitative analysis, with accessible artifacts and a sensible discussion of averaging pitfalls across RD curves. The paper includes side-stream overhead in bitrates and discloses entropy-coding details.
- Significance: The work establishes a template for end-to-end neural transform coding that is now influential: nonlinear analysis/synthesis with GDN, proxy training via noise, learned priors, and evaluation with a real bitstream. The perceptual improvements despite optimizing MSE are noteworthy and practically relevant.

Limitations and considerations (do not block acceptance)
- Entropy model: Independent marginals across space/channels leave coding gains on the table versus context/adaptive or autoregressive models; the authors acknowledge this and partially mitigate with simple adaptation. This likely understates what the learned transform could achieve with a stronger entropy model.
- Comparisons: Baselines are JPEG and JPEG2000. A comparison to stronger modern baselines (e.g., HEVC intra/BPG) would strengthen claims, though at the time may be outside the standard scope. Still, the consistent MS-SSIM gains are persuasive.
- Variable-rate capability: The approach trains separate models per λ. This is less convenient than a single model supporting multiple rates; the paper notes related work in this direction but does not offer a multi-rate variant.
- Runtime and model footprint: Encoding/decoding efficiency is claimed qualitatively; quantitative runtime and model size comparisons to JPEG2000 are not provided.
- Ablations: The role of GDN vs simpler nonlinearities is discussed but not quantified. An ablation could isolate contributions of the transform, the entropy model, and the relaxation.

Feedback to improve the paper
- Add quantitative runtime/memory metrics (CPU/GPU encode/decode time, model parameters) to contextualize deployment feasibility.
- Provide a brief ablation: GDN/IGDN vs ReLU/sigmoid; linear-only baseline with learned scalar quantization; parametric vs nonparametric marginals. This would solidify what drives the gains.
- Report aggregate RD gains with a robust summary (e.g., BD-rate over Kodak) for both PSNR and MS-SSIM, alongside per-image plots.
- If feasible, include a comparison to a stronger modern codec (e.g., BPG) or at least discuss expected performance gaps due to the simpler entropy coder.
- Clarify how side information scales with image size and λ; report its percentage of the total bits across rates.
- Briefly discuss prospects for a single multi-rate model (e.g., via learned scales or hyperpriors) and stronger context models, to indicate the path forward.

Questions for the authors
- What are the encoding/decoding times and model sizes compared to JPEG2000 on the Kodak set (CPU-only and optionally GPU)?
- How sensitive are results to the independence assumption in the entropy model? Do you observe notable additional gains if you use stronger spatial contexts or autoregressive models?
- Can you quantify the bit gap between the learned differential entropy and the realized arithmetic-coding bitrate across rates?
- How does performance change if GDN/IGDN is replaced with pointwise nonlinearities given the same parameter budget? Any quantitative ablations?
- Could a single model be trained to cover multiple λ via latent scaling or conditional parameters, and how would its RD optimality compare?
- Do the results hold on additional datasets beyond Kodak (e.g., Tecnick), and for higher resolutions?
- Have you tried training directly with a perceptual loss (e.g., MS-SSIM) in this framework, and if so, how does RD/perceptual performance change?
