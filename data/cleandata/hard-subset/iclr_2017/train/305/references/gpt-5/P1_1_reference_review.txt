Summary and contributions
The paper tackles end-to-end optimization of lossy image compression by directly learning nonlinear analysis/synthesis transforms, the quantized latent distribution, and an entropy code to optimize the rate–distortion trade-off. The key technical ingredients are: (1) a cascaded convolutional transform with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and its inverse (IGDN) to produce a low-complexity, more Gaussianized latent; (2) a differentiable surrogate for quantization by replacing rounding with additive uniform noise, enabling stochastic gradient descent on a relaxation of the discrete rate–distortion objective; (3) nonparametric, factorized marginal models for the latents; and (4) an implemented entropy coder (CABAC-like) to report actual bitrates rather than entropy estimates. The paper carefully relates the relaxed objective to VAEs while clarifying why rate–distortion optimization differs conceptually and practically. Empirically, the method yields consistent perceptual improvements (MS-SSIM) over JPEG/JPEG2000 and better PSNR for most images, especially at low rates, with fewer blocking/ringing artifacts.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, well-motivated end-to-end framework that bridges classic transform coding and modern deep learning, with a differentiable rate–distortion objective and an implemented entropy code reporting real bitrates.
- Convincing empirical evidence of improved perceptual quality and competitive or superior PSNR over established baselines, supported by ablations validating the quality of the quantization/noise relaxation.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper directly addresses the core difficulty in lossy compression—jointly optimizing transform, quantization, and entropy coding—by replacing piecemeal design with a single end-to-end objective. The use of uniform noise as a surrogate for quantization and marginal likelihood for rate is well grounded (Gray & Neuhoff) and makes the problem tractable with SGD.
- Relation to literature: The connection to VAEs is explained precisely, including how the relaxed objective matches ELBO terms under Gaussian likelihood/MSE and how compression differs from generative modeling in the role of λ and discrete vs differential entropy. The paper also situates itself against contemporary neural compression work (Gregor et al., Toderici et al.) and classical transform coding.
- Technical soundness: The model design choices—GDN/IGDN, factorized nonparametric priors, parameter constraints/normalizations—are motivated by prior work on Gaussianization and stability. The paper evaluates the bias/variance of the relaxed rate and distortion versus the discrete objective, supporting the surrogate’s validity across rates.
- Empirical rigor: Training on a large set (ImageNet subset) and testing on standard datasets (Kodak, classic images) is appropriate. Reporting actual bitstreams using an implemented entropy code is a major strength compared to prior works relying on entropy estimates. Visual comparisons and MS-SSIM curves consistently favor the proposed method; PSNR is also improved for most images, especially at low rates, aligning with the qualitative artifact reduction.
- Practicality: At test time, coding reduces to feed-forward transforms plus scalar quantization and entropy coding, which is attractive for deployment. The authors note training is costly but inference is efficient.

Concerns and limitations (do not preclude acceptance)
- Baselines: Comparisons omit stronger modern baselines (e.g., BPG/HEVC intra), which by 2016–2017 were widely used in learned compression comparisons. This omission makes it harder to place the method versus state-of-the-art codecs.
- Entropy model: The latent prior is fully factorized with modest context adaptation in the CABAC coder; spatial dependencies are not exploited as strongly as in modern codecs. While this choice isolates the transform’s benefits, it likely underutilizes achievable rate savings.
- Multi-rate usage: Training a separate model per λ raises storage/maintenance concerns versus single-codec solutions or progressive coding; it would be helpful to demonstrate a conditional mechanism or rate-control knob within one model.
- Evaluation breadth: Kodak is small; broader quantitative results on larger test sets would strengthen generalization claims. No user study is provided (though MS-SSIM trends are supportive).
- Efficiency reporting: Encoding/decoding time, memory, and energy are not quantified, which are important for practical adoption.

Suggestions to improve the paper (for the camera-ready)
- Add a comparison to BPG/HEVC-intra if feasible, at least on Kodak, and discuss how the proposed coder fares at equal bitrate in PSNR/MS-SSIM and in visual artifacts.
- Provide runtime and complexity metrics (CPU/GPU, image sizes), and model size per λ; discuss the feasibility of a single conditional model covering multiple rates.
- Extend evaluation to a larger, diverse test set (e.g., Tecnick, CLIC validation) and summarize aggregate R–D (with careful alignment of rate points as discussed in the appendix).
- Include ablations: GDN vs pointwise nonlinearities at matched parameter budgets; effect of stages; factorized nonparametric prior vs simple (e.g., Gaussian/Laplacian) priors; with/without CABAC adaptation.
- Clarify fairness details: JPEG/JPEG2000 parameterization for PSNR vs MS-SSIM targets; color handling (RGB vs Y’CbCr) and chroma subsampling choices.
- Release code/models and the entropy coder to facilitate reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1) How does your method compare to BPG/HEVC-intra on Kodak in PSNR/MS-SSIM and visually? If not available, can you comment on expected gaps given your current entropy model?
2) What are typical encoding/decoding times and memory footprints for, say, 512×512 images on CPU and GPU? Are you real-time at common resolutions?
3) Can you train a single model with a continuous rate-control parameter (e.g., scaling the latent or injecting noise level) to cover multiple rates while remaining near the convex hull?
4) How sensitive are the gains to GDN/IGDN versus ReLU/ELU when matching parameter count/depth? Could you include quantitative ablations?
5) How much bitrate do you leave on the table due to the factorized latent prior and simple raster-scan CABAC? Have you tried simple spatial context (e.g., causal predictors) or channel-wise autoregression?
6) Beyond Kodak, do results hold on larger test sets? Any failure modes where JPEG2000 still clearly dominates in PSNR or visually?
7) Could you provide more details on side-information overhead for λ and model selection, and how that scales if many λ values are supported?

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution that convincingly demonstrates the advantages of end-to-end learned nonlinear transforms for image compression with real bitstreams and improved perceptual quality. I recommend acceptance.
