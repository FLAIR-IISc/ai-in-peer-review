Summary and contributions
The paper tackles end-to-end lossy image compression and proposes a learned nonlinear transform coding framework that jointly optimizes analysis/synthesis transforms and an entropy model for a target rate–distortion trade-off. Key ideas:
- A multistage convolutional analysis/synthesis architecture with generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN), inspired by visual neuroscience, to Gaussianize and decorrelate latents.
- A principled, differentiable relaxation of quantization by replacing rounding with additive uniform noise, enabling stochastic gradient optimization of rate (via differential entropy of the relaxed latents) and distortion (MSE).
- Nonparametric, per-channel marginal density models for the relaxed latents, trained jointly; and an actual entropy coder (CABAC-like) to report real bitrates instead of entropy proxies.
- A clear connection to VAEs, while emphasizing critical differences for compression (discrete coding, operating along the entire rate–distortion curve).
Empirically, the method delivers consistent perceptual improvements and often lower MSE/PSNR than JPEG and JPEG2000 on Kodak and other images, with particularly strong gains at low bitrates. Visual artifacts are more natural (e.g., simplified contours rather than blocking/ringing). The authors also verify that the continuous relaxation is a good proxy for the discrete problem.

Decision: Accept
Reason: The paper introduces a bold and influential end-to-end framework for learned lossy compression, including a differentiable quantization relaxation and a full bitstream implementation, and demonstrates substantial perceptual gains over widely deployed codecs. The conceptual advance and practical feasibility outweigh remaining limitations.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and impact: Unifying transform learning, entropy modeling, and rate–distortion optimization end-to-end is a significant step beyond classical transform coding and beyond contemporary generative modeling papers that do not target true bitstreams. The additive uniform noise relaxation is elegant, simple, and empirically validated; connecting it to VAEs clarifies the landscape and differences. Implementing an entropy coder and reporting actual bits is an important and rare step that boosts credibility.
- Motivation and positioning: The work is well placed with respect to classical compression (transform coding, quantization theory) and modern deep generative work (VAEs). The discussion of related learned compression (Gregor et al., Toderici et al.) is fair and highlights differences in objectives and evaluation.
- Evidence and rigor: The authors optimize on a large dataset, evaluate on standard test images, and provide both subjective and objective metrics (PSNR, MS-SSIM). The verification that the relaxed loss tracks the discrete rate and distortion is valuable. The qualitative comparisons are compelling across bitrates; quantitative results show clear perceptual gains and frequent PSNR gains, especially at low rates.
- Design choices: GDN/IGDN is a distinctive, efficient nonlinear mechanism that plausibly explains the improved visual quality, and the authors provide preliminary ablation insight (other activations need larger capacity). The nonparametric marginal models reduce model mismatch, and the bitstream uses adaptive arithmetic coding.

Limitations (not decision-critical, but worth noting)
- Separate models per λ: A single model operating across bitrates would be more practical for deployment. The paper acknowledges this and contrasts with Toderici et al.
- Entropy modeling: The independence assumption across latents and the simple raster-scan context leave spatial/conditional dependencies underexploited. This likely leaves bits on the table relative to modern context models.
- Baselines: Comparisons are restricted to JPEG/JPEG2000. Stronger engineering baselines like BPG (HEVC intra) would sharpen the picture, though the conceptual advances here remain substantial.
- Architecture search and ablations: The network design is somewhat ad hoc; more systematic exploration and explicit ablations (e.g., removing GDN, changing depth/width) would strengthen conclusions about what drives performance.
- Computational aspects: Encoding/decoding complexity and throughput relative to JPEG2000 and learned baselines are not systematically reported.

Feedback to improve the paper
- Add a comparison to a stronger traditional baseline (e.g., BPG) and, if feasible, to contemporary learned codecs under similar evaluation (actual bits, same datasets).
- Provide ablations: GDN vs ReLU/sigmoid at matched parameter counts; depth/width/stage count; effect of nonparametric vs parametric (e.g., logistic mixture) marginal models; impact of CABAC adaptation.
- Explore a single-model solution across rates (e.g., scale-space hyperparameterization or learned quantization step) to assess how much performance is lost vs per-λ training.
- Report runtime and memory usage for encoding/decoding and the entropy coder, to contextualize deployability.
- Clarify training stability and constraints for GDN/IGDN; include any regularization that was crucial.
- Clean up references (some entries are incomplete) and provide code or sufficient implementation details for reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- How close are your realized bitrates to the measured discrete entropy of the quantized latents? Can you quantify the coding overhead of your CABAC scheme across bitrates?
- Did you try a simple hyperprior or autoregressive context over latents (beyond per-channel adaptation) to capture dependencies? What rate gains do you expect from such context modeling?
- Can a single model controlled by a scale parameter (or λ at test time) approximate your per-λ models without large losses? Did you test variable quantization step sizes within one trained transform?
- What is the encoding/decoding throughput on typical hardware, and how does it compare to JPEG2000 and BPG?
- How sensitive are results to the piecewise-linear resolution of the marginal models? Would parametric families (e.g., Gaussian scale mixtures, mixtures of logistics) be competitive?
- Could you share quantitative ablations isolating the contribution of GDN/IGDN vs. standard activations at matched capacity?
- For color images, are the transforms exploiting cross-channel correlations effectively, and how do results compare against coding in YCbCr spaces?

Overall, this is a compelling and forward-looking paper that opens a new direction for learned compression with a complete and principled formulation. I recommend acceptance.
