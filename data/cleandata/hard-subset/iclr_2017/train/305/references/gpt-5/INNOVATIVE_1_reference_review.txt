Summary
The paper tackles lossy image compression by optimizing the entire pipeline end-to-end for rate–distortion performance. It proposes a nonlinear transform coding framework built from cascaded convolutions and generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN), uses a principled continuous relaxation of quantization via additive uniform noise to enable gradient-based training, learns nonparametric marginal models for entropy estimation, and implements an actual entropy coder (CABAC) to report real bitrates. The authors connect their formulation to variational autoencoders while emphasizing its distinct rate–distortion objective and discrete evaluation. Empirically, on Kodak and additional images, the method yields perceptually cleaner reconstructions than JPEG/JPEG2000, improves MS-SSIM substantially across rates, and typically improves PSNR, especially at low rates. They also verify that the relaxed objective correlates well with realized rate and distortion after quantization and coding.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong conceptual novelty and a bold, coherent end-to-end treatment of transform coding that bridges learned representations and practical compression (including real bitrates). (2) Empirically convincing improvements in perceptual quality with a plausible mechanism (learned nonlinear transforms), and a principled optimization strategy that has since influenced the field.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and significance: Jointly optimizing analysis/synthesis transforms and entropy models for a given rate–distortion tradeoff is a classic but hard problem. Recasting it with a differentiable proxy loss (uniform-noise relaxation) and training the whole stack is a substantive and timely contribution.
- Methodological novelty and clarity:
  - GDN/IGDN as spatially adaptive joint nonlinearities are well motivated by prior work on natural image statistics and shown to be effective here with relatively shallow networks.
  - The additive uniform noise relaxation is natural for quantization and is validated empirically. The paper makes the important choice to evaluate with actual entropy coding, avoiding pitfalls of comparing differential entropy to discrete bitrates.
  - Nonparametric, per-latent marginal modeling is a pragmatic, flexible component that reduces model mismatch while keeping the code simple.
  - The relationship to VAEs is thoughtfully articulated, clarifying similarities and key differences (objective, discretization, and the role of λ).
- Empirical support:
  - Qualitative results convincingly show the absence of linear basis artifacts (blocking/ringing) and a “progressive simplification” of structure that is perceptually preferable.
  - MS-SSIM gains across images and rates align with the visual evidence; PSNR improvements at most rates, especially low, support the claim under a traditional metric.
  - The authors verify the relaxation fidelity (rate bias diminishes at finer quantization; distortion proxy is unbiased with small variance).
- Scope and practicality:
  - Trained on a large, diverse subset of ImageNet and tested on standard datasets; code stream includes side information; encoding/decoding are efficient once trained.
  - The paper is upfront about limitations (independent latent assumption, simple entropy coding contexts, separate models per λ), which are reasonable for a first end-to-end demonstration.

Points tempering enthusiasm (not decision-critical)
- Baselines: Comparisons are limited to JPEG and JPEG2000. Inclusion of a stronger modern baseline (e.g., BPG/HEVC intra) would sharpen the claims, though arguably outside the era’s norm.
- Entropy modeling: Assuming factorized latents and a simple raster context limits bitrate gains; more expressive entropy models (context, hyperpriors) could further improve performance.
- Variable-rate: Training separate models per λ is less convenient than a single variable-rate model; although the authors acknowledge this and contrast with Toderici et al., it remains a practical limitation.
- Reporting: Compute complexity (FLOPs, runtime) and memory footprint are not quantified; these would help assess deployability.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Strengthen comparisons: If possible, add BPG/HEVC-intra results on Kodak and report MS-SSIM/PSNR gaps; at minimum, discuss expected performance versus such codecs.
- Complexity: Provide encoder/decoder runtime and parameter counts, and discuss trade-offs versus classic codecs.
- Ablations: Include results comparing GDN/IGDN to ReLU/sigmoid with matched capacity to quantify the specific contribution of GDN; add an ablation for parametric (e.g., Laplace) vs nonparametric marginals.
- Entropy coding: Quantify the gap between modeled entropy, arithmetic-coded bitrate, and an oracle coder to calibrate coding efficiency; report overhead from side information.
- Generalization: Add results on another standard test set (e.g., Tecnick) and for RGB images in the main text; include brief user study or at least more systematic perceptual metrics if feasible.
- Presentation: Clean up several garbled references and citation years/venues; ensure equations and variables are consistently defined (e.g., notation for densities and supports).

Questions for the authors
1. How large is the average gap between the relaxed differential entropy, the discrete entropy of quantized latents, and the final arithmetic-coded bitrate across λ? Please quantify with mean and variance.
2. What is the computational cost (encode/decode time, FLOPs) versus JPEG/JPEG2000 on CPU/GPU? How does model size scale with channels/stages?
3. How sensitive are results to the independent latent assumption? Did you try simple spatial contexts or shared hyperpriors, and what were the gains?
4. Can a single model be adapted at test time to different rates (e.g., via learned scale parameters or a global λ knob), or is retraining per λ strictly necessary in your setup?
5. Do GDN/IGDN still dominate when controlling for network capacity depth/width? Any insights on when GDN is most beneficial?
6. How much side information (bits) is sent per image (λ index, model IDs, etc.) and how does it scale with image size?
7. Could you provide more details on CABAC context design and adaptation strategy? How many contexts per channel and what features are used?
8. Beyond Kodak, how does performance vary on high-resolution images and smartphone photos with demosaicing artifacts or noise?

Overall, this is a bold, foundational step toward learned image compression with a practical, end-to-end pipeline. Despite some missing baselines and limited entropy modeling, the conceptual novelty and convincing qualitative/quantitative evidence justify acceptance.
