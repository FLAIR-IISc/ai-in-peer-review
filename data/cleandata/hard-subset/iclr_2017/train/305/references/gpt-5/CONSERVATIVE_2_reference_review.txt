1) Summary of claims and contributions
- The paper tackles lossy image compression by jointly optimizing rate and distortion end-to-end over an analysis transform, uniform scalar quantization, a synthesis transform, and an entropy model/codec.
- The core technical device is a continuous relaxation of quantization via additive uniform noise, enabling SGD, together with nonparametric marginal modeling of latents. The transforms are convolutional cascades with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and an approximate inverse (IGDN).
- The objective corresponds to a relaxed rate–distortion Lagrangian; the authors analyze its connection to VAEs and empirically check the bias of the relaxation in rate and distortion.
- They implement a practical entropy coder (CABAC-style) and report actual bitrates, not just entropy estimates.
- Experiments (trained on ImageNet subset; tested on Kodak and standard images) indicate improved perceptual quality, better MS-SSIM across the board, and often better PSNR than JPEG and JPEG 2000, especially at low rates.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Methodological soundness: a principled, end-to-end RD framework with a viable workaround for quantization non-differentiability and an actual entropy coder.
- Empirical evidence: consistent perceptual improvements and competitive to superior RD vs established baselines (JPEG/JPEG2000), with sanity checks on the relaxation.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and placement: The work addresses a longstanding problem (transform coding and rate–distortion optimization) and is well-situated relative to classical codecs and contemporary learned models (VAEs; Toderici et al.). The paper carefully distinguishes differential vs discrete entropy and reports real bitstreams, which is a notable strength.
- Technical rigor: The uniform-noise relaxation is standard but appropriately justified and empirically validated; the independence assumption in the latent space is explicit; the marginal models are learned nonparametrically. The linkage to the VAE objective clarifies the differences in goals and constraints.
- Completeness: Beyond a network, the inclusion of a coder and reporting of realized bitrates improve credibility compared to many prior learning-based compression papers at the time.
- Limitations that temper the claim strength:
  - Baselines: Restricting comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 omits stronger modern intra codecs (e.g., BPG/HEVC-intra), which were publicly available and widely used for benchmarking. This weakens claims of state-of-the-art RD performance.
  - Entropy coding and modeling: The adopted CABAC scheme is intentionally simple and spatially naive. While this isolates transform gains, it also complicates fairness against JPEG 2000, whose coder exploits richer contexts. Some of the observed gains could change under stronger context models.
  - Practicality/rate control: Training a separate model per λ is operationally inconvenient. The paper acknowledges this but does not quantify the overhead or explore mechanisms for a single model operating across rates.
  - Experimental scope: Kodak is standard but small. Results on additional, diverse datasets (e.g., high-res natural photos) and statistical summaries with confidence intervals would strengthen the generality claims. Runtime and memory/model-size benchmarks vs baselines are not reported.
  - Ablations: There is limited ablation on key design choices (GDN vs pointwise nonlinearities, depth/width, independence assumption impact, marginal model granularity). The appendix notes preliminary observations, but a more systematic study would bolster the causal claims about what drives the gains.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful; not all impacting the decision)
- Add stronger baselines: Include BPG (HEVC intra) and, if feasible, WebP or AVIF (or at least HEVC intra) to contextualize performance against the best classical codecs.
- Provide complexity metrics: Report encoding/decoding speed (CPU/GPU), memory footprint, and model size, and compare to JPEG 2000 and BPG. Include the practical overhead of storing separate models for each λ.
- Generalization: Evaluate on at least one additional image set with higher resolution and varied content (e.g., Tecnick or a held-out subset of ImageNet/COCO), and provide aggregated RD curves with error bars or paired per-image rate savings.
- Ablations:
  - Replace GDN/IGDN with ReLU/LeakyReLU/Swish under comparable parameter budgets to quantify the specific contribution of GDN.
  - Assess the impact of the marginal independence assumption by adding a simple spatial/context model to the entropy coder; quantify latent dependency residuals (e.g., mutual information between neighboring latents).
  - Study model capacity vs performance (channels, stages) to explore Pareto trade-offs.
- Practicality of single-model operation: Explore a shared transform with per-channel scaling or learned rate controllers to traverse the rate–distortion curve without retraining per λ.
- Reproducibility: Release code and pretrained models; clearly state all preprocessing and training schedules, and clarify whether any model parameters (e.g., marginal tables) must be transmitted in a real system.

Questions for the authors
- How does the method compare to BPG/HEVC-intra in PSNR and MS-SSIM across bitrates on Kodak and at least one larger dataset? If not evaluated, can you provide results?
- What are the encoding/decoding times and memory footprints on standard hardware, and how do they compare to JPEG 2000 and BPG?
- What is the total size of the parameters per λ (transforms plus marginal tables), and how would you envisage standardizing/distributing these in practice? Could a single model cover multiple rates with minor side-information?
- How sensitive are results to the independence assumption? Do you observe significant spatial dependencies in ŷ, and how much RD gain is left on the table by not modeling them?
- Can you provide ablations showing the incremental gains from GDN vs pointwise nonlinearities at matched parameter counts? Similarly, what fraction of gains derives from the transform versus the entropy model?
- Does performance hold on higher-resolution images and diverse content (e.g., graphics/text, line art)? Any failure cases?
- Beyond MSE, if trained directly for MS-SSIM, how do PSNR and visual artifacts change? Are there stability issues with the relaxation under non-Euclidean distortions?

Overall, this is a careful and impactful piece of work that advances learned compression with a principled RD formulation and a practical coder. Strengthening baselines and ablations would further solidify the claims.
