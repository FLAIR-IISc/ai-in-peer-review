Summary of contributions
- Proposes an end-to-end differentiable nonlinear transform coding framework for lossy image compression. The analysis/synthesis transforms are cascades of convolutions with generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN). Quantization is handled during training via an additive uniform-noise relaxation.
- Jointly optimizes rate and distortion (MSE) by learning the transforms and nonparametric marginal priors for latents; evaluation uses an actual arithmetic coder (CABAC-like) to report bitrates rather than entropy estimates.
- Empirically, on Kodak and several standard images, reports improved MS-SSIM across the board and improved PSNR over JPEG/JPEG2000 for most images, especially at lower bitrates. Claims perceptual advantages (fewer ringing/blocking artifacts).

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Evaluation lacks comparison to contemporary state-of-the-art codecs (e.g., HEVC-intra/BPG), and presents mixed PSNR gains versus strong established baselines (JPEG2000) at higher rates; (2) Evidence and analysis are insufficient to support broad claims of superiority—no user study, limited datasets, no runtime/model-size analysis, and multiple per-rate models make practicality unclear.

Supporting arguments
- Baselines: Comparing only to JPEG and JPEG2000 is inadequate to establish state-of-the-art performance. By the time of submission, HEVC intra (e.g., BPG) was a well-known baseline with strong R–D performance. Without this, the extent of improvement is unclear, especially since the method underperforms JPEG2000 for some images/rates.
- Evaluation scope and rigor: Results are mostly on Kodak and a few classic test images; this is small and may not reflect diverse content. Claims of “substantial” perceptual improvements are based on MS-SSIM and qualitative figures without a human study. The discussion acknowledges mixed PSNR outcomes; summary curves across datasets are deemphasized rather than rigorously standardized (e.g., BD-rate over matched anchors).
- Practicality: The approach trains a separate model per λ. This limits deployability compared to single-model variable-rate or scalable codecs and complicates comparisons at matched rate points. There is no analysis of computational cost (encode/decode latency), model size, or memory—key for any compression method. The claim that run-time is “efficient” is not quantified.
- Entropy modeling and coding: The independence assumption across latents and simple context adaptation (per-channel, raster scan) are known to be suboptimal. While the authors implement an arithmetic coder, they acknowledge it may underexploit spatial/contextual dependencies. It remains unclear whether the reported bitrates are competitive once a stronger entropy model (as in JPEG2000/HEVC) is used for the proposed latents.
- Methodological clarity: The uniform-noise relaxation is standard; the paper checks the bias qualitatively but does not report systematic error bounds across rates/datasets. The marginal nonparametric density training details suggest potential mismatch after quantization; more ablation is needed.
- Novelty vs prior art: Connections to VAEs and transform coding are well drawn, but the core design—convolutional analysis/synthesis with a continuous relaxation and learned per-latent priors—is close to emerging autoencoder-based compression work. The primary architectural novelty is GDN/IGDN, but ablations are limited; the paper concedes that similar results are achievable with other nonlinearities given more capacity. This weakens the centrality of GDN to the claimed gains.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Include strong baselines: BPG (HEVC-intra), WebP (intra-only), and ideally a modern JPEG2000 implementation tuned for R–D. Report BD-rate/BD-PSNR against these baselines on standard datasets (Kodak, Tecnick, CLIC).
- Ensure fair color handling: Compare with 4:4:4 settings or report both 4:2:0 and 4:4:4 to avoid bias from chroma subsampling differences. Use consistent color transforms and evaluation protocols for PSNR/MS-SSIM.
- Provide runtime and model-size metrics: encode/decode time on CPU/GPU, parameter counts, and memory footprint. Compression methods must be assessed on efficiency as well as R–D.
- Strengthen perceptual evidence: either run a small-scale MOS study or at least add LPIPS/VMAF metrics to triangulate perceptual quality claims.
- Add ablations: (i) GDN vs ReLU/SiLU and depth/width-controlled capacity; (ii) impact of the independence assumption vs simple cross-channel/spatial context models; (iii) effect of uniform-noise relaxation vs straight-through estimators; (iv) analysis/synthesis architectural choices.
- Practicality: Explore a single model supporting multiple rates (e.g., learned per-channel quantization scales, rate-control through latent scaling, or a variable-rate prior). This improves deployability and comparability.
- Generalization: Evaluate cross-dataset performance (e.g., DIV2K, Tecnick) without fine-tuning to demonstrate robustness.

Questions for the authors
- How does your method compare to HEVC-intra/BPG on Kodak and a larger dataset in PSNR and MS-SSIM across low and high bitrates? Please report BD-rate numbers.
- What are the encode/decode times, memory footprint, and parameter counts for your largest RGB model on typical hardware?
- How sensitive are results to the independence assumption in the latent space? Can simple context modeling (e.g., autoregressive over spatial neighbors) further reduce bitrate, and by how much?
- How large is the discrepancy between the relaxed objective and actual R–D after training, measured systematically over multiple rates and datasets?
- Can you provide a variable-rate single model and compare its R–D gap relative to your per-λ optimized models?
- How do results change if you (a) remove GDN, (b) replace with BN+pointwise nonlinearity, or (c) increase depth with standard activations to match parameter counts?
- Are the improvements consistent on high-resolution photographs outside Kodak (e.g., Tecnick 120 images)? Please share aggregate metrics and per-image plots.
- Will you release code, trained models, and the entropy coder to ensure reproducibility?
