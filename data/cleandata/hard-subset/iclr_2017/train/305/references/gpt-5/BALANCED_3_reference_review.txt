1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a principled, end-to-end trainable image compression system that directly optimizes the classical rate–distortion objective using learned nonlinear analysis/synthesis transforms and an explicit entropy model. Key ideas include: (i) a cascade of convolutional stages with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and an approximate inverse (IGDN), (ii) a practical and differentiable relaxation of quantization via additive uniform noise that yields a continuous objective approximating both rate (via differential entropy of the relaxed latent) and distortion (MSE), (iii) nonparametric, factorized marginal models for latents trained jointly with the transforms, and (iv) evaluation with an implemented arithmetic coder (CABAC-style) to report actual bit rates. The paper carefully relates its relaxed objective to the VAE framework while emphasizing differences relevant to compression. Empirically, on Kodak (and additional examples), the method achieves better PSNR than JPEG/JPEG2000 for most images, especially at lower rates, and substantially better MS-SSIM across the board, with noticeably fewer classic artifacts. The paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and demonstrates a complete codec rather than proxy metrics alone.

2) Decision: Accept
Reasons:
- Significance and rigor: A clean, well-placed formulation of end-to-end image compression with actual bitrates, linking rate–distortion training to a VAE-like relaxation, and delivering convincing perceptual and quantitative gains over widely used baselines.
- Completeness and clarity: The work includes a full entropy coder, careful training details, and strong analysis of the relaxation quality, enabling credible claims and practical feasibility.

3) Supporting arguments
Addressing the three key questions:
- Problem tackled: Learning analysis/synthesis transforms and an entropy model that jointly optimize the rate–distortion trade-off for images, overcoming non-differentiability from quantization while producing a practical codec.
- Motivation and placement: The approach is well grounded in transform coding and information theory, with a thoughtful bridge to VAEs. It builds on prior work on GDN for Gaussianization and perceptual transforms, and contrasts to generative compression and recurrent/binary approaches. The literature placement is appropriate and honest about differences.
- Do the results support the claims? The authors (a) show that the additive-uniform-noise relaxation yields good empirical approximations to discrete rate and distortion, (b) implement an arithmetic coder to measure actual bits, (c) report consistent MS-SSIM gains and generally improved PSNR vs JPEG/JPEG2000, and (d) provide extensive qualitative evidence of reduced artifacts. The methodology is sound. Some evaluations could be broader (see below), but the presented evidence supports the main claims.

Strengths:
- End-to-end rate–distortion optimization with a simple, effective relaxation of quantization.
- Use of GDN/IGDN yields compact, effective models with strong perceptual performance.
- Actual entropy coding and bitrates, not just entropy estimates.
- Clear relation to VAEs, but tailored to compression objectives and constraints.
- Careful engineering and transparency in training/architecture details.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- Baselines lack newer codecs (e.g., HEVC-intra/BPG, WebP) that were competitive at the time; this may understate the challenge at medium/high rates.
- Entropy model is factorized; spatial/context modeling is minimal, likely leaving rate on the table.
- One model per rate (λ); no single model supporting variable bit rates.
- Limited ablation: the unique contribution of GDN vs. more standard nonlinearities is discussed but not rigorously quantified; architectural sensitivity is only briefly explored.
- Evaluation is primarily on Kodak and qualitative examples; no human study for perceptual quality; limited analysis of runtime/complexity.

Overall, these are opportunities for strengthening rather than fatal issues. The core contribution stands.

4) Additional feedback and questions (to help improve the paper; not all affect my decision)
Suggestions:
- Expand baselines to include a strong modern codec (e.g., BPG/HEVC-intra), at least on a subset, to better contextualize performance.
- Provide ablations: (i) replace GDN/IGDN with pointwise nonlinearities (ReLU, PReLU, Swish) at matched parameter counts, (ii) vary number of stages/channels, (iii) linear-only baseline with learned quantization. Quantify PSNR/MS-SSIM and bitrate.
- Strengthen the entropy model with simple contexts or a learned hyperprior; even a modest context model could narrow the gap between estimated and realized rates.
- Explore a single model handling multiple rates (e.g., learned scale parameters, conditional/hypernetwork, or variable quantization step).
- Add runtime and memory benchmarks for encoding/decoding on CPU/GPU, and discuss feasibility for deployment.
- Report bitstream overhead breakdown (side information vs latent symbols) and estimate coder efficiency vs entropy.
- Consider a small-scale user study, or additional perceptual metrics, to corroborate MS-SSIM gains.
- Clarify generalization across resolutions/content (training on 256×256, testing on arbitrary sizes).

Questions for the authors:
- How close are arithmetic coder bitrates to the entropy of the learned marginals in practice? Can you report average gaps and the contribution of model mismatch vs finite-precision coding?
- How sensitive is training to the additive uniform noise assumption? Did you try annealing, straight-through estimators, or alternative relaxations?
- Can you share quantitative results for ablations replacing GDN/IGDN with simpler nonlinearities at similar model sizes? How many extra parameters/stages are needed to match performance?
- Does the model trained on 256×256 patches generalize well to high-resolution images without tiling artifacts? Any issues with boundary handling?
- What are typical encode/decode times and memory footprints? Are there bottlenecks in GDN/IGDN or the entropy coder?
- Would a hyperprior or simple spatial context in the entropy model yield significant gains, particularly at low rates?
- For color images, do you compress in RGB or a learned color space? Any benefit from a learned color transform?

This is a strong, impactful paper that advances learned image compression with a principled, practical method. I recommend acceptance.
