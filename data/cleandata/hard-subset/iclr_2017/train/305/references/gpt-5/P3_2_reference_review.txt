Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles end-to-end optimization of lossy image compression, directly optimizing a nonlinear analysis/synthesis transform pair and an entropy model for rate–distortion (R+λD) performance. The key methodological step is a continuous relaxation of quantization using additive i.i.d. uniform noise, enabling stochastic gradient descent while keeping a principled connection to the discrete problem. The model uses cascaded convolutions with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and its learned inverse (IGDN), and a nonparametric factorized prior over latents. Unlike much prior ML work, the paper implements an actual entropy coder (CABAC-like) and reports real bit rates. The approach is related to VAEs with a clear mapping between terms, but is explicitly optimized for the rate–distortion trade-off rather than generative likelihood. Empirically, on Kodak and other images, the method offers improved MS-SSIM and competitive/better PSNR vs JPEG/JPEG2000 across most rates, with markedly more natural artifacts at low rates.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- A principled, well-motivated formulation that bridges classical transform coding and modern deep learning, with a simple yet effective relaxation of quantization and an implemented entropy code yielding real bits.
- Strong empirical evidence of perceptual and quantitative gains over widely deployed baselines, with sound analysis of the relaxation’s fidelity and a thoughtful positioning relative to VAEs and prior neural compression efforts.

Supporting arguments
- Methodology: The additive-uniform relaxation is elegant and practical. It preserves a meaningful connection to discrete entropy (via p(y)∗U), makes both rate and distortion differentiable, and is validated empirically (bias small and diminishing at finer quantization). Modeling latents with independent nonparametric marginals is a reasonable first step that reduces model mismatch while keeping training stable. Using GDN/IGDN is well-motivated by prior work on Gaussianization and captures higher-order dependencies beyond linear transforms.
- Relation to literature: The paper situates itself well relative to transform coding (JPEG/2000), vector quantization, VAEs, and contemporary ML compression (Gregor et al., Toderici et al.), and carefully explains why generative training and discrete compression objectives differ. The discussion clarifies implications of the λ trade-off vs. VAE slack variance, and why evaluating real bits matters (addressing concerns raised by Theis et al.).
- Empirical evaluation: Results are compelling. The paper reports actual bitstreams, includes side information, and compares to standard codecs. Visual examples show qualitatively different, more natural failure modes (edge/contour preservation, texture simplification) than blocky/ringing artifacts of linear transforms. MS-SSIM gains are consistent; PSNR is often better, especially at low rates. The analysis on the relaxation’s bias/variance is informative.
- Feasibility: Despite high training cost, inference is efficient (feed-forward convs + scalar quantization + entropy coding). The approach is implementable end-to-end.

Limitations and concerns (do not preclude acceptance)
- Single-rate models: Training a separate model per λ limits practicality (on-device storage/standardization of multiple parameter sets). A multi-rate conditioning or scalable coding strategy would strengthen applicability.
- Entropy model simplicity: Assumes factorized latents with per-channel, spatially shared marginals and a relatively simple coding order. This likely leaves bits on the table compared to context/adaptive models used in modern codecs; thus the reported gains are even more impressive, but it would help to quantify the gap between estimated entropy and coded bits across rates.
- Ablations: The central claim that GDN/IGDN is especially effective would be stronger with systematic ablations vs ReLU/sigmoid, number of stages/channels, and alternative priors (e.g., logistic mixtures). Preliminary comments are promising but not quantified in the main text.
- Evaluation breadth and fairness: Kodak is standard but small; results on larger/diverse datasets (e.g., BSDS500, CLIC) and high-resolution photos would build confidence. More detail on JPEG2000 settings and adaptive coder strength would help ensure comparability.
- Practical metrics: No reporting of model size, FLOPs, encoding/decoding time, or memory—important for deployment and comparisons to engineered codecs.

Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not essential for decision)
- Provide ablations: (i) replace GDN/IGDN with pointwise nonlinearities; (ii) vary depth/width; (iii) compare nonparametric vs parametric priors. Quantify R–D impacts.
- Report computational metrics: Parameter counts, model size per λ, runtime (CPU/GPU) for encode/decode, and memory footprint.
- Multi-rate strategies: Explore conditioning on λ or using learned channel-wise quantization scales to enable a single model to cover multiple rates. Discuss trade-offs vs per-λ optimality.
- Stronger entropy modeling: Evaluate spatial context models (e.g., masked conv priors, hyperpriors) or improved coding orders; quantify bit savings relative to current CABAC design.
- Broader evaluation: Add results on larger datasets and 4K images; include BD-Rate summaries where feasible, acknowledging caveats.
- Reproducibility: Release code/models and bitstreams to facilitate adoption and standardization.

Questions for the authors
- How many parameters per λ, and what is the decoder footprint if multiple λ models must be shipped? Could you amortize parameters across λ (e.g., via conditional normalization/scales)?
- How close are the coded bit rates to the estimated entropies across rates and images? A table of average gap (bits/pixel) would be useful.
- Did you evaluate alternative priors (e.g., mixtures of logistics) or simple spatial context (autoregressive) during training? If so, how did they affect R–D and training stability?
- Can you provide systematic ablations quantifying GDN/IGDN’s benefit vs ReLU/sigmoid at the same capacity?
- What are typical encode/decode times and FLOPs for 512×512 RGB images? How does runtime scale with depth/channels?
- How sensitive is the method to the size/composition of the training set? Does performance improve significantly with more data?
- For color images, do you process in RGB or a decorrelated color space? Any observed gains from color transforms?

Overall, this is a strong, well-motivated paper that advances neural image compression with a principled objective, practical training, and convincing empirical results using real bitstreams. I recommend acceptance.
