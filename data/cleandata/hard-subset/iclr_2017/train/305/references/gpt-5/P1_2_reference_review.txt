1. Summary and contributions
The paper tackles lossy image compression by jointly optimizing the analysis transform, quantization, entropy model, and synthesis transform to minimize a rate–distortion objective. The approach replaces hard quantization during training with additive uniform noise, enabling end-to-end training via SGD, and uses nonparametric marginal models and an actual entropy coder (CABAC-style) to report real bitrates. The transforms are cascaded convolutions with generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN), inspired by vision models, and are trained separately for distinct rate–distortion trade-offs (λ values). The paper also clarifies the relationship to variational autoencoders, while emphasizing key differences arising from discrete coding and rate–distortion optimality. Empirically, the method achieves improved MS-SSIM across images and bitrates, and better PSNR than JPEG and JPEG 2000 on most images, with markedly more natural visual artifacts at low rates.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, principled end-to-end formulation that connects classical rate–distortion optimization to modern deep models through a well-justified continuous relaxation, with an implemented entropy coder and real bitrates.
- Convincing empirical evidence of perceptual and often PSNR improvements over widely used baselines, coupled with analysis validating the relaxation accuracy.
- Clarity of exposition and positioning relative to VAEs and prior transform coding; practical feasibility demonstrated.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Lossy image compression remains important. Optimizing rate and distortion jointly is challenging; transform coding dominates practice. Providing a learnable nonlinear transform that is optimized end-to-end directly for rate–distortion is both impactful and timely.
- Motivation and related work: The background on transform coding, GDN, quantization challenges, and the link to VAEs is accurate and well-placed. The paper distinguishes its goal (discrete rate–distortion with real bitrates) from generative modeling and previous neural compression efforts. Citing JPEG/JPEG2000, VAE works, and related neural compression approaches is appropriate; a limitation is the absence of BPG/HEVC-intra comparisons (strong baseline circa 2016–2017).
- Methodological soundness:
  - Quantization relaxation via additive uniform noise is principled (matches marginal moments and yields a continuous proxy whose differential entropy matches discrete entropy at integers). The paper empirically verifies the rate and distortion approximation quality.
  - The use of nonparametric marginal models trained jointly is sensible and reduces model mismatch; the independence assumption is standard but acknowledged as limiting (and the simple raster scan coding under-exploits spatial dependencies).
  - Training per-λ is coherent with the convex hull interpretation of rate–distortion optimality.
  - Implementation details (filters, GDN parameterization, normalization, Adam schedule) are adequate and reproducible; CABAC-like coding with explicit side information makes the evaluation credible.
- Empirical support:
  - Evaluation on Kodak and standard images shows consistent MS-SSIM gains and frequent PSNR gains, with strong qualitative improvements (suppression of ringing/blocking, graceful simplification at low rates).
  - Using actual bitrates strengthens claims. The paper also shows the bias/variance behavior of the relaxed rate vs discrete counterparts.
  - Limitations: baselines do not include more modern codecs; average summaries are discussed carefully, but broader aggregation and statistical analysis could be stronger.

4. Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Baselines: Please add comparisons to BPG (HEVC-intra) or WebP where possible; these are stronger than JPEG2000 and were available at the time. This will situate performance vs the best classical codecs.
- Practicality: Report model size, encoding/decoding runtime on CPU/GPU, and memory footprint. These matter for deployment.
- Single-model flexibility: Training separate models per rate is a practical drawback. Consider and/or report a single model with a learned rate control (e.g., conditional transforms, learned quantizer step size, or λ conditioning).
- Entropy model: The independence assumption and simple raster scan leave bits on the table. A brief experiment with spatial context models or channel-wise hyperpriors would indicate headroom.
- Ablations: Provide quantitative ablations on: GDN vs ReLU/other nonlinearities (the text mentions preliminary findings), number of stages/channels, and the impact of nonparametric vs parametric priors.
- Fairness and color handling: Clarify color space conversions and chroma subsampling across methods; ensure PSNR/MS-SSIM are computed consistently (e.g., on RGB vs Y’CbCr luma only) to avoid bias.
- Generalization: Report performance on more datasets (e.g., Tecnick, CLIC) and at higher resolutions; discuss sensitivity to training-set size and preprocessing choices (downsampling/cropping).
- Reproducibility: Consider releasing code/models, or at least more detailed hyperparameters, to accelerate adoption.

5. Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the GDN/IGDN choice? Can you provide a controlled ablation (e.g., ReLU or PReLU with similar parameter counts/stages) showing the RD gap?
- How large is the gap between your simple entropy coder and an oracle that uses the learned continuous priors without adaptation? Conversely, how much could spatial context models reduce rate beyond your CABAC scheme?
- What is the computational complexity (ms/MP, GFLOPs) and parameter count for the reported models at different λ? Is real-time decoding on CPU feasible for HD images?
- Can the approach be extended to a single model supporting a continuum of rates (e.g., via λ-conditioned transforms or adaptive quantization scales) without retraining per λ?
- How stable is training with the uniform-noise relaxation across different seeds and training set sizes? Any observed failure modes (e.g., degenerate priors, vanishing gradients)?
- Do you observe different behaviors for RGB vs grayscale models beyond capacity scaling? Does training in Y’CbCr with chroma downsampling improve RD or perceptual quality?
- Given the connection to VAEs, have you tried richer priors (e.g., mixtures, flows) for the latents while maintaining the discrete coding pipeline? What are the trade-offs?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, carefully executed paper that makes a substantive contribution to learned image compression and offers a clean bridge between classical rate–distortion and modern deep learning frameworks. I recommend acceptance.
