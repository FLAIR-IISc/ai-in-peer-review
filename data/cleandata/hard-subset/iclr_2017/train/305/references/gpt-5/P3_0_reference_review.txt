Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles end-to-end optimization of lossy image compression by jointly learning analysis and synthesis transforms together with an entropy model, directly for a rate–distortion (R+λD) objective.
- It addresses the non-differentiability of quantization by replacing rounding with additive uniform noise during training, enabling stochastic gradient descent while preserving a principled relation to the discrete problem.
- The transforms are cascades of convolutions and a generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearity, with an approximate inverse (IGDN) at the decoder. The learned code space is assumed factorized and modeled with nonparametric univariate densities.
- The paper relates the relaxed objective to the ELBO in variational autoencoders, clarifying differences between RD-optimized compression and generative modeling.
- Crucially, it evaluates actual bit rates via an implemented entropy coder (CABAC), not just entropy estimates, and demonstrates improved perceptual quality and state-of-the-art performance vs JPEG and JPEG2000 on Kodak and standard images, despite training with MSE.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, well-motivated methodological contribution: a practical, principled end-to-end optimization framework that bridges classical transform coding and modern deep learning, and reports actual bitrates.
- Compelling empirical evidence of improved visual quality and competitive-to-better RD curves, with careful discussion of the relaxation quality and an implemented bitstream.

Supporting arguments
Novelty and positioning:
- The use of additive uniform noise as a quantization surrogate is known, but the paper integrates it cleanly into a RD-optimized, deployable compression pipeline and ties it to a concrete entropy coder. This goes beyond generative modeling efforts (e.g., VAEs, DRAW/conceptual compression) by (i) targeting RD trade-offs directly, (ii) operating in a discrete domain at evaluation time, and (iii) reporting actual bits.
- The adoption of GDN/IGDN as joint, spatially adaptive nonlinearities is well motivated by prior work on Gaussianization and biological plausibility; the empirical efficiency of GDN for compression is a meaningful insight.

Methodological soundness:
- The relaxation is carefully justified: uniform noise yields a continuous density whose point masses match the discrete pmf at integer grid points; the authors empirically quantify bias/variance of both relaxed distortion and entropy and show small biases (with expected behavior at coarse quantization).
- The factorized code model is clearly a simplifying assumption. The paper is upfront about the mismatch between the training density (per-element, independent) and the practical entropy coder (spatially shared per channel with limited context), and shows CABAC adaptation only modestly helps. This is a limitation but does not undermine the main claim; rather, it delineates clear avenues for further gains (context models/hyperpriors).
- Training per-λ models is a pragmatic way to map out the convex hull; while less convenient than a single variable-rate model, it aligns with RD theory and simplifies analysis.

Empirical evaluation:
- The qualitative results are strong: reduced blocking/ringing, more natural edge/contour rendering, and consistently higher MS-SSIM across rates, despite MSE training. PSNR is competitive and often superior at low–mid rates.
- Reporting actual bitrates with a concrete bitstream is a significant step forward relative to contemporaneous learning-based works that used entropy estimates.
- The evaluation set (Kodak, classic images, additional photos) is conventional and relevant. The discussion of the difficulty of fair aggregation across methods is thoughtful; per-image RD curves are provided online.

Limitations and concerns:
- Independence assumption in the latent space leaves compression efficiency on the table compared to modern context/hyperprior models. The entropy coder’s rudimentary spatial adaptation exacerbates mismatch with the trained density; a learned context model or hyperprior would likely improve rates further.
- The nonparametric marginal modeling appears to be per-element during training, but the entropy coder shares distributions per channel over space. This mismatch could introduce suboptimalities and possible overfitting in training; tying the marginals per channel during training (or learning a more expressive shared model) may be preferable.
- Results are primarily against JPEG/JPEG2000. Around the time of submission, HEVC-intra/BPG was a strong baseline for intra coding; inclusion would strengthen claims. At minimum, a discussion of expected gaps vs HEVC-intra would be helpful.
- Practicality: per-λ models require both sides to share parameter sets; the bitstream only signals λ. This is acceptable as a research prototype but raises deployment questions (model storage, standardization).
- Aggregation: While the paper argues against single-number summaries, including standard metrics like Bjøntegaard delta rate over Kodak would make comparisons crisper.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all affect my decision)
- Provide timing and compute complexity for encode/decode vs JPEG2000 (CPU and GPU), and memory footprint. GDN/IGDN can be expensive; practical throughput matters for adoption.
- Add ablations: (i) replace GDN with pointwise nonlinearities at comparable parameter counts; (ii) effect of number of stages; (iii) training with tied marginals per channel vs per-element. The appendix hints at preliminary findings; concrete numbers would strengthen the argument for GDN.
- Report color (RGB) results more prominently; detail the colorspace and chroma handling to ensure fair comparison to JPEG/J2K.
- Consider a simple variable-rate mechanism (e.g., per-channel scaling, learned quantization step or importance maps) to approach a single-model solution across rates.
- Clarify how the nonparametric densities are regularized to avoid degenerate solutions; discuss stability/sensitivity to the spline resolution and range adaptation.

Questions for the authors
- Can you quantify the train–test gap induced by the mismatch between per-element nonparametric densities used in training and the per-channel shared, weakly adaptive CABAC used at test time? Have you tried training with per-channel shared marginals to match the coder?
- How does performance change if you introduce even a simple spatial context (e.g., using causal neighborhoods) in the entropy model? Any preliminary results?
- Can you provide Bjøntegaard delta-rate numbers vs JPEG2000 on Kodak for PSNR and MS-SSIM to summarize across images?
- What are the encode/decode runtimes and model sizes for the λ models evaluated? How does performance scale with the number of channels/stages?
- For RGB, which colorspace is used internally, and how are chroma subsampling differences handled in comparisons to JPEG/J2K?
- Did you evaluate against HEVC-intra/BPG? If not, what gap would you anticipate at comparable rates, given your factorized model?
