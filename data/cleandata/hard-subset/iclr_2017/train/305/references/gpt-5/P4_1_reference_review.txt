1. Summary of claimed contributions
The paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on learned nonlinear analysis/synthesis transforms with generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN), optimized for a Lagrangian rate–distortion objective. The key technical device enabling training is a continuous relaxation of quantization, replacing rounding with additive uniform noise to obtain usable gradients and to estimate both rate (via differential entropy) and distortion during training. The latent code’s marginals are modeled non-parametrically and assumed independent, and an actual entropy coder (CABAC-style) is implemented so evaluation reports true bitrates rather than entropy estimates. The method is trained separately for multiple λ values to trace the rate–distortion curve, and is evaluated on Kodak and additional images for both grayscale and RGB. Despite being trained with MSE, the method yields images with fewer perceptually objectionable artifacts than JPEG/JPEG2000 and shows improved MS-SSIM at most bitrates, with PSNR gains on most images, especially at lower rates. The paper also clarifies the relationship to variational autoencoders while highlighting important differences relevant for compression.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Technically sound and well-motivated end-to-end formulation that closes the loop by implementing a real entropy coder and reporting actual bitrates.
- Strong empirical evidence of improved perceptual quality and generally better rate–distortion trade-offs versus established baselines, supported by clear methodology and analyses of the quantization relaxation.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: Joint optimization of analysis/synthesis transforms and entropy model for lossy image compression under a rate–distortion objective. Traditional pipelines optimize stages separately and largely rely on linear transforms; this work seeks a principled, trainable nonlinear alternative.
- Motivation and placement: The approach is well connected to prior transform coding, divisive normalization for image statistics, and variational inference. The paper carefully contrasts its RD objective with generative modeling aims, and justifies the use of GDN based on prior Gaussianization results. It also situates itself relative to contemporaneous learned compression/generative approaches and explains why training with actual bitrates matters.
- Support for claims and rigor: 
  - The continuous relaxation is justified and empirically validated: the relaxed distortion is essentially unbiased; the rate estimate’s bias shrinks at finer quantization. This addresses a frequent concern with gradient approximations in quantized models.
  - The architecture and training procedure are described in detail, including stabilization techniques and parameterizations. The entropy coding scheme is specified, including side information and adaptive modeling across spatial positions within channels.
  - Experiments include both PSNR and MS-SSIM and qualitative comparisons. The method consistently improves perceptual quality (MS-SSIM and visual artifacts) and usually PSNR. Results are on standard datasets (Kodak) with additional examples; rate–distortion curve aggregation caveats are transparently discussed.
- Novelty: While elements such as quantization noise modeling and variational training exist, their integration into an end-to-end, deployable codec with GDN-based nonlinear transforms, nonparametric marginals, and measured bitrates represents a substantive, novel contribution to learned image compression. The analysis of the VAE connection under the RD objective is insightful.

Limitations (non-fatal):
- The independence assumption in the latent space for entropy modeling underutilizes spatial/contextual structure; the simple raster-scan CABAC contexts are limited compared to modern codecs. This likely leaves bitrate on the table.
- Separate models per λ complicate deployment and differ from single-model variable-rate methods.
- PSNR is not universally better; comparisons are limited to JPEG/JPEG2000 and do not include stronger modern baselines (e.g., HEVC intra-based image coding) that were available.
- Computational complexity and runtime are not quantified; ablations on GDN vs. simpler nonlinearities are mentioned but limited.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily affecting the decision)
- Provide quantitative encode/decode runtimes and memory footprints to contextualize practicality.
- Include ablations: replace GDN/IGDN with pointwise nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU, tanh) at similar parameter counts; remove non-parametric marginals; vary depth/width to show efficiency gains attributable to GDN.
- Strengthen entropy modeling: add simple spatial/context contexts or autoregressive factors to reduce the independence gap; quantify bitrate gains from adaptation versus a fixed model.
- Compare against stronger baselines (if feasible), and report BD-rate summaries per metric to complement per-image plots.
- Consider a single model operating across rates (e.g., conditioning on λ or using learned scale parameters) to improve practicality.
- Provide human perceptual study results to corroborate MS-SSIM and qualitative observations.
- Clarify how color handling (e.g., color space, gamma) is managed during training/evaluation to ensure fair PSNR/MS-SSIM comparisons.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the architecture (number of stages, channels, filter sizes) and to the GDN form? Can you provide an ablation quantifying the trade-off between GDN and increased depth/width with pointwise nonlinearities at equal parameter budgets?
- What are the average encoding/decoding times per megapixel on your hardware, and the parameter count/memory requirements for the analysis/synthesis transforms?
- How much bitrate is lost due to the independence assumption? Can you report the gap between true coded bits and the sum of marginal cross-entropies, and what fraction is recovered by CABAC adaptation?
- How robust are the trained models to distribution shifts (e.g., different datasets or content types)? Any evidence of overfitting given the ImageNet-derived training set?
- How large is the side information overhead per image (including λ index), and how is λ selected at test time?
- Did you experiment with a single conditional model to cover multiple rate points? If so, how did its RD performance compare to per-λ models?
- For RGB, which color space is used internally, and is training performed in linear or gamma-corrected space? How does this choice affect PSNR/MS-SSIM outcomes?

Overall, the paper is clear, technically sound, and offers a meaningful advance for learned image compression with careful evaluation using real bitrates.
