1) Summary of claims and contributions (generous but precise)
The paper proposes a complete, end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on nonlinear transform coding. The main contributions are:
- A practical rate–distortion (R+λD) training objective that sidesteps the non-differentiability of quantization by replacing rounding with additive uniform noise, jointly optimizing analysis and synthesis transforms and the entropy model.
- Use of cascaded convolutional transforms with generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN), shown to be effective at Gaussianizing image statistics, enabling efficient scalar quantization in the latent space.
- A nonparametric, factorized marginal model for latent coefficients, learned jointly with the transforms, and a working entropy coder (CABAC-like) that yields real bitstreams, enabling evaluation in actual bits rather than differential entropy.
- An analysis connecting the relaxed objective to variational autoencoders, clarifying similarities and critical differences with generative modeling.
- Empirical validation on Kodak (and additional images), showing consistent perceptual improvements and strong MS-SSIM gains over JPEG/JPEG 2000 across bitrates, and PSNR gains for most images, particularly at low rates. The paper also empirically validates that the relaxed loss is a good proxy for the discrete rate and distortion.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (i) Strong, well-motivated technical contribution that enables end-to-end RD optimization with a clear and validated relaxation of quantization; (ii) A complete, practically evaluable system with real bitstreams and consistent empirical improvements over strong baselines, coupled with clear exposition and sufficient training/evaluation details.

3) Supporting arguments
Addressing the core review questions:

- What problem is tackled? The paper tackles lossy image compression with the objective of optimizing the trade-off between rate (bit budget) and distortion, learning both the analysis/synthesis transforms and probability models end-to-end.

- Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? Yes. The difficulty of high-dimensional vector quantization and the standard transform–quantize–entropy-code pipeline are accurately described. The use of additive uniform noise as a relaxation is standard in quantization theory and is carefully justified and empirically validated. The choice of GDN/IGDN is motivated by prior work on Gaussianizing natural image statistics and by biological inspiration; the paper also contrasts GDN with batch normalization. The discussion situating the method relative to VAEs is technically sound and clarifies why evaluating real bits is essential. Related work (Toderici et al., Gregor et al.) is appropriately discussed, including differences in objectives and evaluation.

- Do the results support the claims with rigor? Largely yes. The paper:
  • Optimizes separate models for each λ and evaluates using a CABAC-style coder with all side information included.
  • Verifies that the relaxed loss approximates discrete entropy and MSE well, with the expected biases diminishing at finer quantization.
  • Provides extensive qualitative evidence and MS-SSIM curves showing consistent perceptual gains, and PSNR improvements for most images, especially at low rates.
  • Gives sufficient architectural and training detail (filters, strides, parameterization, optimization procedure, stability constraints) to assess technical soundness.

Limitations that temper the claims, but do not outweigh the contributions:
- The entropy model assumes independent latents; spatial/contextual dependencies are only weakly exploited in coding, likely leaving rate untapped relative to advanced codecs.
- Models are trained per λ rather than using a single adaptable model across rates.
- Baselines focus on JPEG/JPEG2000; more modern baselines (e.g., HEVC intra/BPG, WebP) are not included.
- Evaluation is primarily on Kodak and a small set of canonical images; broader datasets would strengthen generalization claims.
- Limited ablation on the necessity of GDN versus simpler nonlinearities; preliminary notes suggest efficiency but lack thorough quantification.

Overall, the method is technically sound, clearly presented, novel in its end-to-end RD setup with real coding, and supported by convincing empirical evidence.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily affecting the decision)
- Baselines and datasets: Add comparisons to HEVC intra/BPG and WebP, and include larger modern datasets (e.g., Tecnick, CLIC, DIV2K) for stronger generalization evidence.
- Ablations: Provide quantitative ablations on (i) GDN/IGDN vs. ReLU/sigmoid activations at matched parameter counts and depths, (ii) number of stages/channels, (iii) effect of factorized vs. simple context models in the latent distribution, and (iv) training with vs. without filter normalization constraints.
- Single model across rates: Discuss/attempt strategies to obtain multiple rate points from a single model (e.g., per-channel quantization scales, learnable λ-conditioning) and compare RD envelopes.
- Entropy modeling: Evaluate stronger entropy models (e.g., simple hyperprior or autoregressive contexts) to quantify the gap left by the independence assumption, even if the focus remains on the transform.
- Efficiency reporting: Include parameter counts, MACs, and encoding/decoding runtimes on CPU/GPU, as well as memory footprint, to contextualize deployment feasibility.
- Aggregation of RD results: Provide standardized aggregate metrics (Bjøntegaard deltas or matched-rate averages) alongside per-image curves to summarize trends without overemphasizing any bitrate regime.
- Reproducibility: Clarify whether code/models will be released and specify random seeds and exact data splits/preprocessing.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is RD performance to the independence assumption in the latent space? Did you try simple spatial/channel-wise context models or hyperpriors, and what was the bit-rate gain?
- What are the parameter counts and typical encode/decode runtimes for grayscale and RGB models per megapixel? Any observed bottlenecks?
- How does training per-λ compare to training a single model with a variable quantization scale at test time? What RD gap do you observe?
- Can you quantify the gap between differential entropy estimates and actual bit rates across the entire RD curve, not only at selected points?
- How robust is the learned piecewise-linear marginal estimation to sampling range selection? What fraction of mass tends to fall outside the modeled support, and how is tail mass handled in practice?
- Did you evaluate using a luminance/chroma color space (e.g., YCbCr) for the analysis transform vs. RGB? Any impact on RD?
- To what extent does CABAC adaptation reduce model mismatch vs. a non-adaptive coder in your pipeline? Could a better scan order or simple contexts close more of the gap to JPEG2000’s entropy coding?
- Given the good perceptual outcomes under MSE, have you tried substituting MS-SSIM or a learned perceptual metric in gp for RGB, and what trade-offs in PSNR/RD arise?
