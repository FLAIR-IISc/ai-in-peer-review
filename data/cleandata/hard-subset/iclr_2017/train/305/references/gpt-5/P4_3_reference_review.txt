1. Summary of contributions
The paper tackles end-to-end optimization of lossy image compression by jointly learning an analysis transform, a synthesis transform, and an entropy model to directly optimize the rate–distortion trade-off. The key ideas are:
- Replacing non-differentiable scalar quantization with additive uniform noise to obtain a continuous, differentiable surrogate objective that approximates both rate (via differential entropy) and distortion (MSE).
- Using cascaded convolutional stages with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and its inverse (IGDN) as the core nonlinearity, motivated by their ability to Gaussianize natural image statistics.
- Modeling latents with independent, non-parametric marginal densities (piecewise linear) learned jointly with the transforms.
- Training separate models per λ to trace the rate–distortion curve; then performing real uniform quantization and implementing a CABAC-based entropy coder to report actual bit rates, not just entropy estimates.
- Empirical validation on Kodak and other images showing improved perceptual quality over JPEG and JPEG 2000 across bitrates, strong gains in MS-SSIM, and competitive/better PSNR on most images; and an analysis of the accuracy of the continuous relaxation vs the discrete objective. The paper also clarifies the relationship to variational autoencoders while highlighting conceptual differences from generative modeling.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Technically sound and well-motivated end-to-end rate–distortion framework with a principled relaxation of quantization and a practical, complete codec (including entropy coding).
- Clear empirical support: improved perceptual quality, stronger MS-SSIM, solid PSNR at low-to-mid rates, and explicit measurement of real bitrates; plus an analysis validating the surrogate loss.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The work addresses the classical intractability of joint high-dimensional quantization by learning nonlinear transforms that shape the latent space to suit scalar quantization and marginal entropy models. The use of GDN/IGDN is well grounded in prior work on natural image statistics and is a plausible choice for compression.
- Methodological rigor: The surrogate objective is carefully derived; additive uniform noise for quantization relaxation is standard and the paper empirically quantifies its bias/variance, showing small distortion bias and expected differential-to-discrete entropy gap that diminishes at finer quantization. The factorized, non-parametric marginal modeling is reasonable and trained jointly. The relationship to VAEs is precisely stated, including the role of λ and limitations when using non-Euclidean/perceptual distortions.
- Completeness: Unlike many contemporaneous works, the paper implements an entropy coder and reports actual bitrates, includes side information, and discusses coding contexts and adaptivity. Training/evaluation details and architecture choices are described in the appendix.
- Empirical evidence: Results span grayscale and RGB, show consistent perceptual benefits over JPEG and JPEG 2000, and improved MS-SSIM across images and rates. PSNR is often better, especially at low rates. Visual examples are convincing and align with the narrative (fewer ringing/blocking artifacts; contour preservation).
- Clarity and positioning: The paper is clearly written, places the work in the context of transform coding and recent learning-based methods, and fairly discusses related approaches (e.g., Gregor et al., Toderici et al.).

Caveats (do not outweigh the strengths for acceptance):
- The entropy model is factorized and the coding context is relatively simple; more advanced context modeling or hyperpriors could further improve rates.
- Baselines are limited to JPEG and JPEG 2000; modern codecs (e.g., HEVC intra/BPG) are not included.
- Variable-rate operation requires separate models per λ; a single adaptive model would be more practical.
- Evaluation is primarily on Kodak and a small set of classic images; larger modern benchmarks and runtime/complexity metrics would strengthen the case.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not necessarily central to the decision)
- Baselines: Include comparisons to contemporary strong baselines (e.g., BPG/HEVC-intra, WebP) with matched color handling to contextualize PSNR/MS-SSIM performance.
- Fairness and metrics: Clarify color processing fairness (e.g., JPEG 4:2:0 vs your codec’s chroma handling). Report per-channel metrics and Y-only metrics consistently for RGB. Consider reporting VMAF or additional perceptual metrics.
- Complexity: Provide model size, FLOPs, and encode/decode times on standard hardware. This helps assess deployability.
- Variable-rate coding: Discuss strategies to obtain a single variable-rate model (e.g., scaling latents, learned importance maps, or conditioning on λ).
- Ablations: Systematically ablate GDN vs ReLU/sigmoid, number of stages, channel counts, and the non-parametric vs parametric entropy models to quantify where gains come from.
- Entropy modeling: Explore/quantify benefits from richer priors (e.g., hyperpriors, spatial context models) and more sophisticated context-adaptive arithmetic coding.
- Generalization: Add results on larger datasets (e.g., Tecnick, CLIC) and higher-resolution images; comment on overfitting risk and robustness across seeds.
- Reproducibility: If feasible, release code/models or more detailed implementation specifics (e.g., training schedules, initialization, data preprocessing).

Questions for the authors
- Training stability and reproducibility: How sensitive are learned transforms and final RD performance to random initialization and training seed? What is the variance across runs?
- Model footprint and speed: What are parameter counts, memory requirements, and encode/decode runtimes for grayscale and RGB models at representative resolutions?
- Side information: Precisely what side information is transmitted and how large is it relative to total bitrate? Are model parameters assumed known a priori, and is λ-indexing the only per-image overhead?
- Color handling and fairness: JPEG uses 4:2:0 subsampling; does your method operate in full-resolution RGB? How do you ensure fair PSNR/MS-SSIM comparisons across color pipelines?
- Entropy model: Did you measure the gap between arithmetic-coded bitrates and the discrete entropy under your learned marginals? How much does CABAC adaptivity reduce model mismatch?
- Relaxation fidelity: During training, how well does the relaxed objective predict final discrete RD for a held-out validation set? Do you tune λ post hoc to match a target rate?
- Dataset and overfitting: With ~6.5k training images at 256×256, did you observe overfitting signs? How does performance change with larger/diverse training data?
- Variable rate: Could you condition the analysis transform or latent scales on λ (or a rate-control parameter) to obtain a single model covering multiple points on the RD curve? What challenges did you encounter?

Overall, the paper presents a clear, technically sound, and practically relevant framework for learned image compression with convincing empirical benefits and a well-justified optimization approach.
