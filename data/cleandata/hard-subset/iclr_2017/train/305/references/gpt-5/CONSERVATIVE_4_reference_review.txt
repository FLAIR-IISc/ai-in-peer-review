Summary
This paper proposes an end-to-end learned image compression system that jointly optimizes analysis and synthesis transforms and a probability model for entropy coding under a rate–distortion objective. The key technical choices are: (i) cascaded convolutional transforms with generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN), (ii) a continuous relaxation of quantization using additive uniform noise to enable SGD, and (iii) training separate models for different λ values along the rate–distortion curve. The method is evaluated with an actual arithmetic entropy coder (CABAC-like) to report true bit rates, and compared against JPEG and JPEG 2000 on Kodak (and some additional images). The paper also clarifies the relationship to VAEs while emphasizing the discrete coding constraints.

Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons: The paper offers a principled, end-to-end formulation of learned transform coding that is methodologically sound and implemented to produce real bitstreams, with consistent qualitative and quantitative improvements over established baselines. However, the empirical evaluation lacks stronger modern baselines and standard aggregate metrics, and ablations are limited.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem addressed: The paper tackles the classical lossy image compression problem, aiming to jointly optimize the transforms and entropy model under rate–distortion, with the practical challenge that quantization is non-differentiable.

2) Motivation and positioning: The approach is well motivated. Using uniform-noise relaxation is a reasonable, established surrogate for quantization; connecting the objective to VAEs clarifies the modeling choices and their implications. The use of GDN is grounded in prior work on Gaussianizing natural image statistics. The work is placed against transform coding (JPEG/J2K) and related learned methods (Gregor et al., Toderici et al.), highlighting differences in objective (RD vs generative), discreteness, and evaluation with real bitrates.

3) Evidence and rigor:
- Methodological soundness: The relaxed objective is carefully derived; the equivalence to a VAE-style objective under MSE is correct; the formulation accounts for actual entropy coding. The independence assumption on latents is explicit, and the nonparametric marginal modeling is reasonable.
- Empirical: The authors verify that the relaxed loss approximates actual RD reasonably. They report RD curves and visualizations, and include both PSNR and MS-SSIM. Using a real arithmetic coder is a strong point.
- Weaknesses: Baselines are limited to JPEG and JPEG 2000; no comparison to modern codecs available at the time (HEVC-intra/BPG, WebP), which weakens claims of state-of-the-art performance. The evaluation primarily relies on Kodak (small), with heterogeneous additional examples; aggregate metrics (e.g., BD-rate across a fixed dataset) are not provided. Ablations (e.g., GDN vs ReLU, number of stages, effect of latent independence) are largely anecdotal. A single model per λ is less practical than a unified model with continuous rate control; this is acknowledged but not addressed experimentally. Runtime/complexity comparisons are not quantified.

On balance, the technical contribution (end-to-end RD optimization with actual bitstreams and strong qualitative gains) is significant, but the empirical section needs strengthening to meet the bar for broad claims.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all are acceptance criteria)
- Baselines and metrics:
  - Include HEVC-intra/BPG comparisons (PSNR and MS-SSIM) on a fixed dataset with BD-rate summaries. If computationally heavy, at least provide a subset and report aggregate BD-rate over Kodak.
  - Add WebP and optimized JPEG 2000 settings as further references.
- Dataset and reporting:
  - Complement Kodak with a larger, standard test set (e.g., Tecnick, or a held-out ImageNet subset) and report aggregate statistics.
  - Provide per-image RD curves in the appendix plus a concise aggregate table (BD-rate vs J2K/BPG).
- Practicality:
  - Discuss the cost of storing multiple models (one per λ). Quantify the overhead of transmitting the λ index and the operational implications. Explore whether a single model with a continuous rate knob (e.g., gain factors) approximates the per-λ optima.
- Ablations:
  - Quantify GDN’s contribution vs ReLU/sigmoid for equal parameter budgets and stages.
  - Analyze residual correlations in latents and the potential gain from simple contexts or hyperpriors (even a small autoregressive or spatial predictor) to bound headroom.
- Entropy coding:
  - Detail the contexts used and compare your coder to a stronger context model to assess how much of the gap to J2K lies in entropy modeling vs transform quality.
- Complexity:
  - Report encoding/decoding throughput and memory vs JPEG/J2K and HEVC-intra, at least approximate GPU/CPU timings.
- Reproducibility:
  - Release code/models and the exact training/evaluation pipeline. It would greatly increase impact.

Questions for the authors
1) How does your method compare to HEVC-intra/BPG in PSNR and MS-SSIM on Kodak and one larger dataset? Please provide BD-rate numbers.
2) What is the aggregate BD-rate vs JPEG 2000 on Kodak (PSNR and MS-SSIM)? The current per-image plots are informative but a standard summary would help.
3) Can a single model with a rate-control mechanism (e.g., learned gain or λ-conditioned layers) match the per-λ models? If not, how many models are practically needed?
4) How sensitive are results to the independence assumption in the latent space? What is the measured residual correlation, and how many bits could be saved with simple spatial/channel contexts?
5) Provide ablations quantifying the impact of GDN vs pointwise nonlinearities at matched parameter counts and stages.
6) What are typical encode/decode speeds and memory footprints on standard hardware?
7) How large is the side information in practice (model index, image size, etc.) relative to total bits at low rates?
8) At very low bitrates, does the uniform-noise relaxation introduce bias relative to true quantization gradients? Have you tried straight-through estimators and compared RD?
