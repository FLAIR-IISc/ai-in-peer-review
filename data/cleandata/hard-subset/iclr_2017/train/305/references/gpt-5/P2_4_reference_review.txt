1) Summary of contributions
- Problem: Learn an image compression system by directly optimizing the whole pipeline (analysis transform, quantization, synthesis transform, and entropy model) for the rate–distortion trade-off.
- Approach: Use a nonlinear transform coder with cascaded convolutions and generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN). Replace non-differentiable quantization with additive uniform noise to obtain a continuous, differentiable surrogate of both distortion and entropy. Learn nonparametric marginal models for the latents and implement an actual entropy coder (CABAC variant) to report real bitrates.
- Claims: (a) End-to-end optimization yields better rate–distortion than JPEG/JPEG2000 on most images (particularly at low rates, and strongly in MS-SSIM), (b) despite MSE training, reconstructions look perceptually more natural (fewer blocking/ringing artifacts), (c) the continuous relaxation closely tracks discrete rate/distortion, enabling practical training.
- Contributions: A principled rate–distortion training objective, clear connection to VAEs while insisting on discrete coding and true bitstreams; a practical, efficient architecture using GDN; thorough qualitative and quantitative evaluation on standard datasets with actual coded bits.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, well-motivated methodological contribution that directly addresses a core challenge (optimizing through quantization) with a principled surrogate and delivers a deployable compressor.
- Empirical evidence is compelling: consistent perceptual gains, improved MS-SSIM across rates, and competitive-to-better PSNR versus JPEG/JPEG2000 with real bitstreams.

3) Supporting arguments
- Significance and novelty: Prior work often stopped at differential entropy surrogates or lacked real entropy coding; this paper closes that gap and compares on actual bits. The additive uniform noise relaxation is well-justified, commonly used in quantization theory, and validated empirically here (bias analysis vs. discrete entropy/distortion). Using GDN as a learned joint nonlinearity is a meaningful architectural advance over purely linear transforms with pointwise activations.
- Correctness and rigor: The objective corresponds to classical rate–distortion with a clear continuous relaxation; the relationship to VAEs is correctly derived and the distinctions (discrete coding, λ trade-off) are well articulated. The entropy model is explicit (marginal, nonparametric), and the authors implement a reasonable entropy coder to substantiate rate claims. The training protocol, architectural details, and hyperparameters are documented; figures show that the surrogate tracks discrete metrics well.
- Empirical evaluation: Kodak is a standard test set; they include additional canonical images and RGB experiments. They report both PSNR and MS-SSIM, show per-image curves (with online supplement), and include qualitative comparisons that align with metrics. While comparisons are limited to JPEG/JPEG2000, the improvements in MS-SSIM and low-rate PSNR are sizable and consistent.
- Clarity: The paper is well written, with a clear problem setup, model description, optimization details, and limitations. The appendix adds sufficient implementation detail to reproduce.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (not necessarily decision-critical)
- Baselines: Including a stronger modern baseline (e.g., HEVC intra/BPG) would better contextualize performance, especially at the time of submission. Even if not fully matched in implementation constraints, a discussion or partial comparison would help.
- Entropy modeling: The independence assumption across latent dimensions and simple raster contexts are likely leaving bits on the table. A brief ablation quantifying the gap to a stronger context model (even offline entropy estimates with spatial context) would inform how much of the remaining headroom is modeling vs. transform capacity.
- Ablations: More systematic analysis of GDN’s contribution versus ReLU/sigmoid-based networks at matched parameter counts and depths would strengthen the architectural claim (the appendix mentions preliminary observations).
- One-model-for-all-rates: Currently, a separate model is trained per λ. Please discuss memory/maintenance trade-offs and whether weight sharing or a conditional model could cover multiple rate points with a single network.
- Runtime and complexity: A small section quantifying encoding/decoding speed, parameter counts, and memory versus JPEG2000 would help assess practicality.
- Dataset generalization: While Kodak is standard, a second held-out dataset (e.g., Tecnick, CLIC val) would increase confidence in generalization.
- Fairness details: JPEG is evaluated with 4:2:0 chroma subsampling; your method compresses RGB without subsampling. Clarify whether this favors JPEG at very low rates and consider reporting Y-channel metrics consistently, or equivalently subsample your RGB to isolate transform-coding effects.
- Presentation: Tighten some reference formats and fix minor typos; clarify the upsampling equation formatting; briefly justify chosen kernel sizes and down/up-sampling factors.

Questions for the authors
- How close are your achieved bitrates to the modeled discrete entropy? Can you quantify the coding overhead (bits/symbol) and how much a stronger context model (e.g., spatial autoregressive modeling of latents) might close the gap?
- Can you provide results against BPG/HEVC-intra at matched rates, at least on Kodak, to contextualize gains relative to contemporary codecs?
- How sensitive is performance to the number of channels and depth? Is there a clear scaling law (bits-per-pixel vs. parameters) and do returns saturate?
- GDN vs. pointwise nonlinearities: At matched parameter budgets and training regimes, how much gain is attributable to GDN specifically? Any visual differences in artifacts?
- Training stability: Did you need curriculum or λ-schedules? How robust is the additive-noise relaxation at very low rates (coarse quantization)? Did you try annealing the noise width?
- Single model for multiple rates: Could you condition the analysis/synthesis transforms on λ (or a side input) to yield a single deployable model across rates without loss?
- RGB handling: Did you explore learned color transforms or chroma subsampling within the model? Any gains vs. compressing RGB naively?
- Generalization: How does the model perform on domains differing from ImageNet photos (e.g., graphics/text, medical images)?

Overall, this paper presents a clear, principled, and practical step forward in learned image compression with convincing empirical benefits and a sound optimization framework.
