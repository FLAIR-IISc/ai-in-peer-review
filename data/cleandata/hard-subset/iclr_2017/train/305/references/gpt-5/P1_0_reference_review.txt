Summary and contributions
The paper tackles lossy image compression by optimizing the full transform–quantize–code pipeline end-to-end for rate–distortion performance. The key ideas are: (1) nonlinear analysis/synthesis transforms built from convolutional layers with generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN), (2) a principled continuous relaxation of quantization using additive uniform noise to enable stochastic gradient optimization of a discrete coding problem, (3) joint learning of nonparametric marginal models in the latent space, and (4) evaluation with an actual entropy coder (CABAC-based) to report true bit rates rather than differential entropy. The method is positioned relative to transform coding and to variational autoencoders; the authors show the exact correspondence under the relaxation with MSE and clarify important differences driven by discrete coding and rate–distortion trade-offs. Empirically, the approach achieves improved rate–distortion (PSNR) versus JPEG/JPEG2000 on most images, substantially better MS-SSIM across the board, and markedly better visual artifacts at low rates. The paper is clearly written, technically careful, and addresses an important, long-standing problem with a modern, principled learning-based approach.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong, well-motivated methodological contribution with a clean and practical relaxation that directly connects to rate–distortion optimization and is implemented end-to-end; (2) Convincing empirical evidence including actual bitstreams, demonstrating improved perceptual quality and competitive PSNR versus widely used standards.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem addressed: The paper aims to learn nonlinear transforms for lossy image compression that optimize the rate–distortion trade-off directly, overcoming the intractability of high-dimensional vector quantization by letting learned nonlinear transforms “shape” the space before simple scalar quantization and entropy coding. This is a clear and important question.

2) Motivation and placement: The work is well positioned between classical transform coding and probabilistic generative modeling. The link to VAEs under the additive-uniform relaxation is insightful and correct, and the authors carefully explain why compression differs in objectives and constraints from generative modeling. The choice of GDN is motivated by prior evidence on Gaussianizing image statistics and by biological plausibility. The paper acknowledges related attempts (e.g., hierarchical generative compression, recurrent binarization) and explains differences.

3) Support for claims and rigor: 
- Methodological soundness: The quantization relaxation via additive uniform noise is standard and appropriate; the authors empirically validate that relaxed rate and distortion track discrete entropy and MSE well across rates. Joint training of transforms and marginal models is clearly specified; optimization details are extensive.
- Empirical rigor: Reporting actual bit rates via an implemented entropy coder is a major plus. Comparisons on Kodak and other canonical images include PSNR and MS-SSIM, and extensive qualitative examples show typical artifacts. The qualitative advantages (fewer ringing/blocking, more natural contour simplification) are consistent with the architecture and with MS-SSIM gains.
- Limitations acknowledged: Independence assumptions in the latent space and a relatively simple entropy coder are discussed; the authors note that stronger context modeling could further improve rates. They also note the separate model per λ choice and discuss potential perceptual metrics.

Areas to improve and questions (to help strengthen the work; not all are decision-critical)
- Baselines and fairness: At the time, JPEG/JPEG2000 are standard references, but including a stronger modern baseline (e.g., intra H.265/BPG) would better characterize the method’s absolute performance. Clarify JPEG/JPEG2000 settings and ensure fair chroma handling; you used JPEG 4:2:0—does your method process RGB at full resolution? For PSNR, you evaluate on Y only; please report RGB PSNR as well or justify the choice.
- Entropy modeling: The independence assumption across latents and simple raster-scan/context model likely leaves bits on the table. Can you quantify the gap between your CABAC implementation and an oracle entropy estimator on your learned relaxed marginals? Any preliminary results with simple spatial-context features or channel-wise conditioning?
- Model capacity and ablations: Provide ablations on the number of stages/channels and on GDN vs ReLU/sigmoid to substantiate efficiency claims (not just notes in the appendix). How sensitive are results to the GDN symmetry constraint and exponent choices?
- Multi-rate usage: You train separate models per λ. Could you enable a single model to operate over multiple rates via a learned scale parameter or conditional prior? Even a preliminary experiment (e.g., learnable “gain” on latents) would be informative.
- Generalization and resolution: Training uses 256×256 crops from ~6.5k images with specific pre-processing. Report performance on higher-resolution test images and discuss boundary handling and tiling if needed. Any signs of overfitting or distribution shift?
- Complexity: Please report encoder/decoder complexity (FLOPs, runtime on CPU/GPU, model size) versus JPEG/JPEG2000 to contextualize deployment, including latency at typical image sizes.
- Perceptual claims: While MS-SSIM results support perceptual quality, consider adding a more recent perceptual metric or a small-scale user study to reinforce qualitative claims, or at least provide more systematic MS-SSIM summaries.

Questions for the authors
- How large is the bit gap between differential entropy of the relaxed latents, the discrete entropy of quantized latents, and the realized bit rate with your CABAC? A per-rate breakdown would be helpful.
- What is the impact of the independence assumption? If you shuffle latents to break spatial correlations before entropy coding, does the bit rate change significantly, indicating remaining dependencies?
- How does the method perform on high-resolution images without cropping, and what padding/tiling strategy do you use?
- Can you share exact training time, number of updates, and convergence behavior across λ? Any evidence of mode collapse in the marginal estimators?
- Do GDN/IGDN still outperform ReLUs when you allow deeper/wider networks matched for parameter count and compute?
- Would training with a perceptual metric (e.g., MS-SSIM) yield consistent rate–distortion improvements without harming measured PSNR? Any early results on color images with a simple perceptual loss?

Overall, this is a strong, timely, and well-executed paper that demonstrates a principled path to learned nonlinear transform coding with real bitstreams and meaningful perceptual advantages. I recommend acceptance.
