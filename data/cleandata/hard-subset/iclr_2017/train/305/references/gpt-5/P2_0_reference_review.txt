1. Summary of contributions
The paper tackles end-to-end lossy image compression by directly optimizing a nonlinear analysis/synthesis transform, the quantizer, and an entropy model for rate–distortion (R+λD). The key ideas are: (a) using cascaded convolutional transforms with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and its inverse (IGDN) to reduce higher-order dependencies; (b) replacing non-differentiable quantization with additive uniform noise to obtain a continuous relaxation whose differential entropy approximates discrete entropy and whose MSE approximates quantization distortion; (c) learning nonparametric, per-latent marginal models for the relaxed code distribution; and (d) evaluating with an actual arithmetic entropy coder (CABAC), reporting true bit rates. Models are trained per λ on ImageNet crops and tested on Kodak and other images. Empirically, the method consistently improves perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) over JPEG and JPEG 2000 at all tested bit rates, and often improves PSNR, especially at low bit rates; visual artifacts are markedly more natural (reduced blocking/ringing). The paper also clarifies the relationship to VAEs and why compression optimization differs (e.g., fixed discretization, λ-sweep along the R–D curve).

2. Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- A principled and well-motivated formulation of end-to-end rate–distortion optimization for image compression, with a sound, differentiable relaxation and an implemented entropy coder yielding real bit rates.
- Strong empirical evidence of perceptual gains and competitive or better PSNR versus widely used baselines (JPEG, JPEG 2000), with qualitative improvements that align with the modeling choices (GDN/IGDN).
This work is likely to be influential and catalyze further research in learned compression.

3. Supporting arguments
On the problem and motivation:
- The paper addresses a central and longstanding problem (transform coding with scalar quantization and entropy coding) and proposes a modern, jointly optimized alternative. The motivation to move beyond linear transforms and pointwise nonlinearities is clearly justified by known higher-order dependencies in natural images.
- The literature placement is strong: the paper accurately contrasts transform coding with recent generative modeling and recurrent learned compression, explaining why a direct rate–distortion objective with discrete codes and an entropy coder is necessary for fair comparison.

On methodological soundness:
- The additive uniform noise relaxation is appropriate: it matches quantization in moments; the authors empirically validate that relaxed MSE is nearly unbiased and that differential entropy positively biases discrete entropy mainly at coarse quantization, with bias diminishing at finer rates. They correctly note that λ rescales the tradeoff, so modest bias does not invalidate the optimization.
- Learning nonparametric per-latent marginals is a good fit given the smoothing induced by uniform noise; the piecewise-linear densities and joint optimization are clearly described. The independence assumption is standard for a first step and is acknowledged as a limitation.
- GDN/IGDN is well motivated by prior work on Gaussianizing image statistics; the architecture and stabilization tricks (Adam, filter normalization, parameter reparameterization) are carefully described.

On empirical support and rigor:
- The authors evaluate with an implemented arithmetic coder and report true rates including side information, avoiding pitfalls of comparing entropy to bit rates. This increases credibility.
- Visual results are compelling and consistent with the claim that nonlinear transforms avoid basis-artifacts typical for linear codecs. MS-SSIM improvements are broad; PSNR gains are present on most images, especially at low rates, and the paper is candid about exceptions.
- The relationship to VAEs is carefully derived: with MSE and independent marginals, the objective matches a VAE ELBO with a specific decoder variance (function of λ). The discussion clarifies why learned compression and standard generative modeling differ in goal and evaluation.

Limitations (do not outweigh the strengths):
- Models are trained separately per λ; there is no single variable-rate model. This reduces practical flexibility and complicates deployment.
- The entropy model assumes independent latents, and the entropy coding uses a simple raster-scan with limited context adaptation. More advanced context/hyperprior models could further improve rate.
- Baselines are limited to JPEG/JPEG 2000. Newer intra codecs (e.g., HEVC/BPG at the time) would strengthen the case. Runtime/complexity numbers are not reported.
- Ablations on the role of GDN vs. simpler nonlinearities are minimal in the main text.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Add ablations quantifying: (i) GDN/IGDN vs ReLU or sigmoid networks under parameter budget; (ii) effect of the nonparametric marginals vs fixed parametric families; (iii) impact of the independence assumption (e.g., simple autoregressive/context models).
- Provide encoding/decoding runtime and memory usage on CPU/GPU, and an estimate vs JPEG/J2K to assess practicality.
- Include an additional baseline such as BPG (HEVC-intra) to contextualize performance, or justify its omission.
- Report aggregate R–D summaries with standard BD-rate metrics to complement per-image curves, acknowledging the caveats discussed.
- Discuss variable-rate extensions (shared model with a scale parameter or learned quantization step) and whether joint training across λ degrades peak R–D optimality.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the independence assumption in the latent space? Have you tried simple contexts (e.g., conditioning on neighboring latents or a hyperprior) and measured bit-rate gains?
- Could you train a single model that operates across multiple rates (e.g., by scaling latents or conditioning on λ) without losing much R–D optimality?
- How robust is the uniform-noise relaxation when quantization is coarse? Do you observe any optimization pathologies at very low rates, and can you bound the entropy bias more formally?
- What are the encoding/decoding times and model sizes for different λ values? Are there architectural choices that trade accuracy for speed effectively?
- How do the results generalize to higher-resolution images and diverse datasets beyond Kodak? Any observed distribution shift effects?
- For color images, do you compress in RGB space or apply a color transform? Does learning the color space jointly improve R–D?
- Can you elaborate on the choice of spline resolution for the marginal models and how often you re-fit their dynamic range? How sensitive are rates to this discretization?
- Do you foresee integrating a better entropy model (e.g., context-adaptive or hierarchical) within your end-to-end training without losing differentiability?
