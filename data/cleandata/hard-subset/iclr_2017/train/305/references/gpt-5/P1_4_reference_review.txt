1) Summary of contributions
The paper tackles end-to-end optimization of lossy image compression. It proposes a nonlinear transform coding framework in which both the analysis and synthesis transforms are learned jointly with a rate term via stochastic gradient descent. The key technical ingredient is a continuous relaxation of quantization: replacing rounding with additive uniform noise, which enables backpropagation while approximating both discrete entropy and distortion. The transforms are cascades of convolutions and generalized divisive normalization (GDN) with an approximate inverse (IGDN), and the latent distribution is modeled with independent nonparametric marginals (piecewise-linear pdfs). Models are trained separately for different Lagrange multipliers λ to trace the rate–distortion (R–D) curve. Unlike many prior learning-based works, the authors implement an entropy coder (CABAC-style) and report actual bitrates, comparing against JPEG and JPEG2000 on the Kodak dataset (and additional images). The method shows improved MS-SSIM across the board and often better PSNR, particularly at low bitrates, with artifacts that are perceptually more natural than blockiness/ringing.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- A principled, well-motivated end-to-end R–D optimization with a practical differentiable surrogate for quantization, coupled with an actual entropy coder, makes the contribution both scientifically sound and practically relevant.
- Strong empirical evidence: consistent perceptual gains (MS-SSIM), frequent PSNR gains, and qualitative artifacts that are substantially more natural than standard codecs.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: Learn image compression transforms that optimally trade off rate and distortion, avoiding hand-designed linear transforms and decoupled component tuning. The paper aims to effectively implement vector quantization via learned nonlinear warping and uniform scalar quantization in the latent space.
- Motivation and placement in literature: The work is well positioned at the interface of transform coding and probabilistic modeling. It appropriately contrasts transform coding’s discrete optimization with generative modeling, clarifies links to VAEs (by specifying a Gaussian likelihood and uniform “posterior noise”), and highlights why directly comparing differential entropy or generative losses to bitrates can be misleading. It discusses related neural compression efforts (Toderici et al.; Gregor et al.) and explains the distinct objectives and evaluation pitfalls. The choice of GDN/IGDN is motivated by prior evidence on Gaussianization and biological plausibility.
- Do the results support the claims? Yes, largely. The authors:
  - Validate the continuous relaxation empirically by comparing relaxed differential entropy and distortion to the actual entropy of quantized codes and MSE (showing small bias/variance, especially at finer quantization).
  - Provide an implemented entropy coder and report real bitrates including side information.
  - Demonstrate consistent improvements in MS-SSIM and frequently in PSNR versus JPEG/JPEG2000, with compelling qualitative results that match the perceptual metric.
  - Give training details and architecture choices, including parameterizations that stabilize learning.
Limitations that temper claims but do not undermine the main contribution:
  - The entropy model assumes independent latents; context modeling is limited compared to modern codecs or later neural hyperpriors.
  - Evaluation focuses on Kodak (24 images) plus additional examples; no user study; no comparison to stronger baselines like BPG/HEVC-intra.
  - Separate models per λ, rather than a single variable-rate model.
  - Limited ablations on GDN vs. standard nonlinearities, and limited runtime/complexity reporting.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Baselines: Including BPG (HEVC intra) or WebP would strengthen the empirical case. Even a subset comparison would help calibrate performance against state-of-the-art hand-engineered codecs.
- Complexity: Report encoding/decoding runtime, memory footprint, and model size; these are important for practical adoption and fair codec comparisons.
- Ablations: Provide quantitative ablations on (i) GDN/IGDN vs. ReLU or sigmoid networks matched for parameter count/depth, (ii) effect of nonparametric vs. parametric latents, and (iii) the contribution of the CABAC adaptation (vs. fixed entropy model).
- Variable-rate coding: Discuss whether a single model could handle multiple rates (e.g., via learned scaling or conditional transforms), and the trade-offs versus per-λ training.
- Entropy modeling: Show spatial statistics of the residual mismatch and quantify how much context modeling could close the gap to the relaxed entropy.
- Dataset: Add diversity in test sets (e.g., Tecnick, CLIC) and report cross-dataset generalization.
- Perception: If possible, include a small-scale user study to corroborate MS-SSIM trends.

5) Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the independence assumption in the latent space? Do simple spatial or autoregressive context models significantly reduce the bitrate for fixed distortion?
- What are the encoding/decoding times and FLOPs for the grayscale and RGB models at different rates, and how do they compare to JPEG2000?
- How large are the model parameters and the transmitted side information? Is the λ index the only side info beyond image size/color flag?
- How robust is the additive-uniform-noise relaxation at very low rates? Do you observe systematic biases in the relaxed rate or distortion that affect training stability or final performance?
- GDN/IGDN stability/invertibility: Are there constraints ensuring IGDN does not explode for out-of-distribution latents? Did you encounter numerical issues at test time?
- Could you train a single conditional model to cover a range of λ via a learned scale or modulation? If so, how much R–D optimality is lost relative to per-λ models?
- How does performance scale with network capacity and depth? Any evidence of diminishing returns beyond the 3-stage design?
- Would training with a perceptual loss (e.g., MS-SSIM) in this end-to-end framework further improve subjective quality without sacrificing rate?

Overall, this paper presents a clear, principled, and influential approach to neural image compression, with careful attention to practical bitrate reporting and a compelling continuous relaxation that makes end-to-end R–D optimization feasible. These merits justify acceptance.
