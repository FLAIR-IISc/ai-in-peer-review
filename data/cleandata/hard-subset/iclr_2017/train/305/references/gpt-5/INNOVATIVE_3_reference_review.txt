Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles lossy image compression from a rate–distortion perspective and proposes a fully end-to-end trainable nonlinear transform coding system. The analysis and synthesis transforms are cascades of convolutions and generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN), optimized jointly with a learned entropy model.
- To enable gradient-based optimization through quantization, the authors introduce a principled continuous relaxation by replacing rounding with additive uniform noise; they then train with a Lagrangian R + λD objective. They also learn non-parametric per-latent marginal densities and implement an actual entropy coder (CABAC-like), reporting real bitrates rather than differential entropies.
- The paper clarifies connections to VAEs, and explains why the compression setting differs, particularly in the role of λ and discrete coding.
- Empirically, on Kodak and other standard images (grayscale and RGB), the method yields consistently better perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) and usually better PSNR than JPEG and JPEG2000, with markedly more natural artifacts at low rates. The authors also validate that the continuous relaxation provides a good approximation to actual rate/distortion.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) Novelty and significance: A clean, bold, end-to-end optimization framework for image compression that integrates learned analysis/synthesis transforms, a differentiable quantization relaxation, non-parametric entropy models, and actual entropy coding. This meaningfully advances how we think about compression in the deep learning era.
2) Empirical and qualitative impact: Clear qualitative gains and competitive (often superior) quantitative results versus strong classical baselines, with real bitrates. Even with an MSE objective, perceptual quality improves substantially—an important and surprising outcome.

Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: Jointly optimizing transforms, quantization, and coding for rate–distortion is a long-standing challenge. The authors’ approach directly targets this with an effective continuous relaxation and a learned, biologically inspired nonlinearity (GDN) that reduces higher-order dependencies—central to compressibility.
- Motivation and placement: The paper carefully situates itself relative to transform coding, to quantization theory, and to recent neural generative and compression models (Gregor et al., Toderici et al.). The contrast with VAEs is insightful and technically sound, especially about λ, discrete codes, and evaluation with real bitrates.
- Correctness and rigor: The loss derivation is standard Lagrangian R–D, the uniform noise relaxation is classical and well justified, and the VAE-style equivalence is explained precisely under MSE. The validation of the relaxation’s bias/variance is valuable. Implementing a CABAC-style coder and reporting true bitrates avoids a common pitfall. Claims about qualitative artifacts are supported with consistent visual evidence across many images.
- Scope and generality: While the architecture is specific (3-stage conv + GDN/IGDN), the framework is general and could be extended to other transforms, entropy models, and perceptual metrics. The approach is clearly a stepping stone toward learned compression systems with stronger priors and context models.

Caveats (not blocking)
- Baselines: Missing BPG (HEVC intra) or other modern codecs; only JPEG/JPEG2000 are compared. Given the ambition, a modern baseline would strengthen claims.
- Entropy model: Independent marginals with modest context (raster-scan adaptation) limit achievable rates; modern autoregressive/hyperprior approaches could improve performance.
- One model per λ: Training separate models per rate is less practical than a single model controllable at test time.
- Evaluation breadth: Kodak is small; broader datasets and resolutions would increase confidence. No user study; perceptual claim rests on MS-SSIM and visuals.
- Efficiency: Training cost and encode/decode time are not quantified.

Constructive feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Add a strong modern baseline (e.g., BPG) and, if feasible, a comparison to contemporary learned methods (Toderici et al.) in bitrates, not entropy.
- Report computational efficiency: train time, encoder/decoder throughput, memory; give parameter counts.
- Provide ablations: effect of GDN vs. ReLU/sigmoid (partly discussed), number of stages, and entropy model capacity (spline sampling density).
- Move toward a single model supporting multiple rates (e.g., via learned scale parameters, learned quantization step, or conditioning on λ).
- Strengthen the entropy model (spatial context/autoregressive; hyperpriors) or at least quantify the gap between differential entropy, modeled discrete entropy, and actual bits under stronger context models.
- Extend evaluation to higher-res images and a larger test set; consider a small-scale user study to corroborate perceptual gains.
- Clean up references (several entries have formatting issues).

Questions for the authors
1) Can you include BPG (HEVC intra) results on Kodak to contextualize the gains? If not, can you provide an analysis estimating the gap to BPG given your current entropy model?
2) How fast is encoding/decoding on a GPU/CPU for typical images, and how does it compare to JPEG/JPEG2000?
3) How sensitive is performance to the independence assumption in the latent space? Have you tried simple context models (e.g., masked autoregressive coding within channels) and measured bitrate improvements?
4) Could your system be modified to support a single model across rates (e.g., by feeding λ, by scaling latents, or via learned quant step), and how does that affect R–D optimality?
5) How crucial is GDN/IGDN at fixed model capacity? Can you provide quantitative ablations isolating their contribution to R–D and perceptual artifacts?
6) The spline-based marginal models use 10 samples per unit interval. Did you observe underfitting in heavy tails? How do you set/adjust the dynamic range during training?
7) You show the noise-relaxation’s bias/variance. Can you bound its impact on the chosen λ and on final R–D performance, or provide a calibration that maps relaxed λ to effective discrete λ?
8) Will code/models be released to promote reproducibility?
