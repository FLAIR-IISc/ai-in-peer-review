1) Summary of contributions
The paper tackles lossy image compression by optimizing the full pipeline end-to-end for rate–distortion. The key ideas are: (i) a learned nonlinear analysis/synthesis transform built from convolutions and generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN), (ii) uniform scalar quantization in the latent space with a continuous relaxation during training via additive uniform noise to enable SGD, (iii) nonparametric marginal models for latent distributions and a simple CABAC-based entropy coder, and (iv) direct evaluation in bits (not just (differential) entropy). The method is trained for different λ values to traverse the rate–distortion curve and shows qualitative improvements and strong quantitative performance versus JPEG and JPEG 2000, including better MS-SSIM across the board and competitive or superior PSNR on most images. The paper also clarifies the connection to VAEs and why the compression objective differs in important ways.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- A principled, well-motivated, and carefully executed end-to-end framework that directly targets rate–distortion with a workable relaxation and demonstrates real bitrates with an entropy coder.
- Strong empirical evidence: consistent perceptual quality improvements and competitive PSNR across a standard test set, with sensible analyses of the relaxation’s fidelity.

3) Supporting arguments
What problem is addressed?
- Jointly optimizing transforms, quantization, and entropy models for lossy image compression to achieve favorable rate–distortion trade-offs, moving beyond hand-designed linear transforms and separate component tuning.

Is the approach well-motivated and well-placed in the literature?
- Yes. The motivation is clear: linear transforms plus independent scalar quantization have known shortcomings (blocking/ringing; poor handling of higher-order dependencies). GDN is well-motivated by prior work on Gaussianizing natural image statistics and biological inspiration. The use of additive uniform noise as a quantization surrogate is grounded in quantization theory and has precedent, but here it’s embedded in a full compression optimization. The relationship to VAEs is carefully articulated, highlighting where the objectives coincide and where compression’s discrete nature, λ-sweeps, and evaluation diverge from generative modeling. Comparisons to contemporary learned compression/generative works (Gregor et al.; Toderici et al.) are accurate and fair.

Do the results support the claims and are they rigorous?
- The paper reports actual bitrates with a working entropy coder and includes side information in the stream. It validates the relaxation by comparing relaxed rates/distortions to their discrete counterparts. Results on the Kodak dataset and additional images show consistent perceptual wins (MS-SSIM and qualitative artifacts) and strong PSNR at many rates. The architecture, training details, and entropy coding are documented well in the appendix, aiding reproducibility. While comparisons are limited to JPEG and JPEG 2000, these baselines are still widely used; reporting MS-SSIM and PSNR is standard. Overall, the evidence supports the main claims.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not necessarily part of my decision)
- Baselines: Consider adding BPG (HEVC-intra) or a modern JPEG 2000 configuration with stronger context modeling if possible; BPG was already well-known and provides a strong traditional baseline. This would further solidify the empirical case.
- Fairness in color handling: You use JPEG with 4:2:0 chroma subsampling. Please comment on 4:4:4 comparisons, and whether your method could be run with chroma subsampling to match settings, or provide separate results to isolate color handling effects.
- Single model vs per-λ: Training a separate model per λ is practical for analysis but inconvenient in deployment. Discuss or attempt a conditional model that modulates rate (e.g., via a gain parameter or side input), and/or report any preliminary results. This is hinted at in related work; quantifying the gap would be valuable.
- Entropy model: The independent-marginal assumption leaves spatial and cross-channel dependencies on the table. Stronger models (context models, hyperpriors, or autoregressive components) would likely improve rates. Even a simple spatial context for the CABAC (beyond raster scan) could help; quantifying the gap would be informative.
- Ablations: You note preliminary findings comparing GDN/IGDN to ReLU-type nonlinearities. Adding a small ablation (e.g., replace GDN with per-channel affine + pointwise nonlinearity at comparable parameter counts) would better isolate the contribution of GDN to both rate–distortion and perceptual artifacts.
- Complexity and latency: Include rough encoding/decoding speed, parameter counts, and memory estimates versus JPEG/JPEG 2000. This will help assess practicality.
- Dataset and generalization: Kodak is small; adding a second dataset (e.g., Tecnick or a held-out ImageNet subset at higher resolutions) would strengthen generalization claims.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the independence assumption in the latent space? Did you try simple spatial/context conditioning or a learned hyperprior? Any empirical estimate of the residual dependency (e.g., bits saved by adaptive coding)?
- How large is the gap between the relaxed differential entropy and realized bitrate across the rate range? Can you quantify the bias/variance across images, not only aggregate plots?
- Can you provide runtime (encode/decode) on standard hardware and parameter counts for each λ model? How does performance scale to megapixel images?
- How were JPEG/JPEG 2000 parameters tuned? For JPEG 2000, did you also try rate-targeted encodes when aggregating R–D across images? For JPEG, did you evaluate 4:4:4 to separate chroma subsampling effects?
- Could your framework support a single model with a learned rate control (e.g., conditioning on λ or a scale map) without materially sacrificing optimality? Any preliminary results?
- Did you test alternative quantization surrogates (e.g., straight-through estimator) and, if so, how did they compare in stability and final R–D?
- For RGB, do the learned transforms mix color channels significantly, or do they approximate a luma/chroma decomposition? Any insight from visualizing filters?

Overall, this paper is clear, principled, and impactful. With stronger baselines and minor additional analysis, it will be even more compelling.
