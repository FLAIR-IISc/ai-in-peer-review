1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes an end-to-end optimized lossy image compression framework based on nonlinear analysis/synthesis transforms trained to minimize rate–distortion. Key elements:
- A cascaded convolutional architecture with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and an approximate inverse (IGDN) serves as the analysis/synthesis transforms.
- Discrete quantization is handled during training via a continuous relaxation that replaces rounding with additive uniform noise; the rate term is modeled using non-parametric per-coefficient marginals. The authors carefully connect this to, and distinguish it from, VAEs.
- For evaluation, the method uses actual rounding quantization and implements a practical entropy coder (CABAC), reporting true bit rates, and compares against JPEG and JPEG 2000 on Kodak (and other standard images), with models trained on ImageNet.
- Results show strong perceptual quality, substantial gains in MS-SSIM across bit rates, and PSNR gains for most images, particularly at lower rates. The method yields artifacts that are qualitatively different and visually preferable to those of linear transform coders.

Overall, the paper makes a principled and well-executed case that end-to-end optimization with nonlinear transforms yields competitive or better compression than established standards, with a sound link to rate–distortion theory and practical coding.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel, well-motivated, and rigorous end-to-end formulation that directly targets rate–distortion with a practical coder and real bitrates, bridging classic transform coding and modern deep learning.
- Strong empirical evidence of perceptual improvements (MS-SSIM, qualitative results) and competitive PSNR, with careful validation of the quantization/noise relaxation.

3) Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? The paper aims to learn an analysis/synthesis transform pair and entropy model that minimize rate–distortion for images, addressing the longstanding challenge of jointly optimizing transform, quantizer, and coder.

Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The formulation closely follows rate–distortion principles, invokes quantization theory (uniform-noise relaxation), leverages GDN motivated by visual neuroscience and prior density modeling work, and situates the approach relative to VAEs, generative models, and transform coding standards. The discussion of differences with VAEs (discrete vs differential entropy, role of λ, and evaluation with real bitrates) is clear and informative.

Do the results support the claims? Largely yes.
- The continuous relaxation is empirically validated: distortion estimates are nearly unbiased and entropy estimates converge as quantization gets finer.
- The use of CABAC with all side information included gives credible bitrates and avoids pitfalls of reporting only entropy.
- On Kodak and other images, the method consistently outperforms JPEG/JPEG2000 in MS-SSIM and qualitative appearance; PSNR improvements are frequent (especially at low rates), though not universal.
- Architecture/training details are substantial, aiding reproducibility.

Caveats:
- Baselines omit stronger modern codecs (e.g., BPG/HEVC intra) which were available at the time and are stronger than JPEG2000, so the strength of the claim “improves over existing methods” should be scoped accordingly.
- The entropy model assumes independent marginals with limited spatial/context modeling; the coder uses a simple raster scan and limited context adaptation. There is likely headroom from richer context/hyperprior models.
- A separate model is trained per λ, rather than a single variable-rate model.
- Complexity/runtime and model size are not quantified; practical deployability deserves more discussion.
- Ablations on the role of GDN vs pointwise nonlinearities are limited (preliminary only).

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Baselines: Include BPG (HEVC intra) comparisons with actual bitrates. Even if your entropy model is limited, this contextualizes gains.
- Complexity: Report model size, MACs, and encode/decode times vs JPEG2000. Memory footprint and latency matter for adoption.
- Ablations: Provide quantitative ablations on (i) GDN/IGDN vs ReLU/BN or other activations at equal parameter budgets; (ii) number of stages; (iii) effect of down/upsampling factors; (iv) learned per-channel quantization step sizes vs fixed unit-step plus scaling normalization.
- Entropy modeling: Show the gap between (a) your non-parametric marginal differential entropy, (b) discrete entropy with independent marginals, and (c) achieved CABAC bitrate. This will clarify where bits are left on the table and motivate more expressive priors or context models.
- Variable-rate compression: Consider a single model with a rate knob (e.g., conditioning on λ or an importance map) to avoid per-λ training and to enable spatially adaptive bit allocation.
- Perceptual metrics: Since the method already shows perceptual gains despite MSE training, adding results trained for MS-SSIM (or a VIF/LPIPS-like proxy) would be valuable, even as a small-scale study.

Questions for the authors
- λ and rate control: What exact λ values were used, and how are target rates achieved in practice? How sensitive are rate/distortion outcomes to λ? Could you expose a continuous rate control at test time (e.g., via scaling or temperature)?
- Entropy coding details: How many contexts are used in CABAC? How much gain comes from adaptation compared to a fixed code? Can you quantify the gap between your achieved bitrate and the true discrete entropy under your (independent) model?
- Generalization/robustness: How sensitive are results to training set size and composition? Did you observe overfitting when increasing model capacity? Any failure cases?
- Architecture choices: The first stage is overcomplete; can you quantify how overcompleteness influences rate–distortion? Did you try learned per-channel quantization step sizes or dead-zone quantization?
- Runtime/complexity: What are typical encoding/decoding times on CPU/GPU for Kodak images, and how do they compare to JPEG2000? What is the parameter count?
- Color handling: For RGB, you train directly in RGB; did you try perceptually motivated color transforms (YCbCr, opponent) before the analysis transform, and how did that affect results?

This is a timely, principled, and practically grounded paper that advances learned image compression and is likely to influence subsequent work.
