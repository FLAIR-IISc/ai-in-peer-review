1) Summary and contributions
The paper tackles end-to-end optimization of lossy image compression by jointly learning analysis and synthesis transforms, the quantization proxy, and an entropy model to optimize the Lagrangian rate–distortion objective. The key ideas are:
- A cascaded convolutional architecture with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and its learned inverse (IGDN), trained per Lagrange multiplier λ, effectively implements a nonlinear transform plus scalar quantization that behaves like a parametric vector quantizer.
- A principled continuous relaxation of quantization using additive unit-width uniform noise enables SGD; the relaxed differential entropy of the noisy latents is used as a surrogate for the discrete entropy of quantized codes. A nonparametric marginal model (piecewise linear densities) is learned for each latent channel.
- The approach is connected to variational autoencoders via a specific generative model and approximate posterior, while emphasizing differences relevant for actual compression (discrete coding, operating at finite λ).
- The authors implement an entropy coder (CABAC-like) and report actual bitrates rather than entropy estimates, comparing against JPEG and JPEG 2000 on Kodak and additional images. Despite being trained on MSE, the method yields better MS-SSIM and typically better PSNR, with qualitatively more natural artifacts (edge/contour preservation, reduced ringing/blocking).

Overall, the paper provides a clean, well-motivated, and practical demonstration that end-to-end learned nonlinear transforms can outperform classical linear transform coding and produce superior perceptual behavior.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Methodological soundness and clear technical contribution: a practical end-to-end RD optimization with quantization relaxation, learned nonlinear transforms (GDN/IGDN), and actual entropy coding.
- Strong empirical evidence of improved perceptual quality and competitive to superior RD performance versus widely used standards (JPEG/JPEG2000), with careful discussion of the VAE connection and evaluation pitfalls.

3) Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The work is well positioned at the intersection of source coding and deep generative modeling. The discussion of the additive-uniform relaxation and its role in RD optimization is careful and justified, and the VAE connection is accurate (including the role of λ and discrete vs differential entropy).
- Model choices: GDN/IGDN are a principled, biologically inspired joint nonlinearity shown previously to Gaussianize image statistics; here, their use yields efficient transforms with relatively few stages. The learned per-latent nonparametric densities reduce model mismatch when estimating rates.
- Evaluation: Reporting actual bit rates with a concrete entropy coder is a notable strength over many contemporaneous works relying solely on entropy estimates. The analysis of the bias/variance of the relaxed objective vs true RD is valuable. Qualitative results and MS-SSIM consistently support the claim of perceptual superiority; PSNR is competitive, especially at low rates.
- Reproducibility and clarity: The architecture, training techniques, and entropy coding strategy are described with sufficient detail to reimplement. The appendix provides practical optimization tricks and normalization constraints for stability.

4) Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Baselines: Consider adding stronger modern baselines (e.g., BPG/HEVC-intra) and/or WebP; JPEG2000 is a fair classical baseline, but state-of-the-art intra coding would strengthen claims.
- Variable-rate coding: Training separate models per λ limits practicality. A single model supporting continuum rate control (e.g., learned scale parameters, conditional priors, or λ-conditioning) would be impactful; at minimum, discuss how λ is chosen to meet a target rate.
- Entropy model: The independence assumption across latents is a primary limitation. Spatial/context or hierarchical priors (hyperpriors) could reduce rate; discuss expected gains and whether your CABAC adaptation significantly narrows the gap.
- Dataset and metrics: Kodak is small. Include results on larger, diverse sets (e.g., Tecnick, CLIC) and report average BD-rate gains to summarize across images/rates. Runtime and complexity comparisons (CPU/GPU encode/decode time, parameter counts) would aid practical assessment.
- Ablations: Provide quantitative ablations on GDN vs ReLU/sigmoid, number of stages, and density model capacity to substantiate claims on GDN efficiency and architectural choices.
- Color handling: Clarify whether optimization occurs in RGB or a luminance-chrominance space, and how MS-SSIM is computed (luma only). Consider reporting chroma distortion or perceptual metrics for color images.
- Bitstream details: Specify side-information overhead in bits and how per-channel marginal models are conveyed (or indexed by λ), and quantify its impact on small images.

Questions for the authors
- Rate control: How do you map a target bitrate to a choice of λ at test time? Can you hit a target rate reliably without iterative search?
- Entropy coding: Are the per-channel marginal PMFs entirely fixed by λ (no per-image transmission)? How much adaptation occurs in the CABAC engine, and what is the measured gap to coding with the learned continuous densities without adaptation?
- Dependence modeling: Did you try simple context models (e.g., causal spatial prediction of latents) or hyperpriors? What RD gains did you observe relative to independent marginals?
- GDN efficiency: Can you quantify the parameter/compute savings of GDN/IGDN vs ReLU-based networks for matched RD performance?
- Generalization: How does performance vary across image content outside Kodak? Any failure cases where artifacts become objectionable?
- Complexity: What are typical encode/decode times for 512x512 images on CPU/GPU? Any memory constraints for embedded deployment?
- Perceptual training: Since MS-SSIM improves despite MSE training, what happens if you train directly on MS-SSIM or a learned perceptual metric?

This is a strong and influential paper that convincingly demonstrates the value of end-to-end optimization for image compression and sets a foundation for subsequent advances in learned compression.
