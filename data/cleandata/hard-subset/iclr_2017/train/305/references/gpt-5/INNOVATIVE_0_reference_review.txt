Summary and contributions
The paper proposes an end-to-end learned image compression system that jointly optimizes the analysis transform, synthesis transform, and entropy model for rate–distortion performance. The core ideas are: (i) a nonlinear transform coding architecture built from cascaded convolutions and learned generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN); (ii) a continuous relaxation of the discrete quantization/entropy terms by replacing rounding with additive uniform noise and learning nonparametric marginal densities, enabling SGD on a rate–distortion objective; (iii) an implemented entropy coder (CABAC-like) with actual bitstream evaluation; and (iv) empirical results that, despite training with MSE, achieve superior visual quality and MS-SSIM relative to JPEG/JPEG2000, with competitive or better PSNR at many rates. The paper also clarifies connections and differences to variational autoencoders and evaluates the fidelity of the continuous relaxation to the discrete objective.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: The paper introduces a clear, novel, and bold direction—true end-to-end rate–distortion optimization for image compression with a practical bitstream—backed by compelling qualitative and quantitative evidence. The continuous relaxation plus learned marginals, combined with GDN-based nonlinear transforms, is original and impactful, and the authors go beyond entropy estimates to actual coding.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and significance: Moving from hand-designed linear transforms to learned nonlinear transforms optimized end-to-end for rate–distortion is a substantial conceptual step. The additive-uniform-noise relaxation and nonparametric marginal modeling make the objective differentiable while staying faithful to the discrete coding regime. Using GDN/IGDN is a distinctive design that seems particularly effective for compression.
- Practical completeness: Many prior learning-based compression papers stop at entropy or differential entropy estimates; this work implements an entropy coder and reports true bitrates, demonstrating feasibility as a complete codec.
- Methodological clarity: The rate–distortion objective and its relaxation are well motivated, and the VAE connection is carefully delineated (discrete vs continuous, role of λ, and evaluation). The paper also measures the bias/variance of the relaxed rate and distortion proxies against the discrete quantities.
- Empirical evidence: On Kodak and additional images, the method shows consistent qualitative advantages (reduction of ringing/blocking, more natural edge/contour handling) and strong MS-SSIM gains across rates, with PSNR competitive or better for most images, especially at lower bitrates.
- Scope for impact: The approach is general and suggests a rich design space (transforms, entropy models, perceptual metrics). It is likely to seed follow-on work across codecs and learned latent models.

Caveats and limitations
- Baselines: Comparisons are limited to JPEG and JPEG2000. Stronger modern intra coding baselines (e.g., HEVC-intra/BPG, WebP) would better situate performance.
- Per-rate training: A separate model is trained for each λ. While acceptable for a proof-of-concept, a single model operating across rates would be more practical; the paper acknowledges this.
- Entropy model capacity: The assumption of independent latents with a simple raster-scan adaptive code underuses spatial/context dependencies that modern codecs exploit. This likely leaves performance on the table.
- Ablations: Limited analysis of architectural choices and the specific contribution of GDN vs other nonlinearities, depth/width, and the effect of the relaxation vs other estimators.
- Complexity and deployment: No thorough runtime/complexity analysis or memory footprint estimates; these matter for codec adoption.

These limitations are not fatal given the novelty and the completeness of the demonstrated system.

Suggestions for improvement (not required for acceptance)
- Add stronger baselines (HEVC-intra/BPG, WebP) and report BD-rate (PSNR and MS-SSIM) on standard sets.
- Provide ablations: GDN vs ReLU/sigmoid, depth/width, number of stages, and the impact of independent vs context/hyperprior entropy models.
- Explore a single multi-rate model (e.g., with learned scale parameters or condition on λ) and quantify the gap to per-λ models.
- Expand evaluation to larger color datasets, possibly 10-bit content, and report complexity (encode/decode time, FLOPs).
- Clarify fairness in MS-SSIM evaluation (e.g., luma-only choices) and include color fidelity metrics.
- Release code/models to support reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- How close are your arithmetic-coded bitrates to the measured entropy of q? Please report overhead (bits/pixel) and the effect of adaptive updates.
- How sensitive is performance to the architecture (number of stages, filter sizes) and to the choice of GDN vs pointwise nonlinearities? Can you quantify the claimed efficiency advantage of GDN?
- Did you try context models or hyperpriors to capture spatial/choral dependencies among latents? What RD gain do you anticipate from such models?
- Can a single model handle multiple rates by conditioning on λ or scaling the latent channels? How large is the RD gap vs training per-λ models?
- How robust is the additive-uniform-noise relaxation? Did you compare to straight-through estimators or annealed noise, and does the relaxation bias meaningfully affect model selection?
- What are the training and inference runtimes (on CPU/GPU), and the memory footprint for typical images?
- How does the model generalize to higher bit depth (10/12-bit), HDR, or non-natural images? Any evidence beyond Kodak?
- Given that MS-SSIM improvements are strong but training was with MSE, can you explain why the learned transform preserves perceptual structure so well? Have you tried optimizing directly for MS-SSIM or a learned perceptual metric within this framework?

Overall, this is a compelling and forward-looking paper that opens a promising path for learned compression with practical bitstreams. Despite some evaluation gaps, the conceptual advance and demonstrated quality gains merit acceptance.
