### 1. Summary

The paper proposes an end-to-end framework for lossy image compression based on learned nonlinear transforms. The architecture consists of an analysis (encoder) and synthesis (decoder) transform, each comprising three stages of convolution, subsampling/upsampling, and a Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) nonlinearity. The entire model is optimized to minimize a weighted sum of rate (estimated entropy) and distortion (MSE), allowing it to trace out the optimal rate-distortion curve. A key technical contribution is the method for enabling gradient-based optimization in the presence of a non-differentiable quantizer: the authors replace the rounding operation with additive uniform noise during training, creating a continuous and differentiable proxy loss function. The paper also provides a clear and insightful discussion of the relationship between this rate-distortion framework and variational autoencoders (VAEs). The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by implementing a full compression system, including an arithmetic entropy coder, and show that it outperforms standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of both PSNR and, more significantly, the perceptual metric MS-SSIM. The qualitative results are particularly impressive, showing a marked reduction in common compression artifacts like blocking and ringing.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Significant Performance Improvement:** The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results for learned image compression, demonstrably outperforming widely-used standards like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The improvement is not only quantitative (in PSNR and MS-SSIM) but also, and more importantly, qualitative. The visual examples show a fundamentally different and more naturalistic degradation at low bitrates, which represents a significant advance for practical image compression.
2.  **Sound and Novel Methodology:** The paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound end-to-end optimization framework. The use of additive uniform noise as a differentiable proxy for quantization is an elegant and effective solution to a challenging optimization problem. This technique, combined with the principled connection to the rate-distortion objective, provides a strong foundation for future work in learned compression.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core strength lies in its successful formulation and execution of an end-to-end learning approach for a classical signal processing problem.

First, the empirical results are compelling. The quantitative comparisons in Figure 7 show a large and consistent improvement over JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of MS-SSIM across a wide range of bitrates. This aligns with the qualitative examples in Figures 5 and 6, where the proposed method avoids the structured, basis-dependent artifacts (blocking in JPEG, ringing in JPEG 2000) that are visually jarring. Instead, the method learns to discard information in a way that appears more like a simplification or smoothing of textures and fine details while preserving important edge structures, leading to a much more natural appearance. The fact that this superior perceptual quality is achieved while optimizing for a simple MSE distortion metric is remarkable and highlights the expressive power of the learned nonlinear transforms.

Second, the methodology is rigorous. The authors do not merely report entropy estimates but implement a complete codec using a context-adaptive arithmetic coder (CABAC). This ensures that the reported bitrates are realistic and that the comparisons to other codecs are fair, which is a common weakness in related literature. The validation of the continuous relaxation (Figure 4) is also crucial, as it demonstrates that the proxy loss function used for training is a reasonably unbiased and low-variance estimator of the true discrete rate and distortion, justifying the entire optimization procedure.

Finally, the paper does an excellent job of positioning itself within the broader literature. The discussion in Section 3.1, which carefully delineates the similarities and critical differences between the proposed rate-distortion optimization and variational autoencoders (VAEs), is particularly insightful. It correctly identifies that while the objectives can be formally related, the goals are different: compression aims to find an optimal trade-off on the R-D curve (controlled by λ), whereas generative modeling often implicitly pushes λ to infinity. This clarification is valuable for both the compression and generative modeling communities.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to be constructive suggestions for strengthening the paper further and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Ablation Study on Architecture:** The paper credits the biologically-inspired GDN nonlinearity for the model's efficiency and performance. While the reference to prior work on density estimation (Ballé et al., 2015) provides good motivation, a direct ablation study within this compression framework would be very valuable. For example, comparing the rate-distortion performance of the proposed architecture against an identical one using more standard nonlinearities like ReLU or LeakyReLU (while keeping parameter counts similar) would precisely quantify the contribution of GDN/IGDN.
*   **Computational Complexity:** For a practical compression method, encoding and decoding speed are important metrics. While the paper states the process is "efficient," providing concrete numbers (e.g., encoding/decoding time for a 512x512 image on a specific CPU/GPU) and comparing them to software implementations of JPEG and JPEG 2000 would significantly strengthen the practical case for the proposed method.
*   **Single Model for Multiple Rates:** The current framework requires training a separate model for each point on the rate-distortion curve (each λ). This is computationally expensive and less flexible than codecs like JPEG 2000, which can produce different bitrates from a single compressed bitstream. It would be beneficial to discuss this limitation and perhaps speculate on future work towards a single, rate-adaptive model, possibly by conditioning the transforms on the λ parameter.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   The entropy model assumes marginal independence of the latent coefficients `y_i`. Have you performed any analysis of the statistical dependencies in the quantized latent space `q`? It seems plausible that spatial correlations might still exist. Could a more sophisticated context model for the arithmetic coder (e.g., using neighboring coefficients as context) lead to further significant gains in compression rate?
*   Could you elaborate on the training process across different λ values? Is each model trained from scratch? Or is it possible to use a form of curriculum learning, where a model trained for one λ is used to initialize the training for an adjacent λ, potentially speeding up convergence?
*   In Section 4, you mention increasing the network capacity (number of channels) for models trained at higher bitrates (larger λ). Was this change driven by observing that the smaller model was becoming a performance bottleneck at these higher quality levels? This could offer interesting insights into the relationship between model capacity and the achievable rate-distortion performance.
