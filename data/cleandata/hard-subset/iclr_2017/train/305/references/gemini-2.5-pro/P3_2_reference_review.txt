### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel framework for end-to-end optimized image compression based on a nonlinear transform autoencoder architecture. The core contributions are: (1) a fully trainable compression system composed of a nonlinear analysis transform (encoder), a uniform quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform (decoder); (2) a clever optimization strategy that replaces the non-differentiable quantization step with additive uniform noise during training, creating a continuous and differentiable proxy for the rate-distortion loss function; and (3) a complete, practical codec that uses an arithmetic entropy coder to achieve actual bit rates, not just entropy estimates. The authors demonstrate that their method, optimized for Mean Squared Error (MSE), not only achieves competitive or superior rate-distortion performance in terms of PSNR compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, but also produces visually superior images with far more natural-looking artifacts, a claim strongly supported by quantitative MS-SSIM measurements.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that presents a significant advance in the field of learned image compression. The methodology is elegant, the connection to variational autoencoders is insightful, and the experimental results are both rigorous and compelling. The key reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Methodological Soundness and Novelty:** The formulation of a differentiable proxy for the rate-distortion objective by replacing quantization with additive noise is a key technical contribution. It elegantly sidesteps the problem of zero gradients from quantization, enabling end-to-end training of the entire compression model with standard stochastic gradient descent. This provides a principled way to jointly optimize the transform and entropy model for any given point on the rate-distortion curve.

2.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The authors go beyond reporting entropy estimates and implement a full codec using a context-adaptive arithmetic coder (CABAC), reporting actual compressed bit rates. This level of rigor is crucial for making credible comparisons to established standards like JPEG 2000 and is often missing in related work. The results, demonstrated on the standard Kodak dataset, show not only competitive PSNR but a dramatic improvement in perceptual quality, as evidenced by the excellent visual examples and strong MS-SSIM scores.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The authors provide a clear motivation, situating their work within the classical transform coding paradigm while highlighting its limitations. The architectural choices, particularly the use of Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN), are well-motivated by prior work from the same group on density modeling of natural images, suggesting that transforms effective at Gaussianizing data are also effective for compression.

A particularly strong aspect of the paper is Section 3.1, which draws a formal connection between their rate-distortion optimization framework and variational autoencoders (VAEs). This discussion is insightful, as it clarifies not only the similarities but also the fundamental differences in objectives. While a VAE aims to maximize the data log-likelihood (pushing λ to infinity in the R-D trade-off), this work correctly frames compression as the problem of finding the optimal trade-off for a *finite* λ. This contextualization demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem and related machine learning literature.

The empirical results are impressive. The fact that a model optimized solely for MSE produces images that are so visually superior to JPEG and JPEG 2000 is a remarkable finding. The artifacts produced by this method—a smooth simplification of texture and contours rather than blocking or ringing—are far less objectionable. This suggests that the nonlinear, data-driven transforms are learning to represent image structures in a way that is more robust to coarse quantization. The consistent and significant gains in MS-SSIM across all bit rates provide strong quantitative support for this qualitative observation.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is excellent, the following points could be addressed to further strengthen it:

*   **Ablation Study on Architecture:** The paper credits the biologically-inspired GDN nonlinearity for the model's efficiency. While the authors mention preliminary results with ReLUs in the discussion, a more formal ablation study in the appendix would be very valuable. For example, a comparison of the rate-distortion performance of a GDN-based model versus a similarly-sized model using a more standard nonlinearity like ReLU or LeakyReLU would quantitatively substantiate the claim that GDN is more parameter-efficient for this task.
*   **Computational Complexity:** A brief discussion of the computational complexity (e.g., multiply-adds per pixel) and practical encoding/decoding times relative to JPEG 2000 would be beneficial. While the paper notes that encoding/decoding is efficient, providing concrete numbers would help assess the practical viability of the proposed method.
*   **Rate Control Mechanism:** The current framework requires training a separate model for each λ (i.e., for each quality level). This is a practical limitation compared to standards like JPEG 2000, which can generate any target bitrate from a single compressed bitstream (via post-compression rate-distortion optimization). The work of Toderici et al. (2016) is mentioned as having a single model for all rates. It would be interesting if the authors could comment on the potential for extending their framework to a single, rate-adaptive model.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you provide more quantitative detail on your preliminary findings comparing GDN to rectified linear units? Specifically, by what factor does the model size or number of stages need to increase to achieve comparable R-D performance?
2.  The paper states that the architecture choices were "somewhat ad-hoc". Have you since experimented with more modern architectures, for instance using residual connections or attention mechanisms, and have these yielded further improvements?
3.  How sensitive is the final performance to the non-parametric density model used for the entropy estimation during training? For instance, if a simpler parametric model (e.g., a Laplace or Gaussian scale mixture) were used, how much performance would be lost? This could clarify the importance of accurately modeling the latent marginals.
