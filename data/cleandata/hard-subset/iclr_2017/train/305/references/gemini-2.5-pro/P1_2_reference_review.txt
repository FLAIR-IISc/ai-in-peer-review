### Summary

This paper introduces a complete, end-to-end optimized framework for lossy image compression based on deep neural networks. The authors propose a nonlinear autoencoder-like architecture where the analysis (encoder) and synthesis (decoder) transforms are composed of cascaded convolutional layers and a biologically-inspired nonlinearity, Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The core contribution is a method for directly optimizing the network's parameters for the classical rate-distortion trade-off. To overcome the non-differentiability of quantization, the paper proposes an elegant continuous relaxation, replacing the rounding operation with additive uniform noise during training. This enables the joint optimization of the encoder, decoder, and a non-parametric density model for the latent representation using stochastic gradient descent. The authors demonstrate that their method, even when optimized for Mean Squared Error (MSE), produces compressed images that are visually far superior to those from JPEG and JPEG 2000, with dramatically fewer blocking, ringing, and aliasing artifacts. These qualitative improvements are backed by strong quantitative results, showing superior performance in terms of PSNR and, most significantly, the perceptual metric MS-SSIM. The work is validated with a fully implemented system, including a context-adaptive arithmetic entropy coder to report actual bit rates.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Novel and Principled Framework:** The paper presents a highly effective and well-motivated framework for learned image compression. The continuous relaxation of the rate-distortion objective is an elegant and powerful solution to the long-standing problem of optimizing a neural network through a discrete quantization step, making end-to-end training feasible. This approach is well-grounded in classic information theory while leveraging the power of modern deep learning.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance and Rigorous Evaluation:** The method achieves exceptional results, marking a significant step forward for image compression. The qualitative improvements over established standards like JPEG 2000 are striking and compelling. These visual gains are strongly supported by quantitative improvements in both traditional (PSNR) and perceptual (MS-SSIM) metrics. The evaluation is scientifically rigorous, notably through the implementation of a full entropy coder to report actual bit rates rather than relying on less reliable entropy estimates.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's strengths are numerous. The approach is grounded in rate-distortion theory, correctly framing the problem as minimizing `R + λD` and tracing the optimal rate-distortion curve by varying λ. The connection drawn to Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) is insightful, clearly articulating the similarities in the objective function while also highlighting the fundamental differences in goals (generative modeling vs. rate-distortion trade-off) that make their formulation uniquely suited for compression.

The technical execution is sound. The use of additive uniform noise as a differentiable proxy for quantization is a clever and effective technique, and the authors validate this approximation empirically in Figure 4, showing a strong correlation between the relaxed loss and the actual rate and distortion after quantization. The choice of GDN/IGDN as nonlinearities is well-motivated by prior work showing their effectiveness in modeling image statistics, which appears to contribute to the model's ability to produce natural-looking reconstructions.

The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. By evaluating on the standard Kodak dataset and providing a wealth of visual examples, the authors make a strong case for their method's superiority. The fact that a model trained on MSE produces such perceptually pleasing results is a remarkable finding, suggesting the network learns to preserve structures that are important for both low MSE and human perception. The inclusion of a CABAC-based entropy coder for final rate measurement adds a level of completeness and credibility that is often missing in related machine learning literature.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

*   **Computational Complexity:** The paper states that encoding and decoding are "efficient" but lacks concrete performance metrics. Providing model size, parameters, and encoding/decoding times (e.g., on a specific GPU/CPU) would be very valuable for assessing the practical viability of the method in real-world applications.
*   **Architectural Ablation:** The authors note that architectural choices were "somewhat ad-hoc". While the chosen architecture is clearly effective, a brief discussion or ablation study on the impact of key components (e.g., number of stages, number of channels, or the contribution of GDN vs. a simpler nonlinearity like ReLU) would provide deeper insight into the source of the performance gains.
*   **Generality of the Framework:** It would be helpful to briefly comment on the generality of the continuous relaxation approach. Is its success tied to the specific properties of the GDN transform, or is it expected to work equally well with more standard CNN architectures? This would help position the core contribution of the training method more broadly.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  The continuous relaxation with uniform noise is a key element of your work. Did you experiment with other noise distributions? For instance, for a non-uniform input distribution to the quantizer, the quantization error is not strictly uniform. Would using a different noise model during training impact performance?
2.  Your method requires training a separate model for each λ, resulting in a collection of models to cover the rate-distortion curve. This is less flexible than codecs like JPEG 2000, where a single bitstream can be truncated for progressive decoding. Have you considered approaches for training a single, rate-adaptive model, for example by making λ an input to the network?
3.  You mention that GDN/IGDN transforms are more efficient than architectures using simpler nonlinearities like ReLUs. Could you provide a more quantitative sense of this efficiency? For example, to achieve a similar rate-distortion performance point, roughly how many more parameters or computational operations would a ReLU-based model require compared to your GDN-based one?
