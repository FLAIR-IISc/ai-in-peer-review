### Summary of the paper

This paper presents a novel end-to-end learning framework for lossy image compression. The proposed model is a non-linear transform code, composed of an analysis transform (encoder), a uniform quantizer, and a synthesis transform (decoder). The transforms are constructed as a three-stage cascade of convolutions, subsampling/upsampling, and a biologically-inspired non-linearity known as Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). To overcome the non-differentiability of the quantization step during training, the authors propose a continuous relaxation by replacing quantization with additive uniform noise. This allows for joint optimization of all model parameters via stochastic gradient descent on a rate-distortion loss function. The paper demonstrates that this approach, even when optimized for a simple metric like MSE, produces images that are visually superior to JPEG and JPEG 2000, replacing common artifacts like blocking and ringing with more natural-looking blur. The authors provide a complete system, including a CABAC-based entropy coder to report actual bit rates, and show quantitative improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000 in both PSNR and, more significantly, MS-SSIM.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Significant presentation issues and lack of clarity in core methodological details:** The paper is hampered by numerous typographical errors, inconsistent notation, and poorly defined equations. Most critically, the mathematical formulation of the core GDN/IGDN operations (Equations 3 & 4) is ambiguous and appears to contradict the description in the text. This lack of precision undermines the paper's reproducibility and the reader's confidence in the work.
2.  **Insufficiently rigorous experimental comparison:** While the results against JPEG and JPEG 2000 are promising, the evaluation omits a comparison to modern, high-performance conventional codecs such as BPG (based on HEVC). As BPG significantly outperforms JPEG 2000, its absence makes it difficult to assess whether the proposed method is truly state-of-the-art.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

My decision to recommend rejection, despite the novel ideas and promising visual results, is based on a substantial accumulation of flaws that, in aggregate, fall below the standard of rigor and clarity expected for an ICLR publication.

**1. Lack of Methodological Clarity and Precision:**

*   **Ambiguous GDN/IGDN Formulation:** Equation (3) defines the GDN operation as `u = w * (β + Σγw^2)^12`. The exponent `12` is presumably a typo for `1/2`. More importantly, the text describes this as "divisive normalization", yet the equation shows a multiplication. A divisive normalization would typically be `w / (...)` or `w * (...)^(-1/2)`. This is a critical point of confusion regarding the core non-linearity of the model. A similar typo and potential ambiguity exist for the IGDN in Equation (4).
*   **Undefined or Inconsistent Notation:** Several pieces of notation are used without clear definition or are used inconsistently. For example, in Eq. (10), `ZM` is used without definition (presumably a typo for `Z`). In Eq. (11), the parameter vector `φ` is defined for `g_a`, but the loss function is written as `L(θ,φ)` while the expression uses `ψ(i)` and `ga(x;φ)`. A stray `ϕ` is also mentioned in the text following the equation. The notation `ψ(i)` is introduced to parameterize the marginals, but its relationship to the index `i` in the sum `∑_i` is confusing.
*   **Incomplete Technical Descriptions:** The description of upsampling in Equation (5) is incomplete. It specifies values only for integer-grid locations, setting others to zero. This is not a standard upsampling method (e.g., bilinear, transposed convolution). While the subsequent convolution may perform interpolation, this is not explicitly stated, leaving a gap in the architectural description.
*   **Formatting and Referencing:** The paper exhibits a general lack of care in its presentation. The footnote on the first page interrupts the word "transformation". There are numerous typographical errors (e.g., "continous domain"). Most strikingly, the reference section is completely malformed, with BibTeX errors like `\?`, `\Q2016\E`, and inconsistent, incomplete entries. This level of carelessness is unacceptable and reflects poorly on the manuscript's preparation.

**2. Incomplete Experimental Evaluation:**

*   **Outdated Baselines:** By 2016/2017, BPG had been established as a much stronger baseline for image compression than JPEG and JPEG 2000. Comparing a new method only to these older standards provides an incomplete picture of its performance. To make a compelling case for advancing the state of the art, a comparison with BPG is essential.
*   **Justification of GDN is Weak:** The paper attributes much of its visual success to the "biologically-inspired" GDN. However, the claim that simpler non-linearities like ReLUs are less efficient is admitted to be "provisional" and based on "limited experiments". Without a proper ablation study comparing GDN to simpler, more standard activation functions within the same framework, the necessity of this complex non-linearity is not rigorously established.
*   **Practicality Concerns:** The method requires training a separate, large model for each point on the rate-distortion curve (i.e., for each λ). This is a significant practical disadvantage compared to methods that can use a single model to cover a range of bit rates. While the authors acknowledge this, the severity of this limitation is downplayed.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While I am recommending rejection, I believe the core ideas of the paper are valuable and the work could be made acceptable with significant revision.
*   **Thorough Proofreading and Correction:** Please meticulously correct all equations, notation, typos, and especially the reference list. The mathematical description of the core components (GDN, upsampling) must be unambiguous and precise.
*   **Strengthen Experimental Evaluation:** The most impactful addition would be a comparison against BPG. This would provide a true benchmark of the method's performance. Furthermore, a rigorous ablation study comparing GDN/IGDN with more common deep learning non-linearities (e.g., ReLU, GeLU) would substantially strengthen the claims about GDN's efficiency and importance.
*   **Improve Discussion of Limitations:** A more upfront discussion of the one-model-per-rate limitation would be appreciated. You could also discuss potential avenues for future work to address this, which would strengthen the paper.
*   **Provide Summary Metrics:** While the argument against summarizing R-D curves in Appendix 6.3 has some merit, it is standard practice to provide summary metrics like average BD-rate savings. Providing both individual plots and a carefully explained summary would be more comprehensive and convincing.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you please provide the exact, corrected mathematical formula for the GDN transform in Equation (3)? Is it multiplicative as written, or is it divisive, in which case the exponent should be -1/2?
2.  Can you clarify the upsampling procedure? Does the subsequent convolution in Equation (6) learn to perform interpolation from the sparse, zero-padded input provided by Equation (5)?
3.  Given that a separate model is trained for each λ, how much computational effort (e.g., GPU-hours) is required to trace out a full rate-distortion curve for a given image type (e.g., RGB)?
4.  Were any attempts made to compare against the BPG codec? If so, how did your method perform? If not, could you provide a rationale for its exclusion?
