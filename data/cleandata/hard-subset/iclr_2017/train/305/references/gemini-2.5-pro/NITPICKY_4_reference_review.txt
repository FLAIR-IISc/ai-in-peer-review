### **Summary of Contributions**

The paper presents a framework for end-to-end optimization of an image compression model. The model is based on a nonlinear analysis and synthesis transform, analogous to an autoencoder, which is optimized to minimize a rate-distortion objective. The transforms consist of cascaded stages of convolution, down/upsampling, and a generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearity. A key contribution is the method for optimizing this system in the presence of a non-differentiable quantizer: the authors propose a continuous relaxation by replacing quantization with additive uniform noise during training. This allows for stochastic gradient descent optimization of the entire model. The authors also draw an insightful connection between their rate-distortion objective and the objective function of variational autoencoders. The proposed method is evaluated against JPEG and JPEG 2000, demonstrating superior performance in terms of PSNR at lower bit rates and substantial improvements in perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) and visual appearance across all bit rates.

### **Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents a conceptually novel and promising direction for image compression with impressive qualitative results, I must recommend rejection. My decision is based on two primary factors:

1.  A significant number of technical and notational ambiguities, combined with methodological choices that are not rigorously justified (e.g., an "ad-hoc" architecture), undermine the scientific rigor and reproducibility of the work.
2.  The paper suffers from pervasive and severe formatting issues, particularly in the bibliography and figure presentation, which suggests a lack of care and brings the overall quality of the manuscript into question.

### **Supporting Arguments**

My concerns are detailed below, ranging from high-level methodological clarity to specific notational errors.

**1. Methodological and Notational Imprecision:**

*   **Errors in Core Equations:** The definitions of the core GDN and IGDN operations appear to contain typographical errors. In Equations (3) and (4), the exponents are written as `(...) 12`. This is presumably meant to be `(...)**0.5` or `sqrt(...)`, but as written, it is nonsensical. Such an error in the central equations of the proposed transform is a serious flaw.
*   **Undefined Notation:** In Equation (10), the expression `for all n ∈ ZM` is used. The term `ZM` is not defined anywhere in the manuscript. It is likely a typo for `Z`, but this kind of sloppiness creates unnecessary confusion for the reader.
*   **Ambiguous Upsampling:** The upsampling operation in Equation (5) is defined as a simple nearest-neighbor scheme. This is a surprisingly basic choice in a deep learning context where learned transposed convolutions are standard. Furthermore, the claim that it can be "implemented jointly with their adjacent convolution" is not immediately obvious with this formulation and requires further clarification.
*   **Unjustified Approximations and Heuristics:** The paper dismisses the positive bias of the rate approximation seen in Figure 4 by stating that "a constant bias... would simply alter the effective value of λ". This argument is insufficient, as it assumes the bias is constant across all images and rate points, which is unlikely to be true, potentially compromising the optimality of the learned trade-off. Furthermore, the appendix mentions critical training details are handled by undefined "heuristics" (e.g., adapting the range of the spline approximation), which severely hinders reproducibility. The network architecture itself is described as "somewhat ad-hoc". Without ablation studies or a more principled design rationale, the results feel more like the product of extensive tuning than a robustly understood method.

**2. Deficiencies in Evaluation and Presentation:**

*   **Simplistic Entropy Coder:** The authors commendably implement an actual entropy code. However, they describe it as a "simple" implementation that "only crudely exploits spatial adaptation". This is a critical weakness. JPEG 2000, the primary benchmark, utilizes the highly sophisticated, context-adaptive EBCOT algorithm. Comparing a state-of-the-art nonlinear transform paired with a crude entropy coder against a standard with a highly optimized one makes it difficult to fairly assess the contributions. The performance gains could be significantly different if both methods used coders of comparable sophistication.
*   **Lack of Self-Contained Quantitative Summary:** In Appendix 6.3, the authors argue against summarizing rate-distortion curves and instead direct the reader to an external website. While I appreciate the transparency, a scientific paper must be self-contained. The community has standard, albeit imperfect, metrics for summarizing performance, such as the Bjontegaard Delta (BD)-rate. Omitting any form of aggregate quantitative summary across the test set is a significant omission.
*   **Pervasive Formatting Errors:** The manuscript is riddled with formatting problems that go beyond minor typos.
    *   The bibliography is completely broken. Nearly every entry contains erroneous characters (`\.,?`, `\Q2016\E`), repeated author blocks, and missing information. This is not a minor issue; it reflects a profound lack of attention to detail in the paper's preparation.
    *   None of the figures in the main text (Figures 2, 3) or the appendix (Figures 8-17) have captions, making them extremely difficult to interpret without hunting for context in the text.
    *   The URL in the main text is not formatted as a proper footnote.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The discussion in Section 5 on preliminary results with ReLU and sigmoid nonlinearities is weak. This should either be formalized into a proper ablation study within the paper to support the claims about GDN's efficiency or be removed entirely.
*   The paper would be significantly strengthened by either improving the entropy coder to be more competitive or, for the purpose of a controlled comparison, using a simple, non-adaptive arithmetic coder for all methods to isolate the performance gains of the transform itself.
*   I strongly urge the authors to perform a thorough proofreading and re-formatting of the entire manuscript, paying special attention to equations and the bibliography, before any future submission.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the correct form of Equations (3) and (4) for the GDN/IGDN transforms?
2.  Could you explicitly define the heuristic used to adapt the range of the spline approximation for the marginal probability models, as mentioned in Appendix 6.1?
3.  Given the "ad-hoc" nature of the architecture, were any experiments conducted to analyze the sensitivity of the model's performance to hyperparameters like the number of channels or the size of the filters?
