### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel end-to-end framework for lossy image compression based on learned nonlinear transforms. The authors propose a model architecture consisting of an analysis transform, a quantizer, and a synthesis transform. The analysis and synthesis transforms are composed of multiple stages of convolution, downsampling/upsampling, and a biologically-inspired nonlinear activation function known as Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN).

A key contribution is the method for optimizing the entire system directly for a rate-distortion objective. To overcome the non-differentiability of the quantization step, the authors introduce a continuous relaxation by replacing quantization with additive uniform noise during training. This allows the parameters of the analysis and synthesis transforms, along with a non-parametric density model for the latent representation, to be jointly optimized using stochastic gradient descent.

The framework is evaluated by training models for different points on the rate-distortion curve. The authors demonstrate the feasibility of their approach by implementing a full compression system, including a context-based arithmetic entropy coder, and reporting actual bit rates. When compared to standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000 on the Kodak dataset, the proposed method shows superior performance. Although optimized for Mean Squared Error (MSE), the method produces compressed images that are qualitatively and perceptually superior, exhibiting significantly fewer of the typical blocking and ringing artifacts associated with linear transform codes. This claim is supported by quantitative results using the MS-SSIM perceptual metric.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Technical Soundness and Novelty:** The paper presents a complete, principled, and technically sound framework for end-to-end learned image compression. The proposed continuous relaxation of the rate-distortion objective is an effective method for enabling gradient-based optimization in the presence of a quantizer, representing a significant technical contribution.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method is shown to outperform well-established and highly-engineered standards like JPEG 2000, not only in terms of PSNR at lower bit rates but also by a substantial margin in perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) across all bit rates. The results are rigorously validated using actual bit rates from a practical entropy coder, which strengthens the claims significantly.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is exceptionally clear in its presentation. It motivates the problem by situating it within the classical transform coding literature and then methodically develops its proposed solution. The authors provide an insightful comparison to variational autoencoders (VAEs), carefully articulating the similarities in formulation while highlighting the fundamental differences in objective (rate-distortion trade-off vs. generative modeling), which helps place the work within the broader context of modern deep learning.

The technical approach is sound. The core challenge in optimizing a compression model is the discrete nature of quantization, which yields zero gradients almost everywhere. The authors' solution—replacing quantization with additive uniform noise—is well-justified, as uniform noise is a classical approximation for quantization error. The paper provides an empirical validation (Figure 4) showing that this relaxation provides a good estimate of the true rate and distortion, which confirms the validity of the training procedure. Another element of technical rigor is the use of a real entropy coder (CABAC). Many works in learned compression report only entropy estimates, which can be optimistic. By reporting actual compressed file sizes, the authors demonstrate that their system is not just a theoretical model but a practical compression pipeline.

The novelty and significance of the work are high. While not the first paper to apply deep learning to compression, it provides one of the most convincing and complete demonstrations of a learned system outperforming strong, industry-standard baselines. The results are compelling: the visual quality of the compressed images (e.g., Figure 5) is remarkably better than JPEG and JPEG 2000 at similar bit rates. The artifacts are much more natural, preserving smooth contours and sharp edges rather than introducing the familiar blockiness or ringing. This substantial improvement in perceptual quality, achieved while optimizing for the simple MSE metric, suggests that the flexible, nonlinear model class is highly effective at capturing the perceptually relevant structure of natural images.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   A practical limitation of the proposed method is that a separate model must be trained and stored for each point on the rate-distortion curve (i.e., for each λ). A discussion of the trade-offs of this approach versus methods that use a single model to cover a range of bit rates would be valuable. It would also be interesting to see comments on future work towards developing a single, rate-adaptive version of this architecture.
*   The paper notes that the entropy coding scheme is relatively simple and does not exploit spatial dependencies in the latent space as sophisticatedly as modern codecs. This is, in a way, a strength, as it implies the performance gains stem primarily from the learned transforms. However, a brief discussion could be added about the potential for further gains by incorporating a more powerful, learned context model for entropy coding (e.g., using an autoregressive model on the latents).
*   The discussion on the efficiency of GDN/IGDN nonlinearities compared to more standard ones like ReLUs is interesting but stated as "provisional." While a full comparative study is likely beyond the scope of this paper, the argument could be strengthened by including a single quantitative data point (e.g., a PSNR value at a specific bit rate for a model of similar complexity trained with ReLUs) to make the efficiency claim more concrete.

### 5. Questions for Authors

*   The paper mentions that encoding and decoding with the trained models are "efficient." Could you provide a more quantitative comparison of the computational complexity (e.g., in FLOPS or measured execution time on a specific platform) of your codec versus a software implementation of JPEG 2000 for a typical image size? This would help in assessing the practical viability of the method.
*   The non-parametric density model for the latent variables (`pỹi`) is a key component of the rate estimation. How sensitive is the final compression performance to the hyperparameter of this model (i.e., the number of sampling points per unit interval in the piecewise linear approximation)?
*   The models were trained on a large dataset of natural images (ImageNet). Have you performed any experiments to assess how well the models generalize to out-of-distribution data, such as medical imagery, computer-generated graphics, or text documents, which have very different statistics?
