**Summary of Contributions**

The paper proposes a novel framework for lossy image compression based on end-to-end optimization of a nonlinear transform code. The transforms consist of a cascade of convolutions and a biologically-inspired nonlinearity, Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). A key technical contribution is a method for optimizing the system in the presence of a non-differentiable quantizer by replacing it with additive uniform noise during training, enabling the use of stochastic gradient descent. The authors frame this approach within the context of rate-distortion theory and also draw insightful parallels and distinctions with variational autoencoders (VAEs). Unlike many learning-based approaches that report entropy estimates, this work implements a complete codec using an arithmetic encoder and reports actual bit rates. The method is shown to outperform JPEG and JPEG 2000, not only in terms of PSNR on most images but also substantially in terms of perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) and the subjective visual appearance of compressed images.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents a promising and well-motivated approach with visually compelling results, I am recommending rejection due to two primary reasons:
1.  The manuscript is beset by a significant number of errors in mathematical notation, imprecise terminology, and vague methodological descriptions.
2.  The presentation is unacceptably sloppy, culminating in a reference section that is so severely malformed as to be unusable. This lack of attention to detail undermines confidence in the rigor of the work.

**Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The core ideas of the paper are strong, but the execution of the manuscript is flawed. My decision is based on the accumulation of many minor-to-moderate issues that collectively question the paper's readiness for publication.

1.  **Mathematical Notation and Clarity:**
    *   **GDN/IGDN Definition:** In Equations (3) and (4), the exponent is written as `12`. This is highly ambiguous. Given the context of normalization, this is almost certainly a typographical error for `1/2` (i.e., a square root). This is a critical error in the definition of the central nonlinearity of the proposed model.
    *   **VAE Formulation:** In Equation (13), the identity matrix in the Gaussian covariance is denoted by `1`, whereas the standard notation is `I`. This is a minor but jarring inconsistency.
    *   **Upsampling Definition:** The definition of upsampling in Equation (5) is incomplete. It specifies the output for integer divisions but leaves the "otherwise" case undefined (presumably it should be zero, but this must be stated).

2.  **Imprecise Terminology and Vague Methods:**
    *   **"Dimensionality":** In Section 4, the authors state that their three-stage network, which subsamples by factors of 4, 2, and 2, results in an output with "half the dimensionality of the input." This is confusing. The spatial resolution is reduced by a factor of 16x16=256. The text implies "dimensionality" means the total number of scalar values in the representation (`width * height * channels`), but this is non-standard and should be explicitly defined to avoid confusion with the common meaning of vector-space dimension.
    *   **Density Estimation:** The non-parametric estimation of the marginal densities `p_y_tilde_i` is a key component of the method. However, the Appendix states that a "heuristic" is used to adapt the range of the spline approximation after every 10^6 gradient steps. The nature of this heuristic is not specified, rendering a critical part of the optimization process non-reproducible.
    *   **Entropy Coder:** The authors commendably implement a full entropy coder. However, they note that their probability model is shared "across space within each channel," which is a strong independence assumption that ignores spatial correlations. While they acknowledge this is a crude approach compared to JPEG 2000, this potentially suboptimal design choice makes the rate-distortion comparison less direct. The gains shown may be attributable to the transform, but the reported bitrates could be higher than what a more sophisticated coder would achieve with the same transform, potentially disadvantaging their own method in the comparison.

3.  **Manuscript Preparation:**
    *   **References:** The bibliography is in a state of extreme disrepair. It contains numerous errors, such as `Ballé et al\.,? 2015, Ballé et al\.,`, `Heeger and J.,? 1992, Heeger and J.,`, and partially mangled entries like `Marpe et al\.,? \Q2003\E, Marpe et al\.,`. This is not a minor formatting issue; it renders the reference list nearly useless and suggests a profound lack of care in preparing the manuscript for submission.
    *   **Formatting:** The funding footnote in the introduction is placed unconventionally after the first paragraph rather than being attached to the author names.

While the visual results are impressive and the core idea is valuable, the sheer number of errors and oversights prevents me from recommending acceptance. The work holds great promise but requires a thorough round of revision and proofreading to meet the standards of a top-tier conference.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The connection drawn to VAEs is excellent and provides valuable context. This is a strength of the paper.
*   The empirical validation of the continuous relaxation (Figure 4) is a good piece of analysis that supports the main methodological contribution.
*   The honesty about underperforming JPEG 2000 in PSNR on some images is appreciated. It would strengthen the paper to include a brief analysis of *why* this might be the case (e.g., are there specific image features like fine textures that the model struggles with at higher bitrates?).
*   The IGDN transform, described as a learned single-iteration inverse inspired by LISTA, is an interesting idea buried in the appendix. This might be worth highlighting more prominently.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please confirm the exponent in the GDN/IGDN equations (3 & 4)?
2.  Could you provide a precise, algorithmic description of the heuristic used to adapt the range of your non-parametric density models during training?
3.  The text notes your method underperforms JPEG 2000 in PSNR for some images. Do you have a hypothesis for this? Does it correlate with specific types of image content?
4.  Given that your entropy coder does not exploit spatial redundancies, while JPEG 2000's does, how confident are you that the R-D performance gains are not being underestimated? Have you considered pairing your transform with a more standard, context-aware entropy coder to see how it affects the results?
