### Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

**Summary of Contributions:**
This paper presents a novel, end-to-end learned framework for lossy image compression. The authors replace the traditional pipeline of separate, hand-engineered components (linear transform, quantizer, entropy coder) with a unified system based on nonlinear analysis and synthesis transforms that are optimized jointly for a rate-distortion trade-off. The core of the model consists of a deep autoencoder-like architecture using cascades of convolutions and a biologically-inspired nonlinearity, Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). A key technical innovation is the use of a continuous relaxation—replacing the non-differentiable quantization step with additive uniform noise during training—to enable optimization via stochastic gradient descent. The authors demonstrate that their system, even when optimized solely for Mean Squared Error (MSE), produces images that are perceptually far superior to JPEG and JPEG 2000 at comparable bit rates, and often outperforms them on standard metrics like PSNR as well.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This is an excellent paper that introduces a bold and highly effective new direction for a classical and well-studied problem. My decision is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Significant Conceptual Novelty:** The paper's main strength is its conceptual leap. It moves image compression away from the decades-old paradigm of separately optimizing fixed linear transforms and entropy models, and into the modern paradigm of end-to-end deep learning. This is not an incremental improvement but a fundamental rethinking of the problem, leading to a system that learns its own representation tailored specifically to the statistics of natural images and the rate-distortion objective.

2.  **Impressive and Insightful Results:** The empirical results are remarkable. The fact that a model trained on MSE can so thoroughly eliminate the visually jarring artifacts (blocking, ringing) characteristic of linear transform codes is a major finding. This suggests the learned nonlinear transforms are capturing perceptually relevant structures in a way that fixed bases like DCT or wavelets cannot. The strong performance on the MS-SSIM metric validates this visual superiority quantitatively.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The authors tackle the central challenge of optimizing a system with a non-differentiable quantization step with an elegant and effective solution. The continuous relaxation using additive noise is a clever insight that serves as a proxy for both the rate (via differential entropy) and distortion, and the paper provides empirical evidence (Fig. 4) that this approximation is effective. This technical contribution is significant and could potentially be applied to other problems involving discrete latent variables.

Furthermore, the paper provides a thoughtful connection of their rate-distortion framework to variational autoencoders (VAEs). While noting the similarities, the authors astutely highlight the fundamental differences in objective: VAEs are generative models aimed at maximizing the data log-likelihood (a lossless compression regime, in a sense), whereas their method explicitly optimizes the trade-off between rate and distortion, which is the core of lossy compression. This discussion adds conceptual depth and correctly positions the work within the broader machine learning literature.

While one could point to areas for improvement—the entropy model is relatively simple and does not exploit spatial redundancies as aggressively as state-of-the-art codecs, and a separate model must be trained for each rate-distortion point—these are minor limitations in the face of the paper's core achievement. The authors have successfully demonstrated the viability and power of their end-to-end approach, building a complete and functional compression system that yields state-of-the-art results from a perceptual standpoint. The new direction this paper charts for the field of image compression is far more valuable than any minor methodological shortcomings.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **The Role of GDN:** The paper credits the biologically-inspired GDN nonlinearity for some of its success. While the authors mention preliminary results with other activation functions (like ReLUs), a more formal ablation study would be highly valuable. For instance, a comparison against a similar-capacity network using standard Conv-ReLU-BatchNorm blocks would help disentangle the contributions of the end-to-end optimization framework itself versus the specific architectural choice of GDN. Is GDN truly more parameter-efficient, or can its performance be matched by simply using a deeper or wider ReLU-based network?
*   **Rate Adaptability:** A practical drawback of the proposed method is the need to train and store a separate network for each point on the rate-distortion curve. The authors acknowledge this when comparing to Toderici et al. (2016). It would be beneficial to discuss potential avenues for creating a single, rate-adaptive model. Could λ be provided as an input to the network, or could a single latent representation be quantized at different levels of precision? A brief discussion of these future directions would strengthen the paper.
*   **Generality of Learned Priors:** The marginal probability models are trained on ImageNet. It would be interesting to know how well the model performs on out-of-distribution data (e.g., medical images, line drawings, text). Does the adaptive component of the CABAC entropy coder compensate for mismatches in the prior, or is performance significantly degraded?

This work is an excellent example of how deep learning can provide new perspectives and superior solutions to long-standing problems. It is a well-executed, insightful, and impactful paper that will surely inspire a great deal of follow-up work.
