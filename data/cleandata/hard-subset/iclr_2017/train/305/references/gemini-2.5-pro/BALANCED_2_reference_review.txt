### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel framework for creating a high-performance lossy image compression system by optimizing a nonlinear transform code in an end-to-end fashion. The core of the method consists of a pair of analysis and synthesis transforms, structured as deep convolutional networks incorporating a biologically-inspired non-linearity known as Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). A key technical contribution is the method for optimizing the standard rate-distortion objective despite the non-differentiability of the quantization step. The authors propose a continuous relaxation, replacing quantization with additive uniform noise during training, which allows for stable optimization via stochastic gradient descent. The framework is trained to minimize a weighted sum of rate (estimated using a non-parametric density model of the latent codes) and distortion (measured by MSE). The authors demonstrate the practical viability of their approach by implementing a full codec using a context-adaptive binary arithmetic coder (CABAC) and evaluating it on standard datasets. The results show that their method significantly outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000, particularly in terms of perceptual quality (MS-SSIM), and produces compressed images with much more natural-looking artifacts, avoiding the blocking and ringing common to linear transform methods.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Effective Optimization Framework:** The paper introduces a well-motivated and elegant solution to the difficult problem of end-to-end optimization for lossy compression. The use of a continuous relaxation (additive uniform noise) to approximate the non-differentiable quantization process is a clever and powerful technique. The authors provide strong empirical evidence (Fig. 4) that this proxy objective function is a good approximation of the true discrete rate-distortion objective, lending high confidence to the training procedure.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance and High-Quality Results:** The proposed method achieves compelling results that advance the state of the art in learned image compression. The quantitative results show clear improvements over established standards like JPEG and JPEG 2000 in both PSNR and, most notably, MS-SSIM across a wide range of bit rates. The qualitative results presented are excellent; the compressed images replace the typical blocky or ringing artifacts with more perceptually pleasing simplifications, such as smoothing of fine textures, which is a significant practical advantage.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper is exceptionally well-executed and presents a complete, self-contained contribution to the field of image compression.

*   **Principled Model Design:** The choice of the GDN non-linearity is not arbitrary but is grounded in prior work on its effectiveness for modeling the statistics of natural images. This provides a strong justification for the model architecture and likely contributes to its excellent performance.
*   **Methodological Rigor:** The evaluation is thorough and fair. Unlike some related works that only report entropy estimates, the authors implement a full entropy coding scheme (CABAC) to report actual bit rates, which is the correct way to compare against practical compression standards. The experiments are conducted on a standard benchmark (Kodak dataset), and the authors provide extensive supplementary materials online for further inspection, demonstrating transparency and confidence in their results.
*   **Clarity and Insightful Discussion:** The paper is very clearly written and easy to follow. The discussion in Section 3.1, which carefully relates the rate-distortion objective to the variational autoencoder (VAE) framework, is particularly insightful. It clarifies the deep connections between these two fields while also highlighting the fundamental differences in their goals (rate-distortion trade-off vs. generative modeling), which is a valuable contribution to the community's understanding.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to be constructive suggestions for future work and to further strengthen the paper, and they do not detract from my positive assessment.

*   **Model Ablations:** The paper notes that the architecture choices are "somewhat ad-hoc" and that GDN seems more efficient than standard ReLUs. Including a small-scale ablation study in the appendix comparing GDN to more standard CNN activation functions (e.g., ReLU with Batch Normalization) would provide concrete evidence for the benefits of GDN and strengthen this claim.
*   **Rate Adaptability:** The current method requires training a separate, specialized model for each point on the rate-distortion curve (i.e., for each λ). This is computationally expensive and less practical for deployment than a single model that can operate across multiple bit rates. It would be valuable to see a discussion on potential paths to achieve rate adaptability within this framework, perhaps by conditioning the transforms on the quality parameter λ, as has been explored in other work.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The paper states that encoding and decoding are efficient, but providing concrete performance figures (e.g., encoding/decoding time for a standard image size on a specific hardware platform) would be very helpful. This would allow readers to better assess the practical trade-offs of this method compared to highly optimized, hardware-accelerated traditional codecs.
*   **Entropy Model:** The current entropy model assumes the latent coefficients are marginally independent. This is a simplifying assumption, and it is likely that significant spatial or cross-channel dependencies remain. Exploring more powerful, context-adaptive entropy models (e.g., using autoregressive priors on the latents) could unlock further gains in compression efficiency and is a very promising avenue for future work.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points in their rebuttal:

1.  Figure 4 shows that the differential entropy provides a biased estimate of the discrete entropy, especially at low bit rates. You argue that a constant bias simply alters the effective λ. However, could this *rate-dependent* bias systematically affect the optimization, potentially leading to solutions that are suboptimal for the true discrete objective in the low-rate regime where the bias is most pronounced?
2.  The IGDN is described as an "approximate inverse" of the GDN transform. How significant is the reconstruction error of the transform pair itself (i.e., `gs(ga(x))`), in the absence of quantization? Understanding this "inversion gap" could provide insight into what portion of the total distortion is attributable to the quantization versus the imperfect inversion of the transform.
3.  For higher bit rates, you increase the channel capacity of the networks. Does this suggest that a single, fixed-capacity architecture is not optimal across the entire rate-distortion curve? How was the decision made on when to increase capacity, and how sensitive is the performance to this choice at different bit rates?
