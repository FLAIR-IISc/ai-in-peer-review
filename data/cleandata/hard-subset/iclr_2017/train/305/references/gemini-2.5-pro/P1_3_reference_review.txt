### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel end-to-end learning framework for lossy image compression. The core contribution is a complete compression system based on a nonlinear analysis and synthesis transform pair, which is jointly optimized for a rate-distortion objective. The architecture employs a cascade of convolutional layers and a biologically-inspired nonlinearity called Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). A key technical innovation is the use of a continuous relaxation—replacing the non-differentiable quantization operation with additive uniform noise—to enable training with stochastic gradient descent. The authors demonstrate that their method, despite being optimized for Mean Squared Error (MSE), achieves state-of-the-art rate-distortion performance against JPEG and JPEG 2000. More significantly, it produces compressed images with substantially better perceptual quality, avoiding common artifacts like blocking and ringing that plague linear transform-based methods.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Significant Breakthrough in a Classical Problem:** The paper presents a complete, novel, and highly effective end-to-end framework for image compression that convincingly outperforms well-established standards like JPEG 2000. This represents a significant milestone, demonstrating the power of deep learning for this fundamental signal processing problem.
2.  **Methodological Rigor and Soundness:** The methodology is elegant and rigorous. The use of a continuous relaxation for the non-differentiable quantization step is a crucial and well-justified technical contribution. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, including a full entropy coder to report actual bit rates (not just entropy estimates) and comparisons using both objective (PSNR) and perceptual (MS-SSIM) metrics.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's strengths are numerous and compelling.

*   **Novelty and Impact:** This work is a pioneering demonstration that a deep neural network, optimized end-to-end, can surpass highly engineered, specialized codecs. The approach moves beyond the traditional paradigm of separately designing transforms, quantizers, and entropy coders, showing that joint optimization of all components leads to superior performance. This has had a significant impact, opening up a new and vibrant research area in learned compression.

*   **Technical Soundness:** The formulation of the rate-distortion objective and its continuous relaxation is a key insight. The authors validate that this proxy loss is a good approximation of the true discrete rate-distortion objective (Figure 4). The connection drawn to Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) in Section 3.1 is particularly insightful; it grounds the work in the broader context of generative modeling while clearly articulating the crucial differences in objectives (R-D trade-off vs. evidence lower bound maximization).

*   **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** A major strength is the reporting of actual compressed bit rates from a functional CABAC-based entropy coder. This is a higher standard of evidence than much related work that relies on entropy estimates, which can be unreliable. The quantitative comparisons on the standard Kodak dataset show superior performance over JPEG and JPEG 2000 in both PSNR and, most dramatically, MS-SSIM. The qualitative results presented (Figures 5, 6, and in the appendix) are striking and provide powerful, intuitive evidence for the method's superiority. The fact that the model produces such perceptually pleasing results while being trained on MSE is a remarkable and important finding, suggesting the model learns a representation that aligns well with perceptually relevant image structures.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

*   **Role of GDN:** The discussion on the specific contribution of the GDN nonlinearity is interesting but presented as preliminary. The paper would be strengthened by a more direct ablation study comparing GDN to more standard nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU, GeLU) within the same framework. While the current text notes that GDN appears more parameter-efficient, a quantitative comparison of R-D performance for models with comparable complexity would make this point more concrete.

*   **Variable-Rate Compression:** The paper notes that a separate model is trained for each point on the rate-distortion curve (each λ). This is computationally expensive for training and less practical for deployment than a single model that can operate at multiple bit rates. While this is a common limitation in early learned compression works, a brief discussion of potential avenues for achieving variable-rate compression within this framework would be valuable for future work (e.g., conditioning the network on λ, or using scalable representations).

*   **Architectural Justification:** The appendix mentions that the network architecture choices are "somewhat ad-hoc". While understandable, a brief justification for the design (e.g., why three stages? why these specific downsampling factors?) could provide helpful intuition for readers looking to build upon this work.

### **Questions for the Authors**

Answering these questions could further clarify aspects of the work.

1.  **Optimization Stability:** Could you comment on the training stability of the rate-distortion loss? Did you encounter any particular challenges in balancing the rate and distortion terms during optimization, especially for the extreme values of λ that correspond to very low or very high bit rates?

2.  **Entropy Model:** The entropy model assumes the latent coefficients are marginally independent. Have you investigated the residual dependencies in the latent space after the analysis transform? Do you believe a more sophisticated entropy model that captures spatial or cross-channel context could lead to further significant rate savings?

3.  **Computational Complexity:** How does the encoding and decoding time of your model compare to a software implementation of JPEG 2000 (e.g., OpenJPEG) on a CPU and/or GPU? While training is an offline cost, inference speed is a critical factor for practical deployment.
