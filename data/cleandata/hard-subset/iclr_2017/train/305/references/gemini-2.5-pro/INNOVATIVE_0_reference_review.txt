### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel, end-to-end framework for optimizing a lossy image compression system using deep learning. The core contribution is the formulation of the classical rate-distortion optimization problem in a way that is amenable to stochastic gradient descent. The proposed model replaces the fixed linear transforms of traditional codecs (like DCT in JPEG or wavelets in JPEG 2000) with a cascade of learned nonlinear transformations, specifically using convolution and a bio-inspired Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) nonlinearity.

To overcome the non-differentiability of the quantization step, the authors propose a clever continuous relaxation, replacing quantization with additive uniform noise during training. This allows them to jointly optimize the parameters of the analysis (encoder) and synthesis (decoder) transforms to minimize a weighted sum of rate (estimated via differential entropy) and distortion (MSE). The paper demonstrates that this approach yields a compression model that is not only competitive with JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of PSNR, but produces images with vastly superior perceptual quality, eliminating common artifacts like blocking and ringing.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that presents a bold and highly novel approach to a classical and important problem. My recommendation for acceptance is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Conceptual Novelty and Elegance:** The paper successfully reframes the entire image compression pipeline as an end-to-end learning problem. The formulation, particularly the continuous relaxation of quantization to enable gradient-based training, is an elegant and significant technical contribution. This work represents a fundamental shift away from the traditional paradigm of separately engineered compression components.

2.  **Impressive Empirical Results and Visual Quality:** The method produces compressed images that are perceptually far superior to those from established standards like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The fact that this is achieved while optimizing for a simple MSE metric is remarkable, and strongly suggests that the learned nonlinear representations capture the statistics of natural images in a much more meaningful way than linear transforms. This is not an incremental improvement; it is a qualitative leap in the nature of compression artifacts.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this paper is its ambitious re-imagining of the compression problem. By learning the transforms from data, the model adapts to the complex statistics of natural images, rather than relying on a fixed, general-purpose basis. The choice of the GDN nonlinearity, motivated by computational neuroscience, appears to be a crucial element, enabling the model to achieve high performance with a relatively compact architecture. This provides a compelling example of how principles from a different field can inspire powerful new machine learning models.

The technical solution to the optimization problem is also a major strength. The use of additive noise as a differentiable proxy for quantization is a simple yet powerful idea that makes the entire framework feasible. The authors provide a clear validation (Fig. 4) showing this approximation is effective, which is crucial for the credibility of the training procedure. Furthermore, the insightful connection drawn to Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) helps place this work within the broader context of modern generative modeling, while also clearly articulating the key differences in objectives (exploring the R-D curve vs. maximizing the evidence lower bound).

The empirical validation is thorough and convincing. Rather than relying solely on entropy estimates, the authors implement a full entropy code (CABAC) and report actual bit rates, making the comparison to existing codecs fair and complete. The visual results presented (Figs. 5, 6) are striking. The proposed method degrades gracefully, preserving smooth contours and sharp edges while simplifying texture, which is far more visually pleasing than the blocky and ringing artifacts of JPEG and JPEG 2000. This subjective improvement is quantitatively supported by a large gain in the MS-SSIM perceptual metric (Fig. 7), confirming that the visual superiority is not just anecdotal. While the model is not uniformly superior in PSNR across all images and bit rates, the fact that it is highly competitive—and often better, especially at low rates—while offering huge perceptual gains makes it a clear success. I value the qualitative leap in output far more than a marginal gain in PSNR.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While my assessment is highly positive, the following points could help strengthen the paper further. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from my recommendation to accept.

1.  **Architectural Ablation:** The paper mentions that the architecture (number of filters, stages, etc.) was "somewhat ad-hoc". While a full exploration is beyond the scope of one paper, a brief discussion or simple ablation study could provide valuable insight. For instance, how critical is the three-stage design? What is the performance impact of using fewer or more channels? This would help the community understand the key architectural drivers of performance.

2.  **Deeper Analysis of GDN:** The discussion provides a preliminary comparison of GDN to ReLUs, suggesting GDN is more parameter-efficient. This is a very interesting point. If possible, a more quantitative comparison or a deeper qualitative analysis of what GDN enables would be welcome. Does it learn to perform local contrast normalization in a way that ReLUs cannot? Visualizing the feature maps after the GDN layers might offer some intuition.

3.  **Complexity Analysis:** For practical applicability, a discussion of the computational complexity (e.g., in MACs per pixel) for the encoder and decoder would be very useful. The paper notes that encoding/decoding is "efficient," but quantifying this relative to a fast JPEG 2000 implementation would be a valuable addition.

### Questions for Authors

To clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  The model is trained separately for each trade-off parameter λ, resulting in a different set of network weights for each point on the rate-distortion curve. This seems computationally intensive and means a practical system would need to store multiple models. Have you considered approaches to learn a single model that can operate at multiple bit rates, as explored in the Toderici et al. (2016) paper you cite?

2.  The model was trained on natural images from ImageNet. How well do you expect it to generalize to out-of-distribution data, such as medical images, computer-generated graphics, or screen content, which have very different statistics?

3.  Could you provide more intuition on why optimizing for MSE produces such perceptually pleasing results? Is it primarily the power of the GDN transform, or does the end-to-end optimization of the rate-distortion objective inherently encourage the model to discard high-frequency "noise-like" textures first, which aligns well with human perception?
