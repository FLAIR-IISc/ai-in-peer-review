### Summary of Contributions
This paper presents a novel framework for end-to-end optimization of a lossy image compression system. The proposed model is an autoencoder-like architecture consisting of a non-linear analysis transform, a quantizer, and a non-linear synthesis transform. The transforms are built from cascades of convolutions and a biologically-inspired Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) non-linearity. The core technical innovation is a method for optimizing the rate-distortion objective using stochastic gradient descent despite the non-differentiable nature of quantization. This is achieved by replacing the quantizer with additive uniform noise during training, creating a continuous and differentiable proxy loss function that effectively approximates both the rate (entropy) and distortion terms. The authors rigorously evaluate their method by implementing a full entropy coding scheme (based on CABAC) to report actual bit rates. They demonstrate that their system, optimized for Mean Squared Error (MSE), significantly outperforms standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of rate-distortion performance, particularly when measured by the perceptual metric MS-SSIM. The qualitative results are a key strength, showing that the compressed images exhibit far more natural-looking artifacts than traditional methods.

### Decision and Key Reasons
**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Effective Optimization Framework:** The paper introduces a principled and effective method to train a deep compression model end-to-end for the classical rate-distortion objective. The continuous relaxation of quantization via additive noise is a clever solution that allows for gradient-based optimization of a previously intractable problem.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance and Superior Visual Quality:** The method achieves compelling results, outperforming strong baselines like JPEG 2000 on both objective (PSNR) and, most notably, perceptual (MS-SSIM) metrics. The visual examples provided are striking; the model learns to discard information in a perceptually pleasing way, avoiding the typical blocking and ringing artifacts of linear transform codes and representing a significant step forward in compression quality.

### Supporting Arguments
The paper's approach is well-motivated by the limitations of traditional compression schemes, where components are designed and optimized separately. By formulating the problem in an end-to-end learning framework, the authors allow the transform, quantization strategy (implicitly), and probability model to be jointly optimized for the dataset statistics.

The technical execution is sound. The connection to Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) is insightful, clarifying both the similarities in the objective function and the crucial differences that make this work a compression method rather than a generative model (i.e., optimizing for the R-D trade-off via λ). The choice of the GDN non-linearity is well-justified by the authors' previous work on density modeling, suggesting it provides an efficient inductive bias for natural image statistics.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. A major strength is the reporting of actual compressed bit rates using a real arithmetic coder, rather than relying solely on entropy estimates, which can be misleading. This demonstrates the practical feasibility of the entire compression pipeline. The evaluation on the standard Kodak dataset and the consistent, significant gains shown in the MS-SSIM metric provide strong evidence for the claims of improved perceptual quality. The visual results in Figures 5 and 6 speak for themselves and strongly support the quantitative findings.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement
These points are intended to be constructive suggestions to further strengthen the work and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

1.  **Ablation Study on Non-linearity:** The paper argues for the efficiency of the GDN/IGDN transforms. While preliminary results with other activations (like ReLU) are mentioned in the discussion, a more formal ablation study would be very valuable. Including a rate-distortion curve for an identical architecture using a more standard non-linearity (e.g., ReLU or LeakyReLU) would precisely quantify the contribution of the GDN transform to the final performance and model compactness.
2.  **Computational Complexity:** For practical applications, the computational cost of encoding and decoding is a critical factor. The paper states that the process is "efficient" but does not provide quantitative figures. It would significantly improve the paper to report encoding/decoding times (e.g., ms per image on a specific GPU/CPU) and compare them to the software implementations of JPEG and JPEG 2000 used in the experiments.
3.  **Single Model for Multiple Bit Rates:** A practical drawback of the current method is the need to train a separate model for each point on the rate-distortion curve (i.e., for each λ). This is computationally expensive and inflexible. It would be beneficial to discuss potential avenues for developing a single, rate-adaptive model. For instance, could the model be conditioned on a rate parameter λ, allowing a single network to operate across the entire R-D curve? A brief discussion of this future direction would be welcome.

### Questions for Authors
1.  The entropy model is based on non-parametric marginal distributions for each channel, which are then fed into a CABAC encoder. This model does not exploit spatial dependencies within the latent representation. More recent learned compression methods often use powerful autoregressive models as priors to capture such dependencies. Could you speculate on the potential performance gain if your framework were combined with a more sophisticated, learned spatial context model for entropy coding?
2.  The approximate inverse transform, IGDN, is motivated by the LISTA algorithm and uses a single iteration. How sensitive is the final reconstruction quality to the accuracy of this inverse? For instance, what is the reconstruction error of `gs(ga(x))` without any quantization, and does improving this "inversion error" correlate with better rate-distortion performance after quantization is introduced?
3.  The experiments show that optimizing for MSE leads to surprisingly large gains in MS-SSIM. Your prior work optimized for a perceptual metric directly. Could you provide some intuition as to why MSE optimization with this particular architecture (deep, non-linear transforms) yields such perceptually pleasant results, in contrast to MSE optimization with linear transforms which leads to severe artifacts?
