### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel, end-to-end optimized framework for lossy image compression. The core of the method is a nonlinear transform coder composed of an analysis transform, a uniform quantizer, and a synthesis transform. The transforms are implemented as deep convolutional networks incorporating a specialized nonlinearity known as generalized divisive normalization (GDN). A key contribution is the optimization strategy: to overcome the non-differentiability of quantization, the authors propose a continuous relaxation of the rate-distortion objective by substituting the quantizer with additive uniform noise. This allows for joint optimization of all model parameters via stochastic gradient descent. The paper thoughtfully relates this framework to variational autoencoders (VAEs), highlighting key conceptual differences. Crucially, the authors go beyond reporting entropy estimates and implement a context-adaptive arithmetic entropy coder (CABAC) to provide actual compressed bit rates. The results demonstrate that the proposed method often outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of PSNR and consistently achieves superior perceptual quality as measured by MS-SSIM, producing images with significantly fewer visual artifacts.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

1.  **Lack of Methodological Rigor and Clarity:** The paper suffers from numerous ambiguities and omissions in the description of the methodology. Critical details necessary for reproducibility are either relegated to the appendix, poorly explained, or missing entirely. The mathematical notation is frequently imprecise and contains formatting errors.
2.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation and Presentation:** The experimental setup has significant weaknesses, most notably the use of different model architectures for different rate points, which is not adequately justified. Furthermore, the paper is marred by an exceptionally high number of formatting errors, particularly in the bibliography, which severely undermines the perceived quality and care of the work.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

My decision is based on a meticulous examination of the paper, which, despite its promising ideas, fails to meet the stringent standards of rigor and clarity expected for publication at ICLR.

**1. Methodological Flaws and Lack of Clarity:**

*   **Ambiguous Notation and Formatting:** The paper is littered with notational and formatting errors that impede understanding. For instance, the square root in Equations (3) and (4) is incorrectly typeset as `12` instead of `^(1/2)`. In Equation (13), the identity matrix is denoted by `1`, which is non-standard and confusing. In Equation (5), the upsampling operation is defined to be zero for non-integer coordinates, a vague description that could imply different padding schemes. The term `ZM` in Equation (10) is an apparent typo for `Z`. Such errors, while minor individually, collectively suggest a lack of careful preparation.
*   **Omission of Critical Details from Main Text:** Several crucial design choices are mentioned only in the appendix, yet they are fundamental to the model's implementation. For example, Section 3's notation `ψ(i)` suggests a unique probability model for *every* latent coefficient, which seems computationally prohibitive. The appendix clarifies that parameters are shared across spatial locations within a channel, a vital detail that should be stated upfront in the main methodology section. Similarly, the facts that GDN exponents were fixed and weight matrices were forced to be symmetric are essential details absent from the main text.
*   **Insufficient Detail for Reproducibility:** The description of the non-parametric density model is incomplete. The authors state they used a "heuristic to adapt the range of the spline approximation," but this heuristic is never described. Furthermore, the appendix lists a series of optimization techniques (DCT parameterization, specific GDN parameter constraints, filter renormalization) that appear essential for successful training but are presented as secondary implementation details.

**2. Weaknesses in Experimental Setup and Reporting:**

*   **Inconsistent Model Architecture:** The authors state that for higher bit rates, they "increased the network capacity by increasing the number of channels." This implies that different rate points on the R-D curves are generated by entirely different, separately trained models. This is a significant limitation, as it means the method does not provide a single, scalable codec. This fact is underplayed in the main text and contrasts unfavorably with methods that seek a single representation for all rate points, a weakness the authors themselves point out in Toderici et al. (2016).
*   **Unsubstantiated Claims:** The discussion includes "preliminary results" suggesting GDN is more efficient than standard nonlinearities like ReLUs. However, these claims are explicitly labeled "provisional." Speculative or preliminary findings are not a substitute for a rigorous ablation study, which is necessary to substantiate the paper's claims about the benefits of its chosen architecture.
*   **Presentation Quality:** The bibliography is in a state of extreme disarray. It contains garbled entries (e.g., `Ballé et al\.,? 2015`), duplicate citations, inconsistent formatting, and missing information. This level of sloppiness is unacceptable and reflects poorly on the entire submission.

While the core idea of end-to-end optimization with a continuous relaxation is strong, the execution and presentation of the work are not. The accumulation of these many small-to-medium-sized flaws makes the paper difficult to trust and nearly impossible to reproduce, leading me to recommend rejection.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended to help improve the manuscript and are not core to my decision, which rests on the issues detailed above.

*   The connection drawn to VAEs in Section 3.1 is a major strength of the paper. It is well-argued, insightful, and clearly situates the work within the broader machine learning literature.
*   The implementation of a full entropy coder to report actual bit rates is highly commendable. This practice should be a standard in the field, and the authors are right to emphasize it.
*   The discussion in the appendix regarding the challenges of summarizing rate-distortion curves across a dataset is excellent and demonstrates a sophisticated understanding of the evaluation problem.
*   To strengthen the paper, I strongly recommend a complete overhaul of the presentation, starting with a professionally formatted bibliography and a thorough proofreading of all mathematical notation. Key methodological details from the appendix should be integrated into the main body of the paper. Finally, the claims about the superiority of GDN should either be substantiated with a proper ablation study or removed.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you please provide a precise description of the heuristic used to adapt the range of the piecewise linear density models during training?
2.  What was the rationale for training different model architectures for different rate regimes? Have you attempted to train a single, high-capacity model that can operate across the entire R-D curve by varying λ, and if so, how did its performance compare?
3.  The paper simplifies the GDN transform by fixing exponents and enforcing weight symmetry. What was the impact of these constraints on performance compared to the more general GDN formulation from your prior work? Was this simplification necessary for stable training?
