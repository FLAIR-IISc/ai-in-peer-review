### 1. Summary

This paper introduces a novel, end-to-end framework for optimizing a lossy image compression system using deep learning techniques. The core contribution is a complete codec, comprising a nonlinear analysis transform (encoder), a quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform (decoder), which are jointly optimized to minimize a rate-distortion objective. The authors make several key contributions:
1.  They design an expressive encoder/decoder architecture based on a cascade of convolutions and a biologically-inspired nonlinearity, Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN).
2.  They propose a clever and principled method to overcome the non-differentiability of quantization during training by replacing it with additive uniform noise, which serves as a continuous and differentiable proxy for both the rate (via differential entropy) and distortion terms.
3.  They provide a thoughtful analysis of the relationship between their rate-distortion framework and Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), highlighting crucial differences in objectives.
4.  Crucially, unlike much related work that only reports entropy estimates, they implement a full entropy code (based on CABAC) and report actual compressed bit rates, allowing for direct and fair comparison with standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000.

The empirical results are impressive. While optimized for MSE, the method produces images that are perceptually far superior to JPEG and JPEG 2000, replacing blocking and ringing artifacts with more natural-looking smoothing. This qualitative improvement is supported by quantitative gains in the MS-SSIM metric across all tested bit rates. The method is also competitive or superior in terms of PSNR, especially at lower bit rates.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Clear Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Methodological Significance and Rigor:** The paper presents a complete, well-motivated, and technically sound solution to a classic problem. The use of additive noise as a differentiable proxy for quantization is an elegant and effective technique that enables true end-to-end optimization. The scientific rigor is exceptionally high, demonstrated by the implementation of an actual entropy coder to report real-world bit rates, a critical step often omitted in related machine learning literature.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance:** The results convincingly demonstrate a significant leap forward in compression quality. The visual examples are striking; the proposed method avoids the typical artifacts of linear transform codes and produces reconstructions that are far more plausible and pleasant to the human eye. This is a substantial achievement, and the strong performance on the perceptual MS-SSIM metric provides quantitative backing for these visual observations.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's strengths are numerous and justify a strong recommendation for acceptance.

First, the problem formulation is clear and well-grounded in classical rate-distortion theory. The authors successfully bridge the gap between this theory and modern deep learning optimization. The continuous relaxation technique described in Section 3 is the central technical innovation. By showing that this proxy is a good empirical fit for the true discrete rate and distortion (Figure 4), the authors validate their entire training procedure. This makes the work not just an engineering success but a methodological contribution.

Second, the paper excels in its contextualization within the literature. The discussion in Section 3.1, which carefully distinguishes the rate-distortion objective from the VAE objective, is particularly insightful. The authors correctly point out that VAEs typically optimize a lower bound on the data likelihood (equivalent to minimizing R+D where λ is fixed and large), whereas compression requires sweeping the R-D curve by varying λ. This nuanced discussion elevates the paper and clarifies its contribution relative to generative modeling.

Third, the experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors use a standard dataset (Kodak) and compare against widely used benchmarks (JPEG, JPEG 2000). The most commendable aspect is the reporting of actual bit rates. This avoids the potentially misleading comparison between theoretical entropy and practical bit rates and demonstrates that the proposed system is a viable, complete compression pipeline. The visual results presented in Figures 5 and 6 are compelling evidence of the method's superiority. The artifacts generated by the learned model (primarily smoothing and loss of fine texture) are far less objectionable than the structured blocking and ringing artifacts of JPEG/JPEG 2000. The fact that this strong perceptual performance was achieved while optimizing for MSE is remarkable and speaks to the power of the learned nonlinear representation.

### 4. Additional Feedback and Questions

The paper is excellent, but the following points could help strengthen it further and provide avenues for future work. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from my decision to accept.

*   **Rate-Adaptability:** A significant practical limitation is that a separate model must be trained for each trade-off parameter λ, resulting in a distinct set of transforms for each point on the rate-distortion curve. This is computationally expensive and makes deployment difficult, as a practical codec needs to smoothly cover a range of bit rates. In contrast, traditional codecs and some competing learned methods (e.g., Toderici et al., 2016) use a single model. A discussion of this limitation and potential paths toward a single, rate-adaptive model would be valuable.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The paper states that encoding/decoding is "efficient" but does not provide quantitative metrics. For practical application, details on computational cost (e.g., MACs/pixel, encoding/decoding time on CPU/GPU) and model size are crucial. How does the complexity compare to a software implementation of JPEG 2000?
*   **Ablation of GDN:** The use of GDN is well-motivated by prior work from the authors on density modeling. However, the discussion (Section 5) on its necessity compared to simpler nonlinearities like ReLUs is noted as "provisional". A more formal ablation study, even on a smaller scale, comparing GDN to ReLU/leaky ReLU within the same architecture would strengthen the claim that GDN is particularly efficient for this task.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the importance of the GDN/IGDN nonlinearities? Your preliminary results suggest they are more parameter-efficient than ReLUs. Do you believe this is due to their specific mathematical form (divisive normalization) being a better inductive bias for natural image statistics, or could it be related to other properties like smoothness?
2.  Have you considered methods for training a single, rate-adaptive model? For instance, could λ be provided as an input to the network, or could the latent representation be progressively quantized or pruned to achieve different rates with a single set of transforms?
3.  Regarding the entropy model, you use a simple spatial raster scan and adapt probabilities within each channel. Have you explored more sophisticated context models that leverage spatial neighborhood information in the latent space (similar to modern context models in standard codecs) to further improve rate?
