### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel, end-to-end optimized framework for lossy image compression. The core contribution is a deep learning model, structured as a nonlinear analysis/synthesis transform pair (akin to an autoencoder), that is trained to minimize a rate-distortion objective. The authors overcome the key challenge of non-differentiable quantization during training by introducing a continuous relaxation, replacing the rounding operation with additive uniform noise. This allows for joint optimization of the entire pipeline—from image to compressed representation and back to image—using stochastic gradient descent. The architecture itself is notable for its use of Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN), a biologically-inspired nonlinearity, which the authors have previously shown to be effective for density modeling. The framework is not just a theoretical model; the authors implement a complete compression system, including a context-adaptive binary arithmetic coder (CABAC), to report actual bit rates on standard benchmark datasets. The main claims are that this method surpasses traditional codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of PSNR, and, more significantly, produces visually superior images with far more natural-looking artifacts, a claim supported by large gains in the MS-SSIM perceptual metric.

### **Recommendation: Accept**

This is a strong and significant paper that should be accepted. It provides a groundbreaking and principled framework for applying deep learning to the classical problem of image compression. The key reasons for this recommendation are:

1.  **Methodological Innovation and Soundness:** The continuous relaxation of the quantization step is an elegant and effective solution to a major obstacle in training such systems. This enables true end-to-end optimization, a significant departure from the separately-tuned modules of traditional codecs.
2.  **Empirical Strength and Rigor:** The paper's claims are thoroughly supported by compelling results. The authors do not merely report entropy estimates but implement a full entropy coder to measure actual bit rates. Comparisons are made against strong, standard baselines (JPEG, JPEG 2000) on a widely used benchmark (Kodak dataset), demonstrating superior performance in both objective (PSNR) and perceptual (MS-SSIM, visual inspection) terms. The qualitative results are particularly striking and convincing.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper is exceptionally well-executed. The problem of jointly optimizing rate and distortion is fundamental to compression, and traditional methods have long relied on heuristic, separate optimization of transforms, quantizers, and entropy coders. This work shows that a unified, data-driven approach can discover transforms that are far better suited for the task.

The connection drawn to Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) in Section 3.1 is insightful. It situates the work within a familiar modern machine learning context while clearly articulating the crucial differences. Specifically, it highlights that compression is about optimizing the rate-distortion *trade-off* (finding the entire R-D curve via λ), whereas generative modeling is typically concerned with maximizing the likelihood (the λ → ∞ regime). This distinction clarifies the paper's objective and contribution.

A major strength is the demonstration of a complete, practical system. Many learning-based papers stop at reporting entropy as a proxy for rate. By implementing an actual arithmetic coder, the authors close the gap between theory and practice, making their bit rate comparisons to JPEG/JPEG 2000 direct and fair. The results are impressive: the method not only achieves better PSNR at lower bit rates but also fundamentally changes the nature of compression artifacts. Instead of the blockiness of JPEG or the ringing of JPEG 2000, the artifacts are smoother and more semantic, resembling a painterly simplification rather than a signal processing failure. This subjective improvement, quantified by MS-SSIM, is arguably the most impactful result of the paper and points to the power of learned representations to capture perceptually relevant image structure.

### **Additional Feedback and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the paper is excellent, the following points could further strengthen it:

*   **Ablation Study on GDN:** The authors motivate the use of GDN with prior work on density estimation and suggest it is more efficient than standard nonlinearities like ReLU. However, this claim is only supported by "preliminary results" mentioned in the discussion. A small ablation study comparing GDN/IGDN to a more standard CNN architecture (e.g., using ReLU or LeakyReLU) with a comparable number of parameters would be highly valuable. This would help disentangle the benefits of the end-to-end optimization framework itself from the specific architectural choice of GDN.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The paper states that encoding and decoding are "efficient" but provides no quantitative data. For a compression algorithm, practical viability depends heavily on computational cost. A comparison of encoding/decoding times or FLOPs/pixel against software implementations of JPEG and JPEG 2000 would be a very useful addition and would help assess the real-world applicability of the method.
*   **Variable Rate Compression:** The current framework requires training a separate model for each point on the rate-distortion curve (i.e., for each λ). This is a practical limitation. While addressing this is likely beyond the scope of this single paper, it would be beneficial to explicitly acknowledge this limitation in the discussion and perhaps gesture towards future work on single models that can produce variable bitrates.
*   **Entropy Model Context:** The entropy model uses marginal probabilities for each channel, adapted across spatial locations. This is a relatively simple context model. Modern codecs use more sophisticated spatial context models (e.g., predicting the probability of a coefficient from its causal neighbors). Integrating a more powerful, learned context model could potentially yield further gains in compression efficiency. This could be noted as a promising direction for future research.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the claim that GDN is more efficient than other nonlinearities for this task? Specifically, could you provide a quantitative comparison (e.g., PSNR at a matched bit rate) between your proposed architecture and one where GDN/IGDN is replaced with a more standard block like Conv-ReLU-Conv, while keeping parameter counts roughly equivalent?
2.  The continuous relaxation with additive uniform noise is a central component of your method. This approximation is known to be most accurate for fine quantization (high bit rates). Did you observe any training instability or degradation in the approximation's quality in the very low bit rate regime, where quantization is coarse?
3.  Regarding computational performance: Could you provide approximate figures for encoding and decoding complexity (e.g., in MACs per pixel or milliseconds per image on a specific hardware platform) and compare them to a library implementation of JPEG 2000?
