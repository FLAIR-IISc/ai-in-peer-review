### Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

**Summary**

This paper presents an end-to-end learning framework for lossy image compression. The proposed model is an autoencoder-like architecture where the analysis and synthesis transforms are composed of convolutional layers and a specialized non-linearity, Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). To overcome the non-differentiability of quantization, the authors introduce a continuous relaxation by replacing the quantizer with additive uniform noise during training. This allows for joint optimization of the transforms and a probabilistic model of the latent codes for rate-distortion performance using stochastic gradient descent. The authors claim that their method outperforms standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000, particularly in terms of perceptual quality, producing images with more natural-looking artifacts at low bit rates.

**Decision: Reject**

I recommend rejecting this paper in its current form. While the proposed approach is interesting and the qualitative results are visually compelling, the work suffers from two significant methodological weaknesses that undermine its central claims:

1.  The core optimization relies on a continuous relaxation of the rate-distortion objective whose validity and impact on the final solution are not sufficiently justified. The paper acknowledges a bias in the rate approximation but dismisses its effect on the optimization without rigorous evidence.
2.  The empirical comparison to established baselines is not entirely fair due to the use of a self-admittedly simplistic entropy coder. This makes it difficult to ascertain whether the reported performance gains stem from the novel transform or are confounded by differences in entropy coding efficiency.

**Supporting Arguments**

The primary contribution of this work is an end-to-end optimization procedure for a learned compression model. The soundness of this procedure is therefore paramount. The authors' method of replacing quantization with additive uniform noise is a clever engineering solution to enable gradient-based learning. However, its theoretical and practical implications are not adequately explored. Figure 4 shows that the proxy for rate (differential entropy) is a "somewhat positively biased estimate of the discrete entropy for the coarser quantization regime." This is precisely the low-bit-rate regime where the method claims its most significant visual advantages. The authors argue that a constant bias simply alters the effective value of λ, but this is an oversimplification. It is not clear that the bias is constant, and more importantly, a biased gradient estimator can lead the optimization to a suboptimal point for the true discrete objective. A more thorough analysis is needed to demonstrate that the gradients of the relaxed loss function are sufficiently aligned with the gradients (or sub-gradients) of the actual rate-distortion objective. Without this, the claim of "end-to-end optimization" is weakened, as the model may be optimizing a proxy that does not faithfully represent the target problem.

Secondly, the paper's claims of superior rate-distortion performance are not fully supported by the experimental setup. While the authors are to be commended for implementing an actual entropy code rather than relying solely on entropy estimates, they state that their CABAC implementation "only crudely exploits spatial adaptation of the probability model compared to existing coding methods such as JPEG 2000." JPEG 2000's performance relies heavily on its sophisticated, context-aware EBCOT entropy coding scheme. Comparing a novel transform paired with a simple entropy coder against a standard transform paired with a highly optimized one introduces a significant confounding variable. The reported bit rates for the proposed method may be artificially inflated due to this suboptimal coding. Consequently, it is impossible to isolate the true performance of the learned nonlinear transform. A fairer comparison would require a more sophisticated entropy coding backend or, at a minimum, a detailed analysis of the statistics of the quantized latent variables to argue why a simple coder is sufficient. Furthermore, the paper notes that PSNR improvements over JPEG 2000 are not consistent across all test images, which tempers the claims of general superiority.

Finally, the paper heavily relies on the use of the bio-inspired GDN nonlinearity. While prior work from the authors has shown its effectiveness in density modeling, its specific contribution to compression performance here is not clearly isolated. The claim that other nonlinearities like ReLUs "generally require a substantially larger number of model parameters/stages" is presented as provisional and based on "a limited set of experiments." To justify the inclusion of this non-standard and more complex module, a more rigorous ablation study comparing it to established activation functions within the same architectural framework is necessary.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The paper is well-written, and the connection drawn to VAEs is insightful. The visual quality of the compressed images is impressive and suggests that learned, nonlinear approaches hold significant promise for image compression. My critique is aimed at increasing the rigor of the work to match the significance of its claims.

1.  **Practicality:** The proposed method requires training a separate model for each point on the rate-distortion curve (i.e., for each λ). This is a significant practical disadvantage compared to codecs like JPEG 2000, which offer quality and resolution scalability from a single codestream. This limitation should be acknowledged and discussed.
2.  **Complexity:** Could you provide details on the computational complexity of the proposed method (e.g., multiply-adds per pixel for encode/decode, parameter count) and compare it to established codecs? This is a critical factor for any practical compression system.
3.  **Entropy Bias:** Can you provide a more formal argument or empirical evidence to show that the bias in the rate estimation during training does not lead to transforms that are suboptimal for the true, discrete rate-distortion objective?
4.  **Ablation Study:** To strengthen the claims regarding GDN, would it be possible to train models with identical architectures but replacing GDN/IGDN with more standard blocks (e.g., convolution followed by ReLU or other common activations) and present a direct comparison on the rate-distortion curve? This would clarify the specific benefit of your chosen nonlinearity.
