### 1. Summary

This paper introduces a complete, end-to-end framework for optimizing a lossy image compression model. The model consists of a nonlinear analysis transform (encoder) and a synthesis transform (decoder), both structured as deep convolutional networks. A key feature of the architecture is the use of a biologically-inspired nonlinearity, Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN), which prior work has shown to be effective for modeling natural image statistics.

The central contribution is the method for optimizing the entire system directly for a rate-distortion trade-off. To overcome the non-differentiability of the quantization step, which is essential for compression, the authors propose an elegant continuous relaxation. During training, they replace the quantizer with additive uniform noise. This allows the rate to be approximated by the differential entropy of the noisy representation and the distortion to be computed on the reconstructed image, making the entire loss function differentiable and trainable with stochastic gradient descent.

The authors train separate models for different points on the rate-distortion curve. Crucially, they evaluate their system not just on entropy estimates but on actual bit rates achieved by a practical entropy coder (based on CABAC). The experimental results demonstrate that their method is highly competitive with, and often superior to, standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000 when measured by PSNR. More strikingly, the method produces visually superior compressed images that exhibit significantly fewer of the typical blocking or ringing artifacts, a claim supported by substantial gains in the MS-SSIM perceptual metric.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Principled and Effective End-to-End Optimization:** The paper presents a novel and methodologically sound framework for optimizing a compression system for the classical rate-distortion objective. The proposed continuous relaxation of the quantization step is a clever and effective technique that enables end-to-end training with gradient-based methods, directly addressing a fundamental challenge in learned compression.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance and Compelling Visual Quality:** The method achieves excellent empirical results, outperforming strong, widely-used baselines like JPEG 2000 on standard benchmarks. The most compelling evidence for the success of this approach is the remarkable visual quality of the compressed images. Even when optimized for MSE, the model learns to produce reconstructions that are perceptually much more pleasant than traditional methods, effectively trading off fine texture for clean, sharp edges and contours. The inclusion of a full entropy coder makes the reported bit rates concrete and the comparisons fair.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's strength lies in its combination of a well-motivated methodology and rigorous, impressive empirical validation.

**Methodology:** The formulation of the problem is clear and builds directly from classical rate-distortion theory. The main innovation—using additive uniform noise as a differentiable proxy for quantization—is well-justified. It provides a continuous density for which differential entropy can be computed as a proxy for rate, and it models the statistical effect of quantization error for the distortion term. The authors validate this approximation empirically in Figure 4, showing a reasonable correspondence between the training objective and the final discrete performance, which strengthens confidence in the method. The discussion connecting the framework to Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) is insightful, clarifying both the similarities and the crucial differences, particularly that compression explicitly optimizes for the R-D trade-off (controlled by λ) rather than simply maximizing model evidence.

**Architecture and Evaluation:** The choice of the GDN nonlinearity is well-motivated by prior work on natural image statistics, and the preliminary results mentioned in the discussion suggest it provides a more parameter-efficient architecture than standard ReLUs. The evaluation is thorough and convincing. By implementing a CABAC-based entropy coder, the authors avoid the common pitfall of relying on potentially inaccurate entropy estimates and instead report real-world bit rates. This makes the results directly comparable to existing codecs and far more meaningful. The performance is assessed on the standard Kodak dataset using both a traditional metric (PSNR/MSE, for which the model was optimized) and a perceptual one (MS-SSIM). The consistent and significant improvement in MS-SSIM (Figure 7) provides strong quantitative support for the qualitative observation that the method produces visually superior images with fewer artifacts (Figures 5 and 6).

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These suggestions are intended to help strengthen the paper further and are not critical for my decision.

*   **Computational Complexity:** The paper notes that encoding/decoding is efficient, but providing concrete numbers would be very valuable for assessing practical viability. Please consider adding runtime measurements (e.g., ms per image for a given resolution) for your encoder and decoder, and comparing them to the software implementations of JPEG/JPEG 2000 used in the evaluation.
*   **Single Model for Multiple Rates:** A practical limitation of the proposed method is the need to train and store a separate model for each point on the R-D curve. In contrast, traditional codecs can generate any bit rate from a single compressed stream. While developing a single, rate-adaptive model is a significant research challenge, it would be beneficial to explicitly discuss this limitation and potential future directions to address it (e.g., conditioning the network on λ, or using a scalable representation).
*   **Entropy Model:** The current entropy model assumes independent marginals in the latent space, which is a simplification. The performance of learned compression systems is often significantly boosted by incorporating more sophisticated, context-adaptive entropy models (e.g., autoregressive priors). While the current simple model is sufficient to demonstrate the power of the learned transforms, acknowledging this and framing it as an avenue for future performance gains would add valuable context.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   In Section 4, you state that the analysis transform's output has "half the dimensionality of the input." Could you please clarify this calculation? Given the spatial downsampling factors of 4, 2, and 2, the spatial resolution is reduced by a factor of 16. I assume the dimensionality is calculated as `height * width * channels`. It seems the large increase in channel count (e.g., to 128) more than compensates for the spatial reduction. An explicit formula would be helpful.
*   You mention that for higher bit rates (larger λ), you increased the network capacity (number of channels). Why is more capacity required for higher bit-rate models? Is the intuition that accurately reconstructing fine details (required for low distortion) necessitates a more powerful synthesis network?
*   The GDN/IGDN transforms seem key to the model's performance. The paper notes that the exponents in the GDN formulation were fixed for simplicity. Did you perform any experiments where these exponents were also learned? It would be interesting to know if this provided any additional benefit or if the fixed-exponent version is sufficient for this compression task.
