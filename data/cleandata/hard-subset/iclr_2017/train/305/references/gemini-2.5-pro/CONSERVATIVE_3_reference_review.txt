### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel end-to-end framework for optimizing a lossy image compression system. The proposed model is structured as a nonlinear transform coder, akin to a variational autoencoder, consisting of an analysis transform (encoder), a quantizer, and a synthesis transform (decoder). The key challenge of the non-differentiable quantization step is addressed by replacing it with additive uniform noise during training, enabling gradient-based optimization of the entire system. The transforms themselves are composed of a cascade of convolutions and a specialized nonlinear activation function known as Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The system is trained to minimize a rate-distortion loss for different trade-off parameters (λ). The authors evaluate their method against JPEG and JPEG 2000 on standard test sets, demonstrating competitive or superior performance in terms of PSNR and substantial improvements in perceptual quality as measured by MS-SSIM.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Insufficient Justification for the Core Methodological Approach:** The central technique of using additive uniform noise as a proxy for quantization is presented as a practical solution but lacks a rigorous validation of its impact on the optimality of the final solution. The paper's own analysis (Figure 4) reveals a systematic bias in the rate estimation, particularly at lower bit rates, which casts doubt on whether the optimization is converging to a truly rate-distortion optimal point for the discrete problem.
2.  **Inconsistent and Incomplete Empirical Evaluation:** While the results are promising, the proposed method does not consistently outperform the well-established JPEG 2000 baseline on the primary optimization metric (MSE/PSNR). The paper notes that performance is better "for most (but not all) test images," which is not a sufficiently strong result to justify adopting a significantly more complex and unconventional framework. Furthermore, the claims regarding the superiority of the GDN nonlinearity are not supported by a proper ablation study against more standard neural network components.

### Supporting Arguments

**On Methodological Soundness:** The primary innovation is the continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable end-to-end training. The authors argue that this is a valid approximation, citing that uniform noise models quantization error well in terms of marginal moments. However, for a rate-distortion optimization framework, the impact on the entropy (rate) term is equally critical. Figure 4 shows that the differential entropy of the relaxed model is a "somewhat positively biased estimate of the discrete entropy for the coarser quantization regime." The authors dismiss this by stating that a constant bias would simply alter the effective value of λ. This is an oversimplification. The bias is not shown to be constant across the distribution of coefficients or across different images, and it is most pronounced in the low-rate regime, a critical area for compression performance. An un-quantified, non-constant bias in one of the two terms of the objective function undermines the claim of performing principled rate-distortion optimization. It is unclear if the learned transforms are optimal for the actual discrete problem or merely for the relaxed proxy problem.

**On Empirical Evidence:** The core claim of a new compression method is its rate-distortion performance. When measured by PSNR (the metric the system was optimized for), the results are mixed. For a method to supplant a standard like JPEG 2000, it should demonstrate a clear and consistent advantage. The fact that it sometimes underperforms is a significant weakness. While the improvements in MS-SSIM and the qualitative visual results are impressive, they are secondary to the performance on the explicit optimization target. The pleasing visual appearance, which seems to stem from smoothing out textures, might be considered a desirable artifact, but it is an artifact nonetheless, and it is not what the system was trained to produce.

Furthermore, the paper heavily leans on the use of the GDN transform, citing its biological inspiration and efficiency in density modeling. However, the contribution of this specific architectural choice to the final compression performance is not isolated. The discussion mentions "preliminary results" suggesting GDN is more efficient than rectified linear units, but this is not sufficient. A rigorous ablation study comparing GDN/IGDN to cascades of standard convolutional layers with ReLU or other common nonlinearities, matched for parameter count or computational complexity, is necessary to substantiate the claim that this specific, less conventional component is critical to the model's success. Without this, it is difficult to distinguish the contribution of the specific architecture from the general benefit of end-to-end optimization.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper but are not the primary basis for my decision.

*   **Baselines:** For a work in this area, comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 is necessary but may no longer be sufficient to claim state-of-the-art performance. Including a comparison against a more modern, conventional codec like BPG (which is based on HEVC intra-frame coding) would provide a much stronger benchmark for the actual rate-distortion performance.
*   **Analysis of Failures:** The paper would be strengthened by an analysis of the images and bit rates where the proposed method underperforms JPEG 2000 in PSNR. Are there specific image characteristics, such as fine textures or high-frequency geometric patterns, that the model struggles to represent efficiently compared to wavelet-based methods?
*   **The VAE Connection:** The connection to VAEs is interesting but feels somewhat post-hoc. Does framing the problem in the VAE context provide any novel insights or algorithmic advantages beyond what is already derived from the classical rate-distortion perspective? The discussion of how the λ trade-off differs from the typical VAE objective is useful but could be expanded.

I would like the authors to address the following questions to clarify my understanding:

1.  Could you provide a more thorough analysis of the impact of the biased rate approximation on the final learned transforms? For instance, how do the resulting rate-distortion curves for the *discrete* system compare if you deliberately introduce a similar bias into a traditional transform coding optimization pipeline?
2.  Can you provide a quantitative ablation study comparing the GDN/IGDN architecture to an architecture of similar complexity that uses standard ReLU nonlinearities? This is crucial for supporting the claim that the choice of nonlinearity is a key contributor to your results.
3.  Given that the model was optimized for MSE, what is your hypothesis for why it so significantly outperforms the baselines on MS-SSIM? Is it an inherent property of the deep, nonlinear transform to preserve structure in a way that aligns with MS-SSIM, even when not explicitly trained to do so?
