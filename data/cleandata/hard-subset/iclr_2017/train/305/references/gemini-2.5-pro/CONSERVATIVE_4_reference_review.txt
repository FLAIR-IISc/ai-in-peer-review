### **Summary**

This paper introduces an end-to-end framework for optimizing a lossy image compression model. The model consists of a nonlinear analysis transform, a uniform quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform. The transforms are structured as a three-stage cascade of convolutions and a specialized nonlinearity called Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). A key contribution is the method for enabling gradient-based optimization despite the non-differentiable nature of quantization; the authors replace the rounding operation during training with additive uniform noise, creating a continuous and differentiable proxy loss function. This allows the entire system to be optimized to minimize a rate-distortion objective. The authors demonstrate through experiments on the Kodak dataset that their method, while trained on MSE, produces images with superior visual quality compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, which is supported by substantial gains in the MS-SSIM metric. The method also generally outperforms these established baselines in terms of PSNR, particularly at lower bit rates.

### **Decision**

**Reject.**

While the work presents an interesting and novel application of deep learning to a classical problem, the claims of superiority over established methods are not supported with sufficient rigor. The primary reasons for this decision are:
1.  The methodological justification for key architectural choices, specifically the use of the unconventional GDN nonlinearity, is weak and not substantiated by direct comparative experiments within the paper.
2.  The experimental comparison to baselines, while extensive visually, lacks the quantitative rigor standard in the compression literature, making it difficult to assess the scale and consistency of the claimed improvements.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The core idea of training a compression system end-to-end is compelling. However, the paper's execution and evaluation fall short of the high standards required to convincingly demonstrate an advance over decades of well-established research in image compression.

**1. Insufficient Justification for Architectural Choices:** The paper places significant emphasis on the GDN transform, citing its biological inspiration and prior success in density modeling. However, its specific contribution to the performance of this compression system is not clearly isolated or justified. The discussion section mentions that preliminary results with rectified linear units (ReLUs) required more parameters to achieve similar performance, but this is a critical piece of evidence that should be a formal result in the experimental section, not a provisional comment. Without a rigorous ablation study comparing GDN to standard nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU, tanh) within the same architectural framework and training procedure, it is impossible to determine if the reported gains stem from the novel nonlinearity or simply from the end-to-end optimization of a deep convolutional network. The work is presented as a new solution based on these specific transforms, yet the necessity of these transforms over more conventional ones is not proven.

**2. Lack of Rigorous Quantitative Comparison:** The evaluation against JPEG and JPEG 2000 is problematic.
*   **Baselines:** While JPEG and JPEG 2000 are canonical, they are also 2-3 decades old. A truly compelling case would require comparison against more modern and competitive conventional codecs, such as the intra-frame coding modes of HEVC or VVC, which represent the current state of the art in transform coding.
*   **Evaluation Metrics:** The authors rightly point out the flaws of PSNR and show impressive visual results and MS-SSIM scores. However, they dismiss the practice of summarizing rate-distortion performance across a dataset (Appendix 6.3). This is unconvincing. The compression field has standard practices for this, such as reporting Bjøntegaard-Delta (BD)-rate savings, which provide a concise and well-understood summary of the average bit rate reduction for the same quality. The absence of such aggregate metrics makes it difficult to gauge the overall performance of the method beyond inspecting individual plots, which, as the authors note, can vary significantly.
*   **Entropy Coding:** The paper implements a relatively simple context-adaptive arithmetic coder (CABAC). The authors themselves state it "only crudely exploits spatial adaptation" compared to the entropy coding in JPEG 2000. This introduces a significant confounding variable. Are the gains a result of a superior transform, or is the comparison skewed by differences in the entropy coding stage? A more controlled comparison would involve reporting the entropy of the quantized coefficients for both the proposed method and the baseline, thereby isolating the performance of the transform itself from the subsequent coding stage.

**3. Methodological Concerns:** The continuous relaxation of quantization via additive uniform noise is a clever engineering solution to enable training. The paper provides empirical evidence that it is a reasonable approximation (Fig. 4). However, the theoretical connection between optimizing this proxy objective and the original discrete rate-distortion problem remains heuristic. A deeper analysis of the approximation error and its effect on the final optimized solution would strengthen the methodological foundation.

### **Additional Feedback**

The following points are suggestions for improving the paper and are not central to the rejection decision.
*   The connection drawn to Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) is insightful. However, the characterization of generative models as only being relevant for the lossless (λ → ∞) regime seems overly simplistic. The trade-off in the VAE's ELBO between reconstruction and the KL-divergence term is directly analogous to the rate-distortion trade-off, and this parallel could be explored more deeply.
*   Training a separate model for each point on the rate-distortion curve is computationally expensive and impractical for many applications. The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of this approach and potential avenues for developing a single, rate-adaptive model.
*   The visual results are indeed impressive. The artifacts produced are much more natural than the blocking or ringing common to linear transform codecs. This is a strong point of the paper and suggests that even if the quantitative performance is debatable, the qualitative aspect of this research direction is highly promising.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you provide an ablation study showing the rate-distortion performance of your framework with GDN/IGDN blocks replaced by a comparable number of parameters' worth of standard convolutional layers with ReLU activations? This would help clarify the specific contribution of the GDN nonlinearity.
2.  Could you report the average BD-rate savings for both PSNR and MS-SSIM over the entire Kodak dataset against JPEG 2000? This would provide a standard, aggregate measure of performance that is currently missing.
3.  Given that the entropy coder is a potential confounding factor, can you provide a comparison based on the estimated entropy of the quantized latent representations of your model versus the quantized wavelet coefficients of JPEG 2000 for a given distortion level?
