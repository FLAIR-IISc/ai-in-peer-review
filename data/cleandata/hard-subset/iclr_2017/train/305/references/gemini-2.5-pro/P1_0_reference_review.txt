### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel, end-to-end framework for optimizing a lossy image compression system. The core contribution is a complete codec based on a nonlinear autoencoder architecture that is trained to minimize a weighted sum of rate and distortion. The architecture consists of an analysis transform (encoder) and a synthesis transform (decoder), both constructed from cascades of convolutions and a biologically-inspired nonlinearity, Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN).

A key technical innovation is the method for overcoming the non-differentiability of the quantization step, which is essential for end-to-end gradient-based optimization. The authors replace the hard quantization function with additive uniform noise during training, creating a continuous and differentiable proxy for the true rate-distortion loss. This clever relaxation allows the differential entropy of the noisy representation to approximate the discrete entropy (rate) of the quantized values, and the distortion of the reconstructed noisy representation to approximate the distortion of the quantized one.

The paper provides an insightful connection between this relaxed optimization problem and Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), while also carefully highlighting the fundamental differences in goals and formulation (rate-distortion trade-off vs. generative modeling). The evaluation is rigorous: the authors implement a full entropy coding scheme based on CABAC to report actual bit rates, rather than relying on entropy estimates. The proposed method is compared against standard codecs (JPEG and JPEG 2000) on the Kodak dataset, demonstrating superior performance in terms of PSNR at lower bit rates and substantial gains in perceptual quality (measured by MS-SSIM) across all bit rates.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Effective Optimization Framework:** The paper presents a principled and effective solution to the long-standing challenge of end-to-end optimization for lossy compression. The continuous relaxation of quantization via additive uniform noise is an elegant and well-motivated technique that successfully enables the joint optimization of the encoder, decoder, and entropy model parameters. This provides a powerful general framework for designing new compression codecs.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance and Compelling Qualitative Results:** The method demonstrates strong empirical results, outperforming the widely used JPEG 2000 standard on most images in terms of PSNR, and significantly outperforming it in terms of the perceptual metric MS-SSIM. The qualitative results are particularly striking; the compressed images exhibit much more natural-looking artifacts (e.g., smoothing of textures, simplification of contours) compared to the blocking, ringing, and aliasing artifacts characteristic of linear transform-based methods. This is a significant step forward for practical image compression.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's strength lies in its clear problem formulation, elegant technical solution, and thorough experimental validation.

The connection drawn to VAEs in Section 3.1 is a major contribution. By reformulating their loss function in the language of variational inference, the authors bridge the gap between classical information theory (rate-distortion) and modern deep generative modeling. This not only provides a deeper theoretical grounding for their method but also clarifies the subtle but crucial differences: transform coding explicitly optimizes the rate-distortion trade-off via the hyperparameter λ, whereas generative models typically aim to maximize the data log-likelihood (corresponding to the λ → ∞, or lossless, regime). This discussion is valuable for both the compression and machine learning communities.

The experimental rigor is exemplary. Many papers in this area report only entropy estimates, which can be misleading. By implementing a full CABAC-based entropy coder and reporting actual compressed file sizes, the authors demonstrate that their system is not just a theoretical model but a practical, complete compression pipeline. The validation of the continuous approximation (Figure 4) provides confidence in the training methodology, showing that the proxy loss function is a reasonable and effective surrogate for the true discrete objective. The visual results presented are not cherry-picked; the paper provides a link to a comprehensive set of results, showing consistent perceptual improvements across many images and bit rates, which strongly supports the main claims.

Finally, the use of GDN as the core nonlinearity is well-motivated by prior work on its effectiveness in modeling natural image statistics. While the paper suggests other nonlinearities might work, the efficiency of GDN seems to be a key factor in achieving high performance with a relatively shallow 3-stage architecture.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are suggestions for strengthening the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

1.  **Computational Complexity:** The paper mentions that encoding/decoding is efficient but does not provide quantitative data. A comparison of the computational complexity (e.g., MAC operations per pixel, or actual CPU/GPU runtimes) against JPEG 2000 would be a valuable addition for assessing the practical viability of the proposed method, especially for real-time or mobile applications.
2.  **Training Cost and the λ Hyperparameter:** The current method requires training a separate, specialized model for each point on the rate-distortion curve (i.e., for each λ). This is a significant practical limitation. It would be beneficial to discuss this limitation more explicitly in the main text and perhaps briefly speculate on future work that could address it, such as training a single model conditioned on a rate or quality parameter.
3.  **Architectural Sensitivity:** The appendix notes that architectural choices were "somewhat ad-hoc." The paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion or ablation study on the sensitivity of the results to architectural changes, such as the number of stages, the number of filters, or the downsampling factors. This would provide readers with a better understanding of the key design principles.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  The comparison to other nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU) in the discussion is brief and described as "provisional." Could you provide more quantitative detail on this? For example, to achieve similar PSNR/MS-SSIM, roughly how much deeper or wider did a ReLU-based network need to be compared to the GDN-based one?
2.  The entropy model is based on independent marginals for each channel, with adaptation across spatial locations via CABAC. This is simpler than the sophisticated spatial context models used in modern video codecs. Do you have an estimate of how much performance might be gained by incorporating a more powerful, spatially-aware context model into your entropy coder?
3.  The generative model defined in Eq. 13 assumes a fixed variance spherical Gaussian, where the variance is tied to λ. This implies that the model assumes the same level of uncertainty for all pixels given the latent representation. Have you considered learning the variance as part of the synthesis network, which could allow the model to allocate more "uncertainty" (and thus fewer bits) to complex, hard-to-reconstruct textures?
