### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel end-to-end learning framework for lossy image compression. The proposed model consists of a nonlinear analysis transform, a uniform quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform. The transforms are implemented as a cascade of convolutions and a biologically-inspired Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) nonlinearity. A key contribution is a method for optimizing the rate-distortion objective directly using stochastic gradient descent, despite the non-differentiable nature of quantization. This is achieved by replacing the quantizer with additive uniform noise during training, creating a continuous and differentiable proxy loss function. The authors draw an insightful connection between this relaxed objective and the objective of variational autoencoders. The method is evaluated against JPEG and JPEG 2000, demonstrating superior performance both in terms of PSNR and, more significantly, in terms of perceptual quality as measured by MS-SSIM and visual inspection. The resulting compressed images exhibit far fewer of the typical blocky or ringing artifacts associated with linear transform codes.

### Decision: Reject

While the paper presents a promising direction for learned image compression with impressive qualitative results, I must recommend rejection. My decision is based on several critical flaws that undermine the technical soundness and rigor of the work. Specifically: (1) there appears to be a fundamental mathematical error in the definition of the core GDN nonlinearity; (2) a crucial inconsistency exists in the formulation of the continuous relaxation for quantization, which is central to the optimization method; and (3) the paper suffers from an egregious lack of care in its presentation, most notably a deeply flawed bibliography.

### Supporting Arguments

1.  **Incorrect Formulation of Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN):** The paper’s core nonlinearity is the GDN transform, described in Equation (3). The equation is given as `u = w * (β + Σ γw^2)^(1/2)`. This is a multiplicative gain control. However, as its name and the cited literature (e.g., Ballé et al., 2015) imply, divisive normalization should be a *divisive* operation, where the response is divided by a normalization pool. The correct form should involve a negative exponent, such as `w / (β + Σ γw^2)^(1/2)`. This is not a minor typo; it fundamentally misrepresents the key architectural component of the model. If the implementation used the correct divisive form, then the paper fails to accurately describe the method. If the implementation used the multiplicative form as written, the connection to divisive normalization literature is misleading. This error casts serious doubt on the technical description of the work.

2.  **Inconsistent Continuous Relaxation of Quantization:** The optimization strategy hinges on replacing quantization with additive uniform noise `∆y`. The text states this noise is from `U(0, 1)`, the uniform density on the unit interval. However, the subsequent derivation relies on properties that only hold for a zero-mean noise distribution, such as `U(-0.5, 0.5)`. Specifically, Equation (10) states that the density of the relaxed variable `ỹ` evaluated at an integer `n` is equal to the probability mass of the quantized variable `q` in the bin centered at `n`. This is only true if the noise is centered on zero (i.e., `y_tilde = y + U(-0.5, 0.5)`). A non-zero mean noise (`U(0,1)`) would introduce a systematic bias, invalidating this crucial approximation. The later mention in Section 3.1 of `U(ỹi; yi, 1)` (a uniform density centered on `yi` with width 1) further contradicts the `U(0, 1)` statement. This inconsistency in a cornerstone of the proposed methodology is a significant flaw.

3.  **Lack of Scientific Rigor and Professionalism:** The paper is rife with formatting errors, notational ambiguities, and signs of extreme carelessness, which fall far below the standards expected for an ICLR publication. The bibliography is particularly egregious. It is incomplete, inconsistently formatted, and contains bizarre artifacts (e.g., `Ballé et al\.,? 2015`, `Malo and Jesús,? \Q2006\E`). Many citations are missing essential information like conference or journal names. This prevents readers from verifying sources and reflects a profound lack of diligence. Other examples include: non-standard notation for the identity matrix (`1` in Eq. 13), unconventional use of square brackets for function arguments (`L[...]` in Eq. 7), and misleading notation for the density model parameters (`ψ(i)`) that obscures the parameter sharing scheme described only in the appendix. While individual issues may be minor, their sheer volume indicates a rushed and sloppy preparation that undermines confidence in the work's overall quality.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

*   **Clarity of Notation and Terminology:** The main text should clearly state that the parameters for the marginal probability models are shared across spatial locations within each channel, rather than having a unique model for every single coefficient index `i`. Using more standard deep learning terminology, such as "strided convolution" and "transposed convolution" instead of describing the down/upsampling operations procedurally, would improve clarity.
*   **Contradictory Statements:** The appendix (Section 6.3) argues that "summaries of rate–distortion are of limited use," yet the main paper presents exactly such a summary plot in Figure 7. This is contradictory. While the discussion of the difficulties in averaging RD curves is valid, the authors should resolve this contradiction and present their summary results with the appropriate caveats.
*   **GDN/IGDN details:** The simplification of GDN (fixing exponents, symmetric `γ`) is an important detail that should be mentioned in the main text, not just the appendix, as it defines the precise model variant being used.
*   **Proofreading:** The entire manuscript requires meticulous proofreading to fix the numerous typos, grammatical errors, and formatting inconsistencies before any future submission.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the exact mathematical form of the GDN transform used in your implementation? Is it the multiplicative form presented in Equation (3), or the standard divisive form?
2.  What was the precise probability distribution used for the additive noise `∆y` during training? Was it uniform on `[0, 1]` as stated in the text, or uniform on `[-0.5, 0.5]` as required by the derivation and suggested by other parts of the text?
3.  In Equation (13), the term `(2λ)⁻¹1` appears as the covariance of a Gaussian distribution. Is `1` intended to denote the identity matrix? If so, I strongly suggest using standard notation (e.g., `I`) to avoid confusion.
