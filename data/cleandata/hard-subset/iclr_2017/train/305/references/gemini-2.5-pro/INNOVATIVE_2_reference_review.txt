### Summary

This paper presents a novel, end-to-end framework for lossy image compression based on learned nonlinear transforms. The authors replace the traditional pipeline of a linear transform, quantization, and entropy coding—whose components are typically designed and optimized separately—with a unified system akin to a deep autoencoder. A key contribution is the method for making this system trainable via stochastic gradient descent despite the non-differentiable nature of quantization. They achieve this by replacing the quantizer with additive uniform noise during training, creating a continuous and differentiable loss function that approximates the true rate-distortion objective. The paper draws an insightful connection between this relaxed objective and the evidence lower bound (ELBO) used in Variational Autoencoders (VAEs). The proposed model, featuring biologically-inspired Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) transforms, is trained on MSE but demonstrates striking qualitative improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000, producing images with far more natural-looking artifacts. These visual gains are corroborated by significant improvements in the MS-SSIM metric, and the method also achieves competitive or superior PSNR performance, particularly at lower bit rates.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a bold and impressive paper that tackles a classical engineering problem with a modern, end-to-end learning approach. My recommendation for acceptance is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The paper introduces a significant new direction for image compression. The core idea of relaxing the non-differentiable quantization step with additive noise is a simple yet powerful concept that unlocks end-to-end optimization of the rate-distortion trade-off. The formal connection drawn to VAEs is particularly insightful, grounding this practical engineering problem in the language of modern generative modeling and providing a solid theoretical motivation for the approach.
2.  **Compelling and State-of-the-Art Results:** The method doesn't just offer an interesting new idea; it delivers outstanding results. The qualitative improvement over highly-engineered standards like JPEG 2000 is dramatic. While trained on MSE, the model learns to discard information in a perceptually graceful way, preserving smooth contours and avoiding the blocky or ringing artifacts that plague linear transform codes. This is a breakthrough in its own right and demonstrates the immense potential of learning the entire compression pipeline jointly.

### Supporting Arguments

The strength of this work lies in its ambitious reframing of the image compression problem. For decades, the field has been dominated by incremental improvements to the transform coding paradigm. This paper breaks from that tradition by proposing a fundamentally different, data-driven solution.

The authors' approach to optimization is the main technical contribution. By creating a continuous proxy for the rate-distortion objective, they have developed a principled way to apply the full power of deep learning to this domain. This is far more systematic than prior related works that may use generative models but then apply ad-hoc quantization schemes. Here, the entire system—from the analysis transform to the synthesis transform and even the probability models for entropy coding—is jointly optimized for a single objective.

The experimental validation is thorough. The authors wisely do not stop at reporting entropy estimates, a common pitfall in related literature. Instead, they implement a complete compression system with a CABAC-based entropy coder and report actual bit rates on a standard benchmark dataset (Kodak). This makes their results directly and fairly comparable to existing standards. The visual evidence presented in the paper is stunning. At similar bit rates, the authors' method produces images that are substantially cleaner and more pleasant to view than those from JPEG 2000. The fact that this is achieved while optimizing for raw MSE is remarkable and suggests the learned nonlinear transforms are capturing perceptually relevant structures in natural images. This claim is further supported by the excellent performance measured by MS-SSIM.

While the model architecture itself may not be exhaustively explored, the use of GDN is an interesting choice that connects the work to computational neuroscience and demonstrates that non-standard nonlinearities can be highly effective. The paper is well-written, clear, and places its contributions effectively within the context of both classical compression and modern machine learning.

### Additional Feedback

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   The claim that GDN/IGDN transforms are more efficient than architectures with simpler nonlinearities like ReLUs is very interesting but is qualified as being based on "preliminary results." While not central to the paper's main contribution, strengthening this section with a more direct, controlled comparison would make the architectural claims more concrete.
*   The paper mentions that the architecture choices are "somewhat ad-hoc." While the current architecture clearly works well, a brief discussion of the design principles (e.g., why three stages? why these specific downsampling factors?) could provide more intuition for the reader.
*   The comparison plots in the appendix are excellent. It would be beneficial to include a summary plot in the main paper (perhaps using a metric like Bjøntegaard-delta rate) to give a concise, quantitative overview of the performance gains across the entire dataset, even with the caveats about averaging mentioned in the appendix.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  The paper states that encoding/decoding is "efficient" but provides no concrete numbers. Could you provide a rough comparison of the computational complexity (e.g., in terms of multiply-adds per pixel or wall-clock time) of your method against the JPEG 2000 implementation you compared against?
2.  A separate model is trained for each point on the rate-distortion curve (i.e., for each λ). From a practical standpoint, this is a limitation. Have you considered or experimented with methods for training a single, rate-adaptive model, for instance by making λ an input to the network?
3.  How crucial is the non-parametric, piecewise linear model for the coefficient densities? Have you experimented with simpler parametric models (e.g., a Laplacian or a Gaussian mixture model)? How much performance is gained by using the more flexible non-parametric approach?
