### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel, end-to-end framework for lossy image compression based on nonlinear transforms. The proposed model consists of an analysis transform (encoder) and a synthesis transform (decoder), both constructed from a cascade of convolutional layers and a non-standard, biologically-inspired nonlinearity called Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The core idea is to directly optimize the parameters of these transforms for a rate-distortion objective. A key technical challenge is the non-differentiability of the quantization step. The authors overcome this by proposing a continuous relaxation, replacing quantization with additive uniform noise during training, which allows for optimization via stochastic gradient descent. The paper demonstrates that this approach, when optimized for Mean Squared Error (MSE), produces images that are visually superior to JPEG and JPEG 2000, exhibiting fewer blocky or ringing artifacts. The authors support their claims with quantitative comparisons using PSNR and MS-SSIM on the Kodak dataset and other standard test images.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Insufficient Justification for Architectural Choices:** The paper's central architectural element is the Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) nonlinearity. However, the work does not provide a compelling, empirical justification for this choice over more conventional nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU) within the context of this specific compression task. The claims of GDN's efficiency are based on "preliminary results" and deemed "provisional" by the authors themselves, which is insufficient for a rigorous evaluation of the method's core components.

2.  **Weakness in the Rigor of Baseline Comparisons:** The paper claims superior rate-distortion performance over established standards like JPEG 2000. However, rate-distortion performance is a function of both the transform and the entropy coding scheme. The authors acknowledge that their implemented entropy coder "only crudely exploits spatial adaptation," which is a significant departure from the highly sophisticated, context-adaptive entropy coding used in JPEG 2000. This disparity in the entropy coding stage makes it difficult to ascertain whether the reported performance gains are due to the superiority of the proposed transform or simply reflect differences in the coding pipeline. Consequently, the quantitative comparisons, particularly for PSNR where the proposed method is not consistently superior, are not fully convincing.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this paper is the elegant formulation of the end-to-end training objective, particularly the use of additive uniform noise as a differentiable proxy for quantization. This is a valuable contribution for training such networks. The connection drawn to Variational Autoencoders is also insightful and helps position the work within the broader machine learning literature. Furthermore, the qualitative results presented are impressive; the compressed images clearly suffer from fewer of the classic artifacts that plague linear transform-based methods.

Despite these strengths, the methodological rigor has significant weaknesses.

Regarding the architectural choice of GDN, the paper motivates it by referencing prior work from the same authors on density modeling and its biological inspiration. While interesting, this does not constitute sufficient evidence for its necessity or superiority in an image compression task. The authors' discussion section explicitly states, "Preliminary results indicate that qualitatively similar results are achievable with other activation functions we tested, but that rectified linear units generally require a substantially larger number of model parameters/stages to achieve the same rate–distortion performance." This is a critical claim that should be a central part of the experimental results, not a provisional remark in the discussion. A proper ablation study comparing GDN against a standard Conv-ReLU architecture with a comparable parameter count is necessary to validate the contribution of this specific nonlinear transform. Without it, the contribution appears to be more about the training framework than the specific architecture, yet the architecture is presented as a key element.

The second major issue concerns the comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000. These standards are complete systems, refined over many years, with highly optimized components. The JPEG 2000 standard, for instance, uses the EBCOT algorithm for its entropy coding, which is extremely effective at exploiting spatial redundancies in the quantized coefficients. The authors' implementation of a CABAC-based coder with limited spatial adaptation is unlikely to be competitive. Therefore, when the proposed method shows only marginal or sometimes inferior PSNR performance (e.g., Fig. 15), it is unclear if a more sophisticated entropy coder could have pushed JPEG 2000's performance significantly higher, erasing the claimed benefits. While MS-SSIM results are more favorable, the claims of outperforming established standards on standard metrics like PSNR need to be supported by a more controlled comparison. A truly fair comparison would require isolating the effect of the transform from that of the entropy coder.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not the primary basis for my decision.

*   The practice of training a separate model for each point on the rate-distortion curve (i.e., for each λ) is computationally burdensome and less practical than methods that learn a single model capable of operating at multiple bit rates. While the authors' approach is sound for mapping out the optimal R-D frontier, a discussion of this limitation and potential paths toward a single, rate-adaptive model would strengthen the paper.

*   The paper's clarity in explaining the continuous relaxation and its relation to VAEs is excellent. This section is a strong point and provides a solid theoretical underpinning for the optimization strategy.

*   The visual results are the most compelling part of the paper. The reduction in artifacts is undeniable and suggests that this line of research is very promising. Leaning more heavily into the perceptual quality argument, perhaps with a user study, could be a fruitful direction if the quantitative PSNR claims remain difficult to substantiate rigorously.

**Questions for Clarification:**

1.  Could you provide a direct, quantitative comparison (R-D curves for both PSNR and MS-SSIM) of your GDN-based model against an equivalent model where GDN/IGDN blocks are replaced with more standard components like convolutional layers followed by ReLU activations? This would directly test the claim that GDN is a more efficient parameterization.

2.  Could you elaborate on the specific limitations of your entropy coder? For example, what kind of spatial context is not being exploited that is typically used in the JPEG 2000 entropy coder? An estimate of the potential performance gap due to this would help in interpreting your R-D curves.

3.  In section 3, it is stated that the proxy distortion (using additive noise) is empirically evaluated. Figure 4 shows that the relaxed distortion is "mostly unbiased" with "relatively small variance". Could you provide the actual mean and variance of the error between the relaxed distortion and the true distortion across the test set for several λ values? This would add rigor to the validation of your training proxy.
