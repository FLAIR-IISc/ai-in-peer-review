### **1. Summary**

This paper presents a novel framework for end-to-end optimization of a lossy image compression system. The proposed model is an autoencoder-like architecture comprising a nonlinear analysis transform, a quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform. The transforms are built from cascades of linear convolutions and a specific, biologically-inspired nonlinearity called Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). A key contribution is the method for handling the non-differentiable quantization step during training; the authors propose a continuous relaxation by substituting quantization with additive uniform noise, which enables optimization of the entire model for a rate-distortion objective using stochastic gradient descent. The framework is trained to minimize a weighted sum of rate (estimated using a differentiable proxy for entropy) and distortion (measured by MSE). The authors demonstrate a complete compression pipeline by implementing a context-adaptive binary arithmetic coder (CABAC) to produce an actual bitstream. The method is evaluated against standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000 on the Kodak dataset. The results show that, despite being optimized for MSE, the proposed method produces images that are perceptually superior, a claim supported by significant gains in the MS-SSIM metric and compelling qualitative examples. The method also achieves competitive or superior PSNR performance, particularly at lower bit rates.

### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Technical Soundness and Novelty:** The paper introduces a technically sound and elegant solution to the problem of end-to-end training for lossy compression. The use of additive uniform noise as a differentiable proxy for quantization is a critical and effective innovation that allows for direct optimization of a rate-distortion objective. The framework is well-motivated and thoughtfully positioned relative to both classical compression and modern generative models.
2.  **Empirical Rigor and Compelling Results:** The experimental validation is rigorous and thorough. The authors go beyond reporting entropy estimates by implementing a full entropy coder and reporting actual bit rates, making their comparisons to established codecs direct and meaningful. The quantitative and qualitative results are impressive, demonstrating a clear advantage over JPEG and JPEG 2000 in perceptual quality across all bit rates, which represents a significant advance in image compression.

### **3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The core challenge in training compression models is the non-differentiable nature of quantization. The paper's main technical contribution is a well-justified continuous relaxation of this step. By replacing rounding with additive uniform noise, the authors create a loss function that is differentiable with respect to the transform parameters. The soundness of this approximation is empirically verified in Figure 4, which shows a strong correlation between the relaxed loss terms and the actual discrete entropy and distortion values post-quantization. This validation lends significant credibility to the training methodology.

The paper distinguishes itself from related work in deep learning by demonstrating a complete and practical compression system. Many contemporary papers report performance using entropy as a proxy for rate. This work, however, implements a CABAC-based entropy coder, meaning its reported bit rates are based on actual compressed file sizes. This is a crucial step for scientific rigor, as it accounts for the overhead of entropy coding and the accuracy of the probability model, allowing for a fair and direct comparison against established standards like JPEG and JPEG 2000.

The results strongly support the paper's claims. While optimized for MSE, the model yields reconstructions that are visually far more pleasing than those from JPEG and JPEG 2000, as shown in Figures 5 and 6. The typical blocking and ringing artifacts of linear transform codes are replaced by a more naturalistic simplification of textures and contours. This qualitative observation is quantitatively confirmed by substantial improvements in the MS-SSIM metric (Figure 7), indicating that the learned nonlinear representations capture perceptually relevant image structures more effectively than traditional linear bases. The discussion in Section 3.1, which carefully contrasts the rate-distortion objective with the objective of variational autoencoders, further demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem and solidifies the paper's contribution.

### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended as constructive feedback and are not critical to the decision.
*   The paper acknowledges that the network architecture choices are "somewhat ad-hoc". While the results are excellent, the paper could be strengthened by a more systematic study of the architecture. For instance, an ablation study analyzing the contribution of the number of stages or the specific choice of the GDN nonlinearity versus a more standard one (e.g., ReLU) would provide valuable insights. The provisional comment on the parameter efficiency of GDN is very interesting and would be a strong addition if supported by more detailed experiments.
*   The current framework requires training a separate model for each quality level (i.e., for each value of λ). This is computationally intensive and impractical for applications requiring a flexible quality setting. A discussion of this limitation and potential avenues for creating a single, rate-adaptive model would improve the paper's forward-looking perspective.
*   The entropy model assumes independent marginals for the latent codes, and the spatial context used in the CABAC implementation is based on a simple raster scan. While sufficient to demonstrate the system's effectiveness, performance could likely be improved by incorporating more sophisticated spatial context models to better exploit statistical dependencies in the latent space, which is a standard practice in modern codecs.

### **5. Questions for the Authors**

*   The paper mentions preliminary results suggesting that GDN/IGDN transforms are more parameter-efficient than networks using ReLU. Could you elaborate on these experiments? Specifically, how much deeper or wider did a ReLU-based model need to be to achieve rate-distortion performance comparable to the GDN-based model?
*   Training a separate model for each λ is a practical limitation. Have you considered or experimented with training a single model that is conditioned on the rate-controlling parameter λ (or a similar quality parameter), which would allow for variable-rate compression from one model?
*   The analysis transform results in a latent representation `y` that is half the dimensionality of the input `x`. Was this reduction factor chosen empirically? How sensitive is the model's performance to the dimensionality of this latent representation?
