### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel, end-to-end optimized framework for lossy image compression that significantly advances the state-of-the-art. The core contribution is a complete system, comprising a nonlinear analysis transform, a quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform, which are jointly optimized to minimize a rate-distortion objective. The authors overcome the key technical challenge of optimizing through the non-differentiable quantization step by introducing an elegant continuous relaxation: replacing quantization with additive uniform noise during training. This allows for stochastic gradient descent-based optimization of the entire autoencoder-like architecture. The architecture itself is notable for its use of generalized divisive normalization (GDN), a biologically-inspired nonlinearity, which the authors argue is particularly effective for modeling natural image statistics. The paper provides a rigorous empirical evaluation, comparing against JPEG and JPEG 2000 on standard datasets. Crucially, the authors implement a full entropy coding scheme (CABAC) and report actual bit rates, a step up in rigor from related works that often report only entropy estimates. The results demonstrate not only superior performance in terms of standard metrics like PSNR and, especially, the perceptual metric MS-SSIM, but also a striking qualitative improvement, producing compressed images with far more natural-looking artifacts than traditional methods.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Effective Optimization Framework:** The paper's main technical contribution—the use of additive uniform noise as a differentiable proxy for quantization in the rate-distortion objective—is a simple yet powerful idea that unlocks end-to-end optimization for this classical problem. The authors provide both a clear motivation from first principles and an insightful connection to the variational autoencoder (VAE) framework, while carefully distinguishing their rate-distortion objective from the generative objective of VAEs. This methodology is a significant contribution to the field of learned data compression.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The method produces results that are not just incrementally better but represent a qualitative leap forward. The demonstrated superiority over JPEG 2000, particularly in terms of MS-SSIM and visual appearance at low bit rates, is compelling. The artifacts produced by this method (e.g., smoothing of texture, simplification of contours) are perceptually much less jarring than the blocking, ringing, and aliasing characteristic of linear transform codes. The rigor of the evaluation, including the use of a real entropy coder, lends strong support to these claims.

### Supporting Arguments

The strength of this work lies in its successful synthesis of ideas from deep learning, information theory, and computational neuroscience into a coherent and high-performing system.

The optimization strategy is the paper's cornerstone. By replacing `q = round(y)` with `ỹ = y + Δy`, the authors cleverly transform the non-differentiable problem into a tractable one. The justification that the differential entropy of `ỹ` approximates the discrete entropy of `q` is sound, and the empirical validation in Figure 4 confirms that this relaxation is not just a theoretical convenience but a practical and effective proxy. The detailed connection drawn to VAEs in Section 3.1 is illuminating. It places the work within a broader context of modern generative modeling but also correctly emphasizes the crucial differences: transform coding explicitly navigates the rate-distortion trade-off by varying λ, whereas generative models typically seek to minimize distortion (maximize log-likelihood), corresponding to a high-rate, low-distortion regime.

The architectural choices are also well-motivated. While many deep learning models would default to using standard components like ReLU, the authors leverage their prior work on GDN. The argument that these transforms are more efficient at "Gaussianizing" image statistics provides a strong rationale for their use. The visual results seem to vindicate this choice; the model appears to learn a representation that separates perceptually meaningful structures (edges, contours) from less critical high-frequency texture, which it discards gracefully at lower bit rates. This is a stark contrast to how linear transforms like DCT or wavelets represent all image content, leading to visible basis-function artifacts when coefficients are coarsely quantized.

Finally, the paper's thoroughness is commendable. The authors do not stop at showing promising results with entropy estimates. They implement a context-adaptive entropy coder and report actual file sizes, including all necessary sideband information. This demonstrates the practical viability of the system and allows for a fair and direct comparison with established standards like JPEG 2000, setting a high bar for future research in this area.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While this is an excellent paper, the following points could further strengthen it:

1.  **Ablation Study on GDN:** The authors claim that GDN/IGDN transforms are more efficient than simpler nonlinearities like ReLUs, requiring fewer parameters or stages. This is a critical claim regarding the architectural contribution. While a full exploration is beyond the scope, a single experiment comparing the proposed model to a similarly-sized model using a more standard nonlinearity (e.g., Conv-ReLU-BatchNorm) trained with the same objective would provide concrete evidence for the benefits of GDN in this compression task.

2.  **Computational Complexity:** The paper states that encoding and decoding are "efficient" but provides no quantitative analysis. For a compression algorithm, this is a key practical consideration. A brief discussion of the computational complexity (e.g., multiply-adds per pixel) and perhaps a comparison of typical encoding/decoding times against software implementations of JPEG and JPEG 2000 would be highly valuable for assessing real-world applicability.

3.  **Rate Control and Model Storage:** A separate model is trained for each λ value, resulting in a set of discrete points on the rate-distortion curve. This implies that to support a range of quality levels, a decoder would need to store the parameters for multiple networks. This is a practical limitation compared to standards like JPEG 2000, which can achieve fine-grained rate control from a single compressed bitstream. Acknowledging this limitation and perhaps discussing potential avenues for creating a single, rate-adaptive model would be a valuable addition to the discussion.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the preliminary results mentioned in the discussion comparing GDN to rectified linear units? Specifically, for a model with ReLUs to achieve similar performance, what was the approximate increase in the number of parameters or computational depth required?

2.  Regarding the entropy coding, the model assumes independent marginals for the latent codes `y_i`. Have you explored incorporating spatial or channel-wise dependencies into the entropy model (e.g., using an autoregressive model on the latents)? Do you believe this could yield significant further gains in compression rate?
