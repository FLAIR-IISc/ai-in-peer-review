### **Summary**

The paper proposes a novel end-to-end learned framework for lossy image compression. The method is based on a nonlinear autoencoder-like architecture where the analysis and synthesis transforms are composed of cascaded stages of convolution, down/up-sampling, and a biologically-inspired nonlinearity called Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). The core contribution is a principled method for jointly optimizing the entire model for a rate-distortion trade-off. To overcome the non-differentiability of the quantization step, the authors introduce an elegant continuous relaxation, replacing quantization with additive uniform noise during training, which enables optimization via stochastic gradient descent. The paper demonstrates that this approach, while optimized for Mean Squared Error (MSE), significantly outperforms standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The improvements are not only quantitative in terms of PSNR and especially the perceptual metric MS-SSIM, but also qualitative, producing compressed images with far more natural and visually pleasing artifacts than the blocking and ringing typical of linear transform codes. The work provides a strong bridge between classical compression theory and modern deep generative modeling.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Principled Framework with State-of-the-Art Results:** The paper presents a complete, well-motivated, and highly effective framework for learned image compression. The resulting model achieves state-of-the-art performance, demonstrating significant rate-distortion improvements over widely adopted standards like JPEG and JPEG 2000, particularly in terms of perceptual quality.
2.  **Methodological Soundness and Rigor:** The technical approach is both clever and sound. The continuous relaxation of the rate-distortion objective is a key innovation for enabling end-to-end training. The paper's detailed comparison to Variational Autoencoders is insightful, and its scientific rigor is commendable, particularly the implementation of a full entropy coder to report actual bit rates rather than relying on entropy estimates.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's strengths are numerous and significant.

*   **Impact and Novelty:** This work represents a landmark achievement in learned image compression. It moves beyond incremental improvements and demonstrates a paradigm shift from hand-engineered linear transforms to learned nonlinear ones. The results are compelling, showing that a data-driven approach can produce a compression scheme that is not only more efficient but also creates artifacts that are qualitatively superior and more aligned with human perception.
*   **Technical Contributions:** The main technical contribution is the formulation of a differentiable proxy for the rate-distortion loss function (Eq. 11). By replacing quantization with additive uniform noise and using a non-parametric density model for the latent variables, the authors create an objective that is suitable for gradient-based optimization while remaining a faithful approximation of the true discrete objective (as verified in Fig. 4). This technique is a valuable contribution to the field.
*   **Excellent Connection to Literature:** The authors do an excellent job of placing their work in context. The motivation for using GDN is well-grounded in prior work on natural image statistics. Furthermore, the discussion in Section 3.1, which carefully delineates the relationship between their method and Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), is particularly insightful. It clarifies how rate-distortion optimization differs from standard generative modeling (e.g., optimizing for a specific trade-off parameter λ vs. maximizing the ELBO), which is a crucial distinction for the compression task.
*   **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors compare against strong, widely-used baselines (JPEG, JPEG 2000) on a standard benchmark dataset (Kodak). The use of multiple metrics (PSNR, MS-SSIM) provides a balanced view of performance. The decision to implement and report actual compressed bit rates using a CABAC-based entropy coder is a major strength that sets a high standard for reproducibility and practical relevance, avoiding the pitfalls of comparing entropy estimates with real-world bit rates. The qualitative results provided in the paper and the supplementary website are striking and strongly support the central claims.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to be constructive suggestions for future work and are not critical for the acceptance of this paper.

*   **Ablation of Nonlinearity:** The paper briefly mentions preliminary results comparing GDN to simpler nonlinearities like ReLUs. This is a very interesting direction. A more detailed analysis comparing the rate-distortion performance versus model complexity (e.g., number of parameters or layers) for different activation functions would provide valuable insight into the specific benefits and efficiency of the GDN transform for this task.
*   **Entropy Model Sophistication:** The current entropy model assumes the latent coefficients are marginally independent. While the GDN transform is designed to reduce dependencies, some spatial correlations likely remain. Incorporating a simple spatial context model into the entropy coder (e.g., conditioning the probability of a coefficient on its quantized neighbors) is a standard technique in modern codecs that could potentially yield further rate savings with minimal computational overhead.
*   **Variable-Rate Models:** The current framework requires training a separate model for each point on the rate-distortion curve (i.e., for each λ), which is computationally intensive. It would be a significant practical advantage to have a single model that can operate at multiple bit rates. Could the authors comment on potential ways their framework might be adapted to achieve this, for example, by making the transforms conditional on a rate parameter?

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1.  **Architecture and Dimensionality:** The paper states that the net output of the analysis transform "has half the dimensionality of the input." Does this refer to the total number of elements in the latent tensor, or is the spatial resolution halved in each dimension (resulting in 1/4 of the spatial elements)? Could you also comment on how sensitive the performance is to the degree of spatial downsampling versus the number of feature channels in the latent representation?
2.  **Rate Approximation:** In Section 3, you approximate the discrete entropy H(q) with the differential entropy h(ỹ) of the noise-relaxed variable. Figure 4 shows a small positive bias in this approximation at low bit rates. Could you elaborate on the theoretical relationship between H(q) and h(ỹ)? Is there a known bound or asymptotic relationship that would explain this observed bias and its convergence at higher rates?
3.  **Optimization with Perceptual Metrics:** You chose to optimize for MSE to allow for a direct comparison with traditional methods. Based on your prior work, could you provide some intuition on how the learned filters and the nature of the compression artifacts might change if the model were optimized directly for a metric like MS-SSIM instead of MSE? Would you expect even smoother, more "painterly" results at very low bit rates?
