This paper presents an end-to-end trainable image compression and decompression system that demonstrably outperforms established algorithms like JPEG-2000 in terms of rate-distortion trade-offs. Beyond showcasing the effectiveness of deep learning in a novel application, a significant contribution of this work is the introduction of a differentiable approximation to the rate function. This allows for effective training with varying rate-distortion constraints and, importantly, has the potential to impact research areas beyond image compression – specifically those that could benefit from differentiable approximations of similar functions.

The authors thoughtfully addressed my pre-review question, providing valuable clarification. I initially questioned whether a sufficiently complex network, trained to minimize distortion under fixed rate and quantization constraints, wouldn’t naturally learn to produce codes with maximal entropy within the given range, effectively achieving the theoretical upper bound. While this remains a valid point, the authors convincingly argue that enforcing such behavior *a priori* through architectural constraints would necessitate a more complex network architecture to replicate the performance currently achieved utilizing a separate variable rate encoder for storing the quantization parameter *q*. This constraint, while seemingly sub-optimal, appears to be crucial for achieving the observed compression efficiency within a reasonable network complexity.

The core idea of learning to compress images via an autoencoder framework with a differentiable rate function is well-executed. The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating significant improvements over JPEG-2000 across a range of bitrates on both the Lena and Kodak datasets. The visualizations of reconstructed images at different bitrates clearly illustrate the superior quality achieved by the proposed method. The ablation studies, particularly those exploring the impact of different entropy models, provide valuable insights into the design choices.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be strengthened. While the authors compare against JPEG-2000, a more comprehensive comparison against more recent learned compression techniques (e.g., those employing vector quantization or attention mechanisms) would further contextualize the contributions.  The computational cost of the proposed method compared to JPEG-2000 is briefly mentioned, but a more detailed analysis, perhaps including encoding and decoding times, would be beneficial. Furthermore, the discussion of the entropy model could be expanded. While the authors demonstrate the importance of the entropy estimator, a more detailed explanation of its limitations and potential improvements would be appreciated. 

The paper’s clarity is generally good, but the notation could be made more consistent. For example, the meaning of *q* and its relation to the quantization step size is not always immediately clear. A more explicit definition of all key symbols would improve readability.

Despite these minor points, this is a strong paper with a clear contribution. The differentiable rate function is a particularly valuable addition to the toolkit for research in rate-distortion optimization and related areas. The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. I believe this work will be of significant interest to the ICLR community.



**Overall Assessment:** Strong Accept.



**Strengths:**

*   Novel and effective approach to image compression using deep learning.
*   Significant contribution of a differentiable rate function with broad applicability.
*   Compelling experimental results demonstrating superior performance compared to JPEG-2000.
*   Thoughtful response to pre-review questions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited comparison to more recent learned compression techniques.
*   Lack of detailed computational cost analysis.
*   Entropy model discussion could be expanded.
*   Minor inconsistencies in notation.
