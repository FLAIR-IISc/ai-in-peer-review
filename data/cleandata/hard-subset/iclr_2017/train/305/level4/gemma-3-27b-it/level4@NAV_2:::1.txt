This is a highly compelling paper on image compression using deep neural networks. The presentation is excellent, and the integration of rate-distortion theory into the objective function is seamless and well-motivated. The comparison to a strong baseline (JPEG 2000, rather than just JPEG as in prior work) is a significant strength. I anticipate this work will have a considerable impact on the field.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The primary reasons are the clear and novel approach to end-to-end optimized compression, and the demonstrable improvement in visual quality over established methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000, even when evaluated with objective metrics like MS-SSIM.

The paper tackles the fundamental problem of lossy image compression, specifically addressing the difficulty of jointly optimizing rate and distortion. The approach is exceptionally well-motivated, building on established principles of transform coding but cleverly circumventing the limitations of separate optimization through end-to-end learning. The authors convincingly position their work within the broader context of probabilistic source modeling, lossy compression, and recent advances in generative modeling, drawing insightful parallels to variational autoencoders while clearly delineating the unique constraints imposed by the compression task. The use of a biologically-inspired GDN nonlinearity is a thoughtful choice, grounded in established models of visual processing.

The paper strongly supports its claims. The experimental results on the Kodak dataset are thorough and demonstrate consistent improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of both PSNR and, crucially, perceptual quality as measured by MS-SSIM. The ablation study and discussion regarding the relaxed loss function and its connection to variational autoencoders are also well-reasoned and contribute to the paper’s overall rigor. The authors address potential limitations and provide a comprehensive discussion of related work.

Several points could further enhance the paper. It would be beneficial to include results on standard test images such as Lena, Barbara, and Mandrill (or Baboon, as you noted!), to facilitate direct comparison with a wider range of existing methods. Furthermore, a more explicit comparison to state-of-the-art classical compression techniques (beyond JPEG and JPEG 2000) – for example, those based on optimized transforms like curvelets or wavelet families – would provide valuable context and further solidify the contribution of this work. While the authors acknowledge these methods in the introduction, a direct empirical comparison would be highly valuable. It would also be helpful to clarify the computational cost associated with training the models, beyond mentioning it is “high.” Providing approximate training times or hardware specifications would offer a more complete picture.

I have the following clarifying questions for the authors:

1.  Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of the results to the choice of the downsampling factors (sk) in the analysis transform? Were different configurations explored, and if so, how did they impact performance?
2.  The paper mentions an entropy code is implemented. Could you provide more details on the complexity and computational cost of this entropy coding stage compared to the encoding/decoding time of the analysis/synthesis transforms?
3.  Regarding the relationship to variational autoencoders, you mention that the distortion metric being non-Euclidean could invalidate the equivalence. Could you provide some intuition for *why* a perceptual metric would cause this breakdown, and what implications this has for the interpretation of the optimization process?
4.  In Section 4, you state that the relaxed distortion term is “mostly unbiased.” Could you quantify this bias further, perhaps by providing confidence intervals or statistical tests?
