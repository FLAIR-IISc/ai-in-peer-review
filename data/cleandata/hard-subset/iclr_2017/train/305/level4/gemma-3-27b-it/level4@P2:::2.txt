This paper presents a method for image compression based on end-to-end optimized nonlinear transforms, building upon previous work in rate-distortion optimization. The authors construct an analysis and synthesis network using cascaded convolutional layers and nonlinearities, specifically employing a generalized divisive normalization (GDN) inspired by biological neurons. A key contribution is the joint optimization of the entire model, including the analysis, synthesis, and an adaptive entropy code, for rate-distortion performance. The paper also draws connections between this approach and variational autoencoders (VAEs).

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While the core approach of rate-distortion optimization is not entirely novel, the successful application to deep, fully-optimized compression networks, achieving demonstrably superior performance compared to established standards like JPEG and JPEG 2000, represents a significant advancement. The thorough experimentation and clear presentation further strengthen the contribution.

The paper tackles the challenging problem of image compression, moving beyond separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy coding stages. The approach is well-motivated by the limitations of traditional methods and the potential for learned representations to better capture image structure. Prior work by the authors demonstrates the benefit of optimizing for perceptual distortion, and this work extends that by exploring a more flexible network architecture and incorporating adaptive entropy coding. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of both rate-distortion performance and, crucially, visual quality, as assessed by MS-SSIM. The extensive availability of test images and rate-distortion curves online is also a strong positive.

While the paper is strong, several points could be addressed to further improve it. Equation 10 appears to contain an error; the partition function within the relaxed loss function should likely depend on the synthesis transform, g_s(y; theta). If this is the case, the equivalence to a VAE breaks down for non-Euclidean metrics, a point that deserves further discussion. Additionally, the decision to optimize for MSE instead of a perceptual metric, as done in earlier work, is not fully explained. Given the authors' expertise in perceptual modeling, clarifying the rationale behind this choice would be valuable. It would also be beneficial to disentangle the contributions of the adaptive entropy coding from the improvements gained by the deeper network architecture. Providing results without adaptive entropy coding (as in the previous paper) would help to isolate its effect. More detail regarding the adaptive coder's implementation and performance characteristics would be welcome.

I have a few questions for the authors:

1.  Could you please verify Equation 10 and clarify the dependence of the partition function? If the equivalence to a VAE does not hold for non-Euclidean metrics, could you elaborate on the implications of this?
2.  What motivated the shift from optimizing for a perceptual metric (as in your previous work) to using MSE in this study?
3.  Could you provide results for the proposed method *without* the adaptive entropy coding, to isolate its contribution to the overall performance improvement?
4.  Could you provide more details on the specific implementation of the adaptive entropy coder, including details on the context modeling and arithmetic coding stages? How does it compare to existing, well-established entropy coding methods used in JPEG 2000?
