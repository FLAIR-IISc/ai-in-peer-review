## Review of END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

This paper presents a compelling approach to image compression using an end-to-end optimized framework based on nonlinear transforms. The work is exceptionally well-written and clearly articulates the motivation and methodology. The integration of rate-distortion theory into the objective function feels natural and effective within the proposed architecture. The comparison to a strong baseline, JPEG 2000 (in addition to JPEG), strengthens the contribution and positions the work effectively within the field. I anticipate this paper will have a significant impact on research in image compression and potentially beyond.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a significant advance in image compression, demonstrating superior performance and visual quality compared to established methods. The theoretical grounding and experimental validation are both strong.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core strength of this paper lies in its holistic approach to image compression. By jointly optimizing the analysis and synthesis transforms alongside the quantization step, the authors move beyond the traditionally separate optimization of components in transform coding. The use of a Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) inspired by biological visual systems is a thoughtful design choice, and the results demonstrate its effectiveness in capturing image statistics. The continuous relaxation of the quantization step, allowing for gradient-based optimization, is a clever and crucial innovation. Furthermore, the evaluation using actual bit rates, rather than relying solely on entropy estimates, strengthens the practical relevance of the work. The reported improvements in both objective quality (MS-SSIM) and subjective visual quality are particularly noteworthy. 

The paper’s connection to variational autoencoders is also well-explained and provides a useful theoretical context. While the authors acknowledge the differences, the analogy helps to understand the underlying principles and potential connections to broader areas of research.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

While the experimental results are convincing, expanding the benchmark comparison would further strengthen the paper. Including results on standard test images such as Lena, Barbara, and Mandrill (or Baboon, as the author correctly notes) would provide a more direct comparison to a wider range of existing methods. More importantly, situating the performance relative to state-of-the-art classical compression techniques (beyond JPEG and JPEG 2000) would provide a clearer understanding of the proposed method’s position within the broader landscape of image compression algorithms.  Specifically, a comparison to methods like BPG/HEVC (with lossless options) or more recent learned compression schemes would be valuable.

The discussion section touches on potential future work, such as exploring simpler nonlinearities and the connection to adversarial robustness. Expanding on these avenues could provide further insights and potential directions for research.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. The paper states that the relaxed distortion term appears mostly unbiased. Could you quantify this bias further, perhaps by providing a table showing the difference between the relaxed MSE and the actual MSE after quantization, averaged over a validation set?
2. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the downsampling factors (sk) in the analysis transform? Were different configurations explored, and if so, what impact did they have on the rate-distortion performance?
3. You mention that the initial probability models for the entropy code are fixed after optimization. Does the performance significantly degrade if these models are allowed to adapt further during the encoding process?
4. The computational cost of training is acknowledged. Could you provide more specific details on the training time and resources required (e.g., number of GPUs, training time in days)? This would help assess the practical feasibility of replicating the results.
