This well-written paper presents an end-to-end learning method for image compression. By optimizing for rate-distortion performance and employing a clever relaxation technique, the method learns an efficient image compression scheme directly from a database of natural images. 

The approach is interesting, the results are compelling, and the analysis is quite thorough, making it easy for me to recommend acceptance.

The core idea is to formulate image compression as a differentiable optimization problem, allowing gradient-based learning of both the encoding and decoding stages. This is a significant departure from traditional methods relying on hand-crafted transforms and rate-distortion heuristics. The authors effectively leverage recent advances in autoencoder architectures and optimization techniques to achieve competitive compression performance.

I particularly appreciated the detailed ablation studies presented in the paper. These experiments clearly demonstrate the contribution of each component of the proposed method, such as the specific choice of relaxation and the impact of different loss weighting schemes. The comparisons to existing state-of-the-art methods, including JPEG and JPEG 2000, are well-executed and convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The reported improvements in terms of both PSNR and MS-SSIM are notable.

The theoretical justification for the relaxation is sound and the discussion of its implications for the learned compression scheme is insightful. The authors also acknowledge the limitations of their approach, specifically the computational cost associated with training and the potential for artifacts at high compression ratios. However, these limitations are clearly stated and do not detract significantly from the overall contribution of the work. 

One minor point worth mentioning is the potential sensitivity of the method to the choice of hyperparameters. While the authors provide a reasonable set of default values, a more extensive exploration of the hyperparameter space might be beneficial for future work. Furthermore, it would be interesting to see how the method generalizes to different image datasets beyond the ones used in the experiments. 

Despite these minor suggestions, I believe this paper represents a significant advance in the field of image compression. The authors have presented a novel and effective method that leverages the power of deep learning to achieve state-of-the-art results. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and the experimental results are convincing. Therefore, I strongly recommend acceptance to ICLR 2017.
