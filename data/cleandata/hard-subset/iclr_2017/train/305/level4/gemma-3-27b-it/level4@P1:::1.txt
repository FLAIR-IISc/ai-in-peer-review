This is a very strong paper on image compression using deep neural networks – the most convincing I have read to date. The writing is clear, and the integration of rate-distortion theory into the objective function is seamless. The comparison to a reasonable baseline (JPEG 2000, rather than only JPEG as in previous works) is also a strength. I anticipate this paper will have a significant impact on the field.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a novel and well-supported approach to image compression, achieving promising results and offering improvements over existing methods. The clear presentation and thorough experimentation further strengthen the case for acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The authors tackle a fundamental problem in engineering – image compression – by formulating it within a modern machine learning framework. Their approach of end-to-end optimization of nonlinear transforms, inspired by biological neurons, is a compelling alternative to traditional methods relying on manually designed transforms and separate optimization stages. The method demonstrates superior rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 on the Kodak dataset, and – crucially – a noticeable improvement in visual quality, as supported by objective metrics like MS-SSIM. 

The theoretical connection to variational autoencoders is insightful, and the authors appropriately highlight the differences and constraints necessary to maintain a practical compression scheme. The use of a continuous relaxation of the quantization step, along with the justification for its validity, allows for effective optimization via stochastic gradient descent. Furthermore, reporting performance using actual bit rates, instead of estimated entropy, demonstrates the feasibility of a complete compression solution.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, including results on standard benchmark images like Lena, Barbara, and Mandrill would further strengthen the comparison with existing methods. It would also be beneficial to explicitly position the performance of the proposed method relative to state-of-the-art classical image compression techniques beyond JPEG and JPEG 2000. Including a discussion of how the network compares to these more advanced classical methods would provide a more complete evaluation.

The explanation of the entropy code implementation is well-detailed, but a brief discussion of its limitations and potential for improvement would be valuable.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the choice of the specific GDN architecture (number of stages, filter sizes, downsampling factors)? Was this determined through experimentation, or are there theoretical reasons for these particular choices?
2.  The paper mentions a potential trade-off between the computational cost of training and the performance achieved with different activation functions. Could you provide more details on the experiments conducted with rectified linear units, and quantify the increase in parameters required to achieve comparable results to GDN?
3.  The rate-distortion curves appear to vary considerably across different images. Is there a systematic way to predict or characterize the performance of the method on a new image, beyond simply training a new model? 
4.  Regarding the comparison to state-of-the-art methods: have you evaluated the performance of your method against more recent and advanced classical compression techniques (e.g., those utilizing context modeling or advanced entropy coding schemes)? If so, how does your approach compare in terms of rate-distortion performance and computational complexity?
