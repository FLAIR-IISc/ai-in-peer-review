## Review of END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

This paper presents a compelling approach to image compression, demonstrating an end-to-end trained system that achieves superior rate-distortion performance compared to established algorithms like JPEG and JPEG 2000. A key contribution lies in the introduction of a differentiable approximation to the rate function, enabling effective training across various rate-distortion trade-offs. This technique has the potential to impact areas beyond image compression, offering a valuable tool for tasks benefiting from differentiable approximations of similar functions.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper tackles a significant problem, presents a well-motivated and novel approach, and convincingly supports its claims with comprehensive experimental results. The improvement in visual quality, particularly, is a strong indicator of the method’s effectiveness.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper addresses the challenging problem of jointly optimizing rate and distortion in image compression. Traditional methods typically optimize the transform, quantizer, and entropy code separately, often relying on hand-tuned parameters. This work bypasses this limitation by framing compression as an end-to-end learnable problem, allowing the network to adaptively learn an optimal representation for compression. 

The core innovation—the continuous relaxation of the quantization step—is crucial. It allows for gradient-based optimization, enabling the model to effectively explore the rate-distortion space. The authors successfully demonstrate that this relaxation, while an approximation, yields impressive results and aligns well with the theoretical foundations of variational autoencoders. The explicit connection to VAEs, while not entirely novel, provides a useful perspective and highlights the potential for further exploration.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare their method against JPEG and JPEG 2000 on a standard dataset (Kodak) and additional images, presenting both quantitative metrics (PSNR, MS-SSIM) and qualitative visual comparisons. The consistent improvement in visual quality, as demonstrated in the provided images, is particularly compelling. The inclusion of an actual entropy coding step, rather than relying solely on entropy estimates, further strengthens the validity of the results. The availability of the test images and compression results online is commendable and facilitates reproducibility. 

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, some aspects could be further clarified. 

*   **Complexity vs. Entropy:** The authors address the point about network complexity and entropy maximizing codes, but perhaps a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs is warranted. Explicitly outlining the computational cost of achieving a true entropy-maximizing code versus the benefits of the current approach would be valuable.
*   **GDN Justification:** Although the paper provides previous work showing the effectiveness of GDN for density estimation, a clearer explanation of *why* GDN is particularly well-suited for this compression task, beyond its Gaussianizing properties, would strengthen the argument.
*   **Computational Cost:** A more detailed discussion of the computational resources required for training (e.g., GPUs, training time) would be useful for assessing the practicality of the approach.
*   **Ablation Study:** While the architecture seems well-considered, an ablation study examining the contribution of different components (e.g., GDN vs. ReLU, number of stages) could provide further insight.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions the possibility of optimizing with a perceptual metric instead of MSE. What were the challenges encountered in attempting this, and how did they influence the decision to use MSE for this work?
2.  Could you elaborate on the choice of the piecewise linear function for approximating the marginal densities? Were other options (e.g., Gaussian mixtures) considered, and if so, what were the trade-offs?
3.  The authors mention that the relaxed entropy provides a positively biased estimate of the discrete entropy for coarser quantization. How sensitive are the overall results to this bias, and did you explore methods to further mitigate it?
4.  Could you provide a rough estimate of the training time (in GPU hours) for a typical model configuration?
