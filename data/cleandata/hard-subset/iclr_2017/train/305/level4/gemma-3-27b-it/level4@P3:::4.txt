## Review of END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

This paper presents a novel end-to-end learning method for image compression. The authors develop a framework for jointly optimizing the analysis and synthesis transforms of an image compression model, directly for rate-distortion performance. A key contribution is the use of a continuous relaxation of the quantization step, enabling gradient-based optimization, and drawing connections to variational autoencoders. The method demonstrates improved rate-distortion performance and, crucially, enhanced visual quality compared to standard JPEG and JPEG 2000 compression techniques.

**Decision:** I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The approach is well-motivated, the technical details are carefully presented, and the results demonstrate a significant improvement in both objective metrics and perceptual quality over established compression methods.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles the fundamental problem of image compression, addressing the challenge of jointly optimizing rate and distortion – a historically difficult task. The motivation is strong: existing methods often rely on hand-engineered transforms and separately optimized components. This work directly addresses this limitation through end-to-end learning. The connection drawn to variational autoencoders provides a valuable theoretical perspective, while the practical focus on operating at any point along the rate-distortion curve distinguishes it from purely generative modeling approaches. 

The empirical results are compelling. The authors provide a thorough comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 using a standard dataset (Kodak) and several additional images. The reported improvements in MS-SSIM, alongside the qualitative visual improvements shown in the figures, strongly support the claims of the paper. The inclusion of actual bit rates (rather than entropy estimates) strengthens the practical relevance of the findings. The ablation studies and analysis of the relaxed loss function (Section 4) demonstrate a good understanding of the method's behavior. The extensive supplementary material, including code and additional visualizations, is a significant positive.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not assessment):**

*   **Clarify GDN Intuition:** While the paper explains GDN, a slightly more intuitive explanation of *why* it's beneficial for compression, beyond its Gaussianization properties, might be helpful. Specifically, how does local gain control assist in concentrating energy for more efficient quantization?
*   **Computational Cost:** While the authors acknowledge the high training cost, a more detailed discussion of the computational resources required for training (e.g., GPUs, training time) would be valuable for reproducibility and assessing the practicality of the approach.
*   **Comparison to Ballé et al. (2016):**  The paper builds on previous work (Ballé et al., 2016) by using a more flexible transform. A more direct comparison of the computational cost (number of parameters) and performance differences between the two models would be informative. Was the improvement in performance worth the increase in complexity?
*  **Entropy Coding Details:** While the CABAC implementation is described, details about the context modeling used would be interesting. Were default settings used, or were any adaptations made specifically for this compression scheme?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions a slight bias in the relaxed entropy estimate, particularly for coarser quantization. How sensitive are the optimized transforms to this bias? Could adjusting the piecewise linear spline approximation during training mitigate this effect further?
2.  The choice of network architecture (number of filters, stages, downsampling factors) seems somewhat ad-hoc. Did you perform any systematic architecture search or ablation studies to determine the optimal configuration?
3.  The authors mention potential improvements from using a perceptual metric for training. Do you have any preliminary results with perceptual metrics, and if so, what were the challenges encountered?
4.  Could you elaborate on the trade-offs between the computational cost of training and the resulting compression performance? Is there a point of diminishing returns with respect to model complexity (number of layers, filters)?
