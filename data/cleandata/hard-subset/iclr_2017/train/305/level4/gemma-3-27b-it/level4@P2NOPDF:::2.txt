This paper applies rate-distortion optimization – previously explored with simpler models and entropy schemes – to deep neural networks, incorporating adaptive entropy coding for improved compression performance. The authors also draw connections between their approach and variational autoencoders (VAEs).

While the underlying rate-distortion optimization framework has been previously published, the novelty of this work is incremental. It is not immediately clear if this submission introduces a significant new algorithmic contribution. Notably, a previous iteration of this research focused on optimizing for perceptual metrics, a direction seemingly reversed in this paper with a return to mean squared error (MSE). Despite this shift, the experimental results demonstrate a clear and substantial improvement over JPEG 2000, a compelling achievement. To the best of my knowledge, no other learned compression method has achieved comparable performance levels. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented.

I identified a potential error in Equation 10. The partition function appears to be independent of g_s(y; theta), which would imply that the method is *not* equivalent to a VAE when utilizing non-Euclidean metrics. Clarification and correction of this equation are needed.

The decision to optimize for MSE, rather than a perceptual metric as used in prior work, is not adequately justified. Given the authors’ expertise, the exclusive reliance on PSNR for evaluation is also somewhat surprising. A more comprehensive evaluation, including perceptual quality metrics, would be highly valuable.

A key question remains regarding the individual contributions of adaptive entropy coding and the increased capacity afforded by deeper encoders and decoders. Disentangling these effects is critical for understanding the performance gains. It would be beneficial to see results demonstrating performance without adaptive entropy coding, mirroring the setup of the authors’ earlier work. Furthermore, a more detailed explanation of the adaptive coder – its implementation and its specific impact on performance – would strengthen the paper significantly. I would be willing to reconsider a higher score upon receiving this additional information and clarification.
