This paper presents a novel end-to-end learning method for image compression. The core idea is to jointly optimize a complete compression pipeline – consisting of a nonlinear analysis transform, a uniform quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform – directly for rate-distortion performance. A key contribution is the use of a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization, alongside a compelling connection to variational autoencoders. The authors demonstrate improved rate-distortion performance and, importantly, superior perceptual quality compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 on a variety of test images.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The approach is both theoretically sound and empirically successful, representing a significant advance in the field of image compression. The paper is well-written, clearly explains the methodology, and provides a thorough experimental evaluation.

The paper tackles the fundamental problem of image compression, specifically addressing the difficulty of jointly optimizing rate and distortion. The authors clearly articulate this challenge and position their work within the context of existing approaches like transform coding and variational autoencoders. The motivation for using nonlinear transforms, inspired by models of biological neurons and previous work on Gaussianizing image densities, is well-explained and logically sound. The connection to variational autoencoders provides valuable theoretical grounding and clarifies the optimization objective.

The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The authors compare their approach to established standards (JPEG and JPEG 2000) using both objective metrics (PSNR, MS-SSIM) and qualitative visual comparisons. The consistent improvements in perceptual quality, as evidenced by the MS-SSIM results and the example images, are particularly compelling. The inclusion of an implemented entropy code and reporting of actual bit rates strengthens the practical relevance of the work.

To further strengthen the paper, I have a few suggestions. While the connection to variational autoencoders is insightful, it would be helpful to more explicitly discuss the differences between the optimization objectives in the two frameworks. The authors briefly touch upon this in the discussion, but a more detailed comparison could be valuable. Additionally, while the results demonstrate strong performance on the Kodak dataset and other standard images, exploring the method's robustness to different image types (e.g., medical images, satellite imagery) could broaden the scope of the evaluation. Finally, a discussion of the computational cost of training and encoding/decoding could provide a more complete picture of the method’s practicality.

I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  The paper mentions fixing the exponents in the GDN transform to improve stability. Was this choice investigated further? Would allowing these parameters to be learned impact performance or stability?
2.  The choice of 10 sampling points for the piecewise linear approximation of the marginals pỹi seems somewhat arbitrary. How sensitive are the results to this choice? Was a more systematic exploration of this parameter considered?
3.  The ablation study focuses on comparing the GDN/IGDN nonlinearities with simpler alternatives. How does the performance compare when using a different number of stages (i.e., varying the depth of the network)?
