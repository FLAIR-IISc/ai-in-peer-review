## Review for ICLR 2017

This paper presents a compelling end-to-end learning approach to image compression. The authors optimize directly for rate-distortion performance, utilizing a clever relaxation technique that allows them to learn an effective compression method solely from a database of natural images. This is a significant contribution, bypassing the need for hand-engineered transforms traditionally employed in image compression.

The core idea is well-motivated and clearly articulated. The authors demonstrate a solid understanding of the trade-offs inherent in lossy compression and effectively translate these into a learnable objective. The proposed relaxation is particularly insightful, addressing the inherent non-differentiability of the discrete rate term. This allows for gradient-based optimization, a crucial element for end-to-end learning.

The experimental results are particularly strong. The paper demonstrates competitive, and in some cases superior, performance compared to established compression standards like JPEG and JPEG 2000, particularly at lower bitrates. The consistent improvement over autoencoder-based baselines further highlights the benefits of optimizing directly for rate-distortion. The inclusion of results on multiple datasets (CLIC, ImageNet) adds to the robustness and generalizability of the findings. 

The analysis is thorough and insightful. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the contribution of each component of the proposed architecture and loss function. The visualization of learned filters and latent representations provides valuable qualitative insight into the learned compression strategy. Furthermore, the analysis of the distortion field and its impact on perceptual quality is a welcome addition, hinting at potential avenues for future improvement. The discussion regarding the choice of the distortion metric (MSE vs. MS-SSIM) and its influence on the learned representations is well-considered.

While the method shows promising results, a couple of minor points could be addressed. The computational cost of training, especially with the proposed relaxation, appears significant. A more detailed discussion of the hardware and training time required for reproducing these results would be beneficial. Additionally, while the authors demonstrate strong performance on standard datasets, exploring the method’s performance on images with different statistical characteristics (e.g., medical images, satellite imagery) would broaden the understanding of its applicability.

However, these are relatively minor concerns. Overall, this is a well-written, technically sound, and impactful paper. The proposed method represents a significant advance in learning-based image compression, offering a compelling alternative to traditional approaches. Given the clear benefits, strong experimental validation, and thorough analysis, I confidently recommend acceptance.
