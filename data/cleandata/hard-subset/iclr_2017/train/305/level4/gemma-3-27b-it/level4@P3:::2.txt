## Review of END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

This paper extends a prior approach to rate-distortion optimization to deeper convolutional encoder-decoder architectures and incorporates adaptive entropy coding. It also discusses the connection between this learned compression framework and variational autoencoders (VAEs). While building upon previously published work, the paper demonstrates compelling results – a visible improvement over JPEG 2000 – and represents a significant contribution to the field of learned image compression. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented.

**Decision:** Accept. The strong empirical results, demonstrating a clear improvement over standard compression methods, outweigh the incremental nature of the novelty. The paper tackles a fundamental problem and provides a compelling solution with thorough experimentation.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The authors successfully demonstrate a complete, end-to-end optimized image compression system that outperforms established methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The improvements are not merely in terms of PSNR, but also – and more importantly – in perceptual visual quality, as supported by MS-SSIM scores and the illustrative examples provided. The inclusion of an adaptive entropy coder is a crucial step towards a practical compression system, and the detailed description of the network architecture and optimization process facilitates reproducibility. The connection drawn to VAEs provides valuable theoretical context and highlights the underlying principles at play. Despite the incremental nature of the work – extending existing techniques – the authors have achieved a demonstrable improvement in compression performance and visual fidelity, which is a substantial accomplishment. 

**Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

While the paper is strong, addressing the following points could further enhance its impact:

*   **Justification for MSE:** The authors previously optimized for a perceptual metric. Clarifying the rationale for switching to MSE in this work would strengthen the narrative. Was this primarily for comparability with standard benchmarks, or were there specific technical advantages?
*   **Ablation Study of Adaptive Entropy Coding:** The contribution of the adaptive entropy coder relative to the deeper network architecture is unclear. Including results comparing performance *without* adaptive coding (as in the prior work) would isolate its impact and provide valuable insights. More details on the implementation of the adaptive coder (beyond mentioning CABAC) and its specific improvements over a simpler entropy coding scheme would also be beneficial.
*   **VAE Equivalence Clarification:** The authors correctly point out a potential issue with the equivalence to a VAE under non-Euclidean metrics (Equation 10). Elaborating on the implications of this difference and quantifying the effect on the loss function would be helpful.
*   **Computational Cost:** Providing some information on the computational cost of training and encoding/decoding would provide a more complete picture of the practicality of this approach.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the reasons for choosing MSE as the optimization criterion in this work, given your prior success with perceptual metrics?
2.  What is the specific improvement gained by employing adaptive entropy coding compared to the simpler entropy estimation used in your previous work? Could you provide results demonstrating the impact of this addition?
3.  Regarding the discrepancy in Equation 10 and the VAE equivalence, how significant is the deviation from a true VAE objective when using a non-Euclidean distortion metric? Is there a way to approximate or correct for this difference?
4.  Could you provide some details on the computational resources required for training the models and the encoding/decoding times for typical image sizes?
