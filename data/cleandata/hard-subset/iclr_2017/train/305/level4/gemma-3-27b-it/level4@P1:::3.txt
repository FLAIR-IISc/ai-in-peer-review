This is a strong paper that demonstrates an end-to-end trained image compression and decompression system, achieving better rate-distortion trade-offs than established image compression algorithms (like JPEG and JPEG 2000). In addition to showing the efficacy of deep learning for a new application, a key contribution of the paper is the introduction of a differentiable approximation to the rate function, which the authors show enables effective training across different rate-distortion trade-offs. I expect this will have impact beyond the compression application itself—for other tasks that might benefit from differentiable approximations to similar functions. 

**Decision:** Accept. The paper addresses an important problem, proposes a well-motivated approach, and provides strong empirical evidence supporting its claims.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles the fundamental problem of image compression, a cornerstone of data science and engineering. The authors rightly point out the limitations of traditional transform coding methods that rely on hand-engineered transforms and separately optimized components. Their end-to-end approach, leveraging the power of deep learning, offers a compelling alternative. The theoretical connection drawn between the relaxed loss function and variational autoencoders is insightful, providing a useful context for understanding the method.

The experimental results are convincing. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of both rate-distortion performance and, crucially, perceptual quality as measured by MS-SSIM. The visual examples provided are particularly compelling, illustrating the reduced artifacts and more natural appearance of the compressed images produced by their method. The extensive evaluation across different datasets and bit rates strengthens the credibility of the results.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is generally well-written, some sections could benefit from further clarification. For example, the discussion of the relationship to variational autoencoders in Section 3.1 is somewhat dense and could be made more accessible. Similarly, a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind specific architectural choices (e.g., the number of filters, downsampling factors) would be helpful.

The authors acknowledge the computational cost of training their models. Providing more details on the training infrastructure and time required would be useful for reproducibility. 

The choice of MSE as the distortion metric is pragmatic, but the authors rightly suggest that a perceptual metric might lead to further improvements. Exploring this direction in future work would be a valuable extension.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that the relaxed distortion term is mostly unbiased. Could you provide more quantitative details on the bias and variance of both the relaxed distortion and entropy estimates, perhaps in the form of a table or figure?

2.  You state that the scaling ambiguity in the linear transforms is addressed by renormalization. Does this renormalization process introduce any undesirable side effects, such as instability during training, or does it significantly alter the learned filters?

3.  In the appendix, you describe various optimization techniques employed. Could you comment on the sensitivity of the results to these specific choices? For example, how much does performance degrade if Adam is replaced with a simpler optimization algorithm like SGD?

4.  You mention that preliminary results with rectified linear units require a larger network capacity. Can you elaborate on this observation? Specifically, how much larger does the network need to be to achieve comparable performance, and what architectural modifications are necessary?
