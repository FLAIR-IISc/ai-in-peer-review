This paper presents a compelling end-to-end trained image compression and decompression system that outperforms established algorithms like JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of rate-distortion performance. A key contribution lies in the introduction of a differentiable approximation to the rate function, enabling effective training across different rate-distortion trade-offs. This technique has the potential to impact areas beyond image compression, offering a valuable tool for tasks requiring differentiable approximations of similar functions.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The core idea is novel and well-executed, and the results demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods. The paper addresses a fundamental problem in engineering with a fresh, learning-based approach, and the performance gains are substantial enough to warrant publication.

The paper effectively tackles the problem of image compression by formulating it as an end-to-end optimization problem. This contrasts with traditional methods that optimize the transform, quantizer, and entropy code separately. The authors convincingly demonstrate that jointly optimizing these components with a carefully designed architecture – utilizing cascaded convolutions, nonlinearities inspired by biological neurons (GDN), and a continuous proxy for the discontinuous quantization step – yields superior results. 

The authors’ detailed exploration of the relaxed loss function and its connection to variational autoencoders is insightful, providing a theoretical grounding for their approach. The empirical evaluation, including comparisons to JPEG and JPEG 2000 on the Kodak dataset and other standard images, convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method, not only in terms of rate-distortion metrics but also – crucially – in terms of perceptual quality as measured by MS-SSIM. The provision of test images and rate-distortion curves online is particularly valuable.

Here are some suggestions that might improve the paper further:

*   **Clarify the trade-off with entropy coding:** While the authors mention using an entropy code (CABAC) for fair comparison, a more detailed explanation of its implementation and potential limitations in relation to the state-of-the-art could strengthen the paper. Specifically, discussing how CABAC’s adaptive nature and complexity compares to alternatives employed in JPEG 2000 would be useful.
*   **Ablation study for GDN:** The authors highlight the importance of GDN. Including an ablation study comparing performance with simpler activation functions (e.g., ReLU) with comparable model size would further demonstrate the benefits of this specific nonlinearity.
*   **Computational complexity discussion:** A brief discussion of the computational cost of training and, more importantly, encoding/decoding compared to JPEG/JPEG 2000 would be valuable.
*   **Further exploration of the relationship to VAEs:** While the connection to variational autoencoders is mentioned, elaborating on the specific constraints imposed in the compression model that differentiate it from standard VAEs could provide further insight.

I have a few questions for the authors:

*   You mention the relaxed loss function provides a good approximation to actual rate-distortion values. Could you provide more detail on the observed discrepancies, particularly at different quantization levels, and how these discrepancies might affect the optimization process?
*   The architecture choices (number of filters, subsampling factors) appear somewhat ad-hoc. Were any systematic architecture search methods employed, or is the current architecture based primarily on intuition and experimentation?
*   You state that the visual improvement arises from the ability to better represent features and contours. Can you provide any visualizations of the learned filters or feature maps that support this claim?
*   How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the ImageNet subset used for training? Would performance change significantly with a different subset or a different dataset altogether?
