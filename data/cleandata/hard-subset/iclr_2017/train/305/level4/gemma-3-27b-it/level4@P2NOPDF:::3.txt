This paper presents an end-to-end trainable image compression and decompression system that surpasses the performance of established algorithms like JPEG-2000 in terms of bit-rate versus quality trade-offs. Beyond demonstrating the successful application of deep learning to image compression, a significant contribution of this work is the introduction of a differentiable approximation to the rate function. This allows for effective training across a range of rate-distortion trade-offs and has the potential to impact areas beyond compression, specifically tasks benefiting from differentiable approximations of similar rate-limiting functions. 

I appreciated the authors’ thoughtful response to my pre-review question. I initially questioned whether a sufficiently complex network, aiming to minimize distortion under fixed rate and quantization constraints, wouldn’t naturally learn to produce codes maximizing entropy within the allowed range – effectively reaching the theoretical upper bound. However, the authors convincingly argue that enforcing such behavior would necessitate a specific output structure that, to achieve comparable compression efficiency to the current system, would require a significantly more complex network architecture. Specifically, replicating the functionality currently handled by the separate variable rate encoder used for storing the quantized values (*q*) would add substantial computational burden. 

The innovation lies not simply in achieving competitive compression, but in *how* that compression is achieved – through a learnable, differentiable rate function. This unlocks a pathway to directly optimize for rate-distortion trade-offs within a deep learning framework, something previously inaccessible. The results clearly demonstrate the advantages of this approach.
