This paper presents an extension of rate-distortion optimization to deep neural network encoders and decoders, coupled with adaptive entropy coding. The work also explores connections between this approach and variational autoencoders (VAEs). While the foundational rate-distortion optimization framework has been previously published, this submission focuses on its practical implementation with modern deep learning architectures and more sophisticated entropy modeling.

The novelty of this work is moderate. Although the combination of deep learning with adaptive entropy coding within a rate-distortion framework is a valuable contribution, the core theoretical underpinnings are not entirely new. Notably, a previous iteration of this work optimized for perceptual metrics, and this version’s reliance on mean squared error (MSE) represents a step in a different direction. Despite this, the empirical results are compelling; the demonstrated performance significantly surpasses that of JPEG 2000, and, to the best of my knowledge, no other learned compression scheme has achieved comparable results. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented.

However, I identified a potential error in Equation 10. The partition function appears to be independent of $g_s(y; \theta)$, which would imply a lack of equivalence to a VAE when utilizing non-Euclidean metrics. Clarification and correction of this equation are necessary.

A key question arises regarding the decision to optimize for MSE instead of a perceptual metric, as was done in prior work. Given the authors’ expertise in perceptual modeling, this choice is somewhat surprising. Further, the evaluation is exclusively based on PSNR, which, while standard, feels incomplete given the potential for learned codecs to excel in perceptual quality even with lower PSNR scores. A discussion justifying the use of MSE and the absence of perceptual evaluation metrics would strengthen the paper. 

The contribution of adaptive entropy coding relative to the impact of deeper network architectures is not fully elucidated. Understanding the interplay between these components is crucial. It would be highly valuable to see experimental results isolating the effect of adaptive entropy coding – specifically, performance comparisons with and without adaptation, mirroring the setup of the authors' previous publication. A more detailed explanation of the adaptive coder itself, including implementation specifics and an analysis of its impact on rate-distortion performance, would be beneficial. 

In summary, this is a strong paper with significant empirical results. However, addressing the identified error in Equation 10, providing justification for the choice of MSE optimization and the exclusive use of PSNR, and further dissecting the contributions of adaptive entropy coding would significantly enhance the work and warrant a higher score. I recommend acceptance contingent upon these revisions.
