This nicely written paper presents an end-to-end learning method for image compression. By optimizing for rate-distortion performance and employing a clever relaxation technique to address the non-differentiability of the quantization step, the method learns an efficient image compression system by training on a database of natural images.

I recommend accepting this paper. The approach is well-motivated and the results demonstrate a clear improvement in both objective metrics and, importantly, perceptual quality compared to standard compression methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000.

The paper tackles the fundamental problem of image compression, specifically addressing the difficulty of jointly optimizing rate and distortion. The authors build upon existing work in transform coding, but significantly depart from traditional approaches by advocating for end-to-end optimization of the analysis and synthesis transforms. This is a compelling direction, and the motivation is clearly articulated, drawing connections to both Shannon’s information theory and the challenges of optimal quantization in high-dimensional spaces. The authors position their work effectively within the literature, referencing key papers in the field and highlighting the limitations of existing methods. The choice of a generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearity is well-justified, building on their prior work showing its effectiveness in Gaussianizing image densities.

The paper provides strong support for its claims. The experimental results on the Kodak dataset and other image sets are comprehensive and demonstrate consistent improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of both MS-SSIM and, often, PSNR. The ablation studies and analysis of the relaxed loss function are convincing, and the inclusion of visual comparisons further strengthens the argument. The authors acknowledge the limitations of using MSE as the optimization criterion and suggest future work exploring perceptual metrics, demonstrating a critical awareness of the trade-offs involved. The implementation of an entropy code and reporting of actual bit rates is also a significant strength, demonstrating the practicality of the proposed method.

Here are some additional points that might help to further improve the paper:

*   **Clarification of Training Data:** While the paper mentions using a subset of ImageNet, more details about the specific selection process and pre-processing steps would be helpful. Were the images downsampled to a consistent size before training, or were different sizes handled?
*   **Computational Cost:** A discussion of the computational resources required for training would be beneficial. How long did training take, and what hardware was used? This would give readers a better understanding of the practicality of applying this method.
*   **Generalization to Other Image Types:** The results primarily focus on natural images. It would be interesting to see some evaluation on other types of images, such as medical images or satellite imagery, to assess the generalizability of the method.
*   **Comparison to Recent Learned Compression Methods:** The field of learned image compression is rapidly evolving. A more detailed comparison to other recent methods, such as those based on variational autoencoders or GANs, would be valuable.
*   **The Role of GDN:** The paper strongly advocates for the use of GDN. Could the authors provide some intuition or explanation as to *why* GDN is particularly well-suited for this task, beyond simply citing prior work on Gaussianization?



I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  The authors mention that the relaxed distortion term appears to be mostly unbiased. Could they provide more quantitative details regarding the bias and variance of the relaxed entropy estimate?
2.  In Section 3.1, the authors draw a connection to variational autoencoders. Could they elaborate on the specific constraints they impose that differentiate their approach from standard VAEs, and how these constraints contribute to the discrete problem approximation?
3.   The architecture of the network is described, but the motivation for the specific number of filters and downsampling factors isn’t fully clear. Were these values determined through experimentation or based on prior knowledge?
