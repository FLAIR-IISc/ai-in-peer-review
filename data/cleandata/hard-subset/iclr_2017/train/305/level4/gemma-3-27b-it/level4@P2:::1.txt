This paper presents a compelling approach to image compression using deep neural networks, demonstrating a strong understanding and application of rate-distortion theory. The writing is clear and the framework is well-motivated, setting it apart from previous work in the field. The inclusion of JPEG 2000 as a baseline provides a more rigorous comparison than prior studies, which often focused solely on JPEG. I anticipate this work will have a significant impact on the field of image compression.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper tackles a challenging problem with a novel and well-executed approach, demonstrating superior performance to established compression methods both objectively and subjectively.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper convincingly demonstrates an end-to-end optimized image compression model. The authors successfully integrate concepts from rate-distortion theory with a cascade of convolutional layers and nonlinearities, inspired by biological visual systems. This design choice appears crucial to achieving the reported improvements in visual quality. Crucially, the authors move beyond simply optimizing for MSE and demonstrate visually superior results compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000. The use of a continuous relaxation of the quantization step allows for effective gradient-based optimization, and the comparison to variational autoencoders provides valuable context. The empirical results, while currently focused on the Kodak dataset, are promising and strongly suggest a substantial advancement in image compression technology.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is excellent, expanding the evaluation to include standard benchmark images such as Lena, Barbara, and Baboon would further strengthen the results. Additionally, a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art classical image compression methods (beyond JPEG and JPEG 2000) would be valuable. This would provide a clearer understanding of the relative position of this neural network-based approach within the broader landscape of image compression techniques. Specifically, positioning the results relative to methods like BPG/WebP (which use techniques like linear prediction and entropy coding) would be insightful. A discussion on the computational cost of training and encoding/decoding, even qualitatively, would also be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational resources required for training, encoding, and decoding compared to JPEG/JPEG 2000?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the model to the choice of the GDN parameters, and could you elaborate on the rationale behind fixing the exponents and enforcing symmetry?
3.  You mention potential improvements by optimizing using a perceptual metric directly. Have you explored this further, and if so, what were the results?
4.  How does the performance of this method scale with image resolution? Are there any limitations related to memory or computational complexity for very high-resolution images?
5.  The method seems to excel at preserving smoothness and reducing artifacts. Is there an analysis of *why* the network learns to prioritize these aspects, in relation to the rate-distortion trade-off and the chosen network architecture?
