This is a well-written paper that demonstrates an end-to-end trained image compression and decompression system, achieving better bit-rate versus quality trade-offs than established image compression algorithms, such as JPEG-2000. In addition to showcasing the efficacy of deep learning for a new application, a key contribution of the paper is the introduction of a differentiable version of the "rate" function, which the authors show can be effectively used for training with different rate-distortion trade-offs. I expect this will have an impact beyond the compression application itself, as it may benefit other tasks that could utilize differentiable approximations to similar functions.

The authors provided a thoughtful response to my pre-review question. However, I still argue that to minimize distortion under a fixed range and quantization, a sufficiently complex network would automatically learn to produce codes within a fixed range with the highest possible entropy, thereby meeting the upper bound. Nevertheless, the authors' second argument is convincing - requiring the network to produce such codes would force a specific "form" on how the compressor output is used, which to match the effective compression of the current system, would necessitate a more complex network capable of carrying out the computations currently handled by a separate variable-rate encoder used to store q.
