This paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression method using nonlinear transforms. The authors present a framework for jointly optimizing the analysis and synthesis transforms, as well as the entropy code, to achieve the best rate-distortion trade-off. The method is trained using a variant of stochastic gradient descent, with a continuous proxy for the discontinuous loss function arising from the quantizer.

The paper tackles the specific question of image compression, which is a fundamental problem in engineering. The approach is well-motivated, drawing inspiration from models of biological neurons and previous work on Gaussianizing image densities. The authors provide a thorough discussion of the relationship between their nonlinear transform coding framework and variational autoencoders, highlighting the key differences between the two approaches.

The paper supports its claims through extensive experimental results, including comparisons with standard image compression methods (JPEG and JPEG 2000) on a variety of test images. The results show that the proposed method generally exhibits better rate-distortion performance and a dramatic improvement in visual quality, especially at low bit rates.

One of the key contributions of the paper is the introduction of a differentiable version of the "rate" function, which allows for effective training with different rate-distortion trade-offs. This is a significant innovation, as it enables the use of gradient-based optimization methods for image compression.

Overall, I believe that this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of image compression and has the potential to impact other applications that benefit from differentiable approximations to similar functions.

Additional feedback:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed method, especially in comparison to established image compression algorithms.
* The authors may want to consider providing more insight into the optimization process, such as the choice of hyperparameters and the convergence behavior of the algorithm.
* It would be interesting to see an analysis of the robustness of the proposed method to different types of images and compression scenarios.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed method, especially in comparison to established image compression algorithms?
* How did you choose the hyperparameters for the optimization process, and what was the convergence behavior of the algorithm like?
* Have you considered applying the proposed method to other types of data, such as video or audio?
