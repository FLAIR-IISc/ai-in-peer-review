This paper presents an end-to-end trained image compression and decompression system that achieves superior bit-rate versus quality trade-offs compared to established image compression algorithms, such as JPEG-2000. A key contribution of this work is the demonstration of the efficacy of deep learning in a new application domain. Moreover, the authors introduce a differentiable version of the "rate" function, which enables effective training with varying rate-distortion trade-offs. This innovation is expected to have a broader impact, extending beyond image compression to other tasks that can benefit from differentiable approximations to similar functions.

The authors provided a thoughtful response to my pre-review question, addressing the concerns I raised. While I still argue that a sufficiently complex network, when optimized to minimize distortion under a fixed range and quantization, would automatically produce codes within a fixed range with the highest possible entropy, thereby meeting the upper bound, the authors' counterargument is convincing. By not explicitly enforcing a fixed range, the proposed approach avoids imposing a specific form on how the compressor output is used. Matching the effective compression of the current system would require a more complex network, capable of performing the computations currently handled by a separate variable rate encoder used to store q. This insight highlights the importance of the proposed differentiable rate function in enabling the development of efficient and effective compression systems.

Overall, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of image compression, demonstrating the potential of deep learning in achieving superior compression performance. The introduction of a differentiable rate function is a key innovation, with far-reaching implications for various applications beyond image compression.
