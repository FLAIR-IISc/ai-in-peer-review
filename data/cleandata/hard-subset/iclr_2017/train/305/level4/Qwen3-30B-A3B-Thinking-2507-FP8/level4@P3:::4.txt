

# Review for "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## Summary
This paper presents a compelling framework for end-to-end optimization of image compression through nonlinear transformations. The authors develop a model that jointly optimizes analysis and synthesis transforms, along with quantization, for rate-distortion performance. Their approach uses generalized divisive normalization (GDN) inspired by biological visual systems, enabling efficient compression through cascaded nonlinear transformations. Crucially, they introduce a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to facilitate gradient-based optimization, while still evaluating performance on actual bit rates rather than entropy estimates. The method demonstrates significant improvements in both rate-distortion performance and visual quality compared to standard JPEG and JPEG 2000, with improvements visible across all bit rates and image types. The work is well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of image compression.

## Decision
Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of image compression through a well-executed end-to-end optimization approach that clearly outperforms standard methods. The key reasons for acceptance are:
1. The paper provides a principled approach to optimizing the entire compression pipeline, addressing a long-standing challenge in the field.
2. The empirical results are compelling and consistently demonstrate improvements across multiple metrics and datasets.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper successfully addresses the three key questions for review. 

First, the paper tackles the specific problem of lossy image compression optimization, where the traditional approach separates the design of transform, quantizer, and entropy code. The authors propose a unified framework that jointly optimizes all components through a continuous relaxation of the quantization step, enabling end-to-end training.

Second, the approach is well-motivated and properly situated within the literature. The authors carefully review the history of transform coding, connect their work to biological visual models, and clearly differentiate their approach from related work in variational autoencoders and generative models. The paper demonstrates strong theoretical grounding while maintaining practical relevance to compression applications.

Third, the paper thoroughly supports its claims. The authors provide comprehensive experimental validation across multiple datasets, with both objective metrics (PSNR, MS-SSIM) and visual comparisons. The results consistently show improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000 across all tested bit rates. The authors also carefully address the relationship between their continuous relaxation and actual discrete compression performance, demonstrating that the approximation is valid for practical compression.

## Additional Feedback
While the paper is strong, these suggestions would further improve it:

1. The paper mentions that the method was optimized using MSE, but the visual improvements suggest perceptual optimization might yield even better results. Consider adding a brief discussion about how the authors might incorporate perceptual metrics in future work.

2. The comparison to JPEG 2000 could be strengthened by including results from more recent compression standards like HEIF or AV1, which have surpassed JPEG 2000 in many cases.

3. The paper notes that the method is optimized for grayscale, with RGB extensions requiring additional training. It would be helpful to provide a clear discussion of the computational overhead of the RGB extension and whether it scales efficiently to higher color depths.

4. The authors mention that the GDN/IGDN transforms are more efficient than alternatives like ReLU. A brief comparison of the number of parameters required for equivalent performance would strengthen this claim.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that the method "exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images." Could you elaborate on the specific cases where the method underperforms, and what might explain these exceptions?

2. The authors note that "the additive noise used in the objective function as a relaxation of quantization might also serve the purpose of making supervised classification networks more robust." Could you provide a brief discussion of how this insight might transfer to other domains beyond image compression?

3. The paper mentions that the method's visual quality improvements hold "for all images tested, and at all bit rates." Could you provide additional examples of particularly challenging images (e.g., those with complex textures or fine edges) where the method demonstrates clear advantages over JPEG and JPEG 2000?

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression. The authors have executed a well-designed study with compelling results that advance the state of the art in a meaningful way. I recommend acceptance.
