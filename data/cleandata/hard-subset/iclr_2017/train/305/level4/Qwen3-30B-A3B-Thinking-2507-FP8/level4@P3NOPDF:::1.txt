

This paper presents a compelling and well-executed advancement in neural image compression, establishing itself as the most convincing work in the field to date. The authors successfully integrate rate-distortion theory into their objective function, creating a coherent and theoretically grounded framework that significantly advances the state of the art. The writing is clear, logical, and accessible, effectively communicating complex technical contributions. Crucially, the comparison against JPEG2000 as a baseline—rather than solely against JPEG—provides a much more meaningful and rigorous benchmark, addressing a key limitation in prior work. This thoughtful experimental design strongly supports the paper's claims and positions it for significant impact in both academia and industry.

To further strengthen the paper and ensure its position as the definitive reference in neural image compression, two specific additions are recommended. First, the authors should include quantitative results on standard test images (Lena, Barbara, Baboon) alongside the reported metrics. While the current results are robust, demonstrating performance on these widely recognized benchmarks is essential for direct comparison with classical methods and to meet community expectations. Second, the paper should explicitly clarify the positioning of neural network approaches relative to the absolute best classical methods. The current submission references state-of-the-art techniques but does not sufficiently articulate how the proposed neural method compares to the *very highest-performing* classical approaches (e.g., the methods referenced in the reviewer's prior questions). A dedicated table or discussion comparing the proposed method's rate-distortion performance against the top classical methods (including their PSNR/MS-SSIM values at equivalent bitrates) would resolve this ambiguity and significantly enhance the paper's contribution. The current presentation leaves the reader uncertain whether the neural approach represents a marginal improvement or a substantial leap over the best non-neural alternatives.

The paper's core contributions are robust and well-supported. The use of the rate-distortion objective is not merely theoretical but directly enables the model's superior performance, particularly at low bitrates where traditional methods struggle. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the impact of key design choices, such as the entropy model and network architecture. The results consistently show clear improvements over prior neural compression methods (e.g., Ballé et al., 2016) and, critically, over JPEG2000 across a range of metrics and bitrates. This consistent outperformance, especially in the visually important low-rate regime, is the paper's strongest point.

The technical novelty lies in the elegant fusion of information theory with deep learning, avoiding the common pitfall of treating compression as a mere pixel reconstruction task. The proposed framework is generalizable and provides a strong foundation for future work. The authors effectively argue that their method achieves a better rate-distortion trade-off than previous neural approaches and, as demonstrated by the JPEG2000 comparison, represents a meaningful advancement over established standards.

In summary, this is an outstanding paper that delivers a significant, well-motivated, and technically sound contribution to image compression. The core methodology is compelling, the experimental validation is thorough (with the noted exception of the classical comparison), and the writing is exemplary. With the requested additions—specific results on standard test images and a clear, direct comparison to the absolute best classical methods—the paper will be exceptionally strong and highly likely to become a cornerstone reference in the field. These additions are not merely desirable but necessary to fully establish the work's significance against the most relevant prior art. The paper is clearly of high quality and merits acceptance. The minor requested clarifications would elevate it from excellent to outstanding.
