

This paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression framework that jointly trains nonlinear analysis/synthesis transforms and quantization for superior rate-distortion performance. The authors model the compression pipeline as a nonlinear transformation (using generalized divisive normalization inspired by biological visual systems), followed by uniform quantization and a nonlinear inverse transform. Crucially, they introduce a continuous relaxation of the quantization step via additive uniform noise to enable gradient-based optimization of the entire pipeline—addressing the intractability of direct optimization in the discrete domain. The method achieves state-of-the-art rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 across test images, with dramatic visual quality improvements at all bit rates, supported by both PSNR and perceptual MS-SSIM metrics.

**Decision: Accept**  
This paper merits acceptance primarily for (1) its novel, well-motivated approach to end-to-end compression optimization via continuous relaxation of quantization, and (2) its compelling empirical validation showing significant quantitative and qualitative improvements over standard methods.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The core contribution—using a continuous proxy for quantization to enable joint optimization—is elegantly motivated and rigorously justified. The authors clearly articulate how their relaxation (adding uniform noise to the quantized coefficients) provides a valid approximation of both rate (via differential entropy) and distortion (via marginal moments), with empirical validation in Section 4 confirming minimal bias in the relaxed objective. This avoids the common pitfall of optimizing generative models *for* compression without addressing discrete-domain constraints. The method’s biological inspiration (GDN) is well-placed in the literature, building on prior work in visual coding while demonstrating superior efficiency in Gaussianizing image statistics.  

The results are exceptionally strong. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000 across diverse test sets (Kodak, standard test images, and personal photos), with the most striking advantage being the *visual quality*—the compressed images lack the blocking/aliasing artifacts of linear transform coders (e.g., JPEG) while maintaining better rate-distortion trade-offs. The MS-SSIM results (Figure 7) confirm perceptual improvements across all bit rates, and the PSNR gains (especially at low bit rates) are statistically significant. Critically, the authors implement a complete compression pipeline (including entropy coding) and report *actual bit rates*, not entropy estimates, enabling fair comparison to established methods. This rigor distinguishes the work from prior generative-model-based approaches (e.g., Gregor et al., 2016) that rely on unreliable entropy approximations.

**Additional Feedback**  
To strengthen the paper further:  
1. **Clarify the computational cost trade-off**: The draft notes that encoding/decoding is efficient post-training, but the training cost (using ImageNet) is high. A brief discussion of deployment feasibility (e.g., inference latency on mobile devices) would contextualize the practical impact.  
2. **Expand the "why" behind GDN**: While the paper notes GDN’s efficiency over ReLU, a sentence linking the biological motivation (local gain control) to the observed compression gains would reinforce the design choice.  
3. **Address RGB model limitations**: The RGB model uses separate transforms per channel; a sentence on how this compares to joint color-space optimization (e.g., YCbCr) would improve clarity.  

**Clarifying Questions for Authors**  
1. The paper states that GDN/IGDN achieves better rate-distortion efficiency than ReLU-based transforms, requiring fewer parameters. Could the authors provide a brief comparison of model size (e.g., number of parameters) between GDN and ReLU variants for the same bit-rate performance?  
2. In Section 4, the method outperforms JPEG 2000 at low bit rates but shows modest gains at high bit rates for some images (e.g., Figure 15). Is this due to the fixed quantization bin size (1) in the code space, or are there inherent limitations in the transform architecture for high-fidelity compression?  
3. The entropy coding uses a simple CABAC variant without spatial adaptation. How much of the rate-distortion gap versus JPEG 2000 (which uses advanced spatial context modeling) is attributable to the entropy code versus the core compression transforms?  

This work makes a significant contribution to lossy compression by demonstrating that end-to-end optimization—enabled by a clever continuous relaxation—can surpass decades of engineered transform coding. The paper is well-structured, technically sound, and the results are thoroughly validated. It represents a compelling step toward learning-based compression that delivers both objective and perceptual improvements.
