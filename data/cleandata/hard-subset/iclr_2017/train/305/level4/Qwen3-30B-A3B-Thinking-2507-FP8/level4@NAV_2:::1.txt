

1. The paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression framework using nonlinear analysis and synthesis transformations inspired by biological visual systems. The authors introduce a generalized divisive normalization (GDN) transform that enables joint optimization of the entire compression pipeline for rate-distortion performance. The method demonstrates superior rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 across multiple test images, with particularly notable improvements in visual quality at all bit rates, as supported by both objective (MS-SSIM) and subjective evaluations.

2. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression with strong theoretical grounding and empirical validation, and the comparison to JPEG 2000 provides a meaningful baseline for evaluation.

3. The approach is well-motivated, clearly situated within the literature on rate-distortion optimization and transform coding, and effectively addresses a fundamental problem in image compression. The authors properly justify the use of GDN transformations, which have been previously shown to be effective in Gaussianizing image densities. The empirical results are scientifically rigorous, with careful evaluation across multiple test images and comprehensive comparisons to standard compression methods. The continuous relaxation of the quantization step for optimization is well-justified, and the evaluation on actual bit rates rather than entropy estimates ensures meaningful comparison with existing methods.

4. The paper would benefit from including results on standard test images (Lena, Barbara, Baboon) to provide a more comprehensive comparison with classical methods. Additionally, the authors should clarify the positioning of their method relative to the best previous approaches in the field, particularly in terms of both rate-distortion performance and visual quality. A more detailed comparison with recent state-of-the-art classical compression methods would strengthen the paper's impact.

5. 
1. Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison of their method with state-of-the-art classical image compression methods, including specific benchmarks on standard test images like Lena, Barbara, and Baboon?
2. How does the performance of the proposed method compare to recent neural network-based compression methods that have been published since the initial version of this paper, and what are the key differences in their approaches?
