

This paper presents a compelling end-to-end trainable image compression system that achieves superior rate-distortion performance compared to established methods like JPEG-2000. The core contribution lies in the introduction of a differentiable approximation of the rate function, enabling effective training with customizable rate-distortion trade-offs. This innovation not only demonstrates deep learning’s viability for image compression but also offers a broadly applicable technique for tasks requiring differentiable approximations of rate-like functions. The experimental results are well-justified, with clear comparisons against strong baselines and insightful ablation studies. The work has significant potential to influence both compression research and other domains needing differentiable rate modeling.

The authors provided a comprehensive response to my pre-review query regarding the necessity of the fixed-range quantization constraint. While I initially questioned whether a sufficiently complex model could automatically learn to produce high-entropy representations within a fixed range (thereby approaching the theoretical entropy upper bound under uniform quantization), I now acknowledge the authors' compelling counterpoint. Specifically, enforcing a fixed range with a separate entropy coder (as in standard compression pipelines) requires the compressor to output representations compatible with that specific encoding strategy. To replicate this functionality without the separate entropy coder—i.e., to directly produce entropy-optimized codes—the model would need to implicitly learn the entire compression pipeline’s computational steps. This would necessitate significantly greater architectural complexity to handle what the current system delegates to a dedicated entropy coder. The authors’ approach elegantly avoids this burden by aligning the quantization range directly with the entropy coder’s requirements, yielding a more efficient and practical solution.

The paper’s technical execution is robust, with clear descriptions of the differentiable rate approximation and its integration into the training process. The ablation studies effectively isolate the impact of key components, such as the rate approximation and the quantization strategy. The results consistently show improvements in rate-distortion performance across multiple datasets and metrics, with the model achieving competitive or superior results to JPEG-2000 at lower bitrates. The visualization of reconstructed images further supports the quantitative gains, demonstrating perceptual quality improvements.

The work is well-motivated and addresses a meaningful gap in applying deep learning to compression. The differentiable rate function is a particularly elegant contribution that could extend beyond image compression to other rate-constrained tasks, such as neural network compression or communication systems. The clarity of the writing and the thoroughness of the experimental validation strengthen the paper’s impact.

Minor suggestions for clarity: The paper could briefly clarify how the differentiable rate approximation handles the discrete nature of quantization (e.g., via straight-through estimators) in the main text, though this is adequately covered in the supplementary material. Additionally, while the ablation studies are strong, explicitly stating the computational overhead of the proposed method versus baselines would further contextualize its practicality.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field. The core idea is novel, the implementation is sound, and the results are convincing. The differentiable rate function is a valuable technical tool with clear potential for broader adoption. The authors have successfully demonstrated both the efficacy of their approach for image compression and its conceptual significance for differentiable modeling of rate constraints. I recommend acceptance.
