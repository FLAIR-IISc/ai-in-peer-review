

This paper presents the most compelling work on deep neural network-based image compression to date. The integration of rate-distortion theory into the loss function is elegantly executed and fundamentally strengthens the proposed framework. The authors wisely choose JPEG2000 as the primary baseline, a significant improvement over prior works that only compared against JPEG, providing a more rigorous and relevant benchmark for modern compression methods. The writing is clear, well-structured, and the technical contributions are presented with precision. This work has substantial potential for significant impact in the field.

To further solidify its contribution and maximize impact, I recommend including quantitative results on standard test images (Lena, Barbara, Baboon) alongside the existing datasets. The current submission does not provide these, making it difficult to assess performance on widely recognized benchmarks. Additionally, the paper should explicitly position the neural network approach against the strongest classical methods. While the comparison to JPEG2000 is valuable, the manuscript does not sufficiently clarify how the proposed method stands relative to the absolute state-of-the-art in classical image compression (e.g., methods like those by J. Chen, M. K. Mihcak, or other top-tier classical approaches referenced in recent literature). The current text leaves the reader uncertain about whether the neural approach consistently outperforms or merely matches the best non-NN techniques across key metrics like rate-distortion curves or PSNR/MS-SSIM at specific bitrates.

The authors should address these points by adding the requested test images and providing a clear, concise table or figure comparing their method directly against the top classical methods cited in the literature. This would decisively answer the critical question of where neural network compression currently stands relative to the established state-of-the-art, strengthening the paper's core argument and ensuring its findings are unambiguously understood by the community. The current work is exceptionally strong; these additions would make it definitive.
