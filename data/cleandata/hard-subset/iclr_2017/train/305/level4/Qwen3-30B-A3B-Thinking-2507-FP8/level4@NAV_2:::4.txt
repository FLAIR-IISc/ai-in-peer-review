

1. The paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression method using nonlinear transforms inspired by biological visual systems. The authors develop a framework that jointly optimizes analysis and synthesis transforms along with quantization parameters to achieve superior rate-distortion performance. Their approach introduces a continuous relaxation for the quantization step, enabling effective optimization through stochastic gradient descent. The method demonstrates significant improvements over standard compression techniques like JPEG and JPEG 2000, particularly in visual quality across all bit rates, as evidenced by both objective metrics (MS-SSIM) and perceptual evaluation.

2. I recommend acceptance with the following key reasons: (1) The paper presents a well-motivated approach that effectively bridges biological inspiration with practical image compression needs, and (2) The results demonstrate scientifically rigorous improvements in both objective performance metrics and visual quality across multiple test images.

3. The paper's contribution is well-placed within the literature, building on previous work in neural image compression while making a clear technical advance through the introduction of the continuous relaxation for quantization. The authors provide a thorough theoretical foundation connecting their approach to rate-distortion theory and variational autoencoders, while carefully distinguishing their method from generative modeling approaches. The experimental results are comprehensive, using multiple test images and both objective metrics (PSNR, MS-SSIM) and visual assessment to demonstrate consistent improvements over established methods. The implementation details are sufficiently complete to allow replication, and the authors provide online access to all test images and results for verification.

4. To further strengthen the paper, I recommend including a brief discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, particularly in terms of encoding and decoding times. Additionally, a more detailed analysis of performance across different image categories (e.g., natural scenes vs. textures vs. text) would provide valuable insights into the method's strengths and limitations.

5. I would appreciate clarification on two points: (1) How do the encoding and decoding computational requirements of this method compare to standard compression techniques, particularly for real-time applications? (2) Are there any specific types of images or image features where the proposed method shows particularly strong or weak performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000?
