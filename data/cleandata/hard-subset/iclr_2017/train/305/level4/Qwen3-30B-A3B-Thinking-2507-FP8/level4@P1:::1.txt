

Review for ICLR 2017

1. Summary
This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression method using nonlinear transformations, specifically designed to improve rate-distortion performance over traditional approaches like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The authors develop a framework that jointly optimizes analysis and synthesis transforms, along with quantization parameters, using a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization. The approach leverages generalized divisive normalization (GDN) inspired by biological visual systems, and demonstrates significant improvements in both objective metrics (PSNR) and perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) across a wide range of bit rates. The paper makes a strong case for the effectiveness of end-to-end optimization in image compression, providing a complete solution that operates at any point along the rate-distortion curve.

2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a compelling contribution to image compression with strong empirical evidence and a well-motivated theoretical foundation. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) The approach is rigorously grounded in rate-distortion theory and demonstrates clear, quantifiable improvements over established baselines; (2) The paper provides comprehensive empirical evaluation that thoroughly supports its claims.

3. Supporting Arguments
The paper successfully addresses the core challenge of image compression by developing an end-to-end trainable system that optimizes the entire compression pipeline. The authors' use of GDN nonlinearities to model biological visual systems is well-motivated and effectively captures the statistical structure of natural images. The continuous relaxation of the quantization step for optimization purposes is both theoretically sound and practically effective, as evidenced by the paper's empirical results.

The evaluation is thorough and well-designed, comparing against JPEG 2000 (a more relevant baseline than JPEG alone) across multiple metrics and image sets. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements in visual quality across all tested images and bit rates, with substantial gains in MS-SSIM that correlate with visual perception. The authors' careful distinction between the continuous optimization framework and discrete compression implementation is particularly noteworthy, as it avoids the common pitfall of comparing continuous entropy estimates with actual bit rates.

The paper's contribution is positioned well within the literature, building on previous work in neural network image compression while making significant advances in both methodology and performance. The comparison with related work (particularly Toderici et al. and Gregor et al.) is fair and insightful, highlighting the paper's advantages in systematic optimization and practical implementation.

4. Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, consider the following points:

- The paper mentions "Lena," "Barbara," "Peppers," and "Mandrill" as test images, but it would be helpful to explicitly state how your method compares to state-of-the-art results specifically on these standard test images. Many compression papers include results on these images as a benchmark, so including them would help position your work more clearly within the field.

- While the paper demonstrates good performance across multiple images, it would be valuable to include a more detailed analysis of which image types or features benefit most from your approach compared to traditional methods. This could help readers understand the specific advantages of your framework.

- The paper mentions that "the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested, and at all bit rates." To strengthen this claim, it would be helpful to include a table or figure showing the exact MS-SSIM improvements across the different test images and bit rates, rather than relying solely on qualitative descriptions.

- The paper states "our method exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images." It would be useful to clarify which specific images underperform and why, as this could provide valuable insights for future work.

Questions for Authors
1. The paper mentions that "the compressed images are much more natural in appearance than those compressed with JPEG or JPEG 2000." Could you provide specific examples of image features that your method preserves particularly well compared to traditional methods, and how these features relate to the GDN nonlinearities?

2. The paper states that "the perceptual advantages of our method hold for all images tested, and at all bit rates." Can you clarify whether this holds across different image categories (e.g., natural scenes, textures, man-made objects) or if certain image types benefit more from your approach?

3. The paper compares against JPEG 2000 but doesn't discuss how your method might compare to more recent compression approaches like BPG (Blind Pixel Grouping) or other advanced codecs. Would it be possible to include a brief comparison with these more recent methods to better position your work within the current state of the art?
