

This paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression method using nonlinear transforms, a uniform quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transformation. The authors construct a model with three successive stages of convolutional linear filters and nonlinear activation functions, inspired by biological neuron models. Crucially, they introduce a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable joint optimization of the entire model for rate-distortion performance using stochastic gradient descent. The approach demonstrates superior rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 across various test images, with particularly notable improvements in visual quality supported by objective metrics like MS-SSIM.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong contribution by effectively bridging rate-distortion theory with deep learning for image compression, and the empirical results are compelling. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the paper provides a well-motivated framework that properly addresses the rate-distortion optimization problem with appropriate theoretical grounding, and (2) the empirical results demonstrate clear improvements over standard compression methods, with both quantitative and qualitative evidence supporting the claims.

The paper's technical contribution is significant. The authors successfully address the challenge of optimizing the entire compression pipeline end-to-end, which has been difficult with traditional methods. The use of generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and its inverse (IGDN) as nonlinear transformations is well-justified by prior work on modeling visual systems and Gaussianizing image statistics. The continuous relaxation of quantization to enable gradient-based optimization is a clever and effective solution to a fundamental problem in compression model training. The comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 is appropriate, and the demonstration of improved visual quality across all bit rates is particularly convincing.

However, the paper would benefit from greater clarity in positioning against the best previous methods. While the comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 is appropriate, the authors should explicitly state how their method compares to the absolute state-of-the-art in image compression, including more recent classical methods. The reviewers would benefit from seeing results on standard test images like Lena, Barbara, and Baboon (not Gibbons, as mentioned in the draft review) alongside state-of-the-art classical methods. This would help readers understand precisely where this neural network approach stands relative to the best possible non-deep learning methods.

Additionally, the paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the computational cost trade-offs. While the authors mention that encoding/decoding is efficient, it would be helpful to provide more concrete comparisons of training time versus inference time, and how the model scales with image resolution.

The paper's theoretical connection to variational autoencoders is well-explained, but the differences between compression optimization and generative modeling could be emphasized more. The authors correctly note that their method operates in the discrete domain (unlike most generative models), but the implications for practical implementation could be expanded.

To further improve the paper, I suggest the authors:

1. Include results on standard test images (Lena, Barbara, Baboon) compared to the best classical compression methods, not just JPEG and JPEG 2000.
2. Provide a clearer comparison of their method against the absolute state-of-the-art in image compression.
3. Add a brief discussion of computational trade-offs between training and inference for practical deployment.
4. Clarify the relationship between their method and the best-performing classical approaches in the introduction and discussion.

To help clarify my understanding, I would like the authors to address the following questions:

1. How does your method compare to the best classical image compression methods (e.g., BPG, WebP, or other recent state-of-the-art approaches) in terms of both rate-distortion performance and visual quality?
2. Can you provide more details on the computational cost of training versus inference, and how this compares to traditional compression methods?
3. What are the most significant limitations of your approach that would prevent it from being deployed in real-world applications?

The paper is well-written and makes a compelling contribution to the field of image compression. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent addition to the ICLR conference. The approach is both theoretically sound and empirically validated, and the results demonstrate meaningful improvements over established methods.
