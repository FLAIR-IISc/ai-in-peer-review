

# Review of "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## Summary
This paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression system that jointly trains analysis and synthesis transforms, along with a quantizer, to minimize rate-distortion trade-offs. The authors introduce a novel continuous relaxation of the quantization step, enabling gradient-based optimization of the entire compression pipeline. The method employs cascaded nonlinear transforms with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) inspired by biological visual systems, followed by uniform scalar quantization. The approach demonstrates superior rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 across multiple test images, with particularly striking improvements in visual quality that are consistent across all bit rates. The authors also thoughtfully address the relationship between their framework and variational autoencoders, clarifying key differences in optimization objectives and practical implementation.

## Decision
Accept. This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression by introducing a novel end-to-end trainable framework that outperforms established methods in both rate-distortion performance and visual quality. The continuous relaxation of the quantization step is a particularly elegant and effective solution to the optimization challenge in lossy compression.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a strong case for its contributions through three key aspects:

First, the authors provide a clear and well-motivated solution to a fundamental challenge in compression: the non-differentiability of the quantization step. Their continuous relaxation approach is both theoretically sound and practically effective, enabling joint optimization of the entire compression pipeline. This technique could have broader impact beyond image compression, potentially influencing other domains where discrete representations need to be optimized.

Second, the empirical results demonstrate compelling improvements over established methods. The paper shows not just marginal improvements in PSNR, but significant visual quality gains that are consistently observed across all test images and bit rates. The MS-SSIM metrics corroborate the visual improvements, providing objective evidence for the qualitative advantages. The authors carefully address the challenge of fair comparison by implementing a complete compression pipeline with an entropy code, rather than relying on theoretical entropy estimates.

Third, the paper makes a thoughtful contribution to the understanding of the relationship between compression and generative models. The authors clearly articulate how their framework differs from variational autoencoders, particularly in terms of optimization objectives and practical implementation constraints. This clarifies an important conceptual distinction that has been blurred in previous work.

## Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, consider the following points:

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly regarding inference time compared to standard codecs. While the authors note that encoding/decoding is efficient, providing specific benchmarks would help readers understand the practical trade-offs.

2. The relationship between the GDN nonlinearity and the visual quality improvements could be explored more deeply. While the authors mention preliminary experiments with other activation functions, a more systematic comparison of different nonlinearities' impact on compression performance would strengthen their claims about the efficiency of GDN.

3. The discussion of the practical limitations of the approach (e.g., training data requirements, scalability to higher resolutions) would be valuable for readers considering real-world implementation.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that the method "exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images." Could you provide more specific statistics on how many images showed consistent improvements across all bit rates, and which images were the exceptions?

2. The authors note that their method was optimized using MSE as the distortion metric, yet achieves superior visual quality. How do you expect the performance to change if the method were optimized using a perceptual metric like MS-SSIM directly, rather than MSE?

3. The paper mentions that "the continuous relaxation using additive noise was used only for training purposes." Could you elaborate on the specific impact of this relaxation on the final compression performance? Were there any significant differences between the relaxed loss function and the actual rate-distortion values achieved in practice?

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of image compression. The technical approach is innovative and well-executed, the results are compelling, and the insights about the relationship between compression and generative models are valuable. I believe this work will have significant impact on both the compression community and the broader field of machine learning.
