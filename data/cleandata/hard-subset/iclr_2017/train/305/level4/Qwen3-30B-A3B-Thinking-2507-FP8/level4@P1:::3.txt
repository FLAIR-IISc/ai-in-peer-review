

This paper introduces an end-to-end optimized image compression framework that uses nonlinear transformations to achieve superior rate-distortion performance compared to standard methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The authors propose a novel approach where the entire compression pipeline—analysis transform, quantizer, and synthesis transform—is jointly optimized using a continuous relaxation of the rate function, enabling effective training via gradient descent. The key innovation lies in their differentiable approximation of the rate function, which allows the model to be trained for any desired point along the rate-distortion curve by adjusting a single trade-off parameter. The authors demonstrate that their method not only achieves better rate-distortion performance than JPEG and JPEG 2000 across various bit rates but also produces significantly better visual quality, as confirmed by both subjective evaluation and objective metrics like MS-SSIM.

Decision: Accept.

Key reasons: 
1. The paper presents a well-motivated approach that addresses a longstanding challenge in image compression through a principled end-to-end optimization framework.
2. The experimental results demonstrate clear improvements over standard methods across multiple evaluation metrics and image sets, with strong empirical validation.

Supporting arguments:
The paper makes a compelling case for the viability of their approach. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of traditional transform coding methods and how their end-to-end framework overcomes these limitations by jointly optimizing all components. Their continuous relaxation of the rate function is technically sound, and the theoretical connection to variational autoencoders (while acknowledging important differences) adds depth to their contribution. The experimental validation is thorough, with comparisons across multiple standard image datasets and both PSNR and MS-SSIM metrics. The results consistently show that their method outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000, particularly in visual quality, which is a critical aspect for real-world applications. The demonstration that the method works well for both grayscale and RGB images further strengthens the contribution.

Additional feedback:
While the paper is strong overall, there are a few areas where the presentation could be improved. First, the connection between the proposed method and variational autoencoders (Section 3.1) could be more clearly articulated. The authors note several important differences between their approach and VAEs, but a more structured comparison would help readers better understand the novelty. Second, the paper mentions that "for the two largest values of λ, and for RGB models, we increased the network capacity" but doesn't provide justification for why these specific values of λ required increased capacity. A brief discussion of this phenomenon would be helpful. Third, while the authors acknowledge that using a perceptual metric might further improve visual quality, they don't explore this direction in depth. A short discussion of potential limitations and future work in this area would be valuable.

Questions for authors:
1. The paper states that "the relaxed (differential) entropy provides a somewhat positively biased estimate of the discrete entropy for the coarser quantization regime." Can you provide more detail on the magnitude of this bias and how it might affect the practical implementation of your method?
2. The authors mention that "for any affine and invertible perceptual transform and any translation-invariant metric, it can be shown to correspond to the density" in equation (16). Could you provide a sketch of the proof or refer to a relevant resource where this is formally derived?
3. In the experiments, the authors compare to JPEG and JPEG 2000, but don't mention the compression times for training versus encoding/decoding. Given that the paper emphasizes the efficiency of encoding/decoding, it would be helpful to provide some sense of how long it takes to compress an image using their method versus standard methods.
