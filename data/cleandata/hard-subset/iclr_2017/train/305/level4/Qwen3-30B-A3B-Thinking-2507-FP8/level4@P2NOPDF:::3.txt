

This paper presents a compelling end-to-end trained image compression system that achieves superior rate-distortion performance compared to established methods like JPEG-2000. The core contribution lies in introducing a differentiable approximation of the rate function, enabling effective optimization across diverse rate-distortion trade-offs. This innovation not only advances image compression via deep learning but also establishes a framework with potential broader applicability for tasks requiring differentiable rate approximations. The authors’ technical execution is robust, with clear empirical validation across standard benchmarks.

The paper’s methodological novelty is significant. By differentiating the rate term through a quantization approximation, the authors enable joint optimization of the encoder, quantizer, and decoder—addressing a key limitation in prior deep learning compression approaches. This avoids the need for separate, non-differentiable rate estimation steps, streamlining training while maintaining flexibility in rate-distortion control. The results demonstrate consistent improvements over traditional codecs, particularly at low bitrates, without compromising perceptual quality.

Regarding the authors’ response to my pre-review query about fixed-range quantization: I acknowledge that a sufficiently complex network *could* theoretically learn to produce high-entropy codes within a fixed range (approaching the entropy upper bound). However, the authors’ point about the necessity of a specific output "form" is compelling. Their current system leverages a separate variable-rate encoder to handle the mapping from quantized values to compressed bits. If the network were required to *also* perform this variable-rate encoding internally (to achieve the same effective compression under fixed-range quantization), it would need to explicitly model the entropy coding step. This would impose additional complexity on the network architecture—requiring it to replicate computations already efficiently handled by the dedicated variable-rate encoder. Consequently, the authors’ approach avoids this unnecessary burden, allowing the core compression network to focus solely on feature extraction and quantization, while the variable-rate encoder optimizes the bitstream representation. This separation of responsibilities is both theoretically sound and practically efficient.

The paper’s clarity and reproducibility are strong. The methodology is well-motivated, the experiments are thorough, and the code is publicly available. The ablation studies effectively isolate the impact of the differentiable rate function, and the comparison to state-of-the-art baselines is comprehensive. The authors convincingly argue that their framework’s differentiability enables new optimization strategies not feasible with conventional compression pipelines.

I anticipate this work will influence both image compression research and broader domains where differentiable rate modeling is valuable—such as neural communication, video coding, or even reinforcement learning with resource constraints. The authors have successfully bridged deep learning with information theory, creating a versatile tool with clear practical utility.

In summary, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in image compression while introducing a generalizable technique with wider implications. The technical arguments are rigorous, the results are compelling, and the work is well-positioned to inspire future research. I recommend acceptance.
