

This paper presents a compelling end-to-end approach to image compression using nonlinear transforms optimized for rate-distortion performance. The authors develop a framework that jointly optimizes analysis and synthesis transforms along with a quantizer using stochastic gradient descent, leveraging a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based training. The key innovation lies in using generalized divisive normalization (GDN) inspired by biological visual systems, which enables more effective representation of image statistics compared to linear transforms. The approach demonstrates significant improvements over JPEG and JPEG 2000 across multiple metrics, including both objective measures (PSNR) and perceptual quality (MS-SSIM), with visually superior results at all bit rates.

Decision: Accept.

Key reasons for acceptance: The paper makes a substantial contribution to image compression by demonstrating that end-to-end optimization of nonlinear transforms can achieve superior rate-distortion performance while simultaneously improving visual quality. The authors provide strong empirical evidence supporting their claims through comprehensive comparisons against established baselines, and their theoretical framework connecting rate-distortion optimization to variational inference is well-motivated and clearly articulated.

Supporting arguments: The paper excels in addressing the three key questions for review. First, the specific problem tackled is clearly defined as end-to-end optimization of image compression for rate-distortion performance, moving beyond traditional separate optimization of transform, quantizer, and entropy code components. Second, the approach is well-motivated through careful literature review, demonstrating how biological-inspired nonlinearities (GDN) address limitations of linear transform coding methods. Third, the claims are thoroughly supported by both theoretical analysis and extensive empirical evaluation, including visual comparisons, PSNR, and MS-SSIM metrics across multiple test images.

The paper's strength lies in its practical demonstration that neural network-based compression can outperform established standards, with the authors carefully addressing how their continuous relaxation approach translates to discrete compression performance. Their implementation of a practical entropy code and evaluation using actual bit rates (rather than entropy estimates) provides a fair comparison to existing methods. The visual quality improvements are particularly compelling, as they demonstrate that the approach avoids the blocking and ringing artifacts common in linear transform coding.

Additional feedback: To further strengthen the paper, consider adding a more detailed comparison to the most recent state-of-the-art methods beyond JPEG and JPEG 2000. While the paper appropriately compares against these established baselines, including results from more recent approaches (such as those by Toderici et al. or Gregor et al.) would provide better context for the paper's contribution. The authors should explicitly state where their method stands relative to the best previous methods in the field.

The discussion of the relationship to variational autoencoders is insightful but could be more precise in highlighting the key differences that enable the compression framework to outperform generative models in this specific application. A clearer statement of how their approach differs from previous work in neural image compression would help position their contribution more effectively.

Questions for authors: 
1. How do you plan to address the computational cost of training these models, particularly for large-scale deployment where training resources may be limited? 
2. The paper mentions that GDN/IGDN transforms are more efficient than pointwise nonlinearities, but does this efficiency advantage persist when considering the full end-to-end compression pipeline, including entropy coding? 
3. The authors state that their method works well for grayscale images but note that for color images, they trained separate models. Could the authors comment on whether a unified color model could be developed that might improve performance while reducing model complexity?

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression, demonstrating that end-to-end optimization of nonlinear transforms can deliver both superior rate-distortion performance and perceptual quality. The authors have carefully addressed the practical challenges of implementing a complete compression system, and their empirical results provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of their approach. I believe this paper would make an excellent contribution to ICLR 2017.
