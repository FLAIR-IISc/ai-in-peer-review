

This paper presents a compelling end-to-end approach to image compression that directly optimizes rate-distortion performance through a novel differentiable relaxation of the discrete operations in traditional compression pipelines. The authors demonstrate that by reformulating the compression process as a continuous optimization problem—using a relaxation technique to approximate quantization and entropy coding—they enable effective training via standard backpropagation on natural image datasets. The method learns a joint representation that balances perceptual quality and bitrate efficiency without relying on hand-engineered components, achieving state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks (e.g., CLIC, KODAK) while maintaining competitive computational efficiency.

The technical contribution is well-motivated and clearly articulated. The relaxation mechanism is elegantly designed to bridge the gap between discrete compression operations and differentiable optimization, a key challenge in neural compression. The authors provide thorough ablation studies to validate the necessity of their relaxation strategy, showing significant performance gains over baselines that use naive approximations or discrete optimization. The experimental results are robust, with comprehensive comparisons against both classical methods (e.g., JPEG, JPEG2000) and recent neural approaches, consistently demonstrating superior rate-distortion curves. The analysis of learned representations and quantization behavior further strengthens the paper’s claims, offering valuable insights into how the network adapts to perceptual quality constraints.

A notable strength is the paper’s practical relevance: the proposed framework is designed for end-to-end training and deployment, avoiding the need for complex post-processing or heuristic adjustments common in prior work. The clarity of the methodology and results makes the contribution accessible and reproducible, which is critical for the broader ML community. The writing is precise and well-structured, with figures that effectively illustrate key concepts like the relaxation’s impact on rate-distortion trade-offs.

The only minor limitation is the lack of explicit discussion on the computational overhead of training compared to simpler baselines, though the inference efficiency is well-documented. This is a reasonable trade-off given the substantial performance gains, and the paper’s focus on end-to-end optimization justifies the approach.

Overall, this work makes a significant and well-executed contribution to the field of learned image compression. The method is innovative, the experiments are rigorous, and the results are clearly presented. It advances the state of the art while providing a principled framework that could influence future work in neural compression and related domains. I strongly recommend acceptance.
