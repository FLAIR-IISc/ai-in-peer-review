

This paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression method using nonlinear transforms inspired by biological visual systems, demonstrating significant improvements over standard methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The authors construct a compression pipeline with nonlinear analysis and synthesis transformations, a uniform quantizer, and an adaptive entropy code. Their approach uses generalized divisive normalization (GDN) in a cascaded architecture, optimized jointly for rate-distortion performance using a continuous relaxation of the quantization step. The method achieves superior rate-distortion performance and substantially better visual quality across all bit rates, with improvements supported by both PSNR and MS-SSIM metrics.

Decision: Accept.

Key reasons: The paper makes a strong empirical contribution to image compression by demonstrating clear performance advantages over established standards, with visual quality improvements that are consistently observed across all test images and bit rates. The method is well-motivated, properly contextualized within the literature, and provides a practical implementation with adaptive entropy coding.

The paper effectively addresses the core problem of rate-distortion optimization in image compression through a well-designed end-to-end learning framework. The authors demonstrate that their nonlinear transform coding approach, when optimized for rate-distortion, outperforms traditional methods both quantitatively (PSNR) and perceptually (MS-SSIM). The visual quality improvements are particularly notable, as they represent a significant step beyond the common artifacts of linear transform coding methods.

The relationship to variational autoencoders is well-explained with important distinctions highlighted. The authors correctly note that while the optimization problem resembles variational inference, their method operates in the discrete domain for practical compression, whereas VAEs are continuous. This clarification is valuable for the community.

The paper is well-written with clear explanations of the methodology and comprehensive experimental evaluation. The authors provide thorough comparisons to JPEG and JPEG 2000 across multiple image sets, including Kodak, standard test images, and personal photographs. The results are consistent and compelling, with the visual quality improvements being especially convincing.

Additional feedback: The authors should clarify the relationship between the continuous relaxation used for optimization and the actual discrete implementation. While they note the relaxation is only for training, a brief discussion of how the continuous optimization translates to the discrete implementation would strengthen the paper. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison of the contribution of adaptive entropy coding versus the deeper network architecture, as this appears to be a significant component of the performance gains.

Specific questions for authors:
1. The paper states that the method was optimized for MSE rather than a perceptual metric as in Ballé et al. (2016). Could the authors elaborate on the rationale for this choice, particularly given the strong visual improvements observed with MSE optimization? How do they expect performance to change with perceptual metric optimization?

2. Equation 10 appears to have a potential issue: the partition function should likely depend on g_s(y; theta) rather than being independent of the synthesis transform. If this is a correct observation, how does this affect the equivalence to VAEs for non-Euclidean metrics?

3. The paper mentions that the authors have "preliminary results" showing similar performance with other activation functions, but require more parameters. Could they provide more details about these comparisons, including the specific activation functions tested and the parameter efficiency metrics?

4. The paper claims "dramatic improvement in visual quality for all images at all bit rates." Could the authors provide a more nuanced analysis of where the improvements are most pronounced (e.g., specific image types or bit rate ranges) to help readers understand the method's strengths and limitations?

The authors' work represents a significant contribution to the field of image compression, with strong empirical results that demonstrate clear advantages over established methods. The paper is well-structured, well-written, and makes a compelling case for the effectiveness of end-to-end optimization in image compression. The method is practically implementable with the adaptive entropy coding framework, and the visual improvements are striking and consistent across all tested conditions.
