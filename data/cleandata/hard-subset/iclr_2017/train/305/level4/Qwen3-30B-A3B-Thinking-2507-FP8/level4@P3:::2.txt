

# Review for "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

This paper presents a novel end-to-end image compression framework that jointly optimizes the analysis and synthesis transforms, quantizer, and entropy code for rate-distortion performance. The authors propose a nonlinear transform architecture inspired by biological visual processing (Generalized Divisive Normalization), which they optimize using a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based training. The approach demonstrates superior rate-distortion performance compared to standard methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000, with particularly dramatic improvements in visual quality across all bit rates. The paper makes an important contribution by showing that neural network-based compression can achieve both better objective metrics (PSNR) and perceptual quality than traditional methods, while providing a complete compression pipeline with actual bit rate measurements.

## 2. Decision

**Accept.** The paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of image compression with strong empirical results that demonstrate clear advantages over standard methods. The technical approach is well-motivated and rigorously evaluated, with the authors addressing key challenges in end-to-end compression optimization.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's primary contribution lies in its successful application of end-to-end neural network optimization to image compression, moving beyond traditional transform coding methods. The authors effectively address the fundamental challenge of optimizing the quantizer in the training process through their continuous relaxation technique, which is a significant technical contribution. Their demonstration of both better rate-distortion performance (measured by PSNR) and substantially improved visual quality (measured by MS-SSIM) across all bit rates represents a meaningful advancement over standard compression methods.

The paper is well-positioned within the literature, building on previous work in nonlinear transforms (Ballé et al., 2015) and rate-distortion optimization. The authors carefully distinguish their approach from variational autoencoders (section 3.1), which is important for positioning their contribution within the broader machine learning literature. The empirical validation on standard image datasets is thorough, with comprehensive comparisons to JPEG and JPEG 2000 across multiple metrics and image sets.

While the authors acknowledge that their method was optimized for MSE rather than a perceptual metric (as in their previous work), the fact that they still achieve substantially better visual quality is a strong point in favor of their approach. This suggests the nonlinear transforms themselves capture visual characteristics more effectively than traditional methods.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. **Clarify the relationship between the continuous relaxation and the discrete compression problem.** The paper mentions that the continuous approximation is used strictly for optimization and that evaluation is performed on actual bit rates. It would be helpful to include a brief quantitative analysis of the difference between the relaxed loss and the actual rate-distortion performance (e.g., a comparison of the relaxed optimization values versus the actual bit rates achieved).

2. **Provide more detail on the adaptive entropy coding component.** The authors mention using a CABAC-based entropy code, but it's unclear how much of the performance gain comes from the deeper neural network architecture versus the adaptive coding. A comparison of the same neural network architecture with a fixed entropy code (e.g., Huffman) would help isolate the contribution of the adaptive coding component.

3. **Expand the discussion of the MSE vs. perceptual metric choice.** While the authors acknowledge this is a limitation, it would strengthen the paper to include a brief analysis of what specific visual artifacts are being addressed by the nonlinear transforms that MSE optimization captures.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that the relaxed loss function "may be interpreted as the log likelihood of a generative model" (section 1). However, equation 10 appears to have a potential issue, as the partition function should depend on g_s(y; theta) rather than being independent of the synthesis transform. Could the authors clarify this point and confirm whether the equivalence to a VAE holds for non-Euclidean metrics?

2. Given that the authors previously demonstrated visual improvements with perceptual metrics (Ballé et al., 2016), why did they choose to optimize for MSE rather than a perceptual metric in this work? Would optimizing for a perceptual metric yield even better visual quality?

3. The paper claims "dramatic improvement in visual quality for all images at all bit rates," but the evaluation primarily uses PSNR and MS-SSIM. Could the authors provide a brief qualitative analysis of specific image types or features where their method shows particular advantages compared to JPEG 2000?

4. The paper mentions that "the additive noise used in the objective function as a relaxation of quantization might also serve the purpose of making supervised classification networks more robust." Could the authors elaborate on this potential broader implication for machine learning?

The paper makes a significant contribution to image compression research with a well-executed approach that demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods. With minor clarifications, this work will be an important reference in the field of learned image compression.
