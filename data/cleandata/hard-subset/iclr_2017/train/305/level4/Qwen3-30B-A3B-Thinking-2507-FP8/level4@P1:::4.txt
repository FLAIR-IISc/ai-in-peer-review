

This paper introduces an end-to-end optimized image compression framework using nonlinear transforms, addressing the long-standing challenge of jointly optimizing rate-distortion performance. The authors propose a novel method where analysis and synthesis transforms—constructed via cascaded convolutional layers with generalized divisive normalization (GDN)—are jointly trained using a continuous relaxation of quantization. This relaxation replaces the discontinuous quantizer with additive uniform noise, enabling gradient-based optimization while maintaining fidelity to the discrete rate-distortion problem. Crucially, the method achieves superior rate-distortion performance over JPEG and JPEG 2000 across standard benchmarks, with *dramatically improved visual quality* at all bit rates, validated by both subjective assessment and objective MS-SSIM metrics. The work bridges biological visual modeling (via GDN) with practical compression, demonstrating that end-to-end learning of nonlinear transforms outperforms traditional linear transform coding.

**Decision: Accept**  
The paper merits acceptance for two key reasons:  
1) **The continuous relaxation of quantization is elegantly engineered and scientifically rigorous**, solving a core obstacle in training neural compression models. The authors rigorously justify this approach as a valid proxy for the discrete problem, empirically verifying its accuracy in approximating actual bit rates and distortion (Fig. 4).  
2) **The visual quality improvements are substantial and consistent across all test images and bit rates**, far exceeding the gains typically seen in rate-distortion optimization (Fig. 5–7). This represents a meaningful advance over existing methods, as JPEG/JPEG 2000 artifacts (blocking, ringing) are visibly suppressed.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper’s core contribution—end-to-end optimization of nonlinear transforms for compression—is well-motivated and placed within the literature. The authors clearly position their work against prior transform coding (e.g., JPEG’s DCT) and generative models (e.g., VAEs), highlighting critical distinctions: unlike VAEs, their method operates *discretely* at any rate-distortion point (not just lossless), and they report *actual bit rates* (not entropy estimates), enabling fair comparison to standards. The GDN-based transforms, inspired by biological gain control, are shown to efficiently Gaussianize image statistics (extending Ballé et al. 2015), which directly enables better compression. The experimental validation is thorough:  
- Training on ImageNet subsets and testing on Kodak/standard images (no overlap).  
- Comprehensive comparison to JPEG/JPEG 2000 using both PSNR and MS-SSIM.  
- Empirical validation of the relaxation’s accuracy (Fig. 4), addressing a key concern in similar works (e.g., Gregor et al. 2016).  
The results are compelling: at low bit rates, the method preserves contours and edges without artifacts, while JPEG/JPEG 2000 suffer from visible basis-function leakage (Fig. 5). MS-SSIM improvements are universal (Fig. 7), confirming that the MSE-optimized model inherently aligns with perceptual quality—a significant insight.

**Additional Feedback**  
To further strengthen the paper, consider:  
1. Clarifying the *computational cost* of training versus inference (e.g., "Training requires X hours on Y GPUs; encoding/decoding is O(1) per image"). This is critical for assessing practicality.  
2. Discussing the *limitations of the entropy code* (Section 6.2). The CABAC implementation is basic (e.g., no spatial adaptation), which likely underestimates potential gains. A sentence on how more advanced coding (e.g., JPEG 2000’s context-adaptive models) might close the bit-rate gap would add depth.  
3. Adding a brief note on *scalability to video* or *real-time applications*, given the efficiency of inference.  

**Questions for Authors**  
To ensure confidence in the assessment:  
1. The paper states GDN’s efficiency over ReLU/sigmoid is "provisional" (Section 5). Could you share the *exact bit-rate gains* (PSNR/MS-SSIM) when using ReLU instead of GDN, for a fixed model size? This would clarify the magnitude of the GDN advantage.  
2. In Section 3.1, you note that generative models operate with λ→∞ (lossless), while compression optimizes λ. How does your method handle the *boundary case* λ→0 (extremely low bit rate)? Do the visual artifacts (Fig. 6) remain coherent, or do they become unrecognizable?  
3. The entropy code (Section 6.2) uses a simple raster-scan ordering. How would *spatially adaptive coding* (e.g., modeling correlations between adjacent pixels) impact the final bit rate? Could you estimate this gap?  

This work makes a significant contribution to image compression, demonstrating that end-to-end learning of nonlinear transforms—not just generative modeling—can yield practical, perceptually superior compression. The method is technically sound, well-validated, and opens new pathways for neural compression. I recommend acceptance.
