

1. This paper presents an innovative end-to-end optimized image compression framework that employs nonlinear analysis and synthesis transformations with biologically inspired local gain control. The authors develop a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable joint optimization of the entire compression pipeline for rate-distortion performance. Their method demonstrates superior rate-distortion trade-offs compared to standard compression algorithms like JPEG and JPEG 2000, with significant improvements in visual quality across all bit rates as supported by both perceptual metrics (MS-SSIM) and objective measures.

2. I recommend acceptance with the following key reasons: (1) The method achieves state-of-the-art rate-distortion performance with demonstrable visual quality improvements over established compression standards; (2) The introduction of a differentiable rate approximation enables effective end-to-end optimization that would not be possible with traditional approaches.

3. The paper's technical contributions are substantial and well-supported. The continuous relaxation of the quantization step provides a novel solution to a longstanding challenge in lossy compression optimization, allowing the authors to jointly optimize the entire compression pipeline rather than separately optimizing transform, quantizer, and entropy code components. The empirical results across multiple test images consistently show improvements in both PSNR and MS-SSIM, with the visual quality improvements being particularly striking. The relationship between the proposed framework and variational autoencoders is well-explained and highlights important distinctions that prevent misleading comparisons to generative modeling approaches. The implementation details regarding the entropy coding and experimental setup are thorough and provide a fair comparison to standard methods.

4. To further strengthen the paper, I recommend briefly discussing the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to JPEG 2000, particularly in terms of encoding/decoding time. While the paper notes that encoding/decoding is efficient after training, a quantitative comparison would help readers understand the practical trade-offs. Additionally, including a brief discussion of how the method might generalize to video compression would provide valuable context for future work.

5. Could the authors clarify how the choice of GDN/IGDN parameters affects the network's efficiency in terms of the number of parameters required compared to alternative nonlinearities? Specifically, is the observed efficiency advantage over pointwise nonlinearities primarily due to the specific form of the GDN operation, or would similar advantages be achievable with other multivariate nonlinear transformations?
