

**Review for "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"**

**Summary**  
This paper presents a novel image compression framework based on end-to-end optimization of nonlinear transforms, quantization, and synthesis. The authors propose a system where analysis and synthesis transforms are jointly optimized using stochastic gradient descent with a continuous relaxation of the quantization process. The approach leverages generalized divisive normalization (GDN) inspired by biological visual systems to create a more effective representation than traditional linear transforms. The paper demonstrates that their method achieves superior rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 across various bit rates, with particularly significant improvements in visual quality as measured by MS-SSIM. The authors provide a clear theoretical connection between their framework and variational autoencoders while highlighting key differences relevant to compression applications.

**Decision**  
Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to image compression with clear technical innovation and compelling empirical results. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the introduction of a differentiable approximation to the rate function that enables end-to-end training of the entire compression pipeline, and (2) the demonstration of significant improvements in both objective metrics (PSNR) and perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) across diverse test images.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper's most significant contribution is the development of a continuous relaxation for the quantization step that enables gradient-based optimization of the entire compression pipeline. This is a technically sophisticated solution to a longstanding challenge in compression, where the discontinuous nature of quantization has traditionally prevented joint optimization. The authors provide a thorough theoretical justification for their approach and demonstrate its effectiveness through extensive experiments. 

The empirical results are particularly compelling. The authors show that their method consistently outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 across all tested bit rates, with visual improvements that are both quantifiable (via MS-SSIM) and perceptually evident. Figure 5 provides a clear visual demonstration of how their method preserves contours and edges while reducing artifacts common in traditional transform coding. The paper also addresses a critical gap in the literature by implementing a complete compression system with actual entropy coding, rather than relying on entropy estimates.

The connection to variational autoencoders is well-handled, with the authors clearly explaining both the similarities and crucial differences between their framework and generative models. This nuanced discussion strengthens the paper's theoretical foundation and helps position their work within the broader machine learning landscape.

**Additional Feedback**  
To further improve the paper, the authors might consider: (1) providing more detailed analysis of the network architecture choices, particularly why GDN/IGDN outperforms simpler nonlinearities; (2) exploring the potential for using perceptual metrics during training to further enhance visual quality; and (3) including more discussion about the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to standard compression algorithms, especially regarding inference time.

**Clarifying Questions**  
1. The paper states that GDN/IGDN transforms are "more efficient for compression, producing better models with fewer stages of processing." Could the authors provide more specific evidence for this claim, perhaps through ablation studies comparing network depth/width with different nonlinearities?

2. The authors mention "preliminary results" indicating that similar visual quality can be achieved with other activation functions, but with larger model sizes. Could they elaborate on these results and their implications for the generalizability of their approach?

3. The paper focuses on grayscale and RGB image compression. How might this framework be extended to handle other types of data, such as video or 3D point clouds, where similar rate-distortion trade-offs are relevant?

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of image compression with clear technical innovation and compelling empirical results. The proposed method represents a significant advance over traditional approaches, and the authors have provided a thorough theoretical foundation for their framework. The potential impact extends beyond image compression to other domains requiring rate-distortion optimization with differentiable approximations. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a compelling case for its contributions to the field.
