This paper presents a complete and compelling framework for end-to-end optimized image compression using a deep neural network architecture. The authors propose a model comprising a nonlinear analysis transform, a quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform, which are jointly optimized to minimize a rate-distortion objective. A key contribution is the elegant formulation that makes the objective function differentiable by replacing the quantizer with additive noise, thereby enabling training via stochastic gradient descent. The resulting method is shown to outperform standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000, not just in terms of rate-distortion metrics but, more impressively, in terms of perceptual quality across a wide range of bit rates.

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The work is exceptionally well-executed and provides one of the most convincing demonstrations of a learned image compression system to date. The primary reasons for my recommendation are the principled integration of classical rate-distortion theory into a modern deep learning framework and the comprehensive evaluation that establishes a new benchmark by clearly outperforming a strong baseline like JPEG 2000.

Supporting my decision, the paper is very well-written and the technical approach is sound. The use of generalized divisive normalization (GDN) is well-motivated by prior work in neuroscience and density modeling. The discussion clarifying the connection and crucial differences between this compression framework and variational autoencoders is particularly insightful. The experimental results are thorough, validating the continuous relaxation of the loss function and, importantly, reporting actual bit rates from a practical entropy coder rather than relying solely on entropy estimates. The visual examples provided are striking and strongly support the quantitative improvements measured by MS-SSIM.

To further strengthen the paper, I offer the following suggestions. These points are intended to help improve the final version and are not critical for my decision to accept.
*   The comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 is necessary and well-done. However, to fully situate the work within the broader compression literature, it would be highly beneficial to include comparisons with more modern, state-of-the-art classical codecs (e.g., BPG, based on HEVC intra-frame coding). This would provide a clearer picture of how learned methods currently stand against the best engineered systems.
*   The evaluation on the Kodak dataset is appropriate, but adding results on other traditional test images (e.g., "Lena," "Barbara," "Mandrill") would allow for easier comparison with a wider body of existing work in the image compression field.

I have a few questions for the authors that would clarify my understanding and strengthen my assessment:
*   Could you comment on how the performance of your method compares to state-of-the-art classical compression algorithms? A quantitative comparison on a few standard images would be extremely valuable to understand the current gap between learned and traditional approaches.
*   From the submitted version, it is difficult to precisely position this neural network-based approach relative to the very best existing methods. Could you provide results on classic images like "Lena" and "Barbara" to help clarify this?
