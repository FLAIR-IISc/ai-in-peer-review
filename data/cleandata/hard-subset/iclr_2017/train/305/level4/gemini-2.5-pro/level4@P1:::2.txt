This paper presents a complete, end-to-end framework for image compression based on deep nonlinear encoder-decoder architectures. The core contributions include extending a previously proposed rate-distortion optimization approach to deeper models, incorporating a more sophisticated adaptive entropy coding scheme (CABAC), and providing a detailed analysis of the method's relationship to variational autoencoders (VAEs). The proposed model is trained by minimizing a continuous proxy for the rate-distortion loss function. The empirical results are particularly strong, demonstrating a clear improvement over standard codecs like JPEG 2000 in both quantitative metrics (PSNR, MS-SSIM) and, most notably, in visual quality.

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a complete and well-engineered learned compression system that, to my knowledge, is the first to be shown to convincingly outperform JPEG 2000. The visual quality of the compressed images is a significant step forward, largely eliminating the blocking and ringing artifacts characteristic of linear transform codes.
2.  While the core optimization technique builds on previous work, this paper successfully scales the approach to deeper architectures and integrates a practical entropy coder, resulting in a state-of-the-art model. The thoroughness of the evaluation and the connection drawn to VAEs add significant value.

**Supporting Arguments:**
The main strength of this paper lies in its impressive empirical results. The authors show that their method not only outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of rate-distortion curves (especially at lower bit rates) but also produces images that are qualitatively far superior. The artifacts are much more natural and less visually disturbing than those produced by standard methods. This claim is well-supported by both the provided figures and the quantitative MS-SSIM results.

The paper is also very well-written and the methodology is clearly explained. The derivation of the continuous loss function and its connection to the VAE framework in Section 3.1 is insightful. It helps to position this work within the broader context of modern generative modeling, while also highlighting the key differences that are crucial for optimizing a compression codec (i.e., operating across the entire rate-distortion curve rather than at a single point).

However, the novelty of the core methodology is somewhat limited, as the end-to-end rate-distortion optimization via a continuous proxy was introduced in the authors' prior work. The main contributions here are the scaling of the architecture to deeper networks and the integration of an adaptive entropy coder. Furthermore, the decision to optimize for MSE could be viewed as a step back from their previous work, which used a perceptual metric. Despite these points, the successful engineering and the resulting high-quality system represent a substantial and important contribution to the field.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors:**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision. I would appreciate the authors' response to clarify my understanding.

1.  **Ablation of Contributions:** The paper introduces two major changes compared to prior work: a deeper architecture and an adaptive entropy coder. To better understand the sources of the performance improvement, an ablation study would be very valuable. Could you quantify the performance gain attributable to the deeper architecture versus the gain from using CABAC? For instance, showing the performance of the new architecture with a simpler, non-adaptive entropy code would help isolate the impact of the model itself.

2.  **Choice of MSE for Optimization:** Given the authors' background in perceptual modeling and the excellent visual quality of the results (which is also reflected in the MS-SSIM scores), the choice to optimize for MSE is surprising. Could you elaborate on the motivation for this decision? Was it primarily for a more direct comparison with standard codecs, or were there technical challenges in applying a perceptual metric to this deeper model and for color images?

3.  **On the VAE Equivalence:** I have a question regarding the formal equivalence to a VAE, as described in Section 3.1. In Equation 16, it seems that for a general perceptual transform `gp` and metric `d`, the normalization constant `Z(λ)` would depend on `gs(ỹ;θ)`. If this is the case, the log-likelihood term `log px|ỹ` would not decompose into the distortion term `-λd(...)` plus a constant, which would break the formal equivalence. Could you please clarify if my understanding is correct and under what precise conditions the equivalence holds?

4.  **Details on the Entropy Coder:** While the appendix provides a good overview of the CABAC implementation, more detail would be helpful for reproducibility. It would be beneficial to better understand how significantly the online adaptation of the probability models contributes to the final bit rate, especially in comparison to a well-designed non-adaptive arithmetic coder using the learned marginals. Figure 10 suggests the gains are small, which makes the ablation requested in point (1) even more relevant.
