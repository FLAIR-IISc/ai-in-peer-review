### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel, end-to-end framework for lossy image compression based on learned nonlinear transforms. The architecture consists of a nonlinear analysis transform, a uniform quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform, which are jointly optimized to minimize a rate-distortion objective over a large image database. A key contribution is an elegant method for overcoming the non-differentiability of the quantization step in a gradient-based optimization setting. The authors introduce a continuous relaxation by replacing quantization with additive uniform noise, which serves as a proxy for both the rate (via differential entropy) and distortion terms in the loss function. The paper provides an insightful discussion of the relationship between this framework and variational autoencoders (VAEs), while carefully highlighting the critical differences that make their approach suitable for compression. The resulting models demonstrate state-of-the-art performance, outperforming both JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of rate-distortion on standard benchmarks, and more importantly, producing compressed images with dramatically superior perceptual quality at all bit rates.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that presents a complete and well-executed solution to a classic problem, backed by compelling results. My recommendation for acceptance is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Novel and Principled Optimization Framework:** The paper introduces a well-motivated and effective method for end-to-end optimization of a compression model that includes a discrete quantization step. The continuous relaxation is a clever technical contribution that allows for joint training of the encoder, decoder, and entropy model parameters using standard stochastic gradient descent.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance:** The proposed method not only surpasses widely-used standards like JPEG and JPEG 2000 on objective metrics like PSNR for most images, but it also achieves a remarkable improvement in subjective visual quality. The generated images lack the typical blocking or ringing artifacts and instead exhibit much more natural-looking degradations, a claim strongly supported by superior MS-SSIM scores.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The core challenge in learning-based compression has been the integration of the non-differentiable quantization step into an end-to-end optimization pipeline. This paper's main strength lies in its elegant solution. By replacing the rounding operation with additive uniform noise during training, the authors create a fully differentiable proxy loss function. This approach is well-justified, as uniform noise is a classic model for quantization error, and the differential entropy of the noise-corrupted signal serves as a tight upper bound for the discrete entropy of the quantized signal. The paper further strengthens its theoretical footing by drawing a clear parallel to VAEs, interpreting the rate and distortion terms as components of the KL-divergence objective in variational inference. This connection places the work firmly within the context of modern generative modeling while also clarifying the unique constraints of the compression problem (i.e., optimizing for the entire rate-distortion curve via the λ parameter, rather than a single point).

The empirical validation is thorough and convincing. The authors evaluate their method on the standard Kodak dataset, ensuring no overlap with the training data. The rate-distortion curves (both for PSNR and MS-SSIM) clearly show a performance advantage over JPEG and JPEG 2000, especially at lower bit rates. The most impressive results, however, are the qualitative examples provided. While JPEG and JPEG 2000 break down into visually disturbing artifacts that reveal their underlying linear basis functions, the proposed method learns to simplify images in a perceptually pleasing way, preserving smooth contours and sharp edges while gracefully discarding fine textures. This subjective improvement is objectively confirmed by the MS-SSIM results, which show a substantial gain across all images and bit rates. The inclusion of a functional CABAC-based entropy coder demonstrates that this is a complete compression system, and the reported bit rates are actual, achievable file sizes, not just entropy estimates.

### Additional Feedback and Questions

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Role of GDN:** The discussion section mentions that preliminary results with simpler nonlinearities (e.g., ReLUs) achieve qualitatively similar results but require more parameters or stages. This is a very interesting finding. Formalizing this comparison, even with a small experiment in the appendix, would help disentangle the contributions of the end-to-end optimization framework from the specific architectural choice of the GDN/IGDN transforms. It would strengthen the claim that these biologically-inspired nonlinearities are particularly efficient for this task.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The paper notes that encoding and decoding are efficient. To further bolster the practical relevance of the work, it would be beneficial to include a brief analysis of the computational complexity (e.g., in terms of multiply-accumulate operations or actual runtime on a reference CPU/GPU) compared to a software implementation of JPEG 2000.
*   **Training per Rate-Point:** The current framework requires training a separate model for each point on the rate-distortion curve (i.e., for each λ). This is computationally expensive. Have the authors considered or experimented with methods for training a single, unified model that can be conditioned to operate at different bit rates? This would be a significant step towards even greater practical utility.

I would like to ask the authors the following questions to clarify my understanding:

1.  In Section 4, you note that the relaxed differential entropy provides a "somewhat positively biased estimate" of the discrete entropy in the coarser quantization regime. Could you provide some intuition as to why this bias occurs and why it is positive?
2.  The architecture for analysis and synthesis seems to be symmetric. Was this a design choice for simplicity, or did you find that allowing for asymmetric encoder/decoder architectures did not yield significant improvements?
