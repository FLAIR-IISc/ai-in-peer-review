This work introduces a well-executed, end-to-end trainable system for image compression and decompression. The method successfully outperforms established codecs, including JPEG-2000, in terms of its rate-distortion trade-offs. Beyond demonstrating the utility of deep learning for a new application, the paper's central contribution is the introduction of a differentiable proxy for the "rate" function. The authors show this is critical for effectively training the model across a spectrum of rate-distortion objectives. I anticipate that this contribution will have a broader impact beyond compression, proving useful for other tasks that could benefit from differentiable approximations of similar functions.

The authors provided an insightful response to my preliminary query. I would still contend that a network of sufficient capacity, when optimized to minimize distortion under fixed-range quantization, should implicitly learn to produce codes that maximize entropy within that range (i.e., reach the theoretical upper bound). However, the authors' second argument is persuasive: forcing the network to do so imposes a rigid structure on its output. To match the compression efficiency of the proposed system, this would require a significantly more complex network capable of handling the computations currently performed by the separate variable-rate encoder used to store the quantized codes (q).
