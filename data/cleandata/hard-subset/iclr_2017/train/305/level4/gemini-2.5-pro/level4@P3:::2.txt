### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents an end-to-end framework for optimizing a deep neural network for lossy image compression. The proposed model consists of a nonlinear analysis transform (encoder), a uniform quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform (decoder). The transforms are built from a cascade of convolutional layers and a generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearity, which is biologically inspired. The entire system is trained to minimize a rate-distortion objective using stochastic gradient descent. A key technical contribution is a continuous and differentiable proxy for the loss function, which replaces the non-differentiable quantization step with additive uniform noise, enabling gradient-based optimization. The paper also thoughtfully discusses the relationship between this rate-distortion framework and variational autoencoders (VAEs). The authors demonstrate that their method achieves superior rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, particularly in terms of visual quality as measured by MS-SSIM.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The paper demonstrates a significant leap forward for learned image compression. The proposed method consistently outperforms standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of perceptual quality across a wide range of bit rates. The visual results are compelling, producing images with far more natural-looking artifacts than the blocking and ringing common to transform coding methods. This is a substantial empirical achievement.
2.  **Rigorous and Complete Framework:** The authors present a well-motivated and technically sound framework for end-to-end optimization. The continuous relaxation of the rate-distortion objective is an elegant solution to the non-differentiable nature of quantization. Crucially, the paper goes beyond reporting entropy estimates and implements a complete compression pipeline with a CABAC-based entropy coder, reporting actual bit rates. This level of rigor makes the work a convincing and practical contribution to the field.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this work lies in its impressive results. While optimization was performed using Mean Squared Error (MSE), the resulting visual quality is remarkably high. As shown in Figures 5 and 6, the method avoids the typical artifacts of linear transform codes. Instead of degrading into visible basis functions (e.g., blocks or wavelets), images are simplified in a more perceptually plausible way, preserving smooth contours and sharp edges. This subjective improvement is objectively validated by the MS-SSIM results (Figure 7), which show a consistent and significant advantage over JPEG and JPEG 2000 for all tested images and bit rates.

The methodology is also a key strength. The paper builds upon previous work but extends it to deeper, more flexible architectures and incorporates a more sophisticated, practical entropy coding scheme. The discussion in Section 3.1, which connects the rate-distortion loss to the objective of a variational autoencoder, is insightful. It clarifies the similarities but also highlights the fundamental differences, such as the goal of optimizing over the entire rate-distortion curve (via λ) rather than for a single, maximal-likelihood model. This helps position the work within the broader machine learning literature. The empirical validation in Figure 4, showing that the continuous proxy for rate and distortion is a reasonable approximation of the discrete reality, adds confidence in the training methodology.

While the core idea of rate-distortion optimization via a continuous proxy has been introduced by the authors previously, this paper represents a mature and significant extension. Demonstrating that this approach scales to deeper networks and can be integrated into a full coding pipeline to outperform widely-used standards like JPEG 2000 is a substantial and novel contribution in its own right.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not central to my decision to accept.

*   **Choice of Distortion Metric:** The decision to optimize for MSE for the sake of comparability is reasonable. However, given the authors' background and previous work with perceptual metrics, a discussion on the potential benefits and challenges of optimizing this deeper architecture directly for a metric like MS-SSIM would be a valuable addition. It seems likely that this would push the already impressive visual results even further.
*   **Ablation Study:** The model introduces two main advancements over prior work: a deeper network architecture and an adaptive entropy coder. To better isolate the sources of improvement, an ablation study would be very informative. Specifically, providing rate-distortion curves for the deep architecture with a non-adaptive entropy coder would clarify the performance gain attributable to the adaptive coding scheme.

### Questions for the Authors

Your response to these questions would help clarify some aspects of the work and further strengthen the paper.

1.  Could you elaborate on the relative performance contribution of the deeper network architecture versus the adaptive entropy coder? For example, how much of the gain over JPEG 2000 is due to the adaptivity of the CABAC implementation versus the representation learned by the deeper model?
2.  In Section 3.1, you establish an equivalence between the relaxed R-D objective and the VAE objective under certain conditions, including a translation-invariant metric `d`. Could you clarify if this equivalence holds for non-Euclidean perceptual metrics that may not be translation-invariant? Specifically, if the normalization term `Z(λ)` in Equation 16 were to depend on the output of the synthesis transform `gs(ỹ;θ)`, would this invalidate the direct equivalence?
3.  What were the primary reasons for optimizing for MSE rather than the perceptual metric used in your 2016 work, especially for the grayscale experiments? Was this choice purely for comparability, or were there technical challenges, such as optimization stability, with using a perceptual loss in this deeper framework?
