This paper presents a novel end-to-end framework for lossy image compression using deep neural networks. It is, in my opinion, the most convincing and well-executed paper on this topic to date. The work is characterized by its clear and insightful writing, a theoretically sound methodology, and a robust experimental evaluation. I believe this paper will have a significant impact on the field.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clarity and Quality of Writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The authors clearly motivate the problem and present their proposed architecture and training methodology in a manner that is both accessible and rigorous.

2.  **Principled Approach:** A major strength lies in the elegant integration of rate-distortion theory directly into the learning objective. By formulating the training loss as a weighted sum of rate (the entropy of the latent representation) and distortion (the reconstruction error), the model learns to navigate the fundamental trade-off in compression. This approach is theoretically grounded and fits seamlessly within the end-to-end deep learning framework.

3.  **Strong and Relevant Baselines:** The experimental evaluation is commendable for its use of JPEG2000 as a primary baseline for comparison. This represents a significant improvement over many prior works in learned compression, which often restricted their comparisons to the much older and weaker JPEG standard. Comparing against a more competitive baseline like JPEG2000 provides a more realistic and meaningful assessment of the proposed method's capabilities.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

While the paper is strong, its experimental validation could be further strengthened to provide a more complete and conclusive picture of its performance relative to the broader compression literature.

1.  **Expanded Benchmark Evaluation:** The evaluation would be more comprehensive if it included results on a wider set of standard, widely-recognized benchmark images. The inclusion of classic test images such as Lena, Barbara, and Baboon is standard practice in the image compression community. Reporting performance on these images would facilitate more direct and thorough comparisons with a vast body of existing work.

2.  **Comparison with State-of-the-Art Classical Methods:** While the comparison to JPEG2000 is a crucial and welcome step, the paper would be substantially more impactful if it positioned its results against more recent and powerful state-of-the-art classical compression codecs. To truly assess the practical viability of this learned approach, it is essential to compare it with top-performing traditional methods (e.g., BPG, which is based on the intra-frame coding of HEVC, and other methods discussed during the review period).

3.  **Lack of Clear Positioning:** As a consequence of the points above, a primary limitation of the current submission is that it is difficult for the reader to definitively conclude how this neural network-based approach compares to the absolute state of the art in image compression. The results are highly promising, but without these additional comparisons, one is left uncertain about where this category of methods truly stands. Clearly demonstrating the performance gap (or lack thereof) between this learned model and the best-engineered classical codecs is critical for understanding its contributions and future potential.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the burgeoning field of learned image compression. The methodology is sound, the presentation is clear, and the results are very promising. My main concerns relate to the scope of the experimental evaluation. I strongly encourage the authors to include the suggested comparisons in the final version of the paper. By doing so, they will provide a much clearer context for their work and solidify its position as a landmark contribution. I confidently recommend acceptance.
