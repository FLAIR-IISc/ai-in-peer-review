This paper presents an end-to-end trainable system for image compression and decompression that outperforms established codecs, such as JPEG-2000, in terms of its rate-distortion trade-off. Beyond demonstrating the efficacy of deep learning for a new application, a key contribution is the introduction of a differentiable proxy for the rate function. The authors show that this enables effective end-to-end training for different rate-distortion trade-offs. This contribution will likely have an impact beyond image compression, benefiting other tasks that require differentiable approximations of similar non-differentiable functions.

The authors provided a thoughtful response to my earlier question. I still contend that a sufficiently complex network, when trained to minimize distortion under a fixed-range quantizer, should implicitly learn to produce codes that maximize entropy and thus approach the rate's upper bound. Nevertheless, the authors' counterargument is convincing: forcing the network to learn this behavior would impose a specific structure on the compressor's output. To match the compression efficiency of the proposed system, such an approach would require a more complex network capable of performing the computations currently handled by the separate, variable-rate entropy coder for the quantized representations.
