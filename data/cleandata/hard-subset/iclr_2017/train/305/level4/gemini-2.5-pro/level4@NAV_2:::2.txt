This paper presents an end-to-end learned image compression framework that jointly optimizes a nonlinear analysis transform, a quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform for rate-distortion performance. The transforms are implemented as deep convolutional networks using a biologically-inspired form of nonlinearity (Generalized Divisive Normalization). To overcome the non-differentiability of quantization, the authors cleverly employ a continuous and differentiable proxy loss function based on additive uniform noise, which enables training via stochastic gradient descent. The paper also provides an insightful discussion on the relationship between this optimization problem and Variational Autoencoders (VAEs). The authors demonstrate that their method achieves state-of-the-art results, outperforming standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of both PSNR and, more significantly, visual quality as measured by MS-SSIM.

I am in favor of **Accepting** this paper. My decision is based on two key points:
1.  The empirical results are a significant step forward for learned image compression. The method produces compressed images that are visually far superior to those from JPEG 2000 at comparable bit rates.
2.  While the core optimization technique builds on the authors' prior work, this paper successfully scales the approach to deeper architectures and incorporates a practical entropy coding scheme, resulting in a complete and high-performing compression system.

The primary strength of this work lies in its compelling empirical results. The authors provide extensive quantitative and qualitative evidence that their model surpasses widely used standards. The visual examples clearly demonstrate a marked reduction in the typical artifacts associated with linear transform codes, such as blocking and ringing. Instead, as bit rates are lowered, the model produces simplifications that are more natural in appearance, preserving smooth contours and sharp edges. This substantial improvement in perceptual quality, which is validated by strong MS-SSIM scores, is particularly impressive given that the model was optimized for a simple Mean Squared Error (MSE) metric. The inclusion of an actual entropy coder (CABAC) and the reporting of true bit rates make the comparisons to JPEG and JPEG 2000 rigorous and convincing.

However, the novelty of the core methodology is somewhat limited, as the central idea of using a continuous relaxation for rate-distortion optimization was presented in the authors' previous publications. This work is best viewed as a successful and important scaling-up of that framework. Additionally, the decision to optimize for MSE, rather than a perceptual metric as in their previous work, is well-justified for comparability but feels like a missed opportunity to push perceptual performance even further. The excellent visual results achieved despite this choice are remarkable and suggest that the properties of the architecture may be more critical than the specific loss function, a point that could be further explored.

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision:
*   The paper introduces two main improvements over prior work: deeper network architectures and a more advanced context-adaptive entropy code. The contribution would be clearer if these two factors were disentangled. An ablation study comparing the performance of the new architecture with a simple, non-adaptive entropy code would help quantify the gains attributable to the network versus the coding scheme. Figure 10 in the appendix provides a hint, but a more direct analysis in the main text would be valuable.
*   The discussion on the connection to VAEs is insightful. However, the claimed equivalence under non-Euclidean metrics (Equation 16) hinges on the partition function Z being independent of the synthesis transform's output. It would be beneficial to clarify the exact conditions under which this assumption holds, as it seems that for a general perceptual transform `gp` and metric `d`, `Z` might depend on `gs(ỹ;θ)`.
*   The paper could benefit from a deeper discussion on why optimizing for MSE yields such perceptually superior results. Is this an inherent property of the GDN/IGDN nonlinearities? Exploring this phenomenon could offer valuable insights for the broader field of image generation and representation learning.

I have a few questions for the authors to clarify my understanding:
*   In the context of the VAE formulation with a non-Euclidean metric (Equation 16), could you elaborate on why the normalizing constant `Z(λ)` is not a function of `gs(ỹ;θ)`? If it does depend on `gs`, how does this affect the equivalence between the rate-distortion objective and the variational objective?
*   What is the relative contribution of the deeper architecture versus the context-adaptive entropy coder to the overall performance gains observed over your previous work and other baselines?
*   You justify the use of MSE for comparability and for color images where reliable perceptual metrics are lacking. Given the excellent visual quality achieved, do you have any intuition as to whether the GDN-based architecture is the primary driver of this perceptual performance, potentially making the choice of distortion metric less critical than for linear transform-based methods?
