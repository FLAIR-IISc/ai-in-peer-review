This paper introduces a novel, end-to-end optimized framework for lossy image compression. The proposed model consists of a nonlinear analysis transform, a uniform quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform, all of which are jointly optimized for a rate-distortion objective. The authors make several key contributions: they build flexible transforms using cascades of convolutions and a biologically-inspired Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) nonlinearity; they cleverly overcome the non-differentiability of quantization by introducing a continuous, differentiable proxy based on additive uniform noise; and they provide a compelling connection between their rate-distortion loss and the objective function of a variational autoencoder. The framework is trained over a large image database and evaluated against standard codecs.

I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance. The primary reasons are: 1) The work presents a principled and elegant deep learning-based solution to the classical problem of image compression, successfully framing it as an end-to-end optimization problem. 2) The empirical results are not only competitive with but often superior to established standards like JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of rate-distortion performance, and more importantly, they demonstrate a dramatic improvement in perceptual quality across all tested bit rates.

Supporting these reasons, the paper's approach is methodologically sound and well-executed. The main technical hurdle in such a framework is optimizing through the discrete quantization step. The authors' solution—replacing quantization with additive uniform noise during training—is an effective and well-justified relaxation that enables the use of stochastic gradient descent. The subsequent evaluation is rigorous; instead of relying solely on entropy estimates, the authors implement a full entropy coding scheme (based on CABAC) to report actual compressed bit rates, allowing for a fair and direct comparison with existing methods. The results are convincing, showing clear quantitative gains on the MS-SSIM metric, which aligns with the striking qualitative improvements seen in the example images. The compressed images avoid the blocking and ringing artifacts characteristic of linear transform codes, instead producing visually pleasing simplifications that preserve salient structures.

For the purpose of improving the paper, I offer the following suggestions and questions. These points are meant to be constructive and did not negatively influence my decision.
*   The paper mentions that the network architecture was chosen in a "somewhat ad-hoc" manner. While the current design is clearly effective, a brief discussion on the sensitivity to architectural choices (e.g., number of stages, number of filters) could provide valuable insights for future work in this area.
*   The discussion on GDN versus simpler nonlinearities like ReLU is interesting. Quantifying the efficiency difference, even if based on preliminary results (e.g., approximate parameter count or computational cost to achieve a similar PSNR), would significantly strengthen the argument for using this more complex, biologically-inspired nonlinearity.
*   The framework currently trains a separate model for each rate-distortion trade-off parameter λ. From a practical standpoint, this could be cumbersome. Could the authors comment on the feasibility of training a single, rate-adaptive model that can cover the entire R-D curve? Is it possible, for instance, to treat λ as a conditional input to the network?
*   The entropy model assumes spatial independence for the quantized coefficients within each channel. Given that natural images contain significant spatial structure, it seems likely that dependencies remain in the latent space. Have you considered incorporating a more sophisticated spatial context model into the entropy coder, and if so, what level of improvement might be expected?
