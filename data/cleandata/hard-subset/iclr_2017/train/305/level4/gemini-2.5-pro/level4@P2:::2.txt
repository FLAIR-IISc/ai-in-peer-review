### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a complete, end-to-end framework for optimizing a deep neural network for lossy image compression. The proposed model consists of a nonlinear analysis transform (encoder), a uniform quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform (decoder). The transforms are novelly constructed from a cascade of convolutional layers and a biologically-inspired nonlinearity known as Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). To overcome the non-differentiability of the quantizer, the authors introduce a continuous and differentiable proxy to the rate-distortion loss function, which enables training with stochastic gradient descent. The paper also provides a valuable discussion on the relationship between this optimization objective and the one used for training Variational Autoencoders (VAEs). The entire system, including a context-adaptive arithmetic entropy coder, is trained to minimize a weighted sum of rate and distortion. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments that their method significantly outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000, not only in terms of rate-distortion metrics like PSNR and MS-SSIM but also, and more impressively, in terms of subjective visual quality.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The paper presents a well-engineered and principled learned compression system that achieves results demonstrably superior to established standards like JPEG 2000. The visual quality of the compressed images is particularly compelling, showing a marked reduction in common artifacts like blocking and ringing, which is a significant achievement.
2.  **Methodological Soundness and Clarity:** The approach is methodologically sound, providing an elegant solution to the difficult end-to-end optimization problem by relaxing the discrete quantization step. The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly explaining the model architecture, the optimization procedure, and its connection to related work in generative modeling.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its impressive empirical results. The authors show that their deep, non-linear model, even when optimized for a simple metric like Mean Squared Error (MSE), produces images that are visually far more pleasing than those from JPEG 2000 at similar or even higher bit rates. This claim is strongly supported by the provided examples and the superior performance measured by the MS-SSIM perceptual metric. This work sets a new and important benchmark for the emerging field of learned image compression.

While the core rate-distortion optimization framework builds upon the authors' prior work, this paper represents a substantial extension. The move to a deeper, multi-stage architecture and the integration of a practical, adaptive entropy coder elevate the concept from a proof-of-concept to a complete and highly competitive compression pipeline. The thoroughness of the evaluation, including reporting actual bit rates from a functional entropy coder, adds significant weight to the claims and allows for a fair comparison with existing standards.

The theoretical connection drawn to VAEs is insightful. It situates the work within the broader context of deep generative models while also clearly articulating the key differences—namely, that compression must operate across the entire rate-distortion curve, whereas generative models typically target a single high-rate (or lossless) regime. This discussion helps clarify the unique constraints and objectives of the compression task.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision. I would appreciate the authors' response to clarify my understanding.

1.  **Choice of Distortion Metric:** Could the authors elaborate on the decision to optimize for MSE rather than a perceptual metric, as was done in previous work? The paper mentions this was for easier comparison and to handle color images. However, given that the most significant gains are perceptual (as measured by MS-SSIM and visual inspection), it seems that directly optimizing for a perceptual metric could lead to even better results. Was this explored?

2.  **Ablation of Contributions:** The final model improves upon previous work by incorporating both a deeper architecture and a more sophisticated adaptive entropy coder. To better isolate the sources of improvement, an ablation study would be very helpful. What portion of the performance gain over simpler models comes from the deeper network, and what portion comes from the context-adaptive entropy coding? A comparison to the deep model using a non-adaptive entropy code would be particularly illuminating.

3.  **Clarification on VAE Equivalence:** Regarding the connection to VAEs, I have a question about Equation (16). It appears the partition function, Z(λ), would depend on the output of the synthesis transform, `gs(ỹ; θ)`, when using a non-Euclidean metric. If so, this would mean the distortion term does not correspond to a properly normalized probability density, and the equivalence to the VAE objective would not hold. Could the authors please clarify this point?
