### Summary

This paper presents a novel, end-to-end optimized framework for lossy image compression based on nonlinear transforms. The model consists of an analysis transform (encoder), a uniform quantizer, and a synthesis transform (decoder). The transforms are implemented as a three-stage convolutional network that uses a generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearity, inspired by models of biological vision. A key contribution is the method for optimizing the entire system through the non-differentiable quantization step. The authors introduce a continuous and differentiable proxy for the rate-distortion loss function by replacing quantization with additive uniform noise, which allows for effective training via stochastic gradient descent. The paper thoughtfully relates this formulation to variational autoencoders (VAEs) while clearly distinguishing the goals of compression (optimizing the rate-distortion trade-off) from those of generative modeling. The proposed method is shown to outperform JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of rate-distortion performance, and more significantly, it produces compressed images with substantially higher perceptual quality, as validated by visual examples and the MS-SSIM metric.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Effective Optimization Framework:** The paper's primary contribution is a well-motivated method for end-to-end optimization of a compression system. By introducing a continuous proxy for the rate and distortion terms, the authors successfully navigate the challenge of non-differentiability posed by quantization, enabling the joint optimization of the encoder, decoder, and an entropy model. This technique is elegant and could have a significant impact on other applications involving discrete latent representations.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The proposed method achieves compelling results, outperforming established standards like JPEG 2000 on both objective metrics (PSNR) and, more impressively, on perceptual quality (MS-SSIM). The visual examples clearly demonstrate that the model avoids the typical blocking and ringing artifacts of linear transform codes, instead producing reconstructions that are perceptually pleasing even at very low bit rates.

### Supporting Arguments

The main challenge in training a learned compression model is that the objective function, a weighted sum of rate (entropy of quantized codes) and distortion (reconstruction error), is not differentiable with respect to the network parameters due to the quantization step. The authors' solution is to replace the hard quantization function with additive i.i.d. uniform noise during training. This relaxation serves two purposes: it makes the distortion term differentiable and allows the discrete entropy of the quantized codes to be approximated by the differential entropy of the continuous noisy representation. This continuous proxy loss enables the use of standard gradient-based optimization to tune the entire system for a specific rate-distortion trade-off, controlled by the parameter λ. This is a significant technical advance for the field of learned compression.

The paper provides strong empirical support for its claims. The evaluation on the Kodak dataset and other standard test images demonstrates superior performance over JPEG and JPEG 2000, especially at lower bit rates. The most striking result is the qualitative improvement in the compressed images. While optimized for MSE, the model learns to preserve smooth contours and sharp edges while discarding fine textures, resulting in a natural appearance. In contrast, JPEG and JPEG 2000 produce distracting artifacts that expose their underlying linear basis functions. This visual superiority is quantitatively confirmed by large gains in the MS-SSIM metric, which is known to correlate better with human perception of image quality. The authors also demonstrate the feasibility of their system as a complete codec by implementing a context-adaptive binary arithmetic coder (CABAC) and reporting actual bit rates, rather than just entropy estimates.

The connection drawn to VAEs is insightful, as it places the work within the broader context of modern generative models. At the same time, the authors clearly articulate the fundamental differences: compression models must operate efficiently across the entire rate-distortion curve (parameterized by λ), whereas generative models typically aim to maximize the data likelihood (equivalent to λ → ∞). This discussion clarifies the unique constraints and objectives of the compression task.

### Additional Feedback and Questions

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

1.  **On Explicit Rate Modeling:** The choice to explicitly model the marginal distributions of the quantized coefficients non-parametrically and use their entropy as the rate term is effective. An alternative approach might be to encourage the network to produce latent codes that follow a simple, fixed prior (e.g., uniform within a fixed range) for which an optimal entropy code is known. The current approach seems more flexible, as it allows the model to learn a distribution that can be efficiently compressed by a variable-rate coder. However, a brief discussion or ablation study comparing against a simpler objective that encourages entropy maximization of a bounded latent space could further strengthen the justification for the chosen method.

2.  **Efficiency of GDN:** The paper suggests that GDN/IGDN nonlinearities are more parameter-efficient than alternatives like rectified linear units (ReLU). While the authors mention preliminary results supporting this, including a quantitative comparison would make this claim more concrete. For example, showing the rate-distortion performance of a ReLU-based network with a similar or even greater number of parameters would provide strong evidence for the architectural benefits of GDN.

3.  **Reproducibility:** The appendix provides excellent detail on the network architecture and optimization techniques. This commitment to transparency is commendable and greatly aids in the reproducibility of the work.

**Questions for the authors:**
*   Could you elaborate on the decision to model the marginals `p(ỹ)` non-parametrically as a proxy for the rate, as opposed to training the encoder to output codes that match a fixed, high-entropy prior distribution? What are the key advantages of your approach that justify the added complexity of learning these marginals?
*   Regarding the point made in my pre-review question: while I am now convinced that explicitly modeling the rate and using a variable-rate entropy coder is a well-justified approach, I am curious about the performance trade-offs. Have you experimented with a simpler scheme where the network is trained to maximize the entropy of its output within a fixed range, which is then encoded with a fixed coder?
