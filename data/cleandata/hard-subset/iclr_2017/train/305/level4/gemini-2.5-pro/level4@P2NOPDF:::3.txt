This paper presents a novel image compression system trained end-to-end, demonstrating a rate-distortion performance superior to established codecs like JPEG 2000. Beyond successfully applying a deep learning architecture to a new domain, the paper's core contribution is the introduction of a continuous and differentiable proxy for the rate term in the objective function. This innovation enables effective end-to-end optimization of the network for various rate-distortion trade-offs. I believe this differentiable rate approximation is a significant contribution that may find applications beyond image compression, particularly in other tasks requiring the optimization of discrete, entropy-related objectives.

Following a thoughtful response from the authors to my initial questions, I have further considered the design choices. While one could still argue that a sufficiently powerful network should, in principle, learn to produce latent codes with maximal entropy to minimize distortion under a fixed quantization range, I find the authors' counterargument convincing. Forcing the network to learn a latent distribution that is inherently entropy-maximized (e.g., uniform) would impose a rigid structure on the representation. To achieve the same compression efficiency as the proposed system, such a network would need to become substantially more complex, as it would have to implicitly perform the sophisticated computations that are currently handled by a separate, dedicated variable-rate entropy coder. The authors' method of decoupling the representation learning from the final entropy coding is a more pragmatic and effective approach.
