This paper introduces a novel image compression framework based on a non-linear transform autoencoder, which is trained end-to-end to optimize for rate-distortion performance. The authors make several key contributions. First, they design a complete system comprising an analysis transform, a uniform quantizer, and a synthesis transform that collectively outperform established codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000. Second, and perhaps more significantly, they propose an elegant method to enable gradient-based optimization in the presence of the non-differentiable quantization step. This is achieved by introducing a continuous proxy for the rate-distortion loss function, which replaces quantization with additive uniform noise during training. The resulting system not only achieves better objective metrics (PSNR) at lower bit rates but also shows a dramatic improvement in perceptual quality, as validated by subjective examples and objective MS-SSIM scores.

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are: 1) The proposed end-to-end framework demonstrates state-of-the-art empirical results, achieving a superior trade-off between bit rate and visual quality compared to widely used compression standards. 2) The paper's core methodological innovation—a differentiable approximation of the rate-distortion objective—is a significant contribution that enables the optimization of such models and is likely to be influential for other tasks involving discrete latent variables or quantization.

The claims in the paper are well-supported by rigorous experimentation. The performance of the proposed method is thoroughly evaluated against JPEG and JPEG 2000 on the standard Kodak image dataset. The rate-distortion curves provided clearly illustrate the quantitative advantages, particularly at low bit rates. The qualitative results are even more compelling; the authors show that their method avoids the typical blocking and ringing artifacts of linear transform codes, producing reconstructions that are perceptually much more pleasant. This visual improvement is objectively corroborated by a substantial gain in MS-SSIM across all tested bit rates. Furthermore, the technical approach is sound. The authors validate their continuous relaxation of the loss function, demonstrating that it serves as a reliable and low-variance proxy for the true discrete rate and distortion. The paper also thoughtfully situates the work relative to variational autoencoders, clarifying both the similarities and the crucial differences in objectives between generative modeling and rate-distortion optimization.

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to further improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.
*   The discussion on the trade-off between using an external entropy coder versus having the network learn to produce entropy-maximized codes within a fixed range is insightful. While a sufficiently complex network might learn to implicitly perform entropy coding, the current approach of separating the non-linear transform from a standard variable-rate encoder is a pragmatic and effective design choice. Explicitly framing this as a design decision in the paper, perhaps by discussing the likely increase in network complexity required to match the efficiency of a dedicated coder like CABAC, would further strengthen the motivation for the proposed architecture.
*   The preliminary findings on the efficiency of GDN/IGDN nonlinearities compared to more standard activation functions like ReLUs are very interesting. Expanding on this by providing a more quantitative comparison (e.g., performance vs. model parameters or computational cost) would be a valuable addition, as it would help clarify the specific benefits of using these biologically-inspired transforms.

I would be interested to hear the authors' thoughts on the following question to clarify my understanding:
*   Your framework decouples the learned transform from the entropy coding of the quantized representation `q`. Could you elaborate on the potential challenges or trade-offs of an alternative approach where the network itself is tasked with producing a representation that is directly compressible, for instance, by encouraging it to produce codes that already have high entropy within a fixed, bounded range, thereby obviating the need for a sophisticated external coder like CABAC?
