### **Summary**

This paper presents a complete and convincing end-to-end framework for learned image compression. The method is based on a nonlinear analysis and synthesis transform, implemented as a deep neural network, combined with a uniform scalar quantizer. The central contribution is a novel training methodology that allows for direct optimization of the entire model for rate-distortion performance. By introducing a continuous and differentiable proxy for the quantization operation, the authors are able to minimize a loss function corresponding to the classic `Rate + λ * Distortion` objective using stochastic gradient descent. The network architecture is well-motivated, employing cascades of convolutions and Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) nonlinearities inspired by models of the human visual system. The authors demonstrate that their method outperforms standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of rate-distortion performance (PSNR) and, more significantly, achieves a dramatic improvement in perceptual quality, as measured by MS-SSIM and confirmed through visual inspection.

### **Decision: Accept**

This is an excellent paper that I expect will have a significant impact on the fields of image compression and deep learning. My decision to accept is based on two key strengths:

1.  **A Principled and Well-Executed Framework:** The paper elegantly integrates classical rate-distortion theory into a modern deep learning context. The proposed method for end-to-end optimization is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and effectively addresses the challenge of training in the presence of a non-differentiable quantizer.

2.  **Strong and Compelling Empirical Results:** The claims are thoroughly supported by experiments against strong, relevant baselines (JPEG 2000). The method not only achieves superior quantitative results but also produces compressed images of exceptionally high visual quality, avoiding the typical artifacts of linear transform codes.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's primary strength is its solid theoretical foundation. The formulation of the problem directly from rate-distortion principles is clear and rigorous. The continuous relaxation of the loss function, which replaces quantization with additive uniform noise during training, is a clever and effective solution that enables gradient-based optimization. The authors further enrich the paper by drawing an insightful connection to variational autoencoders, carefully explaining both the similarities and the crucial differences, which helps to position the work within the broader context of generative modeling.

The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. Unlike some previous work in this area, the authors compare against JPEG 2000, a much stronger baseline than JPEG. A particularly commendable aspect of the evaluation is the implementation of a full entropy code to report actual compressed bitrates, rather than relying on entropy estimates, which adds a layer of scientific rigor and demonstrates the practical viability of the system. The qualitative results shown in the paper are striking; the proposed method produces images that degrade gracefully at low bitrates, preserving natural contours and avoiding the blocking and ringing artifacts that plague traditional methods. This subjective improvement is robustly supported by significant gains in the MS-SSIM metric across all tested images and bitrates.

### **Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to further strengthen the paper and are not central to my decision to accept.

*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art Classical Codecs:** While the comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 is essential, these standards are now decades old. The field of image compression has advanced considerably since their introduction. To provide a clearer understanding of where this learned approach stands relative to the absolute state-of-the-art, it would be highly beneficial to include comparisons to more modern, high-performance classical codecs (e.g., BPG, which is based on HEVC). This would help to accurately position the contribution within the broader compression landscape.

*   **Inclusion of Classic Benchmark Images:** The evaluation is conducted on the Kodak dataset, which is a standard choice. However, to facilitate more direct comparison with the vast body of existing compression literature, I would encourage the authors to also report results on other widely-used test images, such as "Lena," "Barbara," and "Mandrill."

### **Questions for the Authors**

Your answers to these questions would help clarify my understanding and strengthen the final version of the paper.

1.  Could you comment on how your method compares to more recent state-of-the-art classical compression algorithms beyond JPEG 2000? From the current submission, it is difficult to assess how this method positions against the best available non-learning-based codecs.

2.  The paper notes that preliminary experiments suggest GDN/IGDN transforms are more parameter-efficient than networks using simpler nonlinearities like ReLUs. Could you elaborate on this? Specifically, how much larger does a ReLU-based model need to be to achieve comparable rate-distortion performance, and are there qualitative differences in the visual artifacts produced by the different models?
