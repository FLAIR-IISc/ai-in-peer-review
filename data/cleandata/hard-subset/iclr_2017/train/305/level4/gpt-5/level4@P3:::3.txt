Summary and contributions
The paper proposes an end-to-end image compression system that jointly optimizes an analysis transform, a scalar quantizer, an entropy model, and a synthesis transform for rate–distortion performance. The transforms are convolutional networks with generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN) nonlinearities. The key technical contribution is a principled continuous relaxation of the discrete rate–distortion objective: replacing quantization with additive uniform noise enables backpropagation through both rate and distortion terms. The authors model latent marginals non-parametrically and implement an actual entropy coder (CABAC-style), reporting true bit rates rather than entropy estimates. They also connect the relaxed objective to variational autoencoders, clarifying similarities and differences. Empirically, on Kodak and additional images (not used for training), the method offers consistent perceptual gains (MS-SSIM) and generally superior or competitive PSNR relative to JPEG and JPEG 2000, with markedly fewer objectionable artifacts at low rates. The paper also validates that the relaxed objective is a good proxy for the discrete objective across rates.

Decision: Accept
Reasons:
1) Strong methodological contribution: a differentiable proxy for the rate term that enables end-to-end optimization of a complete codec, not just a generative model. 2) Convincing evidence: better rate–distortion behavior (especially in perceptual quality) than standard codecs at comparable bit rates, with evaluation using real bitstreams.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Designing image compressors that operate near the rate–distortion frontier is central in signal processing. Existing codecs optimize components in isolation; this work addresses the full end-to-end objective.
- Motivation and placement: The formulation is well grounded in transform coding and quantization theory, and thoughtfully positioned relative to VAEs. The paper clearly explains why generative modeling objectives are not, by themselves, sufficient for discrete lossy compression and why evaluation must use actual bit rates.
- Technical soundness: The additive-uniform-noise relaxation is standard for approximating quantization; the authors verify its bias/variance empirically for both rate and distortion and show it is sufficiently accurate to guide optimization across a wide range of λ. The use of non-parametric marginal models reduces model mismatch. Architectural choices (GDN/IGDN) are justified by prior work on Gaussianizing image statistics and by preliminary ablations.
- Empirical rigor: The system is trained on a large image set and tested on held-out standard datasets. Comparisons include two strong baselines. Results are reported for both PSNR and MS-SSIM, and the paper provides extensive visual evidence consistent with perceptual metrics. Importantly, the authors deliver a full codec with entropy coding and bitstream overheads included.
- Practical relevance: Encoding/decoding is efficient at test time; the method produces smooth, natural degradations rather than blocking/ringing typical of linear transforms, which is valuable in real-world use.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not essential to my decision)
- Comparisons: If possible, include a comparison to a modern high-performing baseline available at the time (e.g., BPG/HEVC intra) to contextualize gains beyond JPEG 2000.
- Single model for multiple rates: Training a separate model per λ produces a family of codecs. Discuss or attempt a single scalable model (e.g., with learned rate control or conditional normalization), and clarify the trade-off versus per-λ optimality.
- Entropy modeling: The current raster-scan CABAC with marginal independence is simple. Reporting results with more advanced context models (e.g., spatial/contextual or hierarchical priors) would likely improve rates and help isolate the contribution of the transform versus the coder.
- Ablations: Provide more systematic ablations on GDN versus standard pointwise nonlinearities (ReLU, leaky ReLU, tanh) for fixed model size/depth to quantify the efficiency gains attributed to GDN/IGDN.
- Complexity and deployment: Add encode/decode throughput and memory measurements on standard hardware. Report parameter counts per λ and discuss feasibility in embedded settings.
- Evaluation protocol: While the paper carefully discusses aggregation issues for rate–distortion curves, a standardized summary (e.g., Bjøntegaard delta rates over matched rate intervals) would help readers synthesize the results across images.
- Color handling: Clarify color space processing and any chroma subsampling choices in your method to ensure fairness with JPEG/JPEG2000.
- Reproducibility: If feasible, release code/models and the entropy coder to facilitate adoption and follow-on work.

Questions for the authors
1) Relaxation gap: Can you quantify, beyond the plots, how the differential-entropy estimate deviates from realized bit rates across λ (e.g., average absolute/relative error), and whether a simple correction improves training?
2) Latent independence: Did you measure residual dependencies among y after training? Would adding lightweight context (autoregressive or spatial hyperpriors) materially reduce rate without changing the transform?
3) Model capacity vs. nonlinearity: For a fixed parameter budget, how does GDN compare to ReLU or parametric ReLU in terms of RD performance? Any insights on where GDN helps most (e.g., low vs. high rates)?
4) Single-vs-multi-rate training: Did you attempt a conditioning mechanism (e.g., on λ or a learned scale) so one model can operate across rates? How does its RD performance compare with per-λ models?
5) Entropy coding details: Your CABAC variant shares marginals across spatial positions within a channel. Did you try simple spatially adaptive contexts (e.g., based on neighboring symbols) and, if so, what was the incremental gain?
6) Visual artifacts: The method tends to simplify textures at low rates. Do you foresee integrating perceptual losses (or texture synthesis priors) to preserve texture statistics where they are perceptually important without harming rates?
7) Runtime: What are the average encode/decode times for Kodak images on CPU/GPU, and how do they compare to JPEG 2000 implementations used?

Overall, this paper presents a clear, well-motivated, and practically relevant advance: a fully differentiable, end-to-end approach to nonlinear transform coding that translates into a working codec with superior perceptual quality and competitive rates. I recommend acceptance.
