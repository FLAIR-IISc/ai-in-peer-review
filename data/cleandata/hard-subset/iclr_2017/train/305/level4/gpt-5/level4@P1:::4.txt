1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression system composed of a nonlinear analysis transform, uniform scalar quantization, and a nonlinear synthesis transform. The transforms are cascades of convolutional layers with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearities and a corresponding approximate inverse (IGDN). To enable joint rate–distortion optimization via stochastic gradient descent despite the non-differentiability of quantization, the authors introduce a continuous relaxation by replacing quantization with additive uniform noise, and they jointly learn the transforms and marginal entropy models. Under certain conditions, the relaxed objective coincides with the evidence lower bound of a variational autoencoder, but the work remains focused on operating at specific points on the rate–distortion curve set by a trade-off parameter. The paper implements an arithmetic entropy coder and reports actual bit rates, and shows improved rate–distortion performance compared with JPEG and JPEG 2000 (especially at low bit rates), with notably better visual quality and higher MS-SSIM across an independent test set.

2. Decision
Accept. The paper presents a well-motivated, end-to-end approach to image compression with a principled relaxation for optimization, and provides compelling empirical results with actual bitrates and strong perceptual quality.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and approach: The work tackles the classical lossy compression problem by optimizing the analysis/synthesis transforms and entropy model jointly for a chosen rate–distortion trade-off. The relaxation via additive uniform noise is a simple and effective way to obtain gradients through quantization, and the authors verify that the relaxed objective closely matches true rate and distortion, especially at finer quantization.
- Technical novelty and placement: Using cascaded convolutions with GDN/IGDN to build a nonlinear transform optimized for compression is well-grounded in both neuroscience-inspired gain control and prior work on Gaussianizing natural image statistics. The discussion clarifies differences from batch normalization and situates the model relative to VAEs, noting the need to target specific operating points on the rate–distortion curve.
- Empirical validation: The evaluation includes grayscale and RGB images, with models trained per λ and tested on Kodak and other standard images not used in training. The paper reports actual bitrates using a CABAC-style arithmetic coder and includes side information overhead. The method generally outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 in PSNR for most images (especially at low rates) and consistently achieves substantially higher MS-SSIM with visually more natural results (reduced blocking/ringing and more faithful contours).
- Scientific rigor: The paper details the architecture, training setup, marginal modeling, and entropy coding, and provides an analysis of the proxy objective’s bias/variance. Comparisons are carefully discussed, including the caveats of aggregating rate–distortion curves across images.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not essential to my decision)
- Clarity on operating points: Emphasize more prominently that separate models are trained for each λ. This is a practical constraint compared to methods with a single model that can vary rate at test time. A short discussion on the implications for deployment would help.
- Entropy modeling: The independent marginal assumption and simple raster-scan coding are acknowledged. Including a brief estimate of the potential gains from a stronger context model (e.g., spatial context or hyperpriors) would contextualize current performance and future improvements.
- Complexity and runtime: A concise summary of encoding/decoding runtime and memory on CPU/GPU, relative to JPEG/JPEG 2000, would strengthen the practical relevance.
- Ablations: The appendix briefly mentions preliminary experiments with alternative nonlinearities. If available, add a compact ablation (e.g., GDN vs. ReLU with matched parameter counts) to quantify the efficiency of GDN/IGDN for compression.
- Evaluation breadth: While Kodak and selected images are standard, adding a short table or figure summarizing performance on a larger and more diverse test set (beyond qualitative galleries) would further support generalization.
- VAE connection: The VAE equivalence is informative. A concise pointer in the main text clarifying when the relaxed objective ceases to correspond to a normalized likelihood (e.g., with non-Euclidean/perceptual metrics) would help readers.

Questions for the authors
- Rate control and model reuse: Since you train a separate model per λ, how sensitive is the performance to λ mismatches at test time? Can a single model with a learned scale parameter (or conditional transforms) approach the same convex hull?
- Entropy coding: Your CABAC-based coder uses independent marginals with limited spatial adaptation. Do you have measurements of the gap between your bitrates and (i) the discrete entropy of your codes, and (ii) a stronger context model? How much improvement do you anticipate from adding simple spatial contexts?
- Relaxation accuracy: The relaxed differential entropy is somewhat positively biased at coarse quantization. Did you observe this affecting the learned transforms in ways that harm discrete performance at very low rates?
- Architecture sensitivity: How robust are results to changes in depth/width and filter sizes? Do shallower networks with more channels close the gap, or is depth critical with GDN?
- Computational cost: What are typical training and inference times per 256×256 image? Any observations on energy/compute requirements relevant to mobile/embedded deployment?
- Color handling: For RGB models, did you explore transforms that operate in a learned color space versus RGB or Y’CbCr? Any differences in rate–distortion or artifacts?
- Generalization: Did you test on high-resolution images (e.g., >1 MP) without retraining? How does performance scale with image size?

Overall, this is a strong and timely paper that advances neural image compression with a principled and practical end-to-end formulation.
