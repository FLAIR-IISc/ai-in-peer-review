Summary and contributions
The paper tackles end-to-end lossy image compression by jointly optimizing analysis and synthesis transforms and an entropy model under a rate–distortion (R–D) objective. The core technical contributions are: (i) a convolutional transform coder using generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN) layers that implement local gain control, (ii) a principled, differentiable surrogate for quantization via additive uniform noise, enabling stochastic gradient descent on the R–D objective, (iii) a clear connection of the relaxed objective to variational autoencoders (VAEs), while keeping the evaluation in the discrete domain with an actual entropy coder, and (iv) thorough empirical results showing improved PSNR for most images (especially at low rates) and markedly better perceptual quality (MS-SSIM) than JPEG and JPEG2000, using real bitrates including side information. The paper is well written, the methodology is grounded in information theory, and the empirical demonstration is convincing.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Principled and well-motivated end-to-end R–D formulation with a sound quantization relaxation, tied to a generative modeling perspective, and supported by actual bitstream evaluation.
- Strong empirical evidence of perceptual gains across bitrates, with competitive (often superior) PSNR to JPEG/JPEG2000, and careful analysis of the relaxed loss vs. true R–D.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses the classic transform coding pipeline and its limitations with linear transforms and independent scalar quantization. The proposed nonlinear, learned transforms with GDN are well motivated by statistics of natural images and prior work on Gaussianization and visual models. The R–D objective is canonical; the additive-uniform-noise relaxation is a standard, justified approximation to quantization that yields a differentiable objective.
- Placement in literature: The link to VAEs is explained precisely, clarifying similarities and key differences (discrete vs. continuous domain, fixed λ trade-off vs. inferred noise scale). The paper situates itself relative to earlier learned compression or generative work (e.g., Gregor et al., Toderici et al.) and articulates why optimizing for R–D with an actual entropy coder is essential for fair comparison to traditional codecs.
- Claims and evidence: The authors validate that the relaxed objective tracks true distortion and rate (showing bias behavior and variance). They report real bitrates using a CABAC-style coder, include side information, and evaluate on standard datasets (Kodak) plus additional images. Perceptually, the results consistently avoid blocking/ringing and preserve edges and contours, aligning with higher MS-SSIM. PSNR is improved on most images, especially at low rates. Implementation and training details (architectures, optimization, density modeling for latents) are presented with care.

Additional feedback and questions to improve the paper
These points are intended to strengthen the work and are not all prerequisites for acceptance.

- Stronger baselines and positioning:
  • Please add comparisons to a more competitive conventional codec such as BPG (HEVC intra) or JPEG XR. This will help position the method against state-of-the-art classical approaches widely used at the time.
  • Since many readers still expect them, include results on standard images (Lena, Barbara, Mandrill/Baboon, Peppers), alongside Kodak. This can be in an appendix with matched bitrates.

- Fairness and methodology of evaluation:
  • Clarify rate control: each λ yields a separate model. How feasible is this in practice? Can the decoder operate with a single model at multiple rates via a tunable λ at test time, or via scale parameters in the entropy model? A brief discussion of multi-rate operation vs. one-model-per-rate would be helpful.
  • Report average encoding/decoding times and model size to contextualize practicality.
  • Quantify side-information overhead explicitly as a fraction of total bitstream at low rates.

- Entropy model and coding:
  • The latent prior assumes independent marginals with piecewise-linear densities. How much rate is left on the table by ignoring spatial and channel dependencies? A small ablation showing gains from (a) your adaptive CABAC vs. a non-adaptive coder and (b) a simple dependency model (e.g., conditioning on neighboring latents or a shared scale) would be informative.
  • Please specify how often the coder’s probability model is updated and whether the adaptation is re-used across images or per-image only.

- Role of GDN and architecture:
  • Provide an ablation comparing GDN/IGDN to standard pointwise nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU, PReLU) with similar parameter counts and depth, reporting both R–D and MS-SSIM. This will isolate the contribution of the joint normalization.
  • Discuss the impact of the number of stages and channel counts on performance and complexity. A brief sensitivity analysis (even on a subset of images) would be valuable.

- Distortion metrics and perceptual quality:
  • You optimize for MSE but evaluate with MS-SSIM as well. If feasible, include a small experiment training with MS-SSIM to show potential further gains and to corroborate your prior claim that perceptual optimization improves visual quality.
  • Consider summarizing aggregate results with a BD-rate/BD-PSNR table across images to complement per-image plots.

- Reproducibility:
  • If possible, release trained models, the entropy coder, and scripts to reproduce bitstreams and figures. This would greatly amplify impact.

Specific questions for the authors
1. How stable is training across different initializations and dataset subsets? Do different runs converge to similar R–D performance and similar transform characteristics?
2. What is the bitrate penalty from using independent latent marginals compared to a simple factorized-with-scale model (e.g., per-channel scale) or a lightweight context model?
3. At very low bitrates, how large is the bias between differential entropy (relaxed loss) and discrete entropy, and does it affect the chosen λ mapping? Can you provide a calibration curve from relaxed rate to actual bits?
4. Can the same analysis/synthesis transforms be shared across rates with only the entropy model adjusted, or is re-training necessary to remain near the convex hull of optimal points?
5. For color images, do you observe any benefit from working in a perceptually-motivated color space (e.g., YCbCr) versus RGB within the learned transforms?
6. Are there failure modes or image classes where the method underperforms JPEG2000 in PSNR at higher rates? Any diagnostic insights?

Overall, this is an excellent and timely paper: principled, clearly written, and empirically convincing. With stronger baselines and a bit more analysis of the entropy model and GDN’s role, it can set a high standard for learned image compression.
