1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper presents an end-to-end learned image compression system that optimizes a nonlinear analysis/synthesis transform pair, a uniform quantizer, and an adaptive entropy coder for rate–distortion performance. The transforms are three-stage convolutional networks with generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN) nonlinearities. To enable gradient-based training despite quantization, the authors introduce a continuous relaxation by replacing rounding with additive uniform noise, and they discuss connections to variational autoencoders (VAEs). They train separate models for different λ values to cover the rate–distortion curve and report actual bit rates via an implemented CABAC-style entropy coder, not just entropy estimates. Empirically, they show consistent rate–distortion gains over JPEG and often over JPEG 2000, with visibly better perceptual quality at matched bit rates.

2. Decision
Weak Accept. The paper demonstrates strong empirical performance with a complete, practical system that produces actual bitstreams and convincingly improves visual quality over JPEG/JPEG 2000. However, novelty relative to prior work on end-to-end learned compression and the authors’ own earlier GDN-based results is somewhat limited, and key ablations (e.g., contribution of adaptive entropy coding) are missing.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and approach: The paper tackles end-to-end optimization of transform coding under a rate–distortion objective. The use of GDN/IGDN, additive uniform noise as a differentiable proxy for quantization, and a learned marginal model for entropy coding is well motivated and grounded in the literature. The idea of treating the uniform-noise relaxed objective as akin to a VAE is clearly articulated.
- Novelty and placement: Similar rate–distortion training with quantization relaxation and GDN was previously published by the authors; here the architecture is deeper and the entropy coding is upgraded from simple models to an adaptive arithmetic coder. These are solid engineering improvements, but the conceptual novelty is moderate.
- Claims vs. evidence: The experimental results are compelling. The use of actual bitrates via CABAC strengthens the evaluation, and the visual quality gap to JPEG/JPEG 2000 at low and medium rates is clear. The training/evaluation methodology is described carefully. That said, it is hard to disentangle where the gains come from (deeper transforms vs. GDN vs. adaptive coding) because ablations are not provided.
- Theoretical clarity: The VAE connection is interesting but appears to have a technical issue. Equation 10 appears to be incorrect; I believe the partition function should depend on gs(y; θ). If so, the approach is not equivalent to a VAE for non-Euclidean/perceptual metrics, as the normalizing constant would depend on θ through gs.

4. Additional feedback and questions to improve the paper (these are meant to help and are not strictly decisive for my assessment)
- MSE vs. perceptual metrics:
  - Why did you optimize MSE rather than a perceptual metric as in your previous work? Given the authors’ expertise, it is surprising that even the evaluation was only performed in terms of PSNR. Please clarify the rationale and, if possible, include results for a perceptual metric (e.g., MS-SSIM) both for training and evaluation, or explain trade-offs you observed.
- Contribution of adaptive entropy coding:
  - Please quantify the contribution of the adaptive entropy coder vs. the deeper analysis/synthesis transforms. An ablation showing performance with a non-adaptive coder (as in your prior paper) would be very informative.
  - Provide more detail on the adaptive coder design, contexts, and adaptation schedule, and report the bit-rate gap between your arithmetic code and the measured discrete entropy (and its dependence on rate).
- VAE correspondence and Equation 10:
  - Equation 10 appears to be wrong, and I believe the partition function should depend on gs(y; θ). If this is correct, the equivalence to the VAE objective does not hold for non-Euclidean/perceptual metrics because the normalizing constant depends on θ through gs. Please check the derivation, specify all dependencies, and delineate precisely when the VAE interpretation is valid.
- Ablations and architectural choices:
  - Please provide ablations on: depth/width of the transforms; GDN/IGDN vs. pointwise nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU or sigmoid); with/without down/up-sampling; and the effect of marginal model parameterization granularity. This would clarify what drives the gains.
  - Report the effect of training separate models per λ versus a single conditional model that can trade off bits and distortion at test time.
- Practical considerations:
  - Provide encoding/decoding runtime and memory comparisons with JPEG/JPEG 2000 on a common hardware platform. This helps assess deployment feasibility.
  - Clarify exactly which side information is included and its overhead at different rates; quantify its proportion of the total bitstream.
  - Discuss generalization: how sensitive are results to the training dataset, and how do models trained on ImageNet perform on other distributions?

Questions for the authors
- Can you confirm whether the partition function in the relaxed generative view depends on θ via gs? If so, how does this affect the claimed equivalence to VAEs, especially for non-Euclidean metrics or with a non-identity perceptual transform gp?
- What motivated the choice to optimize MSE rather than a perceptual metric, and to evaluate primarily with PSNR? Do you have results with MS-SSIM-optimized models and corresponding perceptual evaluations?
- How much of the observed bitrate savings comes from the adaptive entropy coder versus the learned transforms? Can you report rate–distortion curves with a fixed, non-adaptive code to isolate this effect?
- How critical is GDN/IGDN to your results? What is the performance gap to ReLU-based architectures at similar parameter counts and depths?
- What is the computational cost of training and inference, and how does it scale with image size and the number of channels?

Overall, despite limited conceptual novelty, the paper presents a strong, complete system with convincing empirical gains and high-quality visual results. Addressing the points above would further strengthen the contribution.
