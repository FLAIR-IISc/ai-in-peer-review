1. Summary of contributions
This paper presents a complete, end-to-end optimized image compression system built from cascaded convolutional transforms with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) in the analysis path and an inverse GDN (IGDN) in the synthesis path. The codec optimizes a principled rate–distortion objective using a continuous relaxation of quantization (additive uniform noise), enabling stochastic gradient descent despite the non-differentiability of rounding. The authors connect the relaxed objective to variational autoencoders, while emphasizing key differences relevant to compression. Importantly, they train the transforms and marginal models per operating point (λ), implement an actual entropy coder (CABAC-like), and report real bit rates on standard test sets. Empirically, the method generally outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 in PSNR at low to medium rates and achieves consistently superior perceptual quality (MS-SSIM), with artifacts that are visually more natural than those from linear transform coders.

2. Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- A principled, well-motivated rate–distortion formulation with a differentiable proxy that leads to a practical, complete codec and demonstrably strong performance.
- Clear and consistent perceptual gains across images and bit rates, supported by MS-SSIM and by actual bitstream outputs rather than entropy estimates alone.

3. Supporting arguments
- What problem is addressed? The paper tackles end-to-end optimization of lossy image compression, jointly learning analysis/synthesis transforms and entropy models to minimize R + λD under scalar quantization.
- Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? Yes. The use of GDN/IGDN is grounded in prior work showing strong Gaussianization and coding efficiency; the continuous relaxation is standard in quantization theory and is carefully tied to VAEs while explaining why compression demands differ (discreteness, operating at multiple λ). The paper compares to JPEG and JPEG 2000, which are reasonable baselines, and it implements a real entropy coder.
- Do the results support the claims? Largely yes. The authors verify that the relaxed loss tracks the discrete rate and distortion, especially at finer quantization. They provide extensive qualitative evidence and MS-SSIM gains at all rates. PSNR improvements vs JPEG 2000 are image- and rate-dependent, but favorable overall at lower rates. Reporting real bit rates strengthens credibility. The architecture/training details and ablations (e.g., early comments on alternative nonlinearities) are informative, though more systematic ablations would further reinforce conclusions about GDN’s efficiency.

Limitations that temper the strength of claims:
- Baselines do not include the strongest non-neural classical codecs beyond JPEG 2000, so the relative position vs state of the art outside neural methods is not yet fully established.
- The transforms are trained separately per λ; the practicality of deploying multiple models versus a single model operating across rates is not fully addressed.
- The entropy model assumes independent marginals with limited spatial adaptation (raster scan), which may underutilize context compared to modern codecs.

4. Additional feedback and questions (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Please include results on Lena, Barbara, and Baboon (not “Gibbons”), along with clear comparisons to strong classical state-of-the-art methods beyond JPEG 2000. It is important to position neural approaches relative to the best prior non-neural codecs.
- Clarify the mapping between λ and achieved bit rate. Is there a simple procedure for targeting a desired bpp, and how stable is the rate under distributional shift?
- Discuss the practicality of maintaining distinct models per λ. Can a single trained model approximate the same convex hull (e.g., by varying quantization or injecting variable noise) without retraining?
- Provide more detail on the entropy coder’s contribution. How close are your bit rates to the discrete entropy and to an oracle using the learned marginals? How much do CABAC’s adaptations matter?
- Expand the ablation on nonlinearities: quantitative RD curves comparing GDN/IGDN vs ReLU/sigmoid with similar parameter counts would substantiate the efficiency claim.
- Generalization: report cross-dataset performance beyond Kodak and a few standard images. Do the learned marginals and transforms transfer well?
- Complexity: include encoding/decoding runtime and memory metrics and compare to JPEG 2000, to illuminate deployment trade-offs.
- Color handling: specify color space conversions, whether training was in RGB or a luma–chroma space, and how this affects RD and visual quality.
- Relaxation fidelity: quantify any residual bias between the relaxed differential entropy and discrete entropy across rates and channels; discuss how this impacts optimality along the RD curve.
- Reproducibility: consider releasing code/models and the exact entropy coding configuration to facilitate fair comparisons.

Questions for the authors
- How is λ selected in practice for a target bit rate, and how robust is this mapping across images?
- Can your framework be extended to a single model that supports multiple rates (e.g., learned scaling, conditional transforms, or learned importance maps) without materially degrading RD?
- To what extent do dependencies between coefficients remain after GDN? Would modest context modeling (e.g., causal neighbors) in the entropy coder yield additional gains?
- How sensitive are results to the independence assumption in the latent space and to the spline density parameterization?
- Do you observe any systematic cases where PSNR is worse than JPEG 2000 at higher rates? What are the failure modes?
- Could the method be directly optimized for MS-SSIM (or another perceptual metric) while preserving the benefits you show for MSE training?

Overall, this is a compelling, well-executed paper. With stronger classical baselines and a few clarifications, it will provide a clear benchmark for end-to-end learned image compression.
