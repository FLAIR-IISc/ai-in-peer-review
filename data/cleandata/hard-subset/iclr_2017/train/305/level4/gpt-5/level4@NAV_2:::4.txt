1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a complete end-to-end image compression system built from cascaded convolutional analysis/synthesis transforms with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and its approximate inverse (IGDN), a uniform scalar quantizer, and an implemented entropy coder. The key technical idea is to optimize the rate–distortion objective directly by replacing the non-differentiable quantization step with additive uniform noise, yielding a continuous proxy loss suitable for stochastic gradient descent. The authors relate this relaxed objective to variational autoencoders under specific conditions, but emphasize that compression requires operating along the entire rate–distortion curve via a trade-off parameter. The method is trained per rate point (per λ) on a large image dataset, models marginal latents non-parametrically, and evaluates performance using actual bitrates via a CABAC-style entropy code. Experiments on standard test images (including the Kodak set) show improved rate–distortion over JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most images (especially at lower rates) and consistently better perceptual quality judged by MS-SSIM and visual inspection, despite training with MSE.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (i) a principled, end-to-end rate–distortion optimization with a well-justified continuous relaxation that is empirically validated; (ii) strong, practically relevant results with actual bitrates and clear perceptual gains over established codecs, delivered by a complete codec (not just entropy estimates).

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and approach: The paper tackles the classic lossy image compression problem by jointly optimizing transform, quantization surrogate, and entropy model for rate–distortion performance. The additive-uniform-noise relaxation is well motivated, standard in quantization theory, and here is tied to a nonparametric marginal model for accurate entropy estimation.
- Relation to literature: The connection to VAEs is clearly drawn, while important distinctions for compression (discrete codes, operating across the RD curve, reporting actual bitrates) are articulated. Positioning relative to JPEG/JPEG 2000 and prior learned codecs is appropriate.
- Methodological completeness: The inclusion of an implemented entropy coder and reporting of true bitrates (not differential entropy) makes the comparison credible. The per-λ optimization maps out the convex hull of achievable RD trade-offs.
- Empirical evidence: The paper verifies that the relaxed loss tracks true RD, and presents consistent gains in MS-SSIM and frequent PSNR improvements, with qualitatively superior artifact profiles (reduced blocking/ringing, better contour preservation). Both grayscale and RGB models are evaluated.
- Clarity and rigor: The model, training objective, architectural choices (three-stage conv + GDN/IGDN), and optimization details are described thoroughly, with reasonable ablations discussed (e.g., preliminary notes on alternative nonlinearities).

4. Suggestions to improve (constructive, not essential to the accept decision)
- Multi-rate modeling: Consider a single conditional model operating across λ (e.g., via conditioning or learned scaling) to avoid training separate models per rate and to enable fine-grained rate control at test time.
- Ablations: Provide systematic comparisons for (i) GDN/IGDN vs pointwise nonlinearities (ReLU/sigmoid) at matched parameter counts; (ii) number of stages and receptive field size; (iii) different marginal density parameterizations and sampling resolutions; and (iv) alternative uniform-noise widths to probe sensitivity of the relaxation.
- Entropy modeling: The independence assumption across latents is a limiting factor. Exploring simple spatial/context models or channel-wise autoregressive/contextual priors could further improve coding gains and reduce mismatch handled by CABAC adaptation.
- Complexity reporting: Include encoder/decoder runtime, model size, and memory footprints on standard hardware, plus a breakdown of analysis/synthesis vs entropy coding time.
- Evaluation breadth and fairness: Clarify JPEG/JPEG2000 configuration choices (e.g., color subsampling, target-rate vs target-quality), and consider additional datasets/resolutions to assess generalization and robustness to acquisition artifacts.
- Perceptual objectives: Given the strong MS-SSIM gains, a brief experiment training with a perceptual loss (or a mixed MSE/perceptual loss) could be illuminating.
- Side information: Quantify the overhead for transmitting λ and any model indices, and report its impact on bitrates at very low rates.

Questions for the authors
- Entropy coding details: How many contexts and what adaptation schedule are used in the CABAC-style coder? Are marginal models shared per channel across all spatial positions, and how frequently are they updated during coding?
- Relaxation fidelity: How sensitive is RD performance to the exact width and centering of the additive uniform noise? Did you try non-uniform relaxations or learned dequantization?
- Per-λ training: Could your architecture be conditioned on λ at inference time (e.g., via FiLM or scaling of latent variances) to enable a single multi-rate model without sacrificing RD optimality?
- Independence assumption: Did you measure residual dependencies among latents post-GDN, and if so, how large are they? Would a simple hyperprior or context model capture enough structure to improve rates?
- Computational profile: What are the average encode/decode times and GPU/CPU requirements for Kodak-resolution images? How does runtime scale with the number of channels and stages?
- Color processing: Which color space is used internally for RGB models, and is chroma subsampling applied for fair baselines when comparing to JPEG/JPEG2000?
- Generalization: How does the model perform on high-resolution photographs or domains not seen during training? Any failure cases where JPEG 2000 still dominates in PSNR at higher rates?
- Side information and robustness: Beyond λ, is any parameter adaptation transmitted per image (e.g., marginal tables)? If so, what is the typical overhead and how robust is decoding to model mismatch?
