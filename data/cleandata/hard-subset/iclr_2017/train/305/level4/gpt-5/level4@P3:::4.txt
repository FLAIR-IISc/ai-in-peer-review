Summary and contributions
The paper tackles end-to-end optimization of lossy image compression, directly minimizing a rate–distortion objective over the entire codec: a learned nonlinear analysis transform, uniform scalar quantization, a learned nonlinear synthesis transform, and an entropy coder. The transforms are implemented as three-stage convolutional cascades with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) and its approximate inverse (IGDN), inspired by gain-control mechanisms in biological vision and previously shown to Gaussianize image statistics effectively. To enable SGD-based training despite the non-differentiability of quantization, the authors introduce a continuous relaxation by replacing rounding with additive uniform noise and learn nonparametric, factorized marginal models for the latents. They train separate models for different Lagrange multipliers λ to trace the rate–distortion curve, and evaluate with an actual entropy coder (CABAC-like), reporting bitrates rather than differential entropies. Empirically, on Kodak and other common test images, the method generally outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 in PSNR at many bitrates and shows consistent and substantial gains in MS-SSIM, with compelling visual quality (fewer blocking/ringing artifacts). The paper also clarifies connections and differences to variational autoencoders.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- A principled, well-motivated end-to-end compression framework that unifies analysis/synthesis transforms, quantization, and entropy coding with a sound relaxation, and reports actual bitrates.
- Strong empirical evidence of improved rate–distortion and markedly better perceptual quality across diverse images and bitrates, with careful analysis and fair baselines.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Learning-based image compression is important and timely. The paper directly optimizes the classical R+λD objective, addressing the core difficulty (non-differentiable quantization) with an elegant uniform-noise relaxation that is standard in quantization theory and validated empirically here.
- Motivation and placement in literature: The approach builds on transform coding and modern deep architectures, with GDN/IGDN motivated by decades of work on local gain control and prior evidence for Gaussianization. The paper thoughtfully situates itself relative to VAEs, recurrent neural compression, and prior conceptual compression work, articulating why compression requires discrete coding and fixed operating points along the R–D curve. It also positions the contribution versus JPEG/JPEG 2000 under a common evaluation protocol.
- Evidence and rigor: The methodology is clearly specified (architecture, training, entropy model), the relaxation is justified and tested, and the evaluation uses actual entropy coding and includes side information. Results span grayscale and RGB, multiple rates, and multiple quality metrics (PSNR and MS-SSIM), with qualitative visual comparisons. The claim that the approach improves perceptual quality is supported both subjectively and via MS-SSIM. Limitations (e.g., factorized latents, λ-specific models) are acknowledged.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (constructive; not all are decision-critical)
- Single model for variable rate: Training one model per λ is practical but less convenient than a single variable-rate model. Discuss strategies for rate control within one model (e.g., scale parameters, conditional gains, or learned rate-conditioning).
- Entropy model capacity: The independence assumption over latents likely leaves bits on the table. A brief experiment with simple spatial/context models or a learned hyperprior would clarify potential gains and the trade-off with complexity.
- Ablations and architecture: Provide ablations quantifying the impact of GDN/IGDN vs. pointwise nonlinearities at matched parameter budgets and depths, and sensitivity to network capacity and number of stages.
- Complexity and deployment: Include encode/decode runtimes, model sizes, and memory footprint to contextualize practicality versus JPEG 2000. Discuss potential for fast inference and embedded deployment.
- Fairness details: Clarify color space handling and chroma subsampling settings for all baselines; ensure MS-SSIM is computed consistently across methods (e.g., on luma vs. RGB).
- Relaxation–discretization gap: The rate bias at coarse quantization is acknowledged; adding a small analysis on how this affects λ selection and final R–D operating points would strengthen confidence.
- Reproducibility: Consider releasing code/models and the bitstream format to facilitate comparisons.

Questions to the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the factorized latent assumption? Do you observe strong spatial/context dependencies that an autoregressive or hyperprior model could exploit, and what are the expected bitrate savings?
- Can the same trained transform operate across a range of λ by modulating a small set of parameters (e.g., per-channel scales), or do the transforms qualitatively change across rates?
- How does GDN/IGDN compare to ReLU or PReLU networks when depth/parameters are matched, especially at low bitrates? Are the observed GDN advantages primarily from joint normalization or better conditioning?
- What are the average encoding/decoding times and model sizes on the reported test images? How do they scale with image resolution?
- The CABAC variant uses raster scan with limited context. Have you tested more structured scan orders or contexts (e.g., per-subband, local neighborhoods), and how much additional gain is feasible?
- How robust is the uniform-noise relaxation to training instabilities at very low rates? Did you need special tricks (e.g., gradient clipping, variance scheduling)?
- For RGB, did you explore alternative color spaces (YCbCr, opponent) or learned color transforms in the first stage, and do they affect rate–distortion?

Overall, this is a strong paper: principled, clear, and empirically convincing. It makes a compelling case for end-to-end learned transform coding with discrete entropy coding and should be of broad interest to the ICLR community.
