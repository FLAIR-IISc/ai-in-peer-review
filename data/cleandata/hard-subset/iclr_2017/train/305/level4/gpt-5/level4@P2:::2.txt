Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a complete, end-to-end image compression system built from cascades of convolutional layers and a biologically inspired joint nonlinearity (GDN/IGDN), trained to optimize rate–distortion. The approach replaces the discontinuous quantization step during training with additive uniform noise, enabling stochastic gradient descent while closely approximating the discrete objective. The authors also implement an actual entropy coder (CABAC-style), report true bit rates rather than entropy estimates, and analyze the connection of their relaxed objective to variational autoencoders. Empirically, the method yields strong rate–distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, with notably improved perceptual quality, and competitive PSNR/MS-SSIM on the Kodak set and other images.

Decision: Accept (weak)
- Reason 1: Strong empirical performance and a credible, practically complete system (including an entropy coder) that outperforms JPEG 2000 across many images, with markedly better visual quality.
- Reason 2: Solid, clear paper that extends previous work to deeper transforms and demonstrates feasibility with real bitstreams; although the novelty is incremental, the results are compelling and timely.

Supporting arguments
- Technical soundness: The continuous relaxation for end-to-end optimization is well motivated and empirically validated; the training setup and architecture choices are described in sufficient detail to reproduce the approach.
- Practical significance: Reporting actual bit rates with a concrete entropy coder strengthens the contribution relative to many learned compression papers that compare only entropy estimates.
- Comparison baseline: JPEG and JPEG 2000 are appropriate baselines. The visual comparisons and MS-SSIM results support the claim of improved perceptual quality. PSNR is competitive and often better, especially at low rates.
- Novelty: The core idea of end-to-end rate–distortion optimization with a noise-relaxed quantizer was introduced in prior work by the authors; here the main additions are deeper convolutional transforms, GDN/IGDN throughout the stack, and an adaptive entropy coding implementation. These are valuable but incremental.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not central to the accept decision)
- Clarify VAE equivalence: In the variational interpretation, the partition function in the non-Euclidean case (your Eq. 16) should generally depend on θ via gs (and possibly gp), not only on λ. Please revise the statement of equivalence and clearly delimit the conditions under which it holds.
- Motivation for MSE: Prior work by the authors showed advantages of optimizing a perceptual metric. Please justify the choice of MSE for training (e.g., interpretability, color handling, stability), and, if possible, add a small-scale comparison where the same architecture is trained with a perceptual objective to quantify any trade-offs.
- Ablations on coding: Separate the contributions of the deeper nonlinear transforms vs. adaptive entropy coding. A comparison using a non-adaptive entropy code (or a fixed marginal model) would help isolate the impact of adaptation.
- Architectural ablations: Given the emphasis on GDN/IGDN, include an ablation replacing GDN with standard pointwise nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU) at matched parameter count to quantify its contribution to rate–distortion and perceptual quality.
- Generalization and efficiency: Briefly discuss computational cost (encode/decode time), memory, and model size, and how these scale with image size and number of channels. Any observations about robustness across datasets beyond Kodak would be useful.
- Reporting: Since the paper emphasizes perceptual quality, consider adding more systematic MS-SSIM summaries (alongside per-image plots) to complement PSNR summaries.

Questions for the authors
- What was the primary reason for using MSE rather than a perceptual objective during training in this work (e.g., simplicity, comparability, color support, stability)? Could you share results for a perceptual-loss-trained variant with the same architecture?
- How much of the observed gain comes from the adaptive entropy coder vs. the learned transforms? Please provide rate–distortion curves with (i) your trained transforms + non-adaptive entropy code and (ii) adaptive code with a simpler transform, if possible.
- In your VAE-related derivation, does the partition function in the non-Euclidean case depend on θ via gs? If so, how does this affect the claimed equivalence and the optimization?
- How sensitive is performance to λ at test time? Do models trained at a particular λ generalize well to nearby rates if you adjust quantization or the probability model, or is per-λ training essential?
- Could a single model operate across multiple rates (e.g., by scaling latent variables or using learned rate controllers), and how would its performance compare to per-λ models?
- Can you quantify the contribution of GDN/IGDN vs. pointwise nonlinearities at matched depth/parameters, both in RD and in perceptual artifacts?
- What are typical encode/decode times on CPU/GPU for Kodak-sized images, and what is the model size? Are there bottlenecks that would hinder deployment?

Overall, despite incremental novelty relative to the authors’ earlier work, this paper presents a robust, complete, and empirically strong compression system. Addressing the theoretical clarification and ablations would further strengthen the contribution.
