Summary and contributions
The paper presents a complete, end-to-end learned image compression system optimized for rate–distortion performance. The codec comprises cascaded convolutional analysis and synthesis transforms with generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN), uniform scalar quantization, and an implemented entropy coder (CABAC-style). To enable gradient-based training, the authors replace quantization with additive uniform noise and optimize a relaxed rate–distortion objective; they also discuss conditions under which this relaxation aligns with a VAE-style log-likelihood. Empirically, the method outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 in rate–distortion (especially at lower bitrates) and delivers notably better perceptual quality, corroborated by MS-SSIM. The paper is clearly written, positions the work in both transform coding and generative modeling literatures, and evaluates using actual bitstreams rather than entropy estimates, strengthening the practical relevance.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) Strong empirical significance: consistent improvements over JPEG/JPEG 2000 in bit rate at comparable or better distortion, with clear perceptual gains and evaluation on actual coded bitstreams.
2) Technical completeness and clarity: a coherent end-to-end formulation (including training relaxation and a working entropy coder), coupled with an insightful connection to VAEs that clarifies when the relaxation is principled.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The work addresses end-to-end optimization of transform coding—historically a pipeline of separately tuned components—by jointly learning nonlinear analysis/synthesis transforms and marginal models to navigate the rate–distortion trade-off. This is well motivated, grounded in classic rate–distortion theory and modern deep learning practice, and well placed relative to both conventional codecs and generative models.
- Methodological soundness: The additive-uniform-noise relaxation is standard in high-resolution quantization theory and is used here in a way that closely approximates discrete entropy and MSE; the authors verify approximation quality empirically. The choice of GDN/IGDN is supported by prior work showing its efficiency in Gaussianizing image statistics and, here, significantly reduces the depth/parameters needed for good compression performance.
- Empirical support: Results are reported on images not used for training, with side information included in the bitstream, and a practical arithmetic coder in the loop. Visual comparisons and MS-SSIM gains are compelling and consistent with the architecture’s ability to suppress linear transform artifacts (blocking/ringing) while preserving edges and contours. Reporting actual rates, rather than raw entropy estimates, avoids a common pitfall in learned compression papers.
- Novelty: While the authors’ prior work introduced end-to-end optimization with a simpler architecture and a perceptual metric, this paper advances architectural capacity (deeper conv–GDN stacks), implements a practical entropy coder, extends to RGB, and carefully examines the VAE connection. Though incremental conceptually, the step to a fully operational codec with state-of-the-art performance against widely used baselines is substantial and impactful.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not central to decision)
- Clarify VAE equivalence: In Eq. (16), the partition function Z(λ) should in general depend on θ via gs. If the metric is not Euclidean or gp is not affine/invertible, please make explicit whether px|ỹ is properly normalizable and how the dependence on θ affects the equivalence. This would help avoid confusion about when the VAE interpretation strictly holds.
- MSE vs perceptual training: The paper motivates MSE (comparability, color handling). It would still be valuable to add a short ablation comparing MSE-trained vs perceptual-metric–trained models (e.g., MS-SSIM or your prior perceptual transform) at matched architecture/capacity, at least on luminance, to quantify the trade-offs (PSNR vs MS-SSIM vs appearance).
- Contribution of adaptive entropy coding: Please report the rate gap between (i) the relaxed differential entropy, (ii) the discrete entropy estimate, and (iii) the realized bit rate with and without adaptation. A small ablation comparing your adaptive coder to a non-adaptive arithmetic coder would isolate how much gain stems from adaptation versus the learned transform.
- Architectural ablations: Briefly quantify the role of GDN vs ReLU/Leaky ReLU at fixed depth/width (you allude to preliminary findings). A small table or figure would strengthen the claim that GDN/IGDN is parameter-efficient for compression.
- Fairness in color handling: Since JPEG uses 4:2:0 by default, while your model operates in RGB without subsampling, clarify how comparisons are normalized (e.g., report both 4:2:0 and 4:4:4 JPEG where appropriate, or provide luminance-only comparisons) to preempt concerns about chroma treatment.
- Complexity and throughput: A brief note on encode/decode runtimes and model size relative to JPEG 2000 would help readers gauge deployment feasibility, especially given claims about efficiency once trained.

Questions for the authors
1) VAE connection and normalization: For Eq. (16), should Z(λ) be Z(λ,θ) given the dependence on gs? If not, why does the normalizer not depend on θ? For non-Euclidean metrics or non-affine gp, can px|ỹ be normalized, and do you still claim equivalence to a VAE objective?
2) Relaxation accuracy: Could you provide quantitative summaries (mean bias/variance) of the relaxed entropy and distortion approximations versus actual bit rate and MSE across all test images and rates?
3) Adaptive coding ablation: What is the average bit-rate gain of your adaptive coder over a non-adaptive arithmetic coder when using the same learned transforms and marginal models?
4) Role of GDN: At fixed depth/width, how much degradation occurs when replacing GDN/IGDN with ReLU or tanh? Conversely, how much additional capacity is needed for ReLU networks to match GDN performance?
5) MSE choice: Beyond comparability and color, did you try training with MS-SSIM or a hybrid loss? If so, how did rate–distortion and perceived quality change at low bitrates?
6) Single model across rates: You train separate models per λ. Did you consider a single model with a learned rate-control parameter (e.g., conditioning, feature scaling, or λ-annealing) to cover multiple operating points? What trade-offs did you observe?
7) Color space and subsampling: For RGB experiments, did you explore YCbCr transforms and chroma subsampling in your pipeline? Could this reduce rate further without harming MS-SSIM on luma?
8) Generalization: How sensitive is performance to the training set distribution and preprocessing? Have you tested on larger, more diverse benchmarks beyond Kodak and standard images?

Overall, this is a strong, well-executed paper that delivers practical and scientific value. I recommend acceptance.
