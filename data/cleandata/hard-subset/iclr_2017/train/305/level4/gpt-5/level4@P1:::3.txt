Summary and contributions
This paper presents an end-to-end trainable image compression system that jointly optimizes a nonlinear analysis transform, a uniform quantizer, a learned entropy model, and a nonlinear synthesis transform. The key technical idea is a continuous relaxation of the quantizer: replacing rounding with additive uniform noise enables stochastic gradient descent on a rate–distortion objective that includes a differentiable proxy for entropy. The transforms are implemented as three-stage convolutional cascades with generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN), motivated by biological gain-control and prior work on Gaussianizing image statistics. The authors connect their relaxed objective to variational autoencoders, clarify the differences with generative modeling, and, importantly, evaluate actual bit rates via an implemented CABAC-style entropy coder. Experiments on Kodak, legacy benchmarks, and additional photos show consistent perceptual gains (MS-SSIM) and generally improved PSNR vs JPEG and JPEG 2000, with notably more natural artifacts at low bit rates.

Decision: Accept
Reasons: (1) Strong, well-motivated end-to-end formulation with a practical, differentiable rate proxy and an implemented entropy coder, not just entropy estimates. (2) Convincing empirical results in both rate–distortion metrics and visual quality, with clear ablations on the relaxed vs discrete objective. I expect the differentiable rate formulation to be influential beyond compression.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and framing: The paper tackles the classical rate–distortion optimization for lossy image compression, aiming to learn transforms and an entropy model jointly rather than optimizing transform, quantizer, and entropy coder separately. The formulation is clear and grounded in information theory.
- Motivation and placement: The use of GDN/IGDN is well justified by prior work on Gaussianization and biologically inspired gain control. The discussion relating the relaxed objective to VAEs is careful and clarifies why compression differs (discrete codes; operating along an R–D curve for varying λ; practical bit rates vs differential entropy). Comparisons to prior learned compression efforts (e.g., Toderici et al., Gregor et al.) are fair and insightful.
- Claims and evidence: The authors evaluate actual bitstreams using a CABAC-style coder, include side information, and compare against standard baselines. They empirically validate that the relaxed objective approximates the discrete R–D behavior, including bias trends. MS-SSIM consistently improves, and PSNR is better for most images, especially at low rates. Visual examples substantiate the qualitative advantages (fewer blocking/ringing artifacts, better contour preservation).
- Author response: I raised a concern that, under fixed range and quantization, a sufficiently expressive network might learn to produce codes with maximal entropy within that range, effectively meeting an upper bound without a separate rate model. The authors’ rebuttal—that enforcing such behavior implicitly forces the network to internalize variable-rate coding logic that is more efficiently and transparently handled by an explicit entropy model—was convincing. It clarifies the design trade-off and supports the decision to separate transform learning from entropy coding.

Feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not core to the decision)
- Baselines: If feasible, add comparisons to more modern intra-image baselines (e.g., WebP, BPG/HEVC-intra) to contextualize gains beyond JPEG/JPEG 2000.
- Ablations: Include controlled ablations quantifying the contribution of GDN/IGDN vs pointwise nonlinearities at matched parameter budgets and depth, and the impact of model capacity on R–D performance.
- Single model for multiple rates: Discuss or experiment with a single model handling multiple rates (e.g., via a learned scale, conditional normalization, or a hyperprior), to avoid training separate models per λ.
- Entropy model: The factorized prior over latents is a limitation. Consider noting how spatial/context models or autoregressive/hyperprior approaches might improve coding gains, and clarify the extent to which CABAC compensates for model mismatch.
- Runtime and complexity: Provide encoding/decoding times, memory footprint, and parameter counts, compared to JPEG 2000, on representative hardware. This will help assess deployability.
- Color and metrics: Since MS-SSIM is computed on luma, add color fidelity metrics (e.g., DeltaE, or MS-SSIM on chroma) or qualitative discussion of color artifacts, especially for RGB models.
- λ calibration: Add a simple mapping from λ to expected bpp for practitioners, or show calibration curves and variance across images.
- Evaluation protocol: The appendix rightly notes that set-level R–D summaries are tricky. Consider including per-image aggregate statistics (e.g., median gains) or confidence intervals to complement the comprehensive per-image plots online.

Questions for the authors
1) Independence assumption: How much performance is left on the table by assuming independent latents during training and coding? Did you test simple context models over y (e.g., channel-wise or spatial neighbors) and quantify gains?
2) Single model, multiple rates: Can you condition the analysis/synthesis transforms on λ (or a learned rate-control parameter) to cover a range of rates without retraining?
3) Theoretical bounds: Can you provide formal bounds or conditions under which the additive-uniform-noise relaxation upper/lower bounds the discrete entropy and distortion? How do these bounds vary with λ and quantization coarseness?
4) Stability and reproducibility: How sensitive are results to initialization and training hyperparameters? What fraction of runs reaches comparable R–D performance?
5) Runtime: What are typical encode/decode times and peak memory for 512×512 and 1080p images on CPU vs GPU? Any bottlenecks in the entropy coder?
6) Side information: What is the average overhead of signaling λ and any other header fields? How does this amortize over image sizes?
7) CABAC details: How many contexts are used, and how quickly do they adapt? Did you try more spatially aware scan orders or tiling to improve adaptation?
8) GDN vs ReLU: In your preliminary results, how does performance scale with depth/width to match GDN/IGDN? Any qualitative differences in artifacts?
9) Color handling: For RGB, do you work in RGB directly or a decorrelated color space (e.g., YCbCr) before the analysis transform? Does a learned color transform help?
10) Failure cases: For images where JPEG 2000 outperforms your method in PSNR at high rates, what patterns in the content or spectrum drive that behavior?

Overall, this is a strong, well-executed paper that addresses an important problem with a principled and practical approach. I recommend acceptance.
