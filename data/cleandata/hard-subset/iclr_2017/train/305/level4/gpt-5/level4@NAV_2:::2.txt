1. Summary of contributions
The paper presents an end-to-end image compression framework that jointly optimizes a nonlinear analysis transform, a uniform scalar quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis transform for rate–distortion performance using stochastic gradient descent. The transforms are built from cascades of convolutions and generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN), and the training uses a continuous relaxation that replaces quantization with additive uniform noise. The work extends prior efforts by employing deeper encoder/decoder architectures and moving from a simple entropy estimate to an adaptive entropy coding scheme. It also positions the relaxed objective relative to variational autoencoders. Empirically, the method produces visibly superior reconstructions and improves over JPEG 2000, which is a notable achievement for a learned compression system. The paper is clearly written.

2. Decision
Accept (weak accept), primarily because:
- The method delivers clear, visible improvements over JPEG 2000 and, to my knowledge, sets a new bar for learned image compression performance.
- The work offers a complete, practical system including adaptive entropy coding, and is carefully executed and presented.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: The paper tackles end-to-end rate–distortion optimization for lossy image compression, aiming to learn the analysis/synthesis transforms and code-space distribution jointly, rather than optimizing transform, quantizer, and entropy code separately.
- Motivation and placement in the literature: The approach is well-motivated, leveraging GDN/IGDN to reduce higher-order dependencies and a continuous relaxation to enable SGD, and it situates the formulation relative to prior nonlinear transform coding and VAEs. That said, the core rate–distortion optimization framework has appeared before; the present paper’s novelty lies mainly in deeper transforms and the use of adaptive entropy coding.
- Evidence and rigor: The experimental results show clear visual gains and performance that surpasses JPEG 2000, which is compelling. However, the incremental novelty is modest, and some clarifications are needed about the theoretical link to VAEs and the contribution of the adaptive entropy coder versus the deeper transforms. In addition, in some respects this appears to step back from earlier work that optimized a perceptual objective, as the present paper trains with MSE and emphasizes PSNR in evaluation.

4. Suggestions for improvement (to help the authors; not all affect my decision)
- Clarify the VAE connection: There appears to be an error around Eq. 10; the normalizing constant (partition function) should depend on gs(y; θ). If so, the proposed objective is not equivalent to a VAE under non-Euclidean/perceptual metrics. Please correct or explain the equation and its implications.
- Objective choice and evaluation: Please explain why you optimized MSE rather than a perceptual metric as in your previous work. Given your background, it is surprising that even the evaluation emphasized PSNR; a more thorough perceptual evaluation would strengthen the case.
- Ablations on coding components: Please quantify the contribution of adaptive entropy coding versus that of the deeper analysis/synthesis transforms. Showing performance without adaptation (as in the previous paper) would isolate the gains attributable to the entropy coder and make the overall impact clearer.
- Entropy coder details: Provide more detail on the adaptive coder’s design and its effect on bit rates across settings. This will make the system’s engineering contributions more transparent and reproducible.

Questions for the authors
- Could you clarify the apparent issue around Eq. 10 and the dependence of the partition function on gs(y; θ)? If this dependence holds, does it invalidate the claimed equivalence to VAEs under non-Euclidean metrics?
- What motivated the choice to optimize MSE instead of a perceptual objective, and why focus evaluation on PSNR rather than a perceptual metric as in your earlier work?
- How much of the observed performance gain is due to adaptive entropy coding versus the deeper encoder/decoder? Can you report results without adaptation to isolate its contribution?
