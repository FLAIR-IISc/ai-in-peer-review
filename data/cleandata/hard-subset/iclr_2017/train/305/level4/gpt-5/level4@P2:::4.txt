Summary
The paper proposes an end-to-end optimized image compression framework based on learned nonlinear analysis/synthesis transforms, a uniform scalar quantizer, and entropy coding. The transforms are realized as three-stage convolutional networks with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearities and a corresponding approximate inverse (IGDN). To enable gradient-based training despite quantization, the authors introduce a continuous relaxation that replaces rounding with additive uniform noise and optimize a rate–distortion objective R + λD across a large image dataset. They connect the relaxed objective to variational autoencoders while emphasizing key differences relevant to lossy compression. The method is evaluated with an actual entropy coder (CABAC-like), reporting real bit rates. Experiments on standard test images show improved rate–distortion performance versus JPEG/JPEG2000 (especially at lower rates) and substantial, consistent perceptual gains (MS-SSIM), with noticeably fewer blocking/ringing artifacts.

Decision: Accept
Reasons:
- Well-motivated, principled end-to-end approach that directly targets rate–distortion, with a clever and justified relaxation of quantization.
- Strong and comprehensive empirical evidence, including actual bitstreams, that demonstrates both quantitative and perceptual advantages over established methods.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and approach: The paper answers a clear question—can a learned nonlinear transform coding system, optimized end-to-end for R + λD, outperform hand-designed linear transform coders? The use of GDN/IGDN is well placed in the literature and motivated by known statistical structure of natural images.
- Methodological rigor: The continuous relaxation via additive uniform noise is standard and appropriate; the authors verify empirically that the relaxed rate and distortion track the discrete quantities closely. The connection to VAEs is insightful and clarifies why optimizing for compression differs from standard generative modeling.
- Evaluation: The authors implement an actual entropy coder, include side information in the bitstream, and compare both PSNR and MS-SSIM on a widely used benchmark (Kodak), with both grayscale and RGB results. Visual comparisons substantiate the claim of reduced artifacts. The appendix details architecture, optimization, entropy coding, and evaluation choices, improving reproducibility.
- Scope: Results cover multiple λ values (separate models per rate point), and the analysis examines the bias/variance of the relaxed objective, the impact on visual quality, and comparisons across a range of images.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not critical to the decision)
- Comparisons: If feasible, include comparisons to a strong modern baseline such as BPG (HEVC intra) to further contextualize performance.
- Ablations: Expand ablations on GDN vs. common pointwise activations (ReLU/sigmoid), depth/capacity, and the importance of overcompleteness to separate architectural from nonlinearity benefits.
- Single-model rate control: Discuss mechanisms to support multiple rate points with a single model (e.g., learned rate knobs or conditional parameters), and the trade-offs versus per-λ training.
- Entropy modeling: The current coder uses relatively simple context modeling (raster scan, per-channel marginals). Analyze potential gains from stronger spatial/context models and any gap relative to JPEG2000’s entropy coding.
- Generalization: Add results on alternative datasets (e.g., BSDS, other natural image sets) to test robustness beyond Kodak.
- Practicality: Report model size, encoding/decoding speed on CPU/GPU, and memory requirements to inform deployability.
- Failure modes: Highlight cases where PSNR lags JPEG2000 and analyze what image content causes this; discuss whether perceptual metrics should be used at training time to close the gap.
- Clarity: The VAE connection is useful—consider tightening that section with a concise derivation and a brief remark on when gp and non-Euclidean d yield a valid likelihood. Minor notation streamlining would also help readability.

Questions for the authors
- Rate flexibility: Can you adapt a single trained model to multiple λ values (e.g., via scale parameters or conditional normalization), or is per-λ training essential for optimality?
- Relaxation sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the uniform noise width and to the assumption of i.i.d. noise? Did you try alternative relaxations or straight-through estimators?
- Entropy modeling: What improvement do you expect from stronger context/adaptive models (e.g., spatial neighbors, autoregressive priors) during coding? Any preliminary results?
- Architecture: How much of the perceptual gain stems from GDN specifically versus depth/overcompleteness? Could fewer GDN stages match performance with more conventional activations?
- Practical metrics: What are the encoder/decoder runtimes and model sizes for the reported configurations? Any insights on optimization for embedded deployment?
- Color handling: How are color channels treated relative to luminance? Did you evaluate chroma subsampling variants or alternative color spaces?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code/models and the trained entropy models to facilitate comparison and adoption?

Overall, this is a strong, well-executed piece of work that advances learned compression with a principled formulation and convincing results. I recommend acceptance.
