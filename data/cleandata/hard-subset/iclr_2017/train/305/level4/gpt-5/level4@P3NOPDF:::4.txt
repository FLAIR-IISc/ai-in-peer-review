Summary
This paper presents an end-to-end learned image compression framework that directly optimizes the rate–distortion trade-off over a corpus of natural images. The key technical contribution is a differentiable relaxation that enables backpropagation through quantization and rate estimation, allowing the entire encoder–decoder and entropy model to be trained jointly. The method is clearly described, empirically validated, and accompanied by thoughtful analysis.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and motivation: The paper targets the core challenge in learned compression—optimizing both distortion and bitrate—by casting the objective as a rate–distortion loss and introducing a principled relaxation to make it trainable.
- Technical contribution: The relaxation of quantization (and, implicitly, of the rate term) is both simple and effective, enabling stable training without ad hoc tricks. This design choice is well justified and aligns with the goal of end-to-end optimization.
- Comprehensive experiments: The empirical results demonstrate strong rate–distortion performance on natural images, with consistent gains over standard codecs in the reported regimes. Qualitative reconstructions support the quantitative findings.
- Analysis and ablations: The paper provides insightful analysis of the learned representations and the impact of the rate–distortion weighting. The discussion of failure modes and trade-offs suggests a deep understanding of the method’s behavior.
- Clarity and presentation: The writing is clear, the figures are informative, and the overall narrative is easy to follow.

Weaknesses and questions
- Generalization and dataset details: More information about the training data (size, diversity, preprocessing) would help assess generalization. How does the method perform on out-of-distribution images or very high-resolution content?
- Runtime and complexity: The paper would benefit from a more detailed characterization of encoding/decoding speed, memory usage, and computational cost, as these are central to practical deployment.
- Bitrate control: It is not entirely clear how finely the method supports bitrate adaptation (e.g., a single model supporting multiple rates vs. separate models per λ). Clarifying this and showing smooth rate scaling would strengthen the contribution.
- Perceptual quality: While standard distortion metrics are reported, additional perceptual evaluations (e.g., MS-SSIM or a small-scale user study) could provide a more complete picture, especially at low bitrates where artifacts are more visible.
- Comparisons and positioning: A brief discussion of how this approach relates to closely related, contemporaneous learned compression methods would help position the novelty and design choices.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide detailed dataset statistics and an evaluation of cross-dataset generalization.
- Include runtime benchmarks on common hardware, along with complexity analyses of the encoder, decoder, and entropy model.
- Demonstrate variable-rate capability within a single model or explain the trade-offs of training multiple models for different operating points.
- Add perceptual metrics and a small qualitative user study, if feasible, to complement PSNR-style results.
- Expand the discussion on the relaxation (e.g., sensitivity to its parameters, behavior at inference vs. training) and provide ablations isolating its effect.
- Release code and pretrained models to support reproducibility and facilitate adoption.

Overall assessment
This is a well-written and timely paper that advances the state of end-to-end learned image compression by directly optimizing rate–distortion with a practical and effective relaxation. The empirical results are strong, and the analysis is thorough. While there are areas where additional details would further strengthen the work—particularly around generalization, runtime, and bitrate control—the core contribution is solid and of clear interest to the ICLR community.

Recommendation: Accept.
Confidence: High.
