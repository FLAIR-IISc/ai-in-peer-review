Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a complete, end-to-end image compression system built from cascades of convolutional filters and generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearities, followed by uniform quantization and a learned approximate inverse (IGDN). The authors optimize all components jointly for rate–distortion performance using a continuous relaxation of quantization (additive uniform noise), and implement an actual entropy coder to report true bit rates. They clarify the connection to variational autoencoders while emphasizing key differences relevant to compression (operation along the rate–distortion curve, discrete coding). Experiments on Kodak and other images show consistent perceptual improvements over JPEG and JPEG2000, strong gains in MS-SSIM across bit rates, and frequent PSNR gains, especially at low rates. The writing is clear, the formulation is principled, and the empirical evidence is compelling.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) Strong, well-motivated end-to-end formulation grounded in rate–distortion theory, with a practical codec producing real bitstreams.
2) Convincing empirical results and visible artifact reduction relative to JPEG/JPEG2000, supported by MS-SSIM and often PSNR.

Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: learning an analysis/synthesis transform and entropy model that jointly optimize the rate–distortion trade-off for images, avoiding the suboptimal modularity of traditional transform coding.
- Motivation and positioning: The paper situates the approach within transform coding and connects to variational generative models, while highlighting why compression requires operating along a continuum of λ. Compared to prior deep methods, the use of GDN/IGDN, a continuous quantization proxy, and an implemented entropy coder make the contribution substantive and complete.
- Evidence: The continuous relaxation is justified theoretically and validated empirically; the codec uses CABAC to report actual bits; training on ImageNet and testing on Kodak and other images is appropriate; visual comparisons consistently favor the proposed method, with clear reductions in blocking/ringing. Rate–distortion curves show MS-SSIM gains uniformly and PSNR gains on most images, especially at lower rates.
- Rigor: The architecture and optimization details are documented; the relationship to VAEs clarifies assumptions (independence of latents, proxy noise model). While ablations are limited, preliminary comparisons of GDN vs. simpler nonlinearities are discussed and plausible.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not central to my decision)
- Baselines: Please add comparisons to the strongest conventional codecs available at the time, e.g., HEVC intra/BPG and, if feasible, WebP, and include standard test images (Lena, Barbara, Baboon) with both PSNR and MS-SSIM. This will help position the method against best classical approaches, not only JPEG/JPEG2000.
- Fairness of entropy coding: Clarify how your CABAC configuration compares to JPEG2000’s context modeling. If your context model is simpler, discuss how a stronger context might further improve your bit rates.
- Complexity: Report model size, encoding/decoding time, and memory on a standard CPU/GPU, and how these scale with image size and desired rate.
- Ablations: Provide results quantifying the contribution of GDN/IGDN vs. pointwise nonlinearities, number of stages, and capacity (channels). This will help isolate where the gains come from.
- Single vs. multi-λ models: You train separate models per λ. Discuss the trade-offs versus a single conditional model that can sweep rate (e.g., via learned rate control), and whether your approach can be extended to cover a range of rates in one model.
- Generalization and datasets: Consider adding results on an additional benchmark set beyond Kodak to further demonstrate robustness (e.g., Tecnick or BSDS500), especially for RGB.
- Reproducibility: If possible, provide code, trained models, and a bitstream description to enable reproduction and follow-up research.

Questions for the authors
1) Independence assumption: How sensitive is performance to the assumption of independent latents? Have you tried lightweight spatial/context models over ŷ to capture residual dependencies, and what is the rate impact?
2) Relaxation fidelity: You show the proxy is mostly unbiased. Could you report aggregate statistics (mean/variance of rate and distortion bias) over the test set across λ, and how this translates to RD-curve shifts (e.g., BD-rate)?
3) Entropy coding: What side information is transmitted (exactly) and at what overhead per image? How much gain do you obtain from adaptive modeling during decoding versus a fixed model?
4) Rate control: Given a target bit rate, how reliably can you choose λ to meet it? Have you explored per-image λ selection or post-quantization adjustment to hit rate targets?
5) Color handling: For RGB, do you operate directly in RGB or in a learned color space? How do your choices compare to codecs that use chroma subsampling (e.g., JPEG 4:2:0) in terms of perceptual quality and bit allocation?
6) Complexity: What are typical encode/decode times for a 512×512 image on CPU and GPU? Could you comment on the feasibility of real-time or embedded deployment?
7) Comparative scope: Beyond JPEG/JPEG2000, can you add results for HEVC intra/BPG (and optionally WebP) to better position your approach versus the best classical methods?

Overall, this is the most convincing deep-learning-based compression paper I have seen to date: principled, carefully executed, and with clear qualitative and quantitative gains. Adding stronger baselines, complexity metrics, and a few ablations would make it even stronger.
