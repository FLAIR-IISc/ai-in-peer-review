Summary and contributions
The paper presents an end-to-end trainable image compression system built from cascaded convolutional analysis/synthesis transforms with generalized divisive normalization (GDN/IGDN), a uniform scalar quantizer, and a learned entropy model. The key technical contribution is a continuous, differentiable relaxation of the discrete rate–distortion objective: quantization is replaced with additive uniform noise, and the code distribution is modeled with nonparametric, factorized marginals, enabling stochastic gradient descent. The authors draw a clear connection to variational autoencoders while highlighting the differences required for operational rate–distortion optimization. They train separate models for multiple λ values to cover the rate–distortion curve, implement an arithmetic entropy coder (CABAC-style), and report actual bitrates. Empirically, the method generally outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 in PSNR at lower rates and shows consistent, large gains in MS-SSIM and visual quality across a wide range of images.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
1) Strong, well-motivated end-to-end formulation that tackles the core difficulty of differentiating through quantization via a principled and effective relaxation.
2) Compelling empirical evidence: improved rate–distortion (especially at low rates), markedly better perceptual quality, and evaluation with an operational entropy coder reporting real bits.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses classical transform coding’s modular design (separate transform, quantization, entropy coding) by jointly optimizing a nonlinear transform and entropy model for the rate–distortion trade-off. This is a timely and well-motivated application of deep learning to a long-standing problem where nonlinearity and end-to-end training can plausibly yield large gains.
- Literature placement: The work is well positioned relative to transform coding, VAEs, and contemporary learned compression. The relationship to VAEs is carefully articulated; the distinctions (discrete coding, operating across the RD curve, and using actual bitrates) are important and clearly explained.
- Support for claims: The continuous relaxation is validated empirically, showing small distortion bias and diminishing rate bias at finer quantization. The inclusion of an implemented entropy coder and reporting of real bitrates enhances rigor and comparability. Visual examples align with MS-SSIM gains and the claimed reduction of ringing/blocking artifacts common to linear transforms. Architectural details and training choices are described thoroughly in the appendix.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not central to the accept decision)
- Baselines: While the comparisons to JPEG and JPEG 2000 are standard, adding a modern intra-coding baseline (e.g., BPG/HEVC intra) would strengthen the case, even if only at a subset of operating points.
- One-model-for-all-rates: Training one model per λ is clean and effective; however, exploring a single conditional model that accepts λ (or a learned rate-control signal) at test time could improve practicality.
- Entropy modeling: The factorized marginal model and simple raster-scan CABAC limit spatial/contextual exploitation. Stronger context models (e.g., autoregressive or hyperprior variants) could further reduce rate and narrow any gap with mature codecs’ entropy models.
- Ablations: The paper mentions preliminary results with ReLU-like nonlinearities; a compact ablation comparing GDN/IGDN vs pointwise activations at matched parameter budgets would clarify how much gain comes from the joint normalization.
- Evaluation breadth: PSNR and MS-SSIM are appropriate; reporting on other perceptual metrics or conducting a small-scale user study could substantiate the perceptual claims further.
- Reproducibility: If feasible, releasing trained models and the entropy coding implementation would facilitate follow-up work and fair comparisons.

Notes on the authors’ response to my earlier question
I previously argued that, under fixed-range quantization, a sufficiently expressive network could learn to produce codes that saturate the entropy bound. While this may be true in principle, I find the authors’ counterpoint persuasive: forcing the network to implicitly subsume the role of a variable-rate entropy model is likely to make learning harder and require unnecessary capacity. Decoupling transform learning from entropy coding via an explicit, trainable rate proxy is a cleaner and more efficient design.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the number of stages, channel counts, and filter sizes? Are there simple heuristics for setting capacity vs. λ?
- Could a single model be conditioned on λ (or a learned scale parameter) to cover the entire RD curve without retraining? If attempted, how did it compare?
- How robust is the learned factorized marginal model? Have you measured the rate loss from the independence assumption relative to a simple spatial context model?
- Beyond JPEG/JPEG 2000, have you evaluated against more recent baselines (e.g., BPG) to contextualize the improvement?
- What are the encoding/decoding runtimes and memory usage relative to JPEG 2000 on CPU/GPU?
- For RGB, do you operate in RGB space or a color transform (e.g., YCbCr)? Any color artifacts observed at low bitrates?
- CABAC setup: How many contexts are used per channel, and how much adaptation contributes to the final bitrates?
- MS-SSIM is computed on luma; does the perceptual advantage persist when evaluating on full-color perceptual metrics?

Overall, this is a clear, well-executed paper with a meaningful technical contribution and strong empirical results. I recommend acceptance.
