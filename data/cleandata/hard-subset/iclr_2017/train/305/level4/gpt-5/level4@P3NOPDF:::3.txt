This paper presents an end-to-end trained image compression and decompression system that achieves a better rate–distortion trade-off than established codecs such as JPEG 2000. Beyond demonstrating the efficacy of deep learning for image compression, a notable contribution is the introduction of a differentiable surrogate for the rate term, enabling direct optimization across different rate–distortion operating points. This differentiable rate modeling is likely to have impact beyond compression, as similar surrogates could be valuable in other settings where discrete or non-differentiable objectives impede gradient-based learning.

Strengths:
- Clear problem formulation and a principled approach to optimizing rate–distortion objectives within a neural framework.
- Introduction of a differentiable “rate” function that allows end-to-end training without resorting to brittle approximations or REINFORCE-style estimators; this is a key technical contribution.
- Empirical results that demonstrate consistent improvements over a strong classical baseline (e.g., JPEG 2000) across bitrates, suggesting the approach is robust rather than tuned to a single operating point.
- The methodology appears general and potentially applicable to related tasks that require differentiable proxies for discrete coding costs.

The authors also provided a thoughtful and convincing response to my pre-review question. My initial position was that, when minimizing distortion subject to a fixed range and quantization, a sufficiently expressive network could learn to produce codes with maximal entropy within the admissible range—approaching the information-theoretic upper bound—thereby obviating an explicit rate model. While this remains plausible in principle, the authors’ counterargument is persuasive: forcing the network to implicitly realize a high-entropy, variable-rate representation imposes a specific and complex structure on the learned codes and their usage. In practice, matching the effectiveness of the current system would likely require the network to internalize the computations performed by a dedicated variable-rate encoder used to store the side information (q). This would increase model complexity and training difficulty, and could reduce modularity and controllability of the rate–distortion trade-off. By contrast, the proposed differentiable rate formulation and explicit handling of side information provide a cleaner, more flexible mechanism to target different operating points without entangling them with the reconstruction network’s capacity.

Suggestions and questions for further strengthening the work:
- Provide additional analysis on the fidelity of the differentiable rate surrogate relative to true entropy or actual bit counts, including calibration plots across operating points. This would clarify any bias introduced by the surrogate and its practical significance.
- Discuss the sensitivity of performance to the choice of the rate–distortion trade-off parameter(s), and whether a single trained model can be adapted post hoc across bitrates or whether separate training is required.
- Report computational costs (training time, inference latency) and memory footprint, especially relative to classical codecs and recent neural baselines, to contextualize deployment feasibility.
- Elaborate on the role and overhead of the side information q: what is its dimensionality, how is it encoded, and how does its bitrate scale with image content? An ablation measuring gains attributable to q would help isolate its contribution.
- If possible, include qualitative examples that highlight failure modes or domains where the method underperforms, to delineate the current limits of the approach.

Overall, this is a strong and timely paper. The idea of making the rate term differentiable for end-to-end optimization is both elegant and practical, and the empirical results substantiate the claim that learned codecs can surpass long-standing engineered baselines. The authors’ rebuttal addresses my main conceptual concern by clarifying the benefits of explicit rate modeling and side-information handling, which improves controllability and reduces the burden on the reconstruction network. I expect this work to be influential not only in neural image compression but also in adjacent areas that require differentiable approximations to discrete or non-differentiable objectives. I recommend acceptance.
