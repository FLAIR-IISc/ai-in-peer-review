This is the most convincing paper on deep neural network–based image compression I have read so far. The manuscript is clearly written, and the integration of rate–distortion theory into the training objective is natural and well justified. The choice of JPEG2000 as a baseline—rather than only comparing to JPEG—is appropriate and substantially strengthens the evaluation. I expect this work to have strong impact.

Strengths
- Clear formulation: The rate–distortion objective is woven seamlessly into the framework and motivates the architectural choices.
- Solid baseline: Using JPEG2000 as the primary comparator is reasonable and more rigorous than the common JPEG-only baseline.
- Presentation: The paper is well structured and easy to follow.

Areas for improvement and requested additions
- Benchmark images: Please include results on the standard classical test images (Lena, Barbara, and Baboon). These remain widely used in the traditional compression literature and will facilitate direct comparison.
- Comparisons to classical state of the art: In addition to JPEG2000, include references and, where possible, empirical comparisons to strong classical methods (as noted in my questions). It is important to situate the proposed neural approach with respect to the best prior non-neural methods, not just legacy codecs.
- Positioning and takeaways: The paper would benefit from a concise, evidence-backed statement of how neural methods compare to classical codecs across bit-rate regimes and content types (e.g., textured regions like Barbara, high-frequency content like Baboon). From the current submission, it remains unclear how the two categories are positioned relative to each other.
- Reporting: For any added experiments, provide rate–distortion curves (e.g., PSNR and/or MS-SSIM vs. bits per pixel) and a few representative visual comparisons to support qualitative claims, ensuring identical test sets and fair evaluation protocols.

Overall assessment
This is a strong submission with a principled objective, thoughtful design, and an evaluation that already exceeds the common baseline. With the requested additions—standard classical benchmarks, references to state-of-the-art classical methods, and a clear positioning statement—the paper would offer a more complete and compelling picture of where neural image compression stands relative to the best traditional approaches. I am positive about the contribution and its potential impact.
