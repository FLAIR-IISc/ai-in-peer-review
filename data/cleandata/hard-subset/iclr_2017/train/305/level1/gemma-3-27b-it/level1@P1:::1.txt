This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression method based on cascaded nonlinear transformations inspired by biological visual systems. The core idea is to jointly optimize the analysis and synthesis transforms, along with a uniform quantizer, for rate-distortion performance, employing a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization. The authors demonstrate, through extensive experiments, that their approach achieves competitive and often superior rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, with a particularly noticeable improvement in perceptual quality as measured by MS-SSIM.

**Decision: Accept**

The primary reason for acceptance is the compelling empirical results demonstrating significant improvements in visual quality over established compression standards. While the rate-distortion performance is comparable, the subjective improvement in image fidelity is a substantial contribution. Second, the theoretical grounding linking this approach to variational autoencoders provides a valuable perspective and strengthens the paper's foundations.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of traditional transform coding methods and positioning their approach as a solution leveraging recent advances in deep learning and neuroscience. The connection to biological visual systems, specifically the use of generalized divisive normalization (GDN), provides a strong rationale for the chosen architecture. The authors convincingly argue that linear transforms are insufficient for capturing the complex dependencies in natural images, and that GDN offers a more effective way to represent image data.

The experimental evaluation is thorough. The authors compare their method against JPEG and JPEG 2000 on a standard dataset (Kodak) and a collection of their own images. Crucially, they evaluate performance using both MSE and MS-SSIM, providing a more comprehensive assessment of compression quality. The detailed ablation studies and parameter tuning discussion in the appendix, while lengthy, bolster the credibility of the results. The inclusion of a functioning implementation and publicly available compressed images is a significant strength, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

The theoretical link to variational autoencoders is insightful, providing a framework for understanding the optimization process and highlighting the similarities and differences between the two approaches. The discussion of the limitations and nuances of this connection is particularly commendable.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarity of Loss Function Relaxation:** While the explanation of the continuous relaxation of the quantization step is generally clear, it could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of *why* replacing the quantizer with additive noise approximates the discrete problem well. Perhaps a visual illustration showing the comparison of the probability distributions would be helpful.
*   **Computational Cost:** The paper mentions the high computational cost of training. Providing some specific numbers on training time and resources would be useful for readers considering implementing this method. A discussion of potential strategies for reducing the computational burden would also be valuable.
*   **Generalization to Video Compression:** The paper focuses solely on image compression. Briefly discussing the potential challenges and opportunities for extending this approach to video compression would be insightful.
*   **Choice of GDN:** The authors highlight the efficiency of GDN, but it would be valuable to see a more direct comparison with other common activation functions (e.g., ReLU, sigmoid) in a controlled experiment. This would further strengthen the argument for using GDN.
*   **CABAC Implementation Details:** While the authors describe their CABAC implementation, more details about the context modeling and entropy coding strategies would be helpful for readers interested in replicating their results.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The continuous relaxation of the quantization step seems crucial to the success of the method. How sensitive is the performance to the width of the additive noise (i.e., the quantization bin size)? Have you explored different noise distributions beyond uniform noise?
2.  You mention that the RGB transforms have an increased number of channels for higher bit rates. What is the specific rule used to determine the number of channels as a function of the bit rate?
3.  The paper makes a strong case for the visual superiority of the proposed method. Are there any specific types of images or image features where the method performs particularly well or poorly compared to JPEG/JPEG 2000?
4.  Regarding computational cost, what is the approximate training time on a typical GPU for a given lambda value? And how does the training time scale with the size of the training dataset?
5.  The appendix mentions adapting the range of the spline approximation for the marginals. Could you elaborate on the heuristic used to determine the new range, and how often this adaptation occurs during training?
