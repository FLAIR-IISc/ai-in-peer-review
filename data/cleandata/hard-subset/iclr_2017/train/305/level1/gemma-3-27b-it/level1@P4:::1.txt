This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression method based on cascaded nonlinear transforms, inspired by biological neuron models. The approach jointly optimizes analysis and synthesis transforms for rate-distortion performance, using a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization. The core contribution lies in the design of these transforms using Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) and demonstrating improved rate-distortion performance, particularly visual quality, compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000.

**Reject.** While the paper demonstrates promising results and a technically sound approach, the novelty is incremental and the empirical evaluation, despite its breadth, lacks sufficient statistical rigor and fails to provide a compelling, consistently superior advantage over existing methods.

The primary reason for rejection is the insufficient evidence of a *consistent* and *significant* improvement over established compression standards. While the authors report improved visual quality and rate-distortion performance on the Kodak dataset, the text itself acknowledges variability in performance across different images (see discussion in Section 4). The statement that performance is "better for most (but not all) test images" is not strong enough to justify publication in a competitive conference like ICLR. The extensive online material with individual image comparisons is appreciated, but a more systematic, quantitative analysis (e.g., average improvements with confidence intervals) is needed. The claims of substantial visual improvements are supported by MS-SSIM, but the connection between MS-SSIM scores and perceived visual quality isn’t fully established, and simply stating improvements without rigorous statistical backing weakens the claim. The discussion on the difficulties of summarizing performance across images is valid, but doesn't excuse the lack of a stronger, statistically supported conclusion. 

A secondary, though less critical, reason for rejection is the somewhat verbose presentation. The paper delves into considerable background and related work (e.g., variational autoencoders, biological neuron models) that, while relevant, could be streamlined to focus more concisely on the core contribution. The connection to variational autoencoders, while interesting, feels somewhat tangential to the main goal of compression.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Statistical Significance:** The authors should perform statistical tests to demonstrate the significance of the observed improvements in rate-distortion and MS-SSIM scores. Simply stating that "performance is better" is insufficient.
*   **Ablation Studies:** It would be beneficial to include ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the proposed method, such as the GDN nonlinearity and the cascaded structure. How much performance is lost by using ReLU activations instead of GDN?
*   **Computational Complexity:** The computational cost of training and encoding/decoding should be discussed in more detail. While the authors mention efficiency during decoding, the training cost is likely substantial, and this should be acknowledged.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** A comparison with more recent image compression methods beyond JPEG and JPEG 2000 (e.g., BPG, WebP) would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Loss Function Relaxation:** The impact of the continuous relaxation of the quantization step on the optimization process deserves further investigation. How sensitive is the performance to the width of the uniform noise distribution?
*   **Clarification on λ:** The role of λ and its selection process is not entirely clear. Is there a principled way to choose an appropriate value of λ for a given application?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide statistical analysis (e.g., p-values, confidence intervals) to support the claim of improved rate-distortion performance and MS-SSIM scores?
2.  How does the performance of the proposed method change when using alternative nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU, sigmoid) in place of GDN?
3.  What is the computational cost (training and inference time) of the proposed method compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000?
4.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the noise distribution’s width used for the loss function relaxation?
5.  Can you elaborate on a principled method for selecting an appropriate value of λ for a specific compression application?
