This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression method utilizing cascaded convolutional linear filters and nonlinear activations, inspired by biological neurons. The key contribution lies in jointly optimizing the analysis and synthesis transforms for rate-distortion performance, using a continuous relaxation of the quantization step for gradient-based optimization. The results demonstrate competitive performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, with notably improved visual quality, particularly at lower bit rates.

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting approach and shows promising results, the lack of a clear and compelling advantage over existing methods, coupled with some ambiguity in the experimental setup and evaluation, leads me to recommend rejection. Specifically, the improvements in MS-SSIM, while statistically significant, are not dramatically large enough to justify the complexity of the proposed method, given the computational cost of training. 

The motivation for the approach is strong. The authors correctly identify the limitations of traditional transform coding methods and the difficulties in joint optimization. Their choice of Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) is well-justified, building upon their previous work demonstrating its effectiveness in Gaussianizing image densities. The connection to variational autoencoders is insightful, providing a theoretical grounding for the continuous relaxation of the quantization step. The use of stochastic gradient descent for end-to-end optimization is a logical approach, and the continuous proxy for the discontinuous loss function is a clever solution to enable gradient flow. 

However, the support for the claims isn't entirely convincing. The paper states "dramatic improvement in visual quality," but this is largely supported by subjective examples. While the examples are compelling, a more rigorous perceptual study (e.g., user preference tests) would strengthen this claim. The MS-SSIM improvements, while positive, are often marginal and don’t consistently demonstrate a substantial improvement across all images.  The comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 isn't entirely fair. The authors acknowledge the potential for CABAC to offer further gains, but also note it likely falls short of JPEG 2000’s entropy coding. The choice of evaluation metric, specifically the combination of MSE and entropy, is not thoroughly explained. The rationale behind the specific network architecture (number of filters, downsampling factors) is also somewhat ad-hoc. The extensive appendix attempts to address implementation details, but the complexity itself raises concerns about reproducibility and practical deployment.  The claim of better rate-distortion performance needs quantification for *most* images, but the discussion admits that performance varies across images.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Study:** It would be beneficial to include an ablation study to assess the contribution of different components of the model, such as the GDN nonlinearity or the cascaded structure. How much performance is gained by moving from pointwise nonlinearities to GDN, for example?
*   **Computational Cost:** The paper doesn’t adequately address the computational cost of training and encoding/decoding. A comparison of training time, encoding speed, and decoding speed with JPEG/JPEG 2000 would be valuable.
*   **Perceptual Evaluation:** Conduct a formal perceptual study with human subjects to assess the visual quality of the compressed images. This would provide stronger evidence for the claimed “dramatic improvement.”
*   **Entropy Coding Details:** Provide a more detailed explanation of the entropy coding scheme, including the context modeling and adaptation strategies.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Explore the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters, such as the trade-off parameter λ and the learning rate.
*   **Generalization:** Discuss the generalization ability of the model to images outside the training set. How does performance degrade with images from different domains?
*   **Reproducibility:** Release the code and trained models to facilitate reproducibility and further research.

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  What is the approximate training time required for a single model (given λ) on a specific hardware configuration?
2.  What is the computational complexity of the encoding and decoding stages compared to JPEG/JPEG 2000?
3.  Can you provide a statistical analysis of the MS-SSIM improvements across the test images, including p-values and effect sizes?
4.  How was the value of the hyperparameter λ chosen? Was any form of hyperparameter optimization performed?
5.  Given the observation that performance varies significantly across images, could the model be adapted or personalized for individual images or image classes to improve compression efficiency?
