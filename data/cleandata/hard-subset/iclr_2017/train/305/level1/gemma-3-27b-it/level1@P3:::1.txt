This paper presents a novel end-to-end optimized image compression method utilizing cascaded convolutional linear filters and nonlinear activations, inspired by biological neurons. The key innovation lies in jointly optimizing the analysis and synthesis transforms for rate-distortion performance using stochastic gradient descent, cleverly addressing the non-differentiability of the quantization step with a continuous relaxation. The results demonstrate superior rate-distortion performance and, more importantly, improved visual quality compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000. This is a significant contribution to the field, bridging the gap between learned representations and practical image compression.

**Reject** – While the paper demonstrates promising results and a compelling approach, the experimental validation lacks sufficient rigor and clarity, particularly concerning the entropy coding and the comparison with established methods. The reliance on a single implementation of CABAC and the somewhat hand-wavy discussion of its limitations raise concerns about the fairness and reproducibility of the results.

The core idea of end-to-end optimization of the entire compression pipeline, including the nonlinear transforms, is well-motivated. The authors correctly identify the limitations of traditional transform coding methods that rely on separately optimized components and highlight the potential benefits of learning a joint representation optimized for both rate and distortion. The connection to variational autoencoders is insightful, providing a theoretical grounding for the approach and clarifying the role of the relaxed loss function. The use of generalized divisive normalization (GDN), inspired by biological models, is also a strong choice, given its demonstrated ability to Gaussianize image densities.

However, the paper’s support for its claims, particularly regarding the performance improvement over JPEG and JPEG 2000, feels incomplete. The description of the entropy coding implementation is somewhat superficial. While the authors acknowledge the potential for improvement with more advanced techniques (like those found within JPEG 2000 itself), the comparison is performed using a single CABAC implementation. This raises questions about whether the observed improvements are due to the learned transforms or simply a more efficient entropy coder. Furthermore, the multiple caveats surrounding the comparison (e.g., specific JPEG 2000 settings, chroma subsampling) and the lack of statistical significance testing for the rate-distortion curves weaken the conclusions. The qualitative comparisons, while visually compelling, are subjective and lack quantitative backing beyond MS-SSIM, which, while useful, isn't a perfect measure of perceptual quality. The extensive appendix, while detailed, exacerbates the issue of needing to sift through a large amount of information to assess the validity of the claims.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Entropy Coding Details:** Provide a more detailed explanation of the CABAC implementation, including the context modeling used, and justify the choice of this specific implementation. A comparison with other readily available entropy coding libraries (e.g., those integrated into JPEG 2000) would strengthen the results.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Perform statistical significance testing on the rate-distortion curves to determine whether the observed improvements are statistically significant.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to isolate the contribution of different components of the model (e.g., GDN nonlinearity, cascaded structure) to the overall performance.
*   **Computational Cost:** Provide a more thorough discussion of the computational cost of training and encoding/decoding, especially in comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000.
*   **Robustness to Hyperparameters:** Investigate the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, number of filters) and provide guidelines for choosing appropriate values.
*   **Loss Function Analysis:** A more in-depth analysis of the continuous relaxation of the quantization step would be beneficial. What are the limitations of this approximation, and how does it affect the optimization process?
*   **Clarify λ’s Role:** Expand on the discussion of λ and its interpretation. Is it truly a trade-off parameter, or does it have a more nuanced effect on the optimization process?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the entropy coding, what efforts were made to optimize the CABAC implementation specifically for the output of the learned transforms? Were any parameters of the CABAC encoder tuned?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the learning rate and the number of filters in the convolutional layers?
3.  Can you provide a breakdown of the computational cost of training the model and encoding/decoding images, compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000?
4.  The paper mentions a potential connection to adversarial examples. Has any investigation been conducted into the robustness of the learned transforms to small perturbations in the input images?
5.  You state the relaxed distortion term is mostly unbiased. Can you provide more details on how this was verified? Was this done independently of the test dataset used for performance evaluation?
