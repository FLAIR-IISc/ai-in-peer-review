### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel framework for lossy image compression based on a non-linear autoencoder architecture, which is optimized end-to-end for the rate-distortion trade-off. The authors make several key contributions. First, they design an encoder and decoder (analysis and synthesis transforms) using a cascade of convolutional layers and a biologically-inspired non-linearity, Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN), which has been shown to be effective for density modeling of natural images. Second, to overcome the challenge of optimizing through the non-differentiable quantization step, they propose an elegant continuous relaxation by replacing the quantizer with additive uniform noise during training. This allows the entire model to be trained with stochastic gradient descent. Third, the paper provides an insightful connection between this relaxed rate-distortion loss and the objective function of variational autoencoders (VAEs), while carefully explaining the fundamental differences between the goals of compression and generative modeling. Finally, the authors demonstrate the practicality of their approach by implementing a full entropy coding scheme and showing that their method outperforms standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000 on the Kodak dataset. The most significant claim is a dramatic improvement in perceptual quality at all bit rates, which is supported by both visual examples and quantitative evaluation using the MS-SSIM metric.

### **Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are:

1.  **Novel and Effective End-to-End Framework:** The paper successfully formulates and solves the end-to-end optimization problem for image compression in the presence of quantization. The continuous relaxation technique is a key contribution that enables the training of deep, non-linear transforms for this classical problem.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance and Superior Visual Quality:** The proposed method achieves compelling rate-distortion performance, surpassing widely-used standards like JPEG and JPEG 2000. More importantly, the visual quality of the compressed images is substantially better, replacing the typical blocking and ringing artifacts with more perceptually pleasing simplifications of texture and contours.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper is strong across all key reviewing criteria.

**Motivation and Placement:** The problem of lossy image compression is fundamental, and the authors correctly identify the limitation of traditional transform coding methods where components are designed and optimized separately. The motivation for an end-to-end learned approach is therefore very strong. The paper situates its work well, drawing from concepts in transform coding, computational neuroscience (GDN), and modern deep generative models (VAEs). The discussion in Section 3.1, which clarifies the relationship to VAEs, is particularly insightful and highlights the unique requirements of a compression system (i.e., explicitly optimizing the rate-distortion trade-off via λ).

**Technical Soundness and Rigor:** The core technical proposal—using additive noise as a differentiable proxy for quantization—is well-justified. The authors provide an empirical validation in Figure 4, showing that the relaxed loss function is a reasonable and low-variance estimator of the true rate and distortion, which strengthens confidence in the training methodology. Furthermore, the evaluation is rigorous. Rather than relying solely on entropy estimates, the authors implement a full CABAC-based entropy coder and report actual bit rates, enabling a fair and direct comparison with existing standards. This demonstrates the feasibility of the system as a complete compression pipeline.

**Empirical Results:** The results are excellent. The rate-distortion curves in Figure 7 show a clear and consistent advantage over JPEG and JPEG 2000, especially when measured with the perceptual MS-SSIM metric. The visual examples provided (e.g., Figures 5 and 6) are the most compelling evidence. At low bit rates, where traditional codecs exhibit severe artifacts, the proposed method produces images that are much more natural and structurally coherent. The model appears to have learned to preserve important structures like edges while discarding less critical texture, which is a hallmark of a perceptually-aware compression scheme, despite being trained on MSE. This qualitative improvement is a significant achievement.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Computational Complexity:** The paper mentions that encoding and decoding are efficient but does not provide quantitative complexity figures. A comparison of the computational cost (e.g., in multiply-accumulate operations per pixel) against JPEG 2000 would be a valuable addition for assessing the practical viability of the method, especially for resource-constrained applications.
*   **Architecture Ablation:** The network architecture is described as "somewhat ad-hoc." While the chosen architecture is clearly effective, a brief discussion or ablation study on key architectural choices (e.g., the number of stages, the impact of subsampling factors) could provide useful insights into what makes the model successful.
*   **Variable Bit Rate Model:** A practical limitation is that a separate model must be trained for each point on the rate-distortion curve (each λ). This contrasts with systems that use a single model capable of operating at multiple bit rates. While this is outside the scope of the current work, it would be beneficial to acknowledge this limitation in the discussion and perhaps comment on potential future directions to address it.

### **Questions for the Authors**

Your response to these questions would help clarify my understanding and increase my confidence in the assessment.

1.  You make a compelling case for the efficiency of GDN/IGDN nonlinearities compared to simpler ones like ReLUs. Could you provide more quantitative data on this? For a given performance target, how much larger or deeper did a ReLU-based network need to be to match the rate-distortion performance of the GDN-based model?
2.  The approximation of quantization error with signal-independent additive noise is a core part of your training method. This approximation is likely less accurate at very low bit rates where quantization error becomes large and highly signal-dependent. Did you observe any training instabilities or performance issues in the low-rate (high λ) regime that could be attributed to this approximation?
3.  You note that your entropy coder only "crudely exploits spatial adaptation." How much of the performance gain over JPEG 2000 do you attribute to the learned transforms versus the entropy coding stage? Do you believe a more sophisticated entropy coder that models spatial context could yield significant additional gains?
