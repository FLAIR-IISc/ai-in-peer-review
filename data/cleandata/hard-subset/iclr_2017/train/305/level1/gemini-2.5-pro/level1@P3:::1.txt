### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel, end-to-end optimized framework for lossy image compression. The core contribution is a complete compression system, analogous to traditional transform coding, but where the analysis and synthesis transforms are nonlinear and learned jointly using stochastic gradient descent. The architecture consists of a multi-stage convolutional autoencoder that utilizes a specialized, biologically-inspired nonlinearity called Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN). A key technical innovation is the method for handling the non-differentiable quantization step during training: the authors propose a continuous relaxation by replacing quantization with additive uniform noise. This allows for the direct optimization of a rate-distortion objective function. The authors draw an insightful connection between their relaxed loss function and the objective of Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), while carefully delineating the conceptual differences between compression (trading off rate and distortion across a curve) and generative modeling (modeling the data distribution). The method is evaluated rigorously, not just by reporting entropy estimates, but by implementing a full entropy coding scheme (CABAC) to produce actual bitstreams. The results demonstrate that the proposed method is not only competitive with or superior to JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of PSNR, but also yields a dramatic and consistent improvement in perceptual quality, as evidenced by visual examples and MS-SSIM scores.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Effective Framework:** The paper introduces a principled and elegant end-to-end learning framework for a classical and challenging problem. The continuous relaxation of the quantization step is a clever and practical solution that enables gradient-based optimization of the full rate-distortion objective, a significant hurdle for learned compression.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The method produces results that are not just incrementally better, but represent a qualitative leap in compressed image quality. The visual examples are exceptionally compelling, replacing the structured artifacts of linear transform codes (blocking, ringing) with much more natural-looking degradations. This subjective improvement is convincingly corroborated by objective gains in the MS-SSIM perceptual metric across all tested bit rates.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this work lies in its successful formulation of image compression as an end-to-end optimization problem. Traditional methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000 rely on separately designed components (e.g., DCT/wavelets, quantization tables, entropy coders). This paper demonstrates the power of joint optimization, allowing the transforms to adapt specifically to the statistics of the training data, the properties of the quantizer, and the rate-distortion trade-off itself.

The handling of the non-differentiable quantizer is a critical contribution. By substituting quantization with additive uniform noise, the authors create a differentiable proxy for both the rate (via differential entropy) and the distortion. Figure 4 provides convincing evidence that this approximation is effective, enabling the successful training of the deep, nonlinear transforms.

The architectural choices are also well-motivated. The use of Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) is justified by prior work showing its effectiveness in modeling natural image statistics. The authors' claim that this nonlinearity is more efficient than standard choices like ReLUs for this task is plausible and points towards the importance of inductive bias in the network architecture.

Finally, the scientific rigor of the evaluation is commendable. Many papers in related areas report only entropy estimates, which can be a poor proxy for the final bit rate of a practical system. By implementing a full CABAC-based entropy coder and reporting actual compressed file sizes, the authors demonstrate that their system is not just a theoretical model but a complete, feasible compression method. The consistent and significant improvements shown in MS-SSIM (Figure 7) provide strong quantitative backing for the visually obvious improvements, solidifying the paper's claims. The connection drawn to VAEs is also a valuable contribution, bridging the gap between the compression and generative modeling communities and clarifying the distinct goals of each.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

1.  **Computational Complexity:** The paper states that encoding and decoding are "efficient" but provides no quantitative analysis. For a compression method to be practical, its computational cost is a key consideration. It would be beneficial to include encoding/decoding times on a reference platform (CPU/GPU) and perhaps a FLOPs analysis, comparing them to software implementations of JPEG and JPEG 2000.
2.  **Ablation Study on GDN:** While the use of GDN is well-motivated, the claim that it is more efficient than simpler nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU) is presented as "provisional." The paper would be stronger if this claim were substantiated with a direct empirical comparison, for instance, by training a ReLU-based model with a similar parameter count and comparing its rate-distortion performance. This would help isolate the contribution of this specific architectural choice.
3.  **Rate Adaptivity:** The current method requires training a separate, specialized model for each point on the rate-distortion curve (i.e., for each λ). This is highly impractical for deployment. It would be valuable for the authors to discuss this limitation and perhaps speculate on potential avenues for creating a single, rate-adaptive model that can cover a range of quality levels without retraining.
4.  **Entropy Model:** The current entropy model assumes the quantized coefficients are marginally independent. This is a simplification, as spatial correlations almost certainly remain. While the current approach is effective, performance could likely be improved further by incorporating a more sophisticated, context-adaptive entropy model that learns and exploits these dependencies, similar to what is done in modern video codecs.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you provide data on the computational complexity of your encoder and decoder, for example, in terms of processing time for a standard image size, and how it compares to the OpenJPEG implementation you used as a baseline?
2.  In your preliminary experiments comparing GDN to rectified linear units, did you find that ReLUs were unable to achieve the same performance, or that they simply required a deeper/wider network? A little more detail here would clarify the specific advantage of GDN.
3.  The need to train distinct models for each rate-distortion trade-off point is a practical drawback. Have you considered any strategies for developing a single model that can be dynamically adjusted at inference time to produce different bit rates?
4.  Your entropy model relies on an independence assumption. Do you have an intuition for how much performance might be gained by moving to a more powerful, spatially-aware probabilistic model for the latent codes, and do you see a straightforward way to integrate such a model into your end-to-end framework?
