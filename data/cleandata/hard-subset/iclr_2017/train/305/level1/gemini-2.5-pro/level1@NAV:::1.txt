**Summary of Contributions**
This paper presents a complete and well-designed framework for end-to-end learned image compression. The authors propose a deep, non-linear transform architecture composed of convolutional layers and Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN), a nonlinearity inspired by biological vision. A core contribution is the clever use of additive uniform noise as a continuous and differentiable proxy for the discrete quantization step, which enables the entire model to be jointly optimized for a rate-distortion objective via stochastic gradient descent. The paper demonstrates that this approach outperforms standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000, not only on standard distortion metrics like PSNR but, more impressively, by producing visually superior images free from common compression artifacts.

**Recommendation:** Accept

This is an excellent paper that clearly advances the state-of-the-art in image compression. My recommendation is based on two key strengths: (1) the novelty and elegance of the end-to-end optimization framework which successfully tackles the non-differentiability of quantization, and (2) the strength of the empirical results, which show a remarkable and convincing improvement in perceptual quality over established, highly-engineered compression standards.

**Supporting Arguments**
The paper’s methodology is rigorous and its claims are well-supported. The continuous relaxation of the loss function is a principled way to enable gradient-based learning for a rate-distortion objective. The experimental validation is thorough; by implementing a full entropy coder and reporting actual bit rates on a standard dataset, the authors provide a fair and credible comparison to existing methods. The most compelling evidence lies in the visual examples. The proposed method gracefully degrades images by smoothing details while preserving contours, avoiding the blocky or ringing artifacts characteristic of linear transform codes. This subjective improvement is convincingly validated by the objective MS-SSIM metric, which shows a consistent and significant advantage for the proposed method across all tested bit rates. The clear discussion differentiating the approach from variational autoencoders further strengthens the paper.

**Additional Feedback**
The following points are suggestions for improvement and are not part of my decision assessment.
*   **Complexity:** The paper states that encoding and decoding are efficient, but a quantitative comparison of computational complexity (e.g., in operations-per-pixel) against JPEG 2000 would provide a more complete picture of the method’s practicality.
*   **Architecture:** The benefits of the GDN nonlinearity are well-motivated, but the claim that it is more parameter-efficient than standard activations like ReLUs is stated as preliminary. Quantifying this in the main paper would further justify the architectural choice.

I have a few questions for the authors to help clarify my understanding:
*   The current approach requires training and storing a separate model for each point on the rate-distortion curve (i.e., for each λ). Have you considered methods to create a single, rate-adaptive model capable of targeting different bit rates?
*   How sensitive is performance to the non-parametric, piecewise-linear model of the latent coefficient distribution? Could a simpler parametric model achieve comparable results with lower complexity?
