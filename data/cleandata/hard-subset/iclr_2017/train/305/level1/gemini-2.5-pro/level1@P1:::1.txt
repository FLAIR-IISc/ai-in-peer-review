### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel, end-to-end framework for lossy image compression based on deep neural networks. The proposed model consists of three main components: a nonlinear analysis (encoder) transform, a uniform scalar quantizer, and a nonlinear synthesis (decoder) transform. The transforms are built from a cascade of convolutional layers and a specific form of nonlinearity known as Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN), which is inspired by models of biological vision. The central contribution is a method for optimizing the entire system directly for a rate-distortion objective. To overcome the non-differentiability of the quantization step, the authors introduce a clever continuous relaxation, replacing the rounding operation with the addition of uniform noise during training. This allows for end-to-end optimization using standard stochastic gradient descent. The paper provides a valuable theoretical connection between this relaxed objective and the framework of variational autoencoders (VAEs), while carefully highlighting the fundamental differences in their goals. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method by implementing a complete codec, including an arithmetic entropy coder, and show that it outperforms standard codecs like JPEG and JPEG 2000 in terms of rate-distortion performance (PSNR) on the Kodak benchmark dataset. More strikingly, the method produces images with significantly higher perceptual quality, a claim supported by both compelling visual examples and substantial gains in the MS-SSIM metric.

### **Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that I recommend for acceptance. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Novel and Principled Optimization Framework:** The paper introduces a well-motivated and effective solution to the core problem of end-to-end optimization for lossy compression. The use of additive uniform noise as a differentiable proxy for quantization is an elegant idea that enables the application of powerful deep learning optimization techniques to this classical problem. The connection drawn to VAEs provides insightful theoretical context.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The method is not just a theoretical proposal; it is a complete, working system that demonstrates significant improvements over widely used standards. The gains are not only quantitative (in PSNR and MS-SSIM) but also, and perhaps more importantly, qualitative. The visual results are excellent, replacing the familiar blocking and ringing artifacts of linear transform codes with much more natural-looking degradations.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's primary strength is its rigorous, end-to-end approach to a problem traditionally addressed by separately designing components. The continuous relaxation of the rate-distortion loss is the key technical innovation that makes this possible. The authors validate this approximation empirically (Figure 4), showing it to be a reliable proxy for the true discrete rate and distortion, which justifies its use for training.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. Unlike some related work that reports only entropy estimates, the authors implement a full CABAC-based entropy coder and report actual bit rates, making the comparison to JPEG and JPEG 2000 direct and fair. The evaluation on the standard Kodak dataset is appropriate, and the results are compelling. While the PSNR improvements are solid, the improvements in MS-SSIM are dramatic and align with the visual examples provided, which show a clear preference for the proposed method at all bitrates. The artifacts produced by this method—typically a graceful smoothing and simplification of textures and contours—are far less objectionable than the structured artifacts of JPEG and JPEG 2000. This marks a significant step forward in learned image compression.

Finally, the architectural choices are well-motivated. The use of GDN, drawing on prior work in density estimation and its connections to computational neuroscience, appears to be a powerful inductive bias for image data, potentially explaining the model's ability to achieve high performance with a relatively compact three-stage architecture.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions aimed at further strengthening the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Single Model for Multiple Rates:** The current framework requires training a separate, independent model for each point on the rate-distortion curve (i.e., for each `λ`). This is computationally expensive and impractical for deployment, where a user would want to smoothly select any quality level. A valuable addition would be a discussion on potential avenues for creating a single, unified model that can operate across a range of bitrates, perhaps by conditioning the network on the quality parameter `λ`.
*   **Ablation Study on GDN:** The paper suggests that GDN is more parameter-efficient than simpler nonlinearities like ReLUs, but notes this is based on provisional results. While the motivation for GDN is strong, the paper would be even more impactful with a small, controlled experiment directly comparing a GDN-based network to a ReLU-based one with a comparable or larger number of parameters. This would more concretely demonstrate the contribution of this specific architectural choice.
*   **Entropy Model:** The authors use a context model that assumes spatial independence within each channel, adapting only across space for the marginal probabilities. This is a reasonable first step, but modern codecs exploit spatial dependencies much more explicitly. Integrating a more sophisticated, spatially-aware entropy model (e.g., using an autoregressive prior on the latent codes) could potentially yield further significant gains in compression rate.

### **Questions for the Authors**

To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  The appendix clarifies that the non-parametric probability models `pỹi` for the latent codes are fixed after training for a given `λ`. Could you confirm that the parameters for these piecewise linear density models do not need to be transmitted as side information and are considered part of the decoder definition for a given quality level?
2.  What is the computational complexity (e.g., MAC operations per pixel, or actual runtime) of the encoder and decoder relative to a software implementation of JPEG 2000? While training is offline, inference speed is a critical factor for practical usability.
3.  How was the set of `λ` values for training chosen? Does training for a sparse set of `λ`s and then using those models result in a smooth and well-behaved R-D curve, or are there significant gaps?
