**Summary of the paper's contributions**

The paper proposes a novel image compression method based on nonlinear transform coding, which achieves state-of-the-art rate-distortion performance and significantly improves visual quality compared to traditional methods like JPEG and JPEG 2000. The authors develop a framework for end-to-end optimization of the compression model, using a variant of stochastic gradient descent to jointly optimize the analysis and synthesis transforms, as well as the entropy code. The method uses a generalized divisive normalization (GDN) joint nonlinearity, inspired by models of biological neurons, to capture the statistics of natural images. The paper also explores the relationship between the proposed method and variational autoencoders, highlighting the differences between the two approaches.

**Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper due to its significant contributions to the field of image compression and its potential impact on the development of new compression methods. The paper presents a well-motivated approach, grounded in a deep understanding of the underlying principles of image compression and the statistics of natural images. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improved rate-distortion performance and visual quality compared to traditional methods.

**Additional feedback**

1. **Comparison to other methods**: While the paper compares the proposed method to JPEG and JPEG 2000, it would be useful to include comparisons to other state-of-the-art methods, such as the work by Toderici et al. (2016) and Gregor et al. (2016).
2. **Ablation studies**: To better understand the contributions of the different components of the proposed method, it would be useful to include ablation studies that evaluate the performance of the method with and without certain components, such as the GDN joint nonlinearity.
3. **Visual quality metrics**: While the paper uses MS-SSIM to evaluate visual quality, it would be useful to include additional metrics, such as PSNR and SSIM, to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
