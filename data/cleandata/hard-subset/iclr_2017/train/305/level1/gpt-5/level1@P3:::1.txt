Summary and contributions
The paper tackles end-to-end optimization of lossy image compression. It proposes a nonlinear transform coding framework consisting of an analysis transform, uniform scalar quantization, and a synthesis transform. Both transforms are learned as three-stage convolutional pipelines with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearities (and an approximate inverse IGDN). The key technical contribution is a practical, differentiable rate–distortion objective: replace quantization with additive uniform noise to optimize a continuous proxy for discrete entropy and distortion; learn per-channel marginal densities nonparametrically; and train the entire system with SGD for different λ values. The authors show an explicit connection to VAEs under this relaxation. Importantly, they evaluate with an actual entropy coder and report real bitrates. On Kodak and other images, the method generally outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 in PSNR at low–mid rates, and markedly improves perceptual quality (MS-SSIM and visual examples) across all rates.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong, well-motivated end-to-end methodology with an elegant and principled relaxation that enables direct optimization of a full compression system, including an entropy coder. (2) Convincing empirical results in both perceptual quality and rate–distortion, with careful evaluation using actual bitrates.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper squarely targets the classical rate–distortion trade-off for images with a practical codec in mind. The approach is well-placed in the literature, bridging transform coding and modern generative models. The connection to VAEs is insightful and clarifies how the relaxed objective relates to a probabilistic model while emphasizing the discrete requirements of compression.

- Methodological novelty and soundness: The relaxation via additive uniform noise is standard in quantization theory but here is systematized for end-to-end training with explicit modeling of the relaxed marginals. The use of GDN/IGDN is well-motivated by prior work on Gaussianization and offers an efficient, spatially adaptive nonlinearity. The independence assumption in the code space is transparent, and the choice of nonparametric marginals reduces model mismatch. The per-λ training cleanly maps the convex rate–distortion frontier.

- Correctness and rigor: The authors validate the relaxation by comparing relaxed entropy/distortion to actual quantized results, showing small bias (disappearing as quantization gets finer). Using a real entropy coder and reporting actual bit rates avoids pitfalls of comparing differential entropies to bits. Architectural and optimization details are documented.

- Empirical evidence: Rate–distortion curves on Kodak and extensive qualitative results strongly support the claims. The perceptual improvements are consistent with MS-SSIM gains and the expected artifact profiles (fewer ringing/blocking; smoother contours). PSNR is competitive to superior versus JPEG 2000 for most images, especially at low rates.

- Broader impact: The work demonstrates that end-to-end learned compression can surpass classic transforms while retaining a practical encoding pipeline. The VAE link and the relaxation technique are likely to catalyze further research.

Caveats and limitations (do not outweigh strengths)
- Entropy model: The independence assumption and simple CABAC with raster-scan contexts leave rate gains on the table. Stronger context or hierarchical/hyperprior models could improve coding efficiency.

- Single-rate models: Training separate models for each λ complicates deployment and rate control; a unified model conditioned on λ or with progressive coding would be preferable.

- Comparisons: Baselines are JPEG and JPEG 2000. At submission time this is reasonable, but the paper would be stronger with comparisons to more modern intra codecs (e.g., HEVC/BPG) and to Toderici et al.’s recurrent model using actual bitstreams.

- Fairness and evaluation nuances: JPEG is 4:2:0 while the proposed method operates in RGB; PSNR is measured on luma only. Although the authors discuss evaluation choices, these differences can influence results; an ablation using a common color space and sampling would help.

- Ablations: While the discussion notes preliminary tests, the paper lacks systematic ablations isolating the impact of GDN vs. ReLU, number of stages, and the nonparametric density model.

- Complexity: Training cost is high; encoding/decoding efficiency is claimed but runtime/compute footprints are not reported.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not decisive)
- Add ablations: (a) replace GDN with BN+ReLU or PReLU at matched parameter counts; (b) vary number of stages/channels; (c) parametric vs. nonparametric marginals. Report PSNR/MS-SSIM and actual bits.

- Strengthen the entropy model: Even a simple spatial context or a shared hyperprior could demonstrate measurable bitrate reductions; report the gap between sum of marginal entropies and achieved bits.

- Rate control: Discuss mapping from λ to achieved bitrate and variance across images; provide a simple procedure to hit target rates.

- Runtime and memory: Include encoding/decoding speed on CPU/GPU and model size.

- Color handling: Evaluate in a common color space (e.g., YCbCr 4:4:4) to ensure fair PSNR comparisons and analyze chroma artifacts.

- Broaden baselines: If feasible, compare to BPG and to a VAE baseline trained with the same architecture but without the discrete constraints.

Questions for the authors
- How predictable is the achieved bitrate as a function of λ across diverse images? Can you provide a calibration curve and variance, and/or a simple rate control scheme?

- What is the bitrate gap between (i) the sum of learned marginal differential entropies, (ii) the discrete entropies after quantization, and (iii) actual bits from your CABAC coder? How much does on-the-fly adaptation reduce this gap?

- How critical is GDN? At matched network capacity, what RD gap do you observe when substituting ReLU/LeakyReLU with or without batch normalization? Please quantify, not only qualitatively.

- Can the same trained model operate across multiple rates (e.g., via scaling of latents, learned step sizes, or conditioning on λ)? What breaks if you attempt that within your current framework?

- How much side information (e.g., λ index, model parameters) is included per image, and is the model assumed to be shared out-of-band? What is the practical deployment scenario?

- Have you tested on datasets beyond Kodak, and on higher-resolution images? Does the gain persist?

- Does training with MS-SSIM (or a differentiable proxy) further improve perceptual quality without harming PSNR excessively?

Overall, this is a timely, well-executed paper that advances learned image compression with a principled and practical end-to-end formulation. I recommend acceptance.
