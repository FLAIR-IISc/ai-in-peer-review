1. Summary of contributions
The paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression system built from cascaded convolutional transforms with generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearities, a fixed uniform scalar quantizer, and a learned approximate inverse (IGDN). The key technical contribution is a differentiable surrogate for joint rate–distortion optimization: replace quantization with additive i.i.d. uniform noise, use nonparametric marginal models for the latent coefficients, and optimize the sum of estimated rate (via differential entropy of the noise-perturbed latents) and distortion (MSE) with stochastic gradient descent. The approach is related to variational autoencoders under specific assumptions, but is explicitly designed to operate at arbitrary points along the rate–distortion curve via a trade-off parameter λ. The authors implement a practical entropy coder (CABAC-style) to report actual bit rates (not just entropy estimates) and demonstrate improved rate–distortion performance over JPEG and JPEG 2000 on Kodak and other held-out images. Visual quality is consistently improved, especially at low rates, and MS-SSIM gains are substantial. The paper is clearly written, technically careful (including an empirical validation of the noise relaxation), and places the work well relative to transform coding, generative modeling, and contemporary neural compression efforts.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, well-motivated technical contribution: principled, differentiable end-to-end RD optimization for transform coding with a quantization surrogate, coupled with a practical entropy coder and evaluation with actual bits.
- Convincing empirical support: broad qualitative improvements, consistent MS-SSIM gains, and PSNR advantages for most images, with careful validation of the relaxation’s fidelity to the discrete objective.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: The paper tackles the longstanding difficulty of optimizing image compression for rate–distortion in the presence of non-differentiable quantization. The proposed relaxation with additive uniform noise is standard in quantization theory and is implemented carefully. The authors empirically verify that the relaxed objective tracks the true RD behavior well across rates (minimal bias for distortion; decreasing bias for rate at finer quantization).
- Motivation and placement in the literature: The paper situates nonlinear transform coding and local gain control (GDN) in the context of natural image statistics and visual neuroscience. It makes clear distinctions from batch normalization and pointwise nonlinearities. The relationship to VAEs is precise: under Gaussian observation models and independent latent marginals, the relaxed objective becomes a VAE ELBO with uniform posterior noise; the differences in goals (compression across λ vs. generative modeling at effectively large λ) are explained. Comparisons to Gregor et al. (conceptual compression) and Toderici et al. (RNN-based variable-rate binary codes) are fair and highlight novelty (end-to-end RD training with true bitrates and transform coding).
- Technical soundness: The transforms are specified in detail (convs, sampling, GDN/IGDN), and training procedures are transparent (Adam, parameterizations to ensure stability and symmetry, filter normalization). The entropy model assumes independent marginals but learns them nonparametrically; the entropy coding implementation and side information are described. The design choices are reasonable and justified empirically. While architecture selection is somewhat ad-hoc, the results and ablations (e.g., preliminary comparisons to ReLU-like nonlinearities) support the chosen GDN/IGDN for efficiency.
- Empirical rigor: Training is performed on a sizable ImageNet subset; testing uses standard Kodak images and additional held-out images. The authors report both PSNR and MS-SSIM and provide a practical entropy coder to measure true bits. They acknowledge difficulties in summarizing RD over diverse images and provide per-image results online.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not necessarily part of the accept decision)
- Variable-rate model: Training a separate model per λ is a practical limitation. Including a conditional or scaled-latent variant supporting a continuum of rates at test time would strengthen applicability.
- Entropy modeling: The independent-marginal assumption likely leaves bits on the table. Even lightweight spatial context or channel-wise autoregressive models could further improve rates without changing the core contribution.
- Ablations: Quantify the contribution of GDN/IGDN vs. pointwise nonlinearities and different depths/widths via controlled ablations at matched parameter budgets. This would clarify the efficiency claim.
- Fairness and reproducibility: Include exact software/library settings for JPEG 2000 (e.g., OpenJPEG options), report side-information overhead magnitude, and consider adding a small-scale human study to corroborate MS-SSIM improvements.
- Complexity: Provide encoder/decoder runtime and memory comparisons (CPU/GPU) versus JPEG/JPEG 2000 to illustrate deployability.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are the RD results to the independence assumption in the latent space? Have you measured the residual dependencies (e.g., via mutual information) and the potential RD gains from adding simple context models?
- The uniform-noise relaxation uses unit-width noise. Did you explore annealing the noise width or alternative straight-through estimators? How does this affect convergence and bias at very low rates?
- How large is the side-information overhead (image size, λ index, etc.) relative to the total bits at low rates? Please provide quantitative numbers.
- Architecture sensitivity: How do RD results change with the number of stages, filter sizes, and channel counts at a fixed parameter budget? Are there diminishing returns beyond three stages?
- GDN/IGDN specifics: You fix certain exponents and symmetries. What is the performance impact if these constraints are relaxed? Are there stability issues?
- Generalization: The models are trained on 256×256 crops. How do they perform on significantly larger images (e.g., 4K) without retraining? Any boundary or tiling artifacts?
- Fair comparison settings: For JPEG 2000, did you also test with context-adaptive options and rate allocation settings beyond defaults? If not, can you provide a sensitivity analysis to ensure the baseline is near-optimal?
- Variable-rate extension: Could the same framework be trained with a hypernetwork or learned scale parameters to support continuous λ at test time with a single model?
- Evaluation breadth: Beyond Kodak and the provided examples, do results hold on other modern datasets (e.g., DIV2K, BSD500)? Any domain shift observations?

Overall, the paper makes a clear, technically solid, and novel contribution to learned compression with strong empirical evidence and practical evaluation.
