

# Peer Review for "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contribution

This paper presents a novel image compression method based on end-to-end optimization of nonlinear transform coding. The authors propose a model consisting of a nonlinear analysis transformation, uniform quantizer, and nonlinear synthesis transformation, all constructed through cascades of convolutional linear filters and nonlinear activation functions inspired by biological visual systems (specifically, generalized divisive normalization or GDN). The key contribution is a framework for jointly optimizing the entire compression pipeline using stochastic gradient descent, overcoming the intractability of optimizing the discrete rate-distortion problem through a continuous relaxation of the quantization step. The authors demonstrate that their method achieves better rate-distortion performance than JPEG and JPEG 2000 across multiple test images, with particularly significant improvements in visual quality as measured by MS-SSIM, despite being trained with MSE as the distortion metric.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper addresses a significant problem in image compression with a well-motivated approach that is clearly situated within the literature. The authors provide a strong theoretical foundation for their optimization approach, clearly explaining the relationship between their method and variational autoencoders while highlighting key differences that make their approach more suitable for compression tasks. The empirical results are convincing and well-documented, with thorough comparisons to standard compression methods across multiple metrics and test images. The visual improvements demonstrated are particularly compelling, as they go beyond standard PSNR metrics to show perceptual benefits that align with human visual perception.

The paper's most significant contribution is demonstrating that end-to-end optimization of a nonlinear transform coding model can substantially outperform traditional methods. The authors successfully show that their approach not only achieves better objective metrics but also delivers visually superior results across all bit rates and test images—a remarkable achievement given that the method was optimized for MSE. This represents a meaningful advance over previous work that either used perceptual metrics (which can be more subjective) or optimized only parts of the compression pipeline.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The paper is well-written and technically sound, but a few improvements would strengthen it further:

1. The paper could provide a more detailed explanation of how the continuous relaxation used during training translates to effective discrete compression. While the authors mention that the relaxation is only used for training, a clearer discussion of the gap between the continuous optimization and the discrete implementation would be valuable.

2. The computational complexity of the method (both in training and inference) should be more thoroughly addressed. The paper mentions that encoding/decoding is efficient, but specific numbers about inference speed compared to standard methods would be helpful for assessing practicality.

3. The authors briefly mention that they are testing simpler nonlinearities but don't provide results. Including a brief comparison of GDN versus other activation functions would help readers understand why the specific nonlinearities were chosen and how much of the performance gain is due to the biologically-inspired components versus the end-to-end optimization framework.

4. The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of how this approach might extend to video compression, which represents a significant practical application of image compression techniques.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that the continuous relaxation of the quantizer is used only during training, but the actual compression uses uniform quantization. Can the authors provide more detail on how they ensure the model optimized with the continuous relaxation performs well with discrete quantization? Specifically, what evidence do they have that the relaxation doesn't lead to a mismatch between the training objective and the actual compression performance?

2. The authors note that their method outperforms JPEG and JPEG 2000 across all bit rates and images, but Figure 7 shows some marginal PSNR improvements for certain images at higher bit rates. Could the authors elaborate on what specific characteristics of these images might lead to these marginal improvements, and whether these are the types of images where traditional methods already perform well?

3. The paper mentions that they train separate models for different λ values (rate-distortion trade-off parameters). How does the computational cost scale with the number of λ values they want to optimize for? Is it feasible to train a single model that can operate at multiple points along the rate-distortion curve without retraining, and if so, how would this be implemented?

This paper represents a significant contribution to the field of image compression, demonstrating the power of end-to-end optimization for a classical problem. The results are compelling, the approach is well-motivated, and the paper is clearly written. With the minor revisions suggested above, this would be an excellent contribution to ICLR 2017.
