

# Review for "END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel image compression framework that optimizes the entire compression pipeline (analysis transform, quantizer, and synthesis transform) end-to-end for rate-distortion performance. The authors introduce a nonlinear transform coding approach using cascaded convolutional layers with generalized divisive normalization (GDN), inspired by biological visual systems. The key innovation is a continuous relaxation of the quantization step that enables stochastic gradient descent optimization of the entire model. The method achieves superior rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 across standard test images, with particularly impressive improvements in visual quality as measured by MS-SSIM. The paper is well-written, thoroughly motivated, and provides comprehensive experimental validation.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image compression by demonstrating that end-to-end optimization of a nonlinear transform coding framework can substantially outperform traditional methods. The key reasons for this recommendation are: (1) the paper presents a novel approach that achieves state-of-the-art results in image compression, and (2) the empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating consistent improvements over standard methods across multiple metrics and test images.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution is its demonstration that end-to-end optimization of a nonlinear transform coding framework can yield better performance than traditional methods. The authors properly position their work in the context of transform coding methods (JPEG, JPEG 2000) and clearly explain why joint optimization is challenging but valuable. Their continuous relaxation of the quantization step is a technically sound and clever solution to an otherwise intractable optimization problem.

The empirical results are strong and well-supported. The paper provides comprehensive comparisons across multiple test images (Kodak, Lena, Barbara, etc.) showing consistent improvements in both PSNR and MS-SSIM. The visual comparisons (Figures 5 and 6) clearly demonstrate the qualitative improvements in visual quality, which is particularly impressive as the method was optimized for MSE but delivers better perceptual quality. The analysis of the continuous relaxation technique (Figure 4) shows that the approximation is reasonable, with the bias disappearing for finer quantization.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, properly citing relevant work including the authors' previous contributions to nonlinear image transforms and their relationship to variational autoencoders. The distinction between their approach and generative models is well-articulated, addressing a key potential criticism.

## 4. Additional Feedback

- The paper mentions that encoding/decoding with their model is efficient, but it would be helpful to provide specific computational complexity comparisons with JPEG and JPEG 2000. A quantitative measure of speedup/slowdown would strengthen the practical relevance of the work.

- The authors mention they're testing simpler nonlinearities (ReLU, sigmoid) but only provide preliminary results. A more comprehensive comparison between different activation functions would help understand the specific contribution of the GDN/IGDN transforms.

- The paper trains separate models for different λ values, which is computationally expensive. It would be valuable to explore whether a single model could be trained to handle multiple rate-distortion trade-offs (as Toderici et al. do), which would be more practical for real-world applications.

- The architecture details (number of filters, filter sizes) are somewhat ad-hoc. A brief sensitivity analysis showing how these choices impact performance would help readers understand the robustness of the approach.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that encoding/decoding with the proposed model is efficient, but doesn't provide specific timing comparisons. Could you provide quantitative measures of computational complexity (in terms of time or FLOPs) compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000?

2. The authors mention preliminary results showing that simpler nonlinearities require more parameters to achieve similar performance. Could you elaborate on the specific performance differences between GDN/IGDN and simpler activation functions in terms of rate-distortion performance and visual quality?

3. In Figure 11, the authors note that JPEG 2000 performance varies significantly based on how rate-distortion points are selected. How did you choose the specific rate points for comparison with your method, and did this choice affect your conclusion that your method outperforms JPEG 2000?

4. The paper was optimized using MSE as the distortion metric, yet the visual quality improvements are substantial. Would optimizing directly with a perceptual metric (like MS-SSIM) further improve visual quality, and how might this affect the rate-distortion trade-off?
