

# Peer Review: END-TO-END OPTIMIZED IMAGE COMPRESSION

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper presents a novel image compression framework that jointly optimizes the entire compression pipeline (analysis transform, quantizer, synthesis transform) using end-to-end learning. The authors propose a cascade of convolutional linear filters followed by generalized divisive normalization (GDN) nonlinearities, inspired by biological visual systems, to better model the statistical structure of natural images. They address the fundamental challenge of optimizing with a quantizer (which introduces discontinuities) by introducing a continuous relaxation using additive uniform noise. The method achieves better rate-distortion performance than JPEG and JPEG 2000 across multiple test images, with particularly impressive improvements in visual quality as measured by MS-SSIM. The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that end-to-end optimization of nonlinear transforms can substantially improve both objective and perceptual compression performance.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of image compression with a well-motivated approach that is both theoretically sound and empirically validated. The key reasons for acceptance are the clear theoretical justification for the continuous relaxation technique and the compelling empirical results showing consistent improvements in visual quality across all test images and bit rates.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-positioned within the literature, carefully distinguishing its approach from both traditional transform coding methods and recent generative modeling work. The authors provide a thorough explanation of how their continuous relaxation technique addresses the optimization challenge posed by quantization, and they correctly highlight key differences between compression optimization and generative modeling (section 3.1). This theoretical foundation is crucial for establishing the credibility of their approach.

The empirical results are particularly strong. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements in MS-SSIM across all test images and bit rates, which is remarkable given that the method was optimized using MSE as the distortion metric. The visual examples (figures 5-7) clearly show that the method avoids the blocking and ringing artifacts common in JPEG and JPEG 2000, instead producing more natural-looking compressions that better preserve contours and edges. This perceptual improvement is especially noteworthy because it represents a fundamental shift in the nature of compression artifacts.

The implementation details are thorough and practical, particularly the use of a proper entropy code (CABAC) rather than relying on entropy estimates for evaluation. This allows for a fair comparison to standard compression methods, which is a significant strength over many recent machine learning-based compression approaches that fail to implement proper entropy coding.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, the authors could:

1. Provide more detailed analysis of the computational complexity, particularly the training time versus inference time trade-offs, and how these scale with image size. This is important for assessing practical deployability.

2. Include a more comprehensive comparison to recent generative image compression methods (e.g., Toderici et al., 2016), especially since the paper already discusses this work in section 5. A table comparing key metrics (PSNR, MS-SSIM, bit rates, computational requirements) would be valuable.

3. Add a section comparing the performance of simpler nonlinearities (ReLU, sigmoid) to GDN/IGDN, as mentioned in section 5. This would better support their claim that GDN is more efficient for compression.

4. Provide more detailed statistics on the performance improvements across the entire test set, rather than focusing on specific examples. A table showing average PSNR and MS-SSIM gains across all test images would strengthen the empirical claims.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that the method "exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images." Could you provide more details on the specific images where the method underperforms, and what characteristics of these images might make them challenging for your approach?

2. The authors mention that they're testing simpler nonlinearities but don't provide a thorough comparison. Could you include a brief comparison in the paper showing how ReLU and sigmoid-based versions perform relative to GDN/IGDN, particularly in terms of rate-distortion performance and model complexity?

3. The paper notes that "for finer quantization, the bias [in entropy approximation] disappears." Could you provide more details on how this bias affects the practical implementation and whether it leads to any noticeable differences in the final compression performance?

4. The authors state that "the convex hull of attainable model distortions and rates" determines the overall performance. Could you elaborate on how your method specifically optimizes for the convex hull, and whether there are theoretical guarantees that your approach finds the optimal convex hull?
