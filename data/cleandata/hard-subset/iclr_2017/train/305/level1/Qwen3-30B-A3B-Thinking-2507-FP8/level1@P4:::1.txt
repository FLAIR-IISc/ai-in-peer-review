

# Review for ICLR_2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper presents an end-to-end optimized image compression method using nonlinear transforms. The authors propose a framework where an analysis transform, uniform quantizer, and synthesis transform are jointly optimized for rate-distortion performance. The transforms are constructed from cascades of linear convolutions and nonlinear activation functions using generalized divisive normalization (GDN), inspired by biological visual systems. A key innovation is the use of a continuous relaxation of the quantization step to enable gradient-based optimization. The method demonstrates superior rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000, with substantial improvements in visual quality as measured by MS-SSIM across all bit rates.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant technical contribution with a well-motivated, technically sound approach that clearly outperforms standard compression methods in both objective and perceptual quality metrics. The end-to-end optimization framework and the use of biologically-inspired nonlinear transforms are novel and well-justified.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision
The paper excels in technical soundness and novelty. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical justification for their continuous relaxation of the quantization step, which enables gradient-based optimization of the otherwise discontinuous rate-distortion problem. They clearly explain the relationship to variational autoencoders while highlighting key differences that are critical for compression applications.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors compare their method against JPEG and JPEG 2000 using both PSNR and MS-SSIM metrics across multiple standard test images. The results consistently show better rate-distortion performance and significantly improved visual quality. The paper includes detailed analysis of the continuous relaxation's accuracy in approximating actual rate-distortion values, which strengthens the technical claims.

The method is practically implementable, with the authors providing details of the entropy coding scheme and demonstrating that encoding/decoding is efficient despite higher training costs. The paper's focus on actual bit rates rather than entropy estimates provides a more realistic comparison to standard compression methods.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of computational complexity, especially regarding the trade-off between training time and encoding/decoding speed. A table comparing these metrics would be valuable for practical adoption.
- The authors mention testing simpler rectified-linear or sigmoidal nonlinearities but don't provide results. Including even preliminary comparisons would strengthen the claim about GDN/IGDN's efficiency advantage.
- A visualization of the rate-distortion trade-off across different λ values would help readers understand how the method performs across the entire rate-distortion curve.
- The discussion of entropy coding could be expanded to better explain how their CABAC implementation compares to the more sophisticated entropy coding used in JPEG 2000.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that the method "exhibits better rate-distortion performance than both JPEG and JPEG 2000 for most (but not all) test images." Can the authors provide more detail on which specific images showed inferior performance and why these particular images might be challenging for their approach?

2. The authors mention they are testing simpler rectified-linear or sigmoidal nonlinearities. What specific results did they observe for these alternatives in terms of rate-distortion performance and visual quality, and how do they compare to the GDN/IGDN approach in terms of model size and computational efficiency?

3. The paper notes that "the relaxed (differential) entropy provides a somewhat positively biased estimate of the discrete entropy for the coarser quantization regime, but the bias disappears for finer quantization." How does this bias specifically affect the optimization process, and did the authors observe any practical impact on the final compression performance?
