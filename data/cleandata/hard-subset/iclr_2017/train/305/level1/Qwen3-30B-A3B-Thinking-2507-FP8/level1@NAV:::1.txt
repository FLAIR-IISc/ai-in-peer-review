

This paper presents an innovative end-to-end optimized image compression method using nonlinear transformations inspired by biological visual systems. The authors propose a framework that jointly optimizes an analysis transform, uniform quantizer, and synthesis transform for rate-distortion performance, using Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) as the key nonlinearity. The method demonstrates superior rate-distortion performance compared to JPEG and JPEG 2000 across multiple test images, with particularly notable improvements in visual quality as measured by MS-SSIM.

Decision: Accept

Key reasons: (1) The paper addresses a fundamental problem in image compression with a well-motivated approach that effectively bridges traditional compression theory with modern deep learning. (2) The empirical results are robust, showing consistent improvements across multiple metrics and test images, with clear visual evidence supporting the claims of superior visual quality.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical justification for their continuous relaxation approach, clearly explaining how it relates to variational autoencoders while highlighting key differences in optimization goals. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, using standard datasets and metrics, and the paper provides extensive visual comparisons to support their claims. The method's practical feasibility is demonstrated through a simple entropy code implementation that achieves actual bit rates, allowing fair comparison to existing compression methods.

Additional feedback: While the paper is strong, the authors might consider providing more details about the computational complexity of their method compared to traditional codecs, especially for real-time applications. The appendix mentions the network architecture is somewhat ad-hoc; a brief discussion of potential architectural improvements could strengthen the paper.

Questions for authors: (1) The paper mentions that rectified linear units require more parameters to achieve similar performance - can you provide a more detailed comparison of the parameter efficiency between different nonlinearities? (2) How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the continuous relaxation (i.e., the uniform noise approximation) and would alternative relaxations potentially yield better results?
