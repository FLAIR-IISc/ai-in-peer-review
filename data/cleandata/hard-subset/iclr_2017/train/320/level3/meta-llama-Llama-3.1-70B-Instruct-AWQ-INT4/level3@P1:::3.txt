The paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing by combining learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to learn visual servoing mechanisms. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach in a simulated environment, where a quadcopter is tasked with following a car. The paper makes several contributions, including the use of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing, principled learning of multi-scale visual feature weights, and good sample efficiency.

The authors' approach is based on servoing the camera in the space of learned visual features, rather than image pixels or manually-designed keypoints. They use a bilinear predictive model to predict how motion of the robot's camera will affect the perceived feature values. The visual servoing policy is learned using a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm, which automatically chooses weights for the most relevant visual features.

The experiments demonstrate that the proposed approach outperforms several baselines, including methods using ground-truth bounding boxes. The authors also show that their approach can learn an effective visual servo using low amounts of data, making it suitable for quick adaptation to new targets.

Overall, the paper is well-written and presents a solid contribution to the field of visual servoing. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach, and the experimental results are convincing. However, there are some minor issues and suggestions for future work that can be addressed.

**Decision:** Accept

**Reasons:**

1. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to visual servoing, combining learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning.
2. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach in a simulated environment, with convincing experimental results.
3. The paper makes several contributions, including the use of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing, principled learning of multi-scale visual feature weights, and good sample efficiency.

**Supporting arguments:**

1. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach, making it easy to understand and reproduce.
2. The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach in a simulated environment.
3. The paper is well-written, with proper citations and references to related work.

**Additional feedback:**

1. Consider evaluating the approach on more complex, diverse, and realistic visual conditions, such as clutter, distractors, varied appearances/motion, and more 3D car models.
2. Investigate end-to-end training/fine-tuning of the convnet to assess representation learning benefits and improve generalization.
3. Improve reproducibility by regrouping/simplifying implementation details and providing open-source code with a concise summary in the paper.
4. Fix typos quoted on p.2.

**Questions for the authors:**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their approach to more complex and realistic visual conditions?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the implementation of the bilinear predictive model and the fitted Q-iteration algorithm?
3. How do the authors plan to address the issue of distractors and irrelevant objects in the scene?
