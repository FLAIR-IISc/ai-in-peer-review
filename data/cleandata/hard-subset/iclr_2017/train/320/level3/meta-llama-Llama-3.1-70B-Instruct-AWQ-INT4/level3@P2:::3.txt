**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing by combining pre-trained convolutional spatial feature maps, bilinear one-step visual dynamics, and reinforcement learning (RL) to learn target-following visual servoing. The authors aim to overcome the limitations of traditional visual servoing methods, which rely on manual design of visual features and dynamics models. The proposed approach learns to predict the visual features of the next frame given the current image and action, and uses this prediction to minimize the error between the target and predicted features. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through controlled simulation experiments, showing clear performance gains over multiple sensible baselines.

**Decision**

I recommend accepting this paper, with two key reasons for this choice:

1. **Novelty and significance**: The paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained convolutional spatial feature maps, bilinear one-step visual dynamics, and RL. This approach has the potential to overcome the limitations of traditional visual servoing methods and achieve better performance in complex and dynamic environments.
2. **Rigorous evaluation and comparison**: The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, comparing it to multiple sensible baselines, including methods using ground-truth bounding boxes. The results demonstrate clear performance gains, which suggests that the proposed approach is a significant improvement over existing methods.

**Supporting Arguments**

The results demonstrate clear performance gains, which suggests that the proposed approach is a significant improvement over existing methods.
* **Principled learning of multi-scale visual feature weights**: The authors propose a principled approach to learning multi-scale visual feature weights via an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration, which is a significant contribution to the field of visual servoing.
* **Good sample efficiency**: The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves good sample efficiency, which is a critical aspect of visual servoing in real-world applications.

**Additional Feedback**

While the paper is well-written and provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed approach, there are several areas that could be improved:

* **Experimental limits**: The authors acknowledge that their experiments are limited to a controlled simulation environment, and suggest that future work should focus on evaluating the approach in more complex and realistic visual conditions.
* **Reproducibility**: The authors provide open-source code, but could improve reproducibility by regrouping and simplifying implementation details and providing a concise summary in the paper.
* **End-to-end training**: The authors suggest that future work could investigate end-to-end training or fine-tuning of the convnet to assess representation learning benefits and improve generalization.
