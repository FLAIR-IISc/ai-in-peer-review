This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing, which involves learning a visual representation and predictive dynamics model to control a robot's actions in response to camera observations. The authors introduce a multi-scale, multi-channel, locally-connected variant of a bilinear model to predict visual features, and a weighted objective that incorporates long-term policy values. They also propose a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm to learn the policy. The experiments demonstrate the benefits of the value-weighted objective and show that pre-trained visual features can aid generalization.

The paper is well-written, mathematically solid, and conceptually exhaustive. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach, and the experiments are well-designed and thorough. The results are impressive, showing that the proposed approach can learn an effective visual servo using low amounts of data and outperforming other methods.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The contribution to representation learning is limited, as the authors rely on pre-trained VGG features and do not learn end-to-end. Additionally, the focus is more on control than on representation learning, and the policy representation is fixed and values are approximated linearly with problem-specific features. This makes the paper less aligned with the ICLR conference, which typically focuses on representation learning and deep learning.

Overall, I recommend accepting this paper as it is valuable to robotics researchers and provides a novel approach to visual servoing. However, I would like to see the authors address the limitations mentioned above in future work.

Accept.

Reasons for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing that combines learned visual features and predictive dynamics models.
* The experiments are well-designed and thorough, demonstrating the benefits of the value-weighted objective.
* The paper is well-written, mathematically solid, and conceptually exhaustive.

Reasons for rejection:

* The contribution to representation learning is limited, as the authors rely on pre-trained VGG features and do not learn end-to-end.
* The focus is more on control than on representation learning, and the policy representation is fixed and values are approximated linearly with problem-specific features.

Additional feedback:

* The authors could consider learning the visual representation end-to-end, rather than relying on pre-trained features.
* The authors could explore other policy representations, such as deep neural networks, to improve the flexibility and expressiveness of the policy.
* The authors could consider applying their approach to other tasks, such as robotic manipulation or navigation, to demonstrate its generality.

Questions for the authors:

* How do the authors plan to extend their approach to learn the visual representation end-to-end?
* How do the authors plan to improve the flexibility and expressiveness of the policy representation?
* What are the potential applications of the proposed approach beyond visual servoing?
