**Summary of the Paper's Contribution**

The paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing by combining learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The authors investigate the use of pre-trained deep features, specifically from the VGG network, to learn an effective visual servo that is robust to visual variation, changes in viewing angle and appearance, and occlusions. The paper introduces a multi-scale, multi-channel, locally-connected bilinear model to predict visual feature dynamics and a weighted objective that incorporates long-term policy values. The authors evaluate their approach using a fitted-value method and demonstrate its benefits through experiments.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

I recommend accepting this paper, with the key reasons being:

1. The paper proposes a novel and well-motivated approach to visual servoing that combines learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, demonstrating its benefits and robustness to various visual variations and occlusions.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper is well-written, mathematically solid, and conceptually exhaustive. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem of visual servoing and the limitations of traditional approaches. They also provide a thorough review of related work, highlighting the contributions of their approach.

The proposed bilinear model is a significant contribution to the field, as it allows for efficient prediction of visual feature dynamics. The authors also introduce a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm, which is claimed to be the first of its kind.

The experiments demonstrate the benefits of the value-weighted objective and show that pre-trained visual features empirically aid generalization. The authors also provide a thorough comparison with other approaches, including classical image-based visual servoing and end-to-end servoing policies.

**Additional Feedback and Questions**

While the paper is well-written and the approach is novel, I have some concerns regarding the contribution to representation learning. The authors rely on pre-trained VGG features and do not learn end-to-end, which may limit the paper's impact on the ICLR community.

I would like to ask the authors to clarify the following points:

* How do the authors plan to extend their approach to learn end-to-end, and what are the potential benefits and challenges of doing so?
* Can the authors provide more insights into the design choices behind the bilinear model, and how it compares to other predictive models in the literature?
* How do the authors plan to apply their approach to more complex tasks, such as robotic manipulation or navigation?

**Minor Comments**

* The paper could benefit from more visualizations of the learned visual features and the predicted feature dynamics.
* The authors may want to consider providing more details on the implementation of the trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the approach and potential avenues for future work.
