**Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing by combining learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The authors introduce a bilinear dynamics model to predict next-frame features from the current frame and action, and formulate visual servoing with a Q-function that learns per-feature-channel weights. They also present an optimization scheme for the Bellman error that exploits the scale-invariance of Q-parameters, enabling an elegant fitted Q-iteration (FQI) approach.

**Decision**

I recommend rejecting this paper, primarily due to the limited scope and unsatisfying nature of the experimental evaluation.

**Reasons for Decision**

1. **Limited Experimental Evaluation**: The experiments presented in the paper are limited to a small-scale, toy simulation environment, which does not adequately demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in real-world scenarios. The lack of domain benchmarks and comparisons to state-of-the-art methods in visual servoing makes it difficult to assess the significance of the results.
2. **Unclear Comparison to Alternative Methods**: The paper presents a thorough exploration of ways to connect action and frame representation, favoring a locally connected bilinear model for balance of compute and expressiveness. However, the experimental comparison between fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected dynamics is not comprehensive, and the reviewer requests more experiments to clarify the advantages of the proposed approach.

**Supporting Arguments**

1. **Technical Novelty**: The paper presents a technically novel and solid formulation of the visual servoing problem, combining learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The introduction of a bilinear dynamics model and the optimization scheme for the Bellman error are notable contributions.
2. **Effectiveness of Per-Channel Weighting**: The experiments demonstrate that per-channel weighting of features is more effective than treating channel errors equally, which is a valuable insight for visual servoing applications.

**Additional Feedback**

1. **Modified VGG Network**: The modification of the VGG network needs experimental validation to demonstrate its effectiveness compared to the original VGG network.
2. **Comparison to Iterative Approach**: The reviewer requests clarification or experiments comparing FQI to an iterative approach that holds the second term in Eq. 5 fixed.
3. **Domain Benchmarks**: The authors should consider evaluating their approach on domain benchmarks, such as the KITTI dataset, to demonstrate its effectiveness in real-world scenarios.

**Questions for Authors**

1. Can you provide more experimental comparisons between fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected dynamics to clarify the advantages of the proposed approach?
2. How does the modified VGG network compare to the original VGG network in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
3. Can you provide more details on the optimization scheme for the Bellman error and how it exploits the scale-invariance of Q-parameters?
