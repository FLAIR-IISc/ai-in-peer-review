This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained convolutional spatial feature maps with a bilinear one-step visual dynamics model and reinforcement learning to learn target-following visual servoing. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a complex synthetic car following benchmark, achieving substantial improvements over multiple sensible baselines, including methods using ground-truth bounding boxes.

The paper tackles the specific question of how to learn visual servoing mechanisms using learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The approach is well-motivated, building on recent advances in deep learning and reinforcement learning. The authors provide a clear and thorough explanation of their method, including the use of pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics models, and fitted Q-iteration for learning the policy.

The paper supports its claims through a series of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The authors compare their method to multiple baselines, including classical image-based visual servoing and model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The results show that the proposed approach achieves better performance and sample efficiency than the baselines.

One of the main contributions of the paper is the demonstration of the usefulness of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing. The authors show that these features can be used to learn an effective visual servo that is robust to visual variation, changes in viewing angle and appearance, and occlusions. Another contribution is the principled learning of multi-scale visual feature weights via an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm. This allows the method to handle distractors and improve its performance.

The paper also provides a good sample efficiency from the chosen Q-function approximator and model-based one-step feature dynamics. The authors demonstrate that their method can learn an effective visual servo on a complex synthetic car following benchmark using just 20 training trajectory samples for reinforcement learning.

One of the limitations of the paper is that the experiments are conducted in a simulated environment, which may not fully capture the complexities of real-world visual servoing tasks. However, the authors provide a detailed description of their simulation environment and the tasks they used to evaluate their method.

To improve the paper, I suggest that the authors evaluate their method on more complex, diverse, and realistic visual conditions, such as clutter, distractors, varied appearances, and motion. Additionally, it would be interesting to investigate end-to-end training or fine-tuning of the convolutional neural network to assess representation learning benefits and improve generalization.

To improve reproducibility, I suggest that the authors regroup and simplify implementation details and provide open-source code with a concise summary in the paper.

Minor issues include fixing typos quoted on page 2.

Overall, the paper is interesting and solid, despite some experimental limitations. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field of visual servoing, and their method has the potential to be widely applicable.

Decision: Accept

Reasons for decision:

1. The paper proposes a novel and well-motivated approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained convolutional spatial feature maps with a bilinear one-step visual dynamics model and reinforcement learning.
2. The paper provides a clear and thorough explanation of the method and supports its claims through a series of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

Questions to authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the simulation environment and the tasks used to evaluate the method?
2. How do you plan to extend the method to more complex and realistic visual conditions?
3. Have you considered end-to-end training or fine-tuning of the convolutional neural network to improve representation learning and generalization?
