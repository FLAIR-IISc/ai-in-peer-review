This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing by combining learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The authors investigate the use of pre-trained features from a VGG network, which are modified to remove spatial invariance, and a bilinear model to predict feature dynamics. The approach is evaluated on a synthetic car following benchmark, demonstrating substantial improvement over conventional methods and model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

I decide to accept this paper with the main reasons being the well-motivated approach, the thorough evaluation, and the significant improvement over state-of-the-art methods.

The paper is well-motivated, as it addresses the limitations of traditional visual servoing methods that rely on manual feature design and analytical dynamics models. The authors provide a clear overview of the problem and the proposed approach, which is well-placed in the literature. The use of pre-trained features and bilinear models is well-justified, and the evaluation is thorough and convincing.

The approach is well-supported by the claims, and the results are scientifically rigorous. The authors provide a detailed description of the experimental setup, the evaluation metrics, and the results. The comparison to other methods, including classical image-based visual servoing and model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms, is thorough and convincing.

One potential critique is that the contribution to representation learning is limited, as the approach relies on pre-trained features and does not learn end-to-end. However, the authors acknowledge this limitation and provide a clear justification for their approach.

Another potential critique is that the focus is more on control than on representation learning, and the policy representation is fixed and values are approximated linearly with problem-specific features. However, the authors provide a clear explanation of their design choices and demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.

To improve the paper, I would suggest providing more details on the implementation of the bilinear model and the reinforcement learning algorithm. Additionally, it would be interesting to see more analysis on the robustness of the approach to different types of visual variation, such as changes in lighting or occlusions.

Questions for the authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the implementation of the bilinear model, including the architecture and the training procedure?
2. How do you handle the case where the pre-trained features are not suitable for the task at hand?
3. Can you provide more analysis on the robustness of the approach to different types of visual variation?
4. Have you considered using other types of features, such as optical flow or depth maps, in addition to the pre-trained VGG features?

Overall, this is a well-written and well-motivated paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of visual servoing. The approach is novel, and the evaluation is thorough and convincing.
