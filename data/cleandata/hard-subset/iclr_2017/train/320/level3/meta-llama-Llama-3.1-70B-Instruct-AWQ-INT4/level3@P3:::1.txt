This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing, which combines learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The authors demonstrate that standard deep features can be used with a bilinear predictive model to learn an effective visual servo that is robust to visual variation, changes in viewing angle and appearance, and occlusions. The method is evaluated on a complex synthetic car following benchmark, where it achieves substantial improvement over conventional approaches and model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

I decide to accept this paper, but with some reservations. The main reason for acceptance is that the approach is technically novel and solidly formulated. The use of a bilinear dynamics model to predict next-frame features from current frame and action is an interesting contribution. Additionally, the formulation of visual servoing with a Q-function that learns per-feature-channel weights is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature.

However, I have some concerns regarding the experimental evaluation. The small-scale, toy simulations and lack of domain benchmarks make it difficult to assess the generalizability of the approach. Furthermore, the modification of the VGG network needs experimental validation, and it is unclear if the modified VGG outperforms the original. I would like to see more experimental comparisons between fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected dynamics, as well as a clarification on the FQI approach and its relation to an iterative approach that holds the second term in Eq. 5 fixed.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and its relation to prior work. The experimental results are presented in a clear and concise manner, although I would like to see more details on the experimental setup and the choice of hyperparameters.

To improve the paper, I suggest the following:

* Provide more experimental comparisons between different dynamics models and feature representations.
* Validate the modification of the VGG network through experimental results.
* Clarify the FQI approach and its relation to other reinforcement learning methods.
* Provide more details on the experimental setup and the choice of hyperparameters.
* Consider evaluating the approach on more challenging and realistic benchmarks.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the choice of hyperparameters for the FQI algorithm and the bilinear dynamics model?
* How did you select the number of iterations for the FQI algorithm, and what is the effect of increasing or decreasing the number of iterations?
* Can you provide more information on the computational efficiency of the approach, including the time complexity of the bilinear dynamics model and the FQI algorithm?
* How do you plan to extend the approach to more complex and realistic scenarios, such as real-world robotic systems or more challenging visual servoing tasks?

Overall, the paper presents a novel and solid approach to visual servoing, but requires stronger experiments to bolster its claims. With some additional experimental results and clarifications, I believe the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
