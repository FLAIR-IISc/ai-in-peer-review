## Review of "Learning Visual Servoing with Reinforcement Learning"

This paper presents an interesting approach to visual servoing by combining pre-trained convolutional features, a bilinear visual dynamics model, and reinforcement learning. The core idea – leveraging the power of pre-trained features to reduce data requirements for adapting to new targets – is well-motivated and addresses a significant challenge in robotics. The authors demonstrate compelling performance gains over several baselines, including methods utilizing ground-truth bounding boxes, and showcase impressive sample efficiency. The release of the CitySim3D environment is a valuable contribution to the community, providing a benchmark for visual servoing research. The use of fitted Q-iteration to learn feature weights is a particularly clever and effective technique for handling distractors.

**Decision: Accept**

My primary reason for acceptance is the strong empirical validation of the proposed approach. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their method outperforms existing techniques in a challenging simulated environment, and the reported sample efficiency is particularly noteworthy. Secondly, the paper presents a well-defined problem, a clearly articulated approach, and a thorough experimental evaluation. 

The paper effectively supports its claims through rigorous experimentation. The comparisons to various baselines, including pixel-based servoing, hand-designed features, and other reinforcement learning methods, provide strong evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The ablation studies (e.g., comparing different feature representations) offer valuable insights into the contribution of each component. The quantitative results are consistently supported by qualitative examples (e.g., sample trajectories).

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, several improvements could further enhance its impact. 

*   **Generalization to More Complex Environments:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of the current environment. Evaluating the approach on more complex, diverse, and realistic visual conditions (e.g., increased clutter, more varied appearances/motion of cars, more 3D car models) would significantly strengthen the conclusions.
*   **End-to-End Training:** Investigating end-to-end training or fine-tuning of the convolutional network could reveal potential benefits in representation learning and improve generalization performance.
*   **Code Clarity and Reproducibility:** While the code is released, improving its organization and providing a more concise summary of the implementation details within the paper would enhance reproducibility. A streamlined setup process would be beneficial.
*   **Clarification on Dynamics Model:** Could the authors elaborate on the choice of bilinear dynamics? What are the limitations of this model, and how might it be extended to handle more complex scenarios?
*   **Impact of Regularization:** How sensitive is the performance to the regularization coefficient (ν) in the fitted Q-iteration? A sensitivity analysis would be helpful.



I would like to understand how the choice of the initial state distribution impacts the learning process. Does the method exhibit sensitivity to the initial conditions, and if so, how can this be mitigated? Also, could the authors comment on the computational cost of the bilinear dynamics model compared to more complex video prediction models?
