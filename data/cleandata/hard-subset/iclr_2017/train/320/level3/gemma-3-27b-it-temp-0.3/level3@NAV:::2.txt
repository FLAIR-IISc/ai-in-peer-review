## Review of "Visual Servoing with Learned Visual Representations"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of visual servoing, moving beyond traditional methods reliant on hand-engineered features and analytical dynamics. The authors propose a compelling approach that leverages pre-trained visual features (VGG), a bilinear model for predicting feature dynamics, and reinforcement learning (specifically, fitted Q-iteration) to learn effective visual servoing policies. A key contribution is the introduction of a multi-scale, locally-connected variant of the bilinear model, coupled with a value-weighted objective function to improve long-term policy performance. The experimental results on a synthetic car following benchmark demonstrate significant improvements over conventional approaches and showcase impressive sample efficiency. The introduction of a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm is also a noteworthy addition.

**Accept.** I recommend accepting this paper. The work presents a well-motivated and technically sound approach to a significant problem in robotics. The experimental validation convincingly demonstrates the benefits of the proposed method, particularly the value-weighted objective. 

The paper successfully addresses the question of whether learned visual features can be effectively used for visual servoing, demonstrating that pre-trained features, when combined with a learned dynamics model and reinforcement learning, can achieve robust and sample-efficient control. The approach is well-placed within the literature, building upon existing work in visual servoing, representation learning, and reinforcement learning. The results are supported by thorough experimentation and ablation studies, confirming the effectiveness of the proposed techniques. The mathematical formulations are clear and correct, and the writing is of high quality.

However, as noted by the area chair, the contribution to representation learning itself is limited. The reliance on pre-trained VGG features, without end-to-end learning, means the paper’s focus is more on control than on learning novel representations. While this is acknowledged, it does position the work slightly outside the core interests of ICLR. 

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Study on Bilinear Model Complexity:** It would be valuable to include an ablation study specifically examining the impact of the locally-connected bilinear model compared to a fully-connected version. This would further justify the design choice and quantify the benefits of sparsity.
*   **Generalization to Different Targets:** While the paper demonstrates generalization to novel *cars*, exploring generalization to entirely different object categories (e.g., pedestrians, bicycles) would strengthen the claims about the robustness of the learned features.
*   **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** A discussion of the sensitivity of the algorithm to key hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, regularization coefficient) would provide further insight into its practical applicability.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   The paper mentions the potential for speedups by using a less computationally demanding network than VGG. Have you explored alternative feature extractors (e.g., MobileNet) and what impact did this have on performance?
*   The value-weighted objective is crucial for performance. Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of *why* this weighting is so effective, beyond simply stating that it encourages long-term behavior? Is there a connection to reward shaping or intrinsic motivation?
*   The locally connected bilinear model introduces sparsity. How was the neighborhood size (nf) determined, and what impact does it have on performance?
