**Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel and technically sound approach for learning visual servoing policies. The core contribution is a cohesive framework that combines pre-trained visual features with a learned, predictive dynamics model and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning algorithm. The authors propose using a bilinear model to predict next-frame features from the current frame and action, and they thoughtfully explore different connectivity patterns, ultimately favoring a locally connected model for its balance of expressiveness and efficiency. A key insight is the formulation of the visual servoing problem with a Q-function that learns per-feature-channel weights, allowing the agent to identify the most task-relevant visual cues. This is complemented by an elegant optimization scheme for the Bellman error that exploits the scale-invariance of the Q-parameters to enable an efficient Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) approach.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper's theoretical formulation is strong and the ideas are novel, I recommend rejection for two primary reasons. First, the experimental validation is insufficient to support the paper's claims, as it is confined to a single, small-scale synthetic environment. Second, several key design choices are not adequately justified through empirical comparison against clear alternatives.

**Supporting Arguments**

The primary weakness of this work is its experimental evaluation. The claims of robustness and sample efficiency are demonstrated on a single, custom car-following simulation. While illustrative, this provides limited evidence of the method's general applicability and fails to benchmark performance against established tasks in the robotics community. The reported sample efficiency (learning from 20 trajectories) is impressive, but its significance is diminished by the simplicity of the task. Without tests on more diverse or standard benchmark environments, the true capabilities of the framework remain unclear.

Furthermore, the paper makes several specific design choices that lack rigorous empirical justification. For instance, the authors argue for a locally connected bilinear model over fully connected or convolutional variants, but a direct experimental comparison of these dynamics models is missing. The proposed FQI optimization is clever, but it is not compared to a more standard iterative approach where the target Q-network is simply held fixed. Finally, the VGG network is modified, but there is no ablation study to show whether this modification provides a tangible benefit over using features from the original network.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors**

To strengthen a future submission, I strongly recommend expanding the experimental section to include more diverse tasks and, if possible, standard robotics simulation benchmarks. Rigorous ablation studies are needed to substantiate the central claims and design choices.

I would like the authors to clarify the following points:
1.  Could you provide an empirical comparison between the proposed locally connected bilinear dynamics model and its fully connected and convolutional counterparts to more concretely justify its selection?
2.  How does the proposed FQI optimization compare, in terms of stability and final performance, to a standard iterative approach where the second term in Eq. 5 uses fixed parameters from the previous iteration?
3.  What is the empirical justification for modifying the VGG network? Does this custom feature extractor provide a measurable performance benefit over using features from the original VGG network for this task?
