### Review

**Summary**

This paper presents a sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing control. The core idea is to combine the strengths of pre-trained visual representations, learned dynamics models, and model-based reinforcement learning. The authors propose using features from a pre-trained VGG network as the perceptual space for the control problem. Within this feature space, they learn an action-conditional, multi-scale, locally-connected bilinear model to predict one-step feature dynamics. The control policy is derived by analytically minimizing the predicted distance to a target feature representation.

The paper's main contribution is a principled method for connecting this greedy, one-step policy to long-term task performance. Recognizing that not all visual features are equally important, the authors introduce a weighted objective function where weights for different feature channels and scales are learned via reinforcement learning. They propose a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm where the Q-function is cleverly approximated as a linear function of the weighted, predicted feature errors. This formulation makes learning the optimal feature weights tractable. Experiments on a simulated quadcopter car-following task demonstrate that this approach is highly effective, significantly outperforming classical visual servoing methods and model-free deep RL in terms of both final performance and sample efficiency (requiring only 20 trajectories). The results also show that using pre-trained features aids generalization to novel target objects.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a well-written, mathematically solid, and conceptually exhaustive paper that makes a valuable contribution. The key reasons for my decision are:

1.  The work presents a novel and effective framework that intelligently combines pre-trained features, a learned local dynamics model, and a sample-efficient RL algorithm to solve a challenging robotics problem with impressive data efficiency.
2.  The proposed value-weighted objective, learned via a specialized FQI algorithm, provides a principled and powerful way to imbue a simple one-step predictive model with long-term foresight, a claim that is convincingly supported by rigorous experiments.

**Supporting Arguments**

The strength of this paper lies in its clever decomposition of the visual servoing problem. Instead of attempting to learn a complex, end-to-end visuomotor policy from scratch, which is notoriously data-hungry, the authors leverage existing components and focus the learning on what is truly task-specific. Using pre-trained VGG features sidesteps the need to learn a visual representation from scratch. The choice of a bilinear model for dynamics is well-motivated and allows for an efficient, analytical policy solution.

The most significant contribution is the integration of reinforcement learning to learn a weighting over the feature channels. This addresses a critical limitation of naively servoing in a high-dimensional feature space: many features correspond to background or irrelevant distractors. By formulating the Q-function as a linear combination of the feature error terms, the authors can use FQI to directly learn the importance of each feature channel for maximizing long-term reward. The experiments clearly demonstrate the benefits of this value-weighted objective; the FQI-trained agent significantly outperforms both an unweighted version and agents trained with a more standard (and less sample-efficient) policy gradient method like TRPO. The reported improvement of over two orders of magnitude in sample efficiency compared to model-free deep RL is a major result. The authors also introduce what appears to be a novel variant of FQI that further improves stability and efficiency, strengthening the paper's contribution.

**Additional Feedback**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Contribution to Representation Learning:** The paper's contribution to representation learning itself is limited, as it relies on fixed, pre-trained VGG features and does not learn the representation end-to-end. While this is a deliberate and successful design choice for sample efficiency, it would be interesting to discuss the possibility of fine-tuning the visual features as part of the learning process. This could potentially yield a representation even better suited for control dynamics, though likely at the cost of requiring more data.

*   **Alignment with ICLR:** The paper's focus is heavily weighted towards control and model-based RL rather than representation learning. The policy representation is fixed (as the solution to a quadratic objective) and the value function is approximated linearly using problem-specific meta-features (the squared errors in the VGG feature space). This framing might make the paper a better fit for a robotics conference (like RSS or ICRA). To better connect with the ICLR audience, the authors could add a brief analysis of the learned weights (`w`): which VGG layers and channels receive high weights? Does this provide any insight into what visual properties are most crucial for this control task?

**Questions for Authors**

1.  The FQI algorithm (Algorithm 1) first solves for scaling and bias parameters (`α`, `b`) before updating the main weights (`θ`). Could you provide more intuition on why this two-step update is more effective or stable than a more standard approach of simply solving for `θ` and `b` in a single optimization step?

2.  Your results indicate that `conv4_3` features perform best, while the deeper `conv5_3` features are less effective, which you hypothesize is due to excessive spatial invariance. Have you considered if this trend holds for more modern architectures like ResNets, which may have different invariance properties?

3.  The bilinear dynamics model is trained using data collected from a hand-coded policy. How sensitive is the performance of the final learned servoing policy to the quality and diversity of the data collected by this initial policy?
