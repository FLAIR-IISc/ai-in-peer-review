### Summary of Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of learning visual servoing policies for target following in a sample-efficient manner. The authors propose a novel method that combines three key components: (1) pre-trained deep convolutional features (from VGG) to provide a robust visual representation, (2) a learned, multiscale bilinear dynamics model to predict one-step changes in these feature maps in response to robot actions, and (3) a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn the final control policy.

The core idea is to frame the policy as a simple servoing mechanism in the feature space, which minimizes a weighted Euclidean distance between the predicted next-step features and the target features. The main technical contribution lies in using a principled and sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn the weights for different feature channels and scales. This allows the system to automatically identify which visual features are most relevant for tracking the target and ignoring distractors. The paper's contributions are supported by strong empirical results in a complex, simulated car-following environment. The method demonstrates substantial improvements in both final performance and sample efficiency (over two orders of magnitude) compared to a range of well-chosen baselines, including classical visual servoing techniques and standard model-free deep reinforcement learning. The authors also contribute an open-source simulation environment to the community for future benchmarking.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to learning-based robotics and control. The primary reasons for my decision are:

1.  **Effective and Novel Approach:** The paper presents a clever and well-motivated synthesis of pre-trained features, learned dynamics, and sample-efficient reinforcement learning. The specific formulation of the Q-function to match the policy's objective, enabling an efficient FQI solution for learning feature weights, is a strong and novel technical contribution.
2.  **Convincing and Thorough Experimental Validation:** The claims are backed by comprehensive experiments that demonstrate significant performance gains over multiple strong baselines. The dramatic improvement in sample efficiency compared to model-free deep RL methods like TRPO is particularly compelling and addresses a critical challenge for applying RL to real-world robotics.

### Supporting Arguments

The problem of sample-efficient visual servoing is highly relevant. Classical methods often require extensive, task-specific engineering of features and models, while standard end-to-end deep RL approaches are notoriously data-hungry. This paper charts a compelling middle ground.

The approach is well-justified and elegant. By leveraging powerful, pre-trained visual representations, the system avoids learning perception from scratch. Learning a simple, local predictive model in this feature space is more tractable than predicting raw pixels and provides the necessary structure for the one-step lookahead policy. The main challenge, which the paper successfully addresses, is determining which of the high-dimensional features are relevant to the task. The proposed FQI method provides a principled way to learn these feature weights, effectively learning a task-specific cost metric that leads to robust long-term behavior.

The experimental evaluation is a key strength of this work. The authors compare their method against a variety of sensible alternatives: classical Image-Based Visual Servoing (IBVS) with hand-crafted features (ORB) and with a state-of-the-art tracker (C-COT), ablations of their own method using different features and weight optimization schemes (CEM), and a standard model-free deep RL baseline (TRPO). The proposed method significantly outperforms all of them. It is particularly impressive that the method outperforms the tracker-based IBVS, even though the latter is provided with the initial ground-truth bounding box. The sample efficiency results are the most striking contribution; learning a high-performing policy from just 20 trajectories is a significant achievement and makes such an approach far more practical for real robotic systems than methods requiring thousands of trials.

### Additional Feedback and Questions

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Generalization:** The experiments are convincing but are limited to a single (though complex) synthetic environment. To further strengthen the claims of robustness, it would be beneficial to see an evaluation on more diverse visual conditions (e.g., different weather, lighting, more background clutter) or, ideally, a demonstration on a physical robot.
*   **End-to-end Learning:** The paper relies on fixed, pre-trained features. It would be interesting to discuss or explore the potential benefits and trade-offs of fine-tuning the feature extractor on the servoing task. While this would likely sacrifice some of the impressive sample efficiency, it might lead to better overall performance and generalization.
*   **Clarity of Experimental Details:** While I commend the authors for planning to release code, the experimental details in the paper are somewhat scattered between the main text and a lengthy appendix. A more consolidated and concise summary of the key hyperparameters and training procedures for the main proposed method would improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility. The paper could also benefit from a final proofreading pass to correct minor typos.

**Questions for the Authors:**
1.  How sensitive is the performance of the learned dynamics model, and consequently the final policy, to the data collection strategy used to train it? Is the initial hand-coded policy sufficient for generalization, or would a more iterative data collection scheme be beneficial?
2.  The two-step update in the proposed FQI algorithm (Eqs. 5 and 6) is an interesting design choice. Could you provide more intuition on the role of the first step, which solves for a global scaling factor `α`? How critical is this step for the stability and convergence of the algorithm compared to a more standard FQI update?
3.  Could you comment on the computational requirements at test time? The comparison to the C-COT tracker (16Hz vs 1Hz) is very helpful. A breakdown of the inference time between the VGG forward pass, the dynamics model prediction, and the final action selection would provide further insight into potential bottlenecks and avenues for optimization.
