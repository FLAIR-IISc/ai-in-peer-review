### Review

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel and sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing policies. The core problem it addresses is how to perform target-following tasks robustly, without relying on hand-engineered features or analytical dynamics models, and with minimal task-specific training data. The proposed solution elegantly combines three key components: (1) powerful, pre-trained visual features from a VGG network to provide a rich and robust perception space, (2) a learned, action-conditional bilinear model to predict the one-step dynamics of these visual features, and (3) a sample-efficient reinforcement learning algorithm based on fitted Q-iteration (FQI) to learn a long-horizon control policy.

The central insight is to formulate a simple, greedy servoing policy that minimizes a weighted Euclidean distance in the predicted feature space. The key contribution lies in using FQI to learn the channel-wise and scale-wise weights for this objective, effectively shaping the one-step cost function to approximate the long-term optimal action-value function (Q-function). This allows the system to learn which features are most relevant for control and robustly track a target. The paper demonstrates this approach on a challenging simulated quadcopter car-following task, showing substantial improvements in performance and a remarkable increase in sample efficiency (over two orders of magnitude) compared to standard model-free deep RL methods like TRPO. The work is well-written, mathematically sound, and provides a compelling case for combining learned models with pre-trained representations for robotic control.

**Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper for two primary reasons:
1.  The proposed method presents a clever and effective synthesis of pre-trained features, model-based control, and value function learning. The formulation of the servoing objective as a parameterization of the Q-function, which is then learned via a sample-efficient FQI algorithm, is a novel and powerful idea for bridging short-term predictive control with long-term objectives.
2.  The experimental results are thorough and convincingly support the paper's claims. The method is shown to be vastly more sample-efficient than strong model-free deep RL baselines and outperforms classical visual servoing techniques on a complex task, while also demonstrating generalization to unseen target objects.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strength lies in its thoughtful integration of existing techniques to solve a practical problem in robotics. Instead of attempting to learn a policy from pixels end-to-end, which is notoriously data-hungry, the authors leverage the representational power of VGG features. This is a well-motivated choice that provides a degree of invariance to lighting and viewpoint changes from the outset.

The choice of a bilinear model for the feature dynamics is also well-justified. It is simple enough to be learned from a small dataset, yet expressive enough to capture local dynamics. Crucially, its linearity with respect to the control input allows for an efficient, analytical solution to the one-step servoing problem, avoiding complex and slow iterative optimization at runtime.

The most significant contribution is the learning of the servoing objective itself. A simple, unweighted feature-space error is myopic. By introducing weights for each feature channel and scale and learning them with RL, the policy is optimized for long-term reward. The specific FQI algorithm proposed is well-suited for this, as the Q-function approximator is linear in the weights to be learned, making optimization stable and efficient. The experiments clearly demonstrate the value of this step; the FQI-optimized policy significantly outperforms policies with hand-tuned or unweighted objectives.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive. The comparison against TRPO highlights the dramatic sample-efficiency gains, showing that the proposed method learns a high-performing policy from just 20 trajectories, whereas TRPO struggles even with over 100 times more data. The comparison with classical IBVS methods, including a state-of-the-art tracker, shows that the learned dynamics model implicitly captures information about the target's motion, leading to better performance. Finally, demonstrating generalization to novel cars validates the hypothesis that using general-purpose pre-trained features aids in transfer.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to further strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

1.  **Contribution to Representation Learning:** While the paper makes excellent use of learned representations, its direct contribution to the field of *representation learning* is somewhat limited, as it relies on a fixed, pre-trained VGG network. The learning is focused on the dynamics model and the control policy (via the Q-function weights). The paper would be even more compelling if it explored fine-tuning the VGG network on the servoing task data or discussed how the chosen features might be suboptimal compared to ones learned end-to-end (given sufficient data). This would better position the work within the ICLR community's core interests.

2.  **Scope of the Bilinear Model:** The bilinear model works very well for the quasi-rigid motion in the presented task. It would be beneficial to include a brief discussion of the model's potential limitations. For instance, how would it fare with highly non-linear dynamics, such as tracking non-rigid objects (e.g., a person walking) or in scenarios with more complex object interactions?

3.  **Analysis of Learned Weights:** The paper demonstrates that learning the weights `w` and `λ` is crucial for performance. A qualitative analysis of these learned weights could provide valuable insights. For example, does the algorithm learn to up-weight channels in `conv4_3` that correspond to "car-like" features? Does it learn to ignore channels that are sensitive to background clutter? A visualization or analysis of the highest-weighted feature channels could strengthen the claim that the algorithm is learning to focus on relevant information.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  The FQI algorithm learns weights for a fixed set of features. Have you considered the possibility of fine-tuning the VGG feature extractor itself? While this would increase the data requirement, it might lead to features even better suited for this specific control task, potentially closing the performance gap with methods that have access to ground-truth state information.

2.  The generalization experiments show robustness to novel cars of the same class. How do you expect the system to perform if tasked with following an entirely different object class (e.g., a pedestrian) without retraining the dynamics model? Would only the FQI-based weight learning stage need to be re-run on a few demonstration trajectories to adapt?

3.  Could you clarify the motivation behind the two-step update within each FQI iteration (Eq. 5 and Eq. 6)? It appears to be a heuristic for stabilizing and accelerating the learning of the Q-function's scale and bias before updating the primary weights. Is there a theoretical justification for this, or was it found to be empirically superior to a standard FQI update?
