### Summary

This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained deep visual features, a learned predictive dynamics model, and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning algorithm. The core idea is to perform servoing in the feature space of a VGG network rather than in pixel space. The authors propose learning a bilinear dynamics model to predict how actions affect these features one step into the future. The visual servoing policy then minimizes the weighted Euclidean distance between the predicted features and a target feature representation. Crucially, the weights for different feature channels and scales are not handcrafted but learned using a specialized Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) algorithm. This FQI method is particularly elegant, as it exploits the structure of the Q-function to create a computationally efficient and stable update rule. The authors demonstrate their method on a simulated car-following task, showing significant improvements in sample efficiency and final performance compared to model-free deep RL (TRPO) and classical visual servoing methods.

### Decision: Reject

My decision is to reject the paper at this time. While the paper introduces several technically sound and interesting ideas, particularly the FQI formulation for learning feature weights, the experimental validation is not strong enough to fully support the claims of the method's superiority and generalizability. The primary reasons for this decision are:
1.  The evaluation is conducted on a single, custom-built synthetic environment, which makes it difficult to assess the true difficulty of the task and compare the results against a wider body of literature.
2.  The paper lacks key ablation studies to justify several important design choices, such as the modification of the VGG network and the specific choice of the bilinear dynamics model architecture.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's main strength lies in its novel formulation. The combination of a learned dynamics model in a pre-trained feature space with a value-based RL method to learn a task-specific distance metric is a compelling direction for sample-efficient robot learning. The proposed FQI algorithm in Section 5.3 is particularly clever. The insight that the optimal policy is invariant to a positive scaling of the Q-function's weight parameters `θ` allows for the two-step update procedure (Eqs. 5 and 6), which likely contributes to the stability and rapid convergence shown in the experiments. This is a solid technical contribution. Similarly, the exploration of a locally connected bilinear model as a compromise between the expressive power of a fully connected model and the parameter efficiency of a convolutional one is well-motivated.

However, the experimental section is not sufficiently rigorous to substantiate the paper's claims.

1.  **Limited and Custom Experimental Domain:** The evaluation is confined to a single car-following task in a custom simulator, "CitySim3D". While visually complex, using a non-standard environment makes the results difficult to contextualize. It is unclear if the performance gains are specific to the particularities of this environment or if they would generalize to other visual servoing tasks or standard robotics benchmarks. The claims of robustness to occlusions and distractors would be much stronger if demonstrated in more diverse and challenging scenarios, or ideally on a physical robot.

2.  **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper makes several critical design choices that are not empirically validated, weakening the conclusions.
    *   **VGG Modification:** The VGG network is modified by removing max-pooling layers and adding dilated convolutions. While the motivation is to preserve spatial information, there is no experiment comparing the performance of this modified network against using features from the standard, off-the-shelf VGG-16. It is therefore impossible to know if this modification was necessary or beneficial.
    *   **Bilinear Model Architecture:** The paper argues for a locally connected bilinear model but does not provide an experimental comparison against the alternatives it mentions, such as a fully connected per-channel model or a convolutional dynamics model. Such a comparison is essential to demonstrate that the chosen architecture provides the best trade-off between performance and efficiency.

3.  **Comparison to Baselines:** While the comparison to TRPO highlights the sample efficiency, the end-to-end TRPO baseline is a standard, often sample-inefficient, model-free algorithm. A comparison against more modern sample-efficient model-based or model-free RL methods would provide a stronger benchmark. Furthermore, the classical IBVS baselines are given ground truth depth information, which is a significant advantage not afforded to the proposed method, making the comparison somewhat uneven.

In summary, the paper presents a promising and technically elegant framework, but the claims are not yet backed by the necessary experimental evidence.

### Additional Feedback

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   The presentation of the method is generally clear. However, the connection between the policy in Equation (3) and the Q-function in Section 5.2 could be made more explicit. Stating that the policy is `argmin_u Q(s,u)` directly links the RL objective to the control policy.
*   The paper mentions that the deepest VGG features (`conv5_3`) perform worse, hypothesizing that they are too spatially invariant. This is an interesting finding and could be explored further, perhaps by visualizing the learned feature weights `w` to see if the algorithm correctly down-weights these features.
*   It is commendable that the authors have made their code and environment publicly available. This greatly aids reproducibility and is a significant positive contribution to the community.

### Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding and assess the contributions more confidently, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  Could you provide an empirical comparison or a more detailed justification for choosing the locally connected bilinear dynamics model over a fully connected (per-channel) or a convolutional variant? How do they compare in terms of predictive accuracy, parameter count, and final servoing performance?
2.  What is the empirical impact of modifying the VGG network with dilated convolutions? A direct comparison of your method using features from the standard pre-trained VGG-16 versus your modified version would be very informative.
3.  Regarding the FQI algorithm in Section 5.3: How does the proposed two-step update (first solving for `α` and `b` jointly, then updating `θ`) compare to a more standard FQI approach where the target network parameters (`θ(k-1)`, `b(k-1)`) are simply held fixed while optimizing the current network parameters? An ablation on this specific algorithmic choice would strengthen the novelty claim of the learning procedure.
