### **Summary of Contributions**
This paper presents a novel and sample-efficient approach to learning visual servoing for a target-following task. The core contribution is a method that elegantly combines pre-trained deep visual features, a learned predictive dynamics model, and a tailored reinforcement learning algorithm. The authors propose using features from a pre-trained VGG network and learning a bilinear model to predict the one-step dynamics of these features in response to robot actions. The key insight is to formulate the control policy as minimizing a weighted distance in this feature space, where the channel-wise weights are learned to optimize a long-term reward. This is achieved via a custom fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm that is particularly sample-efficient, in part due to a clever optimization scheme that exploits the structure of the Q-function approximator. The method is shown to learn an effective servoing policy from only 20 training trajectories in a simulated quadcopter environment, significantly outperforming classical keypoint-based methods and standard model-free deep reinforcement learning approaches in terms of both final performance and data efficiency.

### **Decision: Accept**
My decision is to accept the paper. The primary reason is the novel and technically sound formulation for learning the servoing policy. The insight to structure the problem such that feature weights can be learned efficiently via a specialized fitted Q-iteration algorithm is a strong methodological contribution. This is balanced by a concern that the experimental validation, while internally consistent, is limited to a single simulated environment, which somewhat weakens the claims of general robustness.

### **Supporting Arguments**
The main strength of this work lies in its novel reinforcement learning formulation. Instead of learning a complex, end-to-end policy, the authors define a simple, structured policy that minimizes a weighted one-step prediction error (Equation 3). The central technical contribution is framing this objective within a value-based RL framework. By designing a Q-function approximator that is linear in the feature weights (Section 5.2), the problem of learning a good policy becomes one of learning the correct weights for the Q-function. The proposed FQI algorithm is particularly elegant; the insight that the optimal action is invariant to a positive scaling of the Q-function's weights allows for a policy-independent update step (Equation 5) that stabilizes and accelerates learning. This likely explains the impressive sample efficiency (learning from just 20 trajectories) compared to model-free alternatives like TRPO, which require orders of magnitude more data.

The paper's weakness is the scope of its experimental evaluation. While the experiments are thorough within the chosen domain (a simulated city environment), the claims of robustness to occlusions, viewpoint changes, and appearance variations would be much more compelling if validated on a physical robot or across multiple, diverse simulation benchmarks. The current evaluation on a single, non-standard synthetic environment makes it difficult to assess how well the method would generalize to the complexities and unpredictable noise of the real world. For instance, the bilinear dynamics model, while sample-efficient, might be too simplistic for more complex physical interactions. Despite this, the results presented are significant, demonstrating a clear advantage over both classical visual servoing and standard deep RL baselines in the tested setting. The strength and novelty of the learning algorithm itself are sufficient to warrant acceptance.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**
The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The paper would be significantly strengthened by demonstrating the proposed method on a physical robot, even for a simpler task. This would provide crucial evidence for the method's applicability beyond simulation.
*   The modification to the VGG network (removing max-pooling and using dilated convolutions) is an interesting detail. An ablation study comparing performance with features from the standard, unmodified VGG network would be valuable to justify this architectural choice.
*   The choice of a locally connected bilinear model over alternatives (e.g., a fully connected or a convolutional model) is justified by parameter count and memory constraints. However, an empirical comparison, perhaps on a lower-dimensional feature set where all models are tractable, would provide stronger evidence for this design decision.

**Questions:**
1.  The FQI algorithm introduces a novel intermediate step (Equation 5) to solve for a scaling factor `alpha` and bias `b` before updating the feature weights `theta`. How crucial is this step for the algorithm's performance and stability? How does it compare to a more standard FQI update where the target Q-network is simply a fixed copy of the previous iteration's network (i.e., using `Q_{theta(k-1)}` directly)?
2.  Could you provide more intuition as to why the `conv5_3` features performed worse than shallower ones? Your hypothesis that they are "too spatially invariant" is plausible. Is it also possible that the simple bilinear dynamics model is a poorer fit for these more abstract, high-level features compared to the mid-level features from `conv4_3`?
3.  The comparison with classical Image-Based Visual Servoing (IBVS) is informative. You mention providing these baselines with ground-truth depth for the interaction matrix. How would their performance be affected in a more realistic scenario where depth must be estimated, which would introduce additional noise and error?
