### 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper investigates the problem of visual servoing, proposing a novel method that combines pre-trained deep visual features, a learned predictive dynamics model, and sample-efficient reinforcement learning. The core contribution is a system that can learn robust target-following policies using very little task-specific data. The method leverages features from a pre-trained VGG network and learns an action-conditional bilinear model to predict the one-step evolution of these features. To handle long-term objectives, the authors cleverly formulate the servoing policy as the minimizer of a weighted objective over feature prediction errors. The key insight is to learn these weights using a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm, which allows the simple greedy policy to approximate the optimal long-horizon action-value function. The paper introduces a multi-scale, locally-connected variant of the bilinear model to handle features at different levels of abstraction. Experiments on a complex simulated quadcopter-based car-following task demonstrate that the proposed method significantly outperforms baselines using pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and is over two orders of magnitude more sample-efficient than standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms like TRPO.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a well-motivated and elegant synthesis of pre-trained visual representations, model-based control, and value function learning. The approach of using FQI to learn weights for a simple, dynamics-aware servoing policy is a clever way to achieve sample-efficient learning for a complex visuomotor task.
2.  The empirical results are strong and convincingly support the central claims. The method shows substantial improvement in both final performance and sample efficiency over a range of well-chosen baselines, including classical methods and modern model-free deep RL, highlighting its practical value for robotics applications where data is expensive.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper is well-written, mathematically solid, and provides a conceptually exhaustive treatment of the problem. Its main strength lies in its pragmatic and effective combination of existing techniques to create a novel and highly sample-efficient solution for visual servoing.

Instead of attempting to learn a policy end-to-end from pixels, which is known to be data-hungry, the authors make a series of well-justified design choices. First, leveraging pre-trained VGG features provides a powerful visual representation with useful invariances "for free," allowing the system to generalize to novel target instances as shown in the experiments. Second, learning a simple, locally-linear dynamics model (the bilinear model) in this feature space is much more tractable than learning dynamics in pixel space. This model is sufficient for one-step prediction and, critically, its structure allows for an analytic solution to the control problem.

The most significant contribution is the learning framework. A simple one-step predictive controller would be myopic. The authors address this by introducing weights for different feature channels and scales and framing the learning of these weights as a reinforcement learning problem. The choice of a linear Q-function approximator, whose features are the prediction errors themselves, is elegant. This structure makes it possible to use a very sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn the feature weights that encourage good long-term behavior. The experimental results in Figure 6 are particularly compelling: the proposed FQI method achieves excellent performance with just 20 training trajectories, whereas a standard deep RL algorithm like TRPO requires 50 iterations (over 100x more data) and still fails to match its performance. This demonstrates a clear and significant advance in sample efficiency for this class of problems.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended as constructive feedback for strengthening the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Positioning within Representation Learning:** The paper's primary contribution is arguably in control and sample-efficient RL rather than representation learning, as the visual features are used off-the-shelf. The work would be better contextualized for the ICLR audience if it explicitly discussed the choice *not* to fine-tune the VGG network. It would be interesting to discuss the hypothesis that fine-tuning with a small RL dataset might lead to catastrophic forgetting of useful general features or overfitting to the training environment, thus justifying the use of fixed, pre-trained representations.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The experimental section is strong, but could be enhanced with more detailed ablations. For instance, how critical is the multi-scale feature representation to the final performance? A comparison between the proposed locally-connected bilinear model and a simpler, fully-connected variant on a subset of features could also provide valuable insight into the model design.
*   **Real-World Applicability:** While the simulated environment is complex, a brief discussion on the potential challenges of deploying this system on a physical robot would be a welcome addition. This could include topics like the sim-to-real gap for the learned dynamics model, computational latency of the VGG forward pass, and robustness to unmodeled physical effects.

### 5. Questions for Authors

*   The proposed method's success hinges on the bilinear model being a reasonable approximation of the feature dynamics. How sensitive is the performance of the overall system to the accuracy of this learned dynamics model? At what point of environmental complexity or non-linearity would you expect this modeling assumption to break down?
*   In your FQI algorithm (Algorithm 1), you use a two-step update for the policy parameters `theta` within each FQI iteration (lines 5-7). Could you provide more intuition on why this specific procedure of first finding a scaling factor `alpha` and then refitting is more stable or effective than a more standard approach where the new parameters `theta(k)` are solved for directly against a target Q-function using the fixed previous parameters `theta(k-1)`?
*   Your results show that VGG conv5_3 features perform worse than conv4_3, which you hypothesize is due to excessive spatial invariance. This is an interesting finding. Have you considered whether features from networks pre-trained on dense prediction tasks (e.g., semantic segmentation), which are designed to preserve more spatial detail, might be even better suited for visual servoing than features from a classification network?
