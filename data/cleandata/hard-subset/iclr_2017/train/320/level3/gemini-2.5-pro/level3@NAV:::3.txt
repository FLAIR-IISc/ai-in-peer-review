This paper presents a method for learning target-following visual servoing policies with high sample efficiency. The core idea is to combine pre-trained convolutional spatial feature maps with a learned bilinear one-step visual dynamics model. A policy is then learned via reinforcement learning, which optimizes weights over these multi-scale features to minimize predicted distances to a target feature representation. The authors' key contributions include a principled and sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm for learning feature weights to handle distractors, a quantitative evaluation demonstrating clear performance gains over multiple strong baselines, and the release of a new open-source virtual environment for benchmarking.

I recommend **Accept**. The primary reasons for this decision are the paper's clever and principled combination of established techniques to achieve impressive sample efficiency—a critical challenge in robotics—and the rigorous empirical validation that clearly supports its claims of superiority over sensible baselines.

The approach is well-motivated, leveraging the powerful representations of pre-trained networks while avoiding the immense data requirements of end-to-end training. The proposed fitted Q-iteration method is an elegant formulation that learns to weight multi-scale features to optimize a long-term objective, directly contributing to the method's robustness and data efficiency. The experiments are thorough, comparing not only against learning-based alternatives but also classic visual servoing methods, even those provided with privileged information like ground-truth trackers. The demonstrated performance gains are significant and convincingly support the paper’s contributions. The release of the simulation environment is also a valuable contribution to the community.

To further strengthen the paper, I suggest the following points for consideration. The experimental evaluation could be extended to more complex and diverse visual conditions (e.g., more clutter, varied target appearances/motions, more 3D car models) to better understand the generalization limits. It would also be interesting to see a discussion on end-to-end training or fine-tuning of the convolutional network to assess the potential benefits of representation learning versus the cost in sample efficiency. For reproducibility, regrouping some implementation details into an appendix and providing a more concise summary in the main paper would improve readability. Finally, there are minor typos on page 2 that should be corrected.

I have a few questions for the authors:
1.  Could you comment on the sensitivity of the bilinear dynamics model to larger, non-linear visual changes, such as abrupt changes in target motion or severe occlusions not seen during training?
2.  The fitted Q-iteration method shows excellent sample efficiency. How does its computational cost per iteration compare to the baselines like TRPO, especially as the number of feature channels and scales increases?
