This paper presents a novel and sample-efficient method for visual servoing that combines pre-trained visual features, a learned predictive dynamics model, and reinforcement learning. The core idea is to perform servoing in the feature space of a VGG network rather than raw pixels. The authors propose an action-conditional bilinear model to predict the one-step evolution of these features. To address the challenge of selecting relevant features for control, they introduce a value-weighted objective where weights are learned via a novel and efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm. This approach effectively embeds long-term value into a simple, greedy servoing policy. The contributions are well-supported by experiments on a simulated car-following task, demonstrating substantial improvements in sample efficiency over model-free deep RL and superior robustness compared to classical visual servoing methods.

Decision: **Accept**

This paper is a strong contribution to the intersection of robotics, control, and reinforcement learning. The primary reasons for acceptance are:
1.  The novel and effective synthesis of a learned, local dynamics model in a pre-trained feature space with a sample-efficient FQI algorithm to optimize long-term behavior.
2.  The comprehensive experimental validation, which clearly demonstrates the benefits of the proposed components, particularly the value-weighted objective, and shows strong generalization to novel targets.

The paper is well-written, mathematically sound, and conceptually thorough. The proposed multi-scale, locally-connected bilinear dynamics model is a sensible choice for capturing visual dynamics efficiently. The key insight is to use reinforcement learning not to learn a complex end-to-end policy, but to learn a weighting over features for a simpler, linearized policy. This leads to the main algorithmic contribution: a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm that is shown to be highly sample-efficient. The experiments convincingly show that this approach significantly outperforms model-free deep RL baselines and classical methods that struggle with visual distractors. The main limitation is that the work's contribution to representation learning is indirect; it relies on a fixed, pre-trained VGG network rather than learning features end-to-end. Consequently, the focus is more on the control methodology. However, given the impressive performance, this is a reasonable trade-off, and the work remains a valuable contribution.

**Additional Feedback & Questions:**
*   The discussion could benefit from addressing the potential limitations of relying on ImageNet-trained features for robotics tasks, where the domain gap can be significant.
*   The hypothesis that deeper features perform poorly due to spatial invariance is plausible. A brief analysis or visualization to support this would strengthen the paper.
*   Could you elaborate on the novelty of the proposed FQI algorithm in relation to prior work on regularized methods in fitted value iteration?
*   How sensitive is the performance to the choice of pre-trained network? Have you experimented with other architectures (e.g., ResNet) to see if the findings hold?
