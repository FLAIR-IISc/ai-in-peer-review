- Presents a novel, sample-efficient visual-servoing method combining pre-trained VGG features, a learned bilinear one-step dynamics model, and a tailored reinforcement-learning algorithm.
- Formulates control as minimizing a weighted distance in feature space with channel-wise weights learned to optimize long-term reward.
- Designs a Q-function approximator linear in feature weights and uses a specialized fitted Q-iteration (FQI) exploiting weight scaling invariance for stable, efficient learning.
- Demonstrates strong sample efficiency: learns effective servoing from only 20 training trajectories in a simulated quadcopter environment.
- Empirically outperforms classical keypoint-based IBVS and standard model-free RL (e.g., TRPO) in final performance and data efficiency.
- Decision: Accept, primarily due to the novel, technically sound formulation and elegant algorithmic contribution.
- Main strength: structured RL formulation that reduces policy learning to learning feature weights, enabled by an insightful Q-function design and policy-independent update.
- Key technical insight: optimal action invariance to positive scaling of Q-function weights allows a stabilizing intermediate optimization (alpha, b) in FQI.
- Primary weakness: experimental validation limited to a single simulated environment, weakening claims of robustness and real-world generalization.
- Concern that the bilinear dynamics model may be too simplistic for more complex real-world physical interactions and noise.
- Suggestion: validate on a physical robot (even for a simpler task) to demonstrate applicability beyond simulation.
- Suggestion: ablation comparing modified VGG (no max-pooling + dilated convolutions) to standard VGG to justify architectural changes.
- Suggestion: empirically compare locally connected bilinear model to fully connected or convolutional alternatives on a tractable feature subset.
- Question 1: How crucial is the intermediate alpha/b optimization step in FQI versus a standard target-network FQI update for stability and performance?
- Question 2: Why did conv5_3 features perform worse than shallower features—are they too spatially invariant or is the bilinear model a poor fit for higher-level features?
- Question 3: IBVS baselines used ground-truth depth for the interaction matrix—how would their performance degrade when depth must be estimated with realistic noise?
