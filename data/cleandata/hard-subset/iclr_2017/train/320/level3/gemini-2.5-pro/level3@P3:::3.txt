### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel and sample-efficient method for learning target-following visual servoing. The core proposal is to combine the power of pre-trained deep convolutional features with a learned, one-step visual dynamics model and a reinforcement learning framework. Specifically, the method extracts multi-scale spatial feature maps from a VGG network, learns a simple yet effective bilinear model to predict the one-step evolution of these features given a control action, and then uses a principled fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn weights over feature channels and scales. This allows the policy to focus on task-relevant visual cues and ignore distractors. The final policy minimizes a weighted distance between the predicted features and the target features, which can be solved efficiently. Key contributions include a controlled experimental evaluation that quantifies the benefits of using pre-trained features for servoing, a clear demonstration of superior performance and sample efficiency over multiple strong baselines (including model-free deep RL and classical methods using ground-truth information), and the release of an open-source simulation environment to foster further research.

### **Decision: Accept**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that I recommend for acceptance. The primary reasons for this decision are:
1.  The proposed method cleverly integrates several powerful ideas—pre-trained features, model-based RL, and a structured policy approximator—to tackle the critical challenge of sample efficiency in robotic learning, demonstrating an improvement of over two orders of magnitude compared to standard deep RL approaches.
2.  The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing, providing strong evidence for the method's effectiveness against a comprehensive set of sensible baselines, thereby rigorously supporting the paper's main claims.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strength lies in its well-motivated and principled approach. Instead of relying on data-hungry, end-to-end, model-free RL, the authors leverage the rich representations learned by large vision models. The choice to model dynamics in this feature space, rather than in pixel space, is well-justified and proves effective.

The technical contributions are significant. The use of a bilinear dynamics model is simple but powerful, enabling an efficient, analytic policy optimization. The core of the learning framework is the FQI algorithm for determining feature weights. This provides a principled mechanism for attentional modulation, allowing the agent to learn which feature channels are most indicative of the target's state and which are distractors. This is a more direct and interpretable approach than what is typically achieved with monolithic neural network policies.

The empirical results are compelling. The method is shown to substantially outperform baselines across the board. The comparison is particularly strong because it includes:
*   **Ablations:** Comparing different feature layers (pixels vs. VGG conv1-5) demonstrates that mid-level features (conv4_3) provide the best trade-off between semantic content and spatial precision.
*   **Alternative Learning:** Showing that the proposed FQI outperforms TRPO and CEM for learning the weights highlights the sample-efficiency of the chosen optimization scheme.
*   **Alternative Methods:** The performance gains over end-to-end deep RL (TRPO on pixels) and classical IBVS methods (even those provided with ground-truth bounding box initialization) firmly establish the superiority of the proposed framework. The reported >100x improvement in sample efficiency over model-free TRPO is a standout result.

Finally, the release of the CitySim3D simulation environment is a valuable contribution to the community, lowering the barrier for future research and benchmarking in this domain.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following suggestions are intended to help strengthen the paper further and are not critical for my decision.

*   **Evaluation on More Complex Scenarios:** The experiments are conducted in a relatively clean, simulated environment. To better assess the robustness and generalization capabilities of the method, it would be beneficial to evaluate it under more challenging visual conditions. This could include scenarios with significant background clutter, multiple moving distractor objects, varied weather and lighting conditions, and a wider diversity of target vehicle models with different appearances and motion patterns.
*   **Investigation of Representation Learning:** The current work relies on fixed, pre-trained features. While highly effective and sample-efficient, this approach cannot adapt the visual representation to the specific task. An interesting avenue for future work would be to investigate the effects of fine-tuning the convolutional network's weights during training. While this would likely increase data requirements, it might yield a more specialized representation that further improves performance and generalization, especially to visually distinct targets or environments.
*   **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is generally well-written, but the implementation details are spread across a lengthy appendix. While the detail is appreciated, a concise summary table in the main paper outlining key hyperparameters for the dynamics model and the FQI algorithm could improve readability. The provision of open-source code is highly commendable and greatly enhances the paper's impact and reproducibility.

### **Questions for the Authors**

*   Could you comment on the sensitivity of the learned bilinear dynamics model? How much data is required to learn a sufficiently accurate model, and how well does a trained model generalize if the robot's physical parameters or camera setup were to change slightly?
*   The FQI algorithm learns a weighting over feature channels. Have you performed any analysis or visualization of these learned weights? It would be insightful to see if the model learns to prioritize channels corresponding to semantically meaningful cues (e.g., wheels, edges, windows) that are robust for tracking.
*   The paper hypothesizes that the deepest VGG features (conv5_3) perform worse due to excessive spatial invariance. Is this an inherent limitation, or could it be addressed by architectural changes, such as using features from higher-resolution vision models or modifying the dilated convolution setup?
