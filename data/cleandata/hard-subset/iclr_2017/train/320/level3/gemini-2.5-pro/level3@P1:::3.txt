**Summary**

This paper presents a novel approach for learning visual servoing for target following, combining pre-trained deep convolutional features, a learned model of visual dynamics, and reinforcement learning. The core idea is to perform servoing in the feature space of a VGG network rather than raw pixels. The method first learns a simple, one-step predictive model of how these features change in response to robot actions, using a bilinear formulation. To ensure the system focuses on the target and ignores distractors, a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm is used to learn weights for different feature channels and scales. This effectively learns a task-specific distance metric. The contributions are demonstrated through a series of controlled experiments in a simulated quadcopter car-following task, showing significant improvements in both sample efficiency and final performance over multiple strong baselines, including classical visual servoing methods and model-free deep RL. The authors also contribute an open-source simulation environment to encourage further research in this area.

**Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper for two key reasons:
1.  The work presents a compelling and principled combination of deep feature representations, model-based control, and sample-efficient reinforcement learning to address the classic problem of visual servoing. The formulation of the Q-function approximator, which enables an efficient FQI-based solution for learning feature weights, is a notable contribution.
2.  The experimental results are thorough and demonstrate a substantial improvement over a comprehensive set of relevant baselines. The reported two-orders-of-magnitude gain in sample efficiency over standard deep RL methods is particularly impressive and highlights the practical value of the proposed model-based approach.

**Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this paper is its intelligent system design, which leverages the strengths of several modern machine learning techniques. By using pre-trained VGG features, the method bypasses the need for massive datasets to learn a useful visual representation from scratch. The choice of a bilinear dynamics model is well-justified; it is simple enough to be learned from very little data, yet expressive enough for effective one-step prediction, and its linearity with respect to controls allows for an efficient policy optimization step.

The most critical component is the proposed FQI algorithm for learning feature weights. This elegantly solves the problem of how to select relevant information from high-dimensional feature maps, enabling the system to distinguish the target from distractors. The paper shows that this learned weighting is crucial for good performance. The specific formulation of the Q-function as a linear combination of feature-space error terms is clever, as it makes the Bellman error minimization a tractable regression problem.

The empirical evaluation is strong. The authors compare their method not only against model-free RL (TRPO) but also against classical image-based visual servoing (IBVS) techniques using hand-crafted (ORB) and tracker-based (C-COT) features. The proposed method significantly outperforms all of these, even when the IBVS baselines are provided with ground truth depth information or initialized with ground-truth bounding boxes. The demonstration that the method learns an effective policy from only 20 training trajectories is a powerful testament to its sample efficiency. The experiments also include generalization tests on novel car models, showing that the learned policies are not merely memorizing the training instances.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions aimed at strengthening the paper further and are not critical for my decision to accept.

*   **Generality and Robustness:** The current experiments are conducted in a single, relatively clean simulation environment. To further bolster the claims of robustness, it would be valuable to evaluate the approach under more diverse and challenging visual conditions. This could include more significant clutter and distractors in the background, varied lighting conditions, different weather, and a wider variety of 3D car models with more diverse appearances and motion patterns.
*   **Feature Representation Learning:** The paper relies on fixed, pre-trained VGG features. An interesting direction for future work would be to investigate the effects of fine-tuning the convolutional feature extractor end-to-end with the rest of the system. While this would likely decrease sample efficiency, it might lead to a better task-specific representation and improved generalization. A discussion of this trade-off would be a welcome addition.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors should be commended for their plan to release code and the simulation environment. To further improve reproducibility, the experimental details in the appendix could be slightly reorganized for clarity. Consolidating all hyperparameters for each algorithm into a single table would make it easier for other researchers to replicate the results precisely.

**Questions for Authors**

To help clarify my understanding and assess the work, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  The choice of a bilinear model for the dynamics is motivated by its simplicity and linearity in the controls. Have you explored more expressive, non-linear models for the feature dynamics? What are the anticipated trade-offs between model complexity, prediction accuracy, and the sample efficiency required to learn the dynamics model?
2.  How sensitive is the proposed FQI algorithm to its hyperparameters, particularly the regularization coefficient ν and the number of inner-loop FQI iterations K? The paper reports ν=0.1 and K=10; some analysis of this sensitivity would strengthen the claims of algorithmic robustness.
3.  The feature dynamics model is trained on data collected from a hand-coded policy. Could this introduce bias into the learned model, limiting its applicability to states far from that policy's distribution? Have you considered if a more general dynamics model could be learned from a larger, more diverse dataset (e.g., random actions) and then applied to this task?
