- Method: combines pre-trained convolutional spatial features with a learned bilinear one-step visual dynamics model for target-following visual servoing.  
- Policy: learns via reinforcement learning to weight multi-scale features to minimize predicted distance to a target feature representation.  
- Contribution: principled, sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm for learning feature weights to handle distractors.  
- Empirical result: quantitative evaluation shows clear performance gains over multiple strong baselines, including classic methods with privileged information.  
- Resource: release of a new open-source virtual environment for benchmarking.  
- Recommendation: Accept — due to clever combination of methods, impressive sample efficiency, and rigorous empirical validation.  
- Strength: well-motivated use of pre-trained representations to avoid large data needs of end-to-end training.  
- Strength: fitted Q-iteration provides an elegant long-term objective optimization that improves robustness and data efficiency.  
- Strength: thorough experiments comparing both learning-based and classic visual servoing baselines.  
- Suggestion: extend experiments to more complex/diverse visual conditions (more clutter, varied target appearances/motions, additional 3D models) to probe generalization.  
- Suggestion: discuss end-to-end training or fine-tuning of the convolutional network to assess trade-offs between representation learning benefits and sample efficiency costs.  
- Suggestion: improve reproducibility by moving implementation details into an appendix and adding a concise summary in the main paper; fix minor typos.  
- Question: how sensitive is the bilinear dynamics model to large non-linear visual changes (abrupt target motion, severe occlusions) not seen during training?  
- Question: how does the computational cost per iteration of fitted Q-iteration compare to baselines like TRPO, especially as the number of feature channels and scales increases?
