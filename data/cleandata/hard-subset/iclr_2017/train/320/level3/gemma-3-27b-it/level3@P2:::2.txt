This paper investigates visual servoing using learned visual representations, specifically combining pre-trained deep features with learned predictive dynamics and reinforcement learning. The authors propose a method for servoing in the space of learned visual features rather than raw pixels, using a bilinear model to predict feature dynamics and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn appropriate feature weights. The experiments demonstrate substantial improvements over conventional and deep reinforcement learning approaches in a complex synthetic car following benchmark.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper presents a technically sound and well-executed study, its primary contribution lies more within the realm of control than representation learning, which is a core focus of ICLR. The reliance on pre-trained VGG features, without significant modification or end-to-end learning, limits the novelty and relevance to the conference’s typical themes.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper clearly articulates the problem of visual servoing and its limitations when relying on hand-engineered features and models. The proposed approach is well-motivated, building on recent advancements in deep learning and reinforcement learning. The combination of pre-trained features, a bilinear dynamics model, and fitted Q-iteration appears novel and offers a practical solution for sample-efficient learning of visual servoing policies. 

The experimental results are compelling. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their approach outperforms conventional methods based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and achieves significant sample efficiency gains compared to standard deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The ablation studies and comparisons with different weighting schemes provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of the proposed method. The inclusion of videos further strengthens the presentation.

However, the core contribution revolves around *how* to effectively utilize existing representations (VGG features) for a control task. The work doesn’t significantly advance the state-of-the-art in representation learning itself. The VGG features are used as a fixed input, and the learning process focuses solely on adapting the control policy to those features. A crucial aspect of ICLR is work that learns *better* representations, and this is largely absent here. While the fitted Q-iteration is a novel algorithmic contribution, its value is heavily tied to the pre-defined feature space. The linear approximation of the Q-function, while computationally efficient, also suggests a limited capacity for complex policy learning.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Justification for VGG:** While the authors demonstrate the usefulness of VGG features, a more thorough exploration of *why* these features work well for visual servoing would be beneficial. Is it the semantic understanding encoded in the features, or something else?
*   **End-to-End Learning:** Investigating an end-to-end learning approach, even with a limited scope, could strengthen the paper. For instance, fine-tuning a portion of the VGG network during the reinforcement learning process might reveal valuable insights.
*   **Generalization:** The experiments are performed in a simulated environment. Discussing the potential challenges of transferring the learned policies to real-world scenarios (e.g., domain adaptation, robustness to sensor noise) would add depth to the analysis.
*   **Complexity Analysis:** A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly the fitted Q-iteration algorithm, would be helpful for assessing its scalability.
*   **Comparison to Model-Based RL:** The paper primarily compares to model-free RL methods. Including a comparison to more sophisticated model-based RL approaches could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that deeper VGG features (conv5\_3) performed worse. Do you have a hypothesis as to why this is, beyond the stated reason of spatial invariance? Could it be related to information loss in the deeper layers?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the method to the choice of hyperparameters for the bilinear dynamics model (e.g., number of layers, regularization)?
3.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of training the dynamics model and the Q-function in comparison to end-to-end training of a comparable deep reinforcement learning agent?
4.  What are the limitations of using the linear approximation of the Q-function? How might these limitations affect the performance of the method in more complex scenarios?
5.  Have you explored any techniques for mitigating the potential domain gap between the simulated environment and real-world scenarios?
