- Method: Combines learned visual features, a learned predictive bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning to perform visual servoing (target following) in feature space rather than pixels.
- Algorithmic contribution: Uses a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) approach to identify task-relevant features.
- Empirical result: Strong performance on a synthetic car-following benchmark, outperforming conventional methods and being more sample-efficient than standard deep RL.
- Decision: Reject — experiments lack breadth and robustness to demonstrate real-world applicability.
- Major weakness: Evaluation is limited to a single synthetic environment, reducing confidence in generalization to real robotics tasks.
- Missing comparisons: No evaluation against more recent sample-efficient RL methods (e.g., imitation learning, meta-learning, off-policy corrections).
- VGG modification concern: Replacing max-pooling with dilated convolutions is not empirically justified; no ablation showing benefit over original VGG.
- Attribution ambiguity: Unclear if gains come from learned dynamics/Q-function or from the altered feature representation.
- Dynamics model choice: Preference for locally connected bilinear model presented but lacks thorough empirical comparison with fully connected and convolutional variants.
- Ablation strengths: Some useful ablations (pixels, different VGG layers) are included, but more are needed to isolate contributions.
- Suggested experiment: Add evaluations in more realistic simulations or robotic setups with noisy sensors and realistic physics.
- Suggested comparisons: Include state-of-the-art, sample-efficient RL baselines for a fairer assessment.
- FQI analysis: Recommend sensitivity analysis for the regularization parameter (ν) in FQI.
- Clarify Equation 5: Explain motivation and provide theoretical or empirical justification for jointly solving for α and b.
- Extension: Discuss potential extension to 3D visual servoing tasks.
- Questions for authors: Why prefer locally connected bilinear over fully connected despite complexity? How sensitive is performance to ν? What's the theoretical/empirical rationale for solving α and b jointly? Have comparisons to recent sample-efficient RL been attempted? What are the approach’s limitations and future directions?
