## Review of "Learning Visual Servoing with Reinforcement Learning"

This paper presents a compelling approach to visual servoing by leveraging pre-trained deep features, a learned bilinear visual dynamics model, and reinforcement learning. The authors successfully demonstrate that a policy learned in this manner can effectively follow a target in a complex simulated environment, achieving substantial performance gains over existing methods, even those utilizing ground-truth bounding boxes. A particularly valuable contribution is the release of the CitySim3D environment, which will undoubtedly be a useful benchmark for future research in this area. The approach to learning feature weights using fitted Q-iteration is also well-motivated and shows good sample efficiency, a crucial aspect for practical applications. 

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The core idea is well-defined, the experiments are well-designed and executed, and the results clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The sample efficiency, in particular, is a significant strength.

The primary reason for acceptance is the clear demonstration of improved performance and sample efficiency compared to existing approaches. The ablation studies and comparisons to baseline methods are convincing and highlight the benefits of using pre-trained features and learning the visual dynamics. Secondly, the open-sourcing of the CitySim3D environment provides a valuable resource to the research community. 

However, there are areas for improvement. As the authors acknowledge, the experiments are limited to a synthetic environment. Evaluating the approach on more complex, diverse, and realistic visual conditions (introducing clutter, distractors with varying appearances and motion, and more 3D car models) would further strengthen the claims. Investigating end-to-end training or fine-tuning of the convolutional network could reveal potential benefits in representation learning and improve generalization. Finally, while the code is released, improving reproducibility by further streamlining the implementation and providing a more concise summary of the code structure within the paper would be beneficial. The minor typos on p.2 should also be addressed.

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors:

*   The paper mentions using a bilinear model due to its ability to approximate image dynamics. How sensitive is the performance to the accuracy of this approximation, and how does it degrade with more complex motions or significant changes in lighting?
*   The fitted Q-iteration is a key component. Could you elaborate on the choice of the regularization coefficient (ν) and how it impacts the learned policy? 
*   Regarding the open-source code, are there any specific dependencies or setup instructions that might be challenging for researchers unfamiliar with the codebase?
