## Review

This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing by combining learned visual features, a bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning via Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI). The key contribution lies in learning a policy to control a robot based on the features extracted from images, predicting future features given an action, and efficiently learning feature weights using a scaling-invariant optimization of the Q-function. The authors demonstrate improvements over traditional visual servoing methods and model-free deep reinforcement learning in a simulated car-following environment.

**Decision: Reject** with a strong encouragement to resubmit after substantial revisions.

**Supporting Arguments:**

While the paper presents a technically sound and novel approach, the empirical evaluation is insufficient to support the claims made. The experiments are limited to a small-scale, synthetic environment, lacking comparison to established benchmarks or real-world scenarios. This severely weakens the impact and generalizability of the findings. Furthermore, several reviewer requests from the original submission remain unaddressed, specifically concerning comparisons between different dynamics models (fully connected, convolutional, locally connected) and a detailed analysis of the FQI optimization procedure.

Specifically, the central claim of achieving “substantial improvement” over conventional approaches and “improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude” feels overstated given the scope of the experiments. The lack of results on more complex environments or comparisons to more sophisticated reinforcement learning algorithms calls these claims into question. The modification of the VGG network is also not convincingly validated; it is unclear whether this modification genuinely contributes to performance improvements or simply introduces another layer of complexity.

The proposed FQI optimization scheme is clever and addresses a practical issue within reinforcement learning, but its practical benefits are not fully explored. The comparison to other optimization methods, while present, doesn’t fully clarify the advantage of the proposed scaling invariance approach. A more detailed ablation study examining the impact of this scaling on performance would be beneficial.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Expand Experimental Evaluation:** The most critical improvement would be to evaluate the proposed method on more challenging and realistic environments. Consider using established robotics benchmarks or simulations with more complex dynamics and visual clutter.  A comparison to state-of-the-art reinforcement learning algorithms (e.g., PPO, SAC) on these benchmarks would strengthen the claims of sample efficiency.
* **Dynamics Model Comparison:** Thoroughly compare the performance of the fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected bilinear dynamics models. Provide quantitative results demonstrating the trade-offs between computational cost, expressiveness, and accuracy. The authors initially acknowledge this request but provide only limited results.
* **FQI Analysis:** Elaborate on the impact of the scaling invariance property of the Q-parameters. Conduct experiments comparing FQI to an iterative approach where the second term in Equation 5 is held fixed. This would clearly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed optimization scheme. Provide a visualization of the Q-function’s evolution during training.
* **VGG Modification Validation:** Provide a more rigorous evaluation of the modified VGG network.  Compare its performance to the original VGG network on the visual servoing task and potentially on other related tasks. Analyze whether the modifications genuinely improve the quality of the learned features.
* **Ablation Study:** Conduct an ablation study to understand the contribution of different components of the proposed method. For example, what happens when feature weighting is removed or when a simpler dynamics model is used?
* **Robustness Analysis:** Investigate the robustness of the method to variations in lighting, camera calibration, and target appearance.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. **Generalization:** Beyond the car-following scenario, what types of visual servoing tasks are best suited for this approach? Are there limitations in terms of the complexity of the environment or the target object?
2. **Scalability:** How does the computational cost of the method scale with the dimensionality of the feature space and the complexity of the dynamics model?
3. **FQI Intuition:** Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of why scaling the Q-parameters does not change the optimal policy? A simplified example might be helpful.
4. **VGG Layers:** What was the rationale behind selecting the VGG conv4_3 layer specifically for the best performance? Was a systematic search conducted across different layers?
5. **Real-World Deployment:** What challenges would need to be addressed to deploy this method on a real-world robotic platform?
