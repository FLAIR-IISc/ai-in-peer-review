This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing that combines learned visual features, a learned predictive dynamics model, and reinforcement learning to enable quick adaptation to new targets. The core idea is to move the camera in the space of learned visual features, rather than directly in pixel space, and to learn which features are most suitable for the task at hand using a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm. The authors demonstrate compelling results on a synthetic car following benchmark, achieving significant improvements over conventional and deep reinforcement learning baselines.

**Decision:** Accept with minor revisions.

**Reasons:** The paper tackles an important problem – the sample inefficiency and limited generalization ability of traditional visual servoing methods. The proposed approach is well-motivated, grounded in relevant literature, and offers a promising solution by leveraging the power of deep learning and reinforcement learning. The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating substantial performance gains over several strong baselines, and the ablation studies provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of different components of the proposed system. The availability of an open-source environment and code further strengthens the contribution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing visual servoing techniques, particularly their reliance on hand-designed features and analytical dynamics models. The motivation for using learned features and predictive dynamics is well-explained, drawing upon recent advances in deep learning and control theory. The proposed method is logically structured and builds upon existing RL algorithms (Fitted Q-Iteration) in a clever way to achieve sample efficiency. 

The experimental setup is well-defined, and the comparisons to baseline methods are thorough. The results consistently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach, both in terms of performance and sample efficiency. The fact that the method can learn an effective visual servo using only 20 training trajectory samples is particularly impressive. The ablation studies highlighting the benefits of different feature representations are insightful.  The comparison with TRPO and classical IBVS methods provides a strong validation of the proposed approach. The open-source release of the environment and code is a major asset, enabling further research and development in this area.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

While the paper is strong overall, several minor points could be addressed to further improve its clarity and impact.

*   **Reproducibility:** While code is available, the description of the experimental setup and hyperparameters could be more detailed to facilitate easier reproduction. A concise summary of the code organization would be helpful.
*   **Generalization:** The experiments are conducted in a synthetic environment. While this allows for controlled evaluation, it would be valuable to discuss the potential challenges of transferring the approach to real-world scenarios. Exploring more complex, diverse, and realistic visual conditions (e.g., more clutter, distractors, varied appearances, more 3D car models) would strengthen the findings.
*   **Representation Learning:** Consider exploring the potential benefits of end-to-end training or fine-tuning the convolutional network used for feature extraction. While pre-trained features work well, it's possible that further adaptation to the specific visual servoing task could lead to improved performance.
*   **Minor Typo:** There is a typo on page 2 ("CitySim3D1" should presumably be "CitySim3D").

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The bilinear model appears to be a key component of the approach. What is the sensitivity of the performance to the accuracy of this model? Have you investigated alternative dynamics models or ways to improve the accuracy of the bilinear model?
2.  The choice of the reward function is important for reinforcement learning. How was the cost function designed, and what impact does it have on the learned policy? Were any alternative reward functions considered?
3.  The paper mentions that the deepest VGG feature representation (conv5\_3) performed worse than others. Can you elaborate on why this might be the case? Is it related to the loss of spatial information or other factors?
4.  Could you provide more details on the computational resources required to train and run the proposed system? This would be helpful for researchers interested in implementing the approach.
5.  In the real world, lighting conditions and camera parameters can vary significantly. How robust is the proposed approach to these variations? Are there any techniques that could be used to improve robustness in real-world scenarios?
