## Review for ICLR 2017 Submission

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features, a learned predictive dynamics model, and reinforcement learning. The authors argue that manually designed features and analytical dynamics models limit generalization, and propose a method that learns to servo in the space of learned visual features, rather than directly on pixels. A key contribution is the use of a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn which features are most relevant for the task. The approach demonstrates strong results on a synthetic car following benchmark, achieving substantial improvement over conventional methods and significantly higher sample efficiency than standard deep reinforcement learning.

**Decision:**

**Reject.** While the paper presents a technically sound and novel approach, the experiments lack the robustness and breadth necessary to convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method in a meaningful context.  The reliance on a single synthetic benchmark, coupled with concerns about the modification of the VGG network and the lack of comparison to more recent RL algorithms, makes it difficult to assess the generalizability and impact of the work.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of combining learned feature representations with reinforcement learning for visual servoing is compelling. The authors effectively motivate their approach by highlighting the limitations of traditional methods. The proposed bilinear dynamics model and the use of FQI appear to be well-reasoned and contribute to the sample efficiency of the algorithm. The ablation studies comparing different feature representations (pixel intensities, different layers of VGG) are valuable. 

However, the primary weakness lies in the experimental validation. The car following benchmark, while complex, is ultimately a synthetic environment.  The results, while demonstrably better than the baselines, do not translate to a convincing argument for the method’s applicability to real-world robotics problems.  Crucially, the comparison to other reinforcement learning algorithms is limited. While the paper acknowledges the sample inefficiency of standard model-free deep RL, it doesn’t compare against more recent, sample-efficient algorithms (e.g., those employing imitation learning, meta-learning, or off-policy correction techniques) that have emerged since the cited works.

Furthermore, the modification of the VGG network (replacing max-pooling layers with dilated convolutions) is not adequately justified. The paper states this is to improve spatial invariance, but does not present any empirical evidence demonstrating that the modified network outperforms the original VGG network in the context of this specific task.  Without such a comparison, it's unclear whether the observed performance gains are due to the learned dynamics and Q-function, or simply the altered feature representation. 

Finally, the discussion of the bilinear dynamics model would benefit from a more detailed comparison between the fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected variants. While the authors present a justification for choosing the locally connected model, a more thorough empirical evaluation would strengthen their claim.



**Additional Feedback:**

The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology. However, the following points could improve the manuscript:

*   **Real-World Evaluation:** Consider including results from a simulation environment that more closely resembles a real-world scenario, such as a more realistic physics engine or a simulated robot with noisy sensors.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art RL:**  Include a comparison against more recent sample-efficient RL algorithms.
*   **VGG Network Ablation:** Conduct a controlled experiment to evaluate the performance of the original VGG network versus the modified version in the visual servoing task.
*   **Dynamics Model Comparison:** Provide a more detailed empirical analysis comparing the performance of the different dynamics model variants (fully connected, convolutional, locally connected).
*   **FQI Optimization:** The discussion of the FQI optimization could be enhanced by exploring the sensitivity of the algorithm to the regularization parameter (ν).
*   **Clarify Equation 5:**  Could you clarify the motivation for solving for α and *b* in Equation 5, and explain why this step improves the algorithm's performance?  Is there a theoretical justification for this approach beyond the observation that it speeds up optimization?
*   **Extension to 3D:** It may be valuable to briefly discuss the potential extension of this work to 3D visual servoing tasks.




**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why was the locally connected bilinear model preferred over the fully connected model despite the increased complexity of the latter? Were there any computational constraints that influenced this decision?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the algorithm to the choice of the regularization parameter (ν) in the FQI algorithm?  
3.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for solving for α and *b* jointly in Equation 5? Does this step have a specific theoretical justification, or is it purely an empirical optimization?
4.  Have you considered comparing your approach to more recent sample-efficient reinforcement learning algorithms, such as those employing imitation learning or meta-learning? If so, what were the results?
5.  What are the limitations of the proposed approach, and what are potential directions for future research to address these limitations?
