## Review of “Visual Servoing with Learned Visual Representations”

This paper tackles the important problem of visual servoing, moving beyond traditional methods reliant on hand-engineered features and analytical models. The authors propose a compelling approach that leverages pre-trained visual features (VGG) within an action-conditional bilinear model, coupled with a fitted Q-iteration algorithm for efficient policy learning. A key contribution is the introduction of a multi-scale, locally-connected variant of the bilinear model and a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm. The experimental results on a synthetic car following benchmark demonstrate the effectiveness of the value-weighted objective and the benefits of utilizing pre-trained visual features for generalization. 

I **accept** this paper, primarily due to its strong technical execution and demonstrated empirical success. The work presents a well-reasoned and effectively implemented approach to a challenging robotics problem, achieving significant improvements in sample efficiency compared to existing methods. The mathematical formulation is rigorous, and the experimental validation is convincing.

My positive assessment stems from the clear motivation and solid technical foundation of the work. The authors successfully integrate concepts from representation learning, dynamics modeling, and reinforcement learning in a novel and practical way. The comparison to existing methods, including classical IBVS and deep reinforcement learning, clearly highlights the advantages of their approach. The inclusion of ablation studies (varying feature representations) and the improvement over baseline methods (TRPO, CEM) further strengthens the claims made in the paper. The fact that good results are achieved with only 20 training samples is especially noteworthy.

However, as noted by the area chair, the paper's contribution to *representation learning* is somewhat limited. It relies heavily on pre-trained VGG features, rather than learning a representation end-to-end. While this is a pragmatic choice enabling sample efficiency, it’s a point to acknowledge. Additionally, the policy representation is fixed and value approximation is linear, which arguably shifts the focus more towards control than towards developing a fundamentally new representation learning technique. 

To further strengthen the paper, I suggest the authors address the following:

*   **Discussion of limitations:** Explicitly discuss the limitations of relying on pre-trained features, and potential avenues for end-to-end learning. How might the approach be extended to learn task-specific features alongside the dynamics model?
*   **Analysis of Feature Importance:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of which VGG layers/features are most crucial for performance. This could shed light on what aspects of the visual scene are most relevant for the task.
*   **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the algorithm to key hyperparameters (e.g., regularization coefficient ν) would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Cost:** A more detailed breakdown of the computational cost of the method, especially compared to alternatives, would be valuable.

Finally, I have a couple of clarifying questions for the authors:

*   The locally connected bilinear model (Equation 2) has a parameter `W(l)c,0`. What is the role of this parameter, and how does it differ from the other weights in the model?
*   In the ablation studies, you found that deeper VGG features (conv5_3) performed worse. Is this likely due to a loss of spatial information, or are there other factors at play? Could this be mitigated by incorporating attention mechanisms?
