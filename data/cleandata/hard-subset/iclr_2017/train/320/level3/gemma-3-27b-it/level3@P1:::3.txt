## Review for ICLR 2017 Submission

This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing, combining learned visual features from pre-trained convolutional neural networks, a bilinear predictive model of feature dynamics, and reinforcement learning to enable rapid adaptation to new targets. The authors tackle the challenge of learning effective visual servoing mechanisms with limited data, focusing on the task of target following. They demonstrate significant improvements over conventional approaches and standard deep reinforcement learning algorithms in a complex synthetic car following benchmark.

**Decision: Accept**

The core idea of leveraging pre-trained features and learning a dynamics model for efficient policy learning is compelling and well-executed. The empirical results are strong, clearly demonstrating the benefits of the proposed method over several baselines, and the substantial improvement in sample efficiency is particularly noteworthy.  The authors have provided a publicly available environment and code, promoting reproducibility and further research.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant limitation of traditional visual servoing methods – their reliance on manual feature engineering and precise dynamics models. The authors convincingly argue that learning these components can lead to more robust and adaptable systems. The problem of target following is a relevant and practical application of visual servoing, providing a strong testbed for the proposed approach.

The method is technically sound. The use of a bilinear model for feature dynamics is a reasonable choice, balancing accuracy and computational efficiency. The fitted Q-iteration algorithm effectively learns the weighting of different features, allowing the system to focus on the most relevant visual information for accurate target tracking. The linearization of the bilinear dynamics presented in the appendix simplifies the optimization process, making it computationally tractable.

The experimental results are robust and convincing. The performance gains over image-pixel based methods, hand-designed keypoints, and other reinforcement learning techniques are substantial. The comparisons to state-of-the-art trackers and purely learned policies further highlight the advantages of the proposed approach. The ablation studies (comparing different feature representations and optimization methods) provide valuable insights into the contributions of each component of the system. The reported performance on novel cars demonstrates a degree of generalization capability, although this aspect could be further explored.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, several areas could be addressed to further improve its impact.

*   **Generalization & Complexity:** While the authors demonstrate some generalization to novel cars, exploring more complex and diverse visual conditions (e.g., varying lighting, clutter, different car models, dynamic backgrounds) would strengthen the claims of robustness. Investigating the performance of the approach in more realistic simulated or real-world environments would also be valuable.
*   **End-to-End Learning:**  Exploring end-to-end training or fine-tuning of the pre-trained convolutional network could potentially lead to even better performance. Investigating whether the pre-trained features can be further adapted to the specific visual servoing task could reveal additional benefits of representation learning.
*   **Implementation Details & Reproducibility:** While code is available, providing a more concise summary of the implementation details within the paper itself would improve readability and reproducibility. Grouping common parameters and hyperparameters into a single table would be helpful.  Addressing the minor typos noted (e.g., on page 2) would enhance the overall polish of the paper.
*   **Cost Function Justification:**  A more detailed explanation of the rationale behind the specific cost function used in the reinforcement learning framework could be helpful.  How does this cost function effectively capture the desired behavior of the visual servo?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions limitations of the deepest VGG feature (conv5\_3) due to spatial invariance. Could you elaborate on potential solutions to mitigate this issue, such as incorporating spatial attention mechanisms or using alternative feature extraction techniques?
2.  The improvement in sample efficiency is a significant contribution. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of why the proposed method achieves this improvement compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning approaches? What specific aspects of the algorithm (e.g., Q-iteration, bilinear dynamics) contribute most to this efficiency?
3.  The experiments are conducted in a simulated environment. How do you anticipate the performance of the proposed method to be affected by the reality gap? Are there any specific techniques you would consider to bridge this gap and deploy the system in a real-world setting?
4.  Regarding the open-source code, could you clarify the dependencies and the level of effort required to reproduce the results? Providing a Dockerfile or similar would be highly beneficial.
