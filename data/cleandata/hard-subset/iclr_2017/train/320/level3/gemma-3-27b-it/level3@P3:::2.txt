This paper investigates visual servoing using learned visual representations, specifically focusing on target following. The authors propose an action-conditional bilinear model of visual features (extracted from a pre-trained VGG network) and derive a policy via linearized dynamics. A multi-scale, locally-connected variant of the bilinear model is introduced, and a weighted objective function incorporating long-term policy values is proposed to address the one-step predictive nature of the bilinear model. They introduce a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm for learning the weighting, and demonstrate the benefits of this approach through experiments on a synthetic car following benchmark. The work is exceptionally well-written, mathematically sound, and conceptually thorough. The experiments convincingly demonstrate the value of the value-weighted objective and the pre-trained visual features aid generalization.

**Decision: Accept**

The primary reason for acceptance is the significant contribution to sample-efficient reinforcement learning for robotic control. The paper demonstrates a compelling approach to visual servoing that drastically reduces the data requirements compared to standard deep RL methods, achieving strong performance with only 20 training samples. The introduction of the trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm is also a valuable contribution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper successfully tackles the problem of learning robust visual servoing policies with limited data. The proposed approach—leveraging pre-trained features, a bilinear dynamics model, and fitted Q-iteration—is well-motivated and effectively addresses the challenges of generalization and sample efficiency. The experimental results clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over both conventional image-based visual servoing and standard deep reinforcement learning techniques. The ablation studies effectively isolate the contributions of each component of the system. The thoroughness of the experiments, including comparisons to alternative methods and detailed analysis of feature representations, strengthens the validity of the findings. The mathematical formulation is rigorous and the derivations are clearly presented. The authors thoughtfully address potential limitations and provide comprehensive experimental details.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not impacting the decision):**

While the paper is strong, its primary focus on control rather than representation learning feels somewhat misaligned with the ICLR conference’s emphasis. The reliance on pre-trained VGG features, while pragmatic for achieving strong results, limits the contribution to representation learning itself. Exploring avenues for end-to-end learning or learning task-specific features alongside the dynamics model could strengthen the representation learning aspect.

Furthermore, the justification for the locally-connected bilinear model could be more deeply explored. While the authors mention sparsity, a more detailed explanation of why this structure is beneficial for visual servoing, beyond simply reducing parameters, would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **End-to-End Learning:** Have you explored initial attempts at end-to-end learning (e.g., fine-tuning the VGG features or learning a smaller feature extractor jointly with the dynamics and policy)? If so, what were the challenges encountered, and why did you ultimately opt for the pre-trained VGG approach?
2.  **Locally-Connected Bilinear Model:** Beyond parameter reduction, what specific properties of the visual servoing problem (e.g., local correlations in the feature space, translation invariance) motivated the choice of a locally-connected architecture for the bilinear model?
3.  **Generalization to More Complex Environments:** The experiments are conducted in a synthetic environment. What steps would be necessary to adapt this approach to real-world scenarios with more complex visual clutter, lighting conditions, and dynamics? Are there any anticipated challenges in transferring the learned dynamics model to a different camera or robot platform?
4. **Q-function Linearity:** The paper mentions the Q-function is approximated linearly. Could you elaborate on the limitations of this linear approximation, especially in more complex scenarios where non-linear relationships between features and long-term reward might exist?
