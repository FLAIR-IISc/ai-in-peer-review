This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing by combining learned visual features, a bilinear predictive dynamics model, and reinforcement learning, specifically Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI). The core idea is to learn a policy that servos the camera in the space of learned visual features, rather than raw pixels or hand-designed keypoints, with the goal of rapid adaptation to new targets using minimal data. The authors demonstrate promising results on a synthetic car following benchmark, showing improvements over conventional and model-free deep reinforcement learning approaches.

**Decision: Reject**

The paper presents a technically interesting approach with a solid theoretical foundation. However, the experimental evaluation is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the claimed advantages and justify acceptance into a competitive conference like ICLR. The reliance on a synthetic benchmark, combined with limited comparisons and insufficient ablation studies, raises concerns about the generalizability and practical impact of the proposed method.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Experimental Validation:** The primary evaluation is performed on a synthetic car following benchmark (CitySim3D). While the results show improvements over baselines, the lack of evaluation on real-world datasets or more established robotic benchmarks significantly weakens the claims of broader applicability. The synthetic environment, while controlled, may not accurately reflect the complexities of real-world visual servoing scenarios (e.g., lighting changes, sensor noise, imperfect dynamics).

2. **Insufficient Comparison to Baselines:** The comparison to other methods, particularly deep reinforcement learning approaches, feels incomplete. The authors demonstrate a significant sample efficiency improvement over "standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms," but fail to specify *which* algorithms were compared and with what hyperparameter settings. A more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art deep RL algorithms designed for continuous control (e.g., DDPG, TD3, SAC) is needed. Furthermore, the comparison to classical visual servoing methods (IBVS, PBVS) feels somewhat cursory, and the details of their implementation and tuning are not fully transparent.

3. **Unclear Benefit of Modified VGG:** The authors modify the VGG network architecture with dilated convolutions. While a rationale is provided, there is no experimental validation demonstrating whether this modification actually *improves* performance compared to using the original VGG network. It’s unclear if the improvement in results is due to the dilated convolutions or some other aspect of the pipeline.

4. **Locally Connected Dynamics Justification:** The authors argue for the use of a locally connected bilinear model, but the justification feels somewhat hand-wavy. While the authors state the full connected model is too large, a more systematic exploration comparing fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected models, with a clear analysis of their computational cost and performance tradeoffs, would strengthen the claim.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Real-World Evaluation:**  The most critical improvement would be to evaluate the method on a real-world robotic platform. This would demonstrate the robustness and practicality of the approach in the presence of real-world challenges.
* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more comprehensive ablation studies to isolate the contributions of different components of the pipeline. For example:
    * What is the performance gain from using pre-trained VGG features versus randomly initialized features?
    * How does the choice of bilinear model (fully connected vs. locally connected) affect performance?
    * What is the impact of the feature weighting scheme learned through FQI?
* **Detailed Baseline Comparisons:** Provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art deep RL algorithms, including hyperparameter tuning and statistical significance tests. Report the training time for each method.
* **Clarify FQI Implementation:** The optimization strategy within FQI is described as exploiting the scale invariance of Q parameters, but the iterative process is a bit opaque. Could the authors provide a more concrete explanation, and perhaps even compare performance against a more naive iterative approach where the target Q-function *is* updated at each step (i.e., not holding it fixed as in the proposed method)?
* **Code Release:** Ensure the code is readily available and well-documented to allow for reproducibility and further research.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you elaborate on the choices made for hyperparameter settings for the baseline deep RL algorithms?
* How does the performance of the method change when using the original, unmodified VGG network?
* What were the computational resources required to train the model and run the experiments?
* Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the computational benefits of the locally connected bilinear model compared to the fully connected approach, beyond just stating its reduced size?
* Do you have any plans to evaluate the method on a real robotic platform or a more established visual servoing benchmark?
