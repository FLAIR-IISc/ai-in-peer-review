This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing, combining pre-trained convolutional features, a bilinear one-step visual dynamics model, and reinforcement learning to achieve target-following behavior. The key contribution lies in learning a policy that operates in the feature space, leveraging the robustness of pre-trained features while efficiently adapting to new targets with limited data. The authors demonstrate strong performance in a simulated environment, surpassing several baselines, including methods utilizing ground-truth bounding boxes, and showcase impressive sample efficiency. The release of the CitySim3D environment is also a valuable contribution to the community.

**Accept**

The paper presents a compelling and well-executed approach to a challenging problem. The strong empirical results, coupled with a clear explanation of the methodology and a valuable contribution in the form of a benchmark environment, justify acceptance. The demonstrated sample efficiency is particularly noteworthy and positions this work as a significant step forward in applying deep learning to visual servoing.

The paper is logically structured and clearly written. The motivation for combining learned features, dynamics modeling, and RL is well-articulated, and the proposed method addresses the limitations of traditional visual servoing techniques. The experimental setup is thorough, and the comparisons with relevant baselines are convincing. The ablation studies and analysis of different feature representations provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The authors convincingly demonstrate the benefits of using pre-trained features and learning feature weights through fitted Q-iteration. The substantial improvement in sample efficiency over standard deep RL algorithms is particularly striking.

However, several points could further strengthen the paper:

*   **Generalization to More Complex Environments:** While the results in CitySim3D are promising, the environment appears relatively controlled. Evaluating the method in more complex, diverse, and realistic visual conditions – including greater clutter, more distractors, varied appearances and motion patterns, and a wider range of 3D car models – would significantly enhance the paper’s impact.
*   **End-to-End Training/Fine-tuning:** Investigating the possibility of end-to-end training or fine-tuning of the convolutional network, rather than relying solely on pre-trained features, could reveal additional benefits in terms of representation learning and generalization. Could the features be further tailored to the specific task of visual servoing?
*   **Reproducibility and Implementation Details:** Although the code is released, improving reproducibility by further regrouping and simplifying implementation details would be beneficial. Providing a more concise summary of the key implementation choices and dependencies within the paper itself would make it easier for other researchers to build upon this work.
*   **Minor Typographical Errors:** Addressing the typos noted on p.2 would improve the paper’s polish.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the choice of the bilinear dynamics model: What considerations led to the selection of this specific model over other potential dynamics models (e.g., recurrent neural networks)? Were alternative dynamics models explored, and if so, what were the results?
2.  Concerning the fitted Q-iteration: How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the regularization coefficient (ν)? Was a systematic hyperparameter search conducted to optimize this value, and if so, what were the findings?
3.  Regarding the generalizability: Have you observed any specific failure cases or limitations of the current approach in the simulated environment? What types of scenarios or visual conditions pose the greatest challenges for the system?
4.  On the release of CitySim3D: Are there plans to further expand the environment with more varied scenarios, objects, and sensor modalities?
