## Review of “Visual Servoing with Learned Dynamics and Fitted Q-Iteration”

This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing, combining learned visual features (from VGG networks), a bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning via Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI). The core idea of learning a dynamics model to predict future feature states, coupled with a per-channel weighting scheme for features learned through FQI, is a strong contribution. The paper demonstrates a significant improvement in sample efficiency compared to standard deep reinforcement learning methods on a synthetic car following benchmark. The introduction of a scaling invariance argument for simplifying the FQI optimization is also a clever and mathematically sound move.

**Reject.** While technically innovative and well-formulated, the paper’s reliance on a small-scale, synthetic environment significantly limits the impact of its claims. The lack of evaluation on real-world datasets or more complex simulation environments makes it difficult to assess the generalizability and robustness of the proposed method.

The primary weakness is the experimental validation. The results presented, while promising within the confines of the synthetic benchmark, are not convincing enough to demonstrate the practical utility of the approach. The modifications to the VGG network also lack thorough experimental justification – it’s unclear if these modifications actually improve performance over using the standard VGG architecture. Additionally, the comparison to existing methods feels incomplete; a clearer justification for the choice of baselines would strengthen the findings.

The authors should address the reviewer requests directly. Specifically, a more comprehensive comparison between fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected dynamics models is crucial. It is important to empirically validate the effect of the scaling argument, comparing FQI with an iterative approach that fixes the second term in Eq.5, to demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed optimization.

Further feedback:

*   **Clarify the choice of the reward function:** While mentioned briefly, a more detailed explanation of the cost function used in the reinforcement learning framework would be beneficial.
*   **Ablation studies:** Performing ablation studies to analyze the impact of each component (e.g., bilinear dynamics, feature weighting, FQI) would provide valuable insights.
*   **Robustness to noise:** Investigate the robustness of the approach to noise in the visual features or the action space.
*   **Computational complexity:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed method, particularly compared to other approaches.



I would like to know:

1.  How sensitive is the performance to the specific architecture modifications made to the VGG network? Was a systematic search performed, or were these changes made heuristically?
2.  What is the impact of the discount factor (γ) on the learning process and the final performance of the visual servo?
3.  Could the authors elaborate on the reasons why the deeper VGG features (conv5\_3) performed worse than the shallower ones? Is it simply a lack of spatial information, or are there other factors at play?
