## Review for ICLR 2017

This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features, a learned predictive dynamics model, and reinforcement learning. The authors aim to develop algorithms that can quickly adapt to new targets using only a small amount of training data. Their approach focuses on servoing the camera in the space of learned visual features, utilizing pre-trained deep features (VGG) in conjunction with a bilinear predictive model and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to determine the most relevant features for the task.

**Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper. The approach is well-motivated, grounded in existing literature, and presents a significant advancement in sample-efficient visual servoing. The results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of combining pre-trained features with learned dynamics and reinforcement learning, achieving substantial improvements over conventional methods and standard deep reinforcement learning approaches.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper clearly identifies a critical challenge in visual servoing – the need for manual feature engineering and domain-specific models, which hinders generalization. By leveraging pre-trained deep features, the authors elegantly circumvent this limitation. The use of a bilinear predictive model provides a computationally efficient way to estimate the effects of robot motion on perceived features, crucial for feedback control. 

The key contribution lies in the proposed fitted Q-iteration algorithm, which effectively learns a weighting scheme for the features, enabling the servo to focus on the most relevant visual cues. The demonstrated sample efficiency – achieving effective visual servoing with only 20 training trajectories – is a remarkable achievement, highlighting the power of the proposed approach. 

The experimental validation on a complex synthetic car following benchmark is robust. The authors perform a thorough comparison with several baselines, including approaches based on image pixels, hand-designed keypoints, and standard deep reinforcement learning, consistently demonstrating the superiority of their method. The ablation studies, comparing different feature representations and optimization methods, provide valuable insights into the performance trade-offs. Furthermore, the inclusion of videos showcasing the performance of the algorithm significantly enhances the review process.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where clarification and potential improvement could be considered:

* **Representation Learning Contribution:** As acknowledged in the paper, the contribution to representation learning itself is somewhat limited. The approach relies heavily on pre-trained VGG features. It would be beneficial to briefly discuss the potential for learning end-to-end, even if only conceptually, and why this was not pursued in this work.  Is the performance limited by the pre-trained VGG features themselves?
* **Linearity of Q-function:** The authors emphasize the benefits of the linear Q-function approximation for computational efficiency. However, it’s worth discussing the potential limitations of this linearity, particularly in complex scenarios. Could non-linear function approximators potentially lead to further improvements, and if so, at what cost in terms of sample efficiency or computational complexity?
* **Generalization to Real-World Data:** The experiments are conducted in a synthetic environment. While the results are compelling, it would be valuable to discuss the potential challenges and considerations for transferring this approach to real-world scenarios, such as dealing with sensor noise, lighting variations, and more complex dynamics.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief discussion regarding the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters (e.g., learning rates, regularization coefficients) would be useful. This would provide insights into the robustness of the approach.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of evaluating the bilinear dynamics model, particularly as the dimensionality of the feature space increases? Is there a trade-off between the complexity of the bilinear model and the real-time performance of the visual servo?
2.  You mention that deeper VGG features (conv5_3) performed worse in your experiments. Can you speculate on the reasons for this, beyond the lack of spatial information? Could this be related to the semantic content of those layers being too abstract for precise control?
3.  The fitted Q-iteration algorithm seems crucial to the success of this approach. What alternative Q-learning algorithms were considered, and why was fitted Q-iteration chosen over them? What are the key benefits of fitted Q-iteration in this specific context?
4.  In the case of real-world deployment, would you anticipate needing to fine-tune the pre-trained VGG features on a dataset specific to the target domain? If so, how much data would be required?
