

# Review for ICLR 2017 Paper

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a compelling contribution to robotics by addressing the challenge of visual servoing with minimal data requirements. The authors propose a novel approach that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG), a bilinear predictive model of visual dynamics, and a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm to learn effective visual servoing mechanisms. The key innovation lies in using a weighted objective that incorporates long-term policy values, rather than relying on one-step prediction alone. The authors demonstrate that their method can learn an effective visual servo on a complex synthetic car-following benchmark using just 20 training trajectory samples, showing substantial improvements over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and achieving more than two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency than standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The paper also introduces a multi-scale, multi-channel, locally-connected variant of the bilinear model that enables efficient computation while maintaining robustness to visual variations, occlusions, and changes in viewing angle.

## 2. Decision

Accept. This paper makes a significant contribution to robotics research by demonstrating a practical approach to visual servoing that is both sample-efficient and robust to real-world visual variations. The method's ability to learn effective visual servoing mechanisms with minimal data is particularly valuable for real-world applications where data collection is expensive.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The problem statement clearly identifies the limitations of existing visual servoing approaches that rely on manually designed features and analytical dynamics models, which require extensive application-specific engineering and lack generalization capability. The authors provide a compelling rationale for using pre-trained features from large-scale classification models, supported by empirical evidence from their experiments.

The methodology is mathematically sound and conceptually exhaustive. The bilinear dynamics model is well-justified and efficiently implemented, with the locally-connected variant addressing computational constraints while maintaining performance. The proposed trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm is carefully derived and shown to be effective through rigorous experimentation. The paper's experimental evaluation is thorough, with multiple baselines (including classical image-based visual servoing with hand-designed features, end-to-end deep reinforcement learning, and pixel-based approaches), ablation studies, and careful validation on both seen and novel cars to demonstrate generalization.

The results strongly support the claims, with clear evidence that the proposed method outperforms alternatives in terms of both performance and sample efficiency. The paper demonstrates that pre-trained visual features empirically aid generalization, which is a valuable insight for robotics researchers working on vision-based control.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, the authors could:

1. Clarify the relationship between the paper's contribution (to control) and its limited contribution to representation learning. While the paper focuses on control, explicitly acknowledging the representation learning limitations would better position the work within the ICLR community.

2. Provide more detailed analysis of the computational efficiency beyond the 16Hz vs 1Hz comparison, including training time and resource requirements for different methods.

3. Include a discussion of the approach's limitations, particularly regarding scenarios where ImageNet features might not be relevant (e.g., non-photorealistic environments or domains with different visual characteristics).

Questions for the authors:

1. The paper claims to introduce a "trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm" as "the first." Could you clarify the specific novelty compared to existing fitted-Q iteration methods in the literature?

2. The results show that VGG conv4_3 features work best, but deeper features (conv5_3) perform worse. Could you provide more insight into why deeper features might be less suitable for visual servoing, and whether there are specific properties of conv4_3 that make it ideal for this task?

3. The paper uses a bilinear model for visual dynamics, which is motivated by prior work. Could you elaborate on why a bilinear model is particularly suitable for visual servoing compared to other predictive models, and whether there are theoretical guarantees for this choice?
