- Novel approach combining learned visual features, predictive bilinear dynamics, and reinforcement learning for visual servoing.
- Method operates in the space of pre-trained visual features (VGG) rather than pixels or hand-designed keypoints.
- Uses a bilinear predictive model to learn effective dynamics in feature space.
- Employs a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration to identify which visual features are task-relevant.
- Robust to visual variations, viewing-angle changes, appearance shifts, and occlusions.
- Demonstrated on a complex synthetic car-following benchmark with strong empirical results.
- Learns an effective servo with only 20 RL training trajectories, showing >100x sample-efficiency over model-free deep RL.
- Substantial improvement over pixel-based and hand-crafted feature baselines.
- Theoretical foundation for bilinear dynamics is sound and well-justified.
- Experiments are carefully designed, with appropriate baseline comparisons and comprehensive evaluation.
- Well-written paper with clear mathematical formulation and multi-scale, multi-channel feature motivation.
- Main critique: limited contribution to representation learning since features are pre-trained (not learned end-to-end).
- Suggested clarification: relate and distinguish the value-based approach from recent deep learning visual servoing and end-to-end policy learning.
- Requested intuition: why VGG conv4_3 features work best and why deeper features (conv5_3) underperform.
- Open questions: whether fitted Q-iteration can handle more nonlinear dynamics or if bilinear models suffice for complex tasks.
- Requested experiments: test on more dramatic target appearance changes and scenes with multiple distracting objects.
- Recommendation: accept — valuable, practically relevant contribution reducing data requirements for visual servoing.
