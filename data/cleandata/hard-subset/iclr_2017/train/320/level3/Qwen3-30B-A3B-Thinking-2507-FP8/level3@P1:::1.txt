

This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing that combines learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to enable quick adaptation to new targets with minimal training data. The authors propose using pre-trained VGG features for visual representation and a bilinear dynamics model to predict how feature values change with robot actions. The key innovation is a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm that learns per-channel weights for the features, allowing the system to automatically determine which features are most relevant for the task at hand. The authors demonstrate that their method can learn an effective visual servo on a synthetic car-following benchmark using only 20 training trajectory samples, showing substantial improvement over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and achieving more than two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency than standard model-free deep reinforcement learning.

I recommend rejection. While the technical approach is well-motivated and the formulation of the weighted visual servoing problem with Q-learning is elegant, the experimental evaluation fails to sufficiently support the paper's claims. The synthetic car-following benchmark is limited in scope and lacks real-world validation, which is particularly concerning given the paper's focus on enabling quick adaptation to new targets in practical scenarios. The authors do not compare their locally connected bilinear model against fully connected or convolutional alternatives in sufficient detail, making it difficult to assess the claimed advantages of their specific architectural choice. Furthermore, the modification of the VGG network (removing max-pooling layers and using dilated convolutions) lacks experimental validation showing it outperforms the original VGG architecture on the task.

The paper's claim of "substantial improvement" over conventional approaches is not sufficiently substantiated. The comparison to classical visual servoing methods (e.g., IBVS with ORB features) is limited to a synthetic environment, and the paper does not demonstrate generalization to real-world robotics tasks or diverse environments. The sample-efficiency claim is based on the synthetic benchmark only, without evidence that the approach would scale to more complex real-world scenarios where visual variation is more extreme.

The theoretical contribution is solid, particularly the insight that scaling Q-parameters doesn't change the optimal policy, which enables the efficient FQI implementation. However, the experimental section fails to provide the necessary evidence to support the paper's broader claims about applicability to real-world visual servoing tasks. The authors should have included more diverse benchmarks, including real-world experiments, to validate their claims.

To improve the paper, I recommend the following:
1. Provide a more thorough experimental comparison between the locally connected bilinear model, fully connected model, and convolutional alternatives, including ablation studies on the impact of the VGG modification.
2. Extend the experiments to include real-world robotics tasks or at least more challenging synthetic environments with greater visual variation.
3. Include a comparison against more recent model-based reinforcement learning methods that might offer better sample efficiency.
4. Provide a clearer justification for why the modified VGG architecture is necessary for visual servoing, including performance comparisons with the original VGG network.

I would like to ask the authors to clarify the following:
1. How would your approach perform on a more diverse set of real-world visual servoing tasks, such as object manipulation or navigation in cluttered environments?
2. Can you provide additional experimental evidence comparing the locally connected bilinear model against the fully connected alternative, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and performance on the car-following task?
3. How does your method handle the case where the target object is partially occluded during the servoing process, and what is the performance degradation compared to when the target is fully visible?
