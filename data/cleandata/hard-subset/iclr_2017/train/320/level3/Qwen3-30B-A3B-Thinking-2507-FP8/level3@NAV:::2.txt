

**Review**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of visual servoing in robotics, proposing a novel approach that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG), a bilinear predictive model of visual dynamics, and reinforcement learning to enable efficient learning of visual servoing mechanisms. The authors demonstrate that using pre-trained features with a value-weighted objective significantly improves performance and sample efficiency compared to conventional methods, requiring only 20 training trajectories for effective learning on a complex car-following benchmark. The method incorporates a multi-scale, locally-connected bilinear dynamics model and introduces a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm for learning feature weights that optimize long-term policy values.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a valuable contribution to robotics by providing a practical, sample-efficient solution to visual servoing that generalizes well to new targets with minimal retraining. The technical approach is well-motivated, with strong empirical validation showing more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample efficiency over standard model-free RL algorithms.

The paper's strength lies in its clear problem formulation, solid mathematical foundation, and thorough experimental validation. The authors effectively demonstrate that pre-trained visual features, when combined with their proposed value-weighted objective, enable robust visual servoing that handles visual variation, viewpoint changes, and occlusions. The fitted-Q iteration algorithm is well-designed and empirically validated, showing clear advantages over alternatives like TRPO.

However, I note that the contribution to representation learning is limited, as the approach relies entirely on pre-trained VGG features without learning the representation end-to-end. While this is a reasonable practical choice, the paper could better clarify why end-to-end learning of the visual representation is not pursued and how this limitation might affect future extensions. The focus on control rather than representation learning makes the work more relevant to robotics communities than to representation learning-focused venues like ICLR.

For improvement, the authors should more explicitly discuss the limitations of their approach regarding representation learning and consider addressing how their method might integrate with more recent end-to-end visual representation techniques. Additionally, a more detailed comparison with recent work on visual representation learning would strengthen the positioning of the paper.
