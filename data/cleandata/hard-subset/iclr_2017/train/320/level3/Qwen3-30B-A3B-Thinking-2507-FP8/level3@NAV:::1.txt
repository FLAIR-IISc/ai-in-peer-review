

This paper proposes an innovative approach to visual servoing by combining pre-trained visual features (from VGG), a bilinear predictive dynamics model, and reinforcement learning to learn effective visual servoing mechanisms with minimal data. The authors introduce a bilinear dynamics model for predicting next-frame features, formulate visual servoing with a Q-function that learns per-feature-channel weights, and present a novel optimization scheme for the Bellman error that exploits scale-invariance of Q-parameters. The paper demonstrates that their method can learn effective visual servoing on a synthetic car-following benchmark using only 20 training trajectory samples, showing substantial improvement over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints.

The paper tackles the specific problem of learning visual servoing mechanisms that can quickly adapt to new targets with minimal data, which is a meaningful problem in robotics. The approach is well motivated and well-placed in the literature, building on prior work in visual servoing and reinforcement learning while addressing key limitations of existing methods.

However, the paper fails to adequately support its claims. The experimental evaluation is limited to a small-scale, synthetic environment without domain benchmarks. The authors claim "improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude" but provide no comparison to standard model-free deep RL algorithms in a meaningful setting. Crucially, the paper lacks thorough experimental comparison between fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected dynamics models, which is directly addressed in the key points. The modification of VGG is not validated against the original network, and the paper does not compare FQI to an iterative approach that holds the second term in Eq.5 fixed.

I recommend rejection. While the technical formulation is novel and well-motivated, the insufficient experimental validation undermines the paper's claims. The small-scale simulations and lack of domain benchmarks fail to provide convincing evidence that the method generalizes to meaningful visual servoing tasks. The paper requires significantly stronger empirical validation to support its ambitious claims.

Additional feedback: The authors should conduct comprehensive experiments comparing all three dynamics model variants (fully connected, convolutional, locally connected) across multiple metrics. They should also provide a thorough comparison of FQI against an iterative approach that fixes the second term in Eq.5. Most importantly, they should demonstrate their method on more challenging visual servoing tasks with real-world relevance, not just a synthetic car-following benchmark. The modified VGG network needs to be validated against the original to ensure it's not simply a confounding factor in their results.
