

# Review of "Visual Servoing with Learned Feature Dynamics and Reinforcement Learning" (ICLR 2017)

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The authors propose a bilinear dynamics model to predict next-frame features from current frame and action, which enables effective visual servoing in the space of learned features rather than image pixels or hand-designed keypoints. A key contribution is the formulation of visual servoing as a Q-learning problem with per-feature-channel weights, allowing the algorithm to automatically identify which features are most relevant for the task. The authors introduce an elegant optimization scheme for the Bellman error by exploiting the scale-invariance of Q-parameters, enabling a computationally efficient FQI approach. The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness on a synthetic car-following benchmark, claiming a sample-efficiency improvement of more than two orders of magnitude over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes a technically novel and well-formulated contribution, but the experimental evaluation is insufficient to support the strong claims made.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core technical contributions are strong and well-motivated. The bilinear dynamics model, particularly the locally connected variant, provides an elegant balance between computational efficiency and expressive power. The insight that scaling Q-parameters doesn't change the optimal policy is clever and leads to a computationally efficient optimization scheme. The demonstration that per-channel weighting outperforms uniform weighting is well-supported by the experiments.

However, the experimental evaluation has several critical limitations that undermine the paper's claims. The evaluation is limited to a small-scale, synthetic car-following benchmark with only 5 training cars, which lacks the complexity and diversity of real-world visual servoing tasks. The paper claims "substantial improvement" and "more than two orders of magnitude" sample-efficiency gains, but the baselines are not sufficiently robust or comprehensive. The comparison to prior methods consists primarily of hand-designed feature-based approaches and a single end-to-end CNN policy, without more standard RL baselines like DDPG or SAC.

The paper also fails to adequately validate the modified VGG network. The authors state they "remove some of the max-pooling layers and replace the convolutions that followed them with dilated convolutions," but provide no evidence that this modification is beneficial compared to the original VGG network for the visual servoing task. The claim that "standard deep features... can be used together with a bilinear predictive model to learn an effective visual servo" requires stronger empirical validation.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct more comprehensive experiments comparing fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected dynamics models. The current paper only briefly mentions the locally connected variant as preferable without providing sufficient evidence through direct comparisons.

2. Address the reviewer's request to compare FQI with an iterative approach that holds the second term in Equation 5 fixed. This would clarify whether the authors' optimization scheme provides a genuine advantage.

3. Expand the experimental evaluation beyond the synthetic car-following benchmark. Include more challenging visual servoing tasks, ideally with real-world data, to demonstrate the approach's generalizability.

4. Provide proper validation of the modified VGG network. Show that the modified version outperforms the original VGG network on the visual servoing task, or explain why the modifications are necessary.

5. Include a more detailed analysis of the sample efficiency claims. Show how the 20 training trajectory samples translate to real-world data requirements, and provide comparisons with more standard RL baselines.

The authors' technical approach is sound and innovative, but the experimental evaluation is too limited to justify the strong claims made in the paper. With more substantial empirical validation, this could be a strong paper. As it stands, the evidence supporting the key claims is insufficient to warrant acceptance at ICLR.
