

# Review for ICLR_2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a compelling approach to visual servoing that effectively combines learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The authors tackle the problem of learning visual servoing mechanisms that can quickly adapt to new targets with minimal data, addressing a key limitation in traditional visual servoing approaches that rely on manually designed features and analytical dynamics models. The core contribution is a method that uses pre-trained visual features (from VGG network) in the space of learned visual features rather than image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, combined with a bilinear predictive model and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning approach to learn which features are best suited for the task. The paper demonstrates that this approach can learn an effective visual servo on a complex synthetic car following benchmark using just 20 training trajectory samples, showing substantial improvement over conventional approaches and more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample-efficiency over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature, with a thorough review of related work that clearly positions the contribution. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of both visual servoing and reinforcement learning communities, and their approach bridges these areas effectively. The theoretical foundation is solid, with a clear mathematical formulation of the problem and the proposed solution. The bilinear dynamics model and the fitted Q-iteration approach for learning feature weights are well-justified and novel within the context of visual servoing.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed, with appropriate baselines including conventional pixel-based approaches, hand-designed keypoint methods, and other reinforcement learning methods. The results consistently show the advantages of the proposed approach across different feature representations and on both seen and novel targets. The sample efficiency claim is well-supported by the experiments, with the paper demonstrating that the approach requires only 20 training trajectories compared to the thousands typically needed by standard deep reinforcement learning methods.

The paper is well-written, mathematically rigorous, and conceptually exhaustive. The methodological contributions, particularly the trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm, are clearly presented and well-justified.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where the authors could improve clarity and impact:

1. The paper's contribution to representation learning is somewhat limited since it relies on pre-trained VGG features without learning them end-to-end. The authors could more clearly articulate the boundary between their contribution (to visual servoing control) and the representation learning component, which is borrowed from prior work.

2. The authors might consider a more detailed discussion of when their approach might fail compared to alternative methods. For instance, how would the approach perform with targets that have very different appearance characteristics than those in the ImageNet training set?

3. The paper could benefit from a clearer comparison of the computational efficiency of the proposed approach versus the baselines, particularly the tracker-based IBVS method, beyond just the execution speed. The paper mentions the C-COT tracker runs at about 1Hz while their method runs at 16Hz, but a more comprehensive analysis of computational requirements would strengthen the practical implications.

4. The paper mentions that deeper VGG features generally achieve better performance, but the deepest feature representation (conv5_3) performs worse. A more detailed analysis of why this occurs would be valuable for the community.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that the approach uses "standard deep features, in our case taken from a model trained for object classification." How sensitive is the performance to the choice of pre-trained network (e.g., ResNet, Inception) or the specific layer used for feature extraction? Would there be a benefit to fine-tuning the feature extractor for the visual servoing task?

2. The authors claim to achieve "improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms." How would this comparison change if the baseline algorithms were optimized for the specific visual servoing task (e.g., using domain-specific feature engineering) rather than using standard implementations?

3. The paper uses a fixed feature representation (VGG) throughout the experiments. How would the approach perform if the feature representation itself were learned end-to-end with the dynamics model and policy, rather than relying on pre-trained features? Would the gains in sample efficiency still be observed, or would the need for more data to learn the features outweigh the benefits?
