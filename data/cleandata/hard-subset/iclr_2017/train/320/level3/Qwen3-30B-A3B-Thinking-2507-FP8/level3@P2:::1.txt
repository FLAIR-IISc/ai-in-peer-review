

# Review for "Learning Visual Servoing with Deep Features and Predictive Models"

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing that combines learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to enable rapid adaptation to new targets with minimal data. The authors propose a method that uses pre-trained VGG features (from ImageNet classification) as visual representations, learns a bilinear dynamics model to predict how these features change with robot actions, and employs a sample-efficient reinforcement learning algorithm to learn optimal weights for different visual features. The key innovation is the formulation of a weighted visual servoing policy where the weights are learned via a specialized fitted Q-iteration algorithm that exploits the scale-invariance of Q-parameters to enable efficient learning. The authors demonstrate their method achieves substantial performance improvements over conventional approaches (using image pixels or hand-designed keypoints) and shows an improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms, requiring only 20 training trajectory samples.

## 2. Decision

I recommend rejection.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated approach to visual servoing. The core idea of using learned visual features with a bilinear dynamics model for servoing is novel and well-positioned within the literature. The theoretical foundation for the Q-function approximation and the FQI optimization scheme that exploits scale-invariance of Q-parameters is elegant and well-justified. The authors provide thorough ablation studies showing that per-channel weighting of features is significantly more effective than equal weighting, and they demonstrate clear improvements over conventional methods.

However, the experimental evaluation is insufficient to support the strong claims made in the paper. The experiments are conducted in a synthetic, small-scale car-following benchmark with limited complexity, and the paper lacks evaluation on real-world robotics tasks or more challenging domain benchmarks. The claim of "substantial improvement" and "sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude" is not convincingly demonstrated in a realistic setting. The paper does not provide comparison to state-of-the-art visual servoing methods in more complex environments or on real robot hardware, which is essential for establishing the broader impact of the approach. Without this, the results remain confined to a toy simulation that may not generalize to real-world applications.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct experiments in more complex environments, including real-world robotics tasks or more challenging simulated scenarios that better represent the complexity of real visual servoing applications.

2. Provide a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art visual servoing methods, particularly those that use deep learning approaches for visual servoing.

3. Include ablation studies comparing the performance of the fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected bilinear dynamics models in more detail, as suggested in the key points. The current paper's argument for preferring locally connected dynamics is not sufficiently supported by experiments.

4. Validate whether the modified VGG network (with removed max-pooling layers and dilated convolutions) is indeed necessary for good performance, or if standard VGG features would suffice. The paper claims the modification is "to remove spatial invariance," but this needs experimental verification.

5. Include more analysis of the computational efficiency of the proposed method compared to alternatives, particularly the tracker-based method they compare to.

## 5. Clarification Questions

1. The paper claims "a substantial improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms." Could the authors provide a more precise comparison (e.g., exact number of samples required by their method vs. standard methods) and clarify whether this comparison is based on absolute performance or relative performance?

2. The paper states that "the features learned by large convolutional neural networks on large image datasets, such as ImageNet classification, tend to be useful for a wide range of other visual tasks." Could the authors provide additional experiments or analysis showing that the specific VGG features they use (with their modifications) are indeed more suitable for visual servoing than standard VGG features without modifications?

3. The paper uses a bilinear model for dynamics prediction. Could the authors clarify why a locally connected bilinear model is preferred over a fully connected model (which would be more expressive) given the computational constraints mentioned? A direct comparison of the performance of these two variants would strengthen the paper.

4. The paper states that the FQI algorithm "often achieved most of its performance gain after the first iteration." Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the learning curves (e.g., showing how performance evolves across iterations) to better understand the convergence properties of their approach?
