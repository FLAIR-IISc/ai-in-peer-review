

This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing that combines learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to enable quick adaptation to new targets with minimal data. The authors propose a method that operates in the space of learned visual features rather than image pixels or manually-designed keypoints, using pre-trained VGG features (from ImageNet classification) along with a bilinear predictive model to learn effective visual servoing. A key innovation is the use of a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn which visual features are most relevant for the task, allowing the system to robustly handle visual variations, changes in viewing angle, appearance, and occlusions. The authors demonstrate their approach on a complex synthetic car-following benchmark, learning an effective visual servo using only 20 training trajectory samples for reinforcement learning, showing substantial improvement over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and an improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The primary reasons for acceptance are the paper's strong empirical results demonstrating significant sample-efficiency gains over prior methods and the well-motivated integration of learned visual features with predictive dynamics and reinforcement learning in a novel way that addresses the limitations of standard visual servoing approaches.

The paper provides a thorough and well-structured contribution to the field of visual servoing. The theoretical foundation with bilinear dynamics is sound and well-justified, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how their method leverages the properties of pre-trained visual features for visual servoing. The experiments are carefully designed and executed, with appropriate comparisons to relevant baselines including classical visual servoing methods, end-to-end deep reinforcement learning, and hand-crafted feature approaches. The results consistently demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach, particularly in terms of sample efficiency and robustness to visual variations.

The paper is well-written with clear mathematical formulations and a comprehensive experimental evaluation. The authors effectively demonstrate that standard deep features from classification models can be effectively repurposed for visual servoing when combined with the right learning framework. The multi-scale, multi-channel approach to visual features is particularly well-motivated and contributes to the method's robustness.

The main critique I have is that the contribution to representation learning is somewhat limited, as the method relies heavily on pre-trained VGG features without learning them end-to-end. However, this limitation is well-justified by the authors' empirical findings that pre-trained features provide better generalization than learning features from scratch, and the paper's focus is appropriately on the control aspect rather than representation learning. The method's novelty lies in the integration of these pre-trained features with predictive dynamics and reinforcement learning, which is clearly articulated.

For additional improvement, the authors should clarify the relationship between their approach and recent work on visual servoing with deep learning, particularly the distinction between their value-based approach and end-to-end policy learning. It would also be helpful to provide more intuition about why the VGG conv4_3 features work best and why deeper features like conv5_3 underperform.

I would like to ask the authors to clarify whether their fitted Q-iteration algorithm could be extended to handle non-linear dynamics more effectively, or whether the bilinear model is sufficiently expressive for more complex visual servoing tasks. Additionally, it would be valuable to see how the method performs when the target object undergoes more dramatic appearance changes or when multiple objects are present in the scene that could serve as distractors.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to robotics research, particularly for visual servoing applications where rapid adaptation to new targets is crucial. The approach is both theoretically sound and empirically validated, with clear practical implications for reducing the data requirements of visual servoing systems. The method's efficiency and robustness make it particularly appealing for real-world robotic applications.
