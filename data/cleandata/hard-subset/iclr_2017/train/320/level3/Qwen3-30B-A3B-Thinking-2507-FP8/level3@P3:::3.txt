

# Review for ICLR 2017 Paper

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper presents an innovative approach to visual servoing that leverages pre-trained deep features, bilinear visual dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to learn target-following visual servoing mechanisms. The authors propose servoing directly in the space of learned visual features rather than image pixels or manually-designed keypoints, which enables robustness to visual variations, viewpoint changes, and occlusions. The key contributions include: (1) demonstrating that standard deep features (from ImageNet classification) can be effectively used for visual servoing when combined with a bilinear predictive model; (2) showing substantial performance gains over multiple baselines, including methods using ground-truth bounding boxes; (3) introducing a principled learning method for multi-scale visual feature weights via efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration; (4) achieving remarkable sample efficiency (learning with just 20 training trajectory samples); and (5) releasing an open-source virtual city environment for benchmarking visual servoing. The paper makes a strong case for the utility of pre-trained deep features in visual servoing, which is a significant contribution to the field.

## 2. Decision

Accept (with minor revisions)

## 3. Supporting Arguments

This paper addresses a meaningful problem in robotics: developing visual servoing mechanisms that can quickly adapt to new targets with minimal data. The approach is well-motivated by the limitations of traditional methods that rely on manually designed features and analytical dynamics models, which prevent exploitation of statistical regularities in the world and require extensive application-specific engineering.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous and well-supported. The paper demonstrates substantial improvements over multiple baselines (including methods using ground-truth bounding boxes) with clear performance metrics. The sample efficiency claim (more than two orders of magnitude better than standard model-free deep RL) is convincingly demonstrated through careful experimental design, with appropriate baselines and multiple evaluation metrics. The controlled simulation experiments effectively quantify the usefulness of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing, which is a significant contribution to the field.

The method is well-placed in the literature, building upon recent advances in deep learning and reinforcement learning while addressing a specific limitation of visual servoing approaches. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from prior work in both methodology and empirical results.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To further strengthen this already strong paper, I suggest the following improvements:

1. **Evaluation on More Complex Visual Conditions**: The current experiments are conducted in a synthetic city environment with a limited set of car models. Evaluating the method on more complex, diverse, and realistic visual conditions (including greater clutter, distractors, varied appearances/motion, and more 3D car models) would strengthen the generalization claims and provide a more comprehensive assessment of the approach's robustness.

2. **End-to-End Training Investigation**: The paper states that the VGG features are fixed, but it would be valuable to investigate whether end-to-end training/fine-tuning of the convolutional network could yield further improvements, potentially improving generalization to novel targets.

3. **Reproducibility Improvements**: The paper mentions open-source code but could significantly improve reproducibility by regrouping/simplifying implementation details and providing a more concise summary of the implementation in the main text. A clear README file with step-by-step instructions would be particularly helpful.

4. **Typos**: Please fix the typos mentioned on page 2.

5. **Discussion of Limitations**: A brief discussion of potential limitations in real-world deployment (e.g., domain shift from simulation to reality, sensitivity to camera calibration errors) would provide a more balanced perspective.

## Questions for Authors

1. Could you provide more details about the specific car models used in the experiments? How diverse are they in terms of appearance, size, and shape?

2. In the ablation study (Section 6.2), why does the VGG conv5_3 feature representation perform worse than shallower features? Could this be due to excessive spatial invariance, as hypothesized, or might there be other factors?

3. The paper mentions that the FQI algorithm often achieves most of its performance gain after the first iteration. Could you elaborate on why additional sampling iterations provide diminishing returns?

4. The authors claim "substantial improvement over a conventional approach based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints" but don't provide specific quantitative comparisons to the best hand-designed feature approach in the literature. Could you clarify these comparisons?

This paper is interesting and solid despite the experimental limits noted, and the authors can likely address the suggested improvements in the final version. The work makes a meaningful contribution to the field of visual servoing, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to the ICLR 2017 proceedings.
