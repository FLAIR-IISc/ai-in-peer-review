

**Review for ICLR 2017**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained deep features, bilinear predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to learn target-following behaviors with minimal data. The authors propose a method that operates in the space of learned visual features rather than image pixels or manually designed keypoints. Key contributions include: (1) demonstrating the usefulness of standard deep features (from ImageNet classification models) for visual servoing; (2) introducing a bilinear dynamics model to predict feature changes; (3) developing a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn optimal feature weights for handling distractors; and (4) showing substantial performance improvements over multiple baselines with 20 training trajectories (a two-orders-of-magnitude sample efficiency gain over standard model-free RL).

**2. Decision**

Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to visual servoing with a well-motivated approach that is scientifically rigorous and empirically validated.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper successfully addresses a meaningful problem in robotics: the limitation of traditional visual servoing methods that rely on manual feature engineering. The authors provide a compelling case for using pre-trained deep features, supported by thorough experiments demonstrating their effectiveness. Their key innovation—using a bilinear dynamics model combined with a sample-efficient Q-iteration algorithm to learn feature weights—enables robust visual servoing that handles visual variations, viewpoint changes, and occlusions.

The experimental validation is particularly strong. The authors compare against multiple sensible baselines, including: (a) pixel-based approaches; (b) hand-designed features (ORB, SIFT); (c) end-to-end CNN policies; and (d) classical visual servoing methods using ground-truth bounding boxes. Their method consistently outperforms all baselines, with the most significant gains being the 20-trajectory sample efficiency (vs. thousands for standard RL) and the ability to generalize to novel cars. The use of a synthetic car-following benchmark with a well-documented environment (CitySim3D) adds to the reproducibility of their results.

The theoretical foundation is sound, with a clear explanation of how the bilinear dynamics model and fitted Q-iteration algorithm work together to create an efficient, sample-efficient approach. The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with a thorough related work section that properly positions the contribution.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, these improvements would further enhance its impact:

1. **Expand experimental scope**: The evaluation is limited to a synthetic environment. It would be valuable to test on more complex, diverse visual conditions (e.g., greater clutter, varied object appearances, more complex 3D car models) to better assess real-world applicability.

2. **Investigate representation learning**: The authors use pre-trained features without fine-tuning. It would be insightful to explore whether fine-tuning the convolutional network (or part of it) could improve generalization and performance.

3. **Improve reproducibility**: The paper includes valuable code and environment releases, but the implementation details could be more concise. Consider simplifying the presentation of experimental parameters and providing a clear, step-by-step guide for reproducing results.

4. **Minor improvements**: Fix the typos mentioned on page 2 (e.g., "a minimum amount of data" should be "a minimum amount of data").

**Questions for Authors**

1. Could you provide more details on how the bilinear dynamics model handles occlusions? The paper mentions robustness to occlusions, but it would be helpful to understand the specific mechanisms.

2. In the ablation study (Section 6.3), the TRPO method required 100x more trajectories to achieve comparable performance to your FQI approach. Could you elaborate on why TRPO struggles with this task despite being a standard RL method?

3. The paper claims generalization to novel cars (Section 6.2), but the evaluation uses cars from the same dataset. How would the performance change if tested on cars from entirely different datasets (e.g., different makes/models not seen during training)?

This is a strong contribution to visual servoing that combines multiple learning techniques in a principled way to address a real-world robotics challenge. The sample efficiency gains are particularly impressive, and the method has clear potential for real-world applications. With minor improvements to the experimental scope, this paper would make an excellent contribution to ICLR 2017.
