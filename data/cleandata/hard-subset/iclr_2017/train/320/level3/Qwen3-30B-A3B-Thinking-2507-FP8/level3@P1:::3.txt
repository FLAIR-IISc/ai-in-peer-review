

This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained deep features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to enable quick adaptation to new targets with minimal training data. The authors propose servoing in the space of learned visual features rather than image pixels or manually-designed keypoints, using a bilinear model to predict feature dynamics and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn optimal feature weights. The key contributions include demonstrating that standard deep features (from ImageNet classification models) can effectively support visual servoing, achieving substantial performance improvements over conventional approaches, and showing sample-efficiency gains of more than two orders of magnitude over standard model-free deep RL algorithms.

I recommend acceptance with a score of 7 (Strong Accept). The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating how to effectively combine deep learning with traditional visual servoing concepts to solve a practical robotics problem with improved data efficiency. The core idea is well-motivated and well-placed within the literature, addressing the critical limitation of prior visual servoing methods that require extensive manual feature engineering.

The paper supports its claims through thorough experiments in a well-defined synthetic car-following benchmark. The authors demonstrate clear performance improvements over multiple baselines, including approaches using pixel intensities, hand-designed keypoints, and classical image-based visual servoing with ORB features. The sample-efficiency results are particularly compelling: the method achieves effective performance with just 20 training trajectory samples, while standard model-free deep RL approaches require orders of magnitude more data. The ablation studies comparing different feature representations (VGG conv layers), weighting methods (FQI vs. TRPO vs. CEM), and baseline approaches provide strong evidence for the proposed approach's advantages.

The method's simplicity is noteworthy - the actual servoing policy is extremely straightforward (minimizing Euclidean distance between weighted features), while the innovation lies in the principled learning of feature weights and dynamics model. The use of bilinear dynamics models for feature prediction is well-justified and provides a computationally efficient way to incorporate visual dynamics without requiring complex video prediction models. The open-source release of the CitySim3D environment and code significantly enhances the paper's reproducibility and potential impact.

For improvements, I recommend the authors address the following points to strengthen the paper:

1. The experiments are conducted in a synthetic environment with limited visual complexity. Evaluating on more diverse, cluttered scenes with varied distractors, lighting conditions, and object appearances would better demonstrate the method's robustness to real-world visual variations.

2. Investigating end-to-end training/fine-tuning of the convolutional features would help determine whether the pre-trained features provide sufficient representation or if task-specific adaptation could further improve performance.

3. The implementation details are somewhat scattered across appendices. Consolidating these into a more concise summary in the main text, along with clearer documentation of hyperparameters and training procedures, would greatly improve reproducibility.

4. The paper contains minor typos (noted on p.2) that should be corrected.

To clarify my assessment, I would like to ask the authors:
1. How would the method perform if the target object was not visible in the initial frame (requiring the agent to locate the target first)?
2. Have you considered incorporating attention mechanisms to better handle distractor objects in the scene?
3. What would be the computational overhead of using a more lightweight feature extractor (e.g., MobileNet) instead of VGG-16 for real-time deployment?

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of visual servoing by demonstrating how to effectively leverage pre-trained deep features within a model-based reinforcement learning framework. The method is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages over strong baselines. The sample-efficiency gains are particularly impressive and address a critical practical limitation in robotics learning. While the experimental scope could be expanded, the current results provide strong evidence for the approach's validity and potential impact.
