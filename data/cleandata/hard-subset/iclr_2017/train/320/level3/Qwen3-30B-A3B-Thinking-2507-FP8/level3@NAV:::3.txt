

**Review**

This paper presents a compelling approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained deep features, bilinear visual dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to enable rapid adaptation to new targets with minimal training data. The authors propose a method that operates in the space of learned visual features rather than image pixels or manually designed keypoints, demonstrating substantial improvements over conventional approaches. The key contribution lies in their principled learning of multi-scale visual feature weights using an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm, which enables robust handling of distractors while achieving remarkable sample efficiency—learning effective visual servoing from just 20 training trajectories.

The paper is well-motivated and well-placed within the literature. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of traditional visual servoing methods relying on manually engineered features and analytical dynamics models, and they provide strong justification for leveraging pre-trained deep features. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate performance gains over multiple baselines, including methods using ground-truth bounding boxes, and show more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample efficiency compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning.

**Decision: Accept**  
The paper makes a significant contribution to visual servoing research with a well-executed approach that addresses a meaningful problem. The experimental validation is thorough and scientifically rigorous within the controlled simulation environment, and the open-source release of the CitySim3D environment represents a valuable resource for the community.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper's key strengths include the elegant integration of pre-trained features with model-based dynamics and RL, the principled approach to learning feature weights, and the impressive sample efficiency demonstrated. The method's simplicity—minimizing Euclidean distance between predicted and target features—leads to computationally efficient implementation without sacrificing performance.

**Additional Feedback**  
To further strengthen the paper, I recommend: (1) Expanding evaluation to more diverse visual conditions, including increased clutter, varied object appearances, and additional 3D models as suggested in the key points; (2) Investigating end-to-end fine-tuning of the convolutional network to assess representation learning benefits; (3) Improving reproducibility by simplifying implementation details and providing a more concise summary of the codebase in the paper. The minor typo fix on page 2 is also recommended.

The authors have presented a solid, interesting contribution that would benefit from the suggested improvements, but the paper is already strong enough for acceptance.
