Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes a learned visual servoing framework that operates in the space of pre-trained deep features (modified VGG-16) rather than pixels or hand-engineered keypoints. It combines: (i) a multiscale feature representation, (ii) a learned bilinear predictive dynamics model that maps current features and action to next-step features, and (iii) a simple one-step greedy servoing policy that minimizes the weighted squared error to a goal feature map.
- A key idea is to learn per-channel, per-scale feature weights and control regularization via a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure. The Q-function is constructed so that the greedy action recovers the servoing policy, and the authors exploit an invariance (scaling of θ and a bias term) to stabilize and speed up Bellman error minimization.
- The paper motivates a locally connected bilinear dynamics model as a good trade-off between expressiveness and computational/memory cost; it can capture local spatial structure and actions’ effects while remaining tractable.
- Experiments in a synthetic quadcopter–car-following benchmark show that: (a) deeper VGG features up to conv4_3 outperform pixels and conv5_3, (b) learned feature weighting via FQI substantially outperforms unweighted variants and TRPO under the same feature/dynamics, and (c) the approach is more sample-efficient in RL trajectories than model-free deep RL policies operating on pixels. The method also beats classical IBVS with ORB or tracker-derived features, despite those baselines having privileged information (depth, sometimes car dynamics).

Decision
Weak Reject.
- Primary reasons: (1) Empirical evaluation is limited to a single synthetic domain and lacks standard benchmarks or real-world validation; (2) Several central modeling/algorithmic choices (locally connected dynamics vs alternatives, and the modified VGG architecture) are not sufficiently ablated or compared, leaving key claims under-supported.

Supporting arguments
- Scope and external validity: Claims of robustness to viewpoint, appearance changes, and occlusions are compelling but demonstrated only in one simulator with a single task (target following). There is no evaluation on real robot data, different tasks (e.g., manipulation IBVS/PBVS, navigation with background distractors), or widely used benchmarks. The generality and robustness claims thus rest on limited evidence.
- Dynamics model choice: The paper argues for locally connected bilinear dynamics as preferable to fully connected or convolutional (tied-weight) variants, but the experimental comparison among these options is incomplete. Quantitative comparisons across model capacity, compute/memory, and prediction/servoing performance would strengthen the case.
- Feature backbone modification: The authors remove some pooling layers and add dilated convolutions to VGG. The study does not report a controlled ablation comparing the modified backbone to the original VGG features on the same pipeline (same dynamics, same FQI), nor does it analyze which scales/layers contribute most when learned weights are allowed.
- FQI design and optimization: The scaling/bias trick in Eq. 5 is a neat idea, but its empirical benefit relative to a more standard target-network/iterative scheme is not quantified. It remains unclear whether the improvement is due to the trick or other factors (regularization choices, non-negativity constraints). Variance across seeds and sensitivity to ν, S, K are also not reported.
- Sample-efficiency comparisons: The claimed two orders of magnitude gain over model-free deep RL may be confounded by representation differences. The proposed method leverages pre-trained features and a separately trained dynamics model (10k triplets), while the model-free baseline learns both representation and control. A fairer comparison would either control for representation capacity or vary the amount of pretraining/dynamics data to map out the sample-efficiency frontier.
- Cost function and baselines: The RL cost uses privileged state (true car pose) to shape learning. While this is acceptable as a training signal, it would be helpful to ensure all learning baselines use exactly the same cost/shaping and to report performance sensitivity to the cost design. IBVS baselines receive privileged depth and sometimes dynamics but still underperform, which is interesting; however, additional modern baselines (e.g., photometric servoing with learned Jacobians, or model-based MPC with learned dynamics in pixel/feature space) would help contextualize the gains.

Suggestions to improve the paper (not prerequisites for acceptance)
- Provide ablations:
  - Locally connected vs convolutional (tied weights) vs fully connected bilinear dynamics, matched for parameter count where possible; report prediction error and closed-loop performance vs compute/memory.
  - Modified VGG vs original VGG vs smaller backbones (e.g., AlexNet, ResNet features) with the same downstream pipeline.
  - Per-channel weights vs per-layer weights vs unweighted.
  - One-step greedy servo with dynamics vs short-horizon planning (e.g., 2–5 steps) using the same model.
- Strengthen evaluation:
  - Add at least one real-world dataset or hardware demo (even small scale) or a standard control benchmark with natural images.
  - Quantify robustness to occlusions, distractors, and viewpoint changes via controlled stress tests.
  - Report learning curves, variance across random seeds, and sensitivity to S, K, ν, exploration noise, and number of RL trajectories.
- Clarify and compare FQI variants:
  - Include a baseline that keeps the target Q fixed (standard fitted Q-iteration) to isolate the effect of the scaling/bias optimization in Eq. 5/6.
  - Discuss convergence/stability properties and any failure modes.
- Fairer sample-efficiency comparisons:
  - Separate the cost of dynamics pretraining from RL trajectories when benchmarking sample efficiency, and include model-based baselines that use the same dynamics model but different control/weight-learning schemes (e.g., CEM over weights, LQR-style approximations).
- Provide qualitative analyses:
  - Visualize learned weights across channels/scales to show which features the policy relies on.
  - Analyze failure cases (e.g., similar-looking distractors, severe occlusions).

Questions for the authors
1. Dynamics model comparisons: Can you provide quantitative results comparing locally connected, convolutional (tied weights), and fully connected bilinear dynamics for both prediction accuracy and closed-loop servoing, under similar parameter budgets?
2. FQI optimization: What is the empirical benefit of the α,b “policy-independent” parameter fit (Eq. 5) versus a standard target network or fixed-target fitted Q-iteration? Please include ablations and variance across seeds.
3. Backbone modification: Does the modified VGG (with dilations, reduced pooling) outperform the original VGG features in your pipeline? Which layers/scales matter most after weight learning?
4. Generalization: How does performance change under controlled occlusions, lighting changes, and with backgrounds containing lookalike distractors? Can you quantify robustness rather than show anecdotal rollouts?
5. Sample efficiency: How do results vary with (a) number of RL trajectories and (b) size of dynamics pretraining data? Where are the diminishing returns?
6. Planning horizon: Have you tried multi-step planning or model-predictive control with your bilinear model? Does it help over the one-step greedy policy?
7. Runtime and practicality: What is the end-to-end latency on CPU/GPU, and how does it scale with the number of channels/layers used? Could a lighter backbone (e.g., MobileNet) preserve performance?
8. Reward design: How sensitive are results to the specific pose-based cost (Appendix B)? Could you train with image-only costs (e.g., photometric or feature distances) and get similar policies?
9. Baselines: Can you add photometric IBVS with learned Jacobians, or a model-based controller using your learned dynamics (e.g., iterative LQR in feature space), to better contextualize your approach?

Overall, I find the formulation elegant and the FQI insight neat, and I appreciate the code/environment release. With stronger ablations and broader evaluation, this work could be impactful.
