Summary and contributions
The paper tackles visual servoing for target following by learning to servo in the space of convolutional feature maps rather than pixels or hand-crafted keypoints. The main idea is to (i) use pretrained CNN feature maps (VGG with dilations) that preserve spatial layout, (ii) learn one-step predictive feature dynamics with a bilinear model (locally-connected or per-channel variants), and (iii) learn a sample-efficient servo policy by minimizing a weighted, regularized distance between predicted next-step features and the goal’s features, where the weights are learned via a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure. The Q-function is designed so that its greedy action corresponds exactly to the one-step, weighted feature-matching servo policy, yielding a simple linear Q-approximator in the weights and an efficient update.

On a synthetic quadcopter–car-following benchmark (CitySim3D, code released), the method:
- Shows clear gains over pixel-based servoing and classical IBVS with ORB or tracker-based points.
- Outperforms end-to-end TRPO policies and TRPO-optimized weighted servoing in both performance and sample efficiency (20 training trajectories for RL vs. >100× more for TRPO).
- Demonstrates that intermediate VGG features (up to conv4_3) are particularly effective; conv5_3 can be too invariant.
- Achieves competitive computational speed relative to tracker-based IBVS.

Overall, the paper delivers a principled, modular approach that is simple at execution time, learns quickly, and generalizes to novel car instances better than strong baselines.

Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) Strong, well-motivated combination of pretrained spatial features, lightweight learned dynamics, and a tailored fitted Q-iteration that yields large gains in sample efficiency and performance. (2) Solid empirical evidence with ablations and comparisons to multiple sensible baselines, accompanied by code and an open simulation environment.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses a core robotics problem—target-following visual servoing—where hand-crafted features and analytical interaction matrices can limit robustness and transfer. Using pretrained CNN features to amortize invariances and a learned feature-dynamics model is well grounded in the literature.
- Approach clarity: The servoing policy is transparent (one-step lookahead minimizing feature error) and the Q-function is constructed so its greedy solution matches the policy, enabling a linear parameterization of Q over feature weights and control penalties. The FQI variant further exploits a scale/bias reweighting to stabilize learning and reduce compute.
- Placement in literature: The paper appropriately contrasts against IBVS/PBVS, tracker-assisted servoing, model-free deep RL (TRPO), and video prediction. The bilinear dynamics choice is justified and efficiently exploited (exact linearization w.r.t. actions).
- Empirical support: 
  - Comparisons cover pixels vs. multiple VGG layers, locally-connected vs. fully-connected bilinear dynamics, unweighted vs. learned weights, and multiple policy learning approaches (CEM, TRPO, FQI).
  - Sample efficiency claims are supported: two sampling iterations with 10 trajectories each for FQI suffice, while TRPO requires dramatically more trajectories.
  - Generalization to novel car appearances is assessed; mid-level VGG features transfer best.
  - Classical baselines include IBVS with ORB and with a state-of-the-art tracker (C-COT), as well as end-to-end CNN policies; the proposed method is superior in performance and/or efficiency. PBVS with ground-truth pose unsurprisingly outperforms all image-based methods, which contextualizes the challenge and the gap to full-state feedback.
- Reproducibility: Code and simulator are released; the paper provides many training/evaluation details, hyperparameters, and cost definitions.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not critical to the decision)
- Broaden evaluation:
  - Stress-test with more diverse scenes: cluttered urban layouts, lighting/weather changes, stronger occlusions, dynamic distractors, and more varied vehicle classes/3D models.
  - Consider other targets beyond cars to gauge task transfer.
  - Add real-world or photorealistic sim (e.g., domain-randomized) demonstrations to assess sim-to-real viability.
- Representation learning:
  - Explore fine-tuning the feature backbone end-to-end with the FQI objective or via auxiliary dynamics losses; assess whether light fine-tuning improves generalization while retaining sample efficiency.
  - Compare alternative backbones (e.g., ResNet) or lighter networks for faster runtime.
- Analysis and ablations:
  - Provide a deeper analysis of the learned weights across scales/channels (sparsity, interpretability) and their sensitivity to ν and γ.
  - Report robustness to dynamics/model mismatch and action noise; test multi-step rollout degradation of the bilinear model.
  - Include multi-step planning variants (e.g., MPC over the learned dynamics) as a comparison to assess the benefit of explicit planning vs. the current one-step lookahead.
- Clarity and reproducibility:
  - Consolidate implementation details in a concise “recipe” subsection (feature extraction, layer choices, normalization, dynamics training, FQI hyperparameters).
  - Minor edits: fix typos around p.2 and ensure consistent notation for layers and losses.

Questions for the authors
- Data efficiency: How many total unique sequences are used to train the dynamics (100 trajectories) vs. for FQI (20 trajectories), and how sensitive is performance to these counts? What is the failure mode when using fewer dynamics samples?
- Target specification: In experiments, the goal is the first frame. How does the method handle specifying a goal by a different view (e.g., another car instance, a template crop, or a re-identified object mid-trajectory)? Can the method disambiguate multiple similar distractors in the scene?
- Dynamics model:
  - How does locally-connected vs. fully-connected bilinear dynamics compare for the semantic features (if memory permits smaller channel subsets)? Is there a marked benefit in certain layers?
  - How does performance change with purely constant-velocity baselines or optical flow surrogates?
- FQI details:
  - Sensitivity to the regularization ν and the non-negativity constraint on θ; do negative weights help or harm in practice?
  - Can you report variance across random seeds for FQI and TRPO, and show learning curves w.r.t. sampling iterations?
- Cost design: The cost uses ground-truth 3D car pose from the simulator. How would you approximate this in real settings (e.g., using a detector/pose estimator), and how sensitive is FQI to bias in the cost signal?
- Runtime: What is the exact runtime breakdown (feature extraction vs. dynamics vs. optimization) and GPU/CPU configuration? Could a lighter backbone achieve real-time (>30 Hz) with modest performance loss?
- Generalization: Have you tried training on one car set and testing on visually out-of-distribution vehicles (e.g., trucks, buses) or different environments? How does the choice of VGG layer impact OOD robustness?

Overall, this is a well-executed, clear, and impactful paper that advances sample-efficient visual servoing by leveraging pretrained features and lightweight learned dynamics with a tailored RL objective.
