1. Summary of what the paper claims and contributes
- Problem and proposal: The paper tackles target-following visual servoing and proposes to servo in the space of pre-trained convolutional spatial feature maps (multi-scale VGG with dilated convolutions), using a bilinear, one-step visual dynamics model. The control at each step minimizes a regularized weighted sum of squared distances between the goal feature map and the one-step feature prediction, plus control penalties.
- Optimization and RL: The policy is instantiated by this weighted one-step objective; the weights over feature channels and scales, as well as control regularization, are learned via a sample-efficient, trust-region–flavored fitted Q-iteration (FQI) that exploits a linear Q-function approximator aligned with the servoing objective. This allows the method to learn to focus on target-relevant features and down-weight distractors.
- Empirical contributions:
  - Controlled, systematic experiments in a synthetic city car-following benchmark to quantify the utility of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing, including generalization to novel cars.
  - Clear gains over multiple sensible baselines: pixel-space servoing, hand-engineered keypoints (ORB/SIFT/SURF), IBVS with a state-of-the-art tracker and with ground-truth depth, and end-to-end CNN/TRPO policies (which require far more samples).
  - Principled and efficient learning of multi-scale feature weights via FQI that handles distractors and yields strong sample efficiency: only about 20 RL trajectories were needed to learn the weights, with >100× fewer samples than model-free deep RL baselines.
  - Model-based visual dynamics and a linear Q-function approximator together deliver good sample efficiency and runtime (∼16 Hz vs ∼1 Hz for the tracker-based baseline).
  - Release of CitySim3D and code for reproducibility and benchmarking visual servoing.

2. Recommendation: Accept
Main reasons:
- Novel, well-motivated combination of pre-trained spatial feature maps, simple but effective bilinear one-step dynamics, and a tailored, efficient FQI that learns feature importance to combat distractors.
- Strong empirical evidence with substantive gains and excellent sample efficiency over strong baselines, including methods that use privileged information (e.g., bounding boxes), plus an open-source environment that can benefit the community.

3. Supporting arguments
- What is the specific question? Can we achieve robust, sample-efficient visual servoing for target following by servoing in pre-trained deep feature space with learned short-horizon feature dynamics, and by learning feature weights via RL to handle distractors?
- Is the approach well-motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. It bridges classical IBVS/PBVS (which rely on hand-crafted features/Jacobians) and data-hungry model-free deep RL. It leverages known transferability of ImageNet features and bilinear models for image-motion dynamics, and situates its FQI approach relative to fitted Q-iteration, neural FQI, and TRPO.
- Do results support claims?
  - Pre-trained VGG features (conv1_2–conv4_3) outperform pixels and conv5_3; the analysis matches the expectation that deeper layers may lose necessary spatial detail for servoing.
  - Significant gains over tracker-based IBVS (despite ground-truth depth and seeding), improved runtime, and far better sample efficiency than TRPO (2 vs 50 iterations; ∼100× fewer trajectories while performing better).
  - Generalization to novel cars is demonstrated; mid-level VGG features retain sufficient spatial specificity while providing invariances.
  - The policy structure and Q-approximation are coherent: the greedy action under Q equals the weighted one-step objective, enabling efficient learning of feature weights.
Limitations and scope: Evaluations are in simulation with a limited set of car models (only 5 seen during dynamics training), controlled lighting/structures, and a cost that uses privileged ground-truth car pose. While appropriate for an ICLR contribution focused on representation and sample efficiency, these limit claims of real-world robustness; the paper acknowledges them and releases a simulator to foster further work.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (not required for acceptance)
- Broaden evaluation:
  - Add more diverse visual conditions: clutter, heavy occlusions, challenging distractors, varied weather/lighting/textures, and more 3D car models. Report robustness breakdowns (e.g., conv4_3 under severe occlusions).
  - Explore multi-step planning (e.g., short-horizon MPC) using the learned dynamics vs the one-step greedy controller.
  - Report ablations: locally vs fully connected bilinear models; neighborhood size; effect of each pyramid scale; effect of regularization ν and non-negativity constraints.
- Representation and training:
  - Investigate end-to-end fine-tuning of the convnet and/or dynamics (e.g., with a small learning rate, auxiliary self-supervised losses) to quantify representation learning benefits and generalization.
  - Compare learned weights to learned spatial attention masks; visualize which channels/scales are emphasized.
- RL and reproducibility:
  - Clarify how the “trust-region-like” step in FQI stabilizes learning; provide sensitivity to S, K, and dataset size. Consider a brief theoretical note on identifiability when scaling θ, bias b, and implications for policy invariance.
  - Improve reproducibility by regrouping implementation details (feature extraction, dynamics architecture, normalization, optimization settings) into a concise recipe in the main text. Ensure the code and simulator links are active, versioned, and include scripts to reproduce all figures.
- Minor:
  - Fix typos and reference formatting issues noted on p.2 (e.g., inconsistent author-year punctuation and escaping in the bibliography).

Questions for the authors
- Cost and supervision: The RL cost uses ground-truth car pose in the camera frame. Can you report performance when costs are derived from vision-only estimates (e.g., tracker or detector) to remove privileged information at training time?
- Dynamics capacity: How sensitive is performance to the locally connected neighborhood size and to the number of channels/scales included? Any overfitting observed when increasing capacity?
- Data efficiency: How does performance degrade when reducing (a) the dynamics training set, and (b) the RL trajectories below 20? Can you quantify the relative contributions of model-based one-step dynamics vs Q-weight learning?
- Generalization: Have you tried features from other backbones (e.g., ResNet) or self-supervised features? Do learned weights transfer across targets (cars) without re-learning?
- Planning horizon: Did you try multi-step rollouts with the bilinear model or short-horizon MPC? If so, how did it compare in accuracy/runtime?
- Real-world prospects: Any preliminary results on real UAVs or in photo-realistic simulators? What are the main obstacles (e.g., calibration, latency, photometric gaps)?
- Stability: Does the FQI procedure consistently converge across random seeds? Any cases where non-negativity constraints on weights impaired learning?

Overall assessment
This is an interesting, solid paper that answers an important question with a principled and efficient approach, strong empirical gains, and meaningful resources for the community. Despite experimental limits, the suggested improvements seem tractable for the authors. I recommend acceptance.
