1. Summary of contributions
The paper tackles visual servoing through a learned visual representation and model-based control. The central idea is to servo in feature space, not pixels or hand-crafted keypoints, using pre-trained deep features and a learned, action-conditional bilinear model of feature dynamics, then derive a simple, analytic control law via linearized one-step predictions.

Main contributions:
- Visual features for servoing: Uses intermediate VGG-16 convolutional features (modified to preserve spatial resolution via dilations), aggregated in a multiscale pyramid. Empirically, mid-level conv layers (e.g., conv4_3) support robustness and generalization better than pixels or very deep features.
- Action-conditional bilinear dynamics: Proposes a one-step predictive bilinear model of feature dynamics, including a multi-scale, multi-channel, locally-connected variant that is parameter-efficient and computationally tractable. Linearization is exact in the controls, enabling closed-form action selection.
- Value-weighted servoing objective: Although the dynamics are one-step predictive, the paper learns feature and control weights by aligning the one-step quadratic objective with long-horizon returns, i.e., a fitted-value approach that selects task-relevant features and induces robustness to distractors.
- Fitted-Q iteration with an analytic Q approximator: Defines a Q-function linear in feature and action-weight parameters such that the greedy policy equals the servo policy. Introduces a trust-region–style fitted-Q iteration variant that jointly rescales parameters (α) and fits a bias (b) before the standard FQI update, improving stability and sample efficiency.
- Empirical evaluation: On a challenging synthetic car-following task with a quadrotor, the approach achieves strong performance with very few trajectories (≈20), outperforms classical IBVS with ORB features and tracker-based baselines, and is over two orders of magnitude more sample-efficient than end-to-end deep RL. Results show the benefit of the value-weighted objective and that pre-trained VGG features aid generalization to novel targets.

The paper is well written, mathematically grounded, and conceptually thorough. The problem is well contextualized in the visual servoing and RL literatures.

2. Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, well-motivated integration of representation, dynamics modeling, and fitted-value RL that delivers compelling sample efficiency and robust servoing behavior.
- Clear empirical evidence that the value-weighted objective and pre-trained features provide tangible benefits, with careful ablations and strong baselines.

3. Supporting arguments
Addressing the three review questions:
1) What problem is tackled?
- Data-efficient, robust visual servoing (target following) that can quickly adapt to new targets, by servoing in a learned visual feature space and selecting task-relevant features to ignore distractors.

2) Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature?
- Yes. The paper thoughtfully contrasts classical IBVS/PBVS (feature engineering and analytical Jacobians), end-to-end deep RL (sample-hungry), and recent video-prediction models (often heavy and not tailored to control). The bilinear model is motivated by image Jacobian structure and camera motion; using pre-trained deep features leverages known invariances while avoiding large data requirements. The fitted-value weighting is well connected to fitted Q-iteration and policy evaluation literature.

3) Do the results support the claims?
- Largely yes. The experiments demonstrate:
  - The value-weighted objective (via FQI) outperforms unweighted and TRPO-learned weights with far fewer samples.
  - Mid-level VGG features generalize to novel cars better than pixels or deepest features.
  - The method surpasses classical IBVS with ORB and a tracker baseline; it is markedly more sample-efficient than end-to-end CNN policies.
  - Derivations and the linearization are correct; the action-conditional bilinear model leads to efficient Jacobian computation and closed-form action selection.
Caveats:
  - Evaluation is in simulation; transfer to real hardware is not shown.
  - Representation learning contribution is limited—the VGG encoder is fixed; neither features nor policy representation are learned end-to-end.
  - The Q-function is linear in hand-constructed terms; while this affords stability and interpretability, it narrows the representational scope and may limit ICLR-specific novelty in representation learning.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify and justify the “trust-region FQI” naming/claim. The α,b re-scaling step is elegant; relate it more formally to conservative policy iteration/TRPO ideas or to robust policy evaluation, and provide stability analysis or bounds if possible.
- Discuss end-to-end fine-tuning: even partial adaptation (e.g., last VGG block or a small adapter) might improve performance without losing sample efficiency; report a small-scale study if feasible.
- Extend generalization analysis: quantify domain shift (lighting, weather, distractors), and test robustness to stronger occlusions and moving distractors not present in training.
- Provide more insight into the learned weights: visualize per-scale/channel weights to show how the method attends to task-relevant structures and how that changes across targets.
- Compare to a simple model-based MPC baseline using the same one-step predictive model over a short horizon, to underline why the value-weighted one-step policy suffices.
- Report sensitivity: performance vs. number of RL trajectories (<20, 20–50), discount factor γ, and regularization ν; and ablate the locally-connected vs. fully connected dynamics on at least one semantic feature level.
- Real-world viability: comment on sim-to-real prospects (feature invariances, bilinear model assumptions under real camera motion), and provide preliminary timing on embedded hardware if possible.

Questions for the authors
- Trust-region FQI: Is there a theoretical connection to TRPO/CPI (e.g., monotonic improvement or contraction properties) or is the α,b fit purely heuristic? Any empirical ablations showing instability without this step?
- Non-negativity constraints: How critical are θ ≥ 0 constraints for stability and interpretability? What happens if negative weights are allowed?
- One-step model: Did you try 2–3 step prediction or Dyna-style rollouts? How sensitive is performance to model error, and is there evidence of compounding error avoided by the one-step design?
- Feature choice: Why do conv5 features degrade Q approximation—can you quantify spatial invariance (e.g., receptive field/stride) as a predictor of servo suitability?
- Generalization: How does performance change for targets outside the training distribution (e.g., trucks vs. sedans), or under significant appearance/lighting shifts?
- Sample efficiency: You report ≈20 trajectories for RL. What is the minimal training data to beat the best classical/tracker baseline?
- Real-world transfer: Have you attempted any hardware experiments or photorealistic sim (e.g., domain randomization) to assess transfer? Any failure modes observed?
- Runtime: With VGG as the bottleneck (≈16 Hz), have you evaluated lighter backbones (e.g., SqueezeNet/MobileNet) and the trade-off in performance?
- Ablations: Can you provide results comparing fully connected vs. locally connected bilinear dynamics on semantic features at one scale (perhaps with reduced channels) to isolate the benefit of locality?
- Cost shaping: How sensitive are results to the specific geometric cost in Appendix B and to termination thresholds (FOV, distance τ)? Would learned cost shaping alter the learned weights?
