Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a target-following visual servoing framework that operates in the space of pre-trained convolutional spatial feature maps, uses a bilinear one-step visual dynamics model, and learns a policy by minimizing a regularized, weighted distance between predicted features and a goal. The weights over multi-scale feature channels are learned via an efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) formulation, yielding a principled way to emphasize task-relevant features and downweight distractors. Controlled simulation experiments in a released virtual city benchmark show clear performance gains and markedly better sample efficiency compared to strong baselines, including methods using ground-truth bounding boxes and model-free deep RL.

Decision: Accept
Reason(s): The approach is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature, and the empirical results convincingly support the main claims with strong sample efficiency and consistent improvements over sensible baselines.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Learning data-efficient, target-following visual servo policies that generalize across visual variation by combining pre-trained deep features, a predictive visual dynamics model, and RL.
- Is the approach well motivated? Yes. The paper builds directly on known strengths of pretrained CNN features and bilinear image-motion models, and addresses a key gap in servoing: selecting discriminative features and scales under distractors via a Q-function that matches the servoing objective.
- Do results support the claims? Yes. The method delivers substantial cost reductions versus pixel/hand-crafted features and tracker-based IBVS, and achieves two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency than model-free deep RL. Analyses across VGG layers and ablations on weighting support the design choices. While evaluation is synthetic, it is controlled and rigorous.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Evaluate under more diverse/realistic conditions: cluttered scenes, stronger distractors, varied appearance/motion, additional car/asset models, and domain shifts.
- Explore end-to-end fine-tuning or lightweight adapters for the convnet to quantify representation learning benefits and generalization.
- Improve reproducibility: regroup/simplify implementation details, clarify all hyperparameters, and ensure open-source code accompanies the paper with a concise reproducibility checklist.
- Minor: fix typos noted on p.2.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of backbone/layer (e.g., ResNet, ConvNeXt) and to feature downsampling?
- Can the FQI weight-learning be interpreted as a trust-region or regularized update with stability guarantees? Any convergence diagnostics?
- How robust is the one-step bilinear model to larger motions or dynamic backgrounds; would limited multi-step rollout (MPC) help?
- What is the failure rate under heavy occlusion and similar-looking distractors, and how do learned weights distribute across scales/channels in such cases?
- Any preliminary results in real or photo-real domains (e.g., CARLA) to assess sim-to-real sensitivity?
