The paper tackles visual servoing by combining pre-trained deep visual features, a one-step predictive bilinear dynamics model in feature space, and a fitted Q-iteration procedure that learns how to weight those features for control. The core contributions are: (1) servoing in the space of VGG features (with pooling removed/dilated convs to preserve spatial detail) instead of pixels or hand-designed keypoints; (2) an action-conditional, locally connected multiscale bilinear model that predicts next-step feature maps, enabling efficient one-step lookahead control; (3) a Q-function parameterization that is linear in the feature and control weights, allowing a simple, analytic greedy policy and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn those weights; and (4) a thorough empirical evaluation on a challenging synthetic quadcopter–car-following benchmark showing strong robustness to viewpoint and occlusion and two orders-of-magnitude sample-efficiency gains over standard model-free deep RL. The paper is well written, mathematically clean, and empirically convincing. It also provides actionable insights: deeper VGG features up to conv4_3 generalize best; conv5_3 is too spatially invariant for this task.

Decision: Reject. While the work is solid and valuable for robotics, the primary novelty is in the control side and systems integration; the representation is fixed (pre-trained VGG), and learning is not end-to-end. As a result, the contribution to representation learning—central to ICLR—is limited, and most claims are supported in a simulated setting.

Supporting arguments:
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets sample-efficient visual servoing that is robust to distractors, occlusions, and appearance changes, a well-motivated and long-standing challenge. The literature placement is good: it contrasts with classical IBVS/PBVS, pixel-based photometric servoing, and data-hungry model-free deep RL. The choice to leverage pre-trained features to avoid large data requirements is sensible and supported by transfer learning literature.
- Approach: The multiscale, locally bilinear predictive model is technically appropriate and computationally efficient. Using a Q-function linear in feature/control weights so that the greedy policy coincides with the servoing objective is elegant and yields a tractable fitted Q-iteration scheme. The additional scaling/bias fitting within FQI is a practical stabilization that likely contributes to its sample efficiency.
- Evidence and rigor: Experiments are carefully designed with fixed validation/test splits, ablations across feature layers, comparison against pixel features, hand-crafted keypoints, tracker-based IBVS, end-to-end TRPO policies, and different weight-learning schemes (CEM/TRPO/FQI). The results clearly show: (i) substantial gains from using pre-trained features plus bilinear dynamics; (ii) the necessity of learning feature weights with FQI to prioritize task-relevant channels/scales; and (iii) superior sample efficiency versus model-free baselines. However, all main results are in a synthetic simulator; there is no real-robot validation or domain randomization study to argue for sim-to-real transfer. The method’s learning component does not adapt the visual representation itself, so the representation-learning contribution is modest for ICLR.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper, not all of which affect the decision):
- Clarify the novelty of the fitted Q-iteration variant. The linear Q-parameterization and intermediate scaling/bias optimization are neat; positioning them relative to existing FQI/stabilization techniques (e.g., target networks, double Q, regularization) would help avoid over-claiming novelty and highlight the specific insight exploited here.
- End-to-end fine-tuning: Consider jointly fine-tuning a subset of the visual layers and/or the bilinear dynamics with the RL objective (e.g., via policy gradients through the one-step lookahead or via an auxiliary loss). Even partial adaptation (e.g., last conv block) might improve performance and strengthen the representation-learning angle.
- Real-world validation or sim-to-real: Even a small real-robot experiment (e.g., tabletop object following) or a thorough domain randomization analysis would greatly increase impact and credibility for transfer.
- Ablations and diagnostics:
  - Quantify sensitivity to the chosen pre-trained backbone (VGG vs ResNet) and to layer selection (conv3_x vs conv4_x).
  - Analyze the contribution of the locally connected constraint versus fully connected bilinear dynamics on both accuracy and compute/memory.
  - Report robustness to camera calibration errors and sensor noise.
  - Provide per-feature weight distributions to illustrate which channels/scales are emphasized by FQI.
  - Include learning curves and variance across seeds for FQI and TRPO to assess stability.
- Comparisons: Consider model-based RL baselines such as Dyna-style planning with learned pixel-feature dynamics or PILCO-like methods adapted to feature space, to more directly contrast with learning dynamics for control.

Questions for the authors:
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of pre-trained features? Have you tried alternative architectures (e.g., ResNet, feature pyramids) or fine-tuning the last block during FQI?
- How critical is the locally connected structure in the bilinear model versus fully connected? What are the compute/memory trade-offs and accuracy differences across layers?
- The Q-function is linear in weights with non-negativity constraints. Do negative weights ever help (e.g., to de-emphasize distractors via cancellation)? What breaks if the constraint is relaxed?
- How robust is the method to domain shift (lighting, textures, camera intrinsics)? Did you try domain randomization to approach sim-to-real?
- Can the fitted Q-iteration scheme be extended to multi-step predictive models without sacrificing tractability of the greedy policy?
- How does performance scale with the amount of dynamics data and RL trajectories? Is there a performance plateau, and where is the knee of the curve?
- Could you provide more detail on the stability of the FQI updates (e.g., sensitivity to ν, γ, and exploration noise), and whether target networks or double Q-style tricks were necessary?

Overall, this is a strong robotics/control paper with a clever and effective integration of pre-trained features, predictive dynamics, and fitted Q-iteration. The empirical results are convincing for the stated task. The main reason for my rejection is fit to ICLR: the representation is largely fixed and the novelty is primarily in control and systems design rather than in learning representations.
