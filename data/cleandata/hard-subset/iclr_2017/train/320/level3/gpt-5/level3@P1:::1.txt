Summary of contributions
The paper tackles visual servoing for target following by combining pre-trained visual features, a learned predictive model of feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning method that learns which features matter. The core ideas are:
- Learn a bilinear, action-conditioned predictive model of next-frame feature maps (at multiple scales), enabling one-step lookahead servoing in feature space rather than pixels or hand-crafted keypoints.
- Use a simple, analytic servo policy that minimizes a weighted quadratic distance between predicted features and target features (Eq. 3), with per-channel and per-scale weights and control penalties.
- Learn those weights via a fitted Q-iteration scheme with a Q-function approximator that is linear in the weights and whose greedy action recovers the servo policy. A key insight is that scaling θ and shifting b do not change the greedy action, allowing a fast optimization that alternates fitting α, b and θ (Eqs. 5–6).
- Explore different ways to connect actions and frame representations, arguing for a locally connected bilinear dynamics model as a good balance of expressiveness and computational tractability. Multiscale VGG features (with reduced pooling and dilations) help integrate local and global cues.
Empirically, the method learns effective target following on a synthetic CitySim3D quadrotor-car benchmark, achieving strong robustness to distractors and occlusions, and substantially better sample efficiency than model-free deep RL baselines (20 trajectories vs. hundreds/thousands). The method also outperforms classical IBVS variants using ORB or a tracker, and a TRPO-trained CNN policy.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The experimental validation is limited to a single synthetic setup, with missing ablations needed to support several central claims (e.g., the superiority of the locally connected bilinear model and the benefit of the modified VGG). (2) The reinforcement learning contribution (FQI with scaling invariance) is promising but lacks comparisons to closely related alternatives and sensitivity analyses, making it hard to assess how much of the performance comes from the dynamics, the features, or the new FQI optimization.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses a well-defined and important problem—sample-efficient visual servoing robust to distractors and occlusions—clearly motivated by limitations of hand-engineered features/Jacobians and data-hungry end-to-end deep RL. Positioning in the literature is solid and up-to-date for the venue.
- Technical soundness: The formulation is elegant. The use of a bilinear model makes the Jacobian exact (Appendix A), enabling a convex quadratic action selection and a linear Q-approximator consistent with the servo objective. The “scale invariance” trick for Q-fitting is neat and well-justified for the chosen approximator.
- Claims vs. evidence:
  - Locally connected vs. fully connected/convolutional dynamics: The paper argues the locally connected bilinear model is preferable, but experiments systematically comparing fully connected, convolutional (tied weights), and locally connected variants—especially on the same semantic feature levels—are absent or incomplete due to memory limits. This weakens the claim that locality is the right inductive bias.
  - Modified VGG vs. original: The network is altered (less pooling, dilations) to preserve spatial detail for servoing, which is plausible, but there is no empirical validation against the unmodified VGG features under identical training and weighting to confirm the benefit.
  - FQI optimization: While the fitted Q-iteration with α, b initialization is attractive, the evaluation omits direct comparisons to: (i) a standard fitted Q-iteration that holds the target Q fixed (classic approach), (ii) LSPI or LSTD-Q style baselines with the same features, or (iii) a variant that only learns weights by supervised regression to long-horizon cost-to-go. The TRPO comparisons focus on end-to-end or policy-parameter learning, not on Q-learning variants closest in spirit.
  - Generality and robustness: Results are restricted to one synthetic domain with a specific cost function leveraging privileged state for episode termination. The reported strong gains in sample efficiency over model-free deep RL are compelling, but the absence of tests on standard robotics/vision benchmarks, real-world data, or even other simulated tasks undermines the generality claim.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The paper is clearly written, equations and algorithmic steps are explicit, and code/simulator availability is a strong plus.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Provide a comprehensive ablation:
  - Dynamics architecture: fully connected (per-channel), convolutional (tied weights), and locally connected; vary local neighborhood nf; report parameter counts, training time, and accuracy vs. servo performance.
  - Feature hierarchy: conv1_2 to conv5_3 (already present) plus comparisons of modified vs. original VGG for the same dynamics/weights.
  - Weighting: unweighted vs. per-scale only vs. per-channel+per-scale; analyze which channels get large weights and why.
- FQI analyses:
  - Compare to a conventional target-network-style fitted Q-iteration that holds the second term fixed across inner iterations; report convergence speed, stability, and final performance.
  - Compare to LSPI/LSTD-Q with the same feature vector φ(s,u); and to a pure policy evaluation/regression baseline for θ using Monte Carlo returns.
  - Sensitivity to ν (regularization), discount γ, and to the positivity constraint on θ (how is it enforced?).
- Broaden evaluation:
  - Include at least one additional simulated domain (e.g., a manipulation visual servo, or different target-following dynamics).
  - If feasible, a small real-robot validation showing the method can tolerate modest sim-to-real gap would greatly strengthen the case.
  - Compare to stronger classical baselines: direct/photometric IBVS with modern dense alignment; optical-flow-based control; IBVS with better learned/object-specific keypoints; and MPC with learned dynamics in pixel/feature space.
- Report runtime/memory trade-offs across dynamics variants and the cost of feature extraction; discuss alternatives to VGG to improve speed.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a direct comparison between locally connected, fully connected (per-channel), and convolutional (tied weights) bilinear dynamics on the same semantic feature levels, including parameter counts and accuracy vs. control performance?
- How does the proposed FQI variant compare to a standard fitted Q-iteration that holds the target Q fixed without the α, b scaling step? Does your optimization primarily accelerate convergence or also improve the final policy?
- What is the empirical benefit of the modified VGG (reduced pooling + dilations) versus the original VGG-16 features when used with the same dynamics and weighting?
- How sensitive is performance to the regularization ν, the positivity constraint on θ (how exactly enforced), and the feature scale selection? Do learned weights generalize across targets without retraining?
- To what extent does the cost function rely on privileged information (e.g., termination on out-of-FOV or distance thresholds)? How does performance change with purely image-based rewards?
- Can you report per-iteration sample and wall-clock efficiency for FQI vs. TRPO and other Q-learning baselines, and provide variance across random seeds?
