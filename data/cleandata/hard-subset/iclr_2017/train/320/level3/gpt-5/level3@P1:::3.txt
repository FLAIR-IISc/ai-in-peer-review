1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper tackles visual servoing for target following by combining pre-trained convolutional feature maps with learned one-step visual dynamics and a sample-efficient RL procedure. The core idea is to servo in the space of multi-scale VGG features (with dilations to preserve spatial layout), using a bilinear feature dynamics model to predict how actions affect features, and a greedy controller that minimizes predicted feature distance to a goal image. A fitted Q-iteration (FQI) method with a linear Q approximator over per-channel, per-scale feature weights and control regularization learns which features matter for the task, thereby handling distractors and invariances without large task-specific datasets. The approach is simple, analytic at execution time, and data-efficient. In a 3D city simulator (CitySim3D), the method learns effective car-following with only ∼20 trajectories for RL, substantially outperforming baselines based on pixels, hand-designed keypoints, and model-free deep RL; it is competitive with a strong tracker-based IBVS baseline, and demonstrates generalization to novel car models. The paper releases an environment and code.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- A principled, simple integration of strong visual priors (pre-trained CNN features), a lightweight predictive model (bilinear dynamics), and a tailored Q-function approximator yields a practical, sample-efficient visual servoing solution.
- Clear empirical gains and sample-efficiency improvements over sensible baselines, with careful ablations and analysis of feature layers and optimization methods.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Traditional visual servoing assumes hand-engineered features and interaction matrices; model-free deep RL is too sample-hungry. Servoining in a learned feature space with pre-trained invariances is well motivated and well placed in the literature (IBVS/PBVS, deep features transfer, predictive models, fitted Q-iteration).
- Methodological novelty and soundness: The servoing objective is a weighted, multi-scale feature prediction error with quadratic control costs; because the bilinear dynamics are linear in control, the greedy policy is analytic. Casting the Q-function as linear in the weights (feature and control) aligns the Q-minimization with the servoing objective, enabling an efficient FQI scheme that learns relevance weights under long-horizon costs. The non-negativity constraint on weights maintains interpretability and keeps the greedy step consistent.
- Dynamics learning: The multiscale bilinear predictor is an appropriate compromise between expressivity and data/compute efficiency. Using locally connected bilinear structure is well justified and computationally convenient (exact Jacobians for control).
- Empirical support: 
  - Layer selection: VGG conv1_2 to conv4_3 support accurate Q approximation and generalize better than pixels; very deep conv5_3 loses too much spatial detail, corroborating the spatial-invariance hypothesis.
  - Sample efficiency: With only 20 rollouts for RL, the FQI-weighted servo significantly outperforms TRPO-trained policies, even when TRPO uses >100× more rollouts; CEM and unweighted variants underperform.
  - Baselines: The method outperforms pixel-intensity servoing and ORB-based IBVS, and is competitive with tracker-based IBVS while running substantially faster. It also outperforms end-to-end model-free CNN policies trained from pixels or VGG features given comparable data budgets.
  - Generalization: Performance is strong on both seen and novel cars, indicating the benefit of pre-trained features and the weighting learned by RL.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The algorithm is clearly specified; environment and code are released, with concrete architecture and hyperparameters.

4. Additional feedback to help improve the paper (not required for my decision)
- Broaden evaluation: Include more diverse visuals (cluttered urban scenes, varied lighting/weather, more 3D car models, stronger occlusions), and moving distractors with similar appearance. Reporting robustness curves (e.g., occlusion percentage vs. cost) would strengthen claims about invariance.
- Real-world validation: Even a small-scale hardware demo (e.g., ground robot or drone indoor tracking) would considerably increase impact and address sim-to-real concerns.
- Representation learning: Explore fine-tuning of feature layers and/or joint training of the dynamics model and weights; a small amount of task-specific fine-tuning might further improve generalization and reduce reliance on hand-picked layers.
- Multi-step planning: Compare the greedy one-step servo to short-horizon MPC over the learned dynamics to quantify gains vs. computational overhead.
- Analysis of weights: Visualize learned per-channel, per-scale weights and their sparsity; quantify how weighting suppresses distractors and which semantic channels are selected.
- Method details: Consolidate hyperparameters (γ, ν, σexploration), termination criteria, and computational costs into a concise summary table; include confidence intervals and multiple seeds.
- Clarify bounding-box baselines: Explicitly report where the proposed method stands relative to IBVS with ground-truth bounding boxes and with tracker boxes, and under which assumptions (e.g., access to depth or dynamics).

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the discount factor, regularization ν, and the non-negativity constraint on weights? Would allowing negative weights help disambiguate distractors?
- What is the impact of model error in multi-step rollouts? Does compounding error degrade behavior, and would two-step or MPC planning help in difficult regimes?
- Can you provide ablations on locally connected vs. fully connected bilinear dynamics for VGG features (perhaps with smaller spatial grids) to quantify the trade-off?
- How are exploration noise and data collection schedules chosen? Does performance degrade if the hand-coded data for dynamics is reduced (e.g., 10 trajectories)?
- What failure modes did you observe (e.g., extreme occlusions, rapid target acceleration, out-of-FOV recovery), and how often do they occur?
- Could the approach handle multiple candidate targets in view? How would the weighting adapt if the goal image contains ambiguous views?
- Runtime: What are the exact timings per control step for different feature layers on a typical GPU/CPU, and can a lightweight backbone (e.g., MobileNet) preserve performance?
- Do the learned weights transfer across targets/tasks, or must they be re-learned per target? Any evidence of few-shot adaptation for new object instances?
- Could you comment on stability properties of the closed-loop system under the learned one-step predictive controller? Any empirical Lyapunov-like indicators?

Minor
- Fix typos (e.g., those on p.2) and streamline the implementation description; ensure the code release includes exact model snapshots, feature layer choices, and scripts to reproduce figures.
