Summary and contributions
- Problem: The paper tackles visual servoing for target following, aiming to move a robot’s camera to maintain a desired relative configuration to a moving target. It seeks sample-efficient learning that quickly adapts to new targets and is robust to distractors, viewpoint, lighting, and occlusion.
- Approach: The method servo’s in a learned feature space rather than pixels or handcrafted keypoints. It uses (i) pre-trained VGG features (modified to preserve spatial detail via dilation), (ii) an action-conditional, one-step bilinear predictive model of feature dynamics (including a locally-connected variant), and (iii) a simple greedy, one-step policy that minimizes predicted feature-to-goal distance with multi-scale, per-channel weights. The weights are learned with a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure using a linear Q-approximation whose greedy action coincides with the servo objective.
- Claims and results: On a synthetic quadcopter–car following benchmark, the approach learns effective visual servo policies with very few RL trajectories (∼20), substantially outperforming pixel-based IBVS and hand-engineered keypoints, and being >100× more sample efficient than model-free deep RL baselines. Pre-trained deep features (conv1_2–conv4_3) generalize better to novel cars than pixels or conv5_3. The value-weighted objective via FQI improves over unweighted or TRPO-learned weights. Code, simulator, and videos are released.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Limited representation learning contribution: the method relies on fixed pre-trained features and does not learn representations end-to-end, which makes the work less aligned with ICLR’s focus on representation learning.
2) Evaluation scope and novelty: strong empirical results are confined to a synthetic domain; the algorithmic novelty (a tailored linear FQI for a problem-specific Q-structure) is incremental without theoretical guarantees or broader applicability demonstrated.

Supporting arguments
- What is the specific question? How to combine learned predictive models and RL with pre-trained visual features to obtain a robust, sample-efficient visual servoing policy for target following.
- Is the approach well motivated and placed? Yes. The paper clearly positions itself against IBVS/PBVS with hand-engineered features, model-free deep RL (sample-inefficient), and recent video prediction. Using pre-trained features to amortize invariances and a simple bilinear model for action-conditional feature dynamics is reasonable and well justified by prior work.
- Do results support claims? Largely yes within the synthetic benchmark. The paper provides: (a) comparisons among feature layers (pixels vs VGG conv1_2–conv5_3), (b) ablations on weighting (none/CEM/TRPO vs FQI), (c) comparisons to IBVS with ORB/tracker, and to end-to-end CNN policies with TRPO. The method shows clear gains in cost and sample efficiency. However:
  - The reward/cost uses privileged state (ground-truth car pose) during training, which limits realism and clouds claims about sample efficiency in real settings.
  - No real-robot experiments; generalization is shown only across novel car meshes and initial states, not across environments or real-world visual shift.
  - The proposed FQI variant is practical but lacks theoretical analysis (stability/convergence, approximation error with the linear Q-structure), and the claim of novelty around “trust-region FQI” is unclear and should be precisely positioned relative to regularized FQI and NFQ.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Strengthen the representation aspect: fine-tune the feature extractor with auxiliary losses (e.g., self-supervised predictive objectives) or with the RL signal through a stable scheme; show whether modest fine-tuning improves conv5_3 or narrows the sim-to-novel gap.
- Clarify the “trust-region FQI” claim: specify what constitutes the “trust region” (e.g., scaling α and bias b updates, L2 regularization) and position it against prior regularized FQI/NFQ. If it is not a trust-region method, revise the claim.
- Cost realism: discuss replacing privileged pose-based costs with image-based surrogates or learned reward models; provide an experiment with an image-derived cost (e.g., learned discriminator or distance-to-tracker box) to illustrate feasibility without ground-truth state.
- Theory and stability: provide analysis (even local) of the greedy one-step controller under the bilinear dynamics (e.g., contraction conditions, Lyapunov-style arguments) and discuss failure modes when predictions are biased.
- Broaden baselines: include model-based RL baselines that exploit learned dynamics in latent space (e.g., E2C-style) and Dyna-style model-based policy optimization, and compare sample budgets fairly.
- Robustness: report sensitivity to dynamics dataset size/quality, backbone choice, and domain shift (lighting/weather). Show whether learned weights are sparse and interpretable across scales/channels.
- Fairness and compute: detail hyperparameter search budgets and wall-clock for each baseline; report parameter counts for fully vs locally connected models.

Questions for the authors
1) What precisely is novel about your FQI variant? Is there a trust-region constraint, or is it re-scaling plus L2 regularization? How does it relate formally to regularized FQI and NFQ?
2) How sensitive is performance to the amount and nature of dynamics training data? Do fewer than 100 trajectories suffice? What happens with dynamics mismatch?
3) Are the learned feature weights sparse or concentrated on specific layers/channels? Do they transfer across targets (e.g., from one car to another) without re-learning?
4) Can you replace the privileged pose-based cost with an image-derived cost (e.g., from a tracker or learned reward) and retain performance and sample efficiency?
5) How does the method perform with alternative backbones (ResNet, MobileNet) or modest fine-tuning? Does fine-tuning improve conv5_3’s spatial specificity?
6) What is the failure rate under significant occlusions or distractors not seen in training? Any quantitative robustness analysis?
7) Could the bilinear model be augmented with uncertainty estimates, and would this help the greedy controller (e.g., risk-aware weighting)?

Overall, this is a well-written, technically solid paper with strong empirical evidence that pre-trained features plus simple action-conditional models and a tailored FQI can yield sample-efficient visual servoing. However, the work’s core novelty lies in control and algorithmic engineering rather than representation learning, and the reliance on privileged costs and synthetic-only validation limit its fit for ICLR.
