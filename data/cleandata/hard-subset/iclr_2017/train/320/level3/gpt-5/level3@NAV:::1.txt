Summary
The paper proposes a learned visual servoing framework that operates in pretrained feature space rather than pixels or hand-crafted keypoints. Its contributions are: (i) a bilinear dynamics model that predicts next-frame features from the current frame and action; (ii) a Q-function formulation that learns per-feature-channel weights for a one-step lookahead servoing objective; and (iii) an elegant fitted Q-iteration (FQI) scheme that exploits the scale-invariance of the Q-parameters (θ, b), enabling a stable, sample-efficient policy evaluation/update without an additional function approximator. The paper carefully explores how to couple action and frame representations and argues for a locally connected bilinear model as a good balance of compute and expressiveness. Experiments suggest per-channel weighting outperforms unweighted variants, and the approach improves sample efficiency over standard model-free baselines in a synthetic car-following task.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Empirical validation is too limited (toy simulation, few targets, no standard benchmarks or real-robot results) to substantiate the broad claims. (2) Critical ablations and comparisons—particularly between fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected dynamics and against alternative Q-learning updates—are missing or underdeveloped.

Supporting arguments
- The core ideas are technically solid and novel: the bilinear predictive model in feature space, the per-channel Q-weighting, and the identification of scale-invariance to simplify FQI are well-motivated and clearly presented.
- However, evidence is primarily on a bespoke synthetic environment with a very small number of training trajectories/targets. This limits external validity and leaves unanswered how robust the method is to real-world noise, calibration errors, and dynamics mismatch.
- The claim that locally connected bilinear dynamics dominate fully connected or convolutional alternatives requires controlled comparisons with matched parameter counts and compute; current results are insufficient to justify the design choice.
- The modified VGG (reduced pooling, dilations) is central to performance; ablations versus the unmodified backbone and alternative backbones (e.g., ResNet) are needed to validate this choice.
- The optimization insight in Eq. 5 is appealing, but its practical advantage over a standard approach that holds the target term fixed is not empirically demonstrated.

Additional feedback and questions (to improve the paper)
- Provide a thorough ablation: fully connected vs convolutional vs locally connected bilinear dynamics with matched capacity and FLOPs; include training stability and wall-clock.
- Compare the proposed FQI variant to: (a) classical FQI with a frozen target; (b) an iterative scheme that holds the second term in Eq. 5 fixed. Report convergence speed, sensitivity to ν, and final performance.
- Quantify the benefit of per-channel weights with statistical tests and analyze which channels receive weight (interpretability).
- Validate the modified VGG: end-to-end comparisons to the original backbone and to other features; report whether modifications help prediction accuracy and control.
- Add stronger benchmarks (e.g., standard simulators, real robot or photorealistic domains), robustness tests (occlusion, lighting, dynamics shift), and sample-complexity curves.
- Clarify identifiability under scale-invariance: how do you prevent degenerate θ via regularization, and does b affect policy selection in practice?
