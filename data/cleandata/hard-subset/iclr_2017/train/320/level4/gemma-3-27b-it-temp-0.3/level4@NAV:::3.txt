## Review

**1) Summary**
This work introduces a novel approach to visual servoing – specifically, target following – leveraging spatial feature maps extracted from convolutional networks pre-trained for general image classification. The authors integrate bilinear models representing the one-step dynamics of these visual features at multiple scales with a reinforcement learning framework. A servoing policy is then learned by minimizing a regularized, weighted average of distances to features predicted by the learned visual dynamics model.

**2) Contributions**
The paper makes several key contributions:
+ Rigorous, controlled experiments in simulation demonstrate the value of utilizing pre-trained deep features for visual servoing tasks.
+ The proposed method exhibits clear performance gains compared to a range of relevant baselines, including those utilizing ground truth bounding box information.
+ A principled approach to learning weights for multi-scale visual features is presented, coupled with an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm designed to address the challenge of visual distractors.
+ The method demonstrates strong sample efficiency, attributable to the chosen Q-function approximator and the model-based approach to one-step visual feature dynamics.
+ The authors provide an open-source virtual city environment specifically designed for benchmarking visual servoing algorithms.

**3) Suggestions for Improvement**
- **Increased Benchmark Complexity:** While the environment is beyond a trivial synthetic setting, the experiments would be strengthened by incorporating more challenging visual conditions. This includes increased clutter, more diverse distractors, greater variety in object appearances and motion, and a richer, more diverse environment overall. Specifically, expanding the range of 3D car models (potentially utilizing resources like Google SketchUp) and increasing the number of distractor vehicles (in traffic or parked scenarios) would be beneficial, given the method’s emphasis on robustness to visual variations.
- **End-to-End Training and Representation Learning:** Although the current results are promising, investigating the impact of end-to-end training – including fine-tuning the convolutional network – would be valuable, particularly for improving generalization in more complex visual scenarios. This would also allow for an evaluation of the benefits of deep representation learning specifically for visual servoing, a topic highly relevant to the ICLR audience. While the authors briefly mention the possibility of adaptation, a more thorough exploration would be impactful.
- **Improved Reproducibility:** The formalism and algorithms are clearly articulated; however, the paper contains a substantial amount of practical implementation details presented with varying levels of detail throughout the main text and appendix. Consolidating, simplifying, or reorganizing this information would enhance clarity. Ideally, the paper would summarize the most crucial implementation details in the main sections and provide a link to a complete, open-source implementation of the method.
- **Minor Errors:**
  p.2: "learning is a relative[ly] recent addition"
  p.2: "be applied [to] directly learn"

**4) Conclusion**
Despite the limitations in experimental complexity, this is a compelling and well-executed paper. The authors’ thorough responses to pre-review questions and subsequent revisions demonstrate a strong understanding of the work and a clear ability to address the suggestions outlined above, potentially leading to a significantly improved manuscript.
