This paper explores the advantages of visual servoing through the use of a learned visual representation. The authors introduce a method for learning an action-conditional bilinear model of visual features – extracted from a pre-trained VGG network – and subsequently deriving a control policy via dynamics linearization. A novel multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected bilinear model is presented. To address the single-step prediction horizon of the bilinear model, a weighted objective function is proposed, integrating long-term policy values. The evaluation is performed using a fitted-value approach. The work is presented clearly, with strong mathematical foundations and comprehensive conceptual coverage. The experimental results effectively demonstrate the value of the weighted objective, representing a significant contribution. Notably, this paper appears to be the first to detail a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm. Empirical evidence also supports the benefit of utilizing pre-trained visual features for improved generalization. 

Overall, I recommend this paper for acceptance, as it offers substantial value to the robotics research community. However, considering the focus of this conference, I believe the contribution concerning the “representation” aspect is somewhat limited. While the paper demonstrates the utility of a pre-trained VGG representation, it does not explore end-to-end learning of the representation itself – though it doesn’t necessarily need to. The paper’s emphasis is disproportionately on the control problem compared to representation learning. Furthermore, the policy representation is fixed, and value approximation relies on a linear form utilizing problem-specific features. This does not diminish the paper’s merit, but potentially positions it as less central to the core themes of ICLR.
