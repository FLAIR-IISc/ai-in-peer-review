1) **Summary**

This paper proposes using spatial feature maps from pre-trained convolutional networks for visual serving (especially target tracking) in general image classification. The authors combine a two-linear model of one-step dynamics of visual feature maps across multiple scales with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a serving policy. This policy is learned by reducing the average of a regular weight of the distance of the features predicted by the aforementioned model of visual dynamics.

2) **Contributions**

*   Controlled experiments in simulations measuring the utility of pre-trained deep features for visual serving.
*   Clear performance advantage compared to many realistic baselines, including the use of ground truth bounding boxes.
*    principled learning of multi-scale visual feature weights with an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm to address confounding factors.
*   Good sample efficiency for Q-function approximators and model-based one-step visual dynamics choices.
*   Open-source virtual city environment for visual serving benchmarking.

3) **Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **More Complex Benchmarks:**
    Although the environment is not just a toy synthetic, the tests would greatly benefit from more complex visual scenarios (debris, confusion, variation in appearance and speed, environmental richness and diversity, etc.). At the very least, the reality and diversity of physical presence could be greatly improved by using more realistic and diverse 3D car models, which could be obtained, for example, from Google SketchUp and the environment could be filled with more confusing cars (traffic or parked). This is important because the main desired quality of the method is robustness to visual variation.
*   **End-to-End and Presentation Learning:**
    Although improvements in current synthetic tests are already significant, it would be interesting to measure the impact of end-to-end training (i.e. fine-tuning the convent), as it may be needed for better generalization in more challenging visual scenarios. This would also allow measuring the benefits of deep presentation learning for visual serving, which would be relevant for ICLR (there is no presentation learning yet, although the authors briefly mention that the method could be directly adapted).
*   **Reproducibility:**
    Although the formalisms and algorithms are clearly explained, there is a slightly overwhelming repository of described practical techniques and implementation details with varying levels of description across papers and appendices. It would be helpful to consolidate, simplify, or reconstruct the presentation of these implementation details, but perhaps a better approach would be to simply summarize the most important points and link to an open-source implementation of the method for completeness.
*   **Typography:**
    p.2: "Learning is a relative[ly] recent addition"
    p.2: "To be applied directly to learning"

4) **Conclusion**

Despite the above limitations of the tests, this paper is interesting and challenging, partly thanks to the excellent answers to the pre-review questions and the subsequent improved corrections. It leads me to believe that the authors are able to significantly follow the suggestions mentioned above for improvement, resulting in a better paper.
