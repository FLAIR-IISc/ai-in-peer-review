This paper studies the benefits of visual servoing built on a learned visual representation. The authors first learn an action-conditional bilinear model over visual features extracted from a pre-trained VGG network, and then derive a control policy by linearizing the resulting dynamics. They further introduce a multi-scale, multi-channel, locally connected variant of the bilinear model. Because the bilinear model predicts only one step ahead, the paper proposes a weighted objective that incorporates the long-term value of the current policy. Policy evaluation is handled via a fitted-value approach.

The paper is well written, mathematically sound, and conceptually thorough. The experiments clearly demonstrate the advantage of the value-weighted objective, which I view as a key contribution. The work also appears to be the first to describe a trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm. Empirically, leveraging pre-trained visual features improves generalization. Overall, I recommend acceptance, as the paper will be useful to many researchers in robotics.

That said, within the context of ICLR, the contribution to the “representation” question feels limited. The results show that a pre-trained VGG representation is useful, but the paper does not explore end-to-end learning of the representation. To be clear, I am not arguing it must be end-to-end; rather, the emphasis of the paper is predominantly on control, not on representation learning. In addition, the policy representation is fixed, and the value function is approximated linearly using problem-specific features. This does not diminish the technical value of the work, but it does make it less aligned with what I view as ICLR’s primary focus.
