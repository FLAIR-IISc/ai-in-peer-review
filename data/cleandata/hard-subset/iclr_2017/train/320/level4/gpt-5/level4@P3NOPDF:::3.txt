1) Summary
The paper addresses visual servoing for target following by leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on image classification. The core idea is to model one-step dynamics of multi-scale visual feature maps with bilinear predictors and to learn a servoing policy that minimizes a regularized, weighted distance to the features predicted by this dynamics model. The feature weights are learned via a reinforcement learning procedure tailored to handle distractors, resulting in a policy that is robust to appearance variation without requiring explicit object detectors or bounding boxes.

2) Strengths and Contributions
- Well-designed controlled simulations that isolate and quantify the value of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing.
- Clear performance gains over strong and sensible baselines, including methods that use ground-truth bounding boxes.
- Principled learning of multi-scale feature weights using a trust-region fitted Q-iteration variant that effectively addresses distractors.
- Good sample efficiency, attributed to the bilinear one-step feature dynamics and the chosen Q-function approximator.
- Release of an open-source virtual city environment that provides a useful benchmark for the community.

3) Suggestions for Improvement
- Richer benchmarks and visual diversity:
  - While the environment goes beyond simple synthetic settings, the evaluation would be significantly strengthened by more complex visual conditions, including increased clutter, more diverse distractors, and greater variation in appearance and motion.
  - Consider expanding the set of 3D vehicle models with more realistic and varied assets (e.g., from Google SketchUp or similar sources) and populating the scenes with additional vehicles (both in traffic and parked). This would better stress-test the method’s claimed robustness to visual variation.
- End-to-end and representation learning:
  - The current approach relies on fixed pre-trained features. It would be valuable to report results with end-to-end training that fine-tunes the convolutional backbone, especially under more challenging visual conditions where domain shift may be significant.
  - Such experiments would directly quantify the benefits of learned representations for visual servoing and align well with ICLR’s emphasis on representation learning. The paper notes this is straightforward to incorporate; a concrete ablation would make the case more compelling.
- Reproducibility and presentation of implementation details:
  - The paper and appendix contain many implementation details of varying granularity, which can be overwhelming. Consider reorganizing these into a concise main-text summary of the most impactful techniques, with a clearly structured appendix.
  - Releasing an open-source implementation of the full method (in addition to the environment) would greatly enhance reproducibility and facilitate community adoption.
  - A brief “practical tips and pitfalls” subsection could further aid practitioners, highlighting which components are essential versus optional.
- Clarifications:
  - Provide intuition on when bilinear feature dynamics are expected to hold across different convolutional layers and motion regimes, and discuss failure modes (e.g., large viewpoint changes, heavy occlusions).
  - Include a small sensitivity analysis for the regularization and weighting scheme, and an ablation over feature pyramid levels to disentangle their contributions.

4) Minor Issues and Typos
- p.2: “learning is a relative[ly] recent addition”
- p.2: “be applied [to] directly learn”
- Where possible, standardize notation and symbols across the main text and appendix to reduce cognitive load.

5) Overall Assessment
This is an interesting and solid paper that brings together pre-trained deep visual features, a simple yet effective model of feature dynamics, and a tailored reinforcement learning approach to produce a robust visual servoing policy. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate benefits over strong baselines, including those with ground-truth boxes, and the method exhibits notable sample efficiency. The open-source environment is a useful contribution.

The main limitation lies in the experimental scope: the visual diversity and complexity of the current benchmark are limited, making it difficult to fully assess robustness under realistic, highly variable conditions. Additionally, evaluating an end-to-end variant that fine-tunes the representation would more conclusively establish the role of learned features for this task.

Despite these caveats, the technical approach is well-motivated and carefully executed, and the authors’ responses clarify key design choices. I expect that addressing the suggestions above—particularly expanding the benchmark’s complexity, adding end-to-end ablations, and improving reproducibility through code release and streamlined implementation guidance—would significantly strengthen the paper and broaden its impact.
