Review- 1) Summary
This paper aims to address visual servoing (specifically, target following) by using spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on general image classification. The authors combine bilinear models of the one-step dynamics of multi-scale visual feature maps with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy. This policy is obtained by minimizing a regularized, weighted average of distances to features predicted by the learned visual dynamics model.

2) Contributions
+ Controlled simulation studies that quantify the utility of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing.
+ Clear performance improvements over numerous reasonable baselines, including those that use ground-truth bounding boxes.
+ Principled learning of multi-scale visual feature weights with an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm to handle distractors.
+ Good sample efficiency due to the chosen Q-function approximator and the model-based one-step visual feature dynamics.
+ An open-source virtual city environment for benchmarking visual servoing.

3) Suggestions for improvement
- More complex benchmark:
Although the environment is more than a toy synthetic setup, the experiments would benefit substantially from richer visual conditions (clutter, distractors, broader appearance and motion variation, increased environmental richness and diversity, etc.). At a minimum, realism and appearance diversity could be markedly improved by using a larger pool of 3D car models—including more realistic and varied ones obtainable from sources such as Google SketchUp—and by adding more distractor vehicles (in traffic or parked). This is important because the method’s primary goal is robustness to visual variation.
- End-to-end and representation learning:
While the current synthetic experiments already show significant gains, it would be valuable to evaluate end-to-end training (i.e., fine-tuning the convnet), as this may be necessary for better generalization under more challenging visual conditions. This would also reveal the benefit of deep representation learning for visual servoing, which is relevant to ICLR (there is currently no representation learning, though the method appears straightforward to adapt, as briefly noted).
- Reproducibility:
The formalism and algorithms are clearly presented, but there is a somewhat overwhelming collection of practical tricks and implementation details, explained with varying depth across the paper and appendix. Consolidating, simplifying, or reorganizing these details would help; better yet, summarize only the most important ones in the paper and provide a link to a complete open-source implementation.
- Typos:
p.2: "learning is a relative[ly] recent addition"
p.2: "be applied [to] directly learn"

4) Conclusion
Despite the experimental limitations noted above, this paper is solid and interesting, aided by an excellent response to pre-review questions and the improved revision that followed. This suggests the authors are well positioned to address a substantial portion of the suggested improvements, resulting in an even stronger paper.
