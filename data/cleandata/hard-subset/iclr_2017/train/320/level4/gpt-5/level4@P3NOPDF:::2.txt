Summary
The paper studies visual servoing with a learned visual representation. The authors first learn an action-conditional bilinear dynamics model over visual features extracted from a pre-trained VGG network. A control policy is then derived by locally linearizing these dynamics. The paper introduces a multi-scale, multi-channel, locally connected variant of the bilinear model. Because the model predicts only one step ahead, the authors propose a value-weighted training objective that incorporates long-horizon returns under the current policy, and they perform policy evaluation using a fitted-value approach. The experiments show that the value-weighted objective improves performance and that using pre-trained visual features enhances generalization. The paper also appears to be among the first to outline a trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm.

Strengths
- Clear, well written, and mathematically solid. The modeling choices and derivations are carefully presented.
- The approach is conceptually thorough, connecting learned visual dynamics with control via local linearization.
- The value-weighted training objective is a notable contribution and is convincingly shown to improve performance.
- The empirical finding that pre-trained VGG features aid generalization is useful and relevant for the community.
- The trust-region adaptation of fitted Q-iteration is an interesting algorithmic contribution that could spur follow-up work.

Weaknesses and Concerns (ICLR fit)
- Limited contribution to representation learning. While the paper demonstrates that pre-trained VGG features are useful, it does not pursue end-to-end learning or even fine-tuning of the visual representation within the proposed framework.
- The paper’s emphasis is more on control and optimization (modeling dynamics, linearization, trust-region FQI, and value-weighted objectives) than on learning representations, which diminishes its alignment with ICLR’s core focus.
- The policy class is fixed, and the value function is approximated in a linear form using problem-specific features, further reducing the scope for learning flexible representations within the pipeline.

Suggestions for Improvement
- Provide an end-to-end or fine-tuning variant to assess how much performance and generalization are attributable to the fixed VGG features versus jointly learned representations. Even a negative result or a small-scale comparison would clarify the trade-offs.
- Analyze the sensitivity to the chosen feature extractor (e.g., different VGG layers, ResNet features) and to the structure of the bilinear model (e.g., importance of the multi-scale and locally connected components).
- Discuss the limits of local linearization in this setting, especially in environments with strongly nonlinear dynamics, and clarify how the trust-region mechanism mitigates deviation from the local model.
- Expand on the fitted-value procedure: function class, stability considerations, and how the trust-region constraint is enforced in practice. A brief ablation contrasting standard FQI versus the proposed trust-region variant would strengthen the claim.
- If feasible, compare against a stronger set of model-free baselines or recent model-based alternatives to situate the performance gains more clearly.

Overall Assessment
This is a strong and carefully executed paper that advances visual servoing by combining learned bilinear visual dynamics with policy derivation via local linearization, a value-weighted training objective, and a trust-region variant of fitted Q-iteration. The method is well motivated, technically sound, and empirically supported, and the observation that pre-trained visual features improve generalization is practically important. I recommend acceptance on technical merit and potential impact, particularly for the robotics community.

That said, the paper’s primary contributions lie in control and policy evaluation rather than in representation learning per se. The reliance on fixed VGG features and linear value approximations reduces its alignment with ICLR’s typical emphasis on learning flexible representations end-to-end. If the authors can add experiments or analysis that more directly engage with representation learning—through fine-tuning, end-to-end variants, or deeper study of the feature space—the paper’s fit to ICLR would be significantly strengthened.
