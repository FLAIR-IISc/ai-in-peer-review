Summary and contributions
The paper tackles visual servoing for target following by combining pre-trained deep visual features, learned predictive feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning scheme. The key ideas are:
- Represent observations with multiscale VGG features (modified to preserve spatial detail), and learn a short-horizon bilinear dynamics model that predicts how these features evolve under camera actions.
- Use a simple one-step, model-based servo policy that chooses controls to minimize the predicted next-step discrepancy to a goal feature map.
- Learn per-channel, per-scale feature weights (and control regularization) via a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) approach, using a Q-function linear in the weights so that the greedy action coincides with the servo policy.
- Demonstrate, in a synthetic quadcopter–car-following benchmark, that this approach is substantially more sample-efficient than model-free deep RL, more robust than image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and generalizes across previously unseen cars.

Decision: Reject
Reason(s):
1) Empirical validation is limited and missing critical ablations that directly support the central design choices (locally connected vs fully connected vs convolutional bilinear dynamics; modified vs original VGG; the proposed FQI optimization vs standard target-network approaches). 
2) The evaluation is restricted to a single synthetic benchmark, with robustness claims (to occlusion, distractors, and viewpoint changes) largely qualitative rather than quantified, making it hard to assess generality.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses a timely and important problem: learning visual servoing policies that are robust yet data-efficient. The motivation to leverage pre-trained features to avoid large-scale task-specific data collection, and to pair them with learned predictive feature dynamics, is well placed in the literature. The use of bilinear models to capture image/feature motion under camera control is sensible and computationally attractive.
- Technical approach: The formulation of the servo policy as a one-step optimal control in feature space is elegant and yields an analytically tractable Q-function linear in the feature/control weights. The observation that scaling the Q-parameters does not change the greedy policy is cleverly exploited to stabilize and speed up FQI. Overall, the approach is coherent and the components fit together logically.
- Claims vs. evidence: While the synthetic car-following environment is appropriate for controlled study, several important claims are insufficiently substantiated:
  • The choice of locally connected bilinear dynamics is argued but not thoroughly validated experimentally against the fully connected and convolutional variants across the same features. The paper notes memory issues for high-dimensional features, but even a smaller-scale or per-layer ablation would strengthen the claim.  
  • The modifications to VGG (removing pooling and adding dilation) are motivated, yet there is no quantitative comparison to the unmodified network to show that spatial detail indeed improves servoing.  
  • The FQI optimization scheme is presented as a key contribution; however, there is no direct comparison to a standard fitted Q-iteration with a fixed target network (or to simply learning weights by minimizing a surrogate long-horizon loss), so it’s unclear how much of the gain is due to the proposed optimization versus the policy class itself.
- Experimental scope: Results are encouraging but narrow. Performance is reported on a single synthetic domain with five training cars and a held-out set. Although the paper includes several baselines (IBVS with ORB and tracker-based features, end-to-end TRPO, and un/weighted variants), the robustness claims (occlusions, distractors, viewpoint) are not quantified (e.g., success rate under controlled occlusion levels, performance vs. viewpoint perturbations). In addition, some baselines could be strengthened or clarified (e.g., hyperparameter tuning parity for TRPO; impact of providing ground-truth depth to IBVS; fairness in compute/time budgets).

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all required for acceptance)
- Provide ablations that directly test the core design decisions:
  • Locally connected vs fully connected vs convolutional bilinear dynamics on the same feature layer(s), at least on lower-dimensional layers to avoid memory issues, and with matched parameter counts if possible.  
  • Modified VGG vs original VGG (and possibly a lighter backbone) for servoing performance and run-time.  
  • The proposed FQI optimization vs standard fitted Q-iteration (target network held fixed), and vs simple supervised tuning of weights to minimize cumulative rollout cost on a validation set.
- Quantify robustness: report performance under controlled occlusion levels, distractor density, lighting, and viewpoint perturbations (e.g., success rate, time-to-goal, final error).
- Generalization: show transfer across more target categories or substantially different car appearances; evaluate sensitivity to the choice of VGG layer(s) and to the number of training trajectories.
- Metrics and reporting: include standard deviation/CI across seeds, success/failure rates, and normalized metrics (e.g., distance-to-target over time) to complement the undiscounted cost. Clarify compute budgets and run-time for policy execution and training across methods to support sample- and compute-efficiency claims.
- Realism and transfer: consider an additional benchmark (e.g., a different simulated environment or real-world video/robot experiments) to test generality and potential sim-to-real viability. If real experiments are out of scope, analyze failure modes on real imagery offline.
- Implementation details: discuss stability and convergence properties of the FQI procedure, sensitivity to the regularization ν, number of inner iterations K, and the exploration variance. Provide more detail on how validation data are used to select final parameters to avoid potential bias.

Questions for the authors
1) Dynamics model: Can you provide a quantitative comparison between the fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected bilinear dynamics on the same feature layers (even if on smaller layers), and discuss parameter-count vs performance trade-offs?
2) Feature backbone: How do results change when using the unmodified VGG (with pooling), or a lighter backbone (e.g., fewer layers)? Is there evidence that the dilated, pool-free modification materially improves servoing?
3) FQI vs standard target-network: How does your FQI with α,b re-scaling compare to standard fitted Q-iteration with a fixed target (or a target network updated every few iterations) in terms of convergence speed, stability, and final performance?
4) Robustness: Can you quantify robustness to occlusions, distractors, and viewpoint changes (e.g., controlled ablations)? Do the learned weights concentrate on semantically meaningful channels?
5) Generalization and data efficiency: How does performance scale with the number of training trajectories for RL (e.g., 5, 10, 20, 50)? How sensitive is performance to the choice of VGG feature layer(s)?
6) Baselines: Were TRPO hyperparameters tuned comparably to your method? Given IBVS baselines receive ground-truth depth, can you comment on why they still underperform and whether stronger geometric pipelines (e.g., dense photometric visual servoing with modern optimization) change the outcome?
7) Compute: What are the training and inference times for each method, including feature extraction? Could the approach run in real time on embedded hardware?

I believe these additions would significantly strengthen the paper and make its contributions clearer and more broadly convincing.
