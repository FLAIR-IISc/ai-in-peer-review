1) Summary of the paper’s claims and contributions
The paper addresses visual servoing for target following by operating in the space of spatial feature maps extracted from a convolutional network pre-trained for ImageNet classification. It learns a one-step, multiscale bilinear predictive model of these feature dynamics and uses a simple greedy servo that chooses actions to minimize the weighted Euclidean distance between predicted next-step features and a goal image’s features. Crucially, the method learns the per-channel, per-scale weights (and control regularization) via a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration procedure with a linear Q-function approximator whose greedy action recovers the servoing update. Empirically, the approach:
- Demonstrates that standard deep features (e.g., VGG conv layers with modified pooling/dilation) can support effective visual servoing.
- Achieves robustness to distractors, viewpoint and appearance changes, and occlusions through the combination of learned feature dynamics and learned feature weighting.
- Substantially outperforms classical IBVS with hand-designed features and model-free deep RL baselines, while being more than two orders of magnitude more sample-efficient than standard model-free deep RL.
- Learns effective policies using very limited RL data (about 20 trajectories), with code and a CitySim3D environment released for reproducibility.

2) Decision
Accept. The method is technically sound, well-motivated in the context of prior visual servoing and RL literature, and the empirical results are convincing, showing strong performance and remarkable sample efficiency against sensible baselines.

Key reasons:
- A principled, computationally efficient integration of pre-trained deep features, a bilinear predictive model, and fitted Q-iteration yields a servo that is both simple and effective.
- Clear performance gains over strong baselines (including CNN policies trained with TRPO and classical IBVS variants), with strong sample efficiency and open-source resources.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles target following as a visual servoing problem under realistic visual variation and occlusion, where hand-engineered features and interaction models tend to break down. The choice to reuse pre-trained features leverages invariances learned from large datasets, and the bilinear feature-dynamics model is well motivated by image motion under camera actions.
- Placement in the literature: The work sits at the intersection of IBVS/PBVS, video prediction, and RL. It improves over classical IBVS by learning both the visual representation and the dynamics in feature space, and over model-free deep RL by exploiting a task-aware model and a structured Q-function for sample efficiency. The related work is well covered and appropriate.
- Evidence and rigor: The simulator, tasks, and baselines are thorough and relevant. The comparisons include:
  - Multiple feature choices (pixels vs VGG conv1_2–conv5_3), with conv4_3 performing best and conv5_3 likely too spatially invariant.
  - Weight learning via FQI versus TRPO and no-weighting/CEM, showing FQI’s superior performance with drastically fewer samples.
  - End-to-end CNN policies trained with TRPO that require far more data and still underperform the proposed approach.
  - Classical IBVS using ORB and tracker-based bounding boxes (and variants with ground-truth depth/dynamics), where the proposed approach is both more accurate and significantly faster.
The ablations and qualitative trajectories support the claims about robustness to occlusions and distractors. The fitted Q-iteration formulation, including the analytic structure and optimization steps, is clearly specified.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not necessarily part of the decision)
- Richer benchmarks: While the current simulator is beyond a toy setup, the core claim hinges on robustness to visual variability. Consider expanding the dataset with more diverse and realistic 3D car models (e.g., from SketchUp), richer lighting/weather, and more clutter/distractors (traffic, parked cars). This would better stress-test generalization.
- End-to-end/representation learning: Evaluate fine-tuning the feature extractor (e.g., layers up to conv4) jointly with the bilinear dynamics and/or the weighting via RL or auxiliary losses. This would quantify how much task-specific representation learning improves generalization in harder visual conditions.
- Reproducibility and exposition: The method description is complete but scattered with practical details. Consider consolidating implementation notes into a single subsection or appendix and emphasizing the minimal set needed to reproduce core results, while pointing to the released code for completeness.
- Minor typos:
  - p.2: “learning is a relative[ly] recent addition”
  - p.2: “be applied [to] directly learn”

5) Questions for the authors
- Feature choice and transfer:
  - How sensitive are results to the choice of backbone and layer (e.g., ResNet vs VGG, conv3 vs conv4)? Have you tried batch-norm backbones or lighter models to improve runtime?
  - Did you explore partial fine-tuning of higher layers, and if so, how did it affect sample efficiency and robustness?
- Dynamics model and planning horizon:
  - The bilinear model is one-step; did you empirically assess multi-step rollout error and its impact on servoing? Would limited receding-horizon planning with the same model help in corner cases?
  - Any quantitative comparison between fully connected vs locally connected bilinear variants for semantic features (beyond memory constraints)?
- Fitted Q-iteration details:
  - The Q-function is linear in non-negative weights. How critical are the non-negativity constraints to stability/performance? Did you ever observe degenerate solutions (e.g., collapsing to a small subset of channels)?
  - How sensitive is performance to the regularization coefficient ν and to the α,b scaling step? Please include stability/variance across random seeds.
- Robustness and generalization:
  - Can you quantify robustness to occlusions and distractors (e.g., controlled occlusion ratios, distractor density)? Do weights learned on one target transfer to another without retraining?
  - Have you tested zero-shot transfer to real camera data (even limited qualitative results), and if so, what failed modes did you observe?
- Cost design and termination:
  - How sensitive are results to the cost function and τ threshold? Did alternative costs (e.g., image-based surrogates) change the learned feature weights meaningfully?
- Runtime and deployment:
  - Please provide a detailed runtime breakdown (feature extraction vs dynamics vs policy update) and discuss opportunities for acceleration (e.g., pruning, lighter backbones, caching Jacobians).

Overall, this is a strong and timely paper that advances learning-based visual servoing with a simple, elegant, and sample-efficient approach.
