Summary and contributions
- The paper studies visual servoing for target following by combining pre-trained deep visual features, learned predictive feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning scheme. The core idea is to servo in a learned feature space rather than pixels or hand-designed keypoints.
- A multiscale, locally connected bilinear dynamics model predicts how features change under camera motion, enabling one-step lookahead control.
- The policy minimizes the predicted next-step distance to a goal feature map with learned per-channel, per-scale weights. These weights are learned via a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) formulation with a linear Q-approximation tailored to the servoing objective.
- A key observation—policy invariance to non-negative scaling and bias in the Q-parameters—yields an elegant and efficient optimization of the Bellman error.
- On a synthetic car-following benchmark, the method reportedly achieves substantial gains in sample efficiency (orders of magnitude over model-free deep RL) and improved robustness to distractors and occlusions versus pixel/keypoint baselines.

Decision: Reject (borderline)
Primary reasons:
1) Experimental validation is limited to a synthetic, small-scale setup with missing ablations critical to the paper’s main claims (e.g., dynamics variants, feature choices, and the proposed FQI optimization).
2) Important design choices (e.g., VGG modifications, the relative benefit of the new FQI scheme) are not empirically substantiated, making it hard to assess generality and robustness.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses a timely problem—learning visual servoing that adapts quickly to new targets—by leveraging pre-trained features and predictive models. The motivation is well placed in the literature and clearly argues for feature-space servoing and sample efficiency.
- Approach: The locally connected bilinear dynamics is a reasonable middle ground between fully connected and convolutional alternatives, and the multiscale feature formulation is well motivated. The linear Q-approximation that mirrors the servoing objective is clean and leads to an efficient FQI procedure.
- Claims vs. evidence:
  - Dynamics design: Section 4 argues for locally connected bilinear dynamics, but there is no thorough empirical comparison across fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected variants, nor a clear analysis of accuracy vs. parameter count vs. runtime trade-offs.
  - Feature representation: The modified VGG (removing some pooling and adding dilation) is plausible, yet the paper does not quantify the impact of these changes versus the unmodified network, or compare alternative backbones. The claim that deeper layers (e.g., conv4_3) are best is suggestive but incomplete without such ablations.
  - FQI optimization: The central optimization insight (invariance to scaling and bias) is compelling, but the paper does not compare against a standard fitted Q-iteration with a fixed target (or a common target-network scheme) to isolate the benefit of the proposed two-step procedure.
  - Scope and realism: Results are confined to a single synthetic benchmark. While the environment is nontrivial, the lack of real-world or diverse simulated domains limits confidence in robustness and generality. The comparisons to IBVS and end-to-end policies are useful, but broader baselines (e.g., model-based MPC over learned dynamics, alternative feature predictors) and stronger ablations would strengthen the case.
  - Sample efficiency: The method appears highly sample-efficient relative to model-free RL, which is a clear positive. However, it is difficult to disentangle how much comes from the pre-trained features, the bilinear dynamics, or the FQI optimization without more ablations.

Suggestions to improve the paper (not required for the decision)
- Ablations:
  - Compare fully connected vs. convolutional vs. locally connected bilinear dynamics for accuracy, speed, and memory.
  - Quantify the impact of VGG modifications versus the original architecture, and assess sensitivity to layer choice and downsampling.
  - Contrast the proposed FQI optimization with a standard fitted Q-iteration using a fixed target network to demonstrate the practical gain from the scaling/bias step.
  - Assess sensitivity to the number of training trajectories, reward shaping, and regularization ν.
- Generalization and realism:
  - Include an additional domain or a small-scale real-world test (even if limited) to support claims of robustness to viewpoint changes and occlusions.
  - Report robustness under distractors not seen in training and under camera calibration errors or dynamics mismatch.
- Clarity and reproducibility:
  - Provide more details on the Jacobian computation and the runtime profile (feature extraction vs. control optimization), and include wall-clock latency.
  - Explain failure modes (e.g., when the goal is out of FOV) and how the weighting helps resolve conflicting feature signals across scales.
  - Clarify how goal images are selected and how sensitive performance is to goal misalignment.

Questions for the authors
1) Dynamics choice: Can you provide a head-to-head comparison (accuracy and runtime) among fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected bilinear dynamics? How does locality size affect performance?
2) Feature backbone: How do results change if you use the original VGG (no pooling changes, no dilation) or alternative pre-trained networks? Which layers strike the best spatial invariance/precision trade-off?
3) FQI variant: How does your two-step scaling/bias optimization compare to a standard fitted Q-iteration with a fixed target (or target network)? Does it improve stability, convergence speed, or final performance?
4) Generalization: How well does the learned policy transfer to new targets with markedly different textures/shapes and to environmental changes (lighting, weather)? Any evidence beyond the reported synthetic cars?
5) Real-world viability: What are the main obstacles to deployment on a physical platform? How sensitive is performance to camera calibration errors and actuation delays?
6) Robustness: How does the method behave when the target briefly leaves the FOV or is heavily occluded? Does the feature weighting consistently prioritize the correct channels in such cases?
7) Sample efficiency: Which component contributes most to efficiency—pre-trained features, the bilinear dynamics, or the FQI scheme? Can you quantify this via ablations?
8) Computational cost: What is the per-step latency on typical hardware, and how does it scale with feature resolution, number of scales, and action dimensionality?
