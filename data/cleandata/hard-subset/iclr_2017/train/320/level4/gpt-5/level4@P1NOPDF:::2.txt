This paper studies the benefits of visual servoing using a learned visual representation. The authors first learn an action-conditional bilinear model over visual features extracted from a pre-trained VGG network, and then derive a control policy by linearizing the resulting dynamics. They also introduce a multi-scale, multi-channel, locally connected variant of the bilinear model.

Because the bilinear model predicts only one step ahead, the paper proposes a value-weighted objective that incorporates the long-term returns of the current policy. The evaluation problem is handled via a fitted-value approach. The paper is well written, mathematically solid, and conceptually thorough. The experiments clearly demonstrate the advantages of the value-weighted objective, which I view as an important contribution. The paper also appears to be the first to outline a trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm. Empirically, leveraging pre-trained visual features improves generalization.

Overall, I recommend acceptance. The work is likely to be useful to many researchers in robotics.

That said, within the context of ICLR, the contribution to the “representation” aspect feels limited. The results show that a pre-trained VGG representation is effective, but the paper does not consider end-to-end representation learning. To be clear, I am not arguing that the approach must be end-to-end; rather, the emphasis of the paper is much more on control than on representation learning. In addition, the policy representation is fixed, and the value function is approximated in linear form using problem-specific features. This does not diminish the technical merit of the work, but it makes the paper somewhat less aligned with what I view as ICLR’s core focus on representation learning.
