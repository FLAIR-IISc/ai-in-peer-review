1) Summary
This paper tackles visual servoing for target following by leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on general image classification tasks. The authors combine bilinear models of one-step dynamics of visual feature maps at multiple scales with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy. The policy is learned by minimizing a regularized, weighted average of distances to features predicted by the learned visual dynamics model.

2) Contributions
- Controlled experiments in simulation quantifying the usefulness of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing.
- Clear performance gains over many sensible baselines, including those using ground-truth bounding boxes.
- Principled learning of multi-scale visual feature weights via an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm to handle distractors.
- Good sample efficiency due to the choice of Q-function approximator and the model-based one-step visual feature dynamics.
- An open-source virtual city environment to benchmark visual servoing.

3) Suggestions for improvement
- More complex benchmark:
Although the environment is not purely a toy synthetic one, the experiments would benefit greatly from more complex visual conditions (clutter, distractors, greater appearance and motion variety, richer and more diverse environments, etc.). At a minimum, the realism and diversity of object appearances could be substantially improved by using a larger number of 3D car models—including more realistic and diverse ones obtainable from Google SketchUp—and by populating the environment with more distractor cars (in traffic or parked). This is important, as the main desired quality of the approach is robustness to visual variations.

- End-to-end and representation learning:
Although the improvements are already significant in the current synthetic experiments, it would be informative to measure the impact of end-to-end training (i.e., also fine-tuning the convnet), which may be necessary for better generalization under more challenging visual conditions. This would also quantify the benefit of deep representation learning for visual servoing, which is relevant to ICLR. At present there is no representation learning, although—as the authors briefly note—the method can be straightforwardly adapted.

- Reproducibility:
While the formalism and algorithms are clearly explained, the paper presents a somewhat overwhelming collection of practical tricks and implementation details, described with varying levels of specificity across the paper and appendix. Grouping, simplifying, or reorganizing these implementation details would help. An even better approach would be to summarize only the most important ones in the main text and link to a complete open-source implementation for full reproducibility.

- Typos:
p. 2: “learning is a relative[ly] recent addition”
p. 2: “be applied [to] directly learn”

4) Conclusion
Despite the limitations of the current experiments, this paper is interesting and solid, bolstered by the excellent responses to the pre-review questions and the subsequent improved revision. I am confident the authors can address a significant portion of the above suggestions, which would further strengthen the paper.
