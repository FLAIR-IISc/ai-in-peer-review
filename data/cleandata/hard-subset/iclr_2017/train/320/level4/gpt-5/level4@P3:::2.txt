Summary and contributions
The paper tackles visual servoing for target following by combining learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics, and reinforcement learning. The core idea is to servo in the space of deep features rather than pixels or hand-crafted keypoints, using a one-step action-conditional predictive model and a simple greedy policy that minimizes the predicted feature error to a target. Key components include:
- A multiscale feature representation derived from a pre-trained VGG-16 with modified pooling via dilated convolutions, preserving spatial detail.
- A bilinear, locally connected predictive dynamics model over feature maps, linear in control and trained from image-action-image triplets.
- An analytic policy based on one-step lookahead with an exact linearization (due to bilinearity) and a weighted objective over feature channels and scales.
- A fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure that learns the feature and control weights so that the one-step objective aligns with long-horizon return, using a linear Q-approximator matched to the policy structure and a scaling/bias calibration step for stability.

On a challenging synthetic quadrotor car-following benchmark, the method learns effective servos with very few RL trajectories (about 20) and outperforms classical IBVS baselines (ORB keypoints and tracker-based) and model-free deep RL policies. The study includes ablations across VGG layers and shows best performance around conv4_3, with reasonable generalization to novel cars.

Decision: Accept
- Key reasons: (1) Strong, well-motivated integration of pre-trained visual features with learned predictive dynamics and a tailored, sample-efficient RL procedure; (2) Clear empirical evidence of robustness and large gains in sample efficiency and performance over relevant baselines.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses a well-known limitation in visual servoing—dependence on hand-crafted features and analytical Jacobians—and proposes to leverage invariances captured by modern CNN features while keeping data requirements low. This is well placed in the literature, contrasting with classic IBVS/PBVS and data-hungry model-free deep RL.
- Methodological soundness: The bilinear, locally connected predictive model is a principled choice for short-horizon camera-induced appearance changes and supports efficient Jacobian computation. The policy’s structure leads naturally to a linear Q-approximator, enabling stable and efficient FQI with a useful parameter scaling step. The approach is mathematically clear and the optimization problems are well specified.
- Empirical rigor: Experiments compare multiple feature layers, with/without learned weights, and against strong baselines (TRPO end-to-end from pixels, TRPO with same feature dynamics, classical IBVS with ORB and with a state-of-the-art tracker). Protocols separate training, validation, and test; results include generalization to novel cars. The method shows >100× sample efficiency over model-free deep RL and runs substantially faster than tracker-based IBVS.
- Claims support: The paper substantiates claims about robustness to distractors/occlusions (qualitative rollouts), benefit of deep features (conv1_2–conv4_3 outperform pixels and conv5_3), and sample efficiency of the FQI-based weighting. The analytic mapping from the weighted objective to the Q-function is correct, and the linearization is exact due to bilinearity.
- Scope and limitations: The visual representation is fixed (pre-trained VGG), and only the dynamics and feature weights are learned. While appropriate for sample efficiency and robustness, this limits exploration of task-adaptive representation learning. Experiments are in simulation, and the reward uses privileged state (true car position) for cost computation—reasonable in sim, but with implications for real-world deployment.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not central to my decision)
- Clarify novelty: Emphasize that the contribution is a sample-efficient FQI tailored to the analytic servo objective (with α/b calibration), not a “trust-region” FQI. A schematic of how the policy, dynamics, and Q-approximation interact would help.
- Quantify sample efficiency: Report exact numbers of steps/trajectories for each method (e.g., FQI uses S×10×100 steps; TRPO uses 50×4000, etc.) to make the two-orders-of-magnitude claim more explicit.
- Representation analysis: Provide diagnostics on spatial information retention vs. invariance across VGG layers (e.g., alignment error, gradient sensitivity maps) to support why conv5_3 underperforms.
- Ablations: (1) Compare fully connected vs. locally connected bilinear dynamics on semantic features if memory allows, or provide parameter/compute trade-offs; (2) Evaluate sensitivity to the number of feature channels and scales; (3) Report performance vs. number of RL trajectories.
- Transfer and real data: Discuss how the method might be adapted to real robots, where rewards cannot rely on ground-truth pose. Options include learned vision-based reward proxies, self-supervised target localization, or sparse human feedback.
- Interpretability: Visualize learned feature weights across channels/scales and relate them to semantic content. This could provide insights into which cues drive control.
- Baselines: Consider adding a strong model-based baseline that uses optical flow or learned dense correspondence to reduce potential advantages from privileged representations.

Questions for the authors
- Reward design: The cost uses ground-truth car position in the camera frame. How do you envision implementing a comparable reward in real-world settings? Have you evaluated sensitivity to reward noise or proxy rewards?
- Dynamics model: How does performance degrade with prediction/model error? Have you tried multi-step prediction losses or stochastic/bayesian variants of the bilinear model?
- Feature choice: What criteria guided the selection of conv4_3 as the best trade-off? Would lightweight backbones (e.g., shallower CNNs) achieve similar performance with lower latency?
- FQI stability: How sensitive is the algorithm to ν, the non-negativity constraints, and the α/b calibration? Do you observe divergence without regularization?
- Exploration: With very few trajectories, how critical is the Gaussian exploration scale? Did you try adaptive exploration or prioritized sampling of failure modes?
- Generalization: You train on five cars. How does performance scale with target diversity? Would fine-tuning the dynamics or the feature extractor on a small set of new targets further improve generalization?
- Real-time performance: Most compute is in feature extraction. Have you profiled the controller on embedded hardware or explored pruning/quantization to meet real-time constraints on a UAV?

Overall, this is a clear, thoughtful paper that advances visual servoing by uniting pre-trained deep features, predictive dynamics, and sample-efficient RL. Despite limited emphasis on end-to-end representation learning, the contributions are significant and practically relevant.
