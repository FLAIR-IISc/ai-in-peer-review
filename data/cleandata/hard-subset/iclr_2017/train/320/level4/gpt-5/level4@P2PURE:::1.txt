The paper proposes a novel Q-iteration-based approach to learning visual servoing. The main contributions are:
1. A bilinear dynamics model that predicts next-frame features (y_{t+1}) from the current frame representation (y_t) and action (u_t).
2. A Q-function formulation of servoing that learns channel-specific weights over features.
3. An elegant method for optimizing the Bellman error to learn the Q-function.

Pros:
- The paper carefully explores different ways to couple the action (u_t) and frame representation (y_t) to predict (y_{t+1}). It makes a convincing case for a locally connected bilinear model that balances computational cost and expressiveness.
- Weighting different feature channels to capture the varying importance of objects across channels is more effective than treating all channels equally; this is validated experimentally, as the unweighted variant consistently underperforms.
- The paper addresses the difficulty of optimizing the Bellman error in Q-learning with a solid scheme based on the key observation that scaling the Q-function parameters does not change the induced optimal policy. This enables a more elegant Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) approach compared to typical schemes that keep the target (c_t + γ min_u Q_{t+1}) fixed.

Cons:
- Although Sec. 4 provides thoughtful arguments for the architectural choices, I would have liked to see experimental comparisons among the fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected dynamics models.
- I would also have liked to see a direct comparison between the proposed FQI and an iterative approach that holds the second term in Eq. (5) fixed.

Experimental results:
- Overall, the experiments are not fully convincing due to the small scale and reliance on toy simulations, though the scarcity of benchmarks in this domain is acknowledged.
- As noted in the pre-review, the proposed modification to VGG requires empirical validation; in its current form, it is unclear whether the modified VGG would outperform the original.

Overall, the paper makes a solid technical contribution in terms of novelty and problem formulation, but it would benefit from stronger experiments, as suggested above, to substantiate its claims.
