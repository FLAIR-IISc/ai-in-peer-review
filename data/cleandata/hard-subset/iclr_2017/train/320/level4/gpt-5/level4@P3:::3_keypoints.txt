- Proposes model-based visual servoing using learned convolutional feature space instead of raw pixels or hand-designed keypoints.
- Combines multiscale VGG features (with modified pooling/dilation), a bilinear one-step feature dynamics model linear in controls, and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) to learn per-channel/scale feature weights and control regularization.
- Servoing policy uses a one-step lookahead that minimizes predicted feature discrepancy to a goal image, yielding an analytic linear Q-function and efficient learning.
- Experiments on a synthetic quadrotor–car following benchmark show strong performance and notable sample efficiency (effective with ~20 training trajectories).
- Significantly outperforms pixel-based policies, classical IBVS with hand-crafted features, and model-free deep RL baselines; matches or beats IBVS with a state-of-the-art tracker.
- Code and simulation environment are released.
- Decision: Accept — approach is well motivated, technically sound, and well positioned in literature.
- Strength: Elegant combination of pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, and linear Q formulation leading to a coherent, practical servoing system.
- Strength: Empirical evidence is strong with clear ablations across feature layers, weighting schemes, and competitive baselines; FQI is very sample-efficient.
- Approach rationale: Bilinear, locally connected dynamics fit camera-induced changes and enable tractable control; multiscale features plus learned weights handle distractors and target specificity.
- Rigour: Comparisons include different feature layers, weighted vs unweighted methods, classical IBVS variants, and end-to-end CNN policies; training details and costs are provided.
- Caveat: Experiments confined to simulation with limited appearance diversity and no real-robot validation.
- Caveat: Some baselines use privileged information (ground-truth depth/pose), making comparisons partially diagnostic rather than strictly apples-to-apples.
- Caveat: Reliance on pre-trained features without end-to-end adaptation may limit robustness in harder settings.
- Suggested improvement: Increase benchmark realism/diversity (more car models, traffic, lighting, occlusions) to stress-test robustness.
- Suggested improvement: Explore end-to-end or partial fine-tuning of feature extractor to improve spatial specificity and deeper-layer performance.
- Suggested improvement: Provide more ablations/diagnostics (per-scale contributions, weight sparsity, sensitivity to dynamics data and regularization, locally vs fully connected dynamics).
- Suggested improvement: Clarify and separate baselines with privileged information and summarize baseline inputs for clarity.
- Suggested improvement: Consolidate implementation details and provide ready-to-run reproducibility scripts with fixed seeds.
- Questions for authors: Sensitivity to backbone and layer choice (e.g., ResNet, self-supervised features) and pooling adjustments?
- Questions for authors: Are learned feature weights target-specific or task-generic; how do weights transfer or adapt to new objects?
- Questions for authors: Robustness to dynamics mis-specification (calibration error, latency) and gains from locally connected vs fully connected dynamics?
- Questions for authors: Sensitivity of FQI to regularization ν and scaling/bias fitting; any divergence or failure modes?
- Questions for authors: Quantitative performance under heavy occlusions or moving distractors and distribution of learned weights in such cases?
- Questions for authors: End-to-end latencies on embedded hardware and prospects for lighter backbones like MobileNet?
- Questions for authors: Any experiments with short-horizon (2–3 step) planning using the learned dynamics and their effect relative to one-step servoing?
