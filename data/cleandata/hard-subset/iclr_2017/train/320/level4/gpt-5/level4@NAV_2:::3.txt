1) Summary
The paper tackles visual servoing for target following by operating in the space of spatial feature maps from CNNs pre-trained on generic image classification. It combines multi-scale bilinear predictors of one-step feature-map dynamics with a reinforcement learning procedure to synthesize a servoing controller. The policy is obtained by minimizing a regularized, weighted sum of distances between the model-predicted next-step features and the goal feature maps.

2) Contributions
+ Carefully controlled simulation studies that quantify the value of off-the-shelf deep features for visual servoing.
+ Clear performance gains over a range of reasonable baselines, including those that exploit ground-truth bounding boxes.
+ A principled approach to learning multi-scale feature weights via an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration scheme to mitigate distractors.
+ Strong sample efficiency due to the chosen Q-function parametrization and the model-based one-step feature-dynamics predictor.
+ Release of an open-source virtual city environment to benchmark visual servoing.

3) Suggestions for improvement
- Richer benchmark:
While the setup goes beyond a trivial synthetic scenario, the study would be strengthened by more challenging visual conditions (heavier clutter, varied distractors, broader appearance and motion distributions, and richer environments). In particular, increasing the number and diversity of 3D car assets (e.g., sourced from Google SketchUp) and populating scenes with more distractor vehicles (traffic and parked) would better stress-test the claimed robustness to visual variation.
- End-to-end and representation learning:
Beyond the already positive results, it would be valuable to assess end-to-end fine-tuning of the backbone (i.e., adapting the convnet) to improve generalization under tougher visual conditions. This would directly measure the benefits of representation learning for visual servoing, which is pertinent to ICLR. At present, there is no representation learning, though the method appears readily extendable as briefly noted by the authors.
- Reproducibility:
The formal framework is clear, but the numerous practical heuristics and implementation specifics are presented with uneven granularity across the paper and appendix. Consolidating and streamlining these details would help; more importantly, pointing to a complete open-source implementation of the method (beyond the environment) would fully support reproducibility.
- Typos:
p.2: “learning is a relative[ly] recent addition”
p.2: “be applied [to] directly learn”

4) Conclusion
Despite the limitations of the current experimental scope, the paper is solid and compelling, aided by an excellent response to pre-review questions and a stronger revision. I am confident the authors can address a meaningful portion of the suggestions above, which would further elevate the work.
