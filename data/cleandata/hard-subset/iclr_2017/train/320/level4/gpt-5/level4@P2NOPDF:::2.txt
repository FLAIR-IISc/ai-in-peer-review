This paper studies the benefits of visual servoing built on a learned visual representation. The authors first learn an action-conditional bilinear dynamics model over visual features extracted from a pre-trained VGG network, and derive a control policy via local linearization of these dynamics. They further propose a multi-scale, multi-channel, locally connected variant of the bilinear model to better capture spatial structure. Because the bilinear model predicts only one step ahead, the paper introduces a value-weighted objective that accounts for long-term returns under the current policy, and addresses policy evaluation with a fitted-value approach.

The paper is clearly written, mathematically rigorous, and conceptually thorough. The experiments demonstrate the benefits of the value-weighted objective; I view this as a key contribution. To my knowledge, the work also provides the first formulation of a trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm. Empirically, leveraging pre-trained visual features improves generalization across tasks and settings.

Overall, I recommend acceptance: the ideas and results are likely to benefit many researchers in robotics and control. However, within ICLR’s focus, the contribution to representation learning is limited. The work shows that a pre-trained VGG representation is useful, but does not explore learning the representation end-to-end. While end-to-end learning is not strictly necessary, a substantial portion of the paper concentrates on control and policy optimization rather than representation learning. Moreover, the policy class is fixed, and value functions are approximated linearly with problem-specific features, which further reduces the representation-learning component. These choices do not diminish the practical value of the work, but they do make it less aligned with what I perceive as ICLR’s core emphasis.
