1) Summary
This paper tackles visual servoing—specifically target following—by leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on general image classification tasks. The authors combine bilinear models of one-step dynamics of visual feature maps at multiple scales with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy. The policy is trained by minimizing a regularized, weighted average of distances to features predicted by the learned model of visual dynamics.

2) Contributions
+ Careful, controlled simulation experiments that quantify the value of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing.
+ Clear performance gains over several sensible baselines, including ones that use ground-truth bounding boxes.
+ Principled learning of multi-scale visual feature weights via an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm, which helps handle distractors.
+ Good sample efficiency stemming from the chosen Q-function approximator and the model-based one-step visual feature dynamics.
+ An open-source virtual city environment for benchmarking visual servoing.

3) Suggestions for improvement
- More complex benchmark:
Although the environment is more realistic than a simple synthetic setup, the experiments would benefit from richer and more challenging visual conditions (e.g., increased clutter, stronger distractors, greater variation in appearance and motion, and overall environmental diversity). At minimum, the realism and diversity of object appearances could be improved by introducing a larger number of 3D car models, including more realistic and varied ones (e.g., from Google SketchUp), and by populating the environment with additional distractor cars (both in traffic and parked). This matters because the primary goal of the approach is robustness to visual variation.

- End-to-end and representation learning:
While the reported improvements are already significant in the current synthetic setting, it would be valuable to measure the impact of end-to-end training (i.e., fine-tuning the convnet) for better generalization under more challenging visual conditions. This would also directly assess the benefits of deep representation learning for visual servoing, which is highly relevant to ICLR. At present there is no representation learning component, although the method appears readily adaptable, as the authors briefly note.

- Reproducibility:
The formalism and algorithms are clearly described, but the paper presents a somewhat overwhelming number of practical tricks and implementation details, with varying levels of specificity across the main text and appendix. Grouping, simplifying, or reorganizing these details would improve clarity. An even better approach would be to summarize only the most important ones in a dedicated section and link to an open-source implementation for completeness and reproducibility.

- Typos:
p.2: “learning is a relatively recent addition”
p.2: “be applied to directly learn”

4) Conclusion
Despite the limitations of the experimental setup, this paper is interesting and solid, aided by an excellent response to the pre-review questions and a substantially improved revision. This gives me confidence that the authors can address many of the above suggestions, further strengthening the paper.
