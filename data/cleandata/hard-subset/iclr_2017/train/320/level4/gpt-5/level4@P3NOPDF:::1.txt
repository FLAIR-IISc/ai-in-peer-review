Summary
This paper proposes a Q-iteration approach to visual servoing that combines a bilinear dynamics model for predicting next-step features with a Q-function that learns per-channel importance weights. A key technical insight is that scaling the Q-function parameters does not change the greedy policy, which the authors exploit to devise a more stable fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure for minimizing the Bellman error.

Contributions
- A bilinear, locally connected dynamics model that predicts next-frame features y_{t+1} from the current features y_t and action u_t, chosen as a balance between expressiveness and computational tractability.
- A Q-function that assigns different weights to feature channels, allowing the method to prioritize task-relevant visual signals rather than treating all channels uniformly.
- An optimization scheme for Bellman error minimization that leverages the scale-invariance of the greedy policy, enabling a cleaner FQI-style update compared to methods that hold the target term fixed.

Strengths
- The paper thoughtfully explores design choices for connecting action and frame representation for feature prediction, and makes a convincing case for locally connected bilinear dynamics as a good trade-off between fully connected and convolutional models.
- Learning per-channel weights in the Q-function is a compelling idea that aligns with the intuition that not all visual channels contribute equally to the control objective. The empirical results showing consistent degradation when these weights are omitted support this design.
- The proposed Bellman error optimization is elegant and well-motivated. Recognizing that uniform scaling of Q-parameters does not affect the greedy policy yields a principled normalization that improves FQI stability and avoids ad hoc target-freezing strategies.

Weaknesses and Concerns
- While Section 4 argues for the locally connected bilinear model, the experimental evaluation does not sufficiently compare the three alternatives (fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected) under matched conditions. A direct, quantitative ablation would strengthen the claim that the chosen architecture provides the best balance between performance and efficiency.
- The paper mentions an iterative approach that holds the second term in Equation 5 (the cost-to-go term, c_t + γ min_u Q_{t+1}) fixed, but does not empirically or theoretically contrast it with the proposed FQI-based scheme. A head-to-head comparison would clarify the benefits and trade-offs of the scale-invariant FQI update relative to target-freezing approaches (e.g., convergence speed, stability, data efficiency).
- The experimental scope feels limited: tasks are small-scale and largely toy simulations. While the scarcity of established benchmarks in visual servoing is real, the current evidence base makes it difficult to assess generality and robustness.
- The paper proposes a modification to VGG features, but does not convincingly validate that the modified network improves downstream servoing compared to using standard VGG features directly. Without this ablation, it is hard to attribute observed gains to the architectural choices rather than other factors.

Questions and Suggestions
- Please include a direct comparison among the three dynamics parameterizations—fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected—using identical training data and evaluation metrics. Report accuracy of next-step feature prediction, control performance, and computational cost.
- Provide a clear experimental comparison between the proposed scale-invariant FQI update and an iterative scheme that freezes the target term, analyzing convergence behavior, variance across seeds, and sensitivity to hyperparameters. If possible, include a brief theoretical discussion of when the scale-invariant update is expected to dominate.
- Add an ablation evaluating the impact of learned channel weights versus uniform weighting across a wider range of tasks, and discuss whether the learned weights correlate with task-relevant visual objects or regions.
- Validate the modified VGG choice: compare original VGG features, the proposed modified variant, and potentially lighter backbones. Report both feature prediction accuracy and control success rates to disentangle perception and control contributions.
- If feasible, expand to more challenging and diverse environments (e.g., cluttered scenes, different lighting, distractors), or include transfer tests showing that a policy trained in one setting generalizes to new object instances or viewpoints.
- Clarify computational complexity and training data requirements, particularly for the locally connected bilinear model versus alternatives. A brief sensitivity analysis on dataset size and noise would be helpful.
- Release code and models to facilitate benchmarking and future comparisons in this emerging area.

Writing and Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-structured. The exposition of the Q-function scaling argument is particularly effective. Minor issues:
- Fix small typos (e.g., “objects”).
- Explicitly define all symbols at first use in Section 4 and Equation 5, and ensure consistent notation for the Bellman target c_t + γ min_u Q_{t+1}.

Overall Assessment
This paper offers solid technical contributions to learning-based visual servoing, notably the bilinear locally connected dynamics, per-channel Q-weighting, and a principled Bellman error optimization that exploits scale-invariance. These ideas are well-motivated and, in parts, empirically supported. However, the experimental evaluation is currently too limited to substantiate the central design choices and claimed advantages, particularly regarding the dynamics parameterization, the optimization scheme, and the modified VGG features. Strengthening the experiments along the lines suggested above would significantly improve the paper’s impact and persuasiveness.

In its current form, I view the paper as technically novel and promising, but in need of more comprehensive empirical validation to meet the standard of evidence expected for publication.
