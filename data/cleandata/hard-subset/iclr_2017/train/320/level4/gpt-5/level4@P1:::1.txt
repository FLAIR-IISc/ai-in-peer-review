1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a learning-based visual servoing framework that combines pre-trained convolutional features, a learned bilinear predictive dynamics model, and a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure to learn feature and control weights for a one-step lookahead servoing controller. The key ideas are:
- Learning short-horizon visual feature dynamics with a bilinear model, instantiated as locally connected bilinear dynamics to balance expressiveness and computational tractability.
- Defining the servoing policy as the minimizer of a weighted next-feature prediction error, with feature-channel weights learned via a Q-function that is linear in these weights.
- An elegant FQI scheme that leverages the invariance of the greedy policy to non-negative scaling of the Q-parameters, enabling a more stable and efficient Bellman error minimization.
- Empirical demonstrations on a synthetic car-following benchmark showing robustness to distractors and occlusions, improved sample efficiency over model-free deep RL, and advantages of learning feature weights over unweighted servoing.

2. Decision: Reject
Primary reasons:
- Insufficient empirical validation: the experimental evaluation is limited to a single synthetic setup, with small-scale experiments and no real-world or more diverse benchmarks.
- Missing ablations central to the paper’s claims: the work argues strongly for specific design choices (locally connected bilinear dynamics; VGG modifications), but does not provide thorough comparisons or ablations to support these choices.

3. Supporting arguments
- Question/problem addressed: The paper targets sample-efficient visual servoing that generalizes to new targets with minimal task-specific data, focusing on target following. This is a timely and important formulation.
- Motivation and relation to prior work: The approach is well-motivated by limitations of hand-engineered features and model-free RL sample inefficiency. Using pre-trained CNN features and short-horizon predictive models is sound, and the paper positions itself appropriately with respect to visual servoing, video prediction, and RL literatures.
- Do the results support the claims?
  - Positive: The idea of learning feature-channel weights for the servo objective is compelling and empirically useful; unweighted baselines perform worse. The FQI scheme that exploits scale invariance of Q-parameters is an appealing and principled optimization device. The method appears more sample-efficient than model-free deep RL on the synthetic benchmark.
  - Limitations: The evaluation is constrained to a single simulated task and provides limited evidence of robustness and generalization. The core modeling choice—locally connected bilinear dynamics—is not adequately compared against plausible alternatives (fully connected and convolutional bilinear variants). Similarly, the paper modifies VGG (e.g., removing certain pooling layers and using dilated convolutions), but does not empirically validate whether these modifications actually improve servoing performance versus using the original pretrained features. These omissions make it difficult to assess how much each design choice contributes to the reported gains.

4. Feedback to improve the paper (not all required for acceptance; offered to help strengthen the work)
- Ablations on dynamics modeling:
  - Compare fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected bilinear dynamics head-to-head (accuracy of feature prediction, runtime, memory, and downstream servoing performance).
  - Analyze sensitivity to the spatial neighborhood size and channel independence assumptions.
- Feature representation ablations:
  - Directly compare modified VGG vs original VGG features at matched layers and resolutions, both for prediction quality and servoing performance.
  - Provide a systematic study across VGG layers to quantify the trade-off between invariance and spatial precision (beyond noting conv5_3 underperforms).
- RL/optimization analysis:
  - Provide a direct comparison of your FQI scheme vs an iterative approach that keeps the target term (ct + γ minu Q(st+1, u)) fixed, to empirically validate the benefit of the scaling/bias optimization step.
  - Report convergence behavior, stability, and sensitivity to the regularization coefficient and exploration noise.
- Generalization and robustness:
  - Evaluate on additional target types and environments (e.g., different object categories, lighting/weather changes, camera intrinsics, distractor density) and consider a real-robot or higher-fidelity simulation.
  - Quantify robustness to occlusion and viewpoint changes with controlled stress tests.
- Efficiency and practical deployment:
  - Provide detailed runtime/memory profiling and discuss trade-offs for real-time control (e.g., alternative backbones lighter than VGG).
  - Clarify how the method scales with the number of channels and feature scales.
- Broader empirical comparisons:
  - Include stronger model-based baselines (e.g., learned latent dynamics with planning) and improved model-free baselines with equalized sample budgets and tuned architectures.
  - Add ablations on the number of trajectories used for FQI, and the impact of training data size for the bilinear model.

Questions for the authors
- Dynamics modeling:
  - Can you provide head-to-head comparisons among fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected bilinear dynamics for prediction accuracy and servoing efficacy?
  - How sensitive is performance to the local neighborhood size and to assuming channel-wise independence?
- Feature representation:
  - Do the modified VGG features materially outperform the original VGG features for this task? Please include a direct experimental comparison.
  - How do different VGG layers and the multiscale design trade off spatial precision and invariance for servoing?
- RL and optimization:
  - Empirically, how does your FQI scheme compare to an approach that keeps the target Q fixed when minimizing the Bellman error (i.e., without the scaling/bias step)? Does your method converge faster or to better solutions?
  - How sensitive is performance to the choice of regularization ν, discount γ, and exploration variance?
- Generalization and robustness:
  - How does the policy perform when the target changes class or appearance more drastically than in your current splits? Any results on real images or hardware?
  - How robust is the approach to mis-specified costs or partial observability (e.g., long occlusions)?
- Practical considerations:
  - What is the overall computational budget (training time for dynamics and FQI) and the runtime breakdown at test time? Could a lighter backbone (e.g., ResNet-18 or MobileNet) maintain performance?

Overall, the paper presents solid technical ideas and a clean formulation, but requires stronger, more comprehensive empirical evidence to support its central design choices and claims.
