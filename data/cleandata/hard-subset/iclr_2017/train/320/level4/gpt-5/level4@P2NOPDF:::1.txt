This paper introduces a Q-iteration-based method for learning visual servoing. Its main contributions are:
1) A bilinear dynamics model that predicts next-frame features from the current frame and action.
2) A Q-function formulation that learns channel-wise weights over features.
3) An elegant optimization scheme for minimizing the Bellman error to learn the Q-function.

Strengths:
- The paper thoughtfully explores how to couple actions (u_t) with visual features (y_t) to predict next-step features (y_{t+1}). The argument for a locally connected bilinear model—balancing computational cost and expressiveness—is clear and well-motivated.
- Learning channel-specific weights to capture the varying importance of objects/features is a compelling idea, and the experiments support its value: unweighted losses consistently underperform the weighted approach.
- The proposed treatment of the Bellman error is technically strong. Leveraging the observation that scaling the Q-function parameters does not change the greedy policy enables a cleaner fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure than typical approaches that keep the term (c_t + γ min_u Q_{t+1}) fixed. This is a notable and well-executed insight.

Weaknesses:
- While Section 4 presents solid intuitions for different dynamics architectures, the empirical evaluation is limited. A direct comparison among the fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected bilinear models would substantially strengthen the claims. Even small-scale ablations on the same tasks would be informative.
- The paper argues for the proposed optimization over alternatives that fix the second term in Eq. 5, but the empirical difference between the new FQI variant and such iterative baselines is not quantified. A head-to-head comparison (e.g., convergence behavior, sample efficiency, and final performance) would clarify the practical benefits of the proposed scheme.
- The experiments are small-scale and rely on toy simulations. While the scarcity of established benchmarks in this domain is a real challenge, the current evaluation leaves open questions about robustness, generalization, and sensitivity to design choices.
- The modification to the VGG feature extractor is not sufficiently validated. It remains unclear whether the modified network yields consistent gains over an unmodified VGG for the same tasks. Targeted ablations or transfer tests would help isolate the value of this design choice.

Overall assessment:
The paper offers a technically solid and novel approach to visual servoing, with clear contributions in dynamics modeling, Q-function design, and Bellman error optimization. The ideas are well-motivated and, in parts, elegantly executed. However, the empirical support is not yet commensurate with the conceptual contributions. Stronger experimental evidence—particularly:
- direct comparisons among the proposed dynamics architectures,
- ablations contrasting the proposed FQI optimization against variants that fix the second term in Eq. 5,
- and validation of the modified VGG against the standard backbone—
would significantly bolster the paper’s claims.

In summary, the work is promising and methodologically interesting, but it would benefit from a more comprehensive and rigorous empirical evaluation to demonstrate robustness and practical impact.
