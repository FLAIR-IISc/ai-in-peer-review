1) Summary and contributions
The paper tackles visual servoing for target following by learning to act in the space of visual features rather than raw pixels or hand-crafted keypoints. The method combines (i) pre-trained convolutional features (modified VGG), (ii) a multiscale, multi-channel bilinear predictive model of feature dynamics (including a locally connected variant), and (iii) a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration procedure to learn feature weights and control penalties that align one-step servoing with long-horizon objectives. The policy is the solution of a weighted one-step lookahead objective obtained via an exact linearization of the bilinear dynamics, yielding an analytic and efficient controller and a linear Q-function approximator. Experiments on a challenging simulated quadcopter–car-following task show substantial gains over pixel-based servoing, hand-engineered keypoints, and model-free deep RL, with strong sample efficiency (effective policies from roughly 20 trajectories) and some generalization to novel cars. The paper is clearly written, technically sound, and the empirical analysis convincingly supports the benefits of weighting features via fitted value estimation.

2) Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Strong empirical results and sample efficiency, with clear advantages over both classical IBVS baselines and model-free deep RL alternatives.
- A well-motivated, simple, and computationally efficient formulation that integrates pre-trained features, learned predictive dynamics, and fitted value estimation to produce a robust servoing policy.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses the limitations of hand-engineered visual features and analytic models in servoing, aiming for quick adaptation with minimal data. Leveraging pre-trained CNN features is a pragmatic design choice that provides useful invariances without large-scale task-specific data.
- Approach: The bilinear dynamics model over feature maps is well justified, and the multiscale, locally connected variant balances expressivity and parameter efficiency. The one-step lookahead controller is simple yet effective, and its structure enables a linear Q-function approximation where greedy action selection coincides with the servoing objective.
- Reinforcement learning component: The fitted Q-iteration variant, including a scaling/bias calibration step for policy-independent parameters, is a practical and stable way to learn feature weights and control penalties, aligning the surrogate one-step objective with long-term returns.
- Experiments: The work compares feature hierarchies (VGG layers), ablates weighting strategies (no weighting, CEM, TRPO), and evaluates against strong baselines (IBVS with ORB and state-of-the-art tracking, end-to-end CNN/TRPO). Results consistently favor the proposed method in both performance and sample efficiency. The analysis that deeper layers (up to conv4_3) perform best, while overly invariant features (conv5_3) degrade localization, is informative. Runtime considerations and generalization to unseen cars are discussed.
- Limitations: While pre-trained features aid generalization, the representation is fixed; the paper focuses more on control and value learning than on representation learning per se. The Q-approximation is linear in hand-crafted terms derived from the predictive model, which is problem-specific. These choices do not diminish the contribution to robotics, but they reduce the paper’s alignment with ICLR’s emphasis on learning representations end-to-end.

4) Suggestions to improve (helpful, not central to the decision)
- Clarify novelty: Position the fitted Q-iteration variant relative to existing FQI/NFQ methods and recent stabilizing tricks; explicitly state what is new (e.g., the policy-aligned linear Q-structure and the scaling/bias calibration).
- Representation discussion: Provide a brief analysis of sensitivity to the backbone and layer choice (e.g., VGG vs. ResNet, conv4_3 vs. conv3_x), and whether light fine-tuning on task data improves results without sacrificing sample efficiency.
- Cost/privileged information: The learning cost uses ground-truth target positions; discuss how to approximate this from images alone or via weak supervision, and the gap between training with privileged costs and deployment without them.
- Real-world considerations: Comment on transfer to real hardware, robustness to camera calibration errors, and deployment-time failures (e.g., long occlusions, extreme viewpoint shifts).
- Complexity and scaling: Report parameter counts and training times for the locally connected dynamics; discuss memory/runtime trade-offs and options for faster backbones to increase control frequency.
- Broader scope: Briefly indicate how the method would adapt to other servoing tasks (e.g., grasping, multi-object following) and to non-stationary or deformable targets.

Questions for the authors
- Feature sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to the choice of backbone and feature layer? Did you try alternative networks (e.g., ResNets) or modest fine-tuning?
- Weight transfer: Do learned feature weights transfer across targets of the same class (e.g., from one car to another) without re-learning?
- Dynamics coverage: The bilinear model implicitly captures camera and scene changes. How much of the observed improvement stems from modeling camera motion vs. target motion? Any quantitative ablation?
- Cost signal: The RL cost uses ground-truth positions. Can the method be trained with an image-based cost (e.g., learned reward or proxy cues) without privileged state?
- Stability and convergence: Does the FQI variant have any convergence guarantees under your linear Q-structure and non-negativity constraints? How does regularization weight ν affect stability?
- Exploration: How sensitive are results to the exploration variance and number of trajectories per iteration? Could uncertainty in the predictive model guide exploration?
- Scalability: What are the model sizes and training times for the locally connected dynamics at each scale? Any memory bottlenecks for higher-resolution feature maps?
- Robustness: How does performance degrade with increasing occlusion or lighting shift? Do different scales contribute differently under occlusion, and do learned weights reflect this?
- Controller details: Is the action minimization fully analytical at run time for all scales/channels? Any numerical issues (e.g., ill-conditioning) and how are they handled?
- Beyond car following: What changes are needed to apply the method to other servoing tasks (e.g., manipulation), especially where depth and viewpoint changes are more extreme?

Overall assessment
A clear, well-executed contribution with strong empirical evidence and practical impact for visual servoing. While the representation is not learned end-to-end, the integration of pre-trained features, bilinear feature dynamics, and fitted value learning is compelling and, in my view, merits acceptance.
