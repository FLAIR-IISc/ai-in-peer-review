This paper explores the advantages of visual servoing based on a learned visual representation. The authors first learn an action-conditional bilinear model over visual features (extracted from a pre-trained VGG network), from which a policy is derived by linearizing the dynamics. A multi-scale, multi-channel, locally connected variant of the bilinear model is introduced. Because the bilinear model predicts only one step ahead, the paper proposes a value-weighted objective that incorporates the long-term returns of the current policy. The evaluation is handled using a fitted-value approach. The paper is well written, mathematically rigorous, and conceptually thorough. The experiments also demonstrate the benefits of the value-weighted objective and this is an important contribution of the paper. The paper furthermore appears to be the first to outline a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm. Empirically, the use of pre-trained visual features is shown to aid generalization. Overall, I recommend this paper, as it would benefit many researchers in robotics. However, in the context of this conference, the contribution specifically on the “representation” problem seems limited. It shows that a pre-trained VGG representation is useful, but does not consider learning it end-to-end. This is not to insist it should be end-to-end, but proportionally the paper focuses more on the control problem than on representation learning. Also, the policy representation is fixed and the values are approximated linearly using problem-specific features. This does not make the paper less valuable, but perhaps less aligned with what I think ICLR should emphasize.
