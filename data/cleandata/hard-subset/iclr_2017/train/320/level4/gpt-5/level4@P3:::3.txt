Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a model-based visual servoing approach for target following that operates in the space of learned convolutional features rather than raw pixels or hand-designed keypoints. The method combines (i) multi-scale feature maps extracted from a pre-trained VGG network (with modified pooling/dilation to preserve spatial detail), (ii) a learned bilinear predictive model of one-step feature dynamics that is linear in the controls, and (iii) a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure that learns nonnegative per-channel, per-scale feature weights and control regularization coefficients. The servoing policy is a simple one-step lookahead that chooses controls to minimize the predicted feature discrepancy to a goal image; the specific parameterization yields an analytic, linear Q-function approximator and an efficient learning algorithm. Experiments on a synthetic quadrotor–car following benchmark show strong performance and notable sample efficiency: effective policies are learned with about 20 training trajectories, substantially outperforming pixel-based policies, classical IBVS with hand-crafted features, and model-free deep RL baselines, and matching or beating IBVS with a state-of-the-art tracker. Code and a simulation environment are released.

Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- The approach is well motivated, technically sound, and thoughtfully positioned in the literature, combining pre-trained features, a simple but apt bilinear dynamics model, and an efficient Q-learning scheme into a coherent, practical servoing system.
- The empirical evidence is strong within scope: clear ablations (across feature layers and weighting schemes), competitive baselines (including IBVS and deep RL), and very favorable sample efficiency.

Supporting arguments
- Specific problem: Learning a visual servo for target following that is robust to distractors, viewpoint changes, and occlusions, and that can adapt quickly to new targets with little data. This is a classic robotics setting where generalization and sample efficiency are crucial.
- Motivation and placement: The paper clearly articulates the limitations of hand-engineered features/Jacobians and data-hungry deep RL. It leverages widely validated transfer of ImageNet features and simple camera-induced feature dynamics to build a more sample-efficient alternative. Related work is comprehensive and accurate.
- Approach: The choice of a bilinear, locally connected feature dynamics model is well suited to camera-induced changes and computationally efficient; its linearity in controls enables a tractable one-step lookahead. Casting the servo objective as a linear Q-function in the feature weights is elegant and leads to a stable, efficient FQI procedure. The multiscale design is sensible, and learning feature weights addresses distractors and target specificity.
- Empirical support and rigor: The paper compares (i) different feature layers and pixels, (ii) weighted vs unweighted and alternative weight-learning methods (CEM, TRPO), (iii) classical IBVS with ORB and with an advanced tracker, and (iv) an end-to-end CNN policy. Results consistently favor the proposed method on both seen and novel cars, with clear generalization trends (conv1_2–conv4_3 outperform conv5_3). FQI attains strong performance with two sampling iterations, while TRPO requires far more data and still lags. Runtime comparisons indicate practical control rates. The cost function and termination conditions are specified, and training details are supplied.
- Caveats: Experiments are confined to simulation with limited appearance diversity (few car models), and no real-robot validation. Privileged information is used in some baselines (e.g., ground-truth depth for IBVS, ground-truth poses for PBVS) for diagnostic comparisons; as expected, PBVS with ground truth outperforms all image-based methods. The method relies on pre-trained features without end-to-end adaptation, which may limit robustness in more challenging settings.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (non-blocking, intended to help)
- Benchmark realism and diversity: Increase the variety and realism of car models (e.g., from larger repositories), add traffic and background clutter, vary lighting/weather, and include more occlusions. This would better stress-test robustness—the main selling point of the approach—and strengthen claims about generalization.
- End-to-end/representation learning: Explore fine-tuning of the feature extractor and/or joint training of dynamics and feature weights. Even partial adaptation (e.g., training the last conv block) might meaningfully improve spatial specificity and robustness (particularly for deeper layers like conv5_3).
- Ablations and diagnostics: Report the contribution of each scale and the sparsity/importance pattern of learned weights. Quantify sensitivity to the amount of dynamics data and to the regularization coefficient ν. Compare fully vs locally connected bilinear dynamics across features. Include a small multi-step planning baseline (e.g., MPC with the same learned dynamics) to contextualize the efficacy of one-step servoing.
- Baseline fairness and clarity: Clearly separate baselines with privileged information (e.g., ground-truth depth for IBVS, ground-truth pose for PBVS) from those without. Summarize all baselines and their inputs in one place for quick reference.
- Reproducibility: The paper is detailed, but implementation tips are scattered. Consolidate key engineering choices (feature preprocessing, optimization settings, Jacobian computation, linear solver details) into a single subsection, and provide ready-to-run scripts with fixed seeds to reproduce main figures.
- Minor typos: p.2 “learning is a relatively recent addition”; p.2 “be applied to directly learn.”

Questions for the authors
- Feature choice and transfer: How sensitive is performance to the backbone and layer choice (e.g., ResNet, GoogLeNet, self-supervised features)? Did you try other pre-trained networks or removing/adjusting additional pooling to improve spatial fidelity at deeper layers?
- Adaptation to new targets: Are the learned feature weights target-specific or task-generic? What is the initialization and data requirement when switching to a new object class, and can weights be regularized/shared across targets?
- Dynamics model: How does performance degrade if the bilinear dynamics are mildly mis-specified (e.g., camera calibration error, unmodeled latency), and can you quantify the gains of locally connected vs fully connected dynamics?
- Stability and convergence: How sensitive is FQI to the regularization ν and the scaling/bias fitting step? Do you observe divergence without the α,b initialization? Any failure modes?
- Robustness to distractors and occlusions: Can you provide quantitative breakdowns for sequences with heavy occlusions or moving distractors? How do learned weights distribute across channels in such cases?
- Runtime and deployment: What are the end-to-end latencies (feature extraction + dynamics + control) on typical embedded hardware? Could lighter backbones (e.g., MobileNet) maintain performance while increasing control rates?
- Beyond one-step: Did you experiment with short-horizon rollouts using the same dynamics (e.g., 2–3 steps) and, if so, do they help or hurt given model bias?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, carefully executed paper that advances visual servoing by leveraging learned features and predictive models with a principled, efficient RL formulation. I recommend acceptance.
