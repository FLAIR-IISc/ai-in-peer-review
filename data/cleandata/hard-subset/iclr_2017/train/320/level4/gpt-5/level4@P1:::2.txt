Summary of contributions
The paper studies visual servoing with learned visual representations. It proposes to (i) extract multiscale, multi-channel semantic features from a pre-trained VGG network (with adjusted pooling/dilation for spatial fidelity), (ii) learn an action-conditional bilinear predictive model of these features (including a locally connected variant to reduce parameters), and (iii) derive a simple one-step servoing policy via linearization of the bilinear dynamics. Because this one-step model alone is insufficient for long-horizon performance, the authors introduce a weighted objective over feature channels and scales, with weights learned to reflect long-term value. They formulate a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure with an analytic, linear Q-function approximator whose greedy action recovers the weighted servoing policy. Empirically, the method demonstrates robust target following on a challenging synthetic quadcopter–car benchmark (CitySim3D), shows substantial sample efficiency (e.g., effective policies from 20 training trajectories for RL), improves over pixel/keypoint baselines and model-free deep RL, and leverages pre-trained features for better generalization to novel cars. The paper is well written, mathematically solid, and conceptually thorough. The value-weighted objective and fitted evaluation procedure are key contributions. It also seems to be the first to outline a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm.

Decision
Accept. The paper offers a clear, well-motivated approach that connects learned features, predictive models, and sample-efficient RL for visual servoing, with strong empirical support. My main reservation is that the representation learning contribution is limited (features are not learned end-to-end), which makes the paper slightly less aligned with ICLR’s emphasis on representation learning.

Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: The work tackles sample-efficient visual servoing that must be robust to viewpoint changes, appearance variation, and occlusions, and that can quickly adapt to new targets.
- Motivation and placement: Leveraging pre-trained deep features is sensible given their broad transferability; using bilinear dynamics is well motivated and computationally attractive; and learning feature weights via FQI connects one-step predictive control to long-horizon objectives. Related work in IBVS/PBVS, video prediction, and deep RL is appropriately cited and contrasted.
- Claims and rigor: The approach is specified clearly, with a principled Q-function design that yields the intended greedy policy. The FQI variant with scaling/bias calibration is reasonable and regularized. Experiments compare multiple representations (pixels, VGG layers), dynamics variants (fully vs locally connected), weighting strategies (none, CEM, TRPO), and alternative policies (end-to-end CNN with TRPO, classical IBVS/PBVS, and trackers). Results consistently support the paper’s claims: VGG features up to conv4_3 outperform pixels, the value-weighted objective learned by FQI materially improves performance and sample efficiency over TRPO/CEM, and the approach is competitive with or surpasses strong baselines under realistic noise/occlusion. Code, simulator, and videos are provided.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not central to my decision)
- Scope and positioning: The representation component relies on off-the-shelf features; consider clarifying this choice and its implications for ICLR. A brief discussion of why end-to-end feature learning was not pursued (e.g., sample constraints, overfitting risks) would help.
- End-to-end variants: Even a small-scale experiment fine-tuning the last few VGG blocks, or jointly training features with the bilinear model using the same data budget, would strengthen the representation-learning angle and test whether task-specific adaptation helps beyond conv4_3.
- Analysis of the weighting: Provide more insight into which channels/scales receive high weights, and whether they align with car semantics. This could illuminate how the value function focuses attention.
- Ablations: Add ablations on the bilinear model (fully vs locally connected, kernel size, inclusion of action-independent bias) and the impact of the linearization step on control quality.
- Generalization: Report performance across different pre-trained backbones (e.g., ResNet) and feature layers, and discuss why conv5_3 underperforms (likely excessive spatial invariance).
- Algorithmic clarity: The FQI variant with α and b is described as accelerating policy evaluation; if calling it “trust-region,” clarify the connection to trust-region updates and whether any explicit constraint is enforced.
- Real-world considerations: Comment on transfer to real robots, domain shift, and robustness to calibration errors. Timing breakdowns indicate VGG dominates cost; consider mentioning lightweight feature extractors for deployment.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive is performance to the choice of VGG layer and backbone? Did you try ResNet or lighter models, and do the learned weights consistently down-weight highly invariant deeper layers?
2. In the learned weighting, do high-weight channels correspond to semantically meaningful car features? Any visualization or sparsity patterns across scales?
3. How critical are the action-independent biases in the bilinear model for handling moving distractors or car dynamics?
4. Could you elaborate on the “trust-region” aspect of your FQI variant? Is there an explicit constraint on parameter updates, or is α,b calibration primarily a normalization trick?
5. How does performance degrade when the bilinear model is imperfect? For example, if dynamics are learned from fewer trajectories, does FQI compensate via reweighting?
6. Did you attempt limited end-to-end fine-tuning of feature extractors with the same data budget? If so, how did it compare?
7. How robust is the method to real-world noise (e.g., rolling shutter, illumination changes), camera miscalibration, and deviations from the simulated dynamics?
8. What is the relative benefit of multi-scale features versus single-scale when the target starts far from the goal pose?
9. For the TRPO baseline on the servoing policy, do you observe instability or local minima due to the matrix inverse layer? Any tricks that improved training stability?
10. Could the Q-approximation be expanded beyond linear weights without losing the greedy-policy equivalence, or is linearity essential to maintain tractability?
