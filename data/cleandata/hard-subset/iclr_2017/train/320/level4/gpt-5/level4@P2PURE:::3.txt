1) Summary
This paper tackles visual servoing—specifically target following—by leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on image classification. The method combines bilinear models of one-step dynamics of multi-scale visual feature maps with a reinforcement learning approach to learn a servoing policy. The policy is trained by minimizing a regularized, weighted average of distances to features predicted by the learned visual dynamics model.

2) Contributions
- Controlled simulation experiments that quantify the utility of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing.
- Clear performance gains over several strong baselines, including those using ground-truth bounding boxes.
- Principled learning of multi-scale visual feature weights via an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm to address distractors.
- Good sample efficiency enabled by the chosen Q-function approximator and the model-based one-step visual feature dynamics.
- An open-source virtual city environment for benchmarking visual servoing.

3) Suggestions for improvement
- More complex benchmark:
While the environment is more realistic than a simple synthetic setup, the experiments would benefit from richer visual conditions (e.g., clutter, stronger distractors, greater appearance and motion variability, and more diverse environments). At a minimum, realism and diversity of object appearances could be substantially improved by incorporating a larger set of 3D car models—including more realistic and varied ones obtainable from sources like Google SketchUp—and by adding more distractor vehicles (both in traffic and parked). This is important given that robustness to visual variation is a central goal of the approach.
- End-to-end and representation learning:
Although the current synthetic experiments already show significant improvements, it would be informative to evaluate end-to-end training (i.e., fine-tuning the convnet), which may be necessary for better generalization under more challenging visual conditions. This would also help quantify the benefits of deep representation learning for visual servoing—highly relevant to ICLR—since there is currently no representation learning, even though the method could be straightforwardly adapted as the authors note.
- Reproducibility:
The formalism and algorithms are well explained, but there is a somewhat overwhelming number of practical tricks and implementation details presented with varying levels of specificity across the paper and appendix. Grouping, simplifying, or reorganizing these details would help. Ideally, the paper would summarize only the most crucial implementation points in one section and provide a link to a complete open-source implementation for full reproducibility.
- Typos:
p.2: “learning is a relative[ly] recent addition”
p.2: “be applied [to] directly learn”

4) Conclusion
Despite the limitations of the current experimental setup, this paper is interesting and solid, bolstered by the strong response to the pre-review questions and the improved revision. This gives confidence that the authors can meaningfully address the above suggestions, leading to an even stronger paper.
