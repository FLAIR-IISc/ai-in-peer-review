1) Summary
This paper addresses visual servoing for target following by leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on image classification. The authors model one-step dynamics of multi-scale visual feature maps with bilinear transformations and couple this with a reinforcement learning framework to learn a servoing policy. The policy is trained by minimizing a regularized, weighted distance to features predicted by the learned visual dynamics model.

2) Contributions
- Careful simulation studies that quantify the value of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing.
- Clear performance gains over strong baselines, including those that use ground-truth bounding boxes.
- A principled approach for learning multi-scale feature weights via an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm to handle distractors.
- Strong sample efficiency due to the chosen Q-function approximator and the model-based one-step visual feature dynamics.
- Release of an open-source virtual city environment for benchmarking visual servoing.

3) Suggestions for improvement
- More challenging benchmarks:
While the environment goes beyond a toy setup, the experiments would benefit from richer visual complexity (e.g., clutter, distractors, varied appearance and motion, and more diverse scenes). At minimum, object appearance realism and diversity could be improved by incorporating a larger set of 3D car models—potentially sourced from repositories such as Google SketchUp—and by populating the environment with additional distractor vehicles (both in traffic and parked). This is particularly important given the method’s goal of robustness to visual variation.
- End-to-end and representation learning:
Although the reported gains are promising, it would be valuable to assess end-to-end training—i.e., fine-tuning the convolutional network—to test generalization under more challenging visual conditions. This would also directly measure the contribution of deep representation learning to visual servoing, which aligns with ICLR’s focus. As the authors note, adapting the method for end-to-end learning appears straightforward.
- Reproducibility and presentation:
The formalism and algorithms are clearly presented, but the paper includes an extensive set of practical tricks and implementation details, sometimes described with uneven granularity across the main text and appendix. Consolidating and reorganizing these details would improve clarity. Ideally, summarize the most critical implementation aspects in a dedicated section and provide a link to a complete open-source implementation to ensure reproducibility.
- Typos:
p.2: “learning is a relative[ly] recent addition” → “learning is a relatively recent addition”
p.2: “be applied [to] directly learn” → “be applied to directly learn”

4) Conclusion
Despite the experimental limitations noted above, the paper is solid and interesting, strengthened by the authors’ thorough response to the pre-review and the resulting revisions. I am confident the authors can address many of the suggested improvements, which would further enhance the paper’s impact.
