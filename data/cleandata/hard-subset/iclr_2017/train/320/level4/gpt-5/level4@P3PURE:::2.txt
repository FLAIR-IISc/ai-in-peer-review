This paper investigates the benefits of visual servoing using a learned visual representation. The authors first learn an action-conditional bilinear model over visual features extracted from a pre-trained VGG network, from which a control policy is derived via a local linearization of the dynamics. They present a multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally connected variant of the bilinear model. Because the bilinear model predicts dynamics only one step ahead, they propose a weighted objective that incorporates the long-term values of the current policy. The evaluation problem is addressed using a fitted-value approach. The paper also appears to be the first to outline a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm.

The paper is well written, mathematically solid, and conceptually thorough. The experiments demonstrate the benefits of using a value-weighted objective, which is an important contribution. The use of pre-trained visual features is also shown, empirically, to improve generalization.

Overall, I recommend acceptance, as the work will benefit many researchers in robotics. However, in the context of ICLR, the contribution to the “representation” problem is limited. The results show that a pre-trained VGG representation is useful, but the paper does not consider learning the representation end-to-end. This is not to say it must be end-to-end, but proportionally the paper focuses more on the control problem than on representation learning. In addition, the policy representation is fixed, and the values are approximated in linear form using problem-specific features. This does not make the paper less valuable, but it makes it less aligned with what I believe ICLR should emphasize.
