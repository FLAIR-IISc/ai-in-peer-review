1. Summary of contributions
The paper studies visual servoing using learned visual representations. The authors first train an action-conditional bilinear dynamics model over visual features extracted from a pre-trained VGG network, and derive a servoing policy via linearization of these dynamics. They introduce a multi-scale, multi-channel, locally connected bilinear variant. Because the predictive model is one-step, they propose a weighted objective that encourages long-horizon performance, with weights learned via a fitted-value (fitted Q-iteration) scheme tied to the current policy. Empirically, the value-weighted objective is a key contributor to performance. The paper is clearly written, mathematically careful, and experimentally thorough. It also appears to be the first to describe a trust-region style fitted-Q iteration approach. Finally, the use of pre-trained features is shown to aid generalization.

2. Decision
Accept.
Key reasons: (i) Strong, well-motivated integration of learned predictive feature dynamics with a fitted-value objective, leading to substantial empirical gains and sample efficiency; (ii) Clear evidence that pre-trained features and the proposed weighting improve robustness and generalization.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: learning data-efficient visual servoing for target following that is robust to appearance/viewpoint changes and occlusions without manual feature engineering. This is a relevant and longstanding robotics challenge.
- Motivation and placement: The method leverages robust pre-trained CNN features to reduce data demands, uses a bilinear predictive model appropriate for short-horizon camera-induced feature changes, and learns feature/control weights via fitted Q-iteration to align one-step control with long-term returns. This is well positioned relative to classical IBVS/PBVS and contemporary RL/video prediction literature.
- Support for claims: Experiments on a challenging synthetic car-following benchmark show the proposed approach outperforms pixel/keypoint baselines, improves sample efficiency by large margins relative to end-to-end model-free deep RL, and generalizes to novel targets with mid-level VGG features. Ablations across feature layers indicate performance trends consistent with the argument that overly invariant deep features harm spatial precision. The value-weighted objective learned via fitted Q-iteration is pivotal to the gains. The methodology and comparisons are scientifically sound.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not central to the accept decision)
- Scope relative to representation learning: While effective, the paper’s contribution emphasizes control and fitted-value learning more than representation learning. It shows pre-trained VGG features are useful but does not attempt joint end-to-end representation adaptation. Discussing this design choice and offering evidence on whether limited fine-tuning would help (or hurt) sample efficiency would strengthen the positioning for a representation-focused venue.
- Policy/value parametrization: The fixed policy form and linear Q-approximation over problem-specific feature functionals aid tractability and data efficiency, but clarifying expressivity limits and potential extensions (e.g., learned nonlinear metrics, adaptive feature selection) would be valuable.
- Analysis depth: A brief sensitivity study on choice of VGG layer(s), the number of scales, and locally connected vs fully connected bilinear dynamics would contextualize robustness of the design choices.
- Clarity: Elaborate on the “trust-region fitted-Q” aspect (terminology, constraints, and how it differs from the scaling/bias initialization step). Also, a short computational profile (feature extraction vs optimization) would help practitioners.

Questions for the authors
- Trust-region FQI: What precisely is meant by “trust-region fitted-Q iteration”? Is there an explicit trust-region constraint in the Bellman error optimization, or does this refer to the α-scaling/bias stabilization step? How does this relate theoretically and empirically to standard FQI?
- Representation adaptation: Did you try any feature fine-tuning (e.g., last conv block) under a tight data budget? If not, can you comment on anticipated tradeoffs in sample efficiency and robustness?
- Feature choice sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to the selected VGG layers and the multi-scale weighting? Do locally connected bilinear dynamics consistently outperform fully connected variants when capacity permits?
- Failure modes and robustness: What are the dominant failures (e.g., heavy occlusions, strong lighting changes, fast target maneuvers)? How does the feature weighting handle scenes with multiple distractors of similar appearance?
- Comparisons and budgets: Could you provide exact trajectory/sample counts and wall-clock times for your method vs TRPO/CNN baselines to quantify the reported two orders of magnitude in sample efficiency? Are exploration noise levels and horizon lengths matched across methods?
