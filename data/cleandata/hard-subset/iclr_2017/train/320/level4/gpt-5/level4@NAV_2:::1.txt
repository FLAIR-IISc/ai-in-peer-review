1. Summary of claims and contributions (positive)
- The paper tackles visual servoing for target following by combining pre-trained deep visual features, a learned predictive dynamics model, and reinforcement learning. The central idea is to servo in the space of learned features rather than pixels or hand-engineered keypoints.
- It proposes a multiscale feature representation using a modified VGG-16 (reducing pooling and using dilated convolutions) and learns short-horizon feature dynamics via a bilinear model. A locally connected bilinear variant is advocated as a good trade-off between expressiveness and computational cost.
- The servoing objective is cast as minimizing predicted next-step feature error to a goal, with per-channel and per-scale weights that are learned. A fitted Q-iteration (FQI) scheme with a linear Q-parameterization over feature-error terms and control regularization learns these weights.
- A key observation is that scaling the Q-parameters and bias does not change the greedy policy. Leveraging this, the paper presents an efficient optimization of the Bellman error within FQI.
- Empirically, on a synthetic quadcopter car-following benchmark, the method purportedly learns effective controllers with very limited RL data (≈20 trajectories), outperforming baselines that use raw pixels or hand-designed keypoints and showing much higher sample efficiency than standard model-free deep RL.

2. Decision
Reject. Key reasons: (i) the experimental evaluation is limited (small-scale synthetic setting) and lacks crucial ablations to support core modeling choices; (ii) important methodological claims (modified VGG vs standard VGG; the proposed FQI optimization vs a conventional target-fixed variant) are not empirically validated.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The paper clearly articulates the limits of traditional visual servoing (manual features, analytic Jacobians) and why pre-trained CNN features plus learned dynamics and RL could yield robustness and rapid adaptation.
- Approach: Technically sound and novel in its combination. The locally connected bilinear dynamics model is a reasonable inductive bias for feature evolution under camera motion. Learning feature weights via a Q-function linear in per-channel errors and control penalties is elegant and yields an efficient fitted Q-iteration procedure. The insight about scale invariance of Q-parameters for the greedy policy is neat and practically useful.
- Missing ablations:
  - Dynamics parameterization. Section 4 discusses fully connected vs locally connected bilinear dynamics, but the empirical comparison across the plausible set of choices (fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected) is not sufficiently presented. This is important to justify the advocated inductive bias and its sample efficiency benefits.
  - Feature backbone modification. The paper modifies VGG (less pooling, dilations) to preserve spatial detail for servoing, yet there is no direct comparison to the unmodified pretrained VGG features to substantiate the benefit of this design choice.
  - FQI optimization variant. The proposed optimization leverages the scaling/bias indeterminacy of θ and b. A head-to-head comparison with a standard iterative fitted Q-iteration that holds the target term (ct + γ minu Q(st+1, u)) fixed would clarify whether the proposed variant yields better stability, convergence speed, or sample efficiency.
- Empirical scope: The results are confined to a single synthetic environment and feel narrow for a top venue. Robustness claims (to distractors, occlusion, viewpoint changes) would be more convincing with more granular metrics and broader tests, and/or at least one real-world validation or stronger domain randomization studies.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all items affected my decision)
- Dynamics ablation: Provide quantitative comparisons of fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected bilinear dynamics, including parameter counts, training stability, next-step feature prediction error, and downstream servo performance and speed.
- Feature ablation: Compare modified VGG vs the original VGG-16 features (same layers), and across layers (conv1_2 to conv5_3), reporting both prediction and control metrics. Include a study on the effect of multiscale vs single-scale features.
- RL ablation: Compare the proposed FQI optimization with a conventional target-fixed FQI loop on identical data, reporting Bellman error trajectories, wall-clock time, and final performance. Sensitivity to regularization ν and to the non-negativity constraint should also be shown.
- Robustness analysis: Quantify performance under controlled occlusion levels, lighting changes, and distractor prevalence. Report failure modes (e.g., target loss, drift) with statistics.
- Sample efficiency curves: Show performance vs number of trajectories for your method and baselines (including model-free deep RL), with standard deviations over multiple seeds.
- Statistical reporting: Include means, standard deviations, and number of seeds for all plots; add significance testing where appropriate.
- Computation: Break down runtime per component (feature extraction, Jacobian computation, action optimization) and discuss how choices of feature layers/scales affect control frequency.
- Clarity: Briefly discuss when the myopic one-step feature objective aligns with the long-horizon task cost, and when the learned weights compensate for myopia.

Questions for the authors
- Dynamics model comparison: Can you provide quantitative results comparing fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected bilinear dynamics on both prediction accuracy and servo performance, along with parameter counts and training time?
- VGG modification: How does the modified VGG (reduced pooling + dilations) compare to the standard pretrained VGG-16 on your tasks when everything else is held constant? Please include an ablation across layers and scales.
- FQI variant: How does your α,b-scaling-augmented FQI compare to a standard fitted Q-iteration that fixes the target term while optimizing the current Q? Please report convergence (Bellman error), stability, sample efficiency, and final control performance.
- Generalization: Beyond “novel cars,” how does the policy fare under significant viewpoint changes, heavy occlusions, or background clutter not seen in training? Can you provide controlled tests quantifying these effects?
- Overfitting and parameterization: What is the dimensionality of θ (number of weights across channels and scales) relative to the number of trajectories used for RL? What regularization or sparsity constraints beyond non-negativity are needed to avoid overfitting?
- Policy optimization details: How sensitive is performance to the choice of discount factor γ, the control regularization λ, and the exploration noise during FQI data collection?
- Real-world outlook: Have you attempted any real-robot experiments or sim-to-real transfer? If not, what changes (sensor calibration, feature normalization, dynamics adaptation) would be required, and which parts of the pipeline are most likely to transfer?
