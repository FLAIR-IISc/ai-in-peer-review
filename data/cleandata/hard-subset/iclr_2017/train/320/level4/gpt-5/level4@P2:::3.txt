1. Summary
The paper studies visual servoing for target following by operating in the space of learned visual features rather than pixels or hand-engineered keypoints. It uses multiscale convolutional features from a pre-trained VGG network and learns one-step feature dynamics via a bilinear predictive model. A fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure then learns nonnegative weights over feature channels and scales, so that a simple one-step lookahead controller selects actions that minimize the weighted distance between predicted and target features. The approach is sample-efficient, robust to viewpoint, appearance changes, and occlusions, and outperforms a range of baselines on a simulated car-following benchmark. The authors release the CitySim3D environment and code.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Strong empirical results with substantial sample efficiency and clear gains over sensible baselines, including classical IBVS methods and end-to-end deep RL policies.
- A well-motivated, simple, and effective combination of pre-trained features, lightweight predictive dynamics, and an efficient FQI scheme for feature weighting.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Classical servoing methods depend on hand-crafted features and calibrated dynamics, limiting robustness and reuse. The paper’s hypothesis—that pre-trained CNN features provide robust, spatially informative representations for servoing—fits well with recent evidence on transferability of deep features.
- Method soundness: The controller solves a tractable quadratic objective derived from a bilinear feature-dynamics model that is linear in controls. The Q-function is chosen to match the servo objective, enabling analytic action minimization within FQI. This design explains the method’s computational and sample efficiency.
- Empirical support: On 100 test trajectories, multiscale VGG features (up to conv4_3) consistently outperform pixels and deeper, more spatially invariant features (conv5_3), aligning with the need for spatial detail. Policies trained via FQI achieve strong performance with as few as 20 training trajectories, while TRPO-based policies require two orders of magnitude more samples yet remain worse. IBVS baselines with ORB or tracker-derived boxes underperform the proposed method and run much slower; the gap persists even when baselines are given strong side information (e.g., ground-truth depths).
- Clarity and positioning: The paper provides a clear problem statement, links the bilinear dynamics to prior work on image dynamics, and justifies why one-step predictive control coupled with feature weighting can yield robust long-horizon behavior.

4. Suggestions for improvement (to help strengthen the paper; not central to the accept decision)
- Benchmark complexity and diversity: Increase visual complexity (e.g., clutter, traffic, parked vehicles, illumination/weather variation) and target diversity (more 3D car models from public repositories). This would better stress-test robustness to distractors and appearance change, the paper’s central claims.
- End-to-end adaptation: Explore fine-tuning the visual backbone and/or jointly training features and dynamics on limited task data to quantify the benefit of representation learning under more challenging conditions.
- Reproducibility and presentation: Consolidate implementation details into a single section or appendix checklist (feature normalization, weighting constraints, regularization, optimization settings) and cross-reference the open-source code for completeness. This will make adoption easier and reduce reliance on dispersed “tricks.”
- Minor typos:
  - p.2: “learning is a relative[ly] recent addition”
  - p.2: “be applied [to] directly learn”

5. Questions for the authors
- Feature choice and transfer: How sensitive is performance to the chosen VGG layer(s)? Did you try ResNet or other backbones? Any guidance on selecting layers that balance spatial specificity vs. invariance?
- Weight learning: Are the feature weights target-specific (per object) or shared across targets? If shared, how do they generalize to novel cars? If target-specific, how much data is needed to adapt?
- Regularization and constraints: You constrain weights to be nonnegative and use L2 regularization. How sensitive are results to the regularization coefficient, and did you try sparsity-inducing penalties to prune irrelevant channels?
- Dynamics model capacity: What trade-offs did you observe between fully connected vs locally connected bilinear models (accuracy vs memory/time)? Any evidence that modeling cross-channel interactions helps?
- Cost shaping and termination: Performance depends on the image-space cost and early termination rules. How robust are results to alternative cost functions or discount factors? Any ablations on cost shaping?
- Exploration and stability: How critical is the α,b rescaling step in FQI for stability? What happens if it is removed? How do S (sampling iterations) and K (inner iterations) affect variance and convergence?
- Real-world deployment: What challenges do you anticipate when moving from simulation to hardware (sensor noise, latency, calibration, dynamics mismatch)? Can the bilinear feature-dynamics model be adapted online to handle sim-to-real gaps?
- Occlusions and distractors: Do the learned weights systematically downweight background channels, or is robustness primarily coming from the dynamics model? Any diagnostics on which channels/scales get large weights?

Overall, this is a well-motivated and carefully executed study that advances data-efficient visual servoing by leveraging pre-trained features and simple predictive dynamics within an RL framework.
