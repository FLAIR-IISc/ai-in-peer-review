

Review

This paper addresses visual servoing (specifically target following) by leveraging spatial feature maps from pre-trained convolutional networks. The core innovation lies in combining bilinear models of one-step visual feature dynamics across multiple scales with a reinforcement learning policy learned via regularized weighted minimization of predicted feature distances. The approach demonstrates strong empirical performance in simulation.

**Contributions** are significant and well-executed. The authors provide controlled experiments quantifying the utility of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing—a novel contribution. Performance gains over multiple strong baselines, including those using ground-truth bounding boxes, are clearly established. The principled learning of multi-scale feature weights via trust-region fitted Q-iteration effectively handles distractors, a key challenge. Notable sample efficiency, enabled by the model-based dynamics and Q-function approximator, further strengthens the method. The open-source virtual city environment represents a valuable contribution for future benchmarking.

**Suggestions for Improvement**:

1.  **Experimental Complexity**: While the virtual city environment is a step beyond synthetic toy domains, the current visual conditions lack sufficient complexity to robustly validate the claimed robustness. To better assess performance under realistic variations, the benchmark should incorporate: (a) diverse, high-fidelity 3D object models (e.g., multiple realistic car models sourced from Google SketchUp, not just basic templates), (b) richer distractors (e.g., parked vehicles, traffic, varied object appearances), and (c) greater environmental diversity (e.g., varying lighting, weather, background clutter). Current experiments lack the visual variety necessary to convincingly demonstrate robustness to distractors beyond simple synthetic scenes.

2.  **End-to-End Learning & Representation**: The current method uses fixed pre-trained features, which may limit generalization in more complex visual scenarios. A critical next step is to evaluate end-to-end training (fine-tuning the CNN alongside the servoing policy). This would directly assess the benefit of deep representation learning for visual servoing—a highly relevant question for ICLR. The authors briefly note this adaptability, but including such an ablation study would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and relevance to representation learning.

3.  **Reproducibility**: The formalism and core algorithms are clearly explained. However, the paper and appendix contain an excessive number of implementation details presented with inconsistent depth, making the exposition cumbersome. To enhance reproducibility, the authors should: (a) consolidate and group essential implementation details into a concise, well-structured subsection (e.g., "Implementation Details" in Section 4), (b) simplify or omit less critical technical nuances, and (c) provide a direct link to a complete, well-documented open-source implementation. Summarizing key details and linking to code is preferable to extensive in-paper description.

4.  **Typos**: Minor but distracting errors require correction: p.2, "learning is a relative[ly] recent addition" (remove "ly"); p.2, "be applied [to] directly learn" (remove "to").

**Conclusion**: This is a strong, well-executed paper presenting a novel and effective approach to visual servoing using pre-trained features. The core method is sound, the experiments are thorough within their current scope, and the open-source environment is a valuable asset. While the current benchmark lacks the visual complexity needed to fully validate robustness claims, the authors are well-positioned to address this. The suggestions for improvement—particularly adding more complex visual conditions and exploring end-to-end learning—are crucial for demonstrating the method's full potential and aligning with ICLR's focus on representation learning. The authors' clear and responsive pre-review reply indicates they are capable of implementing these enhancements. With these revisions, this paper would make an excellent contribution to ICLR.
