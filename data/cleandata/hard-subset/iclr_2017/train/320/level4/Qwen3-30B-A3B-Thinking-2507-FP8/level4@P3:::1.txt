

# Review for ICLR 2017

## Summary of Contributions
This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing that integrates learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The authors propose using pre-trained VGG features (modified to maintain spatial information) combined with a bilinear dynamics model to enable robust visual servoing that tolerates visual variations, changes in viewpoint, and occlusions. A key innovation is a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm that automatically learns weights for different visual features to optimize long-term performance. The authors demonstrate their method's effectiveness on a synthetic car-following benchmark using only 20 training trajectory samples, showing substantial improvements over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and a more than two-order-of-magnitude improvement in sample efficiency compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

## Decision
Reject.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses a meaningful problem in robotics with a technically sound approach that demonstrates strong conceptual novelty. However, I have significant concerns regarding the experimental validation and the paper's claims about the necessity of the proposed method's components.

First, the paper's claim that "standard deep features, in our case taken from a model trained for object classification, can be used together with a bilinear predictive model to learn an effective visual servo" lacks sufficient experimental justification. The authors modify the VGG network to remove max-pooling layers and replace them with dilated convolutions, but they do not provide any comparison showing that this modified version outperforms the standard VGG network on the visual servoing task. Without this comparison, it's unclear whether the success of their approach is due to the modified features or simply the use of pre-trained features in general.

Second, the experimental evaluation is limited to a single synthetic environment with a car-following task. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, the paper fails to demonstrate broader applicability. The comparison to classical visual servoing methods (IBVS with ORB features or C-COT tracker) is valuable, but the paper does not address whether these methods could be combined with learned features to achieve similar results. The paper's claim of "substantial improvement" over conventional approaches lacks context regarding the difficulty of the task and the practical utility of the improvements.

The paper's technical contribution is interesting, but the experimental validation is insufficient to support the paper's claims about the necessity and effectiveness of the proposed method. The sample efficiency claim is particularly concerning without a thorough comparison to other efficient RL approaches that might achieve similar results with different methods.

## Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen the paper, I recommend the following revisions:

1. Conduct a thorough ablation study comparing the modified VGG features with the standard VGG features (with max-pooling) to demonstrate the necessity of the architectural modification. This would clarify whether the success of the approach is due to the pre-trained features in general or specifically to the modified feature extraction.

2. Expand the experimental evaluation to include additional tasks beyond the car-following benchmark. This would better demonstrate the generality of the approach and provide more convincing evidence for the paper's claims.

3. Provide a more detailed comparison with other sample-efficient reinforcement learning algorithms that could potentially address the same problem, such as model-based RL methods that don't require the specific feature weighting approach proposed in the paper.

4. Clarify the relationship between the bilinear dynamics model and the feature weighting approach. The paper presents these as separate contributions, but it's not clear how they interact or whether one could be eliminated without significantly impacting performance.

## Questions for Authors
1. Can you provide the performance metrics for the standard VGG features (with max-pooling layers) compared to the modified VGG features on your car-following task? This would clarify whether the architectural modification is necessary for the success of your approach.

2. How would your method compare to a simpler approach that uses the standard VGG features with a simple weighted sum of feature errors (without the bilinear dynamics model and FQI optimization)? This would help determine whether the complexity of your approach is truly necessary.

3. Can you provide more details about the specific limitations of the synthetic car-following benchmark that prevent direct application to real-world scenarios? This would help readers understand the paper's practical relevance.

4. The paper mentions that "the features at each level are obtained from the features below through a downsampling operator d(y^(l-1)) = y^(l) that cuts the resolution in half." How does this downsampling affect the spatial information needed for visual servoing, and why is it necessary to use multiple scales?

The paper presents an interesting technical approach with potential value for the robotics community, but the current experimental validation is insufficient to fully support the claims made. With the suggested revisions, this work could become a strong contribution to the field.
