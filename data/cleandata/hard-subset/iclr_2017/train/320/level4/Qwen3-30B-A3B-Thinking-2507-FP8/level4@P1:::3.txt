

1) Summary  
This paper tackles the visual servoing problem (specifically target following) by combining pre-trained deep visual features with learned predictive dynamics and reinforcement learning. The authors propose a method that uses features from a VGG network trained on ImageNet classification, a bilinear model to predict how these features change with robot motion, and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn optimal weights for the visual features. The approach enables visual servoing that is robust to visual variations, changes in viewing angle, and occlusions, requiring only 20 training trajectory samples for reinforcement learning. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, as well as a more than two-orders-of-magnitude improvement in sample efficiency compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

2) Decision  
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a technically sound approach that effectively combines several established techniques, the experimental validation is limited to a synthetic environment with insufficient visual complexity. The claimed robustness to visual variations is not adequately demonstrated, and the paper fails to show meaningful generalization to more challenging scenarios that would be necessary for real-world applicability.

3) Supporting arguments  
The primary issue is the experimental scope. The authors evaluate their method on a synthetic car-following benchmark with a limited set of car models and minimal visual variation. The environment lacks distractor objects, diverse lighting conditions, complex occlusions, and varied car appearances. While the paper claims robustness to visual variations, the experiments do not include sufficient complexity to validate this claim. For instance, the test set uses only a small number of car models without significant appearance variation, and the environment lacks contextual distractors that would challenge the visual servoing system.

Additionally, the paper does not investigate the potential benefits of end-to-end learning, where the pre-trained convolutional network would be fine-tuned along with the servoing policy. This is particularly relevant for ICLR, as the paper's main contribution is the use of pre-trained features without exploring how representation learning might further improve performance. The claimed improvements over baselines are significant within the narrow experimental setting, but the paper does not provide evidence that these improvements would hold in more complex environments.

The sample efficiency claim is also under-supported. While the authors report using only 20 training trajectories, they do not demonstrate that this advantage persists across multiple environments or tasks. The comparison with standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms is limited to a single synthetic benchmark, and the paper does not address whether the sample efficiency advantage would generalize to more complex tasks.

4) Additional feedback  
- To strengthen the paper, expand the experimental evaluation to include more complex visual conditions: diverse car models with varied appearances, realistic environments with distractors (e.g., other vehicles, pedestrians, complex urban structures), and challenging occlusions. This would better validate the claim of robustness to visual variations.

- Investigate the potential benefits of end-to-end training, where the pre-trained convolutional network is fine-tuned along with the servoing policy. This would provide insight into whether representation learning could further improve performance and better align with the ICLR community's interest in representation learning.

- Improve the organization of implementation details. The current presentation includes an overwhelming number of practical tricks and implementation specifics scattered throughout the paper and appendix. Group and simplify these details, and provide a clear link to an open-source implementation for reproducibility.

- Address the limitations of the current approach explicitly, particularly regarding generalization to more complex, real-world scenarios. Discuss how the method might be extended to handle richer visual environments while maintaining sample efficiency.

- Clarify the relationship between the bilinear dynamics model and the pre-trained features. While the paper mentions that the bilinear model is trained on top of the pre-trained features, it would be helpful to more explicitly explain why this specific model architecture was chosen over other alternatives for visual dynamics prediction.
