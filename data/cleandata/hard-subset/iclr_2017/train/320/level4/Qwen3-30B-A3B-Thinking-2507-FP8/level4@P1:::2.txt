

Summary: This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained visual features, learned predictive dynamics, and reinforcement learning. The authors propose a method that leverages VGG features for visual representation, employs a bilinear model to predict feature dynamics, and uses a weighted objective optimized via fitted Q-iteration to learn an effective servoing policy. The key contribution is demonstrating that just 20 training trajectories are sufficient to learn a visual servo for complex car-following tasks, with substantial improvements over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and more than two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

Decision: Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of visual servoing by demonstrating a highly sample-efficient learning approach that achieves strong performance with minimal training data, and the method is well-motivated and rigorously evaluated.

Supporting arguments: The paper successfully addresses the three key review questions:
1. The paper clearly tackles the problem of learning visual servoing mechanisms that can adapt quickly to new targets with minimal data.
2. The approach is well-motivated by situating it within the literature on visual servoing, learned features, and reinforcement learning.
3. The paper provides comprehensive experimental evidence supporting its claims, showing substantial performance improvements over baselines and robustness to visual variations.

The paper's most compelling contribution is its impressive sample efficiency. Demonstrating effective learning with only 20 training trajectories for a complex visual servoing task is a significant achievement that addresses a key limitation of current methods. The thorough comparison to conventional approaches and standard deep reinforcement learning algorithms provides strong empirical support for the method's advantages.

Additional feedback: While the paper is well-written and technically sound, a few enhancements would strengthen it:
- A more detailed explanation of how the bilinear dynamics model relates to the Q-value function approximation would clarify this critical component.
- Additional analysis on why certain VGG layers (conv4_3) outperform others (conv5_3) would provide valuable insights for practical implementation.
- Including more qualitative analysis of the learned feature weights would help readers understand which visual elements the system prioritizes for servoing.

Questions for authors:
1. The paper notes that VGG conv5_3 features are less suitable for approximating Q-values. Could you provide more analysis on why this layer's features lack the necessary spatial information for effective servoing?
2. How might the approach generalize to more complex environments beyond the synthetic car-following benchmark?
3. The paper uses locally connected bilinear dynamics. How sensitive is the method to the choice of neighborhood size in this model?

The paper makes a valuable contribution to visual servoing research by demonstrating a practical approach that balances sample efficiency with strong performance. While the focus on pre-trained features rather than end-to-end learning is a limitation, the empirical results and methodological insights are compelling and would benefit the robotics community. The work represents a significant step toward enabling visual servoing systems that can quickly adapt to new targets with minimal training data.
