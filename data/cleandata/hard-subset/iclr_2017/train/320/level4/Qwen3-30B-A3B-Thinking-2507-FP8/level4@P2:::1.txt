

**Summary of Contribution**  
This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing that effectively combines learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The authors propose a method that uses pre-trained VGG features (modified with dilated convolutions to retain spatial information) alongside a bilinear predictive model to enable robust visual servoing for target following. A key innovation is a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm that automatically learns optimal weights for visual features, allowing the system to focus on relevant object features while ignoring distractors. The approach demonstrates significant improvements over conventional methods based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, requiring only 20 training trajectories for reinforcement learning—a more than two-orders-of-magnitude improvement in sample efficiency compared to standard model-free deep RL algorithms.

**Decision**  
Accept with minor revisions.  

**Key Reasons for Acceptance**  
1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in robotics (visual servoing) that has been constrained by manual feature engineering and model design. The proposed method elegantly combines deep learning with reinforcement learning to create a flexible, sample-efficient solution.  
2. The technical approach is well-motivated and rigorously developed, with clear theoretical grounding for the bilinear dynamics model and the Q-function optimization. The authors demonstrate strong empirical validation across multiple experimental settings.  
3. The results are scientifically rigorous, showing clear improvements over multiple baselines, including classical image-based visual servoing methods and end-to-end deep RL policies. The sample efficiency gains are particularly compelling.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper's main contributions are well-justified and effectively demonstrated. The bilinear dynamics model (Section 4.2) provides a computationally efficient way to predict feature changes, striking a good balance between expressiveness and complexity. The key insight that scaling Q-function parameters does not affect the optimal policy (Section 5.3) enables a more elegant optimization scheme than standard fitted Q-iteration, which is a significant technical contribution. The experimental results (Section 6) convincingly demonstrate that the method:  
- Achieves robust performance across varying visual conditions (viewpoint changes, occlusions)  
- Generalizes to novel target objects (cars not seen during training)  
- Requires substantially fewer training samples than competing approaches  
- Maintains computational efficiency (16Hz execution speed) compared to tracker-based baselines (1Hz)  

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**  
1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed ablation study comparing the three dynamics model variants (fully connected, convolutional, locally connected). While the authors mention that locally connected models are preferred for computational reasons, the performance differences between these approaches are not explicitly quantified.  
2. The modification to the VGG network (removing max-pooling layers and using dilated convolutions) should be validated against the original VGG architecture. The current experiments do not clearly demonstrate whether the modified network is necessary for the approach's success.  
3. The generalization to other visual servoing tasks beyond car following could be strengthened with additional examples or discussion. While the paper mentions this as a future direction, it would be valuable to include preliminary experiments or analysis of the approach's flexibility.

**Questions for Authors**  
1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the performance differences between the fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected dynamics models? How much of the performance gain comes from the model choice versus other components of the approach?  
2. How would the proposed method perform if the original VGG network (with max-pooling layers) were used instead of the modified version? Would the performance differences be significant enough to justify the network modification?  
3. Could you elaborate on how the approach would generalize to different types of visual servoing tasks beyond car following (e.g., object manipulation or navigation)? Are there specific challenges that would need to be addressed for these applications?  

This paper makes a strong contribution to the intersection of robotics and deep learning, with clear technical novelty and compelling empirical results. The proposed method represents a significant step toward flexible, sample-efficient visual servoing systems that can adapt to new targets with minimal retraining. The suggested improvements would further strengthen the paper's impact and clarity.
