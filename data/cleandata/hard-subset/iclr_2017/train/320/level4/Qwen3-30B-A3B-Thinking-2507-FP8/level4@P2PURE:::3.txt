

1) Summary  
This paper addresses visual servoing (specifically target following) by leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on general image classification tasks. The authors integrate bilinear models of one-step visual feature map dynamics across multiple scales with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy. This policy is derived by minimizing a regularized weighted average of distances to features predicted by the visual dynamics model.  

2) Contributions  
+ Quantitative validation in simulation demonstrating the utility of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing.  
+ Significant performance gains over multiple strong baselines, including approaches utilizing ground-truth bounding boxes.  
+ Principled learning of multi-scale visual feature weights via an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm, effectively mitigating distractor challenges.  
+ Notable sample efficiency, attributable to the Q-function approximator choice and the model-based one-step visual feature dynamics.  
+ Release of an open-source virtual city benchmark environment for visual servoing.  

3) Suggestions for improvement  
- **Enhanced benchmark complexity**: While the environment avoids being a toy synthetic setup, experiments would benefit substantially from greater visual complexity (e.g., increased clutter, distractors, appearance/motion diversity, environmental richness). Specifically, incorporating a larger variety of realistic 3D car models (e.g., from Google SketchUp) and populating scenes with more distractor vehicles (in traffic or parked) would significantly improve realism and diversity of object appearances. This is critical given the method’s emphasis on robustness to visual variations.  
- **End-to-end and representation learning**: Although current synthetic results are compelling, evaluating end-to-end training (including convnet fine-tuning) would clarify its impact on generalization under challenging visual conditions. This would also directly assess the benefit of deep representation learning for visual servoing—a relevant consideration for ICLR, as the current approach does not incorporate representation learning (though the authors note it could be straightforwardly adapted).  
- **Reproducibility**: The formalism and algorithms are clearly explained, but the paper contains an excessive volume of implementation details with inconsistent granularity throughout the text and appendix. Consolidating, simplifying, or reorganizing these details would improve clarity; a more effective solution would be to summarize key implementation choices in the main text and provide a link to the open-source implementation for full reproducibility.  
- **Typos**:  
  p.2: "learning is a relatively recent addition" (remove bracketed "ly")  
  p.2: "be applied to directly learn" → "be applied directly to learn"  

4) Conclusion  
Despite the limitations in experimental scope, this paper presents an interesting and robust contribution, further strengthened by the authors’ thorough responses to pre-review queries and subsequent revisions. This indicates strong capability to address the suggested improvements, potentially yielding an even stronger final paper.
