

1. Summary
This paper addresses visual servoing (specifically target following) by leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on general image classification. The authors integrate bilinear models of one-step dynamics for visual feature maps across multiple scales with a reinforcement learning approach to develop a servoing policy. This policy is optimized by minimizing a regularized weighted sum of distances to features predicted by the visual dynamics model.

2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to visual servoing by demonstrating that pre-trained deep features combined with a model-based reinforcement learning approach can achieve robust target following with exceptional sample efficiency.

3. Supporting arguments
The work provides compelling evidence through well-designed experiments that:
- Pre-trained deep features are highly effective for visual servoing, as quantified through controlled simulation studies.
- The proposed method significantly outperforms a wide range of baselines, including those using ground truth bounding boxes.
- The authors introduce a principled approach to learning multi-scale feature weights using an efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm, effectively addressing the challenge of distractors in the visual field.
- The method achieves remarkable sample efficiency (over two orders of magnitude better than standard model-free deep RL) due to the combination of model-based one-step dynamics and an appropriate Q-function approximator.
- The authors provide an open-source virtual city environment that facilitates benchmarking visual servoing methods.

4. Additional feedback
- More complex benchmark: While the current simulation environment is not trivial, it would benefit from increased visual complexity (e.g., more clutter, diverse distractors, varied appearance and motion, richer environments). Specifically, incorporating a larger variety of 3D car models from sources like Google SketchUp and adding more distractor cars (in traffic or parked) would better test the claimed robustness to visual variations.
- End-to-end and representation learning: It would be valuable to explore the impact of end-to-end training (including fine-tuning the convolutional network) on generalization in more challenging visual conditions. This would also provide insights into the benefits of deep representation learning for visual servoing.
- Reproducibility: The paper contains a substantial amount of implementation details with varying levels of explanation. Streamlining these details by grouping, simplifying, or reorganizing the exposition would improve clarity. Alternatively, summarizing the key implementation aspects in the main text and providing a link to the open-source implementation would enhance reproducibility.
- Typos: Page 2 contains minor typographical errors: "learning is a relatively recent addition" and "be applied to directly learn."

5. Questions for the authors
- How would the performance of the method change if the convolutional network were fine-tuned during the learning process, particularly in more complex environments with greater visual variation?
- Could the authors elaborate on the specific challenges they encountered when implementing the locally connected bilinear dynamics model, and how these might impact scalability to higher-dimensional visual representations?
- What are the limitations of the current approach in handling extreme occlusions (e.g., when the target is completely obscured for extended periods), and how might the method be extended to address these cases?
