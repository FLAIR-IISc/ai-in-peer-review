

This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing by combining learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to enable quick adaptation to new targets with minimal data. The authors demonstrate that standard deep features from pre-trained networks, when used with a bilinear predictive model and sample-efficient Q-iteration, can effectively learn visual servoing that is robust to visual variation, viewpoint changes, and occlusions. The key contribution is the development of a weighted multiscale feature representation for servoing, where the weights are learned through a modified fitted Q-iteration algorithm, allowing the system to focus on relevant visual features while ignoring distractors.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong technical contribution by demonstrating that visual servoing can be learned with just 20 training trajectories, achieving more than two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning methods. The theoretical justification for the bilinear dynamics model and the Q-function optimization approach is compelling and well-supported.

The paper effectively addresses the core challenges in visual servoing: it demonstrates how pre-trained visual features can be leveraged for robust servoing, provides a theoretically sound method for learning feature weights, and shows significant improvements over both conventional approaches (using image pixels or hand-designed keypoints) and standard deep reinforcement learning methods. The sample efficiency claim is particularly strong, with the method requiring only 20 training trajectories to achieve effective performance.

To further strengthen the paper, I suggest the following improvements:
1. The authors should include a more thorough comparison of the fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected bilinear dynamics models, as the paper mentions these variants but does not provide sufficient experimental evidence to support their choice of locally connected model.
2. The modifications to the VGG network (removing max-pooling layers and using dilated convolutions) should be experimentally validated against the original VGG architecture to confirm that these changes are necessary for performance, rather than just assumed.
3. The paper would benefit from additional ablation studies showing how each component (feature weights, bilinear dynamics, Q-iteration) contributes to the overall performance.

To clarify my assessment, I would like the authors to address the following questions:
1. Can you provide more details on why the locally connected bilinear model was chosen over the fully connected variant, particularly regarding the trade-off between computational efficiency and performance?
2. How sensitive is the performance to the specific modifications made to the VGG architecture, and would the method work with the standard VGG-16 network without these modifications?
