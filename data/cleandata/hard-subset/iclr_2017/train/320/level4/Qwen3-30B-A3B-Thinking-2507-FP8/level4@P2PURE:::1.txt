

The paper proposes a novel approach for learning visual servoing via Q-iteration. The main contributions are:  
1. A bilinear dynamics model for predicting the next frame (features) based on action and current frame.  
2. A Q-function formulation that learns channel-specific weights to capture feature importance.  
3. An elegant optimization method for the Bellman error to learn the Q-function.  

Strengths:  
+ The paper effectively explores multiple approaches for connecting action (\(u_t\)) and frame representation (\(y_t\)) to predict next-frame features (\(y_{t+1}\)). It strongly argues for a locally connected bilinear model, balancing computational efficiency and expressive power.  
+ The channel-weighting strategy—assigning distinct importance to different feature channels—proves more effective than uniform error weighting, as validated experimentally (unweighted approaches consistently underperform).  
+ The optimization of the Bellman error is well-handled. The key insight that scaling Q-function parameters does not affect the optimal policy enables a more elegant FQI approach, avoiding the need to fix terms like \(c_t + \gamma \min_u Q_{t+1}\) during optimization.  

Weaknesses:  
- Section 4 makes strong arguments for model choices but would benefit from experimental comparisons among the three dynamics models (fully connected, convolutional, locally connected).  
- The distinction between FQI and an iterative approach (where the second term in Equation 5 is fixed) requires further clarification and empirical validation.  

Experimental Results:  
- The results are limited in scope, relying on small-scale toy simulations. While the lack of established benchmarks in this domain is acknowledged, the experiments do not sufficiently support the claimed advantages.  
- The modification to the VGG network requires experimental validation. As presented, it remains unclear whether the modified VGG outperforms the original.  

Overall, the paper offers solid technical novelty and a well-motivated problem formulation. However, stronger experimental validation—including broader benchmarks, ablation studies, and VGG performance comparisons—is needed to substantiate the claims.
