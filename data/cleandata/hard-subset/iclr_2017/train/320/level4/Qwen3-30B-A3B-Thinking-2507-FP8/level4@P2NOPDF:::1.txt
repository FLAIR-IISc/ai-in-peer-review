

The paper introduces a novel Q-iteration framework for visual servoing, with three core contributions: (1) a bilinear dynamics model predicting next-frame features from current state and action, (2) a Q-function formulation that learns channel-specific weights to prioritize feature importance, and (3) an optimization method for the Bellman error leveraging parameter scaling invariance.  

**Strengths:**  
The bilinear model (locally connected) is well-motivated as a balanced trade-off between computational efficiency and expressiveness for predicting feature transitions. The channel-weighting mechanism significantly outperforms uniform error weighting in experiments, demonstrating that selectively emphasizing relevant features improves servoing accuracy. Critically, the paper resolves a key challenge in Q-learning—optimizing the Bellman error—by recognizing that scaling Q-function parameters does not alter the optimal policy. This insight enables a streamlined Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) approach, avoiding the iterative fixed-point updates typical in standard methods (where $c_t + \gamma \min_u Q_{t+1}$ is held constant).  

**Limitations:**  
The experimental validation requires strengthening. The paper compares dynamics models (fully connected, convolutional, locally connected) theoretically in Section 4 but omits direct quantitative results across these variants, weakening the justification for the bilinear choice. Additionally, the empirical evaluation is limited to small-scale synthetic environments (e.g., simulated robotic arm tasks with minimal visual complexity), lacking benchmarks against prior visual servoing work. Crucially, the modified VGG backbone used for feature extraction lacks experimental validation against the standard architecture; it remains unclear whether the modifications confer tangible benefits, as the paper does not report ablation studies comparing modified vs. original VGG performance.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The technical contributions—particularly the bilinear dynamics model, channel-weighted Q-function, and parameter-scaling-based optimization—are novel and well-formulated. The core ideas address meaningful gaps in visual servoing learning. However, the experimental section fails to sufficiently support the paper’s claims. The absence of comparative dynamics model results, lack of real-world or standard benchmark tasks, and unvalidated VGG modifications significantly reduce the paper’s impact. While the problem formulation is sound, the current evidence does not conclusively demonstrate superiority over existing methods.  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper presents a technically promising approach but requires substantially stronger empirical validation to justify its claims. The authors should: (1) include quantitative comparisons of all three dynamics models; (2) evaluate on standard visual servoing benchmarks (e.g., with real-world imagery or established datasets); and (3) provide ablation results for the modified VGG. Without these additions, the contribution’s practical significance remains uncertain. As submitted, the work offers valuable theoretical insights but lacks the experimental rigor expected for ICLR. The contribution is solid in concept but needs empirical reinforcement to be compelling.
