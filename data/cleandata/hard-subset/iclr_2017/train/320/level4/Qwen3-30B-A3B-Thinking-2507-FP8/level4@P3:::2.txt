

This paper investigates the integration of learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning for visual servoing, with a specific focus on target following tasks. The authors propose a novel approach that uses pre-trained visual features (from a VGG network) combined with a bilinear predictive model to learn visual servoing mechanisms. Crucially, they introduce a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm that automatically selects the most relevant visual features for the task, enabling the system to learn effective visual servoing with minimal training data—just 20 trajectory samples in their car-following benchmark. The method demonstrates significant improvements over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, achieving more than two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency than standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The key reasons for this decision are: (1) the paper makes a scientifically rigorous contribution to the field of visual servoing with strong empirical validation, and (2) the approach provides a meaningful step toward data-efficient robot learning that addresses a practical challenge in robotics.

The paper's contribution is well-motivated and well-placed within the literature. Visual servoing has traditionally relied on manually designed features and analytical dynamics models, which limits generalization and requires extensive application-specific engineering. The authors clearly articulate this limitation and position their work as an extension of prior visual servoing methods while incorporating recent advances in deep learning and reinforcement learning. Their theoretical foundation for using bilinear dynamics models for feature prediction is sound, and the fitted Q-iteration approach they develop is a meaningful contribution to reinforcement learning methodology.

The empirical results strongly support the paper's claims. The authors conduct comprehensive experiments comparing their approach against multiple baselines, including end-to-end CNN policies, classical image-based visual servoing with hand-crafted features, and tracker-based methods. Their method consistently outperforms alternatives while requiring dramatically fewer training samples. The ablation studies (particularly those in Section 6.3) convincingly demonstrate the necessity of their feature weighting approach and the advantages of using pre-trained features. The performance on novel cars (Figure 5) shows promising generalization capability, and the sample efficiency results (Figure 6) are particularly compelling. The authors provide clear evidence that their method can learn effective visual servoing with minimal data, which is a significant practical advantage.

For additional feedback to improve the paper: While the paper makes a strong case for using pre-trained features, it would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of this approach. Specifically, the paper could explore scenarios where pre-trained features might fail and discuss potential strategies for handling such cases. The authors should also clarify how their method would scale to more complex visual servoing tasks beyond the car-following benchmark. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational trade-offs between their approach and the tracker-based baselines, including runtime comparisons on actual hardware.

The paper's theoretical contribution to reinforcement learning (the trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm) is particularly noteworthy. While the draft review correctly identifies that the paper doesn't pursue end-to-end representation learning, this isn't a significant limitation given the paper's specific focus on data efficiency for visual servoing. The authors' choice to fix the visual representation while learning the policy weights is well-justified by their empirical results, which show that pre-trained features provide a strong starting point that enables rapid adaptation.

To clarify my understanding of the paper, I'd appreciate the authors addressing these questions:
1. The paper claims the bilinear model is "locally connected" with a spatial neighborhood, but the mathematical formulation (Equation 2) appears to use a convolution-like operation. Could the authors clarify how the spatial locality is enforced in the model architecture and whether this introduces additional computational complexity compared to a fully connected model?
2. In Figure 5, the performance of the VGG conv5_3 features is notably worse than other feature representations. Could the authors provide more insight into why this deeper feature representation appears less suitable for visual servoing, particularly regarding the spatial information content of these features?
3. The paper states that the approach achieves "substantial improvement over a conventional approach based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints." Could the authors provide a more quantitative comparison of the improvement in terms of both sample efficiency and final performance metrics?

Overall, this is a strong contribution that makes a clear and significant step toward more data-efficient visual servoing. The paper is well-written, the methodology is sound, and the results are compelling. While the representation learning aspect is not pursued end-to-end, the paper's focus on data efficiency for visual servoing aligns well with current challenges in robotics and is a valuable contribution to the field. The method's practical advantages for quick adaptation to new targets make it particularly relevant for real-world robotic applications.
