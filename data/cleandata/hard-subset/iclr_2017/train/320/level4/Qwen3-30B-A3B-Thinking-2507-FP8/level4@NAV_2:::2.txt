

This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing that combines learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to enable quick adaptation to new targets with minimal training data. The authors demonstrate that standard deep features from a pre-trained VGG network, combined with a bilinear predictive model, can effectively learn visual servoing mechanisms that are robust to visual variations, changes in viewing angle, appearance, and occlusions. A key contribution is the sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm that automatically selects the most relevant visual features for the task, enabling effective visual servoing with just 20 training trajectory samples.

I recommend acceptance with the following key reasons: (1) The paper makes a significant contribution to the visual servoing field by demonstrating a method that achieves substantially better sample efficiency than standard approaches, and (2) The technical approach is well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated, and represents a meaningful advancement in the application of deep learning to visual servoing.

The paper is well-written, mathematically sound, and conceptually comprehensive. The experiments provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach, particularly in handling visual variations and achieving robust performance. The demonstration of over two orders of magnitude improvement in sample efficiency compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms is particularly impressive. The use of pre-trained visual features for visual servoing is shown to be effective for generalization across different targets, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

For further improvement, the authors might consider expanding their discussion on the limitations of using pre-trained features versus learning them end-to-end, particularly how their approach might be extended to scenarios where the target object has very different visual characteristics from the training data. Additionally, providing more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of their method compared to alternative approaches would strengthen the paper.

To clarify my understanding of the work, I would appreciate answers to the following questions: How would the authors expect their approach to perform in real-world scenarios where environmental conditions differ significantly from the simulated city environment used in their experiments? Could the proposed method be adapted to handle more complex manipulation tasks beyond target following, and what challenges might arise in such extensions?
