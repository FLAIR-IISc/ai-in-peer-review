

1) Summary  
This paper addresses visual servoing (specifically target following) by leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional neural networks (CNNs) pre-trained on general image classification. The authors propose a novel approach that combines bilinear dynamics models of one-step visual feature evolution across multiple scales with a reinforcement learning algorithm. The servoing policy is learned by minimizing a regularized weighted average of distances to features predicted by the dynamics model.  

2) Contributions  
+ Quantitative validation in simulation demonstrating the utility of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing.  
+ Strong performance relative to multiple baselines, including those using ground-truth bounding boxes.  
+ Principled learning of multi-scale visual feature weights via an efficient trust-region policy iteration algorithm, effectively mitigating distractor challenges.  
+ High sample efficiency, enabled by the Q-function approximator and model-based one-step visual dynamics.  
+ Open-source virtual city environment for benchmarking visual servoing.  

3) Suggestions for Improvement  
- **Enhanced benchmark complexity**: While the environment avoids being a toy synthetic setup, experiments would benefit significantly from greater visual complexity (e.g., increased clutter, diverse distractors, appearance/motion variability, and environmental richness). Specifically, incorporating a larger set of realistic 3D car models (e.g., from Google SketchUp) and populating scenes with parked or moving distractor vehicles would better test robustness to visual variations—a core strength of the proposed method.  
- **End-to-end training and representation learning**: Current results are strong in synthetic settings, but measuring the impact of end-to-end fine-tuning of the CNN would clarify whether deeper representation learning improves generalization under challenging visual conditions. This would also directly address a key ICLR-relevant theme (representation learning), which the current method does not leverage despite being straightforward to integrate (as briefly noted by the authors).  
- **Reproducibility**: The formalism and algorithms are clearly described, but implementation details are scattered across the paper and appendix with inconsistent depth. Streamlining these into a concise summary (with a link to the open-source implementation) would greatly improve accessibility without sacrificing completeness.  
- **Typos**:  
  - p.2: "learning is a relatively recent addition" (remove "[ly]")  
  - p.2: "be applied directly to learn" (remove "[to]")  

4) Conclusion  
Despite the limitations in experimental complexity noted above, this paper presents a solid and interesting contribution. The authors’ thorough responses to pre-review comments and the subsequent revision demonstrate strong capability to address the suggested improvements. I am confident they can substantially refine the work, yielding an even stronger paper for publication.
