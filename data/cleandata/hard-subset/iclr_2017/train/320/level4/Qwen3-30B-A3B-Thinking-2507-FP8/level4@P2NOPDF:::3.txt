

1) Summary  
This paper addresses visual servoing (specifically target following) by leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on general image classification. The authors integrate bilinear models of one-step visual feature dynamics across multiple scales with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy. The policy minimizes a regularized weighted average of distances between observed and predicted feature maps under this dynamics model.  

2) Contributions  
The paper makes several key contributions:  
- Quantitative validation in simulation demonstrating the efficacy of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing.  
- Strong performance gains over diverse baselines, including those using ground-truth bounding boxes.  
- A principled approach for learning multi-scale visual feature weights via trust-region fitted Q-iteration, effectively mitigating distractor interference.  
- High sample efficiency, enabled by the model-based one-step dynamics and Q-function approximation.  
- An open-source virtual city environment for benchmarking visual servoing.  

3) Suggestions for Improvement  
- **Enhanced Benchmark Complexity**: While the environment avoids being a synthetic toy, experiments would benefit significantly from greater visual complexity (e.g., clutter, diverse distractors, varied object appearances/motions, and richer environments). Specifically, incorporating more realistic 3D car models (e.g., from Google SketchUp) and populating scenes with additional distractor vehicles (e.g., in traffic or parked) would substantially improve realism and test robustness—central to the paper’s goals.  
- **End-to-End Training & Representation Learning**: Current results are strong in synthetic settings, but measuring the impact of end-to-end fine-tuning of the CNN would clarify whether representation learning is necessary for generalization in challenging visual conditions. This is highly relevant to ICLR, as the current approach does not leverage deep representation learning (though the authors note it could be adapted).  
- **Reproducibility**: The formalism and algorithms are clearly explained, but implementation details are scattered and overly detailed across the paper and appendix. These should be consolidated (e.g., into a dedicated appendix) or summarized, with a clear link to the open-source code for full reproducibility.  
- **Typos**:  
  - Page 2: "learning is a relatively recent addition" (remove brackets).  
  - Page 2: "be applied to directly learn" (correct to "be applied to directly learn a policy" or similar).  

4) Conclusion  
Despite the need for more complex benchmarks and end-to-end validation, this is a well-executed and interesting paper. The authors’ thorough response to pre-review comments and strong revision demonstrate their capability to address the suggested improvements, which would further elevate the work. The open-source environment and robust empirical results make this a valuable contribution to visual servoing research.
