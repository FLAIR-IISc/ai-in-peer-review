

The paper introduces a novel Q-iteration framework for visual servoing, with three key contributions: (1) a bilinear dynamics model predicting next-frame features from current frame and action; (2) a Q-function formulation that learns channel-specific weights to prioritize salient features; and (3) an elegant Bellman error optimization leveraging parameter scaling to avoid policy bias.  

The bilinear dynamics model is well-motivated, offering a compelling balance between computational efficiency and expressive power when predicting feature transitions. The authors convincingly argue for locally connected over fully connected or convolutional alternatives in Section 4, though the paper would benefit from direct empirical comparisons of these three architectures to solidify this claim.  

The channel-weighting mechanism is a strong technical contribution. By assigning adaptive weights to feature channels (rather than treating all channels equally), the approach consistently outperforms unweighted baselines in experiments, demonstrating its effectiveness in prioritizing relevant visual cues. This is a meaningful improvement over naive error aggregation.  

The Bellman error optimization is particularly noteworthy. The authors recognize that scaling Q-function parameters does not alter the optimal policy, enabling a more robust and elegant fixed-point iteration (FQI) scheme compared to standard approaches that fix the target term. However, the paper does not contrast this FQI variant with iterative methods that hold the second term in Equation 5 constant—this comparison would clarify the method’s advantages.  

Experimental validation remains the paper’s weakest aspect. The results are limited to small-scale toy simulations without domain-specific benchmarks, which undermines the claimed practical impact. While the authors acknowledge the nascent state of visual servoing benchmarks, the absence of rigorous comparisons (e.g., against established servoing baselines) leaves the method’s real-world efficacy uncertain. Additionally, the modified VGG architecture used for feature extraction requires explicit experimental validation; it is currently unclear whether the modifications yield measurable performance gains over the standard VGG.  

Overall, the paper presents technically sound contributions with clear conceptual novelty in modeling dynamics and optimizing the Q-function. The core ideas are well-justified, and the channel-weighting strategy is both intuitive and empirically validated. However, the experimental section falls short of supporting the paper’s broader claims. To strengthen the submission, the authors should: (1) include ablation studies comparing the dynamics model variants; (2) provide a clear FQI vs. iterative optimization comparison; and (3) validate the modified VGG’s impact through controlled experiments. With these additions, the work would make a stronger contribution to the field.
