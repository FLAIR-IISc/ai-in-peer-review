

This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing by leveraging a learned visual representation. The authors propose learning an action-conditional bilinear model of visual features (extracted from a pre-trained VGG network) to derive a control policy via linearized dynamics. They introduce a multi-scale, multi-channel, locally-connected variant of this bilinear model to enhance representational capacity. To address the limitation of one-step-ahead dynamics prediction, the paper develops a weighted objective that integrates long-term value estimates of the current policy, evaluated using a fitted-value iteration method. The work is well-written, mathematically rigorous, and conceptually thorough. Experiments convincingly demonstrate the advantage of the value-weighted objective, establishing it as a key contribution. Additionally, the paper introduces the first trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm for this context, and empirically shows that pre-trained visual features improve generalization performance. Overall, this is a strong contribution with clear potential to advance robotics research, and I recommend acceptance.  

However, the paper’s contribution to the *representation learning* problem is relatively modest, which slightly diminishes its alignment with ICLR’s core focus. While the empirical success of pre-trained VGG features is valuable, the work does not explore learning these representations end-to-end—a critical direction for representation-focused venues like ICLR. The authors acknowledge the utility of pre-trained features but allocate disproportionate effort to the control mechanics (e.g., bilinear dynamics, value weighting, and policy derivation) relative to the representation component. Specifically, the visual representation remains fixed (not jointly optimized with the policy), and value approximation relies on problem-specific feature engineering rather than a unified, learnable representation. This approach, while effective for control, sidesteps the central challenge of *how to learn optimal visual representations* for servoing tasks. Consequently, the paper’s representation insights are largely observational (pre-trained features work well) rather than generative (how to design or learn such features).  

For context, ICLR prioritizes work that advances *representation learning* as a fundamental problem—not merely applying existing representations to new tasks. While the control innovations are sound and the paper is technically impressive, the limited engagement with representation learning as a core research question reduces its impact for this venue. The authors make a compelling case for *using* pre-trained features but do not address *how to learn them better* for visual servoing. This gap is notable given the field’s ongoing efforts to unify representation learning and control.  

In summary, this is a well-executed study with significant contributions to visual servoing control, but its representation component feels supplementary rather than central. The work would be stronger if it had explored end-to-end feature learning or derived deeper insights into representation design. As-is, it is a solid robotics paper but not a transformative contribution to representation learning—making it a borderline fit for ICLR. Despite this, its methodological rigor, clear experiments, and practical relevance warrant acceptance, though it may appeal more broadly to robotics conferences.
