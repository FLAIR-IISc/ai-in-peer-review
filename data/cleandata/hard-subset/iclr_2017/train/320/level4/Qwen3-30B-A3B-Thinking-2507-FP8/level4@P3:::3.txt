

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of visual servoing—robot control based on visual feedback—with a novel approach that combines learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The authors propose a method that uses pre-trained deep features (from VGG network) for visual servoing, rather than relying on manually designed features or image pixels. Their key innovation is a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm that learns optimal weights for visual features, enabling the robot to robustly follow targets despite visual variations, changes in viewpoint, occlusions, and distractors. The authors demonstrate that their approach can learn effective visual servoing with just 20 training trajectory samples, showing substantial improvements over conventional pixel-based or hand-designed keypoint approaches, and achieving more than two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency than standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

## 2. Decision
Accept.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of visual servoing with clear scientific rigor. The authors answer the three key questions effectively:

1. **Problem**: The paper clearly identifies the problem of visual servoing's reliance on manually designed features and models that limit generalization capability and require extensive application-specific engineering.

2. **Motivation**: The approach is well-motivated by the observation that features learned for image classification (e.g., from ImageNet) often generalize well to other visual tasks. The authors thoughtfully position their work within the literature, acknowledging the limitations of prior methods that either rely on hand-designed features or require large amounts of training data.

3. **Claims Support**: The paper provides strong empirical evidence supporting its claims. The authors demonstrate clear performance improvements over multiple baselines (including pixel-based approaches, hand-designed features, and standard reinforcement learning methods) on a complex synthetic car-following benchmark. The sample efficiency claim is particularly well-supported with experiments showing the method requires just 20 training trajectory samples compared to thousands needed by standard reinforcement learning approaches. The ablation studies and comparisons to alternative optimization methods (TRPO, CEM) further strengthen the claims.

## 4. Additional Feedback
The paper is well-written and makes a strong contribution. To further improve it, I recommend:

- **More Complex Benchmark**: While the synthetic environment is well-constructed, incorporating more complex visual conditions (e.g., greater scene clutter, diverse object appearances, varied lighting conditions) would better demonstrate the robustness claims. Adding more diverse 3D car models and distractor objects would enhance the realism and challenge of the benchmark.

- **End-to-End Learning**: The authors briefly mention that their method could be adapted for end-to-end training by fine-tuning the convolutional network. Including experiments that compare the performance with and without fine-tuning would provide valuable insights into the role of representation learning in visual servoing.

- **Implementation Clarity**: The paper contains many implementation details that could be better organized. Grouping these details into a clear methodology section and providing a link to the open-source implementation (which is already available) would improve readability without adding significant length.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper demonstrates strong performance on a synthetic environment. How would the approach perform on more realistic, real-world visual servoing tasks where lighting conditions, occlusions, and scene complexity are significantly greater than in the CitySim3D environment?

2. The paper uses a bilinear model for visual dynamics. How does the computational complexity of this model scale with the number of feature channels and spatial resolution, and what are the implications for real-time deployment on resource-constrained robots?

3. The authors claim their method achieves "robustness to visual variation, changes in viewing angle and appearance, and occlusions." Could the authors provide specific examples from their results where the method successfully handles these challenges compared to baselines?

4. The paper uses a single pre-trained VGG network for features. Would using multiple pre-trained networks (e.g., ResNet, Inception) for feature extraction potentially improve performance, and how would this impact the sample efficiency of the learning process?
