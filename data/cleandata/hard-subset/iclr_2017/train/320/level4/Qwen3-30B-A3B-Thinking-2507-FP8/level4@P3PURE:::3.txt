

1) Summary  
This paper addresses visual servoing (specifically target following) by leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on general image classification tasks. The authors propose a method combining bilinear models of one-step visual feature dynamics across multiple scales with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy. This policy is trained by minimizing a regularized weighted average of distances to features predicted by the visual dynamics model.  

2) Contributions  
+ Demonstrated utility of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing via controlled simulation experiments.  
+ Significant performance improvements over multiple strong baselines, including approaches using ground-truth bounding boxes.  
+ A principled method for learning multi-scale visual feature weights using an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm, effectively mitigating distractor challenges.  
+ Improved sample efficiency, enabled by the Q-function approximator and the model-based one-step visual feature dynamics.  
+ Open-sourced virtual city environment for benchmarking visual servoing.  

3) Suggestions for Improvement  
- **Enhanced Benchmark Complexity**: While the environment avoids being purely synthetic, experiments would benefit from greater visual complexity (e.g., increased clutter, diverse distractors, varied object appearances/motions, and richer environmental diversity). Specifically, incorporating a larger set of realistic 3D car models (e.g., from Google SketchUp) and populating scenes with more distractor vehicles (e.g., in traffic or parked) would significantly strengthen the evaluation of robustness to visual variations—a core goal of the approach.  
- **End-to-End Training and Representation Learning**: Although current results are compelling in synthetic settings, the impact of end-to-end training (including fine-tuning the convolutional network) should be assessed. This is crucial for robust generalization under challenging visual conditions and directly aligns with ICLR’s focus on representation learning. The method could be straightforwardly adapted for this purpose, as briefly noted by the authors.  
- **Reproducibility**: The formalism and algorithms are clearly described, but the paper contains an excessive number of implementation details presented with inconsistent granularity. These should be grouped, simplified, or reorganized. A more effective approach would be to summarize key implementation choices in the main text and provide a link to a complete open-source implementation.  
- **Typos**:  
  - p.2: "learning is a relatively recent addition" (remove "[ly]")  
  - p.2: "be applied to directly learn" (remove "[to]")  

4) Conclusion  
Despite these limitations in experimental scope, the paper presents a solid and interesting contribution. The authors’ thorough response to pre-review comments and subsequent revisions further demonstrate their capability to address the suggested improvements. With these refinements, the work has the potential to become significantly stronger.
