

This paper examines the advantages of visual servoing through a learned visual representation. The authors introduce an action-conditional bilinear model of visual features (extracted from a pre-trained VGG network) to derive a policy via linearization of the dynamics. They present a multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of this bilinear model. To address the limitation of the bilinear model predicting only one-step dynamics, the paper proposes a weighted objective incorporating long-term values under the current policy, evaluated using a fitted-value approach. The manuscript is well-written, mathematically rigorous, and conceptually thorough. Experimental results substantiate the benefits of the value-weighted objective, representing a key contribution. This work also appears to be the first to formalize a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm. Empirically, the use of pre-trained visual features enhances generalization. Overall, I recommend accepting this paper for its significant value to the robotics research community. However, within the scope of this conference, I consider the contribution to the "representation" problem to be limited. While the paper demonstrates the utility of pre-trained VGG features, it does not explore end-to-end representation learning. This is not a requirement, but proportionally, the paper devotes more attention to the control problem than to representation learning. Additionally, the policy representation remains fixed, and value approximation relies on linear forms using problem-specific features. While this does not diminish the paper's overall merit, it makes the work less aligned with ICLR's typical focus.
