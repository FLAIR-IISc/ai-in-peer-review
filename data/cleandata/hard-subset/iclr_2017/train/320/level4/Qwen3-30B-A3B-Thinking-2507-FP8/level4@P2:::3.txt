

1. Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in visual servoing: the reliance on manually designed features and analytical dynamics models that limit generalization and require extensive application-specific engineering. The authors propose a novel approach that combines pre-trained deep features (from ImageNet classification), bilinear predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to learn visual servoing mechanisms. Their key insight is to servo the camera in the space of learned visual features rather than image pixels or manually-designed keypoints. They demonstrate that this approach can learn effective visual servoing with minimal data (just 20 training trajectory samples) and shows substantial improvements over conventional approaches based on pixels or hand-designed features, as well as orders-of-magnitude better sample efficiency than standard model-free deep reinforcement learning.

2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a clear contribution to a well-motivated problem in robotics with strong empirical results, and the approach is well-placed in the literature.

3. Supporting Arguments
The paper effectively addresses the problem of visual servoing by leveraging pre-trained deep features and learned dynamics models to overcome the limitations of traditional approaches. The authors provide a comprehensive experimental evaluation comparing their method against multiple baselines, including classical image-based visual servoing with hand-designed features, end-to-end deep reinforcement learning, and state-of-the-art visual trackers. Their results demonstrate significant improvements in both performance and sample efficiency, with the proposed method achieving comparable performance to classical approaches while requiring orders of magnitude fewer training samples. The use of a bilinear model for visual feature dynamics and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn feature weights is theoretically sound and well-justified. The implementation details are clear, and the code and environment are publicly available, enhancing reproducibility.

4. Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, consider the following improvements:

- Expand the benchmark environment to include more complex visual conditions (e.g., additional distractor objects, varied lighting conditions, and more realistic 3D car models) to better test the claimed robustness to visual variations.
- Explore end-to-end training of the convolutional network to fine-tune features specifically for the visual servoing task, which could further improve performance in more challenging visual conditions.
- Streamline the presentation of implementation details, particularly in the appendix, to make the paper more accessible to readers. Consider consolidating the technical details into a more concise format and providing a clear link to the public code repository.

5. Clarifying Questions
- How does the performance of your method scale with the complexity of the visual environment (e.g., number of distractor objects, variation in lighting conditions, or occlusion patterns)?
- What are the computational requirements of your approach compared to the classical IBVS methods, and how might these be reduced for real-time deployment?
- Could the bilinear dynamics model be extended to handle more complex visual phenomena such as object deformation or significant changes in viewpoint that might occur during actual robot operation?
