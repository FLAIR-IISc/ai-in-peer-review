

The paper proposes a novel framework for visual servoing that combines learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The key contribution is a method that leverages pre-trained deep features (from VGG) to represent visual observations, employs a bilinear model to predict feature dynamics, and uses a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn optimal feature weights for servoing. The authors demonstrate that their approach can learn effective visual servoing policies using only 20 training trajectories, significantly outperforming conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and achieving over two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency than standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

I recommend rejection. The technical contribution is interesting, but the paper lacks sufficient experimental validation to support its claims.

The primary reason for rejection is the insufficient experimental evidence. The paper claims substantial improvements over conventional approaches, but the experiments are conducted solely on a synthetic car-following benchmark with limited diversity. The evaluation lacks comparison to relevant baselines, including more recent deep reinforcement learning methods for visual servoing, and doesn't demonstrate generalization to real-world scenarios. The claim about using "just 20 training trajectory samples" is particularly problematic, as it's unclear whether this represents a single trajectory or multiple trajectories, and there's no analysis of performance variability across different training samples.

Additionally, the paper's experimental validation of the modified VGG architecture is inadequate. The authors modify the VGG network by removing max-pooling layers and using dilated convolutions, but they don't provide ablation studies comparing the modified VGG to the original architecture or to alternative feature extraction methods. This makes it impossible to determine whether the improvements are due to the feature representation or the specific modifications made to the network.

The paper's claim that "standard deep features, in our case taken from a model trained for object classification, can be used together with a bilinear predictive model to learn an effective visual servo" is not sufficiently supported. The authors show results with a specific synthetic environment but don't demonstrate the approach's robustness across diverse environments or tasks. Without this validation, it's difficult to assess whether the approach generalizes beyond the specific benchmark used.

Additional feedback to improve the paper:
1. Conduct a thorough ablation study comparing the original VGG features with the modified ones, and provide evidence that the modifications are necessary for visual servoing.
2. Include more comprehensive experiments across diverse environments and tasks to validate generalizability, including real-world experiments or a wider range of synthetic environments with varying lighting conditions, occlusions, and object types.
3. Clarify the "20 training trajectory samples" claim by specifying whether these are total trajectories or per target, and provide performance statistics across multiple random seeds to demonstrate consistency.
4. Compare with more recent deep reinforcement learning methods for visual servoing beyond the "standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms" mentioned in the abstract.

Questions for the authors:
1. What is the specific nature of the 20 training trajectories? Are they all from the same car or from different cars? How does performance vary with the number of training trajectories?
2. How does the modified VGG network (with removed max-pooling layers and dilated convolutions) compare to the original VGG network in terms of feature quality for visual servoing? Could the same improvements be achieved with a different feature extraction method?
3. The paper claims the approach is robust to visual variation, changes in viewing angle, and occlusions. How does the method handle these challenges in the synthetic environment, and how would it generalize to real-world scenarios with more complex variations?
