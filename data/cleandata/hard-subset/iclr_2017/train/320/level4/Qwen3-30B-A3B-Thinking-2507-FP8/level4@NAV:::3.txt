

1) Summary  
This paper addresses visual servoing (specifically target following) by leveraging spatial feature maps extracted from convolutional networks pre-trained on general image classification. The authors integrate bilinear models of one-step visual feature map dynamics across multiple scales with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy. The policy is optimized by minimizing a regularized weighted average of distances to features predicted by the visual dynamics model.  

2) Contributions  
+ Quantitative assessment in simulation demonstrating the utility of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing.  
+ Substantial performance gains over multiple reasonable baselines, including approaches utilizing ground truth bounding boxes.  
+ Principled learning of multi-scale visual feature weights via an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm, effectively mitigating distractor challenges.  
+ High sample efficiency, enabled by the Q-function approximator choice and the model-based one-step visual feature dynamics.  
+ Open-source virtual city environment for visual servoing benchmarking.  

3) Suggestions for improvement  
- Enhanced benchmark complexity:  
  While the environment avoids being a purely synthetic toy, experiments would significantly benefit from increased visual complexity (e.g., clutter, distractors, appearance/motion diversity, environmental richness). Specifically, incorporating a larger set of diverse 3D car models (e.g., from Google SketchUp) and populating scenes with more distractor vehicles (in traffic or parked) would substantially improve realism and object appearance diversity—critical for validating robustness to visual variations.  
- End-to-end and representation learning:  
  Although current synthetic results are compelling, evaluating end-to-end training (including convnet fine-tuning) would clarify its necessity for generalization in challenging visual conditions. This would also quantify the benefit of deep representation learning for visual servoing, a relevant topic for ICLR (the current method lacks representation learning, though the authors note it could be straightforwardly adapted).  
- Reproducibility:  
  While the formalism and algorithms are clearly described, the paper contains a considerable volume of implementation details with inconsistent depth. Consolidating or reorganizing these details (or summarizing key aspects in the main text with a link to the open-source implementation) would enhance clarity.  
- Typos:  
  p.2: "learning is a relatively recent addition"  
  p.2: "be applied to directly learn"  

4) Conclusion  
Despite the limitations in experimental scope, this paper presents a compelling and robust contribution, further strengthened by the authors' thorough response to pre-review queries and subsequent revisions. This suggests they are well-positioned to address the suggested improvements, potentially yielding an even stronger final version.
