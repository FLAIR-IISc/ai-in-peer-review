

This paper presents a compelling approach to visual servoing that effectively combines pre-trained visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The authors propose a novel method that leverages VGG features for visual servoing, using a bilinear model to predict feature dynamics and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn optimal feature weights. The key contribution is demonstrating how these components work together to create a visual servoing system that is robust to visual variations, changes in viewing angle, and occlusions, while requiring remarkably few training samples—just 20 trajectories for reinforcement learning.

I recommend acceptance for this paper. The primary reasons for this decision are the paper's strong empirical results and the clear demonstration of sample efficiency improvements over existing methods. The authors show a more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample efficiency compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms, which represents a significant practical contribution to the field.

The paper is well-structured, mathematically rigorous, and conceptually thorough. The proposed bilinear dynamics model with its locally-connected variant provides an elegant solution to the challenge of visual servoing in high-dimensional feature spaces. The fitted Q-iteration approach for learning feature weights is particularly noteworthy, as it effectively addresses the long-term optimization problem without requiring complex multi-step planning. The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate clear advantages over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints.

The paper's contribution to the representation learning aspect is valuable, though I have some concerns about its scope. The use of pre-trained VGG features is well-justified and empirically validated, but the paper doesn't explore end-to-end learning of the visual representation. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, the paper focuses primarily on the control problem rather than the representation learning problem. This is not a flaw in itself, but it does mean the paper's contribution is more about the integration of existing representations with reinforcement learning rather than advancing representation learning itself.

To improve the paper, I suggest the authors add a more detailed discussion about the limitations of using pre-trained features versus end-to-end learning. Specifically, they could explore scenarios where the target object's appearance differs significantly from the training examples, and how the current approach might handle such cases. Additionally, a deeper analysis of why certain VGG feature layers perform better than others for visual servoing would strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution.

I have two specific questions for the authors:
1. How would the method perform if the target object had significantly different appearance (e.g., color, texture, or shape) from the training examples? Could the approach be extended to handle such cases without extensive retraining?
2. The paper demonstrates impressive sample efficiency, but how does the method scale to more complex environments with higher-dimensional state spaces? Would the computational requirements remain manageable as the task complexity increases?

The paper makes a strong contribution to the visual servoing literature by demonstrating a practical, sample-efficient approach that can be quickly adapted to new targets. The method's robustness to visual variations and its ability to learn from minimal data make it particularly valuable for real-world robotics applications. The clear presentation of the methodology, the thorough experimental validation, and the availability of code and videos all contribute to the paper's strength.

The authors have successfully addressed the key challenges in visual servoing by combining multiple learning techniques in a principled manner. Their approach represents a significant step toward more flexible and adaptable visual servoing systems that can be quickly deployed for new tasks with minimal retraining. This work will undoubtedly benefit many researchers in robotics and reinforcement learning.
