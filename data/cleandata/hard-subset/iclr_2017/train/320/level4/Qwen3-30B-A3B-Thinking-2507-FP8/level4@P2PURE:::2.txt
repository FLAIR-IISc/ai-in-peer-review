

This paper investigates the benefits of visual servoing through a learned visual representation. The authors propose first learning an action-conditional bilinear model of visual features (extracted from a pre-trained VGG network) from which a policy is derived via linearization of the dynamics. They introduce a multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of this bilinear model. Given that the bilinear model predicts dynamics only one step ahead, the paper proposes a weighted objective incorporating long-term values under the current policy. The evaluation problem is addressed using a fitted-value iteration approach. The paper is well-written, mathematically rigorous, and conceptually comprehensive. Experiments demonstrate the benefits of the value-weighted objective, representing a key contribution. Additionally, the paper appears to be the first to outline a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm, and the use of pre-trained visual features is empirically shown to aid generalization. Overall, I recommend this paper as it would be valuable to many robotics researchers. However, in the context of this conference, I find the contribution specifically to the "representation" problem to be limited. While the paper demonstrates that pre-trained VGG representations are useful, it does not consider learning these representations end-to-end. This is not to suggest end-to-end learning is required, but the paper allocates disproportionate effort to the control problem relative to representation learning. Furthermore, the policy representation remains fixed, and values are approximated in linear form using problem-specific features. This does not diminish the paper's value, but it may make the work less aligned with ICLR's typical focus.
