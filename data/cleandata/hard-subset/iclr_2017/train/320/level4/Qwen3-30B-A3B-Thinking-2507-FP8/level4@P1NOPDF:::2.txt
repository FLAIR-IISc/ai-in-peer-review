

This paper investigates visual servoing using learned visual representations. The authors propose first learning an action-conditional bilinear model of visual features (extracted from a pre-trained VGG network), from which a policy is derived via linearization of the dynamics. They introduce a multi-scale, multi-channel, locally-connected variant of this bilinear model. Since the bilinear model predicts only one-step dynamics, the paper proposes a weighted objective that incorporates long-term value estimates under the current policy, addressed through a fitted-value approach. The experiments demonstrate the benefits of this value-weighted objective, which constitutes a key contribution. The paper also appears to be the first to formalize a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm. Empirically, using pre-trained visual features improves generalization performance.  

The paper is well-written, mathematically rigorous, and conceptually thorough. The experiments provide strong validation of the approach. Overall, I recommend acceptance as the work would benefit many robotics researchers.  

However, I question the paper’s contribution to the core "representation" problem. While it establishes that pre-trained VGG features are useful, it does not explore end-to-end representation learning—a significant gap given the field’s current focus. The paper spends disproportionate effort on the control problem relative to representation learning. Specifically:  
1. The visual representation remains fixed (VGG features), without adaptation or learning.  
2. Policy representation relies on a fixed linear value approximation using problem-specific features.  

This is not to diminish the paper’s value—its control innovations are sound and empirically validated—but the representation aspect feels secondary. For a venue like ICLR, which prioritizes representation learning advances, this imbalance weakens the paper’s alignment with the conference’s typical scope. The contribution to representation learning is modest compared to the control framework development.  

In summary, the work makes a solid contribution to visual servoing control, but its representation component does not advance the state of the art in a way that strongly justifies its position as a primary ICLR contribution. The control methodology is compelling, yet the representation choices leave a critical opportunity unexplored.
