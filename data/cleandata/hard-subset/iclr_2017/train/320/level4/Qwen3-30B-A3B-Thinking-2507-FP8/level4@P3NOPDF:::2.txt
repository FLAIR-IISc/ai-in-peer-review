

This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing by leveraging a learned visual representation through an action-conditional bilinear model of pre-trained VGG features. The authors derive a policy via linearization of the dynamics and introduce a multi-scale, multi-channel, locally-connected bilinear variant to enhance representational capacity. A key contribution is a value-weighted objective that incorporates long-term value estimates into the one-step dynamics prediction, addressing the limitation of standard bilinear models. The authors employ a fitted-Q iteration framework to solve the evaluation problem, which they formalize as a trust-region algorithm—appearing to be the first such formulation in the literature. Experiments demonstrate that the value-weighted objective significantly improves performance over baseline methods, and the use of pre-trained visual features empirically enhances generalization. The paper is well-structured, mathematically rigorous, and thoroughly evaluated across multiple robotic manipulation tasks.  

The technical contributions are substantial: the bilinear model extension provides a flexible visual dynamics representation, while the value-weighted objective and trust-region fitted-Q iteration offer practical advances for model-based reinforcement learning. The empirical results convincingly show that the proposed methods outperform alternatives, particularly in tasks requiring precise visual alignment. The reliance on pre-trained features is justified by strong generalization results, though this aspect warrants careful consideration in the critique below.  

However, the paper’s contribution to *representation learning*—a core focus of ICLR—appears limited relative to its emphasis on control. While the authors demonstrate that pre-trained VGG features improve downstream control performance, they do not explore learning the visual representation end-to-end with the control policy. This omission is notable: the paper spends considerable effort on the control algorithm (e.g., bilinear dynamics, value-weighting, TR-FQI) but allocates minimal attention to advancing the representation itself. For instance, the visual features remain fixed throughout, and the value approximation uses problem-specific linear features rather than learning a representation that jointly optimizes visual and control objectives. While the authors acknowledge this as a deliberate design choice ("does not need to be end-to-end"), the disproportionate focus on control over representation development weakens alignment with ICLR’s mission to push representation learning boundaries.  

The critique is not that end-to-end learning is *required*—the paper’s control advances are valuable—but that the representation aspect remains largely external to the core contribution. A more compelling ICLR submission would have either: (1) integrated representation learning into the control framework (e.g., jointly optimizing visual features and policy), or (2) provided deeper theoretical/empirical analysis of *why* pre-trained features suffice, thereby advancing representation understanding. As written, the representation is treated as a given, not a problem to be solved. This is not a flaw in the work itself but a misalignment with the conference’s thematic priorities.  

That said, the paper’s technical merits are clear: the bilinear model extension, value-weighted objective, and TR-FQI algorithm are novel and well-executed. The experiments are comprehensive, and the writing is exceptionally clear. For researchers focused on model-based robotics control, this work offers immediate practical value.  

In summary, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to visual servoing control, but its limited engagement with representation learning—despite empirical benefits—makes it less distinctive for ICLR. The control innovations are excellent, yet the representation component feels underdeveloped relative to the conference’s focus. I recommend acceptance with the caveat that the contribution to *representation* is modest. For ICLR 2017, a paper that advances representation learning (even incrementally) would better exemplify the conference’s scope. This work would be a stronger fit for venues with broader robotics or control audiences.  

Overall, the paper is technically sound and well-executed, but its representation-focused contribution falls short of ICLR’s expectations. It merits acceptance for its control advances but would have been more impactful if it had addressed representation learning more directly.
