

Review  
The paper introduces a novel approach for learning visual servoing via Q-iteration, with the following core contributions:  
1. A bilinear dynamics model for predicting next-frame features from current frame and action.  
2. A Q-function formulation that learns channel-specific weights to prioritize feature importance.  
3. An elegant optimization method for the Bellman error, leveraging the insight that scaling Q-function parameters does not affect the optimal policy.  

**Strengths**  
+ The paper thoroughly explores connections between action (\(u_t\)) and frame representation (\(y_t\)) for predicting next-frame features (\(y_{t+1}\)). The argument for a locally connected bilinear model effectively balances computational efficiency with expressive power.  
+ The channel-weighting strategy for error calculation—prioritizing feature channels relevant to task performance—proves superior to uniform weighting. Experimental validation confirms that unweighted error consistently degrades performance.  
+ The optimization scheme for the Bellman error is well-motivated. By recognizing that scaling Q-parameters preserves the optimal policy, the method enables a cleaner Fixed-Point Q-Iteration (FQI) approach compared to conventional methods that fix \((c_t + \gamma \min_u Q_{t+1})\).  

**Weaknesses**  
- Section 4 presents strong theoretical arguments for the dynamics model choices, but experimental comparisons between fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected variants are missing. Such comparisons would substantiate the claimed trade-offs.  
- The distinction between the proposed FQI method and an iterative approach that fixes the second term in Equation 5 remains unexplored. A direct comparison would clarify the method’s advantages.  
- Experimental results are limited to small-scale toy simulations, which fail to demonstrate robustness in real-world scenarios. While the absence of domain-specific benchmarks is acknowledged, stronger validation is needed.  
- The modified VGG architecture requires experimental validation. Without comparative results against the original VGG, it is unclear whether the modification confers tangible benefits.  

**Overall Assessment**  
The paper presents technically sound contributions with clear problem formulation and novel insights into visual servoing. However, the experimental evaluation lacks rigor and scale, weakening the claims of practical applicability. To strengthen the submission, the authors should: (1) include comparative experiments for dynamics models, (2) validate the modified VGG, and (3) expand results beyond toy settings. The core technical novelty is compelling, but stronger empirical support is essential.
