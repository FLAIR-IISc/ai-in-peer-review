

This paper presents a novel Q-iteration approach for visual servoing, with three core contributions: (1) a bilinear dynamics model predicting next-frame features from current state and action, (2) a channel-weighted Q-function formulation that adaptively prioritizes feature channels, and (3) an elegant Bellman error optimization method leveraging parameter scaling invariance. The technical contributions are well-motivated and address key challenges in visual servoing learning.

The bilinear dynamics model (Section 3.1) is a strong technical choice. The authors convincingly argue for locally connected bilinear layers over fully connected or convolutional alternatives, balancing expressiveness and computational efficiency. While the theoretical justification is sound, the paper would benefit from comparative experiments on these variants (e.g., ablation studies on feature prediction accuracy or convergence speed). Without such results, the claim that "locally connected strikes the optimal balance" remains largely unverified empirically.

The channel-weighted Q-function (Section 3.2) is a particularly compelling contribution. By learning per-channel weights to emphasize salient features (e.g., target object regions), the approach avoids the suboptimal equal-weighting of feature errors common in prior work. The experimental validation (Table 1) clearly demonstrates superior performance over unweighted baselines, confirming the practical value of this insight. This is a significant improvement over naive error aggregation.

The optimization scheme (Section 4.2) effectively addresses a critical challenge in Q-learning: solving the Bellman error. The key insight—that scaling Q-function parameters preserves the optimal policy—enables a cleaner Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) formulation than typical approaches that fix the second term (c_t + γ min_u Q_{t+1}). This leads to a more principled and stable learning procedure. However, the paper does not explicitly compare this method to standard iterative FQI (where the Q_{t+1} term is updated iteratively), which would strengthen the claim of "elegance" and practical advantage.

The experimental evaluation, while conceptually sound, is a notable weakness. Results are confined to small-scale simulated environments (e.g., 2D robot arm with synthetic objects), lacking real-world validation or comparison to established visual servoing benchmarks (e.g., tasks involving real robots or standard datasets like RoboCup). The claim that "lack of benchmarks in this domain needs to be recognized" is valid but insufficient; the paper should either propose new benchmarks or demonstrate scalability to more complex settings. The absence of real-world results significantly limits the practical impact of the proposed method.

Additionally, the paper modifies the VGG feature extractor for visual servoing but provides no experimental evidence that this modification is beneficial. The claim that "the modified VGG outperforms the original" (Section 5) is unsupported without direct comparison to standard VGG features. This is a critical omission, as the feature representation fundamentally underpins the approach's success.

Overall, the paper makes a solid technical contribution with a well-motivated problem formulation and novel methodological insights. The bilinear dynamics, channel weighting, and optimization scheme represent meaningful advances in learning visual servoing policies. However, the experimental section fails to adequately validate these contributions. The current results—limited to toy simulations and lacking benchmark comparisons or feature extraction validation—do not sufficiently support the paper's claims of effectiveness. The technical novelty is strong, but the empirical evidence is insufficient for the claims made.

The authors should address these gaps by: (1) including comparative experiments for the dynamics model variants, (2) adding real-world or more complex simulation results, and (3) providing a direct comparison of the modified VGG against standard VGG features. Without these, the paper’s impact is diminished despite its sound theoretical foundation. The core ideas are promising, but the evidence required to convince the community is missing. The paper would be substantially stronger with these additional experiments.
