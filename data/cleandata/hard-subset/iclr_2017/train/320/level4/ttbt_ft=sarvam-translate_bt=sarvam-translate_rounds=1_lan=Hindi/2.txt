This paper examines the benefits of visual servoing using a learned visual representation. The authors propose first learning an action-conditioned bilinear model (obtained from the Pre-Trained VGZ Net) of visual features, from which a policy can be derived using the linearization of dynamics. A multi-level, multi-channel, and locally connected version of the bilinear model is presented. Since the bilinear model only predicts dynamics one step ahead, this paper proposes a weighted objective that incorporates the long-term values of the current policy. The evaluation problem is addressed using a fitted-value approach. This paper is well-written, mathematically sound, and conceptually complete. The experiments also demonstrate the benefits of using a value-weighted objective, and this is a significant contribution to this paper. It seems that this paper is the first to outline a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm. The use of pre-trained visual features to help generalize empirically is also shown. Overall, I recommend this paper because it will benefit many researchers in robotics. However, in the context of this conference, I find the contribution on the "representation" problem particularly limited. It shows that a pre-trained VGZ representation is useful, but it does not consider end-to-end learning. This does not mean it should be end-to-end, but proportionally speaking, the paper spends more time on the control problem than on representation learning. Also, the policy representation is fixed, and the values are estimated linearly using problem-specific features. This does not make the paper less valuable, but perhaps it is less aligned with what I think ICLR should be.
