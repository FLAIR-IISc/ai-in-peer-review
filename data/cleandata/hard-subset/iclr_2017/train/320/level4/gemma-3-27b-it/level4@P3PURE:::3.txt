## ICLR 2017 Review

**1) Summary**

This paper proposes a method for visual servoing – specifically, target following – leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on general image classification tasks. The authors combine bilinear models of the one-step dynamics of these visual features at multiple scales with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy. This policy is learned by minimizing a regularized, weighted average of distances to features predicted by the learned model of visual dynamics.

**2) Contributions**

+ Controlled experiments in simulation quantify the benefits of using pre-trained deep features for visual servoing.
+ Clear performance improvements are demonstrated over several sensible baselines, including those utilizing ground truth bounding boxes.
+ A principled approach to learning multi-scale visual feature weights, coupled with an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm, addresses the problem of visual distractors.
+ Good sample efficiency is achieved through the choice of Q-function approximator and the model-based one-step visual feature dynamics.
+ An open-source virtual city environment is provided, offering a valuable benchmark for visual servoing research.

**3) Suggestions for Improvement**

- **More Complex Benchmark:** While the environment is not trivial, the experiments would be strengthened by more challenging visual conditions, including increased clutter, distractors, variations in appearance and motion, and richer environmental diversity. Specifically, the realism and diversity of object appearances could be substantially improved by utilizing a larger number of 3D car models – incorporating more realistic and varied designs obtainable from sources like Google SketchUp – and by populating the environment with more distractor vehicles (in traffic or parked). This is crucial, as a primary strength of the proposed approach is its claimed robustness to visual variations.
- **End-to-End and Representation Learning:**  Although the current synthetic experiments show significant improvements, it would be valuable to assess the impact of end-to-end training (i.e., also fine-tuning the convolutional network). This may be necessary for improved generalization in more complex visual scenarios and would allow a measurement of the benefits of deep representation learning for visual servoing – a topic highly relevant to ICLR. While the authors briefly mention the potential for adaptation, exploring this aspect would significantly enhance the paper.
- **Reproducibility:** The formalism and algorithms are clearly explained; however, the paper contains a substantial number of practical implementation details described with varying levels of specificity throughout the text and appendix. Grouping, simplifying, or reorganizing this exposition would be beneficial. A more effective approach would likely be to summarize the most important details within the main sections and provide a link to a complete, open-source implementation of the method.
- **Typos:**
  p.2: "learning is a relative[ly] recent addition"
  p.2: "be applied [to] directly learn"

**4) Conclusion**

Despite the limitations of the current experimental setup, this paper presents an interesting and solid contribution, particularly in light of the excellent responses to pre-review questions and the subsequent revision. This demonstrates the authors' capability to address the suggested improvements, potentially leading to an even stronger publication.
