This paper explores the advantages of visual servoing through the use of learned visual representations. The authors introduce a method to learn an action-conditional bilinear model of visual features extracted from a pre-trained VGG network. This model then forms the basis for deriving a control policy via dynamics linearization. Notably, the proposed bilinear model employs a multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected architecture. Recognizing the limitations of a single-step prediction horizon inherent in the bilinear model, the authors introduce a weighted objective function that incorporates long-term policy values, effectively addressing credit assignment. Policy evaluation is performed using a fitted-value iteration approach.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, mathematically rigorous, and demonstrates a thorough exploration of the problem space. The experimental results clearly highlight the benefits of the value-weighted objective, representing a significant contribution of this work. Furthermore, the paper appears to present the first trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm in this context. The empirical validation also confirms the utility of leveraging pre-trained visual features for improved generalization. 

Overall, I recommend this paper for acceptance as it is likely to be valuable to a broad audience within the robotics community. However, when considered specifically within the scope of ICLR, I believe the contribution regarding the “representation learning” aspect is somewhat limited. While the work demonstrates the effectiveness of using a pre-trained VGG representation, it does not investigate learning the representation end-to-end. This is not necessarily a deficiency – end-to-end learning isn't always required – but the paper dedicates a disproportionate amount of effort to the control problem relative to the representation learning component. 

Additionally, the policy representation itself is fixed, and value functions are approximated linearly using features specific to the task. This does not diminish the paper’s overall merit, but it may position it slightly outside the core focus of ICLR, which often prioritizes more generalizable and learned representations. Despite these points, the work is a solid and impactful contribution to visual servoing, and I believe it warrants acceptance.
