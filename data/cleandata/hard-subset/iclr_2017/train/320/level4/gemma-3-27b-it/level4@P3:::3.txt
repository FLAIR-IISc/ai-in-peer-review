This paper proposes to tackle visual servoing (specifically target following) using spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on general image classification tasks. The authors combine bilinear models of one-step dynamics of visual feature maps at multiple scales with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy. This policy is learned by minimizing a regularized weighted average of distances to features predicted by the aforementioned model of visual dynamics.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a novel and effective approach to visual servoing, demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods, particularly in sample efficiency. The combination of learned visual features, dynamics modeling, and reinforcement learning is well-motivated and the experimental results are convincing.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core strength of this work lies in its ability to learn a visual servoing policy with remarkably few training samples – just 20 trajectories. This is a significant advancement over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning, which typically requires orders of magnitude more data. The use of pre-trained features leverages existing knowledge and reduces the need for extensive task-specific data collection. The bilinear dynamics model provides a computationally efficient way to predict the effects of actions on the visual features, and the fitted Q-iteration algorithm effectively learns the optimal weighting of these features. 

The experimental setup, though synthetic, is well-designed and demonstrates the robustness of the approach to visual variations, changes in viewpoint, and occlusions. Crucially, the authors provide a thorough comparison to several baselines, including methods using hand-designed features and ground truth information, demonstrating the advantages of their approach. The availability of the CitySim3D environment and code promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the results are promising, the environment’s realism could be improved. Increasing the diversity of 3D car models and adding more complex background clutter would provide a more challenging testbed and better demonstrate the generalizability of the approach.

Exploring end-to-end training, by fine-tuning the convolutional network alongside the dynamics model and reinforcement learning components, would be a valuable extension. This would investigate the potential benefits of learning task-specific representations and could lead to further performance gains. The authors briefly mention this possibility, and pursuing it would align well with the ICLR focus on representation learning. 

The paper contains a wealth of implementation details. While thoroughness is appreciated, streamlining the presentation of these details, perhaps by summarizing key choices in the main body and providing a detailed implementation guide as supplementary material, would enhance readability. 

Finally, a minor point: a few typographical errors exist (e.g., p.2: "learning is a relative[ly] recent addition", p.2: "be applied [to] directly learn").

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The bilinear dynamics model assumes a relatively simple relationship between action and feature changes. How does the performance degrade when the dynamics become more complex and non-linear (e.g., due to more realistic physics or faster camera movements)? Have you explored more sophisticated dynamics models?
2.  In the experiments, the cost function includes a term that penalizes the agent for trajectories that reach the maximum length. Could you elaborate on the motivation behind this term, and how it affects the learning process? Does it influence the exploration strategy?
3.  The ablation study compares performance against a TRPO baseline. Could you comment on the computational cost of TRPO compared to FQI, and whether the observed performance difference justifies the increased computational expense of TRPO?
4.  Regarding end-to-end training, what are the primary challenges you anticipate in adapting the current framework to fine-tune the convolutional network alongside the other components? Are there specific stability concerns or architectural modifications that would be necessary?
