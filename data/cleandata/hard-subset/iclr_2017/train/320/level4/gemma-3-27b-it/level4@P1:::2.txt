This paper investigates the benefits of visual servoing using a learned visual representation. The authors propose to combine learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to learn visual servoing mechanisms. Specifically, they first learn an action-conditional bilinear model of visual features (obtained from a pre-trained VGG network) from which a policy can be derived using a linearization of the dynamics. A multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of the bilinear model is presented. Since the bilinear model only predicts the dynamics one step ahead, the paper proposes a weighted objective that incorporates long-term value estimates learned via fitted Q-iteration. The experiments demonstrate the benefits of using this weighted objective, representing an important contribution. The paper also appears to be the first to outline a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm. Furthermore, the use of pre-trained visual features is shown empirically to aid generalization. 

Overall, I recommend rejecting this paper. While well-written, mathematically sound, and conceptually thorough, I find its core contribution to be incremental, particularly within the context of ICLR.

My primary concern is the limited focus on the “representation” problem. The paper demonstrates that a pre-trained VGG representation is useful, but does not explore learning the representation end-to-end or significantly adapting it to the task. While end-to-end learning isn't necessarily required, the paper dedicates proportionally more attention to the control problem than to representation learning.

Secondly, the policy representation is fixed, and values are approximated in a linear form using problem-specific features. This approach, while effective, feels less aligned with the focus on learning complex representations often emphasized at ICLR.

**Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Incremental Contribution:** The combination of existing techniques—pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics models, and fitted Q-iteration—while effective, doesn't represent a substantial breakthrough. The novelty lies more in the specific application of these techniques to visual servoing and the proposed algorithm for fitted Q-iteration.
*   **Representation Learning Focus:** ICLR typically favors papers with significant advances in representation learning. This paper largely relies on a fixed, pre-trained representation without exploring its adaptation.
*   **Linear Approximation:** The use of a linear Q-function approximation, while simplifying the problem, limits the potential expressiveness of the learned policy.

**Additional Feedback (to help improve the paper, not part of the rejection assessment):**

*   **Ablation Studies:** More detailed ablation studies could help isolate the contribution of each component (e.g., the bilinear model, the fitted Q-iteration, the specific VGG features used).
*   **Comparison to End-to-End Methods:** A brief comparison to end-to-end learning approaches, even if preliminary, would help contextualize the paper’s approach and justify the design choices.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** A more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding the fixed representation and linear Q-function approximation, would strengthen the paper.
*   **Clarify Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A discussion of the sensitivity of performance to hyperparameters (e.g., learning rates, regularization coefficients) would be valuable.
*   **Code Availability:** The availability of code is excellent, but providing more detailed documentation and instructions would enhance reproducibility.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Have you explored fine-tuning the VGG features during the reinforcement learning process? If not, why?
*   What is the computational cost of the bilinear dynamics model compared to simpler dynamics models? Is the added complexity justified by the performance gains?
*   How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the regularization coefficient (ν) in the fitted Q-iteration algorithm?
*   Could you elaborate on the limitations of the linear Q-function approximation and discuss potential alternatives?
