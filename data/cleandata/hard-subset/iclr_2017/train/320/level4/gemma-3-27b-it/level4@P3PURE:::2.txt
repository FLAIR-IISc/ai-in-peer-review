This paper investigates the benefits of visual servoing using a learned visual representation. The authors propose learning an action-conditional bilinear model of visual features – extracted from a pre-trained VGG network – from which a control policy can be derived via linearization of the dynamics. They present a multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of this bilinear model. Recognizing that the bilinear model predicts only one-step-ahead dynamics, the paper introduces a weighted objective function that incorporates the long-term values associated with the current policy. Evaluation is performed using a fitted-value approach. The paper is well-written, mathematically sound, and conceptually thorough. The experiments clearly demonstrate the benefits of employing a value-weighted objective, representing a significant contribution of this work. Furthermore, this paper appears to be the first to outline a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm. Empirical results also show that leveraging pre-trained visual features aids generalization. Overall, I recommend this paper as it would be beneficial to many researchers in robotics. 

However, within the context of this conference, I find the contribution specifically regarding the “representation” problem to be somewhat limited. While the paper demonstrates the utility of a pre-trained VGG representation, it does not explore learning this representation end-to-end. This is not necessarily a requirement, but the paper dedicates proportionally more attention to the control problem than to representation learning. Additionally, the policy representation is fixed, and values are approximated linearly using problem-specific features. This does not diminish the paper’s value, but positions it perhaps less directly within the scope of ICLR’s focus.
