This paper investigates the benefits of visual servoing using a learned visual representation. The authors propose learning an action-conditional bilinear model of visual features – extracted from a pre-trained VGG network – from which a policy can be derived via linearization of the dynamics. They present a multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of this bilinear model. To address the model’s single-step-ahead prediction of dynamics, the paper introduces a weighted objective function that incorporates long-term policy values. The evaluation is performed using a fitted-value approach. 

The paper is well-written, mathematically sound, and conceptually thorough. The experiments convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the value-weighted objective, representing a significant contribution. Notably, this work appears to be the first to outline a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm. Furthermore, the empirical results show that leveraging pre-trained visual features aids generalization. Overall, I recommend this paper as a valuable resource for many researchers in robotics.

However, considering the focus of this conference, I find the contribution concerning the “representation” problem to be relatively limited. While the paper demonstrates the utility of a pre-trained VGG representation, it does not explore learning the representation end-to-end. I do not suggest that it *should* be end-to-end, but the paper dedicates proportionally more attention to the control problem than to representation learning. Additionally, the policy representation is fixed, and values are approximated linearly using problem-specific features. This does not diminish the paper’s value, but it may be less aligned with the spirit of ICLR, which often prioritizes more generalizable learning approaches.
