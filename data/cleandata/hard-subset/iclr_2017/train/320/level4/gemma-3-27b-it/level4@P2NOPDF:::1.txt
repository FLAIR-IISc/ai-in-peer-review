This paper introduces a novel approach to visual servoing, framing it as a reinforcement learning problem and utilizing Q-iteration for learning. The core contributions lie in: (1) a bilinear dynamics model to predict future frame features based on current features and action; (2) a Q-function formulation that learns channel-specific weights, effectively prioritizing informative features during servoing; and (3) an elegant optimization method for learning this Q-function based on properties of the Bellman error.

The paper demonstrates a thoughtful exploration of different dynamics models connecting action and frame representations for next-frame prediction. The authors convincingly justify their choice of a locally connected bilinear model, balancing computational cost and representational power. A strength of this work is the channel-wise weighting scheme within the Q-function. Intuitively, this allows the agent to focus on relevant object features, and the experimental results support the claim that weighting channels yields superior performance compared to uniform weighting.  Furthermore, the method for optimizing the Bellman error is a significant contribution. By recognizing the invariance of the optimal policy to scaling of the Q-function parameters, the authors enable a more stable and efficient form of Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) than traditional approaches that directly minimize the Bellman residual.

However, the paper would benefit from more comprehensive experimentation regarding the dynamics model comparison. While Section 4 provides justification for the selected model, presenting a direct empirical comparison between the fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected approaches would strengthen the argument.  Additionally, a clearer differentiation between the presented optimization method and standard FQI, specifically relating to the fixed second term in Eq. 5, would be valuable.

The experimental results, while demonstrating proof-of-concept, are somewhat limited in scope and scale, relying on toy simulations. It is important to acknowledge the current lack of established benchmarks in visual servoing, but more extensive evaluation would significantly bolster the claims. Specifically, the modification to the VGG network—a key element of the feature extraction process—requires more rigorous experimental validation. As highlighted in the pre-review discussion, it remains unclear whether the modified VGG consistently outperforms the original network; demonstrating this improvement is crucial. 

Overall, this paper presents a technically sound and novel contribution to the field of visual servoing. The proposed method offers a promising approach to learning effective control policies directly from visual feedback.  However, the paper’s impact would be greatly enhanced by more robust and extensive experimental validation, particularly concerning the dynamics model comparison and the VGG modification. Addressing these points will solidify the claims and demonstrate the full potential of this research.
