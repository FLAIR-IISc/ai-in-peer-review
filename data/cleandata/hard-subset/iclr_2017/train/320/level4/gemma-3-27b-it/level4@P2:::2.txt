This paper investigates the benefits of visual servoing using learned visual representations. The authors propose learning an action-conditional bilinear model to predict the dynamics of visual features extracted from a pre-trained VGG network, and then deriving a control policy through linearization of these dynamics. A multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of this bilinear model is presented. To address the limitations of one-step prediction, the paper introduces a weighted objective function that incorporates long-term policy values, estimated using a fitted Q-iteration approach. 

I recommend rejecting this paper. While well-written, mathematically sound, and conceptually thorough, its primary contribution lies in control, rather than representation learning, which is a core focus of ICLR. The experiments demonstrate the benefits of a value-weighted objective and the proposed trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm, representing a valuable contribution. However, the paper largely relies on utilizing a pre-trained VGG representation and does not explore learning a representation end-to-end, even if not necessarily optimally. The disproportionate focus on the control problem, coupled with a fixed policy representation and linear value function approximation using problem-specific features, makes the work less aligned with the conference’s emphasis on innovative representation learning techniques.

Specifically, the paper successfully demonstrates that pre-trained VGG features are useful for this task and generalize well. However, it does not delve into *why* these features are effective, nor does it explore adaptations or improvements to these features tailored to the visual servoing problem. Furthermore, the fixed policy representation and linear approximation of the Q-function, while simplifying the problem and enabling efficient computation, limit the potential for more complex and potentially powerful solutions. While these choices are justifiable, they move the work away from the core focus of ICLR.

To improve the paper, the authors could:

*   **Discuss the limitations of using pre-trained features.** A more nuanced discussion of why end-to-end learning was not pursued, and the potential drawbacks of relying on features trained for a different task, would strengthen the work.
*   **Provide ablation studies focused on feature choice.**  Analyzing the importance of different layers within the VGG network or exploring alternative pre-trained networks could offer further insight.
*   **Investigate more sophisticated policy representations.**  Briefly exploring the feasibility of non-linear function approximators for the Q-function could highlight the trade-offs made in the current approach.
*   **Clarify the novelty of the fitted Q-iteration.** While claiming to be the first to outline a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm, more detail about how this differs from existing techniques would be beneficial.



I have the following questions for the authors:

*   What were the primary reasons for choosing not to explore end-to-end learning of the visual representation?
*   How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the VGG network layer used for feature extraction?
*   Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with replacing the linear Q-function approximation with a more complex non-linear one (e.g., a small neural network)?
*   Could you provide a detailed comparison of your trust-region fitted Q-iteration to standard fitted Q-iteration methods? What specific modifications were made and why?
