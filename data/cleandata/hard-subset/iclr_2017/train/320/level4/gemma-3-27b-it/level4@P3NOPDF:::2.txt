This paper investigates visual servoing using learned visual representations, presenting a compelling approach to robotic control. The authors propose learning an action-conditional bilinear model of visual features extracted from a pre-trained VGG network. This model forms the basis for a control policy derived through dynamics linearization. Notably, the paper introduces a multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of this bilinear model. Recognizing the short-sightedness of a single-step dynamic prediction, the authors develop a weighted objective function that incorporates long-term policy values, improving performance. Evaluation is conducted using a fitted-value approach.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, mathematically sound, and demonstrates a thorough understanding of the relevant literature. The experimental results clearly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed value-weighted objective, constituting a significant contribution. Furthermore, this work appears to be the first to outline a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm within this context. Empirical validation also confirms the utility of leveraging pre-trained visual features for improved generalization. Overall, I recommend this paper for acceptance, as it offers valuable insights for a broad range of robotics researchers. 

However, assessing the contribution specifically within the scope of ICLR, I find the novelty regarding the “representation learning” aspect to be somewhat limited. While the paper successfully demonstrates the effectiveness of a pre-trained VGG representation, it does not explore learning the representation end-to-end. It is not necessarily a requirement for the work to be end-to-end learned, but the emphasis of the paper leans more heavily toward the control problem, with comparatively less attention given to investigating alternative or learned representations. 

Additionally, the policy representation itself is fixed, and the value function is approximated linearly using problem-specific features. This choice, while pragmatic, restricts the potential for learning a more generalizable policy. This doesn’t diminish the paper’s value, but it positions it perhaps slightly outside the core focus of ICLR, which often prioritizes innovations in representation learning and more flexible function approximation methods. 

Despite these observations, the paper presents a strong, well-executed, and impactful contribution to the field of visual servoing. The proposed method is novel, the analysis is rigorous, and the experiments are convincing. The benefits demonstrated are substantial, and the clarity of presentation makes the work accessible.
