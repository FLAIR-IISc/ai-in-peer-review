This paper investigates the benefits of visual servoing using a learned visual representation. The authors propose to combine learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to learn visual servoing mechanisms, specifically focusing on target following and rapid adaptation to new targets. They leverage features from a pre-trained VGG network and model action-conditional dynamics using a bilinear model, employing a multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant for improved performance. A weighted objective function, designed to incorporate long-term policy values, is optimized using a fitted Q-iteration approach.

I recommend rejecting this paper. While technically sound and conceptually thorough, the primary contribution doesn’t strongly align with the core focus of ICLR, particularly concerning novel representation learning. The paper convincingly demonstrates that pre-trained VGG features are *useful* for this task, but it does not address the challenges of learning or adapting the representation itself – a central theme at ICLR. 

My primary concern is the limited exploration of the representation learning aspect. The work relies on a fixed, pre-trained feature extractor and doesn’t investigate learning the features end-to-end or adapting them specifically for the visual servoing task. While end-to-end learning isn’t necessarily required, the paper dedicates significantly more attention to the control problem and the fitted Q-iteration algorithm than to the representation learning aspect, resulting in an imbalance. Furthermore, the policy representation is fixed, and the value function is approximated linearly using problem-specific features, which, while effective, doesn’t push the boundaries of representation learning as I would expect from an ICLR submission.

The paper is well-written and mathematically rigorous. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, demonstrating the benefits of the weighted objective and highlighting the novelty of the trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm. The authors demonstrate a significant improvement in sample efficiency compared to standard deep reinforcement learning methods, which is commendable. However, these strengths primarily relate to the control aspects of the problem, rather than novel contributions to representation learning.

To potentially improve this work, I suggest the authors consider the following:

*   **Explore adaptation of the pre-trained features:** Even a limited investigation into fine-tuning the VGG features or learning a small adaptation layer on top of them could strengthen the contribution to representation learning.
*   **Discuss the limitations of the fixed feature representation:** A more thorough discussion of the potential drawbacks of using a fixed feature extractor and how these might impact performance in more complex scenarios would be valuable.
*   **Clarify the novelty of the fitted Q-iteration algorithm:**  While the authors claim novelty, a more detailed comparison to existing fitted Q-iteration methods, outlining the specific innovations and their impact, would be helpful.
*   **Ablation Study:** A study to demonstrate the contribution of different parts of the pipeline - e.g. bilinear dynamics model, multi-scale features, weighted objective - would provide further insight into the strengths of the approach.

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   How sensitive is the performance of the system to the choice of pre-trained features (e.g., different layers of the VGG network, or features from a different network altogether)?
*   What is the computational cost associated with learning and evaluating the bilinear dynamics model, and how does it compare to other dynamics modeling approaches?
*   The regularization parameter ν appears in Equation 6. How was this parameter tuned and what impact does it have on the learned policy?
*   How would the approach perform if the target object undergoes significant non-rigid deformations or changes in appearance?
