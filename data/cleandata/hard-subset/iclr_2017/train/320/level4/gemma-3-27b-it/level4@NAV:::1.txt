This paper introduces a novel Q-iteration based approach to visual servoing. The key contributions include: 
1. A bilinear dynamics model for predicting subsequent frame features (y_{t+1}) given an action (u_t) and the current frame (y_t).
2. A formulation of the servoing problem using a Q-function that learns channel-specific weights for feature importance.
3. A refined method for optimizing the Bellman error to facilitate learning of the Q-function.

Regarding strengths, the paper thoroughly investigates various methods for linking actions and frame representations to predict future features. The authors convincingly justify their choice of a locally connected bilinear model, highlighting its advantageous balance between computational cost and representational power. 

However, while Section 4 provides a reasonable rationale for the design choices, additional experimental comparisons would be beneficial. Specifically, a more direct comparison of the fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected dynamics models would strengthen the analysis.

Further strengths lie in the concept of weighting feature channels to reflect object importance – an approach that demonstrably outperforms unweighted methods as shown in the results. Moreover, the paper addresses the challenging problem of Bellman error optimization within a Q-learning framework. The proposed optimization scheme, leveraging the invariance of the optimal policy to scaling of the Q-function parameters, offers an elegant alternative to conventional fixed-point iteration schemes.

That said, a clearer distinction between the presented method and standard Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI), particularly regarding the fixed second term in Eq. 5, would be valuable.

The experimental results, while presented, are somewhat limited in scope and scale, relying on relatively simple simulations. It’s important to acknowledge the current lack of established benchmarks in this domain, however. Additionally, the proposed modification to the VGG network requires further empirical validation to demonstrate clear performance gains over the original architecture.

In conclusion, the paper presents a technically sound and novel contribution to the field. While the technical novelty and problem formulation are strong, bolstering the experimental analysis as suggested would significantly strengthen the paper’s overall impact.
