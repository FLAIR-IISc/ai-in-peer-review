## Review of Paper: [Paper Title – Insert Here]

**1) Summary**

This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing, specifically target following, by leveraging spatial feature maps extracted from convolutional networks pre-trained for general image classification. The authors integrate bilinear models of one-step visual feature dynamics – operating at multiple scales – with a reinforcement learning framework. This combination allows for learning a servoing policy that minimizes a regularized, weighted average of distances to features predicted by the learned visual dynamics model.

**2) Contributions**

The key contributions of this work are significant and well-articulated:

* **Quantitative Evaluation of Pre-trained Deep Features:** The authors provide controlled experiments in simulation that convincingly demonstrate the benefit of utilizing pre-trained deep features for visual servoing tasks.
* **Strong Performance Against Baselines:** The proposed method consistently outperforms a range of relevant baselines, including those relying on ground truth bounding box information, showcasing its effectiveness.
* **Principled Multi-Scale Feature Weighting:** The paper introduces a principled approach to learning weights for visual features at multiple scales, employing an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm to address the challenges posed by visual distractors.
* **Sample Efficiency:** The choice of Q-function approximator, coupled with the model-based approach to one-step visual feature dynamics, results in impressive sample efficiency during policy learning.
* **Publicly Available Benchmark Environment:** The authors release a virtual city environment designed for benchmarking visual servoing algorithms, a valuable resource for the research community.

**3) Suggestions for Improvement**

While the results are promising, several areas could be addressed to further strengthen the paper:

* **Increased Environmental Complexity:** The current simulation environment, while not trivial, would benefit from increased complexity to better evaluate the robustness of the proposed method. Specifically, the visual conditions could be enriched with greater clutter, more diverse distractors (e.g., increased traffic density, parked vehicles), and a wider range of object appearances and motions. Expanding the diversity of 3D car models—potentially utilizing resources like Google SketchUp—and populating the environment with more distractors would be a significant improvement, directly addressing the claim of robustness to visual variations.
* **Exploration of End-to-End Learning and Representation Learning:** Although the presented results are substantial, exploring end-to-end training – including fine-tuning the convolutional network – could reveal further performance gains, particularly in more challenging visual scenarios. Furthermore, the inclusion of representation learning, though briefly mentioned as a potential extension, would be highly relevant to the ICLR audience. Investigating whether the method can learn even more effective representations directly tailored to visual servoing would be a valuable contribution.
* **Improved Clarity of Implementation Details:** The paper currently presents a detailed, yet somewhat fragmented, account of numerous practical implementation details. While thoroughness is appreciated, a more structured presentation would improve readability. Consider grouping and simplifying these details, or, ideally, focusing on the most crucial aspects within the main text and providing a comprehensive open-source implementation to allow for full reproducibility and exploration of the remaining intricacies.
* **Minor Textual Corrections:** A few minor typographical errors were noted:
    * p.2: “learning is a relative[ly] recent addition” – consider removing "[ly]".
    * p.2: “be applied [to] directly learn” – consider removing "[to]".

**4) Conclusion**

Despite the limitations outlined above, this is a strong and interesting paper. The quality of the response to pre-review questions and the subsequent revisions demonstrate the authors' engagement and responsiveness. I am confident that they are capable of addressing the suggested improvements, which would result in an even more compelling and impactful contribution to the field of visual servoing. I recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing the suggestions outlined above.
