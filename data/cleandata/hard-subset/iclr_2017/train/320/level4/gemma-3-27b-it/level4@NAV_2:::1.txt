The paper proposes a novel approach for learning visual servoing based on Q-iteration. The main contributions of the paper are: 1) a bilinear dynamics model for predicting next frame (features) based on action and current frame, 2) a formulation of servoing with a Q-function that learns weights for different feature channels, and 3) an elegant method for optimizing the Bellman error to learn the Q-function.

I recommend **rejecting** the paper. While the technical contributions are promising, the experimental validation is limited and does not convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness and generalizability of the proposed approach. The reliance on a synthetic environment and the lack of comparison to more established reinforcement learning baselines, beyond those presented, raise concerns about the practical impact of this work.

The paper effectively explores different approaches to connecting action and frame representation to predict next-frame features, arguing convincingly for a locally connected bilinear model that balances computational cost and expressive ability. The weighting of different feature channels, allowing the algorithm to focus on relevant objects, is a valuable idea and demonstrably improves performance compared to an unweighted approach. Furthermore, the optimization scheme for the Bellman error, capitalizing on the invariance of the optimal policy to scaling of Q-function parameters, provides an elegant improvement over typical FQI implementations.

However, the paper lacks a comprehensive experimental comparison of the different dynamics models considered (fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected). While justifications are provided, a more direct empirical evaluation would strengthen the argument for the chosen locally connected approach.  I would have also liked to see a more detailed analysis of the impact of the proposed FQI optimization scheme compared to a standard iterative approach where the target Q-function in Eq. 5 is held fixed.  The experimental results, while positive within the scope of the presented benchmarks, are limited by the reliance on a synthetic environment.  The transfer to real-world scenarios is not addressed. The modification to the VGG network is presented without strong empirical validation; it's unclear whether the modified architecture offers any advantage over the original.  Furthermore, the performance gains relative to more standard deep reinforcement learning approaches require further scrutiny and comparison to more contemporary methods. 

To improve this paper, I suggest the following:

*   **Expand the experimental evaluation:**  Include comparisons to more established deep reinforcement learning algorithms (e.g., PPO, SAC) on the same benchmark. Consider evaluating the approach on a real-world dataset or in a simulated environment with greater visual complexity and realism.
*   **Provide a more thorough comparison of dynamics models:** Conduct experiments to directly compare the performance of the fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected bilinear models.
*   **Analyze the benefits of the proposed FQI optimization:**  Present a comparative analysis of the proposed FQI scheme versus a standard iterative approach for solving the Bellman error.
*   **Investigate the impact of the VGG network modification:**  Provide experimental results demonstrating the benefit of the modified VGG network compared to the original architecture.
*   **Address the limitations of the current benchmarks:** Discuss the limitations of the synthetic environment and the challenges of generalizing the results to real-world applications.

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   What is the computational cost of the proposed method compared to other visual servoing techniques, particularly in terms of inference time?
*   How sensitive is the performance of the approach to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the learning rate, regularization coefficient, and exploration noise?
*   Could you elaborate on the rationale behind using a bilinear model for the feature dynamics, and what alternative models were considered?
*   How does the performance of the method degrade as the visual complexity of the environment increases (e.g., more distractors, varying lighting conditions)?
*   What steps would be required to deploy this approach on a real robot platform, and what are the anticipated challenges?
