## Review of “Learning Visual Servoing with Q-Iteration”

This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing based on Q-iteration, incorporating a learned dynamics model and channel-wise weighting of feature errors. The core contributions are a bilinear dynamics model for predicting future frame features, a Q-function formulation that learns channel-specific weights, and an elegant optimization scheme for learning this Q-function based on scaling invariance.

**Strengths:**

The paper demonstrates a thoughtful exploration of methods for connecting actions and frame representations to predict subsequent frame features. The authors convincingly argue for a locally connected bilinear model as a strong balance between computational efficiency and representational power. This choice appears well-justified given the complexity of the visual domain.

A particularly compelling aspect of this work is the introduction of channel-wise weighting within the Q-function. The intuition – that different feature channels correspond to varying object importance – is sound and effectively addresses the limitations of treating errors uniformly across all channels. This is effectively validated by experimental results which consistently show performance degradation when using unweighted errors.

Furthermore, the paper tackles the challenging problem of Bellman error optimization in Q-learning. The authors present a solid and insightful optimization scheme leveraging the key observation that scaling the Q-function parameters does not alter the optimal policy. This enables an elegant approach, resembling Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI), and offers an improvement over conventional optimization schemes that directly minimize the Bellman residual (ct + γ min Q_{t+1}).

**Weaknesses:**

While Section 4 provides a reasonable rationale for the design choices regarding the dynamics model, I believe the paper would benefit significantly from a more comprehensive experimental comparison of the three proposed approaches: fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected dynamics. Quantitatively comparing their performance would strengthen the argument for the selected bilinear model.

Similarly, the optimization strategy presented is insightful, but a clear differentiation between the proposed method and standard FQI, particularly regarding the implications of holding the second term in Eq. 5 fixed, would enhance understanding. A more explicit discussion of the trade-offs involved in this choice is warranted.

**Experimental Evaluation:**

The experimental results, while demonstrating the efficacy of the approach, are somewhat unsatisfying due to the limited scale and use of toy simulations. This limitation, however, is partially mitigated by the acknowledged lack of established benchmarks in this domain. Nevertheless, scaling the experiments to more complex scenarios would greatly bolster the paper’s claims.

The proposed modification to the VGG feature extractor – while conceptually interesting – requires further experimental validation. Currently, it’s unclear whether the modified VGG architecture demonstrably outperforms the original. Demonstrating the benefits of this modification is crucial to justifying its inclusion.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically novel and well-formulated contribution to the field of visual servoing. The proposed approach is grounded in a sound understanding of Q-learning and offers a compelling solution to the challenges of learning from visual data. However, the relatively limited experimental evaluation weakens the overall impact. Addressing the concerns regarding comparative dynamics model evaluation, the connection to FQI, and the scalability of the experiments would substantially improve the strength of this work. 

Despite these limitations, the core ideas presented are promising, and the paper represents a solid step towards more effective and adaptable visual servoing systems. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly with respect to strengthening the experimental evaluation.
