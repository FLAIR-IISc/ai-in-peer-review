## Review of “Learning to Visually Servo with Deep Features and Reinforcement Learning” – ICLR 2017

**1. Summary**

This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing, specifically target following, by leveraging spatial feature maps extracted from convolutional networks pre-trained for general image classification. The authors combine bilinear models of one-step visual feature dynamics – computed at multiple scales – with a reinforcement learning framework to learn a robust servoing policy. This policy is optimized by minimizing a regularized, weighted average of distances to features predicted by the learned visual dynamics model.

**2. Contributions**

This work makes several significant contributions to the field of visual servoing:

*   **Empirical Validation of Deep Features:** The paper provides compelling, controlled experiments in simulation demonstrating the effectiveness of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing tasks.
*   **Strong Performance Against Baselines:**  The proposed method achieves demonstrable performance gains compared to a range of sensible baselines, including those utilizing ground truth bounding box information. This highlights the power of learning directly from visual data.
*   **Principled Multi-Scale Feature Weighting:** The authors introduce a well-justified method for learning weights associated with multi-scale visual features, utilizing an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm to mitigate the influence of distracting elements in the scene.
*   **Sample Efficiency:** The combination of a Q-function approximator and the model-based one-step visual feature dynamics results in excellent sample efficiency – a critical factor for practical robotic applications.
*   **Open-Source Benchmark Environment:** The release of a virtual city environment for benchmarking visual servoing is a valuable contribution to the community, facilitating further research in this area.

**3. Suggestions for Improvement**

While this paper presents a strong contribution, several avenues for improvement would further strengthen the work:

*   **Increased Environmental Complexity:**  The current environment, while not trivial, would benefit from increased visual complexity to more thoroughly evaluate the robustness of the proposed approach. Specifically, expanding the variety of object appearances (e.g., utilizing a larger and more diverse set of 3D car models, potentially sourced from platforms like Google SketchUp) and incorporating a greater number of distractor vehicles (in both static and dynamic scenarios) would provide a more challenging and realistic testbed.  Demonstrating performance under conditions of greater clutter and visual variation is crucial, given the claimed robustness of the method.
*   **End-to-End Learning and Representation Learning:** While the results are promising with fixed pre-trained features, exploring the impact of end-to-end training – including fine-tuning the convolutional network during the reinforcement learning process – would be highly valuable. This could potentially unlock further performance improvements and is essential for generalization to more challenging real-world scenarios. Furthermore, investigating the benefits of learning the feature representation *specifically* for visual servoing (i.e., performing representation learning alongside the policy learning) would be highly relevant to the ICLR audience, although the authors acknowledge this as a potential extension.
*   **Improved Presentation of Implementation Details:**  The paper contains a substantial amount of practical implementation detail, presented with varying levels of emphasis throughout the main text and appendix.  While thoroughness is appreciated, a more streamlined presentation would enhance clarity.  Consider consolidating these details into a single, well-organized section, or, preferably, providing a link to a publicly available implementation of the method, with the paper summarizing only the most critical aspects.
*   **Minor Editorial Notes:**
    *   p.2: “learning is a relatively recent addition”
    *   p.2: “be applied to directly learn”

**4. Conclusion**

Despite the limitations in experimental complexity outlined above, this is a strong and well-motivated paper. The authors have demonstrated a compelling approach to visual servoing that effectively leverages the power of deep learning and reinforcement learning.  Their thoughtful responses to pre-review questions and the subsequent revision indicate a clear understanding of the work and a willingness to address constructive criticism. I am confident that the authors are capable of addressing the suggestions for improvement outlined above, leading to an even stronger and more impactful contribution. I recommend acceptance of this paper.
