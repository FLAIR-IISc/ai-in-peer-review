This paper introduces a novel approach to visual servoing, specifically target following, by leveraging spatial feature maps extracted from convolutional neural networks pre-trained for general image classification. The core idea is to combine bilinear models representing the one-step dynamics of these visual features – considered at multiple scales – with a reinforcement learning framework to learn a robust servoing policy. This policy is trained by minimizing a regularized, weighted average of distances to features as predicted by the learned visual dynamics model.

The work presents several key contributions. First, it offers controlled experiments within a simulation environment that rigorously quantify the advantages of utilizing pre-trained deep features for visual servoing tasks. Second, the method demonstrates clear and substantial performance gains compared to several relevant baseline approaches, including those that utilize ground truth bounding box information. Third, the paper details a principled approach to learning weights for multi-scale visual features, employing an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm designed to mitigate the impact of distracting elements in the scene. Furthermore, the approach exhibits strong sample efficiency, attributable to the chosen Q-function approximator and the model-based representation of one-step visual feature dynamics. Finally, the authors publicly release a virtual city environment designed to serve as a benchmark for visual servoing research.

Several areas could benefit from further development. While the current environment is beyond a simple toy example, the experiments would be strengthened by increased visual complexity – encompassing factors like clutter, distractors, a wider range of object appearances and motions, and greater overall environmental richness and diversity. Specifically, the realism and diversity of objects could be improved by incorporating a larger set of 3D models, potentially sourced from resources like Google SketchUp, and increasing the density of distractor objects within the environment. Given the method's stated goal of robustness to visual variations, this is a particularly important consideration.

Additionally, exploring end-to-end training – including fine-tuning the convolutional network itself – would be valuable, potentially unlocking improved generalization performance in more challenging visual scenarios. Such an investigation would also allow for a direct assessment of the benefits of deep representation learning within the context of visual servoing, a topic highly relevant to the ICLR audience. While the authors acknowledge the possibility of adapting their method for this purpose, explicitly addressing it would strengthen the contribution.

Regarding reproducibility, the paper provides a clear explanation of the formalism and algorithms, but the sheer volume of practical implementation details – presented with varying levels of detail throughout the paper and appendix – can be somewhat overwhelming. Consolidating, simplifying, or reorganizing this information would enhance clarity. Ideally, the authors could summarize the most crucial implementation choices within the main text and provide a link to a publicly available implementation of the method for comprehensive documentation.

Minor typographical errors are present on page 2: “learning is a relative[ly] recent addition” and “be applied [to] directly learn.”

In conclusion, despite these suggestions for improvement, this is a compelling and well-founded paper, significantly strengthened by the authors’ thoughtful responses to pre-review questions and the subsequent revisions. This responsiveness suggests they are well-equipped to address the identified areas for improvement, potentially leading to an even more impactful contribution.
