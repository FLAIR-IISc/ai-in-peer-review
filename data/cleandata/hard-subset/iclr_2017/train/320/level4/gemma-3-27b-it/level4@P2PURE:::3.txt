1) Summary
This paper introduces a method for visual servoing, specifically target following, that leverages spatial feature maps extracted from convolutional networks pre-trained for general image classification. The authors combine bilinear models of the one-step dynamics of these visual features – considered at multiple scales – with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy. This policy is learned by minimizing a regularized, weighted average of distances to features predicted by the learned model of visual dynamics.

2) Contributions
+ The paper presents controlled experiments in simulation that quantify the benefits of using pre-trained deep features for visual servoing.
+ Significant performance improvements are demonstrated compared to several sensible baselines, including those utilizing ground truth bounding box information.
+ A principled approach to learning multi-scale visual feature weights is introduced, employing an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm to address the challenge of visual distractors.
+ The method exhibits good sample efficiency, a result of the chosen Q-function approximator and the model-based approach to predicting one-step visual feature dynamics.
+ An open-source virtual city environment is provided as a benchmark for visual servoing research.

3) Suggestions for Improvement
- More Complex Benchmark:
While the environment is not trivial, the experiments would benefit from more challenging visual conditions, including increased clutter, more distractors, greater variety in object appearances and motion, and a richer, more diverse environment. Specifically, the realism and diversity of object appearances could be enhanced by incorporating a larger and more varied set of 3D car models – potentially sourced from platforms like Google SketchUp – and populating the environment with more distractor vehicles (in traffic or parked). This is particularly important given the method’s intended robustness to visual variations.
- End-to-End Training and Representation Learning:
Although the current results are significant, exploring the impact of end-to-end training – including fine-tuning the convolutional network – would be valuable, as it may be necessary for better generalization to more challenging visual conditions. This would also allow for an assessment of the benefits of deep representation learning specifically for visual servoing, which aligns well with the scope of ICLR. Currently, the method does not incorporate representation learning, though the authors acknowledge its potential for straightforward adaptation.
- Reproducibility:
The formalism and algorithms are clearly explained; however, the paper contains a substantial amount of practical implementation details and “tricks” presented with varying levels of detail. Consolidating, simplifying, or reorganizing this exposition would improve clarity. Ideally, the most important implementation details should be summarized in the main text, with a link provided to an open-source implementation of the method for completeness.
- Typos:

4) Conclusion
Despite the limitations of the experimental setup, this paper is both interesting and solid, a quality underscored by the authors’ comprehensive responses to pre-review questions and the subsequent revision. This demonstrates their capacity to address the aforementioned suggestions for improvement, potentially leading to an even stronger final paper.
