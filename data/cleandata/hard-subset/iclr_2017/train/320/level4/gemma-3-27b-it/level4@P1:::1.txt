## Review

The paper proposes a novel approach for learning visual servoing based on Q-iteration. The main contributions of the paper are: (1) a bilinear dynamics model for predicting the next frame (features) based on the action and current frame; (2) a formulation of servoing with a Q-function that learns weights for different feature channels; and (3) an elegant method for optimizing the Bellman error to learn the Q-function.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper presents a technically sound approach and demonstrates promising results in a simulated environment, the limited scale and complexity of the experiments, coupled with a lack of comparison to strong baselines, prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper effectively explores different ways to connect the action (*u<sub>t</sub>*) and frame representation (*y<sub>t</sub>*) to predict next-frame features (*y<sub>t+1</sub>*). The justification for choosing a locally connected bilinear model, balancing computation and expressive ability, is well-articulated. The idea of weighting different feature channels to reflect their importance is also a valuable contribution, demonstrably improving performance compared to unweighted approaches. Furthermore, the proposed optimization scheme for the Bellman error leverages a key observation—that scaling the Q-function parameters does not alter the optimal policy—leading to a more elegant and efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) approach.

However, the experimental validation feels underdeveloped. While Section 4 provides rationale for different model choices, a more comprehensive comparison of the three dynamics models (fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected) would strengthen the analysis.  A direct comparison of the proposed FQI method with a standard iterative approach that holds the target Q-function fixed in Equation 5 would also be insightful. 

The experiments themselves are conducted in a relatively simple synthetic environment. While acknowledging the scarcity of benchmarks in this domain is reasonable, the lack of comparison to more sophisticated or state-of-the-art reinforcement learning algorithms specifically designed for visual control hinders a thorough assessment of the proposed method’s benefits. The modification to the VGG network is also presented without conclusive experimental evidence; it remains unclear whether the modified version genuinely outperforms the original architecture. The experiments reported in the paper do not provide sufficient evidence of generalization to more complex scenarios.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Expand Experimental Evaluation:**  Consider including experiments in a more realistic simulated environment or, ideally, on a real robotic platform.
*   **Stronger Baselines:** Compare the proposed method against established deep reinforcement learning algorithms for continuous control, such as DDPG, TD3, or SAC, tailored for image-based inputs.
*   **Ablation Study of VGG Modification:**  Provide a quantitative analysis demonstrating the impact of the modifications to the VGG network. Show results with both the original and modified VGG architectures.
*   **Clarify Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discuss the sensitivity of the method to key hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, regularization coefficient) and provide guidance on their selection.
*   **Detailed Discussion of Failure Cases:** Analyze scenarios where the method struggles and discuss potential solutions.
*   **Presentation of Results:**  Consider presenting quantitative results with error bars or confidence intervals to better illustrate the statistical significance of the findings.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the locally connected bilinear model compared to the fully connected model, providing a more detailed breakdown of the number of parameters?
*   Why was a discount factor (γ) of 0.9 chosen? Was a sensitivity analysis performed to determine an optimal value for γ?
*   What considerations were made to ensure that the exploration policy (Gaussian noise added to the current policy) was sufficiently diverse to prevent premature convergence to local optima?
*   Do you anticipate that the performance gains of the proposed approach would diminish as the complexity of the environment or the dimensionality of the input images increases? If so, what strategies could be employed to mitigate this issue?
*   Regarding the experimental setup, what is the reasoning behind initializing the quadcopter at random positions relative to the car, and how might this initial distribution affect the results?
