This paper tackles the problem of learning visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The core idea is to move beyond hand-engineered features and dynamics, learning a servoing mechanism directly from data, allowing for quick adaptation to new targets. The approach centers on servoing in the space of learned visual features, utilizing a bilinear predictive model and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to determine the most relevant features for the task.

I recommend **rejecting** this paper. While the proposed approach is novel and the technical details are well-presented, the experimental validation is insufficient to demonstrate a convincing advantage over existing methods, and key design choices lack sufficient justification through experimentation.

The paper's strength lies in its thoughtful combination of established techniques. The motivation for using pre-trained features to leverage existing visual knowledge is strong, and the bilinear dynamics model provides a computationally tractable way to predict feature evolution. The fitted Q-iteration approach, leveraging the property that scaling the Q-function doesn’t change the optimal policy, is a clever optimization strategy. The authors clearly articulate the challenges of visual servoing and position their work within the existing literature. 

However, the experimental evaluation is a significant weakness. The reliance on a synthetic environment, while acknowledged, limits the generalizability of the results. More critically, the comparison between different dynamics models (fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected) is inadequate. The authors argue for the locally connected model, but provide limited experimental evidence to support this choice – a more thorough comparison with ablation studies is needed. Similarly, while the improvement over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning is reported as substantial, the scale of the improvement feels overstated given the simplicity of the benchmark and the lack of comparison with more recent advancements in deep RL. The comparison to classical IBVS methods is useful, but the performance gap appears to be sensitive to ground truth information (depth, car dynamics).

The modification of the VGG network (replacing max-pooling layers with dilated convolutions) is presented without rigorous evaluation. The authors claim it improves performance, but do not demonstrate this empirically, leaving the reader to question the necessity and effectiveness of this modification. Furthermore, the claim of "two orders of magnitude" improvement in sample efficiency over standard deep RL requires stronger justification, particularly given the specific task and simulation environment.

To improve this paper, I suggest the following:

*   **Expand the experimental evaluation:** Include experiments in a more realistic simulation environment or, ideally, on a real robotic platform.
*   **Conduct a more thorough comparison of dynamics models:** Provide a detailed ablation study comparing the performance of the fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected bilinear models. Report statistical significance of any observed differences.
*   **Validate the VGG modification:** Include experiments demonstrating the impact of the modified VGG network on performance. Compare against the original VGG architecture.
*   **Strengthen the comparison to deep RL:** Compare against more recent and competitive deep RL algorithms, potentially using more sophisticated hyperparameter tuning. Consider providing a baseline with the unmodified VGG features as input to a deep RL agent.
*   **Provide more details on the experimental setup:** Clarify the specific hyperparameters used for each experiment (learning rates, regularization parameters, batch sizes, etc.).
*   **Address the sensitivity to ground truth:** Investigate the performance of the IBVS methods without access to ground truth depth and car dynamics.

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   Regarding the VGG modification, what was the rationale for choosing dilated convolutions specifically? Were other modifications considered?
*   The paper mentions a regularization coefficient (ν) in the FQI algorithm. How was this value chosen, and is the performance sensitive to its setting?
*   What is the computational cost (e.g., training time, inference time) of the proposed approach compared to the other methods considered?
*   Could you elaborate on the choice of the reward function? What impact would different reward structures have on the learned policy?
