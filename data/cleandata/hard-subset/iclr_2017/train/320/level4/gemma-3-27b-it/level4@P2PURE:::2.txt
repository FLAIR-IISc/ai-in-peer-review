This paper investigates the advantages of visual servoing with learned visual representations. The authors propose learning an action-conditional bilinear model of visual features – extracted from a pre-trained VGG network – and then deriving a control policy by linearizing the dynamics. They introduce a multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of this bilinear model. To address the model's single-step-ahead prediction horizon, the paper introduces a weighted objective function that incorporates long-term policy values, evaluated using a fitted-value approach. The paper is well-written, mathematically sound, and conceptually thorough. The experiments convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the value-weighted objective, representing a significant contribution. Furthermore, this work appears to be the first to present a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm. Empirical results also show that leveraging pre-trained visual features improves generalization performance. Overall, I recommend this paper for acceptance as it has the potential to be highly beneficial to the robotics research community. However, with respect to the focus of this conference, I believe the contribution concerning visual “representation” is somewhat limited. While the paper successfully demonstrates the utility of a pre-trained VGG representation, it does not explore learning the representation end-to-end – although the authors do not necessarily claim it should. Notably, the paper dedicates more attention to the control problem than to the representation learning aspect. Additionally, the policy representation is fixed, and values are approximated linearly using features specific to the task. This does not diminish the paper's value, but positions it as potentially less central to the core themes of ICLR.
