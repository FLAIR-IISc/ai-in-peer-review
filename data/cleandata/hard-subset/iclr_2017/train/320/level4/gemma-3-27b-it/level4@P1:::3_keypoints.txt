- Decision: Accept.

- Contribution: Combines pre-trained convolutional features, multi-scale bilinear one-step feature dynamics, and fitted Q-iteration to learn a visual servoing (target-following) policy.

- Main strength: Leverages deep pre-trained features and a predictive dynamics model to achieve robust, adaptable visual servoing.

- Sample efficiency: Demonstrates more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample efficiency over standard deep RL, making the approach practical for real-world data-limited settings.

- Empirical validation: Well-designed experiments with extensive comparisons to baselines (including hand-designed features and ground truth) showing clear performance gains.

- Reproducibility: Authors provide an open-source environment and code, improving accessibility and reproducibility.

- Suggestion — benchmark complexity: Increase visual/environmental complexity (more clutter, varied distractors, realistic object appearances) to better test robustness.

- Suggestion — representation learning: Explore end-to-end training and fine-tuning of convolutional features to possibly further improve performance and appeal to the ICLR audience.

- Suggestion — presentation: Streamline and reorganize implementation details (move to clearer supplemental material) and provide a clearer pointer to the code.

- Minor edits: Fix small typos on page 2 ("learning is a relatively recent addition", "be applied to directly learn") for clarity.

- Question 1: Why do deeper VGG features (e.g., conv5_3) perform worse — is loss of spatial information the main cause and how could it be mitigated?

- Question 2: How sensitive is performance to the quality/diversity of the hand-coded policy dataset used to train the dynamics model, and would alternative data collection (e.g., random exploration) change results?

- Question 3: Please provide a detailed computational-time breakdown for each stage (dynamics model training, FQI weight learning, and execution) to assess real-time practicality.

- Question 4: Describe the feature standardization process in detail — mean subtraction and scaling by standard deviation, and whether this is done per channel or across all features.
