This paper proposes a method for visual servoing, specifically target following, by leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained for image classification. The authors combine bilinear models predicting one-step dynamics of visual feature maps at multiple scales with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy. This policy is learned by minimizing a regularized, weighted average of distances to features predicted by the visual dynamics model.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a compelling approach to visual servoing that effectively combines learned representations with reinforcement learning. The significant performance improvements over established baselines, coupled with strong sample efficiency, make this a valuable contribution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of utilizing pre-trained deep features within a model-based reinforcement learning framework is well-motivated. Traditional visual servoing often relies on manually designed features and dynamics models, limiting generalization. This work addresses this limitation by learning these components, enabling adaptation to new targets with minimal data. The experimental results clearly demonstrate the efficacy of this approach. The improvements over pixel-based and hand-designed feature methods are substantial, and the sample efficiency – achieving good performance with only 20 training trajectories – is particularly impressive, exceeding state-of-the-art model-free deep reinforcement learning methods by a considerable margin. The provision of a virtual city environment for benchmarking also aids reproducibility and future research. The fitted Q-iteration implementation appears to be a critical component, enabling efficient learning of appropriate feature weights.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

While the current experiments demonstrate strong results, expanding the complexity of the benchmark environment would further strengthen the paper. Introducing more visual clutter, diverse distractor objects, and richer environment details (e.g., a greater variety of 3D car models sourced from resources like Google SketchUp) would better assess the robustness of the approach, which is a key claimed benefit. 

Exploring end-to-end training, including fine-tuning the convolutional network, could reveal further performance gains and provide insights into the benefits of joint representation learning for visual servoing – a topic particularly relevant to the ICLR audience. Although the authors mention this possibility briefly, investigating it would be beneficial.

The paper contains a wealth of implementation details, dispersed throughout the text and appendix. Consolidating these details, perhaps into a dedicated implementation section or linking to a comprehensive open-source implementation, would improve clarity and reproducibility. This would also aid readers focusing on the high-level contributions of the work.

Finally, a few minor typographical corrections are suggested: "relatively recent addition" (p.2) and "to directly learn" (p.2).

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper highlights the importance of the bilinear dynamics model. Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of the performance to the accuracy of this model? For instance, what happens if a simpler, less accurate dynamics model is used?
2.  Regarding the choice of the VGG network, were other pre-trained networks (e.g., ResNet, Inception) considered? If so, what were the results, and why was VGG ultimately chosen?
3.  The regularization parameter (ν) in the Q-function update is mentioned. How was this parameter selected, and what impact does it have on the learned policy?
4.  In the experiments, the car's movement is predetermined. How might the approach be adapted to scenarios where the target's motion is unpredictable or actively evasive?
5.  You mention that the locally connected bilinear model reduces the number of parameters. Could you quantify this reduction compared to a fully connected model, and discuss the trade-offs in terms of accuracy and computational cost?
