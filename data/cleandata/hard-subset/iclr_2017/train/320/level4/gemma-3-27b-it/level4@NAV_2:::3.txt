This paper proposes a method for visual servoing, specifically target following, leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained for image classification. The approach combines bilinear models of one-step visual feature dynamics at multiple scales with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy. This policy minimizes a regularized, weighted average of distances to features predicted by the learned dynamics model.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a technically sound approach and demonstrates promising results, the reliance on a relatively simple synthetic environment limits the generalizability and impact of the work. The demonstrated improvements, while substantial within the confines of the simulation, need to be validated in more complex and realistic scenarios to justify acceptance at a highly competitive conference.

The paper's strengths lie in its controlled experiments quantifying the benefits of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing, clear performance gains over several baselines (including those utilizing ground truth bounding boxes), a principled learning approach for multi-scale visual feature weights using an efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm, commendable sample efficiency due to the Q-function approximator and model-based one-step dynamics, and the provision of an open-source virtual city environment for benchmarking.

However, several areas require further attention. First, the benchmark environment, while not trivial, could benefit from increased complexity. The visual conditions could be enhanced through increased clutter, diverse distractors, greater appearance and motion variety, and a richer, more diverse environment. Utilizing a larger and more realistic set of 3D car models (e.g., sourced from Google SketchUp) and populating the environment with more distractor vehicles would better assess the method’s robustness to visual variations, a key claimed advantage. Second, exploring end-to-end training, including fine-tuning the convolutional network, would be valuable. This would assess the potential for improved generalization in more challenging visual conditions and investigate the benefits of deep representation learning for visual servoing. Although briefly mentioned, incorporating representation learning would be particularly relevant for this conference. Finally, while the formalism and algorithms are well-explained, the numerous implementation details are scattered throughout the paper and appendix with varying levels of detail. Consolidating and simplifying these details, or linking to a comprehensive open-source implementation, would enhance clarity. 

Specific suggestions include: exploring more complex benchmark environments, investigating end-to-end training and representation learning, improving the organization and accessibility of implementation details, and addressing minor typographical errors (e.g., p.2: "learning is a relative[ly] recent addition," p.2: "be applied [to] directly learn").

To further clarify my understanding and strengthen my assessment, I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  How sensitive is the performance to the specific pre-trained convolutional network architecture (e.g., VGG, ResNet)? Have you explored other architectures and, if so, what were the results?
2.  The bilinear dynamics model appears crucial to the approach. How was the hyperparameter (e.g., regularization) for the bilinear dynamics model chosen, and how does performance vary with these settings?
3.  Regarding the choice of the cost function, what alternatives were considered, and what motivated the selection of the current cost function?
4.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with the different components of the system (feature extraction, dynamics prediction, reinforcement learning)? What are the key bottlenecks?
5.  Given the limitation of the current simulation, what concrete plans do the authors have to evaluate the proposed method in more realistic environments (e.g., using real-world datasets or robotic platforms)?
