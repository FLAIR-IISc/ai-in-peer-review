This paper investigates the benefits of visual servoing using a learned visual representation. The authors propose to combine learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to learn visual servoing mechanisms, focusing on target following with the goal of quick adaptation to new targets. They present an approach that utilizes pre-trained features (from a VGG network) with an action-conditional bilinear model to predict visual feature dynamics, and derive a policy through linearization of these dynamics. A multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of the bilinear model is introduced. To address the one-step prediction horizon of the bilinear model, the authors propose a weighted objective incorporating long-term policy values, optimized using a fitted Q-iteration approach. 

I recommend rejecting this paper. While technically sound and conceptually thorough, the primary contribution lies in the control framework rather than novel representation learning. The paper demonstrates the utility of existing, pre-trained VGG features, but does not explore learning a representation specifically tailored for visual servoing.

The paper is well-written and mathematically rigorous, and the experiments convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed weighted objective and fitted Q-iteration algorithm. The proposed trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm appears to be a novel contribution. The empirical results convincingly show the benefits of the approach, and the comparisons to existing methods are well-executed. However, given the conference focus, the limited exploration of the representation learning aspect is a significant drawback. The policy representation is fixed, and the Q-values are approximated in a linear fashion using problem-specific features, which limits the broader impact on representation learning research.

To potentially improve this work, I suggest the authors consider the following:

*   **Representation Learning Exploration:** While end-to-end learning isn’t necessarily required, exploring modifications or fine-tuning of the pre-trained features specifically for visual servoing could strengthen the representation learning component.
*   **Ablation Study on Feature Resolution:** A more detailed ablation study exploring the impact of different VGG feature levels (e.g., conv1\_2, conv4\_3) on performance, and providing a more nuanced explanation of why deeper features sometimes perform worse, would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Complexity Analysis:**  A clearer discussion of the computational cost associated with the bilinear dynamics model and the fitted Q-iteration algorithm, especially in relation to real-time control applications, would be valuable.
*   **Generalization to More Complex Environments:** Testing the proposed approach in more complex and visually diverse environments would further demonstrate its robustness and generalizability.

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors:

*   In Section 5.2, you mention a "tractable linear approximator for the Q-function." Could you elaborate on the specific mathematical properties that enable this tractability?
*   In Section 6.3, the TRPO policies are trained for 50 iterations, substantially more than the FQI approach. Was any effort made to optimize the TRPO hyperparameters to achieve comparable sample efficiency, or were these settings chosen based on computational constraints?
*   Regarding the choice of the bilinear model, what alternative model structures were considered and why was the bilinear model ultimately selected? What are its limitations?
