1) Summary
This paper proposes a method for visual servoing – specifically, target following – that leverages spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained for general image classification. The authors combine bilinear models of the one-step dynamics of these visual feature maps, at multiple scales, with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy. This policy is learned by minimizing a regularized, weighted average of distances to features predicted by the visual dynamics model.

2) Contributions
+ Controlled experiments in simulation demonstrate the benefits of using pre-trained deep features for visual servoing.
+ Clear performance improvements are shown compared to several sensible baselines, including those utilizing ground truth bounding boxes.
+ The method employs a principled approach to learning multi-scale visual feature weights, using an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm to address the problem of distractors.
+ Good sample efficiency is achieved thanks to the choice of Q-function approximator and the model-based, one-step visual feature dynamics.
+ The authors provide an open-source virtual city environment for benchmarking visual servoing.

3) Suggestions for Improvement
- More Complex Benchmark:
While the environment is not merely a toy example, the experiments would benefit from more challenging visual conditions, such as increased clutter, more distractors, greater variety in object appearance and motion, and a richer, more diverse environment overall. Specifically, the realism and diversity of object appearances could be significantly enhanced by using a larger number of 3D car models – including more realistic and diverse options available from sources like Google SketchUp – and by populating the environment with more distractor cars, simulating traffic or parked vehicles. This is crucial, as a key strength of the proposed approach is its robustness to visual variations.
- End-to-End Training and Representation Learning:
Although the current results are promising, it would be valuable to assess the impact of end-to-end training (i.e., fine-tuning the convolutional network) as it may be necessary for better generalization in more challenging visual conditions. This would also allow for an evaluation of the benefits of deep representation learning for visual servoing, a topic highly relevant to ICLR. Currently, the method does not explicitly perform representation learning, though the authors acknowledge its straightforward adaptability.
- Reproducibility:
The formalism and algorithms are clearly explained, but the paper includes a considerable number of practical tricks and implementation details, presented with varying degrees of detail throughout the paper and appendix. While grouping, simplifying, or reorganizing this information would be helpful, a more effective solution would be to summarize only the most important details in the main text and link to an open-source implementation of the method for a complete understanding.
- Typos:
p.2: "learning is a relatively recent addition"
p.2: "be applied to directly learn"

4) Conclusion
Despite the limitations of the experimental setup, this paper presents an interesting and solid contribution, particularly in light of the excellent responses to pre-review questions and the subsequent revision. This suggests the authors are well-equipped to address the aforementioned suggestions for improvement, potentially leading to an even stronger paper.
