- Paper proposes learning visual servoing (target following) using learned visual features, a bilinear predictive dynamics model, and sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration to select task-relevant features.
- Core claim: move beyond hand-engineered features/dynamics to enable rapid adaptation to new targets by servoing in learned feature space.
- Recommendation: reject due to insufficient experimental validation and lack of justification for key design choices.
- Strength: thoughtful combination of established techniques and clear problem motivation.
- Strength: leveraging pre-trained features to reuse visual knowledge is well motivated.
- Strength: bilinear dynamics model is computationally tractable for predicting feature evolution.
- Strength: fitted Q-iteration trick (scaling Q without changing optimal policy) is a clever efficiency optimization.
- Main weakness: experiments rely on a synthetic environment, limiting generalizability to real-world robotics.
- Main weakness: inadequate comparison between dynamics models (fully connected, convolutional, locally connected); insufficient ablation and statistical analysis.
- Main weakness: claimed large improvement over model-free deep RL feels overstated given simple benchmark and lack of comparison to recent RL methods.
- Main weakness: modification of VGG (replacing max-pooling with dilated convolutions) is presented without empirical validation.
- Main weakness: the “two orders of magnitude” sample-efficiency claim lacks strong justification given task and simulation specifics.
- Main weakness: IBVS comparison appears sensitive to access to ground-truth depth and vehicle dynamics; robustness unclear.
- Suggested fixes: expand evaluation to realistic simulators or real robots and run thorough ablations comparing dynamics models with statistical significance.
- Suggested fixes: empirically validate the VGG modification vs original VGG and include comparisons to more recent deep RL baselines (and an unmodified-VGG RL baseline).
- Suggested fixes: provide full experimental details (hyperparameters, regularization, batch sizes) and study sensitivity to ground-truth assumptions.
- Questions for authors: why dilated convolutions in VGG and were alternatives considered?
- Questions for authors: how was the FQI regularization coefficient chosen and how sensitive is performance to it?
- Questions for authors: what are training and inference computational costs relative to baselines?
- Questions for authors: rationale for chosen reward function and how different reward structures affect the learned policy?
