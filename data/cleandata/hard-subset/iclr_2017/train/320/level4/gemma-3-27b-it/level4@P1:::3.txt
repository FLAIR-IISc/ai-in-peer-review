This paper proposes to tackle visual servoing (specifically target following) using spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on general image classification tasks. The authors combine bilinear models of one-step dynamics of visual feature maps at multiple scales with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy. This policy is learned by minimizing a regularized weighted average of distances to features predicted by the aforementioned model of visual dynamics.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a compelling approach to visual servoing, successfully combining learned visual features, predictive dynamics, and reinforcement learning. The results demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods, particularly in sample efficiency, and the provided experiments are well-designed and clearly presented.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The key strength of this work lies in its ability to leverage pre-trained deep features for a traditionally challenging robotics problem. By learning a predictive model of feature dynamics and utilizing a sample-efficient reinforcement learning algorithm (fitted Q-iteration), the authors achieve robust and adaptable visual servoing. The improvement in sample efficiency – more than two orders of magnitude over standard deep reinforcement learning – is particularly noteworthy and makes the approach practical for real-world applications where data collection can be expensive. The extensive comparison to baseline methods, including those using hand-designed features and ground truth information, convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The open-source environment and code also contribute to the reproducibility and accessibility of the research.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, here are some suggestions that could further improve the work:

*   **More Complex Benchmark:** The current synthetic environment, while not trivial, could benefit from increased complexity. Introducing more visual clutter, a greater variety of distractors (both static and moving), and more realistic object appearances (e.g., a wider range of 3D car models) would strengthen the claims of robustness to visual variations.
*   **End-to-End and Representation Learning:** Investigating end-to-end training, including fine-tuning the convolutional network, could reveal further performance gains and highlight the potential for deep representation learning within this framework. Exploring this aspect would align well with the interests of the ICLR audience.
*   **Implementation Details:** The paper contains a considerable amount of implementation detail scattered throughout the text and appendices. Streamlining and organizing these details, potentially by moving them to a more accessible location within the supplementary material, would improve readability. A clearer pointer to the open-source implementation would also be beneficial.
*   **Minor Typos:** On page 2, "learning is a relatively recent addition" and "be applied to directly learn" could be slightly improved for clarity.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The authors mention that deeper VGG features (e.g., conv5\_3) performed worse. Could you elaborate on why you believe this is the case? Specifically, is the loss of spatial information a primary factor, and how might this be mitigated?
2.  In the experiments, you use a hand-coded policy to generate the initial dataset for training the dynamics model. How sensitive is the performance of the system to the quality and diversity of this initial data? Would a different data collection strategy (e.g., random exploration) significantly impact the results?
3.  Regarding the computational cost, could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the time required for each stage of the process (training the dynamics model, learning the weights with FQI, and execution)? This would help assess the practicality of the approach for real-time applications.
4.  You mention that the features are standardized with respect to the training set. Could you please detail this standardization process? Does it involve subtracting the mean and dividing by the standard deviation, and is this done per channel or over all features?
