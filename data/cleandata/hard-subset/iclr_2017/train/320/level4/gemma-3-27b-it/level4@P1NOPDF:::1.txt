The paper proposes a novel approach to learning visual servoing based on Q-iteration. The main contributions are: 
1. A bilinear dynamics model for predicting the next frame (features) given an action and the current frame.
2. A formulation of servoing with a Q-function that learns weights for different feature channels.
3. An elegant method for optimizing the Bellman error to learn the Q-function.

**Pros:**

+ The paper effectively explores various ways to connect actions (ut) and frame representations (yt) to predict next-frame features (y_{t+1}). The authors convincingly argue for a locally connected bilinear model, which balances computational cost and expressive power.
+ The idea of weighting different channels to capture the importance of objects within those channels appears more effective than treating errors across all channels equally. This is validated experimentally, as the unweighted performance consistently lags behind.
+ Solving the Bellman error is a challenging aspect of Q-learning. This paper presents a robust optimization scheme based on the key observation that scaling the Q-function parameters does not affect the optimal policy. This enables a more elegant Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) approach compared to typical schemes which minimize (ct + \gamma min_a Q_{t+1}). 

**Cons:**

- While Section 4 provides good justification for the design choices, I would have appreciated more experimental results comparing the three dynamics models: fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected.
- I would also like to have seen a more direct comparison between the proposed FQI approach and an iterative approach that holds the second term in Eq. 5 fixed.

**Experimental Results:**

- Overall, I find the experimental results somewhat unconvincing due to the small scale and reliance on toy simulations. However, it’s important to acknowledge the current lack of established benchmarks in this domain.
- As noted in the pre-review discussion, the proposed modification to VGG needs to be experimentally validated. In its current form, it is unclear whether the modified VGG architecture would outperform the original version.

**Overall:**

The paper’s contribution is solid in terms of technical novelty and problem formulation. However, stronger experiments – as suggested above – would significantly bolster the claims made.
