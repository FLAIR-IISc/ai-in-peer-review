1) Summary

This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing, specifically target following, by leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on general image classification tasks. The authors combine bilinear models of one-step dynamics of visual feature maps at multiple scales with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy. This policy is learned by minimizing a regularized weighted average of distances to features predicted by the aforementioned model of visual dynamics.

2) Contributions

+ The authors present controlled experiments in simulation that quantify the usefulness of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing, providing valuable insights into their effectiveness.
+ The proposed method demonstrates clear performance benefits compared to several sensible baselines, including those using ground truth bounding boxes, highlighting its potential.
+ The paper introduces a principled learning approach for multi-scale visual feature weights using an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm, effectively addressing the problem of distractors.
+ The method exhibits good sample efficiency, which can be attributed to the choice of Q-function approximator and the model-based one-step visual feature dynamics.
+ The authors provide an open-source virtual city environment to benchmark visual servoing, facilitating future research and comparisons.

3) Suggestions for improvement

- More complex benchmark:
While the environment is more realistic than a toy synthetic one, the experiments would greatly benefit from more complex visual conditions, such as clutter, distractors, appearance and motion variety, environment richness, and diversity. To enhance realism and diversity, the authors could consider using a larger number of 3D car models, including more realistic and diverse ones obtained from sources like Google SketchUp, and populating the environment with more distractor cars (in traffic or parked). This is crucial, as the primary desired quality of the approach is robustness to visual variations.

- End-to-end and representation learning:
Although the current synthetic experiments already demonstrate significant improvements, it would be interesting to investigate the impact of end-to-end training (i.e., also fine-tuning the convnet) on the method's performance. This would allow for better generalization in more challenging visual conditions and provide insights into the benefits of deep representation learning for visual servoing, which is relevant to the ICLR community (currently, there is no representation learning, although the method can be adapted as mentioned briefly by the authors).

- Reproducibility:
While the formalism and algorithms are clearly explained, the paper and appendix contain a multitude of practical tricks and implementation details described with varying levels of detail. To improve reproducibility, the authors could group, simplify, or reorganize the exposition of these implementation details. Alternatively, they could summarize the most important ones in the main text and provide a link to an open-source implementation of the method for completeness.

- Typos:
p.2: "learning is a relative[ly] recent addition"
p.2: "be applied [to] directly learn"

4) Conclusion

Despite the limitations of the experiments, this paper is interesting and solid, partly due to the authors' excellent response to the pre-review questions and the subsequent improved revision. This suggests that the authors are capable of addressing the suggested improvements, which would lead to an even better paper.
