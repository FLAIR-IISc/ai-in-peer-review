This paper proposes a novel approach for learning visual servoing based on Q-iteration. The main contributions of the paper are threefold: 

1. A bilinear dynamics model for predicting next frame features based on action and current frame.
2. A formulation of servoing with a Q-function that learns weights for different feature channels.
3. An elegant method for optimizing the Bellman error to learn the Q-function.

The paper has several strengths. Notably, it explores different ways to connect the action (ut) and frame representation (yt) to predict next frame features (y_{t+1}). The authors argue in favor of a locally connected bilinear model, which strikes a balance between computation and expressive ability. Additionally, the idea of weighting different channels to capture the importance of objects in different channels seems more effective than treating errors across all channels equally. This is also validated experimentally, where unweighted performance suffers consistently. Furthermore, the paper presents a solid optimization scheme for solving the Bellman error, based on the key observation that scaling Q-function parameters does not affect the best policy chosen. This enables a more elegant fitted Q-iteration (FQI) approach, as opposed to typical optimization schemes that fix (ct + \gamma minu Q_{t+1}).

However, there are some limitations and areas for improvement. While Section 4 makes good arguments for different choices, more experimental results comparing the three approaches (fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected dynamics) would be beneficial. Furthermore, it would be interesting to see the difference between FQI and an iterative approach that holds the second term in Eq. 5 fixed. 

The experimental results presented in the paper are somewhat unsatisfying due to the small scale and toy simulations. However, it is worth recognizing that there is a lack of benchmarks in this domain. Additionally, as pointed out in the pre-review section, the idea of modifying the VGG network needs to be experimentally validated. In its current form, it is unclear whether the modified VGG would perform better than the original version.

Overall, the paper's contribution is solid in terms of technical novelty and problem formulation. However, stronger experiments would be necessary to bolster its claims.
