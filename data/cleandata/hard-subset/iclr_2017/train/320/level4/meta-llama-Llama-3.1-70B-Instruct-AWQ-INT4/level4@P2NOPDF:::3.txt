**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing, specifically target following, by leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on general image classification tasks. The authors combine bilinear models of one-step dynamics of visual feature maps at multiple scales with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy. This policy is learned by minimizing a regularized weighted average of distances to features predicted by the aforementioned model of visual dynamics.

**Contributions**

The paper makes several key contributions:

* Controlled experiments in simulation demonstrate the effectiveness of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing, providing quantitative insights into their usefulness.
* The proposed approach outperforms several sensible baselines, including those using ground truth bounding boxes, showcasing its clear performance benefits.
* The authors introduce a principled method for learning multi-scale visual feature weights using an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm, effectively handling the problem of distractors.
* The choice of Q-function approximator and the model-based one-step visual feature dynamics enable good sample efficiency.
* The open-source virtual city environment provides a valuable benchmark for visual servoing.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

To further strengthen the paper, the following suggestions are proposed:

* **More complex benchmark**: While the virtual city environment is a step beyond synthetic toy environments, the experiments would greatly benefit from more complex visual conditions, such as clutter, distractors, and varied object appearances. Incorporating more realistic and diverse 3D car models, as well as increasing the number of distractor cars, would enhance the realism and diversity of the environment.
* **End-to-end and representation learning**: Although the current results are promising, it would be interesting to investigate the impact of end-to-end training, including fine-tuning the convolutional network. This would allow for a more comprehensive evaluation of the approach's generalization capabilities and the benefits of deep representation learning for visual servoing.
* **Reproducibility**: While the formalism and algorithms are clearly explained, the numerous practical tricks and implementation details scattered throughout the paper and appendix may overwhelm readers. Grouping, simplifying, or reorganizing these details would improve the paper's clarity. Alternatively, summarizing the most important details and providing a link to an open-source implementation would enhance reproducibility.
* **Typos**: Minor corrections are needed on page 2, specifically "learning is a relative[ly] recent addition" and "be applied [to] directly learn".

**Conclusion**

Despite the limitations of the experiments, this paper is well-written and solid. The authors' excellent response to pre-review questions and the subsequent revision demonstrate their capability to address the suggested improvements. With further refinement, this paper has the potential to become even stronger.
