1) Summary
This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing, specifically target following, by leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on general image classification tasks. The authors integrate bilinear models of one-step dynamics of visual feature maps across multiple scales with a reinforcement learning algorithm to derive a servoing policy. This policy is learned by minimizing a regularized weighted average of distances to features predicted by the aforementioned model of visual dynamics.

2) Contributions
+ The paper provides controlled experiments in simulation that quantify the effectiveness of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing.
+ The results demonstrate clear performance benefits compared to various sensible baselines, including those using ground truth bounding boxes.
+ The authors propose a principled approach to learning multi-scale visual feature weights using an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm, effectively addressing the problem of distractors.
+ The method exhibits good sample efficiency due to the choice of Q-function approximator and the model-based one-step visual feature dynamics.
+ An open-source virtual city environment is provided to benchmark visual servoing.

3) Suggestions for improvement
- More comprehensive benchmarking:
While the environment is more realistic than a toy synthetic setup, the experiments would greatly benefit from more complex visual conditions, such as clutter, distractors, varied appearances and motion, and increased environmental richness and diversity. Incorporating a larger number of 3D car models, including more realistic and diverse ones from sources like Google SketchUp, and adding more distractor cars would enhance the realism and diversity of object appearances. This is crucial, as the primary desired quality of the approach is robustness to visual variations.
- End-to-end training and representation learning:
Although the current improvements are significant, it would be interesting to investigate the impact of end-to-end training (including fine-tuning the convnet) on performance, particularly in more challenging visual conditions. This would also allow for measuring the benefits of deep representation learning for visual servoing, which is relevant to ICLR (currently, there is no representation learning, although the method can be adapted as mentioned by the authors).
- Reproducibility:
While the formalism and algorithms are clearly explained, the paper and appendix contain a multitude of practical tricks and implementation details described with varying levels of detail. To improve reproducibility, it would be helpful to group, simplify, or reorganize the exposition of these implementation details. Alternatively, summarizing the most important details in the main text and providing an open-source implementation of the method would enhance completeness.
- Typos:
p.2: "learning is a relative[ly] recent addition"
p.2: "be applied [to] directly learn"

4) Conclusion
Despite the limitations of the experiments, this paper is interesting and solid, thanks in part to the authors' excellent response to pre-review questions and the subsequent improved revision. This suggests that the authors are capable of addressing the suggested improvements, leading to an even stronger paper.
