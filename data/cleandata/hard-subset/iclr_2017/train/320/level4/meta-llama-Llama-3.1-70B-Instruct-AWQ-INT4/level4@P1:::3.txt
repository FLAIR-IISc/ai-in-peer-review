1) Summary

This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing, specifically target following, by leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on general image classification tasks. The authors combine bilinear models of one-step dynamics of visual feature maps at multiple scales with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy. This policy is learned by minimizing a regularized weighted average of distances to features predicted by the aforementioned model of visual dynamics.

2) Decision: Accept

I recommend accepting this paper, with two key reasons for this choice. Firstly, the paper presents a well-motivated and well-placed approach in the literature, tackling the problem of visual servoing using learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. Secondly, the paper supports its claims with substantial experimental results, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach in a complex synthetic car following benchmark.

3) Supporting arguments for the decision

The paper's approach to visual servoing is well-motivated, as it addresses the limitations of traditional visual servoing methods that rely on manually designed features and analytical dynamics models. The use of learned visual features and predictive dynamics models allows for more robust and generalizable visual servoing mechanisms. The paper's experimental results are also substantial, demonstrating significant improvements over conventional approaches and other learning-based methods.

The paper's contributions are multifaceted. The authors provide controlled experiments in simulation, quantifying the usefulness of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing. They also demonstrate clear performance benefits with respect to many sensible baselines, including ones using ground truth bounding boxes. The principled learning of multi-scale visual feature weights with an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm is another significant contribution. Additionally, the paper showcases good sample efficiency thanks to the choice of Q-function approximator and the model-based one-step visual feature dynamics.

4) Additional feedback

While the paper is well-written and the approach is well-motivated, there are a few areas that could be improved. Firstly, the experiments could benefit from more complex visual conditions, such as clutter, distractors, appearance and motion variety, environment richness and diversity, etc. This would help to further demonstrate the robustness of the approach to visual variations.

Secondly, it would be interesting to explore the impact of end-to-end training, including fine-tuning the convnet, on the performance of the approach. This could potentially lead to better generalization in more challenging visual conditions.

Finally, while the paper provides a clear formalism and algorithms, the exposition of practical tricks and implementation details could be improved. Grouping, simplifying, or reorganizing the exposition of these implementation details would help to make the paper more readable.

5) Questions for the authors

* Can you provide more details on how the pre-trained features were selected and fine-tuned for the visual servoing task?
* How do you plan to extend the approach to more complex visual conditions, such as those mentioned above?
* Have you considered exploring the impact of end-to-end training on the performance of the approach?
* Can you provide more details on the computational efficiency of the approach, including the time complexity of the algorithms and the computational resources required?
