This paper explores the benefits of visual servoing using a learned visual representation, proposing a novel approach that combines a learned action-conditional bilinear model with a linearized dynamics-based policy derivation. The authors introduce a multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of the bilinear model, which predicts dynamics one step ahead. To address the limitation of single-step prediction, the paper presents a weighted objective that incorporates long-term values of the current policy, evaluated using a fitted-value approach. The paper is well-written, mathematically sound, and conceptually thorough.

The experimental results demonstrate the benefits of using a value-weighted objective, a significant contribution of this paper. Additionally, the paper appears to be the first to outline a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm. The use of pre-trained visual features is also empirically shown to facilitate generalization. Overall, I recommend this paper as it is likely to benefit many researchers in robotics.

However, in the context of this conference, I find the contribution specifically on the "representation" problem to be limited in scope. While the paper demonstrates the usefulness of a pre-trained VGG representation, it does not explore learning the representation end-to-end. This is not to suggest that end-to-end learning is necessary, but rather that the paper focuses more on the control problem than the representation learning aspect. Furthermore, the policy representation is fixed, and the values are approximated in linear form using problem-specific features. Although this does not diminish the paper's value, it may make it less aligned with the primary focus of ICLR.
