This paper explores the benefits of visual servoing using a learned visual representation. The authors propose a novel approach, first learning an action-conditional bilinear model of the visual features (extracted from a pre-trained VGG network), from which a policy can be derived using a linearization of the dynamics. A key contribution of the paper is the introduction of a multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of the bilinear model.

The bilinear model, however, only predicts the dynamics one step ahead, prompting the authors to propose a weighted objective that incorporates the long-term values of the current policy. To address the evaluation problem, they employ a fitted-value approach. The paper is well-written, mathematically sound, and conceptually thorough.

The experiments demonstrate the benefits of using a value-weighted objective, which is a significant contribution of this paper. Furthermore, this paper appears to be the first to outline a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm. The use of pre-trained visual features is also shown to facilitate generalization empirically. Overall, I recommend this paper, as it has the potential to benefit many researchers in the field of robotics.

However, in the context of this conference, I find the contribution specifically on the "representation" problem to be somewhat limited. While the paper demonstrates the usefulness of a pre-trained VGG representation, it does not explore learning this representation end-to-end. This is not to suggest that end-to-end learning is necessary, but rather that the paper focuses more on the control problem than on the representation learning aspect. Additionally, the policy representation is fixed, and the values are approximated in linear form using problem-specific features. This does not diminish the value of the paper, but rather makes it less aligned with what I consider to be the core focus of ICLR.
