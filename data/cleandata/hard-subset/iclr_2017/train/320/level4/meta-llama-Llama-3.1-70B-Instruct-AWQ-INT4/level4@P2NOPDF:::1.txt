The paper proposes a novel approach to learning visual servoing based on Q-iteration, with three main contributions: (1) a bilinear dynamics model for predicting next-frame features based on actions and current frames, (2) a servoing formulation using a Q-function that learns weights for different feature channels, and (3) an optimization method for minimizing the Bellman error to learn the Q-function.

The paper explores various methods for connecting actions and frame representations to predict next-frame features, ultimately advocating for a locally connected bilinear model that balances computational efficiency and expressive power. However, it would be beneficial to include more experimental results comparing the three approaches: fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected dynamics, as presented in Section 4.

One of the strengths of the paper is its proposal to weight different channels to capture the importance of objects in various channels, which is demonstrated to be more effective than treating errors across all channels equally. This is validated experimentally, where unweighted performance consistently suffers.

The paper also presents a solid optimization scheme for solving the Bellman error, a challenging problem in Q-learning approaches. By leveraging the observation that scaling Q-function parameters does not affect the chosen policy, the authors develop an elegant fitted Q-iteration (FQI) approach, differing from typical optimization schemes that fix the term (ct + γ minu Qt+1).

However, it would be interesting to see a comparison between FQI and an iterative approach that holds the second term in Equation 5 fixed.

While the experimental results are a good starting point, they are somewhat unsatisfying due to the small scale and toy simulations. Nevertheless, it is essential to acknowledge the lack of benchmarks in this domain. Additionally, as mentioned in the pre-review section, the idea of modifying the VGG network should be experimentally validated to determine whether the modified version outperforms the original.

Overall, the paper makes a solid contribution in terms of technical novelty and problem formulation. However, stronger experiments would help to bolster its claims and provide more convincing evidence for the proposed approach.
