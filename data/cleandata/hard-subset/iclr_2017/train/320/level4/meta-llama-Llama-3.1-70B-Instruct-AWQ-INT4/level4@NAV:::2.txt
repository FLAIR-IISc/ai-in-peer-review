This paper explores the advantages of visual servoing through a learned visual representation. The authors propose a novel approach by first learning an action-conditional bilinear model of visual features, derived from a pre-trained VGG network, to obtain a policy via linearized dynamics. Notably, they introduce a multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of the bilinear model. To address the limitation of the bilinear model's one-step prediction, the authors suggest a weighted objective that incorporates long-term policy values. They employ a fitted-value approach to tackle the evaluation problem. The paper is well-written, mathematically sound, and conceptually thorough. The experiments successfully demonstrate the benefits of using a value-weighted objective, representing a significant contribution. Additionally, the paper pioneers a trust-region fitted-q iteration algorithm. The use of pre-trained visual features is empirically shown to enhance generalization. Overall, I recommend this paper for its potential to benefit the robotics research community. However, in the context of this conference, I find the contribution to the "representation" problem to be somewhat limited in scope. While it demonstrates the utility of a pre-trained VGG representation, it does not explore end-to-end learning. This is not to imply that end-to-end learning is necessary, but rather that the paper focuses more on the control problem than representation learning. Furthermore, the policy representation is fixed, and values are approximated linearly using problem-specific features. Although this does not diminish the paper's value, it may not align perfectly with the conference's focus.
