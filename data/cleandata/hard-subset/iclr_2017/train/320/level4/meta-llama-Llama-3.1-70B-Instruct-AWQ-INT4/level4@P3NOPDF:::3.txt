**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing, specifically target following, by leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on general image classification tasks. The authors combine bilinear models of one-step dynamics of visual feature maps at multiple scales with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy. This policy is learned by minimizing a regularized weighted average of distances to features predicted by the aforementioned model of visual dynamics.

**Contributions**

The paper makes several significant contributions to the field of visual servoing:

1. **Controlled experiments in simulation**: The authors provide quantitative results demonstrating the effectiveness of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing in a simulated environment.
2. **Clear performance benefits**: The proposed approach outperforms several sensible baselines, including those using ground truth bounding boxes, in the simulated experiments.
3. **Principled learning of multi-scale visual feature weights**: The authors employ an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm to handle the problem of distractors and learn the weights of visual features at multiple scales.
4. **Good sample efficiency**: The choice of Q-function approximator and the model-based one-step visual feature dynamics enable the approach to learn efficiently from a limited number of samples.
5. **Open-source virtual city environment**: The authors provide a benchmark environment for visual servoing, which can be used to evaluate and compare different approaches.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

To further strengthen the paper, the authors may consider the following suggestions:

1. **More complex benchmark**: While the simulated environment is a significant improvement over toy synthetic environments, it would benefit from more complex visual conditions, such as clutter, distractors, and varied object appearances. Incorporating more realistic and diverse 3D car models and increasing the number of distractor cars would enhance the realism and diversity of the environment.
2. **End-to-end and representation learning**: Although the current approach shows significant improvements, it would be interesting to investigate the impact of end-to-end training, including fine-tuning the convolutional network. This would allow the authors to measure the benefit of deep representation learning for visual servoing, which is relevant to the ICLR community.
3. **Reproducibility**: While the formalism and algorithms are clearly explained, the paper could benefit from a more organized and simplified presentation of the implementation details. Providing an open-source implementation of the method would also enhance reproducibility.
4. **Typos**: There are a few typos in the paper, including "learning is a relative[ly] recent addition" (p. 2) and "be applied [to] directly learn" (p. 2).

**Conclusion**

In spite of the limitations of the experiments, this paper is well-written and solid, thanks in part to the authors' excellent response to the pre-review questions and the subsequent improved revision. The authors have demonstrated their capability to address the suggested improvements, which could lead to an even stronger paper. With some revisions to address the mentioned limitations, this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of visual servoing.
