This paper investigates the benefits of visual servoing using a learned visual representation, proposing a novel approach that combines a learned action-conditional bilinear model with a linearized dynamics policy. The authors utilize a pre-trained VGG network to obtain visual features, which are then used to derive a policy. A key contribution of the paper is the introduction of a multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of the bilinear model.

To address the limitation of the bilinear model in predicting dynamics only one step ahead, the authors propose a weighted objective that incorporates long-term values of the current policy. The evaluation problem is tackled using a fitted-value approach, which is an important contribution of the paper. Additionally, the paper outlines a trust-region fitted-q iteration algorithm, a novel method that has not been previously explored.

The experiments demonstrate the benefits of using a value-weighted objective, showcasing improved performance and generalization. The use of pre-trained visual features is also shown to be empirically beneficial for generalization. Overall, the paper is well-written, mathematically solid, and conceptually exhaustive, making it a valuable contribution to the field of robotics.

I recommend this paper for its significant contributions to the field, particularly in the area of visual servoing and control. However, in the context of ICLR, I find the contribution specifically on the "representation" problem to be limited. While the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of using a pre-trained VGG representation, it does not explore learning the representation end-to-end. This is not to say that end-to-end learning is necessary, but rather that the paper focuses more on the control problem than the representation learning aspect.

Furthermore, the policy representation is fixed, and the values are approximated in linear form using problem-specific features. While this does not diminish the value of the paper, it may make it less aligned with the focus of ICLR, which typically emphasizes representation learning and end-to-end approaches. Nevertheless, the paper's contributions to the field of robotics and control make it a worthwhile read, and its findings are likely to benefit many researchers in the field.
