This paper investigates the benefits of visual servoing using a learned visual representation. The authors propose to first learn an action-conditional bilinear model of the visual features (obtained from a pre-trained VGG net) from which a policy can be derived using a linearization of the dynamics. A multi-scale, multi-channel and locally-connected variant of the bilinear model is presented. Since the bilinear model only predicts the dynamics one step ahead, the paper proposes a weighted objective which incorporates the long-term values of the current policy. The evaluation problem is addressed using a fitted-value approach.

I recommend accepting this paper. The paper is well written, mathematically solid, and conceptually exhaustive. The experiments also demonstrate the benefits of using a value-weighted objective and is an important contribution of this paper. This paper also seems to be the first to outline a trust-region fitted-q iteration algorithm. The use of pre-trained visual features is also shown to help, empirically, for generalization.

My main reason for recommending acceptance is that the paper presents a comprehensive approach to visual servoing, combining learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach in a complex synthetic car following benchmark, showing substantial improvement over conventional approaches and standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

One potential limitation of the paper is that it focuses more on the control problem than the representation learning problem. The policy representation is fixed and the values are approximated in linear form using problem-specific features. However, this does not detract from the overall contribution of the paper, which is to demonstrate the benefits of using learned visual features and predictive dynamics models for visual servoing.

To further improve the paper, I would suggest the authors consider the following points:

* Provide more discussion on the choice of pre-trained visual features and how they relate to the specific task of visual servoing.
* Consider learning the visual representation end-to-end, rather than relying on pre-trained features.
* Investigate the use of more advanced policy representations, such as neural networks, to improve the flexibility and expressiveness of the policy.
* Provide more details on the computational efficiency of the proposed approach, including the time complexity of the algorithms and the computational resources required.

Overall, I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of robotics and visual servoing, and I recommend its acceptance.
