The paper proposes a novel approach to learning visual servoing using Q-iteration, with three main contributions: 
1. A bilinear dynamics model for predicting next-frame features based on actions and current frames.
2. A servoing formulation utilizing a Q-function that learns weights for different feature channels.
3. An elegant method for optimizing the Bellman error to learn the Q-function.

The paper's strengths include its exploration of various methods for connecting actions (ut) and frame representations (yt) to predict next-frame features (y_{t+1}). The authors argue convincingly in favor of a locally connected bilinear model, which strikes a balance between computational efficiency and expressive ability. 

However, Section 4 could be improved by including more experimental results comparing the three approaches: fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected dynamics.

One of the paper's key strengths is its idea of weighting different channels to capture the importance of objects in different channels, which appears more effective than treating errors across all channels equally. This is validated experimentally, as the unweighted performance suffers consistently.

The paper also presents a solid optimization scheme for solving the Bellman error, a challenging problem in Q-learning approaches. The key observation that scaling Q-function parameters does not affect the chosen policy enables a more elegant fitted Q-iteration (FQI) approach, differing from typical optimization schemes that fix (ct + γ minu Qt+1).

However, a comparison between FQI and an iterative approach that holds the second term in Eq. 5 fixed would be beneficial.

The experimental results are somewhat unsatisfying due to their small scale and toy simulations. Nevertheless, it is essential to acknowledge the lack of benchmarks in this domain. Furthermore, as pointed out in the pre-review section, the idea of modifying the VGG network needs to be experimentally validated, as it is unclear whether the modified VGG would outperform the original version.

In summary, the paper's contribution is solid in terms of technical novelty and problem formulation. Nevertheless, the paper could benefit from stronger experimental results to support its claims, as suggested above.
