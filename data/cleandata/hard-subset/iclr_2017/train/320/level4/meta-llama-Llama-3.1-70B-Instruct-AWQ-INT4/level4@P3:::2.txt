This paper investigates the benefits of visual servoing using a learned visual representation. The authors propose to first learn an action-conditional bilinear model of the visual features (obtained from a pre-trained VGG net) from which a policy can be derived using a linearization of the dynamics. A multi-scale, multi-channel and locally-connected variant of the bilinear model is presented. Since the bilinear model only predicts the dynamics one step ahead, the paper proposes a weighted objective which incorporates the long-term values of the current policy. The evaluation problem is addressed using a fitted-value approach.

The paper is well written, mathematically solid, and conceptually exhaustive. The experiments also demonstrate the benefits of using a value-weighted objective and is an important contribution of this paper. This paper also seems to be the first to outline a trust-region fitted-q iteration algorithm. The use of pre-trained visual features is also shown to help, empirically, for generalization.

However, I have some reservations about the paper's alignment with the conference's focus on representation learning. While the paper shows that a pre-trained VGG representation is useful, it does not consider learning it end-to-end. Additionally, the policy representation is fixed and the values are approximated in linear form using problem-specific features. This doesn't make the paper less valuable, but perhaps less aligned with what I think ICLR should be about.

I recommend this paper for acceptance, but I would like the authors to address the following questions:

1. How does the choice of pre-trained features affect the performance of the visual servoing algorithm? Would learning the features end-to-end improve the results?
2. Can the authors provide more insights into the design choices of the bilinear model and the weighted objective? How do these choices affect the performance of the algorithm?
3. How does the proposed algorithm compare to other state-of-the-art visual servoing methods? Are there any specific scenarios where the proposed algorithm outperforms others?

Overall, the paper is well-written and makes a significant contribution to the field of visual servoing. With some additional clarification and insights, it has the potential to be a valuable addition to the ICLR conference.
