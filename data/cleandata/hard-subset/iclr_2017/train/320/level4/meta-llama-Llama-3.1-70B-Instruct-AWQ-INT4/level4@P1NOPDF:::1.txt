The paper proposes a novel approach to learning visual servoing based on Q-iteration. The main contributions of the paper are threefold: 

1. A bilinear dynamics model for predicting next-frame features based on action and current frame.
2. A formulation of servoing with a Q-function that learns weights for different feature channels.
3. An elegant method for optimizing the Bellman error to learn the Q-function.

The paper has several strengths. One notable aspect is its exploration of different methods for connecting the action (ut) and frame representation (yt) to predict next-frame features (y_{t+1}). The authors argue in favor of a locally connected bilinear model, which strikes a balance between computation and expressive ability.

However, the paper would benefit from more experimental results comparing the three approaches: fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected dynamics. While Section 4 presents good arguments for these choices, experimental validation is necessary to support these claims.

One of the paper's key strengths is its idea of weighting different channels to capture the importance of objects in different channels. This approach is more effective than treating errors across all channels equally, as demonstrated experimentally where unweighted performance suffers consistently.

The paper also presents a solid optimization scheme for solving the Bellman error, a challenging problem in Q-learning approaches. The authors leverage the key observation that scaling Q-function parameters does not affect the best policy chosen, enabling a more elegant Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) approach. This contrasts with typical optimization schemes, which often fix the term (ct + \gamma minu Q_{t+1}).

However, it would be beneficial to see a comparison between FQI and an iterative approach that holds the second term in Equation 5 fixed.

The experimental results are somewhat unsatisfying due to the small scale and toy simulations. However, it is essential to recognize the lack of benchmarks in this domain.

Additionally, as pointed out in the pre-review section, the idea of modifying the VGG network needs to be experimentally validated. In its current form, it is unclear whether the modified VGG would perform better than the original version.

Overall, the paper's contribution is solid in terms of technical novelty and problem formulation. However, stronger experiments are necessary to bolster its claims and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
