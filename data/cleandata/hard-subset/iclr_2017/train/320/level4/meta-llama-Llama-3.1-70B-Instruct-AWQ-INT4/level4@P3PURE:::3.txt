1) Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing, specifically target following, by leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on general image classification tasks. The authors combine bilinear models of one-step dynamics of visual feature maps at multiple scales with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy. This policy is learned by minimizing a regularized weighted average of distances to features predicted by the aforementioned model of visual dynamics.

2) Contributions
+ The paper presents controlled experiments in simulation, quantifying the usefulness of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing.
+ The authors demonstrate clear performance benefits with respect to many sensible baselines, including those using ground truth bounding boxes.
+ The proposed method enables principled learning of multi-scale visual feature weights using an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm, effectively handling the problem of distractors.
+ The approach exhibits good sample efficiency, thanks to the choice of Q-function approximator and the model-based one-step visual feature dynamics.
+ The authors provide an open-source virtual city environment to benchmark visual servoing.

3) Suggestions for improvement
- To further enhance the paper, the authors could consider using more complex benchmark environments. While the current environment is not trivial, the experiments would benefit from more complex visual conditions, such as clutter, distractors, appearance and motion variety, environment richness, and diversity. For instance, incorporating a larger number of 3D car models, including more realistic and diverse ones from sources like Google SketchUp, and populating the environment with more distractor cars (in traffic or parked) would improve the realism and diversity of object appearances. This is essential, as the primary desired quality of the approach is robustness to visual variations.
- Investigating the impact of end-to-end training, including fine-tuning the convolutional network, would be valuable. This could provide insights into the benefits of deep representation learning for visual servoing, which is relevant to ICLR. Although the method can be adapted for end-to-end training, the current approach does not involve representation learning.
- To improve reproducibility, the authors could reorganize the exposition of practical tricks and implementation details, which are currently described with varying levels of detail throughout the paper and appendix. A more effective approach would be to summarize the most important details in the main text and provide a link to an open-source implementation of the method for completeness.
- The paper contains a few typos, including "learning is a relative[ly] recent addition" (p. 2) and "be applied [to] directly learn" (p. 2).

4) Conclusion
Despite the limitations of the experiments, this paper is interesting and solid, in part due to the authors' excellent response to pre-review questions and the subsequent improved revision. This suggests that the authors are capable of addressing the suggested improvements, which would lead to an even stronger paper.
