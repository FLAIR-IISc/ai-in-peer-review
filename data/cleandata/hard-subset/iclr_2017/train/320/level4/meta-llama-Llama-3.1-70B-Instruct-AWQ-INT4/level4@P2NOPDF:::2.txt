This paper explores the benefits of visual servoing using a learned visual representation. The authors propose a novel approach, where they first learn an action-conditional bilinear model of visual features extracted from a pre-trained VGG network. From this model, a policy can be derived using a linearization of the dynamics. Notably, the authors present a multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of the bilinear model.

To address the limitation of the bilinear model in predicting dynamics only one step ahead, the paper introduces a weighted objective that incorporates long-term values of the current policy. The evaluation problem is tackled using a fitted-value approach. The paper is well-written, mathematically solid, and conceptually comprehensive.

The experiments demonstrate the benefits of using a value-weighted objective, which is a significant contribution of this paper. Additionally, the paper appears to be the first to outline a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm. The use of pre-trained visual features is also shown to be beneficial for generalization, empirically.

Overall, I strongly recommend this paper, as it has the potential to benefit many researchers in the field of robotics. However, in the context of this conference, I find the contribution specifically on the "representation" problem to be somewhat limited. While the paper shows that a pre-trained VGG representation is useful, it does not explore learning the representation end-to-end. This is not to say that end-to-end learning is necessary, but rather that the paper focuses more on the control problem than the representation learning aspect.

Furthermore, the policy representation is fixed, and the values are approximated in linear form using problem-specific features. While this does not diminish the value of the paper, it may make it less aligned with the focus of ICLR, which tends to emphasize representation learning and end-to-end approaches.
