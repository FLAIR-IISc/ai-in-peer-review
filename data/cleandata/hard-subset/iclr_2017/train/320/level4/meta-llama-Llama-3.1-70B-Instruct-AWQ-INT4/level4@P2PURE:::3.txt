1) Summary
This paper addresses the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on general image classification tasks. The authors combine bilinear models of one-step dynamics of visual feature maps at multiple scales with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy, which is obtained by minimizing a regularized weighted average of distances to features predicted by the aforementioned model of visual dynamics.

2) Contributions
+ The paper presents controlled experiments in simulation that quantify the effectiveness of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing.
+ The results demonstrate clear performance benefits compared to several sensible baselines, including those using ground truth bounding boxes.
+ The authors propose a principled approach to learning multi-scale visual feature weights using an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm, which effectively handles the problem of distractors.
+ The method achieves good sample efficiency, thanks to the choice of Q-function approximator and the model-based one-step visual feature dynamics.
+ The open-source virtual city environment provides a valuable benchmark for visual servoing.

3) Suggestions for improvement
- More complex benchmark environments:
Although the current environment is more realistic than a toy synthetic one, the experiments would greatly benefit from more complex visual conditions, such as clutter, distractors, and increased appearance and motion variety. To improve realism and diversity, the authors could consider using a larger number of 3D car models, including more realistic and diverse ones obtained from Google SketchUp, and populating the environment with more distractor cars. This is crucial, as the primary desired quality of the approach is robustness to visual variations.
- End-to-end and representation learning:
While the current results are significant, it would be interesting to investigate the impact of end-to-end training (i.e., fine-tuning the convolutional network) on performance. This would allow the authors to measure the benefits of deep representation learning for visual servoing, which is relevant to ICLR. Although the method can be adapted for representation learning, it is not currently explored in the paper.
- Reproducibility:
Although the formalism and algorithms are clearly explained, the paper contains a large number of practical tricks and implementation details described with varying levels of detail throughout the text and appendix. To improve reproducibility, the authors could consider grouping, simplifying, or reorganizing the exposition of these implementation details. Alternatively, they could summarize the most important ones in the main text and provide a link to an open-source implementation of the method for completeness.
- Typos:
p.2: "learning is a relative[ly] recent addition"
p.2: "be applied [to] directly learn"

4) Conclusion
Despite the limitations of the experiments, this paper is interesting and solid, partly due to the authors' excellent response to pre-review questions and the subsequent improved revision. This suggests that the authors are capable of addressing the suggested improvements, leading to an even stronger paper.
