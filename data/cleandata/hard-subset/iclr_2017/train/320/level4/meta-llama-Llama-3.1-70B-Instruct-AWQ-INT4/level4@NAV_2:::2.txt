This paper investigates the benefits of visual servoing using a learned visual representation. The authors propose to first learn an action-conditional bilinear model of the visual features, obtained from a pre-trained VGG net, from which a policy can be derived using a linearization of the dynamics. A multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of the bilinear model is presented. Since the bilinear model only predicts the dynamics one step ahead, the paper proposes a weighted objective that incorporates the long-term values of the current policy. The evaluation problem is addressed using a fitted-value approach.

The paper is well-written, mathematically solid, and conceptually exhaustive. The experiments also demonstrate the benefits of using a value-weighted objective and are an important contribution of this paper. This paper also seems to be the first to outline a trust-region fitted-q iteration algorithm. The use of pre-trained visual features is also shown to help, empirically, for generalization.

Overall, I recommend this paper as it would benefit many researchers in robotics. However, in the context of this conference, I find the contribution specifically on the "representation" problem to be limited. It shows that a pre-trained VGG representation is useful, but does not consider learning it end-to-end. This is not to say that it should be end-to-end, but proportionally speaking, the paper spends more time on the control problem than the representation learning one. Also, the policy representation is fixed, and the values are approximated in linear form using problem-specific features. This doesn't make the paper less valuable, but perhaps less aligned with what I think ICLR should be about.

Accept, but I have some concerns regarding the representation learning aspect of the paper.

The paper's main contribution is the development of a visual servoing method that uses learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a complex synthetic car following benchmark, achieving substantial improvement over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints. The use of pre-trained visual features, in this case, the VGG network trained for ImageNet classification, is shown to be beneficial for generalization.

However, I would like the authors to address the following points:

1. The authors mention that they use a pre-trained VGG network, but they do not discuss the possibility of learning the representation end-to-end. Could they provide some insights into why they chose not to do so?

2. The policy representation is fixed, and the values are approximated in linear form using problem-specific features. Could the authors comment on the limitations of this approach and potential future work in this direction?

3. The authors demonstrate the benefits of using a value-weighted objective, but they do not provide a detailed analysis of the trade-offs between the different components of the objective function. Could they provide some additional insights into this aspect?

4. The authors mention that they use a fitted-value approach to address the evaluation problem. Could they provide some additional details on this aspect, such as the choice of the value function approximator and the optimization procedure?

5. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a synthetic car following benchmark. Could they comment on the potential applicability of their method to real-world scenarios and the challenges that they might face in such cases?

6. The authors mention that they use a pre-trained VGG network, but they do not discuss the computational efficiency of their approach. Could they provide some insights into the computational requirements of their method and potential ways to improve its efficiency?

Overall, I believe that this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of robotics, but I would like the authors to address the above points to strengthen their work.
