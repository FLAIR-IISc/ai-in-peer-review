This paper explores the benefits of visual servoing using a learned visual representation. The authors propose learning an action-conditional bilinear model of visual features, obtained from a pre-trained VGG network, from which a policy can be derived using a linearization of the dynamics. A multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of the bilinear model is presented. Since the bilinear model only predicts dynamics one step ahead, the paper proposes a weighted objective that incorporates the long-term values of the current policy. The evaluation problem is addressed using a fitted-value approach.

The paper is well-written, mathematically solid, and conceptually exhaustive. The experiments demonstrate the benefits of using a value-weighted objective, which is an important contribution of this paper. Additionally, this paper appears to be the first to outline a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm. The use of pre-trained visual features is also shown to empirically aid generalization. Overall, I recommend this paper, as it would benefit many researchers in robotics.

However, in the context of this conference, I find the contribution specifically on the "representation" problem to be limited. The paper shows that a pre-trained VGG representation is useful but does not consider learning it end-to-end. While this is not necessarily a requirement, the paper devotes more time to the control problem than the representation learning aspect. Furthermore, the policy representation is fixed, and the values are approximated in linear form using problem-specific features. This does not diminish the value of the paper, but it may make it less aligned with what I expect from ICLR, which tends to focus on representation learning and end-to-end learning approaches.
