The paper proposes a novel approach for learning visual servoing based on Q-iteration. The main contributions of the paper are: (1) a bilinear dynamics model for predicting next frame features based on action and current frame, (2) formulation of servoing with a Q-function that learns weights for different feature channels, and (3) an elegant method for optimizing the Bellman error to learn the Q-function.

The paper does a good job of exploring different ways to connect the action and frame representation to predict next frame features. The authors argue in favor of a locally connected bilinear model, which strikes a balance between computation and expressive ability. However, I would have liked to see more experimental results comparing the three approaches: fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected dynamics.

The idea of weighting different channels to capture the importance of objects in different channels seems more effective than treating errors across all channels equally. This is also validated experimentally, where unweighted performance suffers consistently.

The authors present a solid optimization scheme for solving the Bellman error, based on the key observation that scaling Q-function parameters does not affect the best policy chosen. This enables a more elegant fitted Q-iteration (FQI) approach, as opposed to typical optimization schemes that hold the second term fixed.

However, I would have liked to see a comparison between FQI and such an iterative approach. Additionally, the experimental results are somewhat unsatisfying due to the small scale and toy simulations. While I recognize the lack of benchmarks in this domain, I believe that stronger experiments would bolster the paper's claims.

Furthermore, the idea of modifying the VGG network needs to be experimentally validated. In its current form, it is not clear whether the modified VGG would perform better than the original version.

Overall, the contribution of the paper is solid in terms of technical novelty and problem formulations. However, I recommend that the authors strengthen their experiments to support their claims.

Decision: Accept with minor revisions.

Reasons for decision: The paper presents a novel approach to learning visual servoing, and the authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of their work. While the experimental results are somewhat limited, the paper's technical contributions and potential impact on the field outweigh its limitations.

Additional feedback:

* Provide more experimental results comparing the three approaches to dynamics modeling.
* Validate the performance of the modified VGG network experimentally.
* Consider adding more details about the optimization scheme and the FQI algorithm.
* Provide more context about the limitations of the current approach and potential avenues for future work.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details about the choice of hyperparameters for the FQI algorithm?
* How did you select the number of iterations for the FQI algorithm?
* Can you comment on the potential limitations of using a bilinear dynamics model, and how it might be improved or extended in future work?
