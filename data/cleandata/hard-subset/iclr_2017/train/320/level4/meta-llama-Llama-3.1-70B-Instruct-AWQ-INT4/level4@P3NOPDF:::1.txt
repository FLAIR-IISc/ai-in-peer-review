The paper proposes a novel approach to learning visual servoing based on Q-iteration, with three primary contributions. Firstly, it introduces a bilinear dynamics model to predict the next frame's features based on the action and current frame. Secondly, it formulates servoing with a Q-function that learns weights for different feature channels. Lastly, it presents an elegant method for optimizing the Bellman error to learn the Q-function.

The paper has several strengths. It thoroughly explores various methods to connect the action (ut) and frame representation (yt) to predict the next frame's features (y_{t+1}). The authors argue in favor of a locally connected bilinear model, which strikes a balance between computation and expressive ability. Furthermore, the idea of weighting different channels to capture the importance of objects in different channels appears more effective than treating errors across all channels equally. This is validated experimentally, where unweighted performance suffers consistently. Additionally, the paper presents a solid optimization scheme based on the key observation that scaling Q-function parameters does not affect the best policy chosen, enabling a more elegant Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) approach.

However, there are some areas that require improvement. While Section 4 provides good arguments for different choices, it would be beneficial to include more experimental results comparing the three approaches: fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected dynamics. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method's strengths and weaknesses. Moreover, it would be interesting to see a comparison between FQI and an iterative approach that holds the second term in Equation 5 fixed.

The experimental results presented in the paper are somewhat unsatisfying due to the small scale and toy simulations. However, it is essential to recognize the lack of benchmarks in this domain. The authors should consider experimenting with more complex scenarios to demonstrate the robustness of their approach. Additionally, as pointed out in the pre-review section, the idea of modifying the VGG network needs to be experimentally validated. In its current form, it is unclear whether the modified VGG would perform better than the original version.

In conclusion, the paper makes a solid contribution in terms of technical novelty and problem formulations. However, the experimental results could be strengthened to support the claims made. With more comprehensive experiments and comparisons, the paper could provide a more convincing argument for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
