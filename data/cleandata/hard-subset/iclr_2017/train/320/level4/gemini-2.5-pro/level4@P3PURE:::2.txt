This paper investigates visual servoing using a learned visual representation. The authors propose a method that first learns an action-conditional bilinear model of visual features extracted from a pre-trained VGG network. A policy is then derived from a linearization of these learned dynamics. The paper also presents a multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of this bilinear model. To address the short-sightedness of the one-step dynamics model, the authors introduce a weighted objective that incorporates the long-term values of the current policy, with policy evaluation handled by a fitted-value approach.

The paper is well-written, mathematically solid, and conceptually exhaustive. A key contribution is the experimental demonstration of the benefits of using a value-weighted objective. The paper also appears to be the first to outline a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm. Furthermore, the use of pre-trained visual features is empirically shown to improve generalization.

Overall, I recommend this paper for acceptance, as it will likely benefit many researchers in robotics. However, in the context of this conference, I find the contribution specifically to the "representation" problem to be limited. The work shows that a pre-trained VGG representation is useful but does not consider learning it end-to-end. While an end-to-end approach is not a requirement, the paper's focus is disproportionately on the control problem rather than on representation learning. Additionally, the policy representation is fixed, and the value function is approximated linearly using problem-specific features. These factors do not diminish the paper's value, but they make it less aligned with the central themes of ICLR.
