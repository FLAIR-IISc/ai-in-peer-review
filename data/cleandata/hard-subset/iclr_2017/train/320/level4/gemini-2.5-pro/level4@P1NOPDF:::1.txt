This paper proposes a novel approach for learning visual servoing using a Q-iteration-based method. The main contributions of the paper are:
1.  A bilinear dynamics model that predicts the next frame's features based on the current frame and a chosen action.
2.  A Q-function formulation for visual servoing that learns to weight the importance of different feature channels.
3.  An elegant optimization method for the Bellman error, used to learn the Q-function.

**Strengths:**
+ The paper provides a thorough exploration of different ways to model the interaction between the action (u_t) and the current frame representation (y_t) to predict subsequent features (y_{t+1}). The authors effectively justify their choice of a locally connected bilinear model, which strikes a good balance between computational cost and expressive power.
+ The idea of weighting different feature channels to capture the importance of objects is a promising alternative to treating errors across all channels equally. This is validated by the experiments, which show that the unweighted baseline consistently underperforms.
+ Minimizing the Bellman error is a known challenge in Q-learning. The paper presents a solid optimization scheme based on the key observation that the optimal policy is invariant to the scaling of the Q-function's parameters. This insight allows for a more elegant Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) update compared to typical approaches that require holding the target Q-value term, (c_t + γ min_u Q_{t+1}), fixed during optimization.

**Weaknesses:**
- While Section 4 presents a compelling rationale for the chosen dynamics model, the paper would be strengthened by a direct experimental comparison of the three approaches discussed: fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected.
- It would also be beneficial to see a comparison between the proposed optimization method and a more standard iterative approach that holds the target Q-value term (the second term in Eq. 5) fixed.

**Experimental Results:**
- Overall, the experimental results are somewhat limited due to their small scale and reliance on simulated, toy environments. However, I acknowledge the current lack of standard benchmarks in this domain.
- The suggestion to modify the VGG network requires experimental validation. In its current form, it is unclear whether the proposed modifications offer a tangible benefit over the standard VGG architecture.

**Overall:**
The paper's contributions are solid in terms of technical novelty and problem formulation. However, the claims would be significantly bolstered by stronger and more comprehensive experiments, as suggested above.
