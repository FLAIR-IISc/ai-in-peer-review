This paper proposes a novel approach for learning visual servoing based on Q-iteration. The main contributions include: 1) a bilinear dynamics model for predicting subsequent image features from the current features and action; 2) a Q-function formulation for servoing that learns to weight different feature channels; and 3) an elegant optimization method for the Bellman error.

**Pros:**
*   The paper provides a compelling analysis of different methods for modeling the system dynamics, i.e., predicting the next frame's features (y_{t+1}) based on the current action (u_t) and frame representation (y_t). The authors make a strong case for a locally connected bilinear model, arguing that it strikes an effective balance between computational efficiency and expressive power.

**Cons:**
*   While the arguments in Section 4 are well-reasoned, the paper would be strengthened by an experimental comparison of the three proposed dynamics models (fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected) to empirically validate the choice of the locally connected variant.

**Pros:**
*   The proposed method of learning weights for different feature channels is well-motivated. This allows the model to capture the relative importance of different features, which is a more effective approach than treating errors across all channels equally. This claim is convincingly validated by experiments showing that the unweighted baseline consistently underperforms.
*   The paper presents a solid optimization scheme for the Bellman error, a common challenge in Q-learning. The method is based on the key insight that the optimal policy is invariant to the scaling of the Q-function's parameters. This insight enables a more elegant Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) solution compared to typical schemes that require fixing the target Q-value term (c_t + γ min_u Q_{t+1}) during optimization.

**Cons:**
*   The paper lacks an experimental comparison between the proposed FQI method and a more standard iterative approach where the target Q-value is held fixed. Such a comparison would help quantify the practical benefits of the proposed optimization.

**Experimental Results:**
*   The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, relying on small-scale and simulated toy problems. While the lack of established benchmarks in this domain is a mitigating factor, the results are not fully convincing.
*   Furthermore, the proposal to modify the VGG network requires direct experimental validation. As it stands, it is unclear whether the proposed modifications offer any performance benefit over the original VGG architecture.

**Overall:**
In summary, the paper's primary contributions are solid in terms of technical novelty and problem formulation. However, the claims would be significantly bolstered by a more thorough experimental evaluation that addresses the points raised above.
