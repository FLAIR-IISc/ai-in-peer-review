This paper investigates the benefits of visual servoing using a learned visual representation. The authors propose a method that first learns an action-conditional bilinear model to predict the dynamics of visual features, which are extracted from a pre-trained VGG network. A policy is then derived by linearizing these learned dynamics. The paper also presents a multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of this bilinear model.

Since the bilinear model is myopic (predicting only one step ahead), the authors introduce a weighted objective that incorporates the long-term value of the current policy. To address the policy evaluation problem, they employ a fitted-value approach.

The paper is well-written, mathematically solid, and conceptually thorough. The experiments effectively demonstrate the benefits of the proposed value-weighted objective, which stands as an important contribution of this work. The paper also appears to be the first to present a trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm. Empirically, the use of pre-trained visual features is shown to improve generalization.

Overall, I recommend this paper for acceptance. I believe its contributions will be of significant interest to many researchers, particularly in the robotics community.

However, I have some reservations about the paper's alignment with the ICLR conference. Specifically, I find the contribution to the core problem of "representation learning" to be limited. The paper shows that a pre-trained VGG representation is useful but does not consider learning the visual features end-to-end as part of the control loop. This is not a criticism of the method itself, but the paper's focus is placed much more heavily on the control problem than on the representation learning aspect. Furthermore, the policy representation is fixed, and the value function is approximated as a linear function of problem-specific features.

While these design choices do not diminish the paper's value, they make it feel less aligned with the central themes of ICLR, which typically emphasizes learning representations directly from data.
