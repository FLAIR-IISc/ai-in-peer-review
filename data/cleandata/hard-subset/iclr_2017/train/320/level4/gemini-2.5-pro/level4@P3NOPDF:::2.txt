This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing by learning a predictive model of visual dynamics. The authors propose an action-conditional bilinear model to predict the one-step evolution of visual features extracted from a pre-trained VGG network. This model is enhanced with a multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected architecture. A policy is then derived by linearizing these learned dynamics. To account for the single-step nature of the predictive model, the authors introduce a value-weighted objective that incorporates long-term rewards, with policy evaluation handled by a fitted-value iteration method.

The paper is well-written, mathematically sound, and provides a conceptually thorough treatment of the problem. Its strengths are numerous:

1.  A primary contribution is the value-weighted objective, which effectively bridges the gap between single-step model predictions and long-horizon control. The experiments provide clear and convincing evidence of its benefits over a standard one-step prediction loss. This is an important insight for model-based reinforcement learning.

2.  The formulation of a trust-region fitted-Q iteration algorithm is another significant contribution, potentially of interest to the broader reinforcement learning community. The paper appears to be the first to outline such an algorithm.

3.  The empirical results convincingly validate the authors' choice to use pre-trained visual features, demonstrating strong generalization capabilities across different tasks and environments.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a solid methodology and impressive results that will undoubtedly be beneficial to many researchers in robotics and control.

However, my main reservation concerns the paper's alignment with the primary focus of ICLR, specifically on the theme of representation learning. While the work is excellent, its core innovations lie more in the domain of control and model-based planning than in the learning of representations themselves.

The "representation" aspect of the paper is limited to learning a dynamics model *over* a fixed, pre-existing representation from a VGG network. The work capably shows that this pre-trained representation is useful, but it does not consider learning the visual features end-to-end for the control task. This is not to say that an end-to-end approach is required, but it means that, proportionally, the paper dedicates significantly more effort to solving the control problem than to advancing representation learning.

Furthermore, the policy representation is fixed, and the value function is approximated linearly using problem-specific features. These design choices, while pragmatic and effective, shift the paper's focus further from deep representation learning and more towards classical model-based reinforcement learning and optimal control techniques.

In summary, this is a strong paper presenting valuable contributions that advance the state-of-the-art in model-based visual servoing. My critique is not about the quality of the research, which is high, but rather its thematic fit for this specific conference. The contributions are more heavily weighted towards control theory and RL algorithm design than towards the learning of representations. Despite this reservation, the quality of the work is undeniable, and it demonstrates an effective and novel application of learned models in a complex domain. I therefore recommend acceptance, as the techniques will be of interest to many at the intersection of vision, reinforcement learning, and robotics.
