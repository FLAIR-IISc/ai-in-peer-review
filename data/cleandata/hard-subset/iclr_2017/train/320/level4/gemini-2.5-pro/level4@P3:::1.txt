### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel and sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing controllers. The core problem it addresses is how to control a robot to follow a target using visual input, without relying on manually engineered features or dynamics models. The proposed solution elegantly combines three key ideas: (1) leveraging robust visual representations from a pre-trained convolutional neural network (VGG) to handle visual variations; (2) learning a predictive, multiscale bilinear dynamics model that predicts how these features will change in response to robot actions; and (3) using a reinforcement learning algorithm based on fitted Q-iteration (FQI) to learn a task-specific weighting over the feature channels. This weighting allows the system to focus on the most relevant visual cues for the task at hand. The primary contributions include the specific formulation of a Q-function whose greedy policy corresponds to the servoing objective, and a clever optimization scheme for the Bellman error that improves the stability and efficiency of the FQI procedure. The authors claim their method can learn an effective servoing policy from just 20 training trajectories, demonstrating a significant improvement in sample efficiency over standard model-free deep RL approaches.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Weak Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel Framework and Method:** The paper introduces a compelling framework that thoughtfully integrates pre-trained features, a learned dynamics model, and a highly sample-efficient FQI algorithm. The formulation of the Q-function to learn feature weights and the accompanying optimization procedure (Algorithm 1) are technically sound and represent a novel contribution to learning-based control.
2.  **Weak Empirical Validation:** While the presented results are promising, the experiments are conducted within a single, simulated environment. The paper makes several well-motivated design choices (e.g., the locally connected bilinear model, modifications to the VGG architecture) that are not fully justified through ablation studies, making it difficult to assess the individual importance of these components.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The main strength of this paper lies in its well-grounded and elegant technical approach. The problem of visual servoing is traditionally plagued by the need for brittle, hand-designed features. This work provides a principled way to bypass this by using general-purpose deep features. The key challenge, which the authors identify correctly, is that not all features are equally important for a given task. The solution—to learn a weighting over feature channels via reinforcement learning—is insightful.

The proposed FQI algorithm is particularly noteworthy. Standard Q-learning methods often suffer from instability, partly due to optimizing the Bellman error where the target Q-value is itself changing. The authors' observation that the optimal policy is invariant to a positive scaling of the Q-function's parameters is a key insight. This allows them to introduce an intermediate optimization step (Equation 5) that jointly fits a scaling factor and bias for both the current and target Q-functions, which likely contributes to the method's stability and impressive sample efficiency. This is a more sophisticated approach than simply freezing a target network.

However, the experimental validation could be stronger. The claims of the paper would be more convincing with the following additions:
*   **Ablation Studies:** The paper argues for a locally connected bilinear model over a fully connected one for computational reasons, but an empirical comparison is missing. Similarly, the authors modify the VGG network to remove max-pooling layers in favor of dilated convolutions to preserve spatial information. This is a sensible choice, but its impact on performance is not experimentally verified against the original VGG architecture. Without these comparisons, it is unclear how critical these specific design choices are to the final performance.
*   **Scope of Experiments:** The evaluation is confined to a single, albeit complex, car-following simulation. While creating new robotics benchmarks is challenging, demonstrating the framework's effectiveness on a second, distinct task would significantly strengthen the claims of generality and robustness.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** The comparison to TRPO is very useful for demonstrating sample efficiency. However, the performance gap between the proposed method and the TRPO baselines is substantial, even after 50 sampling iterations for TRPO. While the paper highlights its sample efficiency, this might also suggest that the policy class or optimization for the TRPO baseline could be improved. The comparison to classical IBVS methods is valuable, but it is noted that these methods are provided with ground truth depth, which is a significant advantage not available to the learned method.

Despite these limitations in the experimental section, the technical novelty and the coherence of the proposed framework are strong enough to warrant acceptance. The paper presents a clear and promising direction for learning-based robotic control.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   **Dynamics Model Comparison:** In Section 4.2, you discuss different variants of the bilinear model. It would strengthen your claims to include an experimental comparison in the appendix between the fully connected and locally connected bilinear dynamics, even if only for the lower-dimensional pixel-based features. This would provide empirical data to support your design choice.
*   **VGG Architecture:** Could you provide an ablation that compares your modified VGG network with the original VGG network (using features from its convolutional layers before max-pooling)? This would help quantify the benefit of using dilated convolutions to preserve spatial resolution for this task.
*   **FQI Optimization:** Your FQI algorithm uses a joint optimization step for the scaling factor α and bias b (Equation 5), which is a clever departure from standard methods. Could you provide more insight or an empirical comparison showing how this step specifically contributes to the algorithm's stability and performance compared to a more traditional approach where the target Q-function parameters (θ(k-1)) are held fixed?
*   **Data Collection for Dynamics Model:** The dynamics model was trained on a dataset generated by a hand-coded policy. How sensitive is the performance of the learned dynamics model to the quality of this data collection policy? Would a model trained on purely random actions be sufficient, or is a semi-competent, task-relevant data collection process essential?
*   **Clarity on Cost Function:** The cost function in Equation 9 seems to heavily penalize leaving the task space (car out of FOV). Did you explore simpler cost functions, such as the L2 distance of the target from the image center, and how did they perform?
