### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel and sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing for target-following tasks. The authors address the limitations of traditional visual servoing, which often relies on hand-engineered features and dynamics models, by integrating three key machine learning components: 1) pre-trained deep convolutional features (from a modified VGG network) to provide a robust visual representation, 2) a learned, sample-efficient bilinear model to predict the dynamics of these features in response to robot actions, and 3) a reinforcement learning algorithm based on Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) to learn a task-specific policy. The core contribution lies in formulating the servoing problem such that the RL algorithm learns to weight the importance of different feature channels, effectively teaching the system to focus on the target object while ignoring distractors. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on a challenging simulated quadcopter-based car-following task, where it learns a robust policy from only 20 training trajectories, significantly outperforming classical methods and standard deep RL baselines in terms of sample efficiency.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Novel Technical Formulation:** The paper's primary strength is its clever combination of a simple, greedy servoing policy with a sophisticated reinforcement learning objective. Instead of using RL to learn a complex, end-to-end policy, the authors use a custom FQI algorithm to learn an optimal weighting over feature channels. This weighting is then used within an analytically solvable one-step lookahead controller. This is an elegant approach that effectively addresses the problem of focusing on relevant visual information.

2.  **Exceptional Sample Efficiency:** The claim of learning an effective visual servo from just 20 training trajectories is impressive and well-supported by the experiments. This represents a significant improvement (over two orders of magnitude) compared to model-free deep RL methods like TRPO. This efficiency makes the proposed method practical for robotics applications where data collection is expensive and rapid adaptation to new tasks or targets is desirable.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's contributions are well-motivated and technically sound. The decision to leverage pre-trained features from an ImageNet model is a well-established and sensible strategy for achieving good visual generalization without requiring massive amounts of task-specific data.

The design of the dynamics model and the Q-learning algorithm are the most compelling parts of the work.

*   **Bilinear Dynamics:** The choice of a bilinear model for feature dynamics strikes a good balance between expressiveness and efficiency. It is complex enough to capture the relationship between actions and visual feature changes but simple enough to be learned from a small dataset and to allow for an efficient, analytical solution to the control problem (Equation 3). The justification for a locally connected version to manage the parameter count is logical.

*   **FQI for Feature Weighting:** The formulation of the Q-function as a linear combination of weighted feature prediction errors is the paper's core insight. This allows the Bellman error to be optimized with respect to the feature weights directly. The proposed two-step optimization process for the Bellman error (Equations 5 and 6), which leverages the insight that the optimal action is invariant to a positive scaling of the Q-function parameters, is a clever and non-standard variant of FQI that appears to work well. The experimental results validate this approach, showing that the learned weighted policy significantly outperforms an unweighted version.

While the overall approach is strong, the experimental validation could be more thorough. The reported results convincingly demonstrate the method's superiority over important baselines (TRPO, classical IBVS). However, the paper makes several specific design choices that are well-motivated but not fully justified through ablation studies. For instance, the benefit of the modified VGG network, the locally connected bilinear model, and the unique FQI optimization scheme are not independently verified against their more standard counterparts. A more comprehensive experimental analysis would strengthen the paper's claims by pinpointing the source of the performance gains more precisely. Despite this, the primary claims of the paper—achieving highly sample-efficient visual servoing through a combination of learned dynamics and feature weighting—are well-supported by the existing results.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the paper and are not central to the decision to accept.

1.  **Ablation Studies:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by including ablation studies to justify several key design choices. These would provide deeper insight into why the proposed combination of components is effective.
2.  **Simulation vs. Reality:** The simulated environment appears reasonably complex, providing a strong proof of concept. A brief discussion in the conclusion about the potential challenges of transferring this method to a physical platform (e.g., sim-to-real gap in visual features, unmodeled dynamics) would be a valuable addition.
3.  **Clarity on Dynamics Models:** In Section 4.2, you mention three variants of the bilinear model (fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected). Providing an empirical comparison of these, even if brief and in the appendix, would solidify the choice of the locally connected model.

To clarify my understanding and help build confidence in the results, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  **Justification for Modified VGG:** Section 4.1 describes modifications to the VGG network to reduce spatial invariance by removing pooling layers. Have you performed a comparative experiment using features from the original, unmodified VGG network? This would directly validate the claim that reducing invariance is beneficial for this servoing task.
2.  **Comparison of FQI Variants:** Your FQI algorithm (Algorithm 1) uses a novel two-step update to solve the Bellman error. How does its performance, stability, and convergence speed compare to a more standard FQI approach where the target Q-function parameters are held fixed for a set number of iterations (i.e., using `Q_{theta(k-1)}` instead of `Q_{alpha*theta(k-1)}` in the target term of Equation 6)?
3.  **Computational Cost:** A key advantage of your method is sample efficiency. Could you comment on its computational efficiency? Specifically, how does the training time for your method (including dynamics learning and 2 sampling iterations of FQI) compare to the training time for the TRPO baseline that uses 50 sampling iterations?
