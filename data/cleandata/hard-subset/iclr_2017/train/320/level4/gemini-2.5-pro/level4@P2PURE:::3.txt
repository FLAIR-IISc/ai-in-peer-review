**1) Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of visual servoing for target following by leveraging spatial feature maps from a convolutional network pre-trained on a general image classification task. The proposed method learns a servoing policy by combining a reinforcement learning algorithm with a bilinear model that predicts the one-step dynamics of multi-scale visual features. The policy is optimized by minimizing a regularized, weighted distance between the current features and those predicted by the dynamics model.

**2) Contributions**

*   Presents controlled experiments in simulation that quantify the benefits of using pre-trained deep features for visual servoing.
*   Demonstrates clear performance improvements over several sensible baselines, including those that use ground-truth bounding boxes.
*   Introduces a principled method for learning the weights of multi-scale visual features using an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm, which also effectively handles distractors.
*   Achieves good sample efficiency, enabled by the choice of Q-function approximator and the model-based approach to one-step visual dynamics.
*   Provides an open-source virtual city environment for benchmarking visual servoing algorithms.

**3) Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **More Complex Benchmarks:** While the simulation environment is non-trivial, the experiments would be more compelling if conducted under more complex visual conditions, such as increased clutter, more distractors, and greater variety in object appearance and motion. Specifically, the realism and diversity could be improved by incorporating a larger set of 3D car models (e.g., from Google SketchUp) and by populating the environment with more distractor vehicles in traffic or parked. This is a crucial extension, as robustness to visual variations is a primary claim of the proposed approach.
*   **End-to-End and Representation Learning:** Although the current results are significant, it would be interesting to evaluate the impact of end-to-end training (i.e., fine-tuning the convolutional network), as this may be necessary for better generalization in more challenging visual environments. Such an experiment would also measure the benefit of deep representation learning for visual servoing, a topic of clear relevance to ICLR. As the authors note, the method can be adapted for this, but no representation is learned in its current form.
*   **Reproducibility:** The formalism and algorithms are well-explained, but the paper contains a large number of implementation details and practical tricks scattered throughout the main text and appendix, described with varying levels of detail. While reorganizing this information would be helpful, a more effective solution would be to summarize the most critical details in the paper and provide a link to an open-source implementation for full reproducibility.
*   **Typos:**
    *   p.2: "learning is a relative[ly] recent addition"
    *   p.2: "be applied [to] directly learn"

**4) Conclusion**

Despite the aforementioned limitations of the experimental setup, this is an interesting and solid paper, strengthened by the authors' excellent response to pre-review questions and the subsequent revision. This responsiveness suggests the authors are more than capable of addressing the suggestions above, which would result in an even stronger contribution.
