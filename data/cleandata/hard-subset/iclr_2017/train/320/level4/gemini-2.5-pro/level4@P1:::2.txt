### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel and sample-efficient method for visual servoing that combines pre-trained visual features, a learned dynamics model, and reinforcement learning. The core idea is to perform servoing in the feature space of a VGG network rather than in raw pixel space. The authors first learn an action-conditional, multi-scale bilinear model to predict the one-step evolution of these features. To incorporate long-term objectives, the servoing policy minimizes a weighted combination of feature errors across different channels and scales. The key contribution is an efficient method to learn these weights using a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm, which reshapes the one-step objective to approximate the long-term value function. The experiments on a simulated car-following task convincingly demonstrate that this approach is highly sample-efficient—requiring only 20 trajectories to learn an effective policy—and generalizes well to novel targets.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a complete, well-executed, and highly sample-efficient system for visual servoing that cleverly integrates deep features, model-based control, and value function learning. The proposed FQI method for learning the objective function weights is a novel and significant contribution to model-based reinforcement learning.
2.  The experimental results are thorough and provide strong evidence for the method's effectiveness, showing substantial improvements in performance and sample efficiency over conventional visual servoing and standard model-free deep reinforcement learning approaches.

My recommendation for acceptance is based on the paper's quality and novelty in applying learning methods to a challenging robotics problem. However, my enthusiasm is tempered by the fact that the paper's primary contribution lies in control and reinforcement learning rather than representation learning, which may make it a borderline case for the ICLR venue.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper has several key strengths. It is well-written, mathematically sound, and presents a conceptually coherent and complete system. The central idea of learning weights for a servoing objective via FQI is elegant, bridging the gap between a simple one-step predictive controller and the long-horizon performance required by reinforcement learning. This connection allows for a linear Q-function approximator that is highly sample-efficient, which is a major advantage. The empirical results convincingly highlight the benefits of this value-weighted objective and demonstrate that using pre-trained visual features is an effective strategy for achieving generalization in control tasks.

Despite these strengths, the paper's fit for ICLR is debatable. The primary focus is on the control problem (i.e., how to *use* features) rather than the representation learning problem (i.e., how to *learn* features). The visual representation is taken from a fixed, pre-trained VGG network, and the work does not explore fine-tuning or learning these features end-to-end for the task. Furthermore, the policy class is fixed (as the minimizer of a weighted quadratic objective), and the Q-function is approximated linearly using features derived from the prediction error. While this design is clearly effective and crucial for sample efficiency, it is less general than the non-linear function approximators commonly seen in deep reinforcement learning papers at ICLR. These points do not diminish the quality of the work, but they do place its main contribution more in the domain of robotics and control than in representation learning itself.

### Additional Feedback and Questions

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   **Positioning:** The paper's positioning could be strengthened by more explicitly discussing the trade-offs between using a fixed pre-trained representation (which provides sample efficiency and a strong prior) and fine-tuning or learning the representation end-to-end (which offers the potential for higher asymptotic performance at the cost of data). A brief discussion on this point would help contextualize the design choices for the ICLR audience.
*   **Q-Function Approximation:** The linear approximation for the Q-function is key to the method's sample efficiency. Could you comment on the potential limitations of this choice? For instance, in what kinds of visual servoing problems might this linear approximation be insufficient? Does the overall framework support extension to a non-linear Q-function approximator, and what challenges would that entail?
*   **FQI Algorithm:** The proposed FQI algorithm involves an interesting two-step update procedure (Eq. 5 and 6) to first solve for scale and bias parameters before updating the weights. Could you provide more intuition for this design? For instance, how does this procedure improve stability or convergence compared to a standard FQI update?
