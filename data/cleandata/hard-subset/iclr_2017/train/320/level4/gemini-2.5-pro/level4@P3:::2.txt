### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel and compelling approach to the classic robotics problem of visual servoing, specifically for target following. The authors propose a method that effectively combines three key components: (1) robust visual features extracted from a pre-trained deep convolutional neural network (VGG), (2) a learned, action-conditional bilinear dynamics model that predicts how these features evolve over a single time step, and (3) a sample-efficient reinforcement learning algorithm based on fitted Q-iteration (FQI) to learn a task-specific weighting over the visual features. The core idea is to perform servoing in a weighted, learned feature space rather than directly in pixel space. By doing so, the method can select the most relevant visual cues for the task at hand, leading to robustness against occlusions and distractors. The experiments, conducted in a challenging simulated car-following environment, demonstrate that this approach is not only highly effective but also remarkably sample-efficient, learning a competent policy from just 20 training trajectories and outperforming traditional methods as well as standard deep reinforcement learning algorithms by a significant margin.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

1.  **Exceptional Sample Efficiency:** The paper's primary contribution is a practical and highly sample-efficient method for learning a visual servoing policy. The demonstrated ability to learn a robust controller from only 20 trajectories is a major advance over conventional deep RL methods, which often require thousands or millions of interactions. This is achieved through a clever decomposition of the problem: leveraging pre-trained features for perception, learning a simple local dynamics model, and using a tailored FQI algorithm for long-term planning.

2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors provide a thorough and convincing experimental evaluation. The proposed method is benchmarked against a comprehensive set of baselines, including classical image-based visual servoing (IBVS), a state-of-the-art visual tracker, and model-free deep RL (TRPO). The results clearly show the superiority of the proposed approach, particularly in its ability to generalize to novel target objects. The ablation studies effectively justify the necessity of the feature-weighting scheme learned via FQI.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The strength of this work lies in its intelligent synthesis of existing techniques to create a system that is more than the sum of its parts. By using pre-trained VGG features, the authors sidestep the immense data requirement of learning a visual representation from scratch, a choice well-justified by the goal of rapid adaptation to new targets. The bilinear dynamics model is simple enough to be learned from a small amount of data, yet expressive enough to capture the short-term consequences of the robot's actions in the feature space.

The most significant component is the FQI-based learning of feature weights. This elegantly solves the problem of credit assignment: which of the thousands of features from a deep network are actually important for following a specific target? The proposed Q-function formulation, which is linear in these weights, makes the FQI updates computationally efficient and stable. The resulting policy, which minimizes a weighted one-step prediction error, implicitly optimizes for long-term rewards. The empirical results are striking; the method achieves a far lower cost than a TRPO-based policy that was trained on more than 100 times the amount of data (Figure 6). This highlights the value of incorporating model-based reasoning and a structured policy class.

Furthermore, the paper is well-written, the methodology is clearly explained, and the problem is well-motivated within the existing literature. The release of the code and simulation environment is a welcome contribution that will facilitate reproducibility and future research.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Representation Learning vs. Representation Usage:** The paper's main focus is on the effective *use* of a pre-trained representation for control, rather than on learning the representation itself. This is a pragmatic and well-motivated choice for achieving sample efficiency. However, for the context of ICLR, a brief discussion on the trade-offs of this approach versus a fully end-to-end learned system would be beneficial. For example, could an end-to-end system potentially achieve higher asymptotic performance if given enough data, even if it is less sample-efficient?

*   **Linearity of the Q-Function Approximator:** The choice of a Q-function that is linear in the feature weights is key to the algorithm's efficiency. However, this assumes that the squared prediction error in the VGG feature space is a sufficient statistic for representing the true value function. Could you comment on the potential limitations of this linear approximation and how it might scale to tasks with more complex reward structures or dynamics?

*   **Choice of Pre-trained Network:** The experiments rely on the VGG-16 architecture. Have you considered whether other, more modern architectures (e.g., ResNet, MobileNet) might provide features that are even better suited for this task? A brief comment on the sensitivity of the method to the choice of the base feature extractor would be interesting.

*   **FQI Algorithm Details:** The two-step update within the FQI iteration (solving for `α` and `b` first in Eq. 5, then for `θ` in Eq. 6) is an interesting technique for stabilizing the learning process. Could you provide a bit more intuition on why this particular decomposition is effective? Does it have connections to other methods like LSTD-Q or policy evaluation techniques?
