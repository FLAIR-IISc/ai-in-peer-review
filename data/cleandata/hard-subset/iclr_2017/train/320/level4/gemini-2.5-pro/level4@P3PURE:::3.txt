### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on general image classification tasks. The proposed method combines a bilinear model of the one-step dynamics of multi-scale visual features with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a servoing policy. This policy is learned by minimizing a regularized, weighted average of distances to target features, as predicted by the visual dynamics model.

### Contributions

*   The paper presents controlled experiments in simulation that quantify the utility of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing.
*   The method demonstrates clear performance benefits over several sensible baselines, including those that use ground-truth bounding box information.
*   A principled approach is presented for learning the weights of multi-scale visual features, utilizing an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm to handle distractors.
*   The approach achieves good sample efficiency, attributable to its choice of Q-function approximator and the use of a model-based, one-step visual feature dynamics.
*   The authors contribute an open-source virtual city environment for benchmarking visual servoing tasks, which is a valuable resource for the community.

### Suggestions for Improvement

*   **More complex benchmark:** While the simulation environment is more than a toy problem, the experiments would be more compelling if conducted under more complex visual conditions, such as greater clutter, more numerous distractors, and increased variety in appearance, motion, and environment. For instance, the realism and diversity of object appearances could be improved by incorporating a larger set of 3D car models (e.g., from sources like Google SketchUp) and populating the environment with more distractor vehicles. Strengthening the benchmark in this way is important for validating the method's claimed robustness to visual variations.
*   **End-to-end and representation learning:** The paper relies on fixed, pre-trained features. While the results are significant, an interesting extension would be to investigate the impact of end-to-end training, including fine-tuning the convolutional network, as this may be necessary for generalization to more challenging visual conditions. Furthermore, such an experiment would allow for a direct evaluation of deep representation learning for this task, a topic of clear relevance to the ICLR community. The current work does not involve representation learning, though the authors briefly mention that their method could be adapted for it.
*   **Reproducibility:** While the formalism and algorithms are explained clearly, the paper contains a large number of implementation details and practical heuristics distributed throughout the main text and appendix. The exposition can be difficult to follow due to its volume and varying levels of detail. To improve clarity and reproducibility, the authors could reorganize this information by summarizing only the most critical implementation choices in the paper and providing a link to an open-source implementation for completeness.
*   **Typos:**
    *   p.2: "learning is a relative[ly] recent addition"
    *   p.2: "be applied [to] directly learn"

### Conclusion

Despite the limitations of the experimental setup, this is an interesting and solid paper. The authors' excellent response to pre-review questions and the subsequent revision have already strengthened the work significantly. I am confident that the authors are capable of addressing the suggestions for improvement outlined above, which would result in an even stronger contribution.
