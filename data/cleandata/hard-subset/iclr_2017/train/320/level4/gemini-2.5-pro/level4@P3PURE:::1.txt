This paper proposes a novel approach for learning visual servoing using a method inspired by fitted Q-iteration. The primary contributions are: (1) a bilinear dynamics model to predict the next frame's features given the current frame and an action; (2) a Q-function formulation for visual servoing that learns to weight different feature channels; and (3) an elegant optimization method for the Bellman error to learn this Q-function.

**Strengths:**

*   The authors provide a thorough exploration of different methods for modeling the interaction between the action ($u_t$) and the current frame representation ($y_t$) to predict the next frame's features ($y_{t+1}$). The paper makes a convincing case for a locally connected bilinear model, which effectively balances computational cost with expressive power.
*   The proposed method of learning weights for different feature channels is well-motivated. This allows the model to capture the relative importance of different objects or features, which is a more effective strategy than treating errors across all channels equally. The experimental results validate this contribution, consistently showing degraded performance for an unweighted baseline.
*   The optimization scheme for the Bellman error is a notable strength. The authors leverage the key insight that the optimal policy is invariant to the scaling of the Q-function's parameters. This observation allows for a more elegant fitted Q-iteration (FQI) style update, which contrasts with typical schemes that must hold the target term ($c_t + \gamma \min_u Q_{t+1}$) fixed during optimization.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   While Section 4 presents compelling arguments for the design choices, the paper would be strengthened by an empirical comparison of the three proposed dynamics models: fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected. Such an ablation study would provide concrete evidence for the superiority of the chosen approach.
*   Similarly, it would be beneficial to see an experimental comparison between the proposed optimization method and a more traditional iterative approach that keeps the target Q-value term in Equation 5 fixed. This would quantify the practical benefits of the proposed formulation.
*   The experimental results are somewhat unsatisfying due to their small scale and reliance on simulated tasks. While the lack of established benchmarks in this domain is a mitigating factor, the claims of the paper would be more compelling if supported by results on more complex or realistic problems.
*   The paper proposes a modification to the VGG network but does not provide an experimental validation for this change. It is unclear whether the modified architecture performs better than the original, and an ablation study is needed to justify this design choice.

**Conclusion:**

In summary, this paper presents solid contributions in its novel problem formulation and technical approach to visual servoing. The bilinear dynamics model, channel-weighted Q-function, and the elegant optimization scheme are all compelling. However, the paper's claims are not yet fully supported by the experimental results, which are limited in scale and lack key ablation studies. Strengthening the empirical validation as suggested above would significantly improve the paper.
