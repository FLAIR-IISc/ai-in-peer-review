1) Summary
This work presents an approach for visual servoing, focusing on target following, that leverages spatial feature maps from a convolutional neural network (CNN) pre-trained for image classification. To learn the servoing policy, the authors integrate a reinforcement learning algorithm with multi-scale bilinear models that predict the one-step dynamics of the visual features. The policy is trained by minimizing a cost function defined as a regularized, weighted average of distances to the feature states predicted by this dynamics model.

2) Contributions
+ The paper provides a quantitative analysis through controlled simulations, demonstrating the efficacy of pre-trained deep features for the visual servoing task.
+ The proposed method shows significant performance gains over several well-chosen baselines, even outperforming methods that rely on ground-truth bounding box information.
+ A principled method is introduced for learning the weights of multi-scale visual features. This is achieved using an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm, which effectively addresses the challenge posed by distractors in the scene.
+ The approach exhibits high sample efficiency, which can be attributed to the specific choice of the Q-function approximator and the use of a model-based approach for one-step visual dynamics.
+ The authors contribute an open-source virtual city environment designed for benchmarking visual servoing algorithms.

3) Suggestions for improvement
- Enhancing the benchmark's complexity:
While the provided simulation environment is more than a simple toy problem, the experimental evaluation would be stronger if conducted under more challenging visual scenarios. This includes adding more clutter, a higher density of distractors, greater variety in target appearance and motion, and overall increased environmental richness. Specifically, the realism could be improved by incorporating a wider array of diverse and realistic 3D car models (e.g., from sources like Google SketchUp) and by increasing the number of non-target vehicles, both moving and stationary. Such improvements are crucial for validating the method's claimed robustness to visual variations.
- End-to-end training and representation learning:
The method achieves significant gains with frozen features; however, an investigation into the effects of end-to-end training (i.e., fine-tuning the convolutional network) would be a valuable addition. Fine-tuning may be essential for generalization to the more complex visual conditions suggested above. Furthermore, this would facilitate an analysis of deep representation learning specifically for the visual servoing task—a topic of high relevance to ICLR. While the authors note this is a possible extension, demonstrating it would strengthen the paper's contribution in this area.
- Clarity of Implementation Details for Reproducibility:
The paper's formalism and algorithms are presented clearly. However, the numerous implementation details and practical heuristics are scattered throughout the main text and appendix, which can be difficult for the reader to follow. To improve reproducibility, the authors could consider consolidating and streamlining the presentation of these details. An even better approach would be to summarize the most critical implementation choices in the main paper and provide a link to an open-source implementation containing the complete details.
- Minor Typos:
A few minor typographical errors were noted:
p.2: "relative" should be "relatively"
p.2: "applied directly" should be "applied to directly"

4) Conclusion
Despite the limitations noted in the experimental setup, this is a compelling and well-executed paper. The quality of the work is further reinforced by the authors' thorough response to reviewer feedback and the resulting improvements in the revised manuscript. I am confident that the authors can successfully address the suggestions outlined above. Incorporating these changes would substantially strengthen the paper further.
