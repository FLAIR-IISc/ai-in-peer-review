This paper introduces a novel approach for learning visual servoing policies, specifically for the task of target following. The core contribution is a method that effectively combines pre-trained deep visual features, a learned predictive model of feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning algorithm. The authors propose using spatial feature maps from a VGG network, modeling their one-step dynamics with a multi-scale bilinear model, and then learning a policy that servos in this feature space. A key aspect of the work is a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm that learns to weight different feature channels and scales, enabling the system to focus on the relevant target and ignore distractors. The method is validated in a simulated quadcopter car-following environment, where it demonstrates impressive sample efficiency and superior performance compared to a wide range of baselines.

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons for this decision are: (1) the work presents a principled and highly sample-efficient method that intelligently combines several machine learning techniques to address a classic robotics problem, and (2) the claims are substantiated by a thorough and well-controlled empirical evaluation against strong baselines, significantly outperforming them.

The strength of the proposed method lies in its clever design choices that lead to remarkable data efficiency. By leveraging powerful, pre-trained visual features, the system avoids learning perception from scratch. The subsequent learning of a simple, linear-in-action bilinear dynamics model is far more tractable than general video prediction. The most significant component is the formulation of the Q-function. The authors design a Q-function approximator that is linear in the learnable weights and directly corresponds to the structure of the servoing policy. This allows for a very efficient FQI procedure that learns to select the most relevant features for the task, a crucial step for achieving robustness. This model-based RL approach is shown to be orders of magnitude more sample-efficient than standard model-free deep RL methods like TRPO.

The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors compare their approach against not only pixel-based and hand-crafted feature (ORB) baselines, but also against modern deep RL policies and classic visual servoing methods that use a state-of-the-art visual tracker initialized with ground truth information. The proposed method demonstrates superior performance across these comparisons, even on novel target objects not seen during training. Furthermore, the contribution of an open-source simulation environment is a valuable asset that will facilitate future research in this area.

The following points are suggestions aimed at further strengthening the paper and are not part of the acceptance decision.
*   **Increasing Benchmark Complexity:** The claims of robustness to visual variation are compelling, but the experiments would be even more impactful if conducted in a visually richer environment. I would encourage the authors to consider adding more clutter, a greater number of distractor objects (e.g., parked cars or other traffic), and using a larger set of more diverse 3D models for the target cars to further stress-test the system's generalization capabilities.
*   **Investigating End-to-End Fine-Tuning:** The current approach uses fixed, pre-trained features. An interesting and valuable extension would be to explore the effect of fine-tuning the convolutional network's weights during training. This end-to-end learning could potentially unlock further performance gains and would explicitly introduce a representation learning aspect to the work, which would be of high interest to the community.
*   **Improving Clarity of Implementation Details:** The paper and its appendix contain a large number of implementation details and hyperparameters. For the benefit of readers and to enhance reproducibility, it might be helpful to consolidate these details. A summary of the most critical settings in the main paper, with a clear reference to the publicly available code for the exhaustive list, could improve the paper's clarity.
