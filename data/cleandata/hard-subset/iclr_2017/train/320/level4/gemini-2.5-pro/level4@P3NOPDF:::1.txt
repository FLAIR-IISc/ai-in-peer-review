This paper proposes a novel approach for visual servoing based on the Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) algorithm. The primary contributions include: (1) a bilinear dynamics model for predicting future visual features based on the current features and a given action; (2) a Q-function formulation that learns to weight different feature channels, thereby attending to the most relevant visual information; and (3) an elegant and direct optimization method for the Bellman error.

**Strengths**

1.  The paper provides a thoughtful exploration of different architectures for the dynamics model, which predicts the next state (visual features) given the current state and action. The authors systematically compare fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected bilinear models, making a convincing case for the locally connected bilinear approach as a favorable trade-off between expressive power and computational efficiency.

2.  The proposed Q-function, which learns to weight individual feature channels, is a key strength. This allows the model to dynamically determine the importance of different visual features for the servoing task, a more sophisticated approach than treating all feature errors equally. This design choice is well-justified and its effectiveness is convincingly demonstrated in the experiments, where the unweighted baseline consistently underperforms.

3.  The optimization method for the Bellman error is particularly elegant. By exploiting the observation that the optimal policy is invariant to the scaling of the Q-function's parameters, the authors derive a simplified and effective update rule. This avoids the common practice in FQI of fixing the target Q-value term during optimization, leading to a more direct and potentially more stable learning process.

**Weaknesses**

1.  While Section 4 provides a strong theoretical motivation for the chosen dynamics model, the paper would be significantly strengthened by an empirical comparison of the three discussed approaches (fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected). Such an ablation study would provide concrete evidence to support the claim that the locally connected bilinear model offers the best trade-off in practice.

2.  The proposed optimization scheme for the Bellman error is a novel contribution. However, to fully appreciate its impact, a comparison against a more traditional iterative FQI implementation (i.e., one that holds the target Q-value term `c_t + γ min_u' Q(y_{t+1}, u')` fixed during the update) would be highly valuable. This would quantify the benefits of the proposed solution over standard methods.

3.  The experimental evaluation is the main weakness of the paper. The results are presented on small-scale, simulated tasks which, while useful for demonstrating the core mechanics of the approach, do not sufficiently showcase its potential for real-world applications. While I acknowledge the current lack of established benchmarks in this specific domain, the limited scope of the experiments makes it difficult to assess the robustness and scalability of the method.

4.  Furthermore, the paper proposes a modification to the VGG network but does not provide an experimental validation of this change against the original architecture. This leaves the reader questioning the practical benefit of the proposed modification.

**Overall Assessment**

In summary, this paper presents a technically solid and well-formulated approach to visual servoing. The contributions, particularly the channel-weighted Q-function and the elegant optimization scheme, are novel and interesting. However, the overall impact of the work is currently limited by a lack of rigorous experimental validation. I believe the paper has significant potential, but its claims need to be substantiated with more comprehensive experiments, including the suggested ablation studies and evaluations on more challenging tasks. With stronger empirical support, this could be a compelling contribution.
