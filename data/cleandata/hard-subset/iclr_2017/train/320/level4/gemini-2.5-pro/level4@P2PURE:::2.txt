This paper explores visual servoing using a learned visual representation. The authors propose learning an action-conditional bilinear model over visual features extracted from a pre-trained VGG network. A policy is then derived by linearizing this learned dynamics model. The paper also presents a multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of the bilinear model. To account for the fact that the bilinear model is a single-step predictor, the authors introduce a weighted objective that incorporates the long-term value of the current policy. Policy evaluation is performed using a fitted-value iteration approach.

The paper is well-written, mathematically solid, and conceptually comprehensive. A key contribution is the experimental demonstration of the value-weighted objective's benefits. Furthermore, the paper appears to be the first to introduce a trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm. The experiments also empirically validate that using pre-trained visual features improves generalization. Overall, I recommend this paper for acceptance, as it will be of significant interest to the robotics research community.

However, my main reservation concerns the paper's fit for ICLR. Specifically, the contribution to the core problem of "representation learning" is limited. The work demonstrates the utility of a pre-trained VGG representation but does not explore learning the visual features end-to-end. While end-to-end learning is not a requirement, the paper's focus is weighted much more heavily towards the control problem than towards representation learning. Additionally, the policy representation is fixed, and the value function is a linear approximation over problem-specific features. These points do not diminish the paper's overall value, but they do suggest that its primary contributions may be less aligned with the central themes of ICLR.
