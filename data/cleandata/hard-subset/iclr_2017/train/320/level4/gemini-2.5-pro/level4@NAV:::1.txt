This paper introduces a new Q-iteration-based method for learning visual servoing. The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A bilinear dynamics model to predict subsequent visual features from the current visual state and action.
2.  A visual servoing formulation that employs a Q-function to learn the relative importance of different feature channels by assigning them weights.
3.  A refined optimization procedure for the Bellman error to learn the Q-function.

**Pros:**
+ The authors provide a commendable exploration of various architectures for linking the action ($u_t$) and the visual representation ($y_t$) to predict the next state ($y_{t+1}$). Their justification for selecting a locally connected bilinear model, citing its balance between expressive power and computational efficiency, is well-argued.
+ The concept of weighting feature channels to reflect the significance of different objects is a compelling improvement over treating all channel errors uniformly. This is substantiated by the experiments, which consistently show degraded performance for the unweighted baseline.
+ Optimizing the Bellman error is a known challenge in Q-learning. This work proposes a robust optimization method grounded in the insight that the optimal policy is invariant to the scaling of Q-function parameters. This insight facilitates a more sophisticated Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) solution compared to conventional methods that require fixing the target term ($c_t + \gamma \min_u Q_{t+1}$).

**Cons:**
- Although Section 4 presents a compelling rationale for the chosen model, the paper would be strengthened by an empirical comparison of the three discussed dynamics models: fully connected, convolutional, and the proposed locally connected version.
- Nevertheless, an ablation study comparing the proposed FQI-style optimization with a standard iterative approach where the second term of Equation 5 is held constant would be valuable.

**Experimental results:**
- The experimental evaluation is generally unconvincing due to its limited scale and reliance on simplistic simulations. It is important, however, to acknowledge the absence of established benchmarks in this research area.
- Furthermore, as noted previously, the proposed modification to the VGG architecture lacks experimental validation. Consequently, it remains uncertain whether this modified VGG would offer any performance benefits over the standard version.

**Overall:**
In summary, the paper's primary contributions are technically sound, with notable novelty in its problem formulation and methods. However, the claims require reinforcement through more rigorous and comprehensive experimental validation, as outlined above.
