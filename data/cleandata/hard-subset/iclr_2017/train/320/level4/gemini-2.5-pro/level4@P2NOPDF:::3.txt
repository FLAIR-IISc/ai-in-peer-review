### **Summary**

This paper proposes a method for visual servoing, specifically for target following, that leverages spatial feature maps from a convolutional neural network pre-trained on a general image classification task. The authors learn a servoing policy using a reinforcement learning algorithm. This policy is trained to minimize a regularized, weighted distance to target features. The target features are predicted by a learned, multi-scale bilinear model that captures the one-step dynamics of the visual features in response to camera motion.

### **Strengths**

*   The paper presents controlled experiments in a simulation that effectively quantify the benefit of using pre-trained deep features for the visual servoing task.
*   It demonstrates clear performance improvements over several relevant baselines, including methods that use ground-truth bounding box information.
*   It introduces a principled method for learning the weights of multi-scale visual features. This is achieved using an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm that effectively handles visual distractors in the scene.
*   The approach achieves high sample efficiency, owing to the choice of the Q-function approximator and the use of a model to predict the one-step visual feature dynamics.
*   The authors provide an open-source virtual city environment, which can serve as a valuable benchmark for future research in this area.

### **Suggestions for Improvement**

**1. Benchmark Complexity:**
While the custom simulation environment is a valuable contribution, the experiments would be more compelling if conducted under more complex visual conditions. The paper's central claim of robustness would be significantly strengthened by increasing visual diversity, clutter, and the number of distractors. For instance, the realism and variety of object appearances could be improved by incorporating a larger set of 3D car models (e.g., from sources like Google SketchUp) and populating the environment with more non-target vehicles, both parked and in traffic.

**2. End-to-End and Representation Learning:**
The current approach relies on fixed, pre-trained features. Given the context of ICLR, a valuable extension would be to investigate the impact of end-to-end training by fine-tuning the feature-extracting convolutional network. This would not only potentially improve generalization in more challenging visual scenarios but would also allow for an analysis of deep representation learning specifically for the visual servoing task. The authors briefly mention this possibility, but an experimental evaluation would be a significant addition to the paper.

**3. Reproducibility and Clarity of Implementation:**
The paper clearly explains the core formalism and algorithms. However, the numerous implementation details and practical heuristics are distributed throughout the main text and appendix, which could make replication challenging. The paper's clarity would be improved by consolidating these details into a single, dedicated section. An even better approach would be to summarize the most critical implementation choices in the paper and provide a link to an open-source implementation for full reproducibility.

**4. Minor Typos:**
*   p. 2: "learning is a relative[ly] recent addition"
*   p. 2: "be applied [to] directly learn"

### **Conclusion**

Despite the experimental limitations discussed above, this is an interesting and solid paper. It has been notably strengthened by the authors' thorough response to pre-review questions and the subsequent revisions. The suggestions provided here are intended to further enhance the work. I am confident that the authors are well-equipped to address this feedback, which would result in an even stronger contribution to the field.
