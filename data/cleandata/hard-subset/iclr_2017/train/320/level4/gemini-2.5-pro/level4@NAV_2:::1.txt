This paper presents a method for learning visual servoing by combining pre-trained visual features, a learned predictive dynamics model, and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning algorithm. The core contribution is a framework that learns to predict the evolution of deep features in response to robot actions using a bilinear model. This predictive model is then used within a servoing policy that seeks to minimize the distance to a target feature representation. Crucially, the authors formulate this as a Q-learning problem, where a novel fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm learns to weight different feature channels and scales, effectively selecting the most task-relevant visual information for control. This approach demonstrates impressive sample efficiency and performance on a challenging simulated car-following task.

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are the novel formulation of visual servoing as a problem of learning weighted feature dynamics and the elegant, sample-efficient FQI algorithm proposed to solve it. The paper is well-written and the approach is technically sound. However, this recommendation is contingent on the authors addressing some weaknesses in the experimental validation, which currently does not fully support all of the method's design choices.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper's strengths lie in its well-motivated and technically novel contributions:
1.  **Learned Feature Dynamics:** The paper effectively argues for learning a predictive model in a pre-trained feature space rather than in pixel space. The choice of a bilinear model to capture the feature dynamics is well-justified, and the proposed locally connected variant offers a sensible trade-off between model expressivity and computational complexity.
2.  **Weighted Servoing as Q-Learning:** The idea to learn weights for different feature channels and scales is a key insight. By framing this as learning a Q-function, the system can learn to prioritize features most relevant to long-term task success, rather than treating all feature errors equally. This is validated by the experimental comparison against an unweighted baseline, which performs significantly worse.
3.  **Novel FQI Optimization:** The proposed FQI algorithm is a strong technical contribution. The observation that the greedy policy is invariant to a positive scaling of the Q-function's parameters is cleverly exploited to create a two-step optimization process. This approach, which first solves for an optimal scaling factor and bias before updating the weights, is an elegant alternative to more standard iterative methods and contributes to the method's impressive sample efficiency.

Despite these strengths, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened to better support the paper's claims:
1.  **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** Several key design choices are well-motivated in the text but are not empirically validated. For instance, the superiority of the locally connected bilinear model over a fully connected or convolutional one is not shown. Similarly, the benefit of the modified VGG architecture over the standard one is not demonstrated.
2.  **Lack of Algorithm Comparison:** While the proposed FQI algorithm is elegant, its empirical advantage over a more standard FQI implementation (e.g., one that simply uses a fixed target network) is not shown. Adding this comparison would more clearly highlight the practical benefits of the proposed optimization scheme.
3.  **Simulated Environment:** The experiments are conducted exclusively in a synthetic environment. While the simulation is complex and serves as a good proof of concept, the lack of real-world experiments or a more thorough discussion of the potential challenges of sim-to-real transfer limits the perceived impact of the work.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

The following points are suggestions aimed at strengthening the paper. They are not essential for my decision but would significantly improve the quality of the work.

*   To bolster the claims made in Section 4.2, please consider adding an experimental comparison between the locally connected bilinear dynamics model and other variants, such as a fully connected or a convolutional model. This would provide empirical evidence for the claimed trade-off between performance and efficiency.
*   The novelty of the FQI algorithm in Section 5.3 would be more impactful if compared against a more standard FQI baseline. For example, an experiment where the target Q-function in the Bellman error is simply held fixed for several iterations would provide a valuable point of reference for the performance and convergence speed of your proposed method.
*   The architectural modifications to the VGG network are an interesting component. An ablation study comparing the performance of your system using features from the modified VGG versus the original, off-the-shelf VGG network would help justify this design choice.

**Questions for Authors:**

Your responses to these questions would help clarify my understanding and increase my confidence in the assessment.

1.  In Section 4.2, you motivate the use of a locally connected bilinear model as a compromise between expressivity and parameter count. Could you provide empirical results or further justification comparing this choice against a fully connected or a convolutional dynamics model to quantify this trade-off?
2.  The proposed FQI algorithm in Section 5.3 includes an intermediate step to solve for scaling and bias parameters (Eq. 5). How does this method's convergence and final performance compare empirically to a more standard FQI implementation where the target network parameters from the previous iteration are simply held fixed while updating the current network?
3.  Section 4.1 describes modifications to the VGG network to reduce spatial invariance. Have you experimented with using features from the original, unmodified VGG network? An ablation study would help clarify the impact of these architectural changes on servoing performance.
