### 1) Summary
This paper addresses the problem of visual servoing, specifically for target following, by leveraging spatial feature maps from a convolutional network pre-trained on a general image classification task. The proposed method combines a bilinear model of one-step visual feature dynamics, captured at multiple scales, with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn the servoing policy. The policy is learned by minimizing a regularized, weighted distance to the features predicted by this dynamics model.

### 2) Contributions
+ The paper presents controlled experiments in simulation that quantify the benefits of using pre-trained deep features for visual servoing.
+ The method demonstrates clear performance gains over several strong baselines, including those that use ground-truth bounding box information.
+ A principled method for learning the weights of multi-scale visual features, using an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm to handle distractors.
+ The approach achieves high sample efficiency, resulting from the choice of Q-function approximator and the model-based, one-step visual dynamics.
+ The authors have developed and open-sourced a virtual city environment for benchmarking visual servoing, which is a valuable contribution to the community.

### 3) Suggestions for Improvement
- **More complex benchmark:** While the simulation environment is a step beyond simple synthetic datasets, the experiments would be more compelling if conducted under more complex visual conditions (e.g., increased clutter, more distractors, greater variety in appearance and motion, and richer environments). For example, the realism and diversity of object appearances could be improved by incorporating a larger and more varied set of 3D car models (such as those available from sources like Google SketchUp) and by populating the environment with more distractor vehicles, both moving and parked. Strengthening the benchmark in this way is important, as a key claimed benefit of the proposed approach is its robustness to visual variation.

- **End-to-end and representation learning:** While the current results are significant, the work could be strengthened by exploring end-to-end training (i.e., fine-tuning the convolutional network). This would likely be necessary for generalizing to more challenging visual conditions. Furthermore, this would allow the authors to measure the benefits of *learning* a deep representation specifically for visual servoing, which is a central theme of ICLR. As it stands, the paper uses a fixed, pre-trained representation, and an investigation into representation learning would be a valuable addition, especially since the authors note that their method could be adapted for this.

- **Reproducibility:** The formalism and algorithms are well-explained. However, the paper contains a large number of implementation details and practical heuristics that are scattered throughout the main text and appendix, sometimes with varying levels of detail. This can make it difficult for the reader to grasp the complete picture. To improve clarity and reproducibility, the authors could consolidate these details into a single section or appendix. Even better, they could summarize the most critical details in the paper and provide a link to an open-source implementation, which would ensure full transparency and facilitate future work.

- **Minor Typos:**
    - p.2: "learning is a relative[ly] recent addition"
    - p.2: "be applied [to] directly learn"

### 4) Conclusion
Despite the limitations noted in the experimental setup, this is an interesting and solid paper. The authors' thorough response to reviewer questions and the resulting improvements to the manuscript are commendable. I am confident that the authors are capable of addressing the suggestions above, which would further strengthen an already strong contribution.
