This work explores the application of a learned visual representation to the problem of visual servoing. The proposed approach involves learning an action-conditional bilinear model of visual dynamics, using features extracted from a pre-trained VGG network. A policy is then derived from this model through a linearization of the dynamics. The authors also introduce a multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of their bilinear model. To account for the single-step nature of the dynamics model, the paper presents a weighted objective function that integrates the long-term values of the incumbent policy, with the evaluation being handled by a fitted-value approach.

The paper is well-executed, demonstrating strong mathematical rigor and a comprehensive conceptual framework. The experiments successfully validate the efficacy of employing a value-weighted objective, which stands as a significant contribution. Furthermore, this work appears to be the first to formalize a trust-region fitted-q iteration algorithm. The use of pre-trained visual features is also empirically demonstrated to improve generalization. Overall, I recommend this paper for acceptance, as its findings will likely be of considerable benefit to the robotics research community.

However, in the context of this specific conference, the contribution regarding the "representation" problem is somewhat constrained. The paper shows that a pre-trained VGG representation is effective but does not explore learning the representation in an end-to-end fashion. While an end-to-end method is not a strict requirement, the paper's emphasis is placed more heavily on the control problem than on representation learning. Moreover, the policy representation is fixed, and the values are approximated linearly with problem-specific features. These factors do not diminish the paper's intrinsic value but may render it less aligned with the primary focus of ICLR.
