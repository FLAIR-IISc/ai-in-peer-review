### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel and highly sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing policies for a target-following task. The core idea is to combine the strengths of pre-trained deep visual features, a learned predictive dynamics model, and a specialized reinforcement learning algorithm. Specifically, the authors leverage features from a VGG network, learn a simple bilinear model to predict the one-step evolution of these features given a robot action, and define a servoing policy that greedily minimizes the distance to a target feature representation. The key contribution is a method for learning weights for different feature channels and scales to prioritize task-relevant information. These weights are learned via an elegant and sample-efficient Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) algorithm, which frames the problem as learning the parameters of a linear Q-function approximator. The experiments, conducted in a simulated quadcopter environment, convincingly demonstrate that this approach can learn a robust servoing policy from only 20 training trajectories, achieving a sample efficiency that is orders of magnitude greater than standard model-free deep reinforcement learning methods like TRPO.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Exceptional Sample Efficiency:** The primary reason for my recommendation is the paper's impressive demonstration of sample-efficient learning for a complex visuomotor control task. The proposed combination of pre-trained features, a simple learned dynamics model, and a specialized FQI algorithm is both novel and highly effective, offering a practical solution for robotics applications where data collection is expensive.

2.  **Methodological Soundness and Strong Empirical Support:** The paper is well-written, methodologically sound, and its claims are rigorously supported by a thorough set of experiments. The formulation of the Q-function to directly match the structure of the servoing policy is particularly elegant. While the work's primary contribution is arguably in the domain of reinforcement learning and control rather than representation learning, it provides crucial insights into how to effectively *use* and *specialize* general-purpose learned representations for robotic control, a topic of significant interest to the ICLR community.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's central claim of high sample efficiency is strongly substantiated. The comparison against TRPO is particularly striking: the proposed FQI-based method achieves superior performance using only 20 trajectories, while a standard deep RL approach (TRPO) requires thousands of trajectories (over 100x more data) and still underperforms. This efficiency stems from several well-motivated design choices:
*   Using pre-trained VGG features circumvents the need to learn a visual perception system from scratch.
*   The bilinear dynamics model is simple enough to be learned from a small dataset, yet powerful enough to capture the local dynamics in feature space.
*   The FQI algorithm directly learns a small number of weights for a well-structured policy, avoiding the high variance and data requirements of training a large, general-purpose neural network policy.

The main limitation of the paper, in the context of a representation learning conference, is that the visual features are fixed and not learned end-to-end with the control policy. However, this is a deliberate design choice that is fundamental to achieving the reported sample efficiency. The contribution here is not in creating a new visual representation, but in demonstrating a highly effective framework for adapting a general one to a specific control task by learning which parts of the representation are most important. This is a valuable lesson in itself.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive. It not only compares against a state-of-the-art model-free RL algorithm but also against several baselines, including classical image-based visual servoing (IBVS) with hand-crafted features (ORB) and tracker-based features (C-COT). The proposed method significantly outperforms these alternatives, demonstrating its robustness to occlusions and distractors—a common failure point for classical methods. The ablation study on different VGG layers provides useful insights into which level of feature abstraction is most suitable for this type of task.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Fine-tuning vs. Fixed Features:** While end-to-end training was likely omitted for sample efficiency, it would be valuable to include a brief discussion on this trade-off. Could the VGG features be fine-tuned alongside the Q-function if more data were available? How might this affect the stability of the learned bilinear dynamics model, which currently assumes a fixed feature extractor?

*   **Novelty of the FQI Algorithm:** The proposed FQI algorithm (Algorithm 1) includes a novel two-step update that first solves for scaling (α) and bias (b) parameters before updating the feature weights (θ). This appears crucial for performance. It would be beneficial to provide more intuition or analysis on why this procedure is more stable or effective than a more standard FQI update that solves for all parameters simultaneously. A more explicit contrast with standard FQI or Neural FQI (NFQ) would further highlight the novelty.

*   **Real-World Transfer:** The experiments are conducted in a convincing but simulated environment. Could the authors comment on the expected challenges of transferring this approach to a real-world robotic system? Specifically, would the simple bilinear dynamics model still be sufficient to capture the more complex and noisy dynamics of a physical system?

*   **Policy Representation:** The paper notes that the policy can be represented as a neural network with a matrix inverse operation. It would be interesting to know if this policy was directly compared to a generic CNN policy that also takes the VGG features as input (as opposed to raw pixels, which was tested). The results in Table 4 of the Appendix suggest this was attempted and performed poorly, which is an important finding worth highlighting in the main text.
