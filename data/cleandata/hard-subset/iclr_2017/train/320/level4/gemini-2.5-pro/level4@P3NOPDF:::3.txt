### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing for target following. The core idea is to leverage spatial feature maps from a convolutional network pre-trained on a general image classification task. The authors model the one-step dynamics of these visual features at multiple scales using a bilinear model. This dynamics model is then integrated into a reinforcement learning framework, where a trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm learns a servoing policy. The policy is optimized to minimize a regularized, weighted distance to target features predicted by the dynamics model, effectively enabling the agent to follow a designated target.

### **Strengths**

This paper has several notable strengths:

*   **Effective Use of Pre-trained Features:** The work provides a clear demonstration and quantification of the utility of pre-trained deep features for the visual servoing task, supported by well-controlled simulation experiments.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed method demonstrates significant performance improvements over several well-chosen and sensible baselines, including those that use ground-truth bounding box information.
*   **Principled Learning Framework:** The paper introduces a principled approach for learning to weight multi-scale visual features. The use of an efficient trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm is well-motivated and shown to be effective for improving robustness against distractors.
*   **Sample Efficiency:** The approach achieves notable sample efficiency, which the authors attribute to the combination of a model-based one-step visual dynamics model and an effective Q-function approximator.
*   **Contribution to the Community:** The release of an open-source virtual city environment for benchmarking visual servoing tasks is a valuable contribution that will facilitate future research in this area.

### **Suggestions for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, the following areas could be addressed to further improve its impact:

*   **Increased Benchmark Complexity:** The current experiments are conducted in a simulated environment that, while not trivial, could be made more challenging to better demonstrate the robustness of the proposed method. The evaluation would be significantly strengthened by incorporating more complex visual conditions, such as increased clutter, a higher density of distractors, and greater variety in target appearance and motion. Specifically, using a larger and more diverse set of 3D car models (e.g., from resources like Google SketchUp) and populating the environment with more dynamic and static distractor vehicles would provide a more rigorous test of the approach's ability to handle visual variations, which is a key claim of the paper.
*   **Exploration of End-to-End and Representation Learning:** The current method relies on fixed, pre-trained features. While this is shown to be effective, the work would have a greater impact, particularly for the ICLR community, if it explored end-to-end training where the convolutional network is also fine-tuned. This would not only have the potential to improve generalization in more challenging visual scenarios but would also allow for an investigation into the benefits of learning task-specific representations for visual servoing. As the authors briefly mention, the framework can be adapted for this, and such an experiment would be a valuable addition.
*   **Improved Presentation of Implementation Details:** The paper's formalism and core algorithms are clearly explained. However, the numerous practical implementation details and "tricks" are scattered throughout the main text and appendix, which can be challenging for the reader to follow. To enhance clarity and reproducibility, the authors could consider consolidating these details. A more organized presentation, perhaps summarizing the most critical parameters and design choices in a dedicated section and providing a link to an open-source implementation for the exhaustive details, would be an ideal solution.

### **Conclusion**

Despite the suggested limitations of the experimental evaluation, this is a solid and interesting paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of visual servoing. The authors' thorough response to the pre-review questions and the resulting improvements in the revised manuscript are commendable. This responsiveness, combined with the quality of the work, gives me confidence that the authors can address the suggestions outlined above to produce an even stronger final paper.
