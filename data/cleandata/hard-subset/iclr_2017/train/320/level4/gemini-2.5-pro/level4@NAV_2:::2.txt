This paper presents a method for learning visual servoing control by integrating pre-trained visual features, a learned predictive dynamics model, and reinforcement learning. The core idea is to perform servoing in the feature space of a deep network (VGG) rather than raw pixels. The authors propose learning a simple, action-conditional bilinear model to predict the one-step dynamics of these features. To connect this short-horizon model to long-term task performance, the paper introduces a key contribution: a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm that learns to weight different feature channels and scales. This weighting scheme adapts a simple greedy servoing policy to optimize for the expected long-term reward. The method is validated on a complex simulated car-following task, where it demonstrates the ability to learn an effective policy from a very small number of training trajectories.

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are 1) the novel and effective combination of pre-trained features, a learned dynamics model, and a tailored FQI algorithm that results in remarkable sample efficiency, and 2) the strong empirical evidence supporting the method's superiority over both classical visual servoing techniques and standard model-free deep reinforcement learning approaches.

The main strengths of this work lie in its well-motivated and cohesive framework. By leveraging pre-trained VGG features, the system inherits a degree of invariance to visual variations, which facilitates generalization. The choice of a bilinear model for feature dynamics is simple yet effective, and its linear structure with respect to the controls is elegantly exploited to create a tractable servoing objective. The most significant contribution is the FQI-based procedure for learning feature weights. Formulating the Q-function as a linear approximation of the servoing objective is a clever insight that allows the weights to be learned efficiently, directly optimizing the one-step policy for long-horizon performance. The experimental results are compelling. The ability to learn a robust policy from only 20 trajectories is a major improvement over standard deep RL methods like TRPO, which required more than two orders of magnitude more data for a comparable task. The thorough comparisons against various baselines, including pixel-based models, hand-designed keypoints (ORB), and a state-of-the-art visual tracker (C-COT), clearly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach.

The following points are offered to help improve the paper and are not critical to the acceptance decision.
*   **Contribution Framing:** While the paper effectively uses a learned representation, its main contribution appears to be in the domain of sample-efficient model-based control and reinforcement learning rather than in representation learning itself. The visual features are taken from a fixed, pre-trained network. A brief discussion on the trade-offs of this design choice versus end-to-end fine-tuning or training would be beneficial. For example, acknowledging that end-to-end learning might yield a more task-optimal representation at the cost of the very sample efficiency the paper aims for would help position the work more clearly.
*   **Policy and Q-Function Class:** The paper's policy is derived from minimizing a quadratic objective, and the Q-function is a linear approximation based on this structure. This is an elegant formulation but also a strong modeling assumption. It would be helpful to discuss the potential limitations of this approach. How might the method be extended to tasks requiring more complex, non-linear policies that cannot be derived from this specific objective?
*   **Questions for Authors:**
    *   Could you provide more intuition on the two-step update within your FQI iterations (Algorithm 1, lines 5-7)? Specifically, what is the motivation for first solving for a scalar `α` and bias `b` before updating the full weight vector `θ`? How does this contribute to the stability or performance of the learning process compared to a more standard FQI update?
    *   In the comparison against the C-COT tracker baseline, the paper notes that the classical IBVS method was provided with ground-truth depth for the feature points. Your method does not appear to use explicit depth. Could you comment on how your learned dynamics model implicitly captures geometric information and how its performance might be affected by large variations in target depth?
