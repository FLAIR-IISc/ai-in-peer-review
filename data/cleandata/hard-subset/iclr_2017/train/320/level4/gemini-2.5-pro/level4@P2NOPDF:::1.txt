This paper introduces a novel method for learning visual servoing policies using a Q-learning framework. The core contributions are threefold: 1) a bilinear dynamics model for predicting future visual features based on the current features and a given action; 2) a formulation of the visual servoing problem using a Q-function that learns to weight different feature channels, thereby identifying task-relevant visual cues; and 3) an elegant optimization scheme for the Bellman error based on a key insight about the scale-invariance of the optimal policy.

**Strengths:**

1.  The paper thoroughly explores different architectural choices for the dynamics model that maps the current state (visual features `y_t`) and action (`u_t`) to the next state (`y_{t+1}`). The authors make a compelling case for a locally connected bilinear model, arguing that it strikes an effective balance between the computational efficiency of a convolutional model and the expressive power of a fully connected model.

2.  A significant contribution is the idea of using the Q-function to learn weights for different feature channels. This allows the model to dynamically determine the importance of different visual features for the task at hand, which is more principled than treating errors across all channels equally. The experimental results validate this approach, consistently showing that the unweighted baseline performs poorly in comparison.

3.  The proposed optimization for the Bellman error is a key technical strength. By recognizing that the optimal policy is invariant to the scaling of the Q-function's parameters, the authors derive a direct optimization scheme for the Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI). This approach is more elegant than typical iterative methods that must hold the target Q-value term (`c_t + γ min_u Q_{t+1}`) fixed during optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  While Section 4 provides a strong theoretical justification for the chosen dynamics model, the paper would be significantly strengthened by an empirical comparison of the three proposed approaches: fully connected, convolutional, and locally connected. Such an ablation study would provide concrete evidence to support the claim that the locally connected model offers the best trade-off.

2.  Similarly, to highlight the benefit of the proposed optimization scheme, an experiment comparing it to a more standard iterative approach (i.e., one that holds the target Q-value term in Eq. 5 fixed) would be valuable. This would help quantify the practical advantages of the more elegant FQI formulation.

3.  The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited due to the use of small-scale, simulated environments. While the lack of established benchmarks for this specific task is duly acknowledged, the claims of the paper would be more impactful if validated on more complex or realistic tasks.

4.  The suggestion to modify the VGG network architecture to improve performance is an interesting idea but remains speculative without experimental validation. As it stands, it is unclear whether the proposed modification would outperform the original VGG architecture. An experiment demonstrating this would bolster the paper's contributions.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents solid contributions in its novel problem formulation and technical approach. The bilinear dynamics model, the channel-weighted Q-function, and the scale-invariant optimization scheme are all well-motivated and technically sound. However, the empirical evaluation is not as thorough as the theoretical development. The paper would be substantially stronger if the authors included the suggested ablation studies and demonstrated the method's effectiveness on more challenging tasks. Despite these weaknesses in the experimental section, the technical novelty of the work is significant.
