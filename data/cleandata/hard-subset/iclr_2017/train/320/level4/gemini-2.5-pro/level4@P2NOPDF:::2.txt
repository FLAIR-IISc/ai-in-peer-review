This paper explores visual servoing by leveraging learned visual representations. The core of the proposed method is an action-conditional bilinear model that learns the dynamics of visual features extracted from a pre-trained VGG network. A policy is then derived by linearizing these learned dynamics. The authors further introduce a multi-scale, multi-channel, and locally-connected variant of this bilinear model to improve performance.

To address the short-horizon limitation of the one-step bilinear model, the paper introduces a value-weighted objective that incorporates long-term value estimates of the current policy. This policy evaluation step is handled using a fitted-value iteration approach.

The paper is well-written, mathematically rigorous, and presents a conceptually complete framework. Its strengths are numerous:

*   A key contribution, well-supported by experiments, is the demonstration of the value-weighted objective's benefits for learning control policies.
*   Furthermore, the paper appears to be the first to formalize a trust-region fitted Q-iteration algorithm, which is another notable contribution.
*   The empirical results also validate that using pre-trained visual features is an effective strategy for improving generalization in this context.

Overall, this is a strong paper that I recommend for acceptance. Its findings will likely be of significant interest to the robotics and reinforcement learning communities.

My main reservation, however, concerns the paper's alignment with the central themes of ICLR. While the work is strong, its primary contributions seem to lie more in control and reinforcement learning than in representation learning itself. For instance, the method effectively utilizes a pre-trained VGG representation but does not explore learning these visual features end-to-end with the control objective.

While end-to-end learning is not a strict requirement, the paper's focus is disproportionately on the control aspects (e.g., the bilinear dynamics model, value-weighting, and policy derivation) rather than on the learning of the representation. Similarly, the choice of a fixed policy representation and a linear value function approximation with problem-specific features further emphasizes the control-centric nature of the work over deep representation learning.

To be clear, these choices do not diminish the paper's quality or its value. However, they do position the work as a potentially better fit for a robotics or control conference than for a venue focused primarily on representation learning. Despite this, the quality of the work is high, and it makes solid contributions that are relevant to the broader machine learning community.
