### **Review**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel approach to the classic robotics problem of visual servoing, specifically for target following. The authors propose a method that skillfully combines several powerful concepts: leveraging rich visual representations from a pre-trained convolutional neural network (VGG), learning a predictive model of the feature dynamics using a simple yet effective bilinear model, and employing a sample-efficient reinforcement learning algorithm to intelligently weight these features for the task. The core idea is to perform servoing in the learned feature space rather than directly in pixel space. By learning which features are most relevant, the system becomes robust to distractors, occlusions, and changes in viewpoint. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated in a complex simulated car-following task, where it achieves strong performance with remarkably high sample efficiency—requiring only 20 training trajectories.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a well-executed and solid paper that makes a valuable contribution by bridging classical robotics control with modern deep learning and reinforcement learning techniques. The key reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Effective and Principled Combination of Methods:** The paper successfully integrates pre-trained deep features, a learned dynamics model, and a tailored Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) algorithm. This combination directly addresses a major challenge in robotic learning: the need for high sample efficiency. The approach is well-motivated, leveraging the strengths of each component to create a powerful and practical system.

2.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments in a realistic simulation environment. They provide strong evidence for their claims by comparing their method against a wide array of sensible and challenging baselines, including model-free deep RL (TRPO), classical visual servoing with hand-crafted features (IBVS with ORB), and a state-of-the-art visual tracker (C-COT). The results clearly demonstrate substantial improvements in both performance and sample efficiency.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The central claim of the paper is that combining pre-trained features with a learned dynamics model and a sample-efficient RL algorithm can produce a robust and data-efficient visual servo. This claim is well-supported.

The use of pre-trained VGG features provides a powerful visual prior, sidestepping the need to learn a complex visual representation from scratch, which would require vast amounts of data. The choice of a bilinear model for the feature dynamics is elegant; it is simple enough to be learned from a small dataset yet expressive enough for accurate one-step prediction, which is all the servoing policy requires.

The most critical component is arguably the FQI-based algorithm for learning feature weights. This provides a principled solution to the problem of distractor objects. Instead of manually tuning weights for different feature channels and scales, the agent learns to prioritize features that contribute to long-term success in the following task. The custom Q-function approximator, which is linear in the weights being learned, is a clever design choice that makes the FQI updates computationally efficient and stable. The resulting sample efficiency is impressive: the method achieves strong performance with just 20 trajectories, outperforming a TRPO-based approach that uses over 100 times more data.

The experimental validation is rigorous. The comparison against various baselines is particularly strong. Outperforming classical IBVS is expected, but the method also shows significant gains over a much stronger baseline that uses the state-of-the-art C-COT tracker, even when the tracker is initialized with a ground-truth bounding box. This highlights the benefit of learning task-specific dynamics, which is absent in the classical approach. The ablation-like study comparing features from different VGG layers provides useful insights, and the generalization tests on novel, unseen cars confirm the robustness of the learned policies.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions aimed at further strengthening the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Benchmark Complexity:** While the simulation environment is well-designed, the experiments could be made even more compelling by increasing the visual complexity. To further showcase the robustness of the proposed method, the authors could consider adding more dynamic distractors (e.g., other cars in traffic), increasing the variety of 3D car models, and introducing more diverse environmental conditions (e.g., different lighting, weather).

*   **End-to-End Representation Learning:** The current method relies on fixed, pre-trained features. A natural and interesting extension, particularly for the ICLR audience, would be to explore the impact of fine-tuning the feature extractor (the VGG network) on the servoing task. While this would likely decrease sample efficiency, it might unlock higher performance and better generalization in more challenging visual scenarios. An analysis of this trade-off would be a valuable contribution.

*   **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper is generally well-written, but the description of experimental parameters and implementation details is spread across the main text and a lengthy appendix. For the benefit of readers and to enhance reproducibility, the authors might consider reorganizing these details. For example, consolidating key hyperparameters into a single table in the appendix would be helpful. The public release of the code and environment is highly commendable and largely mitigates this point.

*   **Minor Typos:**
    *   Page 2: "learning is a relative[ly] recent addition"
    *   Page 2: "be applied [to] directly learn"

**5. Questions for the Authors**

To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

*   You demonstrate strong results using fixed, pre-trained features. Do you have any insights or preliminary results on the potential benefits and challenges of fine-tuning the feature extractor end-to-end for this task? How do you expect this would impact the sample efficiency that is a core strength of your current approach?

*   The bilinear dynamics model is a key component. How sensitive is the overall servoing performance to the accuracy of this learned model? For instance, if the dynamics were trained on a significantly smaller dataset, at what point does performance begin to degrade substantially?

*   How does the FQI-learned feature weighting handle scenarios where a distractor is temporarily more visually salient than the target (e.g., another car briefly cutting in front)? Does the policy recover gracefully from such events?
