### **Summary**

This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically for target following, by leveraging spatial feature maps from convolutional networks pre-trained on general image classification. The proposed method combines a bilinear model of one-step visual feature dynamics, captured at multiple scales, with a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn an effective servoing policy. The policy is optimized by minimizing a regularized, weighted distance between the desired target features and the features predicted by the learned dynamics model.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

I recommend **Accept**. The primary reasons are its principled combination of pre-trained features, a learned dynamics model, and sample-efficient reinforcement learning to achieve strong performance on a challenging visual control task, alongside a thorough empirical evaluation against multiple relevant baselines.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strengths are numerous. It presents controlled simulation experiments that clearly quantify the utility of pre-trained deep features for visual servoing, demonstrating significant performance benefits over several sensible baselines, including classical methods and those that use ground-truth bounding boxes. The core technical contribution is a principled method for learning the weights of multi-scale visual features using an efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm. This approach effectively handles distractor objects by focusing on relevant features. Furthermore, the method achieves impressive sample efficiency—learning from only 20 trajectories—a direct result of its model-based one-step dynamics and a well-chosen Q-function approximator. Finally, the authors' contribution of an open-source virtual city environment for benchmarking will be valuable to the community.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are offered as constructive feedback to further improve the paper. They are not critical for my decision.

The experiments would be more convincing if performed under more complex visual conditions, with more clutter, distractors, and greater variety in object appearance and motion. This is particularly important since a key claim is the method's robustness. It would also be valuable to investigate the impact of end-to-end training (i.e., fine-tuning the feature extractor), which may be necessary for better generalization and would allow for an evaluation of deep representation learning specifically for this task—a topic highly relevant to ICLR. Lastly, while the core algorithms are explained clearly, numerous implementation details are scattered throughout the text and appendix. Consolidating these details would improve clarity and reproducibility; a good approach would be to summarize the critical choices in the main paper and refer to the open-source code for a comprehensive account.

Despite the limitations noted in the experimental setup, this is an interesting and solid paper. The authors' excellent response during the discussion phase gives me confidence that they are capable of addressing these suggestions, which would result in an even stronger contribution.
