1) **Summary**

This paper proposes tackling visual serving (specifically, target tracking) using spatial feature maps from a pre-trained convolutional network on common image classification tasks. The authors combine a two-dimensional model of one-step dynamics of visual feature maps on several parameters for serving policy learning with a reinforcement learning algorithm. This policy is learned by minimizing the regular weighted average of the distance of features estimated by the above model of visual dynamics.

2) **Contributions**

*   Controlled experiments in simulation to measure the utility of pre-trained deep features for visual serving.
*   Explicit performance gains with respect to several sensible baselines using a ground truth bounding box.
*   Theoretical learning of multi-level visual feature weights with an efficient trust-area fitted Q-iteration algorithm to handle the problem of distractors.
*   Good sample efficiency for the choice of Q-function approximators and model-based one-step visual feature dynamics.
*   Open-source virtual city environment to benchmark visual serving.

3) **Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **More Complex Benchmark:**

Although the environment is not just a toy synthetic, experiments would greatly benefit from more complex visual conditions (debris, distractors, presence and motion variation, environmental richness and diversity, etc.). At the very least, the realism and diversity of the object's appearance can be greatly enhanced by using a large number of 3D car models, including more realistic and diverse models that can be obtained from, for example, Google SketchUp, and filling the environment with more distracting cars (traffic or parks). This is important because the main desired quality of perspective is robustness to visual variations.

*   **End-to-End and Representation Learning:**

Although improvements to current synthetic experiments are already significant, it would be interesting to measure the impact of end-to-end training (i.e., also fine-tuning the convnet), as this is probably necessary for better generalization in more challenging visual conditions. This would allow for measuring the benefit of learning deep representations for visual serving, which would be relevant to ICLR (there is no representation learning yet, although the method can be directly adapted as the authors briefly mentioned).

*   **Reproducibility:**

The formalism and algorithm are clearly explained, but there is a slightly heavy mass of practical tips and implementation details that are described with various levels of detail throughout the paper and appendices. Grouping, simplifying, or reorganizing the presentation of these implementation details would help, but a better approach would probably be to summarize only the most important ones in the section and link to the open-source implementation of the method for completeness.

*   **Typo:**

P.2: "be directly applied to learning"

4) **Conclusion**

Despite the above limitations of the experiments, this paper is interesting and solid, partly thanks to excellent answers to pre-review questions and subsequent better modifications. It convinces me that the authors are able to largely follow the above suggestions for improvement, which will lead to an even better paper.
