1. Summary of contributions
The paper studies visual servoing for target following by combining (i) pre-trained CNN features (modified VGG-16), (ii) a learned multiscale bilinear predictive model of feature dynamics, and (iii) a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure to learn per-channel, per-scale feature weights and control regularization. The servo policy is deliberately simple: one-step lookahead that chooses actions minimizing the weighted Euclidean distance between predicted next-step features and those of a goal image. The bilinear dynamics make the Jacobian linear in actions, enabling efficient analytic optimization of the policy and a linear Q-function approximator whose greedy action recovers the same policy. The authors introduce a practical trick to jointly fit a global scale α and bias b in the Bellman targets to stabilize FQI when the policy is invariant to scaling and bias. Empirically, on a quadrotor car-following benchmark in the CitySim3D simulator, the method learns effective visual servo policies using only 20 RL trajectories, outperforming pixel-based servoing, hand-crafted keypoints + IBVS, and model-free deep RL (TRPO), and generalizing to unseen car instances. Code, environment, and videos are provided.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Technically sound, well-motivated integration of pre-trained visual features, bilinear feature dynamics, and a tailored, sample-efficient FQI to learn a servoing objective.
- Strong empirical support in a reasonably complex setting with careful ablations (feature layers, weighting methods) and comparisons, demonstrating large gains in sample efficiency and robustness relative to strong baselines.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets visual servoing without hand-engineered features or explicit camera calibration, aiming for fast adaptation to new targets with minimal data. Leveraging pre-trained CNN features mitigates the need for large task-specific datasets, while learned feature dynamics enables model-based lookahead; learning feature weights addresses distractors and relevance. The motivation is clear and well positioned within visual servoing, video prediction, and RL literature.
- Technical soundness: The bilinear dynamics formulation (locally connected variant) is appropriate and computationally tractable; because the dynamics are linear in action, the linearization used in control is exact. The Q-function approximator is linear in weights over per-channel future prediction errors and control penalties, ensuring that greedy minimization recovers the policy. The FQI scheme that first solves for (α, b) to account for scale/bias invariance is reasonable and helps stabilize learning. Training details, constraints (θ ≥ 0), and regularization are described; the modeling choices are coherent.
- Empirical support: Experiments report training data sizes, policies learned from few trajectories (S=2 iterations × 10 rollouts each), and evaluation on 100 test trajectories for seen and novel cars. Ablations across VGG layers reveal that mid-level features (conv1_2 to conv4_3) are best; conv5_3 underperforms, consistent with excessive spatial invariance. Comparisons cover: unweighted vs learned weights; FQI vs CEM vs TRPO for learning weights; model-free TRPO from pixels or features; classical IBVS with ORB and tracker-based features (with GT depth); and runtime analysis. Results consistently favor the proposed method in both performance and sample efficiency; code and environment availability increase confidence in reproducibility.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect the accept decision)
- Real-world relevance: The cost used for RL depends on ground-truth target pose from the simulator. Discuss how costs could be defined from purely visual signals (e.g., self-supervised objectives, learned success detectors) to enable real-world deployment, and whether the learned weights transfer when the cost is changed.
- Baselines: IBVS baselines receive ground-truth depth and tracker initialization; clarify whether your method has comparable oracle inputs (it does not) and consider an IBVS baseline without GT depth to reflect typical practice. Also, consider a model-based baseline that plans multi-step using the same bilinear dynamics to quantify the benefit of the proposed one-step greedy policy plus Q-weight learning.
- Robustness and sensitivity: Provide sensitivity to dynamics model errors, feature choice, and FQI hyperparameters (e.g., ν, γ). Report variance across multiple seeds for both FQI and TRPO to quantify stability.
- Generalization: Results on novel cars are encouraging; consider reporting performance under stronger distribution shifts (e.g., lighting/weather changes, different city layouts), and an experiment on a physical robot or a high-fidelity photorealistic sim to demonstrate practicality.
- Clarity: Briefly justify the non-negativity constraint on θ (beyond monotonicity with “error”) and how it is enforced in the optimizer for FQI. Summarize the parameter count for the locally connected bilinear model and memory/runtime footprint. A figure illustrating the α/b fitting step would help.

Questions for the authors
- How is θ ≥ 0 enforced in solving Eq. (6)? Are you using non-negative least squares with L2 regularization, projected gradients, or another method? Does the constraint materially affect performance?
- What prevents trivial solutions (e.g., θ → 0)? Is the L2 regularization sufficient, or do you normalize θ after each iteration?
- How sensitive is performance to dynamics model accuracy? If you deliberately degrade the bilinear model (fewer channels, smaller neighborhoods), how does control deteriorate?
- Does multi-step planning with your learned dynamics (e.g., MPC over a short horizon) improve performance over the one-step greedy policy, or do model errors negate the benefit?
- Can the learned weights transfer across targets without additional RL (e.g., follow different object categories using the same feature weighting), or is the weighting target-specific?
- How stable is the proposed FQI procedure across random seeds and exploration noise levels? Please report mean/std over multiple runs.
- In the IBVS comparisons, how much does access to ground-truth depth influence performance? Would learned monocular depth (or no depth) narrow the gap?
- What is the computational breakdown (feature extraction vs dynamics vs control solve) and the hardware used? Could a lighter backbone (e.g., MobileNet) retain performance while improving speed?
