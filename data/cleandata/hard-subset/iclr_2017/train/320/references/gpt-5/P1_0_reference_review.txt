Summary and contributions
The paper tackles visual servoing for target following by combining pre-trained deep visual features, a simple yet effective predictive dynamics model in feature space, and a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure to learn feature weights for a one-step lookahead servoing objective. Concretely:
- Visual features: Use intermediate VGG-16 convolutional feature maps (with reduced pooling and dilations to preserve spatial detail) at multiple scales as the perception backbone.
- Dynamics: Learn short-term feature dynamics with bilinear models (including a locally connected variant) that are linear in the control, allowing analytic Jacobians and exact linearization for efficient action selection.
- Control and RL: Define a weighted multiscale quadratic servoing objective whose minimizer gives the control. Choose weights (per feature channel and scale, plus per-action regularization) via a sample-efficient FQI algorithm that leverages a linear Q-function approximator matching the servo objective and exploits invariances to scaling and bias.
- Empirical results: On a synthetic quadcopter–car-following benchmark (CitySim3D), the method learns an effective servo with only 20 RL trajectories, substantially outperforming pixel-based or hand-crafted keypoint IBVS baselines and being far more sample-efficient than TRPO-trained policies. Code and simulator are released.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) A well-motivated and elegant integration of pre-trained features, predictive dynamics, and an analytically structured Q-function enables striking sample efficiency for visual servoing. (2) Thorough and fair empirical comparisons (including classical IBVS/PBVS and deep RL baselines) substantiate the claims; the method is computationally efficient and reproducible.

Supporting arguments
- Clear problem formulation: Expressing servoing as minimizing distance to a fixed goal feature map with one-step predicted features is simple and effective. The multiscale feature representation is well-motivated for robustness and global/local cues.
- Novelty and placement: Prior work either hand-designs features/Jacobians or uses data-hungry model-free RL. Using pre-trained CNN features as servoing “images,” learning a bilinear dynamics in that space, and then learning feature/channel weights via FQI with a linear, policy-consistent Q approximator is a novel combination. The literature on video prediction and model-based control is correctly cited.
- Algorithmic soundness: The bilinear model provides exact linearization in control; the chosen Q-function matches the servoing objective, making the greedy policy equivalent to the servoing minimizer. The two-stage FQI update with joint fitting of scale α and bias b exploits the invariance of argmin_u Q to these parameters, improving stability and efficiency. Non-negativity constraints on weights are sensible.
- Empirical rigor: 
  - Dynamics trained with 10k transition samples using a generic policy; RL uses only 20 trajectories. This separation highlights the small additional data needed to adapt to tasks (weights).
  - Comparisons: Multiple VGG layers vs pixels (with generalization to novel cars), TRPO and CEM alternatives for weight learning, end-to-end TRPO from pixels/VGG, classical IBVS with ORB and state-of-the-art tracker (with GT depth), and PBVS with GT pose. The baselines are strong and receive favorable assumptions (e.g., IBVS depth, tracker priming), yet the proposed method wins in the realistic settings and is dramatically more sample-efficient than TRPO.
  - Ablations: The layer-wise performance trend (conv1_2–conv4_3 better than conv5_3) is plausible and informative; the locally connected bilinear dynamics is well motivated.

Limitations (do not outweigh acceptance):
- Simulator-only evaluation; no real robot results. 
- The RL cost uses privileged ground-truth car pose (not available in deployment), though only for training weights.
- The dynamics training data (10k transitions) is non-trivial; the “20 trajectories” claim applies to the RL phase, not total data.
- No formal convergence guarantees; stability appears empirical.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Clarify and quantify data budgets: separately report data for (i) dynamics learning, (ii) RL for weights, and (iii) total wall-clock and sample counts, to avoid confusion around the “20 trajectories” claim.
- Real-world or sim-to-real evidence: even a small hardware demo or domain randomization experiment would strengthen impact.
- Robustness and ablations:
  - Sensitivity to choice of VGG layer, multi-scale composition, and weight regularization ν.
  - Compare bilinear dynamics to simpler (e.g., affine in features) or flow-based models; analyze the benefit of the locally connected constraint.
  - Test under stronger occlusions, lighting shifts, and camera calibration errors.
- Reward design: Discuss practicality of training without ground-truth pose (e.g., self-supervised objectives, trackers, or learned reward models).
- Analysis: Provide insight into learned weights (channels/scales emphasized) and qualitative failure cases. Explain conv5_3 underperformance beyond spatial invariance.
- Efficiency: Report computational profile per step (feature extraction vs dynamics vs control solve) and potential accelerations with lighter backbones.

Questions for the authors
1. Reward/privileged information: How feasible is the approach when ground-truth pose is not available to define the RL cost? Could a self-supervised or proxy reward suffice (e.g., from a tracker or photometric error)?
2. Dynamics generalization: How well do the learned bilinear dynamics transfer across larger visual/domain shifts (e.g., different cities, weather, camera intrinsics)? Any signs of overfitting to 5 training cars?
3. Weight interpretability: Which layers/channels receive high weights? Do weights correlate with semantic objectness or motion sensitivity?
4. Ablation on dynamics: How critical is the locally connected bilinear structure vs fully connected or simple linear models? What is the performance drop if the action-independent term is removed?
5. Sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to the number of FQI iterations, exploration noise, and regularization ν? Do you observe instability without the α,b pre-fit step?
6. End-to-end fine-tuning: Did you attempt fine-tuning VGG features jointly with dynamics or weights (e.g., via differentiable surrogate objectives)? 
7. Practical deployment: What are the main obstacles to real-world deployment (e.g., latency, calibration, depth/scale ambiguities), and how would you address them?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, thoughtfully designed, and empirically convincing contribution that advances sample-efficient visual servoing by leveraging pre-trained features and predictive models in a principled RL framework.
