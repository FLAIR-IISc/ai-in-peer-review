Summary of contributions
- Problem tackled: Learning visual servoing for target following from images, with minimal task-specific data, robustness to distractors/occlusions, and sample efficiency.
- Method: (1) Use pretrained CNN features (modified VGG-16 with reduced pooling/dilations) at multiple scales as the visual representation. (2) Learn multiscale bilinear feature dynamics (locally connected, linear in control) to predict one-step feature evolution under actions. (3) Define a simple one-step servoing controller that picks the control minimizing the Euclidean distance between predicted next-step features and the goal features. (4) Learn nonnegative per-channel, per-scale feature weights and control regularization via a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) scheme with a linear Q approximator whose greedy policy coincides with the servoing objective; an auxiliary step estimates a global scale α and bias b to stabilize training. 
- Empirical results: On a synthetic quadcopter–car-following benchmark (CitySim3D), the method achieves substantially lower costs than IBVS with hand-designed features or tracking, and than model-free deep RL baselines, with two orders of magnitude fewer RL trajectories (∼20 vs. thousands). Mid-level VGG layers (up to conv4_3) generalize better to novel cars than pixels or conv5_3. Code, simulator, and videos are provided.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Novelty is incremental: the approach combines established components (off-the-shelf CNN features, bilinear/locally linear dynamics, one-step visual servoing, and fitted Q-iteration) with a modest algorithmic tweak (α,b step). 
2) Validation is limited to a single simulated domain with privileged-cost supervision; claims of robustness and generalization are not comprehensively substantiated (no real-world experiments, limited ablations).

Supporting arguments
- Specific question/problem: How to build a robust, sample-efficient visual servo for target following by leveraging pretrained visual features and learned dynamics, while avoiding heavy task-specific data or feature engineering.
- Motivation and placement: Clear and well-situated. The paper contrasts with traditional IBVS (needs hand-crafted features and Jacobians) and data-hungry deep RL. The choice to use pretrained CNN features and a simple predictive model is well motivated, citing transferability of ImageNet features and prior work on bilinear dynamics.
- Technical soundness:
  • Dynamics: The locally connected bilinear model linear in controls is appropriate for small camera motions; linearization of the policy objective is exact due to linearity in u. The Jacobian computation via a forward pass is sound.
  • Policy/Q-link: The Q-function parametrization aligns the greedy action with the weighted one-step servoing objective; nonnegativity constraints ensure meaningful weights. The additional α,b calibration for both current and target Q exploits the scale/bias invariance of the argmin; while heuristic, it is coherent and computationally efficient. Regularization is included.
  • RL setup: Dynamics and features are fixed; only weights are learned, which explains the sample efficiency.
- Do results support claims?
  • Sample efficiency: Yes for RL interaction—FQI with ∼20 trajectories outperforms TRPO policies trained on orders-of-magnitude more samples. However, supervised dynamics training uses 10k (xt,ut,xt+1) triplets; the overall data budget advantage over end-to-end methods is less clear.
  • Robustness/generalization: Mid-level VGG features improve performance and transfer to unseen cars. Pixel- and conv5_3-based policies degrade on novel cars, supporting the choice of representation. Robustness to distractors/occlusions is shown qualitatively; quantitative robustness analyses are limited.
  • Baselines: Comparisons include IBVS with ORB points and with a state-of-the-art tracker (C-COT), and end-to-end CNN policies trained with TRPO. The IBVS-tracker baseline is strong and runs slower; the end-to-end policies underperform and are sample-hungry. Some fairness concerns remain (e.g., dynamics data usage, hyperparameter tuning, variance across seeds not reported).
- Clarity and reproducibility: The method is clearly described with equations, training details, and ablations across VGG layers. Code and simulator are available, which supports reproducibility.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Quantify data efficiency holistically by including the supervised dynamics data; show performance vs. number of dynamics triplets and vs. number of RL trajectories.
- Provide more ablations: effect of (i) learned weights vs. uniform, (ii) per-scale vs. shared weights, (iii) locally connected vs. fully connected bilinear models for semantic features (if memory allows), (iv) regularization strength ν, and (v) number of FQI iterations.
- Report statistical variability across random seeds for all baselines; include confidence intervals.
- Analyze the number of learned weight parameters (per layer/scale) relative to the small RL dataset and discuss overfitting controls (beyond L2 and nonnegativity).
- Evaluate robustness systematically (occlusions, lighting changes, viewpoint shifts), not only qualitatively.
- Discuss goal specification: using the first image as x* may be brittle. Consider alternative goal encodings (e.g., object-centric templates, keypoints on the target) and show sensitivity.
- Clarify computational cost breakdown and potential real-time performance on embedded hardware.
- If possible, include a real-robot evaluation or at least a domain-randomized, photorealistic sim to narrow the sim-to-real gap.

Questions for the authors
1) How many weight parameters are learned in the best-performing configuration (total across channels and scales)? How does performance vary with reducing this dimensionality (e.g., group- or layer-wise weights)?
2) The RL cost uses ground-truth target pose (Appendix B). Could this introduce a mismatch that favors your training procedure? How sensitive is the learned weighting to errors in the cost (e.g., from a learned pose estimator)?
3) How does performance scale with the amount of dynamics data (10k triplets)? What are the minimal dynamics data requirements to retain an advantage over end-to-end RL?
4) Can the bilinear dynamics handle larger inter-frame motions or faster camera dynamics? Have you measured prediction error vs. motion magnitude, and its impact on control?
5) To what extent do the learned weights focus on target-related channels? Can you visualize or interpret the channel/scale importance to verify that distractors are down-weighted?
6) How robust is the approach to changes in camera intrinsics/extrinsics or to moderate miscalibration?
7) In FQI, fitting α and b for both current and target Q-functions each iteration changes the target; did you observe stability issues without this step? Any theoretical justification or empirical ablation?
8) Why does conv5_3 underperform—do you have evidence that spatial invariance is the main factor (e.g., measuring spatial sensitivity of feature maps)?

Overall, the paper is clear and technically sound, and the empirical results are promising. Strengthening novelty and broadening/grounding the evaluation would substantially improve its suitability for ICLR.
