Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a simple yet compelling recipe for visual servoing that fuses (i) off-the-shelf CNN features (VGG) modified to preserve spatial information, (ii) a learned bilinear predictive model of feature dynamics, and (iii) a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) scheme that learns per-channel, multi-scale feature weights so the servo focuses on task-relevant signals. The servoing controller is a one-step lookahead that minimizes the Euclidean distance between predicted next-step features and a goal image; due to the bilinear model, this objective is quadratic in the action and admits an analytic solution. The Q-function is constructed to be linear in the same weights used by the servoing objective, so greedy minimization of Q recovers the servo. A neat scaling/bias trick exploits the invariance of the greedy action to positive scaling and bias, yielding a stable, efficient FQI. On a simulated quadrotor car-following benchmark (CitySim3D), the method learns effective policies using only ~20 trajectories for RL (features fixed; dynamics trained from 10k transitions) and outperforms classical IBVS with ORB or tracking, end-to-end CNN policies, and TRPO-tuned variants, while running at ~16 Hz. Code and environment are released.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Novel and elegant integration that turns the servoing objective into a linear Q-approximation, enabling very sample-efficient RL; (2) Strong empirical evidence of sample efficiency and robustness versus baselines, with a simple controller leveraging pre-trained features.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and insight: The paper’s main idea—aligning the control objective, the predictive model, and the Q-parameterization so the greedy action coincides with analytic one-step servo—is both simple and original. The α,b re-scaling step in FQI is a clever stability/efficiency device rarely seen in vision-based control work. The use of pre-trained features with a bilinear feature-dynamics model at multiple scales offers a fresh, pragmatic alternative to either hand-engineered Jacobians or data-hungry end-to-end RL.
- Significance: Visual servoing that can be adapted to new targets with minimal data is important. The results suggest the approach reduces RL sample requirements by orders of magnitude compared to model-free deep RL, without delicate feature engineering.
- Empirical support: The method consistently beats IBVS with ORB and a tracker, and TRPO-based baselines, including variants that learn the same weights. The ablations across VGG layers are informative: mid-level features (conv1_2–conv4_3) generalize to novel cars, while conv5_3 is too invariant—supporting the design choices. The runtime analysis is adequate, and the release of code/env enhances credibility and reproducibility.
- Methodological clarity: The optimization details, linearization (exact for bilinear), and constraints (non-negative weights) are clear. The RL setting and datasets are described in sufficient detail, and the discussion is grounded in relevant literature.

Caveats (not blocking)
- External validity: All results are in simulation, with a task-specific cost using privileged simulator signals (3D car pose, FOV checks) and early termination. While standard for sim studies, it leaves open how well the learned weighting transfers with real sensors and real reward proxies.
- Fairness of comparisons: The sample-efficiency claim is partly enabled by pre-trained features and a separately trained dynamics model (10k transitions). Model-free baselines do not leverage this privileged structure; a model-based TRPO/MPC baseline with the same bilinear model could strengthen the claim.
- Learned weighting granularity: The approach learns per-channel, per-scale weights, not spatially varying weights. This is elegant and low-dimensional but may limit disambiguation in highly cluttered scenes where spatial attention is needed.
- Scope: The paper studies a single task (following in CitySim3D). Generalization beyond novel cars to broader target/object categories or tasks (e.g., docking/manipulation) is not demonstrated.

Suggestions to improve (for authors)
- Add a real-data experiment or at least a sim-to-real proxy (e.g., photorealistic sim or real video replays) to assess robustness of the pre-trained features + bilinear dynamics combo under sensor noise and calibration errors.
- Report an ablation on the FQI α,b initialization trick: Does it materially improve convergence/sample efficiency vs. vanilla FQI with the same linear Q parameterization?
- Include a model-based baseline that uses the same bilinear dynamics in an MPC or iLQR loop without the RL weighting (or with weights tuned by CEM), to isolate the contribution of Q-learned weights to long-horizon performance.
- Examine spatial weighting or simple attention over feature maps to better handle distractors and partial occlusions.
- Clarify the role of the privileged cost: show results with a purely image-based surrogate reward (e.g., optical-flow or detection-based proxy) to indicate feasibility without simulator pose.
- Explore other backbones (e.g., ResNet features, shallower networks) to reduce compute and test sensitivity to feature invariances.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is performance to errors in the bilinear dynamics? Can you report servo performance as a function of dynamics training set size and model class (fully vs locally connected) for semantic features?
2) Does the α,b joint fitting step in FQI change the fixed points or just speed convergence? Any theoretical or empirical evidence of stability improvements vs. standard target networks?
3) What fraction of channels receive non-negligible weights after training? Do the learned weights concentrate on “car-like” channels consistently across targets, and how stable are they when the target identity changes?
4) How would the method handle a goal image that is not the first frame (e.g., provided by a user or database), especially with mild domain shift? Any results on re-targeting within an episode?
5) Can you report results with an image-based reward only (no pose/FOV oracle) and discuss the resulting sample complexity and performance?
6) How does performance degrade when the target leaves the FOV for several steps and re-enters? Does the bilinear predictor or the weighting mitigate drift in these cases?
7) Did you try learning spatially varying weights or channel groups to improve selectivity? If so, what was the compute/sample trade-off?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, elegant, and practically impactful contribution. Despite limitations in scope and evaluation, the core idea is novel and promising, and I recommend acceptance.
