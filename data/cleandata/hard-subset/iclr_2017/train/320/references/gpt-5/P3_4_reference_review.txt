1. Summary of contributions
The paper tackles visual servoing without hand-engineered features or calibrated Jacobians by combining: (i) fixed, pre-trained convolutional features (VGG, modified to reduce spatial invariance); (ii) a learned, per-scale bilinear feature dynamics model (locally connected per-channel variant) that predicts next-step feature maps given action; and (iii) a sample-efficient reinforcement learning procedure that learns non-negative weights over feature channels/scales and control penalties via a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) scheme. The policy is an analytically solvable one-step lookahead controller that minimizes the weighted squared distance between predicted next-step features and a target feature map. The Q-function approximator is linear in the weights, enabling efficient FQI with a special step to jointly calibrate scale α and bias b. Experiments in a synthetic quadrotor–car-following benchmark (CitySim3D) compare multiple feature layers, pixel intensities, TRPO/CEM training of weights, end-to-end CNN policies, and classical IBVS/PBVS baselines. The method reportedly achieves strong performance and two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency in RL data compared to model-free TRPO, with code and simulator released.

2. Decision
Recommendation: Reject.
Key reasons:
- Limited novelty and scope: the core elements (pretrained features, bilinear first-order dynamics, and fitted Q with a linear approximator) are individually established; the contribution is a pragmatic combination with a lightweight RL layer (global weights), not a substantial methodological advance for ICLR.
- Evaluation relies on simulation and privileged information: the RL cost uses 3D ground-truth target pose and early-termination logic unavailable in realistic deployments; dynamics are trained from 100 hand-coded trajectories (10k triplets), which undercuts the sample-efficiency claim that emphasizes only the 20 RL trajectories.

3. Supporting arguments
- Positioning and motivation: The paper is well written, and the design choices are reasonable: using intermediate VGG layers to retain spatial detail, a locally connected bilinear dynamics that is exact in u and easy to differentiate, and an analytically solvable one-step controller are all sound. The observation that conv4_3 strikes a balance between invariance and spatial precision is consistent with prior findings. The FQI formulation that treats the servo objective as a Q-approximator linear in non-negative weights is elegant and computationally efficient.
- Novelty: Bilinear models for image/feature dynamics and the use of pretrained CNN features are known; fitted Q-iteration with linear approximators is also standard. The novelty lies in (a) embedding the servoing objective as a Q-function with non-negative weights and (b) a practical two-stage optimization with α, b calibration. This is incremental and domain-specific rather than a general RL/representation learning contribution.
- Experimental realism and claims: While the method uses only ~20 RL trajectories, it depends on a sizable supervised dataset for dynamics (100 trajectories) collected with a hand-coded policy. The sample-efficiency claim should be framed as “RL data efficiency given a pre-learned dynamics model,” not a total data-efficiency win. Moreover, the reward shaping uses ground-truth 3D positions and FOV/termination conditions (Appendix B), which are unlikely to be available in real deployments; this weakens the claim of easy adaptation to new targets. Many baselines are favorable (e.g., IBVS given ORB or tracker; end-to-end CNN with TRPO) but still in the same simulation; no real-world results are provided.
- Generalization: The generalization test is limited to novel cars within the same simulator and visual domain. There is no test of cross-domain generalization (textures, lighting beyond CitySim3D, different targets, or camera intrinsics), nor ablations on the amount of dynamics data required, or robustness to dynamics mismatch (e.g., different UAV dynamics).
- Scope of learned weights: The weights w(l)c and λj are global (state-independent). This is a strong simplification; an attention-like, context-dependent weighting could be important in the presence of occluders or clutter. The present choice aids tractability but limits adaptability. The paper notes occlusion robustness qualitatively but does not quantify failure modes or show performance under strong distractors across scenes.
- Missing empirical diagnostics: There is little analysis of prediction quality of the bilinear model (per-scale errors, dependence on action magnitude), or of how planning depth vs one-step lookahead trade off. It would be helpful to see what fraction of performance gain comes from dynamics vs weights vs pretrained features via ablations.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Reframe claims: Clearly separate the supervised dynamics data budget from the RL data budget, and report total sample cost. Provide wall-clock training/compute for all methods, not only Hz at test time.
- Replace privileged costs with image-derived rewards (e.g., photometric alignment, learned discriminators) to strengthen real-world plausibility. Alternatively, demonstrate that RL with your FQI can be driven by self-supervised image costs without 3D ground truth.
- Add real-robot experiments or at least a hardware-in-the-loop setup to validate sim-to-real feasibility and latency constraints.
- Provide ablations: (i) dynamics data size vs performance; (ii) bilinear vs simpler linear vs more expressive predictors; (iii) effect of allowing state-dependent weights (e.g., a small network gating the channel weights) on robustness to occlusion/distractors; (iv) multi-step model predictive control vs one-step lookahead.
- Include predictive model metrics (per-layer MAE/PSNR/flow-like proxies) and relate them to control performance to justify the bilinear assumption.
- Expand baselines: a model-based RL with learned dynamics and MPC (e.g., shooting with the same bilinear model) and modern off-policy actor-critic (with representation learning) on equal data budgets. Also compare against photometric IBVS directly optimized with Gauss-Newton and robust loss.
- Investigate alternative pre-trained features (ResNet, self-supervised representations), and study sensitivity to the VGG modifications (removing pooling vs dilation).
- Analyze failure cases (e.g., heavy occlusion, sudden target loss) and recovery behaviors.

Questions for the authors
- Reward design: How would your FQI weighting be learned without access to ground-truth 3D positions and FOV flags? Can you show an experiment with a purely image-based cost and similar performance?
- Data accounting: What is the total number of frames/actions used to train dynamics and weights? How does performance degrade if the dynamics dataset is reduced to 10 trajectories? Is the RL sample efficiency claim robust under a fixed total data budget across methods?
- State-dependent weighting: Did you try making weights a function of (xt, x*) (e.g., a small MLP over feature statistics) while keeping the same linear-in-parameters Q-form? Does this help in scenes with multiple similar objects?
- Dynamics model validity: How accurate are one-step predictions as a function of action magnitude and scene motion (moving car, occluders)? Does multi-step roll-out degrade rapidly? Any benefit from partial unrolling in the planning objective?
- Generalization: Can the approach follow non-car targets or transfer across different cities/textures without re-training dynamics? What happens when camera intrinsics differ from those used to train the bilinear model?
- Baselines: For photometric IBVS, did you include robust losses and multi-scale alignment? For TRPO baselines, did you tune observation normalization and network capacity comparably to your features?

Overall, this is a well-executed and clearly written study that demonstrates a practical and efficient pipeline in simulation. With stronger evidence on realism (non-privileged rewards, real hardware) and broader ablations, it could become a compelling contribution.
