Summary and contributions
- The paper addresses sample-efficient visual servoing for target following by combining pretrained visual features, a learned predictive feature dynamics model, and reinforcement learning to select feature weights for control. The central idea is to servo in the space of multiscale VGG feature maps, using a bilinear dynamics model to predict how actions change features, and then choose actions by one-step lookahead to minimize weighted feature distance to a goal image. The weights over feature channels and scales, and action regularization, are learned with a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) scheme that exploits the structure of the servoing objective to obtain a linear Q-approximation and efficient learning.
- Key technical elements: (i) multi-scale VGG features with dilated convolutions (to keep spatial info), (ii) bilinear feature dynamics at each scale with exact linearization in controls, (iii) an analytic, linear-in-parameters Q-function whose greedy action is exactly the servoing action, (iv) an efficient FQI routine with a useful α, b scaling step that stabilizes learning and enforces nonnegativity constraints on weights.
- Empirically, on a simulated quadrotor-car following benchmark (CitySim3D), the method learns effective policies with very few RL trajectories (≈20), significantly outperforming pixel-based servoing, IBVS with ORB features, and model-free TRPO policies (both feature-based and end-to-end from pixels) in both performance and sample efficiency. The method generalizes better than pixels to novel cars and runs in real time (~16 Hz), with code and environment released.

Decision: Accept
Reason: The paper presents a well-motivated, novel combination of representation transfer, simple predictive dynamics, and a tailored, sample-efficient RL algorithm that yields strong empirical gains and clear insights for visual servoing. Despite being simulation-only, the approach is principled, practical, and likely to influence follow-up work on data-efficient visuomotor control.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Robust, sample-efficient visual servoing without hand-designed visual features and Jacobians, with quick adaptation to new targets. The paper directly addresses the challenge of using high-level features for control while coping with distractors and occlusions.
- Motivation and placement in literature: Strong. The paper bridges classical IBVS/PBVS (feature design and interaction matrices) with modern deep features and video prediction, and contrasts to model-free deep RL that is data-hungry. It builds on bilinear models for image dynamics and fitted Q-iteration, and clearly explains why pretrained features help, why a bilinear model suffices for short-horizon control, and why learning feature weights matters for picking out the relevant object.
- Do results support claims? Yes, within the scope. There are thorough comparisons: across feature layers (conv1_2 to conv5_3), pixels vs VGG, fully vs locally connected dynamics for pixels, and multiple baselines (IBVS with ORB, tracker-based IBVS, end-to-end TRPO from pixels/VGG, TRPO learning the same servo weights, CEM). The reported sample efficiency (≈2 sampling iterations × 10 trajectories each) for the RL phase is compelling, and the advantage over TRPO is large even when TRPO uses 100× more samples. Generalization to novel cars is demonstrated; conv4_3 emerges as a sweet spot balancing invariance and spatial specificity. The rationale for a linear Q approximator matching the servo objective is elegant and empirically effective.

Strengths
- Elegant algorithmic design that exploits structure: pretrained features, exact linearization of bilinear dynamics, and a Q-parametrization that yields the same greedy action as the servoing objective.
- Strong sample efficiency and compute efficiency relative to model-free baselines; clear ablations across feature layers; comparisons with classical IBVS/tracker.
- Practicality: open-source simulator and code; real-time execution; simple training pipelines (supervised dynamics + small-batch RL for weights).
- Insightful negative/positive results: deeper VGG features (conv5_3) become too invariant; conv4_3 best; pixels brittle to occlusions/distractors.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Simulation-only; no real robot experiments or domain transfer study. The cost function for RL uses ground-truth relative pose, which is not directly available from onboard sensing; while acceptable for training in sim, it limits claims about real-world deployability.
- Scope is narrow (single task family: car following from a quadrotor view). It’s unclear how the approach scales to different tasks/targets without retuning and how robust the learned weights are across objects/backgrounds with drastically different statistics.
- The bilinear dynamics choice is justified and efficient, but analysis of its fidelity vs more expressive models (e.g., short-horizon planning with learned dynamics/MPC) is limited. The method uses one-step lookahead; a comparison to short-horizon MPC in feature space with learned dynamics would strengthen the story.
- Some implementation clarity gaps: which bilinear variant is used for each VGG layer (fully vs locally connected), parameter counts/memory, and how local connectivity constraints affect performance for semantic features.

Questions for the authors
1. Reward specification: RL costs rely on ground-truth car pose in the camera frame. How do you envision specifying rewards in the real world? Could you approximate the cost in feature space (e.g., via learned distance to a goal manifold) and would FQI still work?
2. Dynamics variants: For VGG features, which bilinear variant did you use (fully vs locally connected), and what are the parameter counts and training times? Any performance or stability differences across variants at higher layers?
3. Planning horizon: Did you try short-horizon planning (e.g., CEM over action sequences using your learned dynamics) in the weighted feature space? How does it compare to one-step greedy control in performance and robustness?
4. Generalization: How sensitive are the learned weights to the set of training cars/scenes? If you reweight on one set, how well does the same θ transfer to unseen targets?
5. Data efficiency decomposition: You report ~20 trajectories for RL, but the dynamics model uses 100 trajectories. How does performance degrade if you reduce dynamics data? Any signs of compounding model bias in long rollouts?
6. Failure modes: Can you characterize typical failures (e.g., severe occlusions, lighting extremes, similar distractors) and how feature layer choice or weight regularization affects them?

Suggestions for improvement (helpful, not decisive)
- Add a real-robot or sim-to-real pilot, or at least a study with learned reward proxies that do not rely on privileged pose.
- Include a baseline with MPC over a short horizon using the same learned dynamics and features to contextualize the benefits of the FQI-weighted one-step policy.
- Provide more details on dynamics model architecture per layer, parameter sizes, and training resources; include a correlation between prediction error and control performance.
- Expand ablations on the weighting: sparsity or group lasso to encourage channel selection; visualize which channels/scales get high weights and whether they correspond to car-sensitive maps.
- Clarify the choice of layers and the dilated VGG modifications; report feature computation times and consider lighter backbones to show tradeoffs.
- Clean up references and minor formatting issues.
