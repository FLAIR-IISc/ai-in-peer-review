1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper addresses visual servoing for target following by combining (i) pre-trained convolutional visual features (from a modified VGG-16), (ii) learned multiscale bilinear predictive models of feature dynamics, and (iii) a sample-efficient reinforcement learning procedure to learn feature and control weights. The servoing policy is a simple one-step lookahead that minimizes the Euclidean distance between predicted next-step features and the goal features. The key insight is to parameterize the Q-function linearly in the feature/channel/scale weights so that the greedy action w.r.t. Q coincides with the servoing policy; this yields a tractable fitted Q-iteration (FQI) scheme with an analytic min over actions and a lightweight optimization over weights. The dynamics are learned from triplets (x_t, u_t, x_{t+1}) via a bilinear model (locally connected variant for efficiency), while the feature and control weights are learned via FQI using trajectory costs defined from ground-truth target pose. Experiments on a synthetic quadrotor–car following benchmark show: (a) policies using mid-level VGG features (up to conv4_3) outperform pixel-based servoing and classical IBVS with ORB or tracker-derived points, (b) feature weighting learned by FQI substantially improves over no weighting and TRPO-learned weights with over two orders of magnitude fewer samples, and (c) some generalization to novel car models. Code, simulator, and videos are provided.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The paper presents a clear, technically sound formulation that ties a one-step predictive servoing objective to a linear Q-function approximator, enabling an efficient and stable variant of fitted Q-iteration with strong empirical sample efficiency.
- Empirical results support the main claims: substantial improvements over pixel/keypoint-based servoing and large sample-efficiency gains over model-free deep RL baselines, with ablations across feature layers and weighting strategies.

3. Supporting arguments
Clarity:
- The problem setup, policy form, bilinear dynamics, multiscale feature hierarchy, and FQI procedure are described with sufficient detail. Equations are explicit, and appendices provide optimization details and cost definitions. The release of code and simulator aids reproducibility.

Technical soundness:
- The bilinear model yields exact linearization in controls, making the one-step servoing objective quadratic in actions and enabling closed-form minimization and efficient Jacobian computation. The linear Q parameterization ensures the greedy policy coincides with the servoing objective, justifying the FQI design.
- The optimization trick that rescales θ by α and adjusts bias b to stabilize Bellman regression is reasonable for this linear family; while no theory is provided, the construction preserves the argmin over actions and empirically converges with L2 regularization.
- Non-negativity constraints on weights avoid sign flips that would alter the servoing objective semantics and preserve policy invariances under scaling.

Novelty:
- Using pre-trained features for servoing is not standard; combining them with learned bilinear feature dynamics and a Q-function that exactly mirrors the one-step servoing objective is a neat, principled integration. The sample-efficient FQI over feature/control weights (while freezing features and dynamics) is a practical contribution for quickly adapting to new targets.

Empirical support:
- Comparisons include: pixels vs VGG feature layers (conv1_2–conv5_3), with conv4_3 best; FQI-learned weights vs no weighting and TRPO-learned weights; end-to-end TRPO from pixels; and classical IBVS/tracker baselines (with depth provided). Policies are evaluated on both seen and novel cars, with consistent test sets. Reported runtime indicates practical control rates.
- Claims about sample efficiency are supported by using 20 trajectories for FQI vs tens of thousands of samples for TRPO, while achieving better costs.

Caveats:
- Experiments are limited to a synthetic environment; transfer to real-world systems is untested.
- The RL cost uses privileged ground-truth car pose during training; this may not be available in practice.
- Baseline fairness: the TRPO policies operate in a larger hypothesis class and require many more samples; additional strong model-based or actor-critic baselines could strengthen the comparison.
- Uncertainty in dynamics and robustness to significant domain shift are not addressed.

Overall, the central claims (sample-efficient learning of robust visual servoing via pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, and FQI-learned weights) are supported within the presented domain.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all decision-critical)
- Clarify in the main text that the trajectory cost for RL uses ground-truth target pose (privileged information), and discuss feasible surrogates (e.g., learned pose/flow, self-supervised image-space proxies) for real deployment.
- Include ablations on: number of trajectories for dynamics training vs RL weighting; effect of miscalibrated dynamics; locally connected vs fully connected bilinear models (parameter counts, accuracy, control performance).
- Report variance across random seeds for FQI and TRPO to quantify stability.
- Evaluate additional pre-trained backbones (e.g., ResNet, MobileNet) and discuss trade-offs between speed and performance.
- Consider uncertainty-aware or regularized weighting (e.g., sparsity, group penalties across channels/scales) and analyze learned weight distributions to interpret which features are selected.
- Provide an oracle that uses the same action budget but with perfect knowledge of target pose to bound achievable performance.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the amount of dynamics training data and to model mis-specification? Does reducing the 10k triplets materially degrade servoing?
- The RL cost uses ground-truth 3D pose. What surrogate costs could be used without privileged state, and how would this affect sample efficiency and performance?
- Are the learned weights target-specific or reusable across targets? If per-target, what is the adaptation cost for a new target with limited data?
- Can you quantify the relative contributions of dynamics accuracy vs weight learning (e.g., using perfect dynamics with random weights, and vice versa)?
- How does the method perform when multiple similar distractors are present (e.g., several cars), and what mechanisms help the policy focus on the intended target?
- Did you try negative weights or other constraints/priors (e.g., sparsity)? How critical is the non-negativity constraint for stability or performance?
- Could you report sensitivity to exploration noise σ and discount γ, and provide learning curves with confidence intervals?
- What is the computational profile per control step (feature extraction vs Jacobian vs Q-minimization), and how would a lighter backbone change throughput?
