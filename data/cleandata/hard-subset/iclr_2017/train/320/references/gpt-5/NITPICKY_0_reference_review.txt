Summary and contributions
- The paper studies visual servoing for target following by combining (i) pre-trained visual features (VGG-16 with modified pooling/dilations), (ii) a learned bilinear predictive feature-dynamics model (locally connected or per-channel fully connected) at multiple scales, and (iii) a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) scheme to learn nonnegative weights over feature channels/scales and action regularizers. The control policy is a one-step greedy servo minimizing predicted feature error to a given target feature map.
- Key claims: (1) robustness vs. distractors/occlusion through pre-trained features and weighting; (2) sample efficiency (circa 20 RL trajectories for weighting) enabled by the analytic structure of the Q-function; (3) superior performance in a synthetic quadrotor car-following benchmark vs. baselines (pixels, hand-designed keypoints, a tracker-based IBVS, and TRPO-based policies); (4) some generalization to novel cars.
- Code and simulator are released; appendices describe linearization (exact due to bilinearity) and a hand-crafted cost based on car pose in the camera frame.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons:
1) Limited novelty relative to prior art; the components (pre-trained features, bilinear image/feature dynamics, fitted Q-iteration) are each known, and their combination here is incremental.
2) Experimental support is not sufficiently rigorous: results are confined to a synthetic environment; the reward/cost uses ground-truth 3D car pose not available from images; several baselines (and training budgets) appear mismatched; sample-efficiency claims omit the substantial supervised dynamics data.
3) Numerous clarity and presentation issues (citation formatting, missing figure details/placeholders, incomplete methodological specifics) reduce confidence in reproducibility and scientific polish.

Supporting arguments
Addressing the three key questions:
1) Problem: Learn a robust, sample-efficient visual servoing controller that can adapt to new targets with little task-specific data by exploiting pre-trained features and learned predictive dynamics.
2) Motivation and positioning: The motivation to avoid hand-engineered features/Jacobians is clear and well-grounded in the servoing and RL literature. However, the paper leans heavily on standard building blocks (VGG features, bilinear dynamics approximations, fitted Q-iteration), and the choice to learn only linear weights over feature errors is a narrow design. Related work is reasonably covered, but several important adjacent lines (e.g., modern image-based servoing leveraging deep optical flow or learned dense correspondences; model-based RL with learned latent dynamics for control from images) could be better contrasted.
3) Evidence and rigor:
- The reward uses ground-truth 3D car pose in the camera frame (Appendix B), which is unavailable from the observation alone. While permissible in simulation, this makes claims of sample efficiency for “RL from vision” less convincing and complicates fair comparison to baselines that do not exploit such privileged signals.
- Sample efficiency is emphasized as “20 trajectories,” but the approach also uses 10,000 supervised triplets to learn dynamics (100 trajectories × 100 steps). The abstract/conclusions should disaggregate RL vs. supervised data to avoid overclaiming.
- Baseline fairness/budgets: TRPO baselines use 4000 samples/iter for up to 50 iters; the proposed method also benefits from a strong model and pre-trained features plus 10k dynamics samples. It’s unclear whether model-based baselines (e.g., shallow model-predictive control over the bilinear model without FQI weighting, or FQI with raw pixels but comparable model capacity) were tested. The CNN policy on VGG features “performs worse” than pixels—this suggests the proposed VGG features may not be ideal in end-to-end settings; more ablations would help.
- Generalization: Tests on novel cars are promising for conv1_2–conv4_3; however, no domain shift beyond the CitySim3D visuals is shown; robustness to lighting, weather, and camera perturbations is not quantified.
- No real-robot or real-world dataset evaluation; even a sim-to-real qualitative test would strengthen impact.
- No statistical significance reporting; only mean costs are reported. Success rates, variance, and sensitivity analyses are missing.

Further methodological and presentation concerns
- Q-function/optimization: The two-step FQI with joint scaling α and bias b is nonstandard; there is no convergence or stability justification. The non-negativity constraints on θ, choice of ν, and sensitivity are only briefly mentioned.
- Feature dynamics: Important details are missing: kernel sizes nf, neighborhood extents, number of scales L, channels retained per layer, choice criteria for conv layers, and memory/computation footprints. The rationale for locally connected vs. per-channel fully connected is not quantitatively analyzed (trade-offs in accuracy vs. parameter count vs. speed).
- Control solving: Equation (3) is quadratic in u with exact linearization; yet no explicit closed-form is shown, and numerical stability/conditioning are not discussed.
- Cost shaping and termination: The early termination and cost spikes (Appendix B) may bias training; sensitivity to τ, γ, and horizon T is not analyzed.
- Formatting: Many references contain placeholder tokens (“?, \Q2014\E”), inconsistent formatting, and missing bibliographic fields. There are stray placeholders (“Feature Dynamics Observations from Test Executions Cost”). Figures/tables referenced in text are not present here; ensure all are complete and legible.
- Language: Several typos (e.g., “standarized”), inconsistent notation (vectors vs. scalars), and spacing around math.

Constructive feedback and questions for authors
- Please clarify the data accounting: report supervised dynamics data separately from RL trajectories in all efficiency claims; add ablations varying the amount of dynamics data (e.g., 1k/5k/10k triplets) and show how performance scales.
- Justify the use of privileged pose for reward: Can you define a purely image-based cost (e.g., keypoint reprojection, learned discriminator) and show similar conclusions?
- Baseline fairness:
  - Add a model-based MPC baseline using your bilinear dynamics without learned weighting; also add FQI over pixels with a comparable dynamics model.
  - Equalize training budgets or present sample-performance curves with total environment steps on the x-axis, including dynamics data.
- Provide ablations:
  - With/without multi-scale; with/without weighting (single w); per-layer contributions.
  - Locally connected vs. per-channel FC dynamics accuracy and runtime vs. servo performance.
  - Different pre-trained backbones (ResNet, smaller nets) and effect on runtime (fps) and performance.
- Theory: Elaborate on the α, b fitting step—does it decrease Bellman error monotonically? Any contraction properties under your linear feature map? Empirical stability diagnostics across seeds?
- Realism: Evaluate under camera calibration errors, motion blur, lighting changes, partial occlusions, distractors leaving the FOV; report robustness metrics and failure modes.
- Deployment: Provide a real-robot or at least real-video test (offline servo replay) to show feasibility beyond synthetic data.
- Implementation details to add: nf, L, selected layers, number of channels used per layer, training times, memory, solver conditioning, and the exact closed-form for u in Eq. (3).

With stronger experimental rigor (especially fair baselines, real-world components, and clearer presentation) this line of work could have solid impact; as is, the paper falls short of ICLR standards.
