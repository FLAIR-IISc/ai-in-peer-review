Summary and contributions
The paper tackles sample-efficient visual servoing without manual feature engineering or camera calibration by combining (i) frozen, pre-trained deep features (VGG with modified pooling/dilation), (ii) learned one-step predictive feature dynamics via a bilinear model that is linear in control, and (iii) a reinforcement-learning procedure (a tailored fitted Q-iteration) to learn per-channel, per-scale feature weights and control regularization for a simple greedy one-step servo objective. The policy minimizes a weighted Euclidean distance between predicted next-step features and a goal feature vector, and the linear-in-parameters Q-approximation enables efficient, stable FQI with a normalization step that exploits scaling invariance. Experiments in a 3D quadrotor-car following simulator show that: deep features up to VGG conv4_3 outperform pixels and conv5_3, the proposed FQI is >100x more sample-efficient than TRPO when learning the weights, and the method outperforms classical IBVS using ORB or a state-of-the-art tracker, and outperforms end-to-end CNN RL baselines. Code, simulator, and videos are provided.

Decision: Reject (borderline)
Primary reasons:
1) Evaluation is limited to simulation with reliance on privileged cost signals (ground-truth target pose in camera frame) and does not include any real-world evidence, which weakens claims of practical robustness and sample efficiency for servoing.
2) Methodological novelty is incremental: the core policy is a standard one-step predictive servo with frozen features; the main learning component is a tailored linear FQI over hand-crafted features φ(s,u), with fixed global weights shared across states. While neat and effective, this feels more like a well-engineered combination than a substantive algorithmic advance for ICLR.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The paper is well situated between traditional IBVS/PBVS and model-free deep RL, with a compelling target-following use case. The choice to leverage pre-trained features for invariances while learning dynamics and weights is sensible and well motivated. The related work is broadly adequate; discussion of dense alignment on deep feature pyramids (e.g., deep Lucas–Kanade, feature-space photometric alignment) could be expanded.

- Technical soundness:
  - The bilinear feature-dynamics model, linear in u with locally connected parameters and per-channel independence, is appropriate for small inter-frame motions and enables exact Jacobians and efficient servo optimization. The justification via image-warping bilinearity and Censi & Murray is reasonable, though for deep features the bilinear assumption is heuristic; more analysis of prediction error vs. motion magnitude would help.
  - The Q-function design Qθ,b(s,u)=φ(s,u)^Tθ+b tightly couples the value approximation to the one-step predictive servo objective, ensuring that greedy action selection recovers the servo. The scaling/bias normalization step (solving for α, b jointly for current and target networks) is a nice stabilization trick.
  - A key limitation is that θ are global, state-independent weights per feature channel/scale and per control. This precludes spatial attention and context-dependent feature selection, which is likely important in clutter and under occlusion.

- Empirical support:
  - Positives: Clear ablations across VGG layers; comparisons to TRPO both for learning θ and end-to-end CNN policies; comparisons to classical IBVS (keypoints, tracker) and PBVS. Sample efficiency advantages are significant for learning θ (20 trajectories), although it should be emphasized that the dynamics model is trained on 10k triplets (100 trajectories) generated by a hand-coded controller near the task distribution.
  - Concerns:
    - Reward uses ground-truth 3D car pose in the camera frame and early termination based on FOV, which is not available in most real settings; the practicality of designing an equivalent reward without privileged information is not addressed.
    - All results are in a single synthetic environment with fixed camera intrinsics and motion characteristics; robustness to sensor noise, lighting changes beyond those in simulator, and dynamics/model mismatch is unclear.
    - The “tracker-based IBVS” baseline uses a strong tracker but the discussion around whether Jacobians incorporate target dynamics is inconsistent across the main text and appendix. Please clarify which variant is plotted in the main figures.
    - The method shows generalization to novel cars, but both training and testing are in-domain (same simulator, textures/roads). Out-of-distribution generalization (e.g., domain shifts, new backgrounds) is not shown.

- Novelty and significance:
  - The combination—frozen ImageNet features, bilinear feature dynamics, and a linear Q-approximation tuned to a one-step servo objective—is practical and well executed, but each component is known. The FQI variant uses a straightforward linear regressor with a scaling/bias re-fit; useful but not a major advance. The main conceptual contribution is to show that re-weighting deep features learned for classification, combined with a learned predictive model, suffices for robust servoing with very low RL data, which is interesting but incremental.

Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not decisive)
- Real-world validation: Even a small-scale on-robot experiment (e.g., tabletop IBVS with a moving target, or a drone following a car/person outdoors) would greatly strengthen the impact and address concerns about privileged rewards and simulation bias.
- Reward design without privileged pose: Discuss and/or demonstrate alternatives (self-supervised objectives, learned success detectors, event-based costs, distance-to-template via features) to avoid relying on ground-truth pose during RL.
- State-dependent attention: Extend the weighting to learn spatial masks or state-conditional weights (e.g., small network producing w(s)) while preserving tractability (e.g., quadratic surrogate or alternating minimization). Even a per-layer gate conditioned on global pooled features could help.
- Dynamics analysis: Quantify prediction error vs. action magnitude and show how dynamics error impacts control performance. Compare bilinear vs. learned optical-flow-based warping in feature space.
- Baseline breadth: Add comparisons to dense photometric servo in deep feature space (alignment via Gauss–Newton on conv features), and to modern keypoint detectors/learned descriptors.
- Clarity/details: Provide precise architectural details (which VGG layers used at each scale, nf, number of channels retained per layer, downsampling specifics), parameter counts, and runtime breakdown.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is performance to dynamics model mis-specification? What happens if the hand-coded exploration policy generates a distribution mismatch (e.g., faster target, different altitude)?
2) Are the learned weights θ shared across all tasks/targets, or re-learned per target? Can θ generalize to following different object categories (e.g., buses) without re-training?
3) Can you clarify which IBVS variants (with/without car dynamics in the Jacobian) are reported in the main results?
4) How large is the local neighborhood nf used in the locally connected dynamics? Did you try flow-based warping in feature space?
5) Could the reward be computed from vision signals alone (e.g., bounding box size/centroid stability), and how would that affect FQI?
6) Why not fine-tune the feature extractor end-to-end with the dynamics loss (or joint with RL) to reduce the reliance on fixed VGG invariances? Did you attempt this, and did it hurt sample efficiency?

Overall, this is a thoughtful and well-executed systems paper with promising empirical results, but limited novelty and lack of real-world validation lead me to a (borderline) rejection.
