Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles learning visual servoing for target following by combining pre-trained convolutional features (VGG), a learned bilinear model of feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning procedure to select feature weights. The control law is a simple one-step lookahead that minimizes the predicted feature-space distance to a goal image, yielding a closed-form quadratic minimization in the action.
- Core contributions:
  1) Use of multi-scale, spatially preserved pre-trained features for visual servoing, with modifications (removing pooling, dilated convs) to retain spatial detail.
  2) A bilinear, locally connected predictive model of feature dynamics that is exact in control and efficient to evaluate (analytic Jacobian).
  3) A fitted Q-iteration approach with a linear Q-parameterization in the feature weights (and control regularization), together with a neat α-scaling/bias fitting trick that stabilizes training and preserves the greedy action, enabling very high sample efficiency.
- Empirically, on a nontrivial simulated quadcopter car-following benchmark (CitySim3D), the method achieves substantially lower cost and >100× better sample efficiency than standard deep RL baselines (e.g., TRPO on pixels or on features), and outperforms classical IBVS with ORB features and a tracker-based IBVS baseline. It generalizes to novel cars within the simulator. Code, simulator, and videos are provided.

Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) Clear, original integration of pre-trained features with a bilinear feature-dynamics model and a tailored, linear-in-parameters Q-function that enables highly sample-efficient RL; (2) Strong empirical gains over relevant model-free and classical baselines with very little RL data, plus open-source artifacts that enhance impact.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and boldness: While bilinear image dynamics and fitted Q-iteration exist, their combination with pre-trained CNN features for visual servoing and the specific Q-approximation/weight-learning design is new and elegant. The analytic structure directly shapes the policy, contrasting with end-to-end black-box policies.
- Motivation and placement: The paper positions itself well between classical VS (feature Jacobians, calibration, hand-engineering) and data-hungry deep RL/video prediction, arguing for using invariances of pre-trained features while learning short-horizon feature dynamics and long-horizon weighting. Related work is covered broadly and fairly.
- Empirical support: 
  - Systematic comparisons across feature layers (conv1_2 to conv5_3) show the importance of spatial detail and suggest conv4_3 as a sweet spot.
  - Clear ablations on weighting methods (none/CEM/TRPO) demonstrate the role of FQI and its sample efficiency (2 sampling iterations, 10 trajectories each) versus TRPO (up to 50 iterations, 4000 samples/iter).
  - Comparisons to IBVS variants and tracker-based baselines are informative; the method runs significantly faster than the tracking-based policy.
  - Protocol details (fixed validation/test sets, seen vs novel cars) improve rigor and reproducibility.
- Practicality: The method is simple at runtime (one-step prediction + quadratic action selection), fast enough for control rates (~16 Hz), and relies on features available off-the-shelf.

Caveats (I am willing to overlook given the novelty and promise)
- All results are in simulation; no real-robot validation or sim2real analysis. Costs and early terminations rely on privileged car pose for training, which may be hard to obtain in the real world.
- The dynamics model is trained from hand-coded policy data around cars; sensitivity to this data, to the amount of dynamics data, and to choice of pre-trained backbone is not extensively analyzed.
- The focus is on a single domain (car following). Broader tasks or targets would further support generality.
- The theoretical side of the FQI variant (convergence/approximation properties under the linear-in-weights Q form) is not analyzed.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Clarify data requirements: quantify performance vs number of dynamics training trajectories and vs number of RL trajectories; add a small sample-efficiency curve for both stages.
- Report memory/parameter counts and filter neighborhood sizes for the locally connected bilinear model; discuss regularization and overfitting prevention for dynamics.
- Add robustness analyses: heavy occlusions, distractor vehicles of similar appearance, lighting shifts; maybe a domain randomization test.
- Compare to a simple model-based baseline (e.g., iLQR or MPC) that uses the same learned feature dynamics and costs, to isolate the value of the FQI weighting versus multi-step planning.
- Explore different backbones (e.g., ResNet, self-supervised features) and whether modest fine-tuning of feature layers improves dynamics or control.
- Discuss how one could remove privileged costs in practice (e.g., learned reward from video, self-supervised curvatures, or success indicators).

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is performance to the choice of pre-trained features? Have you tried ResNet backbones or self-supervised features that may better preserve spatial detail?
2) What are the nf and channel dimensions used in the locally connected bilinear model; how many parameters does it have per scale/channel, and how is it regularized?
3) How does performance scale with fewer dynamics samples (e.g., 1k/2k triplets) and with fewer RL rollouts (e.g., 5–10 total trajectories)?
4) The training cost uses ground-truth car poses. How would you construct a deployable reward/cost in the real world, and how sensitive is learning to noise in that signal?
5) Can the approach handle multi-target or target-switching tasks, where the goal image changes mid-trajectory? How would the weighting adapt?
6) Did you try fine-tuning the feature extractor jointly with the dynamics model (or end-to-end with Q-learning), and if so, what sample sizes were required and what were the gains?
7) Could you elaborate on the stability of the FQI procedure? Any observed failure modes or sensitivity to the ν regularization coefficient and to the non-negativity constraint on weights?
8) How does your approach compare to model-based visual MPC using learned feature dynamics with a learned cost, in terms of both performance and sample efficiency?
