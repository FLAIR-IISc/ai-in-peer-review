Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles visual servoing for target following by combining (i) pre-trained convolutional visual features (VGG, modified to preserve spatial information), (ii) a learned short-horizon predictive model of feature dynamics using a bilinear, locally connected architecture across multiple scales, and (iii) a sample-efficient reinforcement learning stage that learns per-channel/per-scale feature weights for a simple one-step greedy servoing controller via a specialized fitted Q-iteration (FQI). The Q-function is linear in the weights and matches the servoing objective, enabling efficient training with few RL rollouts.
- Empirically, on a simulated quadcopter car-following benchmark (CitySim3D), the method outperforms pixel-based servoing, classical IBVS with ORB/tracking features, and model-free deep RL baselines (TRPO), while requiring very few trajectories for the RL stage (≈20), and shows some generalization to novel cars. Code and simulator are released.

Decision: Reject (borderline)
Key reasons:
1) The evaluation is limited to a synthetic simulator and relies on a training cost that uses ground-truth 3D car pose from the simulator; there is no demonstration that the approach can be trained or deployed with realistic signals (e.g., detections/segmentations, no GT pose) or on real hardware.
2) The sample-efficiency claims are not fully comparable: the proposed method uses substantial supervised data to learn the predictive model (100 trajectories, 10k transitions) in addition to the RL data, whereas the main model-free baselines must learn both perception and control end-to-end. This weakens the “two orders of magnitude” claim unless total samples are counted consistently.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Clear and timely. Visual servoing benefits from features that are discriminative yet robust; leveraging large-scale pre-trained features and light-weight dynamics is well motivated. The literature positioning is solid, covering classic IBVS/PBVS, deep RL, and video prediction.
- Technical approach: The idea to servo with one-step lookahead in feature space using a bilinear locally connected predictor is simple, efficient, and plausible for short-horizon camera-induced motions. The multiscale representation and channel/scale weighting are sensible. The Q-function design that makes the greedy action coincide with the servoing objective, and the α,b scaling trick to stabilize Bellman regression, are neat and pragmatic contributions.
- Empirical support: 
  - Ablations across VGG feature layers are insightful; conv1_2–conv4_3 work best, conv5_3 degrades (likely too invariant), aligning with intuition.
  - Comparisons to unweighted variants, CEM, TRPO (both servo-parameterized and end-to-end CNN), and classical IBVS/tracker baselines show strong gains, especially in sample efficiency when counting RL rollouts. Reporting on both seen and novel cars is appreciated.
  - Practicality: The policy runs ~16 Hz with VGG features; faster than the C-COT-based baseline (~1 Hz).
- Limitations affecting significance:
  - Realism of supervision: The RL cost uses ground-truth 3D car pose (Appendix B). This is not available in real settings and is nontrivial to approximate reliably without additional perception (the tracker-of-choice is itself a strong baseline). As a result, it is unclear whether the proposed learning of feature weights can be reproduced without simulator privileges.
  - Scope of evaluation: Only one synthetic domain and a single task (car following). No real-robot results. This limits confidence in robustness to real-world photometric changes, calibration errors, or non-modeled dynamics.
  - Fairness of sample-efficiency comparisons: The supervised dynamics model uses 100 trajectories (10k triplets). If we include this data, the gap to TRPO narrows; the paper emphasizes RL sample efficiency but some baselines are asked to learn everything end-to-end. A comparison to model-based baselines that also benefit from learned dynamics (e.g., MPC with the same bilinear model) would make the attribution clearer.
  - Analysis depth: While empirical results are promising, there is no theoretical analysis of the convergence/stability of the learned weights with the proposed FQI variant, nor sensitivity analyses to model error in the bilinear predictor.

Constructive feedback to improve the paper
- Clarify sample accounting: Report total environment interactions used by all components (dynamics training + RL), and plot performance vs total samples to fairly situate the method versus end-to-end TRPO. Include wall-clock and compute cost comparisons.
- Replace or complement the simulator-only cost with a realistic proxy (e.g., tracker- or detector-based reward, segmentation overlap, or learned reward from weak supervision). Even a controlled study showing robustness to noisy proxy rewards would strengthen applicability.
- Add a model-based baseline using the same learned dynamics (e.g., MPC/iLQR with either pixel or feature-space distances) to separate benefits of (i) dynamics learning vs (ii) Q-weight learning vs (iii) pre-trained features.
- Provide variance/error bars and statistical tests for the main plots; include failure mode analysis (occlusions, distractors, lighting).
- Expand ablations: locally connected vs fully connected bilinear models on semantic features; impact of multiscale vs single scale; conditioning weights on state vs static weights; sensitivity to action discretization and control penalties λ.
- Consider a small real-world validation (even on a table-top visual servo task) to demonstrate feasibility without ground-truth pose rewards.
- Discuss how the cost/reward would be defined in practice and how the approach would behave if the feature dynamics is misspecified or learned with limited data.

Questions for the authors
1) What is the total number of environment interactions used (including dynamics model training) for the proposed method? How does the “two orders of magnitude” claim change when counting all data?
2) Can the RL stage learn effective weights when the reward is defined via a tracker/detector-derived signal instead of ground-truth pose? Any preliminary experiments?
3) Are the learned weights w(l)c fixed across states, or do you consider conditioning them on the current observation/goal? If fixed, how sensitive is performance to domain shift?
4) How sensitive is performance to errors in the bilinear dynamics model? Do multi-step rollout errors degrade the servo materially, and does FQI compensate for model bias?
5) Did you evaluate MPC using the same learned dynamics and feature-space distance, possibly with CEM over actions, as a baseline? If so, how does it compare?
6) Could you provide more details on the stability/regularization of the α,b estimation and any failure modes encountered during FQI?
