1) Summary and contributions
The paper tackles the problem of learning robust, sample-efficient visual servoing policies by combining pre-trained deep visual features, a learned predictive feature-dynamics model, and reinforcement learning. The core idea is to servo by minimizing a weighted Euclidean distance between the predicted next-step features and a goal feature map, where:
- Visual features are extracted from a modified VGG-16 (with reduced pooling and dilations) at multiple scales.
- Short-horizon feature dynamics are modeled with a bilinear model that is linear in the control and trained on passive data.
- A fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure is used to learn nonnegative feature-channel and scale weights, as well as control regularization, such that the greedy one-step servo objective approximates the Q-function. The Q-function is linear in the learned weights, enabling tractable and stable FQI with an analytic greedy action.

Empirically, on a nontrivial simulated quadcopter car-following benchmark (CitySim3D), the method:
- Learns effective policies with only 20 trajectories for RL (after a dynamics model trained on 100 trajectories), outperforming servoing on pixels or hand-engineered keypoints and achieving large sample-efficiency gains over model-free deep RL (TRPO).
- Generalizes to novel cars (conv1_2–conv4_3 features work best; conv5_3 appears too spatially invariant).
- Outperforms baselines including IBVS with ORB features and a state-of-the-art tracker-based IBVS variant, while running substantially faster than the tracker baseline.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- A well-motivated, novel integration of pre-trained features, compact bilinear feature dynamics, and a tailored FQI formulation that yields a simple, analytic, and sample-efficient visual servoing controller.
- Strong, diverse empirical evidence (ablation across features, comparisons to multiple baselines, sample-efficiency analysis) with open-source code and environment, making the contribution impactful and reproducible.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and positioning: Visual servoing is a classic, practically important problem. The paper is well-situated relative to IBVS/PBVS, photometric servoing, video prediction, and deep RL. The motivation—use pre-trained features for invariance, learn simple predictive dynamics for control, and learn task-specific feature weighting via RL—is clear and compelling.
- Technical novelty and soundness: The servo policy is a one-step lookahead with a bilinear dynamics model; the key novelty is casting this into a linear-in-parameters Q-function so that FQI can directly learn feature and control weights. The observation that the greedy action is unchanged by nonnegative rescalings and bias is leveraged to stabilize FQI, and nonnegativity constraints make the optimization more interpretable and stable. The analytic minimization over actions is efficient thanks to linear dynamics in control.
- Empirical rigor: 
  - Comparisons include: pixels vs multiple VGG layers; alternative weight-learning methods (TRPO, CEM, no weighting); end-to-end TRPO from pixels; IBVS with ORB; IBVS with a strong tracker; and PBVS variants with ground-truth pose (upper bounds). 
  - The method shows large gains in sample efficiency (e.g., competitive performance with ~20 RL trajectories vs TRPO requiring >100× more trajectories), generalization to unseen cars, and real-time performance advantages.
  - Ablations convincingly show the importance of feature choice (conv4_3 best), learned weighting via FQI, and predictive dynamics versus end-to-end policies with the same data budget.
- Limitations, but reasonable: Experiments are in simulation; the reward uses ground-truth state for cost computation (reasonable during training; not needed at test time), and the method was not validated on real hardware. Nonetheless, the claims are aligned with the provided evidence.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Clarify dynamics parameterization: The text introduces both fully connected and locally connected bilinear variants. It would help to explicitly state which variant is used for which feature type (pixels vs each VGG layer), the exact tensor shapes, parameter counts, and any regularization used. Currently, Section 4.2 and Appendix C.2 read as slightly inconsistent for semantic features.
- Efficiency details: Report the hardware setup and timing breakdown (VGG feature extraction vs action optimization), and expected performance with lighter backbones (e.g., ResNet-18, MobileNet).
- Weight interpretability: Provide visualizations of learned channel/scale weights to show that the method indeed emphasizes target-relevant features and de-emphasizes distractors.
- Data efficiency curves: Add sensitivity plots for (a) number of dynamics samples, (b) number of RL trajectories, and (c) discount factor γ to better substantiate sample-efficiency claims and guide practitioners.
- Robustness: Evaluate robustness to more severe distractors and domain shifts (lighting, weather, texture randomization), and to the choice of the goal frame x*, including how performance degrades when the goal image is less representative.
- Planning horizon: While the 1-step lookahead is compelling, a short-horizon MPC ablation (using the learned dynamics) would clarify the trade-offs and whether modest horizon increases yield benefits.
- Real-world prospects: Even a small-scale real-robot qualitative demo (or a sim-to-real discussion with domain randomization) would strengthen the paper’s impact.

Questions for the authors
- Which bilinear model was ultimately used per feature level (fully connected vs locally connected), and what are the parameter counts and memory footprints? How were these models regularized to avoid overfitting?
- How sensitive is performance to the amount of dynamics data (10k triplets) versus RL data (20 trajectories)? Can you provide learning curves and sample-efficiency trade-offs?
- How sparse are the learned weights w across channels and scales? Do they meaningfully highlight target regions/channels, and do they generalize across targets?
- Does the method require the goal image x* to be from the initial time step? How robust is it to stale or mismatched goals and larger viewpoint changes?
- Could the approach work with image-only costs (e.g., a learned cost from demonstrations) rather than ground-truth state-based costs?
- Have you explored other pre-trained backbones (e.g., ResNets) or slight fine-tuning of the feature extractor to the servoing domain? If conv5_3 is too invariant, can architectural choices (e.g., fewer strides, anti-aliasing) mitigate this?
- What are the failure modes (e.g., persistent occlusions, multi-target ambiguity), and how often do they occur on the benchmark?
