Summary
The paper studies visual servoing for target following by combining pre-trained convolutional features with a learned bilinear predictive model and a simple one-step lookahead controller. The controller minimizes a weighted squared error between predicted next-step feature maps and a goal feature map; the feature- and scale-specific weights, together with control regularization, are learned via a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure using a linear-in-parameters Q-function whose greedy action coincides with the servoing update. Features come from a modified VGG-16 (reduced pooling, dilations) at multiple scales; feature dynamics are modeled with locally connected bilinear operators. In a simulated quadrotor–car-following benchmark (CitySim3D), the method purportedly achieves strong performance and sample efficiency, outperforming end-to-end TRPO and classical IBVS variants, and shows some generalization to novel cars. Code, environment, and videos are released.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The empirical validation is limited to a single synthetic setup and relies on ground-truth-based reward shaping that is not available in realistic deployments; the claimed robustness and generalization are therefore insufficiently substantiated. (2) Several baselines are arguably disadvantaged (model-free methods and IBVS configurations), making it hard to attribute gains to the proposed components with scientific rigor.

Supporting arguments
- Scope and external validity: All results are in one simulator with a specific camera geometry and a narrow task formulation (goal is the initial image). No real-robot experiments, no sim-to-real, and limited domain randomization. Claims of robustness to occlusions/lighting/viewpoint and of sample efficiency vs. model-free RL remain context-specific and do not demonstrate applicability beyond this environment.

- Reward shaping and supervision: The RL cost uses ground-truth target pose (Appendix B), which is unavailable in realistic visual servoing. While acceptable for training in simulation, it weakens the practical claim that the approach “learns from images” and conflates improvements in control with the strength of privileged shaping. A result with solely image-based rewards (e.g., goal-feature error, task success events) would be more convincing.

- Baseline strength and fairness:
  - TRPO baselines use relatively small networks and fixed exploration std; training appears constrained (e.g., 50 iterations with 4k samples/iter), and CNN policies required seed picking. Stronger, modern off-policy and model-based baselines (SAC/DDPG+HER, MPO, MBPO/MuZero-style planning with learned models) are absent. The “two orders of magnitude” efficiency claim is therefore weak.
  - The policy-with-weights learned by TRPO is represented via a network with a matrix inverse; this engineering choice may introduce optimization challenges that are not inherent to TRPO.
  - IBVS/tracker baselines either rely on keypoints known to be brittle (ORB) or use only four box corners; they also require ground-truth depth and even ground-truth target pose/dynamics in some PBVS variants. This mixture of unrealistic inputs and limited feature sets makes it difficult to judge relative merits. Stronger classical direct/photometric servoing baselines (with pyramids, robust norms, illumination handling), or optical-flow-based servoing, are not fully explored.

- Methodological novelty and justification:
  - The key components—pretrained CNN features and bilinear dynamics—are established ideas. The contribution is mainly their combination with an FQI-based weight learning and the alpha/bias scaling trick. While neat, the Q-function is linear in hand-crafted features of the one-step prediction error and thus quite restricted; there is no theoretical analysis of convergence or suboptimality relative to long-horizon objectives when feature dynamics are biased.
  - The one-step greedy controller hinges on accurate short-horizon feature dynamics. There is no quantified analysis linking prediction error to control performance, nor ablations against alternative dynamics (e.g., optical flow warping, cross-convolutional filters) or uncertainty-aware models.

- Evidence and ablations:
  - Comparisons across VGG layers are informative; however, the observed drop at conv5_3 suggests spatial invariance harms servoing, which is reasonable, but the paper does not analyze why the chosen layers are optimal beyond empirical curves.
  - The reliance on validation trajectories to pick parameters after very small data (S=2, 10 trajectories/iter) risks overfitting; the robustness of weights across targets or environments is not reported.

- Reproducibility: Public code and simulator are strong positives. Some training details are given, though several choices (regularization, scale selection, neighborhood sizes, exact feature sets) could be clarified.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Strengthen baselines: include modern off-policy RL (SAC/DDPG with image encoders), model-based RL with learned latent dynamics and MPC, and a stronger direct photometric servoing baseline (multi-scale Gauss-Newton/IC with robust loss). Consider learned optical flow or PWC-Net warping as a dynamics baseline.

- Remove privileged signals where possible: train with image-based rewards or sparse success signals, or demonstrate that the learned weights/policy transfer when the reward uses only image goal discrepancy.

- Provide real-world or sim-to-real experiments (even small-scale), or extensive domain randomization and calibration perturbations to argue for practical robustness.

- Analyze the learned weights: show which channels/scales are emphasized, whether they correlate with the target’s semantic activations, and how stable they are across cars.

- Quantify model error vs. control performance: plot servo success as a function of prediction error; compare bilinear dynamics to optical-flow warping and to small CNN predictors.

- Fairness in classical baselines: equip IBVS with more robust features or direct methods, and avoid relying on ground-truth depth/pose unless equally available to your method. Alternatively, clearly separate “oracle” baselines.

- Computational aspects: report full inference time breakdown, and compare to lighter backbones (e.g., ResNet-18/MobileNet) to argue deployability.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive is performance to camera calibration errors, latency, and dynamics mismatch? Can you demonstrate robustness to such perturbations?
2. Can the method train with purely image-based rewards (e.g., goal-feature errors) without ground-truth target pose? What is the performance gap?
3. How do results change with other pretrained backbones or with self-supervised features tuned for spatial precision? Any ablations on removing dilations/modified pooling?
4. Why choose bilinear locally connected dynamics over flow-based warping or cross-convolutional filters? Do those alternatives perform better/worse at similar sample/computation budgets?
5. How stable are the learned weights across targets? If trained on 5 cars, do the weights generalize to novel object categories or backgrounds?
6. Could your FQI approach benefit from uncertainty estimates in the dynamics model (e.g., to downweight unreliable features adaptively)?
7. How do you ensure the TRPO baselines are not underfit? Did you try larger networks, learned exploration std, or off-policy algorithms with replay and target networks?
