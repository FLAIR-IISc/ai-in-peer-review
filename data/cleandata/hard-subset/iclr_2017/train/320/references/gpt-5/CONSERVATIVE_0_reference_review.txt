Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles visual servoing for target following by combining (i) fixed, pre-trained CNN features (modified VGG), (ii) a learned multiscale bilinear dynamics model in feature space, and (iii) a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) scheme that learns nonnegative per-channel, per-scale feature weights and control regularization. At execution, the controller performs one-step lookahead using the learned feature dynamics and chooses the action that minimizes a weighted Euclidean distance to the target features.
- The authors claim sample efficiency (≈20 RL trajectories), robustness to distractors/occlusions, and superior performance to pixel-based servoing, ORB/IBVS and model-free RL baselines in a synthetic 3D car-following simulator. Code and simulator are released.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Limited novelty beyond a pragmatic combination of established components (pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, and fitted Q-iteration), with the principal “innovation” being a linear Q-approximation over predicted feature errors and a minor scaling trick. 2) Insufficient empirical validation and fairness: claims of robustness and sample efficiency are demonstrated only in a single synthetic domain with heavy reliance on privileged information and weak/uneven baselines; no real-world or stronger sim-to-real evidence, no stability analysis, and limited statistical rigor.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The goal (sample-efficient visual servoing without hand-designed features) is relevant and well-motivated. Using pre-trained features to reduce data requirements is sensible; a bilinear model in feature space is plausible and computationally convenient. The paper is reasonably placed within visual servoing and RL literatures, though the degree of novelty is modest.
- Methodological soundness: The control law is a standard one-step greedy minimization of predicted feature error, with dynamics linear in control enabling analytic solutions. The FQI formulation essentially tunes nonnegative weights on per-channel/per-scale errors and control penalties so that the greedy policy aligns with long-horizon returns. While practical, this is a constrained linear approximation of Q tied to a particular model structure; no theoretical guarantees (e.g., contraction, stability, or approximation error bounds) are provided. The choice θ ≥ 0 is not justified and may unduly restrict optimal weightings.
- Empirical support and rigor:
  - Scope: All results are in one synthetic environment. Only 5 training cars are used for dynamics learning; “novel cars” are evaluated but diversity is unclear. No real-world or photorealistic tests, no domain shift analysis.
  - Privileged information: The RL cost and evaluation metric depend on ground-truth 3D car pose in the camera frame; this shaped, privileged cost may drive training in ways not available in practice and can inflate perceived sample efficiency.
  - Baselines: The strongest comparisons (TRPO) use policies that do not leverage the learned predictive dynamics, while the proposed method does—this stacks the deck. The comparison to IBVS with ORB or a tracker is appreciated, but alternative strong classical baselines (e.g., photometric IBVS with robust M-estimators and pyramids, optical flow–based servoing, detection/segmentation-aided IBVS) are missing. The end-to-end CNN policy is also disadvantaged by the lack of dynamics knowledge.
  - Statistics: Results are averages over 100 runs without error bars/confidence intervals; hyperparameters and model choices are selected on 10 validation trajectories. No sensitivity analyses (to dynamics data volume, noise, number of RL trajectories, γ, regularization ν, choice of VGG layers) are provided.
  - Ablations: Limited ablation on feature layers; little analysis of the bilinear assumption (local vs fully connected trade-offs for semantic features, effect of window size), the necessity of the RL weighting vs simpler supervised weighting (e.g., weighting by saliency/attention or via tracking labels), or the contribution of each component (pretrained features vs pixels, dynamics vs no dynamics).
  - Stability/robustness: No discussion of closed-loop stability or failure modes; the one-step greedy policy can be brittle under unmodeled dynamics/occlusions. The claimed robustness to occlusions/distractors is not systematically stressed (e.g., controlled occlusion rates, lighting/viewpoint shifts, clutter statistics).
- Claims vs evidence: The “two orders of magnitude” sample-efficiency claim is relative to a model-free method that lacks the same structural prior and predictive model; unsurprisingly it needs far more data. The method is promising, but the evidence does not yet support strong general claims of robustness and generalization beyond the chosen simulator and setup.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Strengthen baselines and fairness: Include photometric IBVS with robust losses and pyramids, optical flow–based controllers, detection/segmentation-aided servoing, and model-based MPC using the same learned dynamics (e.g., CEM/MPC) to disentangle the benefit of your FQI weighting from the benefit of having a predictive model.
- Reduce reliance on privileged cost: Train with image-based costs (e.g., photometric or keypoint/segmentation consistency) and report both privileged-pose metrics and task success metrics; discuss practicality outside simulation.
- Realism and generalization: Add experiments under controlled occlusions, lighting changes, camera noise, and distractor density; evaluate cross-domain generalization (different cities, textures) and, ideally, a small real-robot demo to validate sim-to-real.
- Statistical rigor: Report confidence intervals, multiple random seeds, and sensitivity analyses (amount of dynamics data, number of RL trajectories, γ, ν, θ ≥ 0 constraint).
- Ablations: Quantify the contributions of pre-trained features vs pixels, dynamics vs no dynamics, the bilinear locality, multiscale weighting, and the FQI α,b trick. Analyze failure cases and stability (even empirically).
- Clarity: Justify the nonnegativity constraints on weights; clarify whether optimization has multiple local minima and how regularization affects solutions. Provide runtime breakdown and hardware details.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is performance to the amount and distribution of dynamics training data (10k triplets from a hand-coded policy)? Does it generalize under motion patterns not seen in that data?
2) Why constrain weights to be nonnegative? Could negative weights help de-emphasize misleading channels/scales?
3) Can your method train with purely image-based costs (no ground-truth pose) and retain its sample efficiency?
4) How does the controller behave under strong occlusions or rapid viewpoint changes? Any quantitative stress tests?
5) How fair is the TRPO baseline? What happens if you give TRPO the same feature dynamics predictions as inputs or embed your policy structure within the policy class?
6) Any empirical stability issues (oscillations, divergence) in closed loop? Can you provide convergence diagnostics or safety constraints?
7) What is the effect of replacing VGG with lighter backbones (for speed) or fine-tuning features on task-specific data?
