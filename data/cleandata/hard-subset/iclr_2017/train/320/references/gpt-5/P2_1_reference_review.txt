1. Summary and contributions
The paper tackles sample-efficient visual servoing from images: moving a robot so that its current visual features match a goal image. The authors propose a simple but well-motivated approach that combines (i) pre-trained convolutional features (VGG modified to preserve spatial layout), (ii) a learned, multiscale bilinear predictive model of feature dynamics under robot actions, and (iii) a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure that learns nonnegative per-channel, per-scale feature weights and control penalties so that a one-step lookahead servoing objective aligns with long-horizon returns. A key design choice is to parameterize the Q-function linearly in these weights, enabling efficient fitted Q updates and an analytic action for execution (minimizing a quadratic objective). Empirically, in a 3D simulation (CitySim3D) of a quadrotor following moving cars, the method:
- Outperforms baselines including: image-based visual servoing (IBVS) with ORB or a state-of-the-art tracker, cross-entropy method tuning, and TRPO, both for end-to-end policies from pixels and for learning the same servoing-parameterization.
- Achieves strong sample-efficiency: only 20 trajectories for the RL stage, with pre-learned dynamics from 10k (xt, ut, xt+1) triplets.
- Generalizes better to novel cars using mid-level VGG features (conv1_2–conv4_3), and shows that too-deep features (conv5_3) are overly invariant for servoing.
Code, simulator, and videos are provided.

2. Decision
Recommendation: Accept.
Key reasons: (1) A clean, well-motivated synthesis of pre-trained features, a simple but effective bilinear feature-dynamics model, and a tailored FQI that yields a practical, analytic controller; (2) Strong empirical gains and dramatic sample-efficiency over reasonable baselines, with clear ablations across feature levels and weighting strategies, and reproducible artifacts.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The question—can we leverage pre-trained vision, lightweight predictive models, and off-policy RL to obtain robust, sample-efficient visual servoing—is timely and important. The motivation is well grounded: hand-designed features/dynamics are brittle; model-free RL from pixels is data hungry; pre-trained CNN features bring invariances, but must be targeted to the servoing objective.
- Positioning in literature: The paper situates itself against classic IBVS/PBVS, learning-based servoing, deep RL from pixels, and video prediction. Using a bilinear, locally connected feature-dynamics model is well justified (ties to image motion under camera control) and computationally lean. The FQI formulation that exploits the structure of the servoing objective to get a linear Q-approximator is a nice insight that bridges control and RL.
- Empirical support: The claims are supported by:
  - Comparisons across pixel vs VGG features and depth of features (conv1_2–conv5_3), showing that mid-level features are best and that pixels and overly invariant features generalize poorly.
  - Comparisons to: (a) TRPO learning the same parameterization (2 and 50 iterations), (b) end-to-end TRPO from pixels/VGG features, (c) CEM and unweighted variants, (d) IBVS with ORB/tracker, and (e) PBVS with privileged pose (as an approximate upper bound). The method is both better performing and more data efficient than the learning baselines, and faster than the tracker-based IBVS at runtime.
  - Ablations and implementation details are clear (sampling iterations, K inner FQI iterations, regularization, exploration noise), and the training/evaluation splits, fixed test sets, and generalization to novel cars are described.
  - The policy uses only images and the goal image; privileged information is used only for the cost during training, which is common in simulation-based RL.

Limitations that temper enthusiasm but do not outweigh the positives:
- All evaluation is in simulation. Claims of robustness to occlusions/distractors are qualitatively shown; real-world validation would strengthen impact.
- The RL cost uses privileged ground-truth target pose; discussion of how to define a practical, learnable cost from onboard perception in the real world is limited.
- No theoretical guarantees for the modified FQI (scaling α and bias b per iteration). Nonetheless, the design is reasonable and empirically stable.
- The semantic-feature bilinear model choice and parameter counts for high-dimensional features could be clarified.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Clarify the bilinear model variants actually used for semantic features (channel-wise fully connected vs locally connected). Provide parameter counts and memory considerations for each feature level, and training stability observations.
- Cost design: discuss how one might approximate the training cost in real settings (e.g., detection/tracking-based surrogates), and the potential impact of noisy cost on learned weights.
- Adaptation: the goal is quick adaptation to new targets. Show a small-scale adaptation experiment where the weights are adapted for an unseen car with very few RL trajectories, starting from weights learned on training cars.
- Sensitivity analyses:
  - Vary the number of RL trajectories (e.g., 5, 10, 20, 40) and show performance curves.
  - Study the effect of regularization ν and the nonnegativity constraint on w, λ.
  - Compare to a “no-dynamics” variant (directly minimize feature distance without prediction) to quantify the benefit of the predictive model.
  - Include confidence intervals or variance across seeds for main plots.
- Planning horizon: briefly discuss why a one-step lookahead with learned weights suffices here, and whether multi-step planning with the bilinear model degrades or helps. Even a short receding-horizon variant would be informative.
- Deployment: comment on camera calibration dependence, and whether the learned dynamics and weights are robust to moderate changes in intrinsics or height.

Questions for the authors
- Which bilinear model was ultimately used for the VGG features (fully connected per channel vs locally connected)? What are the parameter counts and training times per level?
- How sensitive is performance to the exact VGG modification (removed pooling layers, dilation choices)? Have you tried more modern, lighter backbones that might improve runtime?
- Can you show adaptation to a completely new target (e.g., a truck/pedestrian) with only a handful of RL trajectories? How do the learned weights transfer?
- How robust is the FQI procedure to noisy costs and partial observability of the target (e.g., missed detections if one used a tracker to define the cost)?
- Do the learned weights w(l)_c concentrate on specific channels plausibly related to car features? Any interpretability or sparsity statistics?
- Could you report success rates and tracking error (not just summed costs), and provide learning curves with standard deviations over multiple seeds?
