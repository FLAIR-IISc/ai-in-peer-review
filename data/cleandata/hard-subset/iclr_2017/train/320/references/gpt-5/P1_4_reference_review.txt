1. Summary of claims and contributions
- Problem: Learn visual servoing policies that can quickly adapt to a new target/object with minimal task-specific data, robust to viewpoint, lighting, and occlusion.
- Approach: Combine (i) fixed pre-trained convolutional features (VGG-16, modified to preserve spatial detail via dilations), (ii) short-horizon predictive feature dynamics learned via bilinear models at multiple scales, and (iii) a simple one-step greedy servo controller that minimizes weighted feature prediction error to a goal image. The weights over feature channels and scales, and control regularization, are learned with a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure using a linear Q-function approximator tailored to the servo objective. A small technical tweak leverages the invariance of the greedy action to scaling and bias of the Q-function to stabilize and accelerate FQI.
- Evaluation: A synthetic quadcopter-car-following benchmark (CitySim3D). Ablations across feature layers (pixels vs VGG conv1_2–conv5_3), dynamics variants (fully vs locally connected), and weight-learning methods (FQI vs TRPO vs CEM). Baselines include end-to-end CNN policies with TRPO, classical IBVS with ORB features and with a state-of-the-art tracker (C-COT), and PBVS with ground-truth pose.
- Main results: Using conv4_3 features with bilinear dynamics and FQI yields strong performance and generalization to unseen cars, outperforming pixels and deeper conv5_3 (too invariant), and substantially more sample-efficient than model-free TRPO (orders of magnitude fewer RL trajectories). Code, environment, and videos are released.

2. Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons:
- A well-motivated, simple, and effective combination of pre-trained features, predictive dynamics, and tailored Q-learning delivers strong gains and impressive sample efficiency on a challenging visual servoing task, with clear ablations and comparisons.
- The paper is carefully engineered, reproducible, and contributes a useful benchmark and code, which is valuable for the community.

3. Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The paper is well situated relative to classical visual servoing (IBVS/PBVS), learned visual servoing, and deep RL. The choice to reuse ImageNet features to gain invariance, then learn task-specific predictive dynamics and feature weightings, is sensible and addresses sample-efficiency constraints that hamper end-to-end deep RL.
- Methodological clarity: The multiscale feature pyramid, bilinear feature dynamics, and one-step greedy controller are explained clearly. The linear Q-approximation that exactly recovers the servoing objective enables tractable FQI; the α,b pre-fit step is a neat observation that meaningfully stabilizes training.
- Empirical rigor: 
  - Ablations across VGG layers demonstrate the tradeoff between invariance and spatial specificity; conv4_3 emerges as a sweet spot.
  - Comparisons to different weight-learning schemes (FQI, TRPO, CEM) show the advantage of the proposed FQI in both performance and sample efficiency.
  - Strong, fair baselines: End-to-end TRPO (with substantial samples) and classical IBVS with both ORB and a strong tracker; PBVS upper bounds contextualize performance.
  - Generalization to unseen cars: Results degrade gracefully for pixels and conv5_3, while conv1_2–conv4_3 maintain performance.
- Practicality: The approach runs at ~16 Hz (dominated by feature extraction), while the tracker baseline is slower. Open-sourcing boosts impact.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decision-critical)
- On sample efficiency claims: Clarify accounting. The reported “20 trajectories” apply to FQI, but the dynamics model is trained from 10,000 (xt, u, xt+1) samples collected with a hand-coded policy. Please discuss the total data budget, its ease of collection, and fairness of comparisons to model-free baselines.
- Reward design: The cost uses ground-truth 3D car pose from the simulator. Discuss how one could define a comparable cost in the real world without privileged state (e.g., via self-supervised image-space surrogates or learned reward models).
- Scope and generality: All results are in a single synthetic domain. Strengthen the paper by adding a second domain (e.g., static object servoing, manipulation) or a sim-to-real pilot to demonstrate broader applicability.
- Dynamics model analysis: Provide quantitative prediction error vs horizon, and show how servo performance correlates with model accuracy. A comparison to an optical-flow-based predictor or a small ConvLSTM would contextualize the bilinear choice.
- Weights and policy analysis: 
  - Report sparsity patterns across channels/scales after FQI; are only a few channels driving behavior?
  - Explore spatially varying weights or attention (currently only per-channel/scale).
  - Sensitivity to regularization ν and exploration variance.
- Statistical reporting: Add confidence intervals or standard errors on average trajectory cost, and significance testing where appropriate.
- Fairness of IBVS baseline: IBVS uses ground-truth depth and tracker initialization; please ensure equivalent access to priors across methods or discuss implications.

5. Questions for the authors
- Data accounting: When including the 10k tuples used to train the dynamics model, how does overall sample usage compare to the end-to-end TRPO baselines? Can you report total environment steps for each method?
- Reward without ground truth: How would you train the feature weights when only images are available (no 3D car pose), both in simulation and real settings? Have you tried an image-based cost (e.g., distance in learned feature space to the goal) and how does it compare?
- Dynamics generalization: The dynamics model is trained with five cars. How sensitive is it to target appearance/domain shift (e.g., trucks, motorcycles)? Did you try training dynamics once and reusing across all targets?
- End-to-end finetuning: Did you attempt joint finetuning of VGG feature layers with the bilinear dynamics to improve spatial specificity while retaining invariance? Even a small finetuning budget might help conv5_3.
- Alternative predictors: How does the bilinear model compare to optical flow–based warping of feature maps or to a lightweight convolutional predictor with action-conditioning (e.g., dynamic filter networks)?
- Robustness: How does performance vary with occlusion severity, lighting changes, and camera calibration perturbations? Any failure cases to share?
- Optimization detail: The Q-function constrains θ ≥ 0. Did you observe cases where relaxing non-negativity helps? How critical is the α,b step (Eq. 5) to stability and convergence?

Overall, this is a solid systems paper with a simple, well-motivated design and thorough empirical support. Addressing the points on data accounting, reward design without privileged information, and broader validation would further strengthen it.
