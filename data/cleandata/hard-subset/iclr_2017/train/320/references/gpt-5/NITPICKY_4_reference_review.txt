Summary and claimed contributions
The paper tackles visual servoing from raw images by combining pre-trained CNN features (VGG-16 with modified pooling/dilation), a learned bilinear predictive model of feature dynamics, and a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure to learn per-channel, per-scale feature weights and control regularization. The servoing policy is a simple one-step lookahead that minimizes a weighted Euclidean distance in feature space to a fixed goal image plus control penalties. The Q-function is linear in these weights so the greedy action recovers the servo objective. Experiments on a synthetic quadcopter-following-car benchmark (CitySim3D) show that (i) semantic features up to VGG conv4_3 outperform pixels and deeper layers; (ii) learned weighting via FQI outperforms unweighted and TRPO/CEM-tuned variants using the same features; (iii) compared to end-to-end TRPO, the proposed approach is much more sample-efficient (20 trajectories for RL vs. >100× more for TRPO). Code, environment, and videos are provided.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Evaluation and sample-efficiency claims are not scientifically fair or convincing due to privileged information and additional training data not counted toward “sample efficiency,” limited to a single synthetic domain. (2) Multiple methodological ambiguities/inconsistencies and significant presentation/formatting issues (including contradictory statements, missing details, and broken references) undercut confidence in correctness and reproducibility.

Supporting arguments
- Unfair sample-efficiency accounting: The paper emphasizes “20 training trajectories for reinforcement learning” but also trains the bilinear feature dynamics on 10,000 triplets (100 trajectories) and relies on pre-trained ImageNet features (effectively millions of images). Comparisons to model-free TRPO that learn from pixels (or even from VGG features without dynamics) are therefore not apples-to-apples. If the dynamics dataset and pretraining are required to reach the reported performance, they should be counted and ablated. The “two orders of magnitude” claim is not adequately substantiated against equally informed baselines (e.g., model-based RL with learned dynamics on pixels, or TRPO with the same pre-trained features plus a comparable model-based/planning component).
- Privileged cost and IBVS/PBVS baselines: The RL cost (Appendix B) uses ground-truth 3D car position relative to the camera, and termination uses FOV checks—valid in sim but not readily measurable in real settings, making transfer and the “sample efficiency” narrative less compelling. Several baselines (PBVS variants) use ground-truth car pose and even ground-truth target dynamics, which are not directly comparable to image-only methods; while presented as upper bounds, they complicate interpretation of absolute performance.
- Methodological ambiguities:
  - In Sec. 4.2–C.2, there is a contradiction about model size: you motivate locally connected bilinear dynamics to reduce parameters vs. fully connected, yet later state you “do not use the latter [locally connected] for semantic features since they are too high-dimensional to fit in memory.” This seems backward; please clarify model variants actually used per feature type and their parameter counts.
  - Eq. (5) regularizes θ even though θ is not a variable of that optimization; likely the regularizer should be on α (or omitted). Non-negativity constraints on θ are stated but the solver/projection is unspecified for Eqs. (5)–(6).
  - The servo policy with one-step dynamics is claimed to “optimize long-term objectives” via Q-learning of weights, but no analysis is provided (e.g., stability, convergence properties) and the experiments do not examine long-horizon failure modes when model bias is present.
- Experimental scope and rigor:
  - Only one synthetic environment is used; no real-world or sim-to-real evidence is provided, despite the central claim that pre-trained features confer robustness to occlusions/viewpoint/lighting. Claims of robustness are qualitative; no systematic stress tests (occlusion levels, lighting shifts, background distractors) or sensitivity analyses are provided.
  - No ablations on key design choices: number of scales, effect of per-channel weighting vs. per-layer weighting, neighborhood size in the locally connected dynamics, choice of VGG layer, role of the control penalty λ. The “conv5_3 too invariant” hypothesis is untested.
  - Statistical reporting lacks variance or confidence intervals; figures/tables in the text are referenced but not shown here, and reproducibility claims would benefit from error bars across seeds.
  - Validation selection: best parameters are chosen on 10 validation trajectories shared across experiments, raising mild overfitting concerns; more robust selection would help.
- Presentation and formatting issues:
  - Numerous typos and grammatical errors (e.g., “standarized”), stray fragments (“Feature Dynamics Observations from Test Executions Cost”), footnotes embedded mid-text, and missing equation/figure references reduce clarity.
  - The bibliography is severely broken: inconsistent citation formats, missing venues/years, odd tokens (e.g., “? \Q2015\E”), and malformed entries (“learning. CoRR,”). This is below conference standards.
  - Implementation details are missing: hardware for timing (16 Hz), exact solver for Eqs. (5–6), initialization/constraints handling, and model parameter counts.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decisive)
- Provide fair baselines that factor in your learned dynamics: e.g., a model-based RL/planner using the same dynamics but different policies; or a model-free method given the same pre-trained features but with comparable sample budgets. Report results counting both dynamics and policy samples.
- Add comprehensive ablations: per-layer/scale weighting, locally vs. fully connected bilinear models, channel selection sparsity, neighborhood size, regularization ν, effect of non-negativity constraints, and removing pretraining (training features from scratch on the 100 trajectories).
- Stress-test generalization: introduce controlled variations in lighting, occlusion, weather, and distractor densities; consider domain randomization; include at least a small real-robot or real-video evaluation.
- Clarify the “goal image fixed throughout the episode” setup and discuss how to handle target appearance drift over long horizons.
- Clean up the bibliography and fix typos/formatting; ensure equations and algorithms are self-contained with solver choices and complexity analyses. Report parameter counts and memory footprints for each dynamics variant.

Questions for the authors
1. How many parameters and what memory footprint do your fully connected vs. locally connected bilinear models have per layer/scale/action? Which exact variant is used for each feature type (pixels vs. VGG layers)?
2. How are the non-negativity constraints enforced in Eqs. (5–6)? What optimizer and stopping criteria are used? Why is θ regularized in Eq. (5) when α is the variable?
3. Do your sample-efficiency numbers include the 100 trajectories used to train dynamics? If not, please report results when counting them, and provide baselines that also exploit a learned dynamics model with a similar data budget.
4. What hardware yields 16 Hz for conv4_3? What fraction is feature extraction vs. dynamics/Jacobian/Q computations?
5. Can you provide variance/error bars across multiple seeds for all plots and key metrics?
6. How sensitive is performance to the choice of goal image and to target appearance drift? Would updating the goal online help?
7. Can you quantify robustness to occlusion/lighting/viewpoint using controlled perturbations rather than qualitative examples?
