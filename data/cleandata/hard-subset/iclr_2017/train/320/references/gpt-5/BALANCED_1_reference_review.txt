1) Summary of contributions
The paper tackles visual servoing by combining (i) pre-trained CNN features (modified VGG), (ii) a learned bilinear predictive model of feature dynamics, and (iii) a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure that learns a task-specific weighting over multi-scale feature channels. The servo policy is a simple one-step lookahead that minimizes predicted feature error to a target image; thanks to the bilinear dynamics, the policy is quadratic in the actions and admits efficient optimization. The Q-function is chosen to be linear in the feature weights so that the greedy action coincides with the servo objective, enabling a computationally efficient, sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration. Experiments in a CitySim3D quadcopter-car following environment show: (a) pre-trained features (especially conv4_3) outperform pixels and hand-engineered keypoints; (b) the proposed FQI learns effective weightings in as few as 20 RL trajectories, beating TRPO-based variants by a large margin in sample efficiency; (c) the approach generalizes to cars unseen during dynamics training; and (d) it runs faster than a strong tracking-based IBVS baseline. Code and environment are released.

2) Decision
Reject (weak). Key reasons:
- Limited empirical validation: results are only in a single synthetic simulator, with the RL cost relying on privileged state (ground-truth car pose). External validity to real robots or even real video data is untested.
- Claims about sample efficiency and effectiveness hinge on a favorable setup (frozen pre-trained features, separate dynamics dataset, privileged training cost) without stronger ablations or alternative supervision that would be feasible in real deployments.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Well posed and timely. Using pre-trained features plus a lightweight predictive model to enable sample-efficient visual servoing is compelling. The idea of learning a task-specific metric (weights) via RL while keeping perception and dynamics fixed is elegant.
- Placement in literature: The paper clearly situates itself relative to IBVS/PBVS, photometric servoing, video prediction, and model-free RL. The observation that pretrained features can serve as robust “visual coordinates” for servoing is interesting and practically relevant.
- Technical soundness: The bilinear locally connected dynamics and the linear-in-weights Q-approximation are coherent and exploit the structure of the servo objective. The invariance of the policy to scaling and bias is used to accelerate FQI; while clever, there is no analysis of stability or convergence under this modified target update.
- Empirical support: 
  - Positives: Strong performance on the synthetic benchmark; clear comparisons across VGG layers; ablations vs. unweighted features and alternative optimizers (CEM/TRPO); speed/runtime comparisons; and generalization to novel cars.
  - Concerns: 
    • Training cost function uses ground-truth target pose and FOV signals from the simulator. This is not available in typical real-world target-following without additional sensors or labels, weakening the practicality of the RL component and the sample-efficiency claim.  
    • The dynamics model requires 100 trajectories from a hand-crafted policy; the sample-efficiency claims focus on only the RL stage (20 trajectories), making comparisons to model-free RL somewhat apples-to-oranges.  
    • No real-world or real-video evaluation (even off-policy replay) to establish robustness to real image statistics, calibration errors, and non-idealities (rolling shutter, vibration).  
    • No comparison to short-horizon model-predictive control over learned dynamics (e.g., 3–5 step receding horizon), which could be competitive given a predictive model.  
    • Limited analysis of failure modes (e.g., severe occlusions, fast target motion, camera saturation, feature drift) and sensitivity to action magnitude (bilinear assumption) or linearization error.  
    • While IBVS baselines are informative, they are given favorable information (depth or even target dynamics in some variants) yet still underperform; conversely, the proposed method uses privileged pose for training the cost. A discussion of fairness and practicality on both sides would help.
- Clarity and reproducibility: Generally clear. Modified VGG description, dynamics learning, and the FQI procedure are well explained. Public code and simulator are strong positives.

4) Additional feedback and suggestions (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Replace privileged training cost with a realistic, vision-only signal: e.g., self-supervised target template matching, tracker confidence, contrastive losses, or distance-to-bbox proxies. Even if noisier, showing FQI still learns useful weights would strengthen the case for real deployment.
- Add a real-data evaluation: off-policy tests on real drone videos, or a hardware-in-the-loop demo. Even a small-scale tabletop or ground robot study would improve external validity.
- Provide an MPC baseline that uses the same learned dynamics with a short planning horizon; quantify the trade-off vs. the proposed greedy one-step servo.
- Analyze stability and convergence: characterize conditions under which the one-step greedy policy reduces true task cost, and discuss how the FQI scaling/bias update affects fixed points.
- Ablations:
  - Weight sparsity/regularization, number of scales and channels, and sensitivity to layer choice.
  - Effect of dynamics dataset size and coverage (camera motion magnitude, target motion).
  - Robustness tests: occlusion duration, lighting changes, distractor density, viewpoint changes.
- Discuss sim-to-real: why ImageNet features plus local bilinear dynamics might transfer; consider domain randomization or fine-tuning dynamics with a small real dataset.
- Fairness of baselines: clarify total data used by each method (dynamics+RL vs. RL-only) and include a model-free RL baseline given access to the same dynamics pretraining data (e.g., behavior cloning warm start or model-based rollouts to augment data).

Questions for the authors
- Can you train without privileged pose-based costs? For example, using a tracker-derived objective or template-matching error. How does performance and sample efficiency change?
- How sensitive is performance to the magnitude of actions and departures from the small-motion/bilinear regime?
- Did you try short-horizon MPC over the learned dynamics? If so, how does it compare in cost and runtime to the one-step greedy servo?
- How does the method perform when the target undergoes non-rigid deformations or when multiple similar distractors exist? Does the weighting focus on semantic channels indicative of the car?
- How robust is FQI to mis-specified dynamics? If you perturb W and B or train on fewer trajectories, how do the learned weights and performance degrade?
- Could the weighting be adapted online (e.g., via off-policy updates) to handle target switches or appearance drift?
- Any preliminary results on real images or hardware? If not, what do you foresee as the primary bottlenecks (e.g., calibration, timing, feature drift), and how would you address them?
