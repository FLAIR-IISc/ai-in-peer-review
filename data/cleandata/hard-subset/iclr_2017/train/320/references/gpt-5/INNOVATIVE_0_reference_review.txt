Summary and contributions
The paper tackles visual servoing without hand-crafted features or calibrated interaction matrices by combining pre-trained deep visual features, learned short-horizon predictive dynamics in feature space, and a sample-efficient RL procedure. Concretely: (i) it uses spatially-preserving VGG features (with pooling removed/dilations) at multiple scales; (ii) it learns a bilinear, locally connected predictive model of feature dynamics conditioned on robot actions; and (iii) it casts servoing as a one-step lookahead minimization in feature space and learns feature/channel weights via a fitted Q-iteration scheme with a linear Q approximator for which the greedy action coincides with the servoing minimizer. The approach is simple at execution (solve a small quadratic), exploits invariances from pre-trained vision, and learns to attend to task-relevant features. On a synthetic quadrotor car-following benchmark, it outperforms classical IBVS with keypoints or tracking, and is substantially more sample-efficient than TRPO-based baselines (achieving strong performance with ~20 RL trajectories; dynamics trained on 100 trajectories). Code, environment, and videos are released.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Novel and compelling formulation that fuses off-the-shelf deep features, action-conditioned bilinear feature dynamics, and an analytic Q-function parametrization enabling fitted Q-iteration for servoing. (2) Strong empirical signal and remarkable sample efficiency vs. standard deep RL, plus consistent gains over classical IBVS, indicating a promising direction for learning-based visual servoing with minimal task-specific data.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper squarely addresses a long-standing bottleneck—dependence on hand-crafted visual features and Jacobians—by leveraging statistical regularities captured in large-scale pre-trained CNNs while retaining the structure and stability of servoing. The case for one-step predictive control with learned feature dynamics is well motivated, especially given the need for quick adaptation to new targets.

- Positioning in the literature: Related work is covered thoroughly (visual servoing variants, deep RL, video prediction). The choice of bilinear models is supported by prior observations about image motion under camera control. The paper’s distinctive angle is the combination of pre-trained features, multiscale bilinear feature dynamics, and an RL-learned weighting that ties the servoing cost to long-horizon returns via a linear Q approximator.

- Technical soundness: The formulation of the servoing objective, multiscale weighting, and the linear Q-function whose greedy action recovers the servo policy is clear and elegant. The locally connected bilinear dynamics provides an efficient, spatially localized Jacobian. The FQI variant that rescales α and fits a shared bias to stabilize target Q computations is reasonable and effective in practice.

- Empirical support: 
  - Ablations across VGG layers show that mid-level features (e.g., conv4_3) strike the best balance between invariance and spatial specificity; conv5_3 degrades, consistent with excessive spatial invariance.
  - The approach generalizes to novel car instances better than pixel-space or too-deep features.
  - Comparisons to: (a) classical IBVS with ORB points (weak), (b) IBVS with bounding box tracker (stronger but slower and still behind), and (c) TRPO policies (end-to-end CNN and policies over the same servo structure). The FQI-trained weights outperform TRPO under tight sample budgets and remain superior even when TRPO uses >100x more trajectories.
  - Execution speed is practical (∼16 Hz with VGG feature extraction dominating), and code/env availability increases credibility and impact.

Areas for improvement (not blocking)
- Realism and scope: All experiments are in a synthetic simulator and a single task (car following). The cost function and termination rely on ground-truth 3D car pose, which is not available in real deployments; while acceptable for training/evaluation in sim, a discussion of proxy costs obtainable from images would strengthen practical relevance.

- Sample accounting: Claims of “20 training trajectories” for RL should clearly separate the 100 trajectories used to learn dynamics. The overall data footprint (100 dynamics + 20 RL) is still attractive, but fair comparisons to baselines should match or acknowledge access to those extra dynamics samples.

- Baseline fairness: IBVS baselines receive ground-truth depth and, in a variant, ground-truth car dynamics; this is generous to IBVS, which is good. For policy learning baselines, a version that also leverages the same learned feature dynamics (e.g., model-based policy gradient or MPC with the bilinear model) would isolate the benefit of FQI weighting vs. other learning strategies.

- Analysis depth: More ablations would be helpful: single vs. multiscale features; fully vs. locally connected bilinear models; sensitivity to model/feature noise; effect of regularization and non-negativity constraints on w, λ; robustness to heavy occlusions and distractors in goal images.

Questions for the authors
1) Sample efficiency accounting: When claiming >2 orders of magnitude sample efficiency vs. deep RL, do you include the 100 dynamics trajectories? Please provide wall-clock and sample counts broken down by (i) dynamics training, (ii) RL for weights.

2) Cost function: Your RL cost uses ground-truth 3D car pose. How would you define a learnable, image-only surrogate (e.g., via learned keypoint/pose estimates or self-supervised photometric objectives), and do you expect your method to remain stable?

3) Generalization: Are the learned feature weights target-specific or shared across targets? If shared, how do they avoid overfitting to car appearance? If target-specific, what is the data requirement to adapt to a new target?

4) Feature choice: How sensitive is performance to the backbone (VGG vs. ResNet), to the dilation/pooling modifications, and to freezing vs. fine-tuning features with dynamics loss?

5) Dynamics model: How does performance change with less dynamics data, with stochastic distractors, or larger camera motions? Any benefit to multi-step predictive losses vs. your one-step bilinear model?

6) Planning horizon: Have you tried limited horizon MPC using the learned dynamics instead of greedy one-step? Does it improve in long-horizon, partially observable cases?

7) Real-world deployment: What are the main obstacles (latency, calibration, feature-dynamics mismatch) and how would you mitigate them?

Overall, this paper introduces a clean and promising direction for learning-based visual servoing that is both novel and practically appealing. I recommend acceptance.
