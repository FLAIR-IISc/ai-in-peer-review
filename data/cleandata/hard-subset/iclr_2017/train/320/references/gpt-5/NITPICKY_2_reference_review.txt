Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles visual servoing for target following by combining pre-trained convolutional features (modified VGG-16), a learned bilinear predictive model for one-step feature dynamics at multiple scales, and a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) scheme to learn nonnegative weights over feature channels and control regularization. The policy is a simple one-step lookahead that selects controls minimizing a weighted Euclidean distance between predicted next-step features and a target feature map. The Q-function is an analytic linear approximator parameterized by the same weights, enabling efficient FQI.
- Empirically, in a synthetic quadcopter–car-following simulator (CitySim3D), the approach outperforms pixel-based servo, classical IBVS with ORB features or tracker-based corners, and TRPO-trained policies, while using very few RL trajectories (≈20). Results suggest intermediate VGG layers (up to conv4_3) are most effective; conv5_3 is too invariant. Code and simulator are provided.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) The RL component relies on a privileged, non-visual cost signal (ground-truth car pose in the camera frame) during training, which undermines the claim of an image-only, easily-adaptable servo and raises serious concerns about real-world applicability and fairness of comparisons.
2) Experimental validation is limited to a single synthetic setup, with no real-world results, and the sample-efficiency claim ignores the substantial supervised dynamics data (10,000 triplets) obtained with a hand-coded policy, making comparisons to model-free RL not apples-to-apples.
3) Methodological clarity and rigor issues (e.g., an apparent inconsistency in Eq. (5) regularization, missing solver details for constrained optimization, ambiguous cost definition upon termination, and referencing/formatting problems) reduce confidence.

Supporting arguments
- Use of privileged cost: Appendix B defines the RL cost in terms of the true 3D car pose relative to the camera, and uses FOV checks and termination conditions that require access to ground-truth state. This information is unavailable in typical deployments where the key selling point is not needing engineered vision. The learned weights therefore exploit a training signal that will not exist in practice. This also complicates fairness relative to IBVS baselines that do not assume such privileged signals for training.
- Sample efficiency claims: The paper emphasizes that only 20 trajectories are used for RL, but the learned dynamics requires 10,000 triplets (100 trajectories) collected with a hand-coded policy and with access to the simulator. In realistic settings, this collection burden is nontrivial. Comparisons to TRPO (50 iterations × 4000 samples) do not factor in the dynamics pretraining cost; furthermore, TRPO is trained end-to-end from pixels without a model—an inherently harder problem—so the claimed “two orders of magnitude” advantage is not strictly comparable.
- Function approximation and Bellman error minimization: The Q-approximator is restricted to the form induced by the one-step predicted squared feature error plus quadratic control costs and linear in θ; there is no theoretical discussion of approximation error, contraction properties, or stability of the FQI procedure under this constrained class. The intermediate step (Eq. (5)) optimizes α ≥ 0 and b; the added L2 penalty is written as ν||θ||^2, which is constant in (α, b) and thus appears ineffective. If the intent is to regularize αθ, it should be ν||αθ||^2. This raises concerns about the correctness/benefit of that step.
- Policy/search details: The constrained nonnegativity θ ≥ 0 is central to interpretability and stability, but the paper does not specify the optimizer, projection, or convergence checks for Eqs. (5)–(6). The “min over u” requires solving a quadratic problem per sample; implementation details (conditioning, damping, handling ill-posed Jacobians) are omitted. Regularization is mentioned as “important,” but the sensitivity to ν and the stability of the solution are not analyzed.
- Cost function ambiguity: The termination penalty in Eq. (9) is defined as (T−t+1)c(·, ·, st) “otherwise,” which is unclear (what is c(·, ·, st) exactly in that branch?). This makes reproducibility of the RL objective hard without relying on the code.
- Generalization and robustness: Results are reported on “novel cars,” but still within the same synthetic domain and data-generation process (same roads, lighting, physics). No robustness tests to camera calibration, dynamics mismatch, or domain shift are provided. The method’s reliance on per-channel global weights (no spatial weighting) raises questions about handling multiple distractors/targets; this is not ablated.
- Comparisons: IBVS baselines are disadvantaged (e.g., ORB failures are expected in urban scenes), while the tracker-based baseline (C-COT) is slowed down by an unoptimized implementation; speed comparisons are not central to the contribution. A model-based MPC baseline using the same learned dynamics but with uniform weights (or learned via supervised regression from goal alignment) would be informative. Also missing: ablations on the local vs fully connected bilinear model for semantic features (omitted due to memory, which itself indicates scalability concerns).
- Presentation and formatting: Numerous citation formatting errors, missing publication details, and footnote misplacements (e.g., footnote 4 reference to the locally connected operator) detract from polish.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Remove the dependence on ground-truth pose for the RL cost. Propose and evaluate a self-supervised, image-based cost (e.g., photometric/feature alignment with robust penalties, goal-conditioned contrastive objectives) and show that FQI learns meaningful weights under this signal.
- Provide real-world experiments or, at minimum, sim-to-real or substantial domain shift tests to demonstrate practical viability.
- Clarify and correct Eq. (5) regularization; specify the optimization algorithms for Eqs. (5)–(6), including handling of nonnegativity constraints and numerical stability.
- Report hyperparameters (e.g., local neighborhood size nf), memory/compute requirements for each dynamics variant, and ablations on these choices.
- Include an MPC baseline using the same bilinear dynamics and immediate feature-space cost without learned weights; also test spatial weighting or attention vs global per-channel weights.
- Provide a fairness discussion for sample-efficiency claims that accounts for the supervised dynamics dataset.
- Improve references and formatting; ensure figures/tables are self-contained and all symbols are defined.

Questions for the authors
1) How would you train the weights without access to ground-truth poses for costs in real deployments? Did you attempt an image-only training signal for RL?
2) In Eq. (5), is the regularizer intended to be ν||αθ||^2? If not, what role does ν||θ||^2 play when optimizing α and b?
3) What optimizer and constraint handling do you use for θ ≥ 0 in Eqs. (5)–(6)? Any issues with conditioning of the quadratic solves for minu Q(s', u)?
4) How sensitive is performance to ν, K, S, and the exploration std? Please provide learning curves and variance across seeds.
5) What is the local neighborhood size nf, and how do results change with nf and with fully connected vs locally connected bilinear models for semantic features?
6) Can you report results with a purely image-based cost during RL training, and on a real robot or in a significantly different simulator?
7) How does the method handle multiple similar objects in view? Would spatial weighting/attention improve over global per-channel weights?
