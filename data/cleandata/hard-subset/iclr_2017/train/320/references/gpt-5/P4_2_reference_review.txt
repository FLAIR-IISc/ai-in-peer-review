1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper addresses visual servoing for target following by combining (i) fixed, pre-trained convolutional features (VGG-16 modified to preserve spatial layout) at multiple scales, (ii) a learned, locally connected bilinear model that predicts next-step feature maps given the current features and control, and (iii) a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure that learns nonnegative weights over feature channels and control regularization so that a simple one-step greedy servoing controller aligns predicted features to a target. The Q-function is designed to be linear in the weights, enabling an analytic action minimization and a computationally efficient FQI variant with a scaling-and-bias initialization step. Experiments on a synthetic quadcopter–car-following benchmark show that the approach achieves robust servoing across viewpoint changes and occlusions, outperforms pixel-based servoing and IBVS baselines with hand-engineered features or a tracker, and attains substantially better sample efficiency than model-free deep RL (TRPO) when counting only RL trajectories. Code, data, and environment are released.

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Empirical evidence is limited to a single synthetic domain with privileged signals in the cost and baselines whose fairness is debatable, making the strength of claims (especially sample efficiency and robustness) insufficiently supported.
- The core technical novelty appears incremental: the controller is a weighted one-step feature alignment with a linear Q-approximation; the main contributions are in method integration and an FQI optimization trick rather than a substantively new learning or control principle.

3. Supporting arguments
Clarity
- The problem, method, and experimental setup are described clearly, with equations and algorithmic details. The multiscale feature construction, bilinear dynamics, and the linear Q-approximation are well explained. Appendices provide optimization details and cost definition.

Technical soundness
- Modeling: Using bilinear, locally connected dynamics for feature-map evolution under camera motion is plausible and computationally attractive. The Jacobian-based linearization being exact for bilinear dynamics is correct.
- Control: The policy is the minimizer of a convex quadratic objective given the linearized dynamics, which is sound. However, there is no analysis of stability or closed-loop performance under model mismatch, and no formal guarantees for the FQI learning of weights beyond empirical performance.
- RL: The FQI parameterization that is linear in weights and the scale/bias fitting step are consistent and computationally efficient. The non-negativity constraints on weights are reasonable but reduce expressiveness; no justification is given beyond interpretability.

Novelty and positioning
- The idea of servoing by aligning predicted features to a goal is known in photometric/feature-based servoing; learning feature dynamics and using pre-trained CNN features are natural extensions seen in recent literature. The main novelty lies in (a) using off-the-shelf deep features at multiple scales with a learned local bilinear dynamic, and (b) designing a Q-function that matches the servoing objective to enable efficient fitted Q-iteration over feature weights. These are meaningful but moderate extensions.

Do results support claims?
- Positive: Clear improvements over pixel-based servoing and ORB-IBVS are shown; conv4_3 features generalize better than shallow or overly deep VGG layers; the proposed FQI is more sample-efficient than TRPO for learning weights; the tracker-based IBVS baseline is competitively strong but slower.
- Limitations: Conclusions about “two orders of magnitude” sample-efficiency over model-free RL count only RL trajectories and exclude the 10k supervised dynamics samples; this undercuts the stated sample-efficiency advantage. All experiments are in a single synthetic environment; robustness to real-world visual variation and dynamics noise is untested. The reward uses privileged ground-truth car pose/FOV for cost computation, raising questions about learnability without such signals (e.g., for real-world deployment). Baseline fairness is mixed: IBVS is given ground-truth depth (often unavailable), while end-to-end CNN baselines may be under-matched to the engineered structure of the proposed method.

4. Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all points affect the decision)
- Account for all data when discussing sample efficiency. Report RL sample counts alongside supervised dynamics data and compare to model-based RL baselines that also exploit dynamics learning.
- Provide confidence intervals/variance over runs for all reported metrics and learning curves.
- Quantify occlusion robustness (e.g., occlusion rate vs performance), not just qualitative examples.
- Ablate components: learned bilinear vs. simple image-warping, local vs. fully connected bilinear, effect of the α/b initialization in FQI, and the non-negativity constraint.
- Analyze sensitivity to VGG layer choice, feature downsampling, and local neighborhood size in the bilinear operator. Consider modern lightweight backbones and feature fine-tuning.
- Evaluate without privileged signals: define a reward based on observable signals (e.g., proxy distances from detections) and test whether the method still learns.
- Extend evaluation beyond the specific car-following scenario (e.g., different target categories, backgrounds, or camera motions) and, if possible, include a real-robot or photorealistic sim-to-real test.

Questions for the authors
- How many total environment interactions are used, including the 10k supervised dynamics transitions and the RL trajectories? Can you report sample efficiency in unified terms across methods?
- Is the cost’s dependence on ground-truth car pose and FOV used only for training? How would you specify a practical, observable reward for real systems?
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of VGG layer and to freezing vs fine-tuning features? Did you try fine-tuning the feature extractor jointly with dynamics?
- What is the quantitative impact of the α/b initialization step in FQI on convergence speed and final performance?
- How robust is the controller to dynamics-model error? For example, if the bilinear predictor is degraded, does performance degrade gracefully?
- Does the non-negativity constraint on weights ever prevent the Q-approximator from matching the desired objective? Any evidence of negative weights being beneficial?
- Can the approach handle moving distractors whose features are highly correlated with the target? Is there a failure analysis for such cases?
- How would the method adapt when the target appearance changes substantially mid-episode (e.g., lighting, articulation), given that the goal image is fixed as the first frame?
