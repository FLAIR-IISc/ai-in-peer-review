Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes a visual servoing method that combines (i) off-the-shelf pre-trained CNN features (VGG with dilations), (ii) a learned bilinear, locally connected feature-dynamics model, and (iii) a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) scheme that learns non-negative weights over multi-scale feature channels so that a simple one-step lookahead servo (minimizing predicted feature error) aligns with long-horizon objectives.
- The Q-function is linear in the weights, making FQI computationally efficient. The method is evaluated on a simulated quadcopter car-following benchmark (CitySim3D), with code released. The authors report strong performance and significantly improved sample efficiency over TRPO-based policies and over classical IBVS baselines (ORB keypoints and a tracker).

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Evidence is limited to a single synthetic benchmark with oracle signals, and core claims of sample efficiency and robustness are not validated in realistic or real-robot settings. 2) Comparisons and experimental design leave important confounds: the “RL sample-efficiency” claim discounts the substantial supervised data used for the dynamics model, relies on a non-realistic oracle cost based on ground-truth 3D target pose, and omits several stronger established baselines.

Supporting arguments
- Methodological soundness and novelty: The idea of servoing in pre-trained feature space with a bilinear dynamics model and learning feature weights via fitted Q-iteration is appealing and leverages established components. The linear Q approximator tied to the servo objective is elegant. However, the proposed FQI variant (scaling α and bias b jointly for current and target Q before updating θ) lacks theoretical justification or analysis of stability/consistency; constraining θ ≥ 0 is reasonable but again untheorized. The paper would benefit from guarantees (or at least empirical robustness studies) for the two-stage Bellman error minimization.

- Fairness and strength of baselines:
  • The sample-efficiency claim relies on only counting 20 trajectories for FQI but ignores the 10k supervised transitions used to train the visual dynamics. In practice, learning those dynamics is a major data budget that should be accounted for when comparing to model-free baselines.  
  • The reward/cost uses ground-truth 3D car position in the camera frame (Appendix B). This is a strong oracle unavailable in real scenarios; it likely makes RL substantially easier. There is no evaluation of learning with only image-based costs (e.g., alignment losses) or sparse feedback.  
  • The model-free baseline is primarily TRPO, which is known to be sample-hungry; stronger and more stable off-policy methods (e.g., DDPG variants of the era, or at least a tuned NFQ baseline) are missing. The end-to-end TRPO policy architecture and training protocol may also be suboptimal.  
  • Classical baselines: the IBVS comparisons give them ground-truth depth and a state-of-the-art tracker initialized with a ground-truth box, but the chosen keypoint baseline (ORB) is weak; stronger dense photometric IBVS (with robust norms/forward compositional Lucas-Kanade) or IBVS with learned depth/flow would be more representative. The “with car dynamics” PBVS/IBVS ablations in the appendix further highlight that oracle signals dominate performance.

- Empirical scope and robustness: Results are confined to one synthetic environment with limited variability. “Novel cars” is a narrow generalization test; there is no test across backgrounds, lighting, camera intrinsics, or sim-to-real. No uncertainty estimates or statistical tests are provided. Policy selection on 10 validation trajectories risks overfitting. It remains unclear whether the learned feature weights truly isolate the target versus suppress distractors; weight visualizations or channel-importance analyses are missing.

- Ablations and diagnostics: Although the paper compares different VGG layers and two bilinear variants, there is no analysis of multi-step prediction quality, sensitivity to dynamics model errors, sensitivity to number of FQI iterations, or effect of the non-negativity constraint. The choice of conv4_3 as “best compromise” is plausible but weakly supported. The claim that conv5_3 is “too invariant” deserves quantitative evidence (e.g., spatial localization metrics).

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all are part of the decision)
- Account for the full data budget: report results when the supervised dynamics model is trained with the same number of transitions as the RL baselines, and/or report total interaction steps to achieve a given return. Include model-free off-policy baselines and NFQ variants that better match your linear Q setting.
- Replace or complement the oracle cost with image-based costs (photometric or deep-feature alignment) to better reflect realistic supervision. Alternatively, show a small real-world experiment where only images are available, to support the robustness claims.
- Strengthen classical baselines: dense photometric IBVS with robust losses, direct alignment in deep feature space without learned dynamics, IBVS with learned depth/flow, and a model-based MPC with your learned dynamics plus a hand-crafted image-space cost.
- Provide statistical rigor: multiple seeds, confidence intervals, and learning curves that include the dynamics training phase.
- Analyze what the FQI-learned weights are doing: visualize per-channel weights, show whether they focus on the target, and test robustness to distractors/occlusions under controlled conditions.
- Clarify computational costs and timing: what are the dynamics model parameter counts, training times, and control latency on typical hardware?
- Discuss calibration requirements and failure modes. How sensitive is the method to camera intrinsics/extrinsics drift? How does performance degrade if the dynamics model is slightly miscalibrated?

Questions for the authors
1) How critical is the oracle cost (ground-truth 3D car pose) to the reported sample efficiency? Can your method learn competitive weights using only image-based costs available in practice?
2) When counting samples, why exclude the 10k supervised transitions used to train the dynamics? How does performance change as you vary this dataset size?
3) How does your FQI variant behave theoretically? Any convergence guarantees or empirical stability studies across seeds/tasks?
4) Can you show weight visualizations to demonstrate the policy actually emphasizes target-related channels and suppresses distractors?
5) How does the method perform with other backbones (e.g., shallower features or self-supervised features) and under domain shifts (lighting, backgrounds, camera intrinsics)?
6) Why not compare to dense photometric IBVS and/or deep-feature alignment baselines without learned dynamics, which are strong established alternatives for tracking/servoing?
7) What prevents deployment on a real platform? Can you provide even a small-scale real-world demonstration or a sim-to-real study?
