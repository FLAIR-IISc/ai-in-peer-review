1) Summary of contributions (positive and generous)
The paper proposes a simple and sample-efficient approach to visual servoing that combines (i) pre-trained convolutional features (modified VGG with dilated convolutions), (ii) a multiscale bilinear model of feature dynamics, and (iii) a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure that learns nonnegative weights over feature channels and scales so that a one-step greedy servoing policy aligns with long-horizon objectives. The policy minimizes a weighted Euclidean distance between predicted next-step features and a target feature map, yielding an analytic action and a linear-in-parameters Q-function approximator. The dynamics are learned from modest data, and the weights are learned with very few trajectories. On a synthetic quadrotor–car-following benchmark, the method significantly outperforms classical IBVS with keypoints, a strong visual tracker baseline, and end-to-end deep RL policies, while being substantially more sample-efficient than policy-gradient methods. Code, simulator, and videos are provided, and the paper includes careful comparisons across feature layers and optimization alternatives.

2) Decision: Reject (borderline)
Key reasons:
- Validation is limited to a single simulated domain and uses ground-truth pose to define the cost, making the real-world feasibility and robustness claims insufficiently supported.
- The methodological novelty is incremental (combining pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, and a linear Q-approximation in an FQI loop), with limited theoretical guarantees about stability or optimality of the one-step servo with learned weights.

3) Supporting arguments
- Empirical scope: All results are in simulation (CitySim3D). The cost/reward function requires ground-truth car pose in the camera frame at t+1. While the policy itself does not use this, the reliance on privileged state for RL training limits realism and transferability. There are no experiments on real robots or real imagery, nor any domain randomization demonstrations that would encourage confidence in sim-to-real transfer.
- Claims of sample efficiency: The headline “20 training trajectory samples for RL” is compelling, but dynamics learning requires 100 trajectories (10k triplets). It would be fairer to report total data budget and to analyze sensitivity to dynamics data scale. The method’s success hinges on a good dynamics model; robustness of the approach when the dynamics are imperfect is not deeply examined beyond local/bilinear approximations.
- Novelty and rigor: The ingredients (pretrained features, bilinear models for feature flows, fitted Q-iteration) are known. The main novelty is the linear-in-weights Q-approximation that makes the greedy servo objective coincide with Q-minimization, enabling efficient FQI. This is elegant and practical but lacks theoretical analysis (e.g., when does the one-step greedy control with learned feature weights approximate long-horizon optimal control? What are stability/convergence conditions given bilinear dynamics and nonnegative weights?).
- Baselines and fairness: The paper compares to TRPO variants and classical IBVS/tracker baselines. However, modern off-policy continuous-control algorithms (e.g., DDPG/TD3/SAC) or model-based RL/planning baselines (MPC/iLQR using learned Jacobians) are not included and could change the relative picture of sample efficiency. The TRPO setup needs more discussion on hyperparameters, architecture capacity, and whether it has access to the same auxiliary structure (e.g., dynamics predictions) to avoid underestimating it.
- Generalization: The method shows modest generalization to novel cars, but only five cars are used in dynamics training. It is unclear how well the learned weighting is target-specific versus reusable across many targets, or how it handles more diverse backgrounds, lighting, or occlusions than those present in the simulator.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The paper is generally well written, with open-source code and simulator. Some reference formatting issues aside, the method is clear, and design choices are motivated.

4) Additional feedback and questions for the authors (to help improve the paper; not all are part of the decision)
Suggestions:
- Provide real-world evidence: a small-scale real-robot or real-video benchmark (even offboard control) would substantively strengthen the paper. Alternatively, demonstrate robust sim-to-real transfer using domain randomization or adaptation.
- Report full data budget: distinguish dynamics data versus RL data; include ablations on the amount/quality of dynamics data and its distribution shift relative to deployment.
- Stronger baselines: include off-policy actor-critic (e.g., SAC/TD3), and a model-based baseline that plans with the learned bilinear dynamics (e.g., short-horizon MPC/iLQR). Compare sample efficiency consistently.
- Analyze stability/optimality: provide conditions (or empirical studies) under which the one-step greedy policy with learned weights leads to stable pursuit of the target; study multi-step rollout error compounding and the benefit of planning beyond one step.
- Target specificity: clarify whether weights learned for one target generalize to new targets without retraining; consider learning target-conditioned weights (e.g., attention over feature channels) and report adaptation cost.
- Cost realism: explore cost functions that do not require privileged pose (e.g., learned self-supervised image-space proxies) and analyze how they affect learning and final performance.
- Uncertainty and robustness: consider modeling predictive uncertainty in the bilinear dynamics and incorporating it into the control objective; examine robustness to occlusion and distractors with systematic stress tests.
- Architectural sensitivity: show results with other backbones (e.g., ResNet, lightweight networks), and ablate the dilated-VGG modification and the effect of feature layer selection.

Questions:
1) How target-specific are the learned weights? If provided a different target object at test time, do you retrain weights, or do weights generalize across targets?
2) How sensitive is performance to the amount and diversity of the dynamics dataset? Please provide learning curves as a function of triplets used.
3) Your reward uses ground-truth pose at t+1. Can you train with image-only costs (e.g., photometric/feature-space alignment) and achieve comparable results?
4) Did you try short-horizon planning (e.g., 2–5 step lookahead) with the bilinear model? Does it improve over the one-step greedy policy?
5) How critical is the nonnegativity constraint on weights? Does removing it harm stability or lead to degenerate solutions?
6) For VGG conv5_3 you report worse results. Is this due to loss of spatial resolution/invariance, or to dynamics model capacity? Would skip connections or multi-layer fusion help?
7) How robust is the method to camera parameter changes or small calibration errors, and to larger viewpoint/lighting shifts than seen in training?
8) Beyond runtime of the VGG forward pass, what is the end-to-end control latency on CPU/GPU, and does this scale with image resolution or number of feature levels?

Overall, the paper presents a neat, pragmatic combination of pretrained features, simple bilinear dynamics, and an efficient FQI scheme for weighting features, with promising results in simulation. With stronger empirical validation (especially beyond simulation) and broader baselines, this work could be significantly strengthened.
