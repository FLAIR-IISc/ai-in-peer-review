Summary and contributions
The paper tackles visual servoing without hand-designed features or calibrated models, asking: can we leverage pre-trained deep features and learned visual feature dynamics to servo robustly and sample-efficiently? The method uses (i) fixed, pre-trained VGG feature maps (with reduced pooling/dilations to preserve spatial layout), (ii) a short-horizon bilinear feature-dynamics model (locally connected variant) learned from off-policy triplets (x, u, x′), and (iii) a simple one-step lookahead servoing policy that chooses the control minimizing the weighted Euclidean distance between predicted next-step features and the goal image features. The feature-channel and scale weights, together with control regularization weights, are learned with a tailored fitted Q-iteration (FQI): the Q-approximator is linear in these weights so that the greedy action recovers the servoing policy in closed form, enabling efficient, sample-light training. On a synthetic quadrotor car-following benchmark, the approach outperforms pixel-based servoing, IBVS variants with ORB/tracker features, CEM/TRPO-tuned versions of the same policy class, and end-to-end CNN policies. It uses roughly 20 trajectories for RL (after a separate dynamics pretraining phase) and shows meaningful generalization to novel cars. Code, simulator, and videos are provided.

Decision
Accept (weak accept). Key reasons:
- A simple, well-motivated, and effective combination of pre-trained features, bilinear feature dynamics, and an analytic Q-approximator yields large gains in sample efficiency and robustness versus strong baselines.
- The empirical study is careful, with ablations across VGG layers, multiple baselines (including classical IBVS with ground-truth depth and state-of-the-art tracker), and consistent evaluation protocols; code/simulator release increases impact.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Visual servoing needs invariances (occlusions, viewpoint, lighting) and task selectivity (focus on the right object). Pre-trained features supply invariances; learned feature dynamics enable prediction; RL selects task-relevant feature channels and scales without requiring large data, aligning the one-step objective with long-term returns. This is well placed relative to classical IBVS/PBVS, photometric servoing, video prediction, and deep RL works.
- Approach: The multiscale feature pyramid and channel-wise, locally bilinear dynamics are plausible for short-horizon camera-induced appearance changes. The one-step servo objective, linear in u via exact Jacobian from bilinear models, gives an analytic control. The Q-function is constructed so that argmin_u Q equals the servoing action; optimizing a linear parameterization via FQI with a scaling/bias pre-fit is elegant and computationally efficient.
- Empirics: 
  - Dynamics trained from 10k triplets (100 trajectories) by a hand-coded exploratory policy; RL uses S=2 sampling iterations × 10 rollouts each (≈20 trajectories) and K=10 inner iterations.
  - Comparisons: pixel vs VGG conv1_2–conv5_3; conv4_3 emerges strong; conv5_3 degrades, supporting the spatial-invariance hypothesis. The method generalizes to novel car appearances better than pixels or deepest VGG features.
  - Against alternatives: TRPO on the same policy class (with matrix inverse head) underperforms in 2 iterations and still lags after 50 iterations and >100× more samples; CEM struggles in high-dimensional weight spaces; end-to-end CNN policies need far more samples and perform worse than the proposed model-based approach. Classical IBVS with ORB features fails in clutter/occlusion; tracker-based IBVS is competitive but slower and still worse on average. Runtime of ~16 Hz is acceptable and mainly dominated by VGG inference.
- Claims: The main claims (sample efficiency, robustness to distractors/occlusion, benefit of learned weights over uniform weighting, and superiority over end-to-end model-free RL and IBVS baselines) are supported on the synthetic benchmark with matched evaluation protocols and repeated trials.

Constructive feedback and suggestions (not all decision-critical)
- Real-world validation: The entire study is in simulation, and the RL cost uses privileged state (3D target pose) for supervision. Demonstrating transfer to a physical robot (or at least to real videos) and/or learning from image-derived rewards (e.g., goal-feature distances, detector outputs) would greatly strengthen the paper.
- Scope/generalization: All tasks are car-following with a fixed camera intrinsics and platform. Showing another task (e.g., servoing to a static object, manipulation, or different targets) would bolster generality.
- Dynamics model analysis: Provide quantitative model accuracy (one-step and multi-step feature prediction error) and a clearer ablation of fully vs locally connected bilinear models and neighborhood size. Discuss robustness to larger motions and model mis-specification.
- Stability and behavior far from goal: Since the policy is one-step greedy in feature space, analyze convergence properties, failure modes when the target is far, and role of multi-scale weights in recovery behavior. A qualitative/quantitative study of occlusion cases and distractor suppression by learned weights would be helpful.
- Baseline fairness: 
  - TRPO details are provided, but include sensitivity to batch size/stepsize and show learning curves under a fixed sample budget comparable to FQI where possible.
  - IBVS uses ground-truth depth and provided initial boxes; also report performance without ground-truth depth or with depth noise to reflect real conditions.
- Efficiency: Consider lighter backbones (e.g., MobileNet) to boost servo rate, and report profiling; show whether features can be shared across scales without repeated computation.
- Clarity: Minor edits to references and notation; define all symbols at first use; include standard deviations or confidence intervals on test costs.

Questions for the authors
1) Reward/cost: In practice, you use simulator ground-truth pose to compute costs. How would you instantiate the learning signal in the real world where that is unavailable? Could the Q-learning operate with only image-based rewards (e.g., goal-feature distances) without collapsing to trivial solutions?
2) Dynamics mismatch: How sensitive is performance to errors in the feature dynamics? Do you observe significant degradation under larger camera motions or unmodeled target motion? Any results with multi-step rollout MPC using the same model?
3) Weight interpretability: Can you visualize learned weights across channels/scales? Do they concentrate on semantically meaningful filters (e.g., car-specific channels)?
4) Generalization: How does performance change with different backbones (ResNet, smaller nets), and under domain shifts (lighting, weather, camera intrinsics)? Any attempts at domain randomization or sim2real?
5) Baselines: For TRPO on the same policy class, what was the best performance under a matched sample budget (≈20–50 trajectories)? For IBVS, how does performance change with depth noise or learned depth?
6) Runtime: What are the exact per-step latencies broken down by feature extraction, Jacobian computation, and Q-minimization? Could you amortize computations across scales or precompute Jacobians?
