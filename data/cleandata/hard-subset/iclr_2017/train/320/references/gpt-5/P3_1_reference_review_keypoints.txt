- Paper combines pre-trained VGG features, learned bilinear feature-space dynamics, and per-channel multi-scale weight learning via fitted Q-iteration for sample-efficient visual servoing.  
- Policy is one-step lookahead with a quadratic objective; bilinear dynamics allow exact control linearization and an analytic quadratic Q-form enabling a linear Q-approximator.  
- Empirical results in CitySim3D car-following show strong performance and generalization with ~20 RL trajectories, outperforming pixel-based servoing, IBVS (ORB/tracker), and TRPO and providing >100× sample-efficiency over model-free RL; code released.  
- Decision: Weak Accept — elegant, well-motivated integration with solid empirical evidence and careful ablations.  
- Novelty: Bilinear dynamics (including locally-connected variant) are data-efficient and permit analytic linearization for tractable control/Q-learning.  
- Novelty: Linear Q-approximator using nonnegative per-channel weights plus two-stage scaling/bias optimization stabilizes FQI without extra function approximators.  
- Strength: Pragmatic middle ground that leverages pre-trained feature invariances while adapting task-specific weights via RL for large sample-efficiency gains.  
- Experimental setup: Dynamics learned from 10k transition triplets; FQI uses 2 sampling iterations × 10 trajectories; comparisons include multiple baselines and PBVS upper bound.  
- Results detail: conv1_2–conv4_3 features outperform pixels; conv5_3 is too invariant; FQI rapidly learns effective weights and outperforms TRPO; tracker-based IBVS is slower and worse; PBVS is an expected upper bound.  
- Reporting: Generalization to novel cars and runtime comparisons (16 Hz vs 1 Hz tracker) are provided.  
- Limitation: All experiments are in simulation; no real-world trials or sim-to-real analysis, limiting external validity.  
- Limitation: Narrow task scope (single car-following task, fixed camera, constant-speed cars); robustness to viewpoint changes, clutter, lighting, or dynamic maneuvers is unclear.  
- Limitation: Reward uses privileged car pose during training, which may not be available in practical settings.  
- Concern: Dynamics data from a hand-coded policy — coverage, long-horizon compounding errors, and occlusion handling are not deeply analyzed.  
- Baseline gap: Missing evaluations against modern alternatives like learned optical-flow/SE(3) alignment or model-based multi-step planning.  
- Theory gap: No formal convergence or closed-loop stability guarantees; stability discussion would strengthen the contribution.  
- Suggestion: Ablate locally-connected vs fully-connected bilinear models and analyze weight sparsity/structure across scales/channels.  
- Suggestion: Explore limited VGG fine-tuning with self-supervised objectives to improve robustness without losing invariances.  
- Suggestion: Analyze failure modes (target leaving FOV, heavy occlusion, abrupt acceleration) and sensitivity to calibration error, feature noise, and latency.  
- Suggestion: Consider L1 or group-sparse regularization on weights to improve interpretability and reduce overfitting.  
- Suggestion: Add discussion/analysis on stability — conditions where the one-step greedy policy decreases long-horizon cost.  
- Suggestion: Include a small real-world demo or sim-to-real experiment if possible.  
- Question: How sensitive is performance to errors in the bilinear dynamics and do errors compound over horizons >1; would MPC-style multi-step planning help?  
- Question: How does the method handle goal images with different viewpoints or target appearance changes (lighting/weather)?  
- Question: Can you quantify learned weight distributions across layers/channels and their correlation with car-relevant filters or sparsity?  
- Question: How crucial is privileged pose reward — could weaker supervision (distance-to-bbox, self-supervised proxies) suffice?  
- Question: What is the impact of camera intrinsics errors and calibration drift on learned dynamics and policy?  
- Question: Does the control law exhibit oscillations or divergence under large disturbances; are there safeguards?  
- Question: How would the approach extend to multiple targets/distractors and can weighting adapt online to a new target without retraining?
