Summary and contributions
The paper tackles visual servoing for target following by combining (i) pre-trained convolutional features (modified VGG), (ii) a learned short-horizon predictive model of feature dynamics using bilinear, locally connected operators, and (iii) a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure that learns channel- and scale-specific feature weights for a simple one-step lookahead servoing objective. The control policy minimizes the predicted Euclidean distance between next-step features and a target feature map; the associated Q-function is linear in the learned weights, enabling efficient FQI with a closed-form inner minimization over actions. The approach is evaluated in a synthetic quadcopter-versus-car benchmark (CitySim3D). Results indicate: (1) strong gains over pixel-based servoing and hand-crafted feature IBVS; (2) best performance with mid-level VGG features (conv3/conv4); (3) substantial sample-efficiency gains in learning the weights compared to TRPO, with effective performance using ~20 RL trajectories; and (4) some generalization to novel cars. Code and environment are released.

Decision: Reject (primary reasons: limited novelty and narrow empirical validation)
- The conceptual novelty is modest: it mainly combines known components (pre-trained features, bilinear feature-space dynamics, and fitted Q-iteration) with a linear Q-parameterization; the key algorithmic contribution is the specific Q-approximation/weight-learning scheme for the one-step lookahead servo controller.
- The empirical evidence, while careful within one synthetic domain, is limited to a single task family in a simulator with a reward that relies on privileged state, without real-world evaluation or broader domains. This raises questions about robustness and external validity.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Well articulated. Using pre-trained features to improve robustness and a bilinear predictive model to connect control to visual features is intuitive and well grounded. The need to learn feature/channel weights to focus on task-relevant signals is compelling.
- Placement in literature: Related work on IBVS/PBVS, deep RL for visuomotor control, and video prediction is covered. The use of bilinear dynamics in feature space (locally connected) is motivated and computationally appealing.
- Technical approach: The formulation of the servoing objective, multiscale feature pyramid, exact linearization due to bilinearity, and linear Q parameterization are clear. The FQI variant (optimizing α and bias b jointly, nonnegativity constraints on weights, L2 regularization) is sensible and efficient.
- Empirical support: The paper presents ablations across feature layers, comparisons to unweighted servoing, CEM/TRPO for weight learning, end-to-end TRPO from pixels/VGG features, and classical IBVS with ORB and with a state-of-the-art tracker. The finding that mid-level VGG features are best is plausible. The sample-efficiency comparison versus TRPO for weight learning is favorable.
- Limitations affecting confidence:
  - Scope: Only one synthetic environment/task, and the reward/cost uses ground-truth 3D target position with early termination based on FOV/distance. It is unclear how this translates to real deployments without privileged information; evaluation metrics are tightly coupled to the training cost.
  - Data accounting: The approach relies on 10,000 triplets (100 trajectories) to learn dynamics plus ~20 trajectories for FQI. The “two orders of magnitude” gain over model-free baselines under-emphasizes the cost of dynamics data collection and pre-training features. A fair comparison should include comparable total interaction budgets or include model-based RL baselines.
  - Baselines: TRPO is used both for end-to-end and for weight learning. However, no model-based planning baseline (e.g., model predictive control over the learned feature dynamics) or modern actor-critic with value regularization is included. IBVS baselines are provided generous information (depth, sometimes dynamics), yet tracker-based IBVS remains competitive.
  - Statistical reporting: Results are given as average costs; there is little discussion of variance, confidence intervals, or success rates, making it hard to assess robustness across seeds/initializations.
  - Generalization: Only “novel cars” are tested; domain shift (lighting/weather/camera intrinsics), dynamics changes, and distractors are not systematically evaluated, though the method claims robustness to such variation.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (constructive; not all affect my decision)
- Fairness and accounting:
  - Report total interaction counts (dynamics data + FQI data) and compare against baselines under matched budgets. Include a model-based MPC baseline using the same bilinear dynamics (with and without learned weights) to disentangle the contribution of FQI from the predictive model.
  - Include a Dyna-style baseline or recent sample-efficient actor-critic with value learning and action regularization.
- Evaluation:
  - Add success rate, distance/heading metrics, and standard deviations/confidence intervals. Show learning curves (mean ± std over seeds) for FQI and TRPO.
  - Provide sensitivity analyses: number of RL trajectories, regularization ν, nonnegativity constraints, and the locality window size in the bilinear model.
  - Examine robustness to domain shifts (textures, lighting, camera intrinsics), partial occlusions, and hard distractors; quantify failures.
- Methodology:
  - Clarify how Eq. (5) and (6) are solved (closed forms vs. standard solvers), computational costs, and convergence behavior. Discuss why weight nonnegativity is needed and whether allowing signed weights helps.
  - Analyze whether multi-step lookahead (e.g., short-horizon MPC in feature space) improves performance over the greedy lookahead.
  - Provide sparsity/interpretability analyses of the learned weights across channels/scales.
- Practicality and real-world transfer:
  - Address how costs/rewards would be obtained without privileged state (e.g., proxy costs from detections or geometric constraints).
  - Include at least a small real-robot or real-video experiment (even if offline) to test the learned servoing policy or its submodules.
  - Explore alternative feature backbones (ResNet, self-supervised features) and the effect of removing the VGG architectural tweaks (dilated convs).

Questions for the authors
1. How do your sample-efficiency claims change when counting the 10,000 dynamics triplets? Can you provide a comparison under a fixed total interaction budget?
2. Why constrain weights to be nonnegative? Did you try signed weights, and what was the effect?
3. How are Eq. (5) and (6) optimized in practice (solver details, convexity, runtime)? Do you observe stable convergence across seeds?
4. Could short-horizon MPC with the same bilinear dynamics outperform the greedy one-step policy, and at what computational cost?
5. The cost uses ground-truth 3D positions. What cost signal would you use in real settings without privileged state, and how sensitive is FQI to noisy proxy costs?
6. How sensitive are results to the chosen VGG layers, the 32×32 downsampling, and the locality window size in the bilinear model?
7. Do the learned weights focus on semantically meaningful channels/regions (e.g., car parts)? Any visualization or sparsity statistics?
8. Have you tested robustness to significant domain shifts (textures/lighting/camera intrinsics), or to fast target motion beyond the bilinear regime?
