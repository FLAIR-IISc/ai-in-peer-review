Summary and contributions
The paper tackles visual servoing by combining pre-trained deep visual features, a learned bilinear predictive model of feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) scheme that learns per-feature and per-scale weights for a one-step lookahead servoing controller. The contributions are: (1) using modified VGG features (with pooling removed and dilations) as spatially sensitive feature maps for servoing; (2) learning multi-scale bilinear dynamics over these features (linear in control), enabling analytic one-step predictions and quadratic control objectives; (3) a linear Q-function approximator tied to the servoing objective, enabling efficient FQI to learn feature weights and control regularization; (4) empirical evaluation on a synthetic quadrotor car-following benchmark, showing improved performance and claimed sample efficiency over pixel-based servoing, keypoint-based IBVS, and model-free RL baselines.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Empirical support and sample-efficiency claims are not convincing: the reinforcement learning uses only 20 trajectories, but the approach depends critically on a separate 10,000-sample dynamics dataset and fixed ImageNet features; comparisons to model-free methods do not control for this substantial extra supervision. Results are only in simulation, with missing statistical rigor and several crucial ablations absent.
2) Technical clarity and consistency issues: ambiguities about which bilinear model is ultimately used for semantic features, the validity of using pre-trained VGG weights after architectural changes (pooling removal + dilation), the details of the constrained optimization in FQI (θ ≥ 0), and incomplete treatment of identifiability/scaling in the weighted objective. Numerous formatting and referencing problems further hinder assessment.

Supporting arguments
- Fairness of comparisons and sample efficiency:
  - Dynamics are trained with 10,000 transitions (100 trajectories), plus access to large-scale pre-trained ImageNet features. The headline “20 trajectories” pertains only to the RL weighting, not total data. Model-free TRPO baselines must learn perception and control end-to-end without that auxiliary dynamics dataset. The claimed “two orders of magnitude” sample-efficiency advantage is therefore not apples-to-apples.
  - The cost function uses ground-truth car pose during training (Appendix B). This privileged signal is not available to end-to-end baselines unless explicitly provided; how this affects learning efficiency is not discussed.
  - No real-robot or real-world validation. Claims of robustness to occlusions/lighting/viewpoint are based solely on simulation.

- Methodological clarity and consistency:
  - Section 4.2 proposes both fully connected and locally connected bilinear dynamics. Later (C.2) the locally connected model is used only for pixels due to memory limits; which exact model is used for conv4_3 (the best-performing feature) is not crisply stated. This matters for reproducibility and for understanding performance/complexity trade-offs.
  - The VGG modification removes pooling and introduces dilations but reuses original weights. This alters strides/receptive fields; the paper should justify why transferring weights from a different topology is valid, and whether any fine-tuning was performed (it appears not). A small ablation comparing original VGG vs modified-dilated (with/without fine-tuning) is needed.
  - The weighted objective has scale non-identifiability between feature weights w and control penalties λ. The FQI step introduces a global scaling α and bias b, but it is unclear how non-negativity constraints (θ ≥ 0) are enforced in practice (projection? non-negative least squares?), and whether this yields stable, identifiable solutions. No sensitivity to ν (regularization) is reported.

- Experimental reporting:
  - Tables and figures referenced (e.g., Fig. 5–6, Table 1–6) are not present here; no standard deviations/confidence intervals; no statistical tests. It’s hard to assess robustness.
  - Generalization to “novel cars” is promising but under-specified: how many novel models, how different from training, what texture/shape diversity, domain randomization, lighting/weather variation?
  - The selection protocol “choose parameters that achieved the best performance on 10 validation trajectories” at each sampling iteration risks implicit overfitting; please clarify whether the same validation set was reused and how test-time leakage is avoided.
  - Runtime: 16 Hz for conv4_3 policy vs 1 Hz C-COT tracker. Is this on GPU/CPU? What are hardware specs? Since VGG feature extraction dominates, alternatives (e.g., MobileNet/ResNet-18) could change conclusions.

- Baselines:
  - IBVS baselines use ground-truth depth; tracker baseline is given a ground-truth initial box. These are strong oracles, but still the method wins. However, the central claim is about data/sample efficiency vs deep RL; here, the advantage is confounded by the large auxiliary dynamics dataset and pre-trained features.
  - Missing ablations: learned bilinear dynamics vs simple image-space warping/flow; learned weights vs uniform weights; effect of number of scales; conv layer choice; role of λ regularizers; removing ν; training with fewer dynamics samples.

- Practicality:
  - Training relies on cost derived from ground-truth pose; how would one obtain a dense supervisory signal in real-world servoing when only a goal image is provided?
  - Policy uses the first frame as target image; discuss resilience if the initial view is imperfect or the target changes.

- Formatting and citation issues:
  - The bibliography contains numerous malformed entries (e.g., “?, \Q2015\E”), duplicate author tokens, missing venues/years, inconsistent styles. Footnotes are inlined; several typos and minor notational inconsistencies appear.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Recast the sample-efficiency narrative to separate: (i) data for learning dynamics, (ii) pre-training on ImageNet, and (iii) data for RL weighting. Provide controlled comparisons where model-free baselines are given access to the same 10k dynamics dataset (e.g., behavior cloning from the one-step controller) or where your method is restricted to the same total interaction budget.
- Add ablations on: dynamics sample size (1k/5k/10k), ν regularization, number of scales, conv layer choice, locally vs fully connected bilinear models, and with/without modified VGG (pooling/dilation).
- Report mean ± std (or 95% CI) over seeds, and include success rates/early termination stats.
- Clarify and justify the VGG architectural modification and confirm whether any fine-tuning is performed.
- Detail the solver for (5)–(6) with θ ≥ 0: optimization method, projection, convergence behavior.
- Discuss how the training cost would be obtained in real deployments, and add a small real-world demo or sim-to-real stress test.

Questions for the authors
1) Which bilinear variant is used for the best-performing conv4_3 features in Section 6? Fully connected per-channel or locally connected? What is the parameter count and memory footprint?
2) How exactly do you enforce θ ≥ 0 in (6)? Do you use NNLS, projected GD, or reparameterization (e.g., softplus)?
3) Can you provide sensitivity curves for ν, number of scales L, and the choice of VGG layer?
4) Did you fine-tune the VGG features after removing pooling and adding dilations? If not, why is transferring weights across a changed topology justified?
5) Please decompose data usage: dynamics samples, RL samples, total environment steps. Can you match total environment interactions with TRPO and re-run the comparison?
6) How is the training cost obtained in practice without access to ground-truth pose? Can your FQI be trained from sparse task rewards (e.g., distance threshold) or image-only metrics?
7) How many “novel cars” are used, and what is the diversity in geometry/appearance/lighting? Any robustness to background changes or camera intrinsics perturbations?
8) What hardware was used for the reported 16 Hz? CPU vs GPU? Could a lighter backbone achieve similar performance?
