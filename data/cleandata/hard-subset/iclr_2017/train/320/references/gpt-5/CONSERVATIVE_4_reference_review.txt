Summary
The paper tackles visual servoing for target following by combining pre-trained convolutional features (modified VGG-16), a learned bilinear feature-dynamics model, and a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) scheme that learns nonnegative weights over multiscale feature channels and per-action regularization. The control at test time is a simple one-step lookahead that minimizes predicted feature error to a target image under the learned dynamics. The contribution is positioned as a sample-efficient alternative to model-free deep RL by leveraging pre-trained features and a structured Q-approximation matched to the servoing objective. Experiments in a synthetic quadcopter–car following environment show that (i) VGG features up to conv4_3 outperform pixels and conv5_3, (ii) the proposed FQI weighting improves over unweighted or TRPO-learned weights, and (iii) the approach is substantially more sample-efficient than TRPO baselines, while outperforming ORB-based IBVS and a tracker-based IBVS baseline.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Empirical validation is limited to a single synthetic domain without real-world evaluation or strong stress tests; comparisons to “standard model-free deep RL” hinge largely on on-policy TRPO, which is an unfair sample-efficiency reference. (2) The technical novelty is modest—a combination of known elements (pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, fitted Q)—and ablations do not convincingly isolate which components are essential. Claims about two orders of magnitude sample-efficiency are insufficiently substantiated against established, off-policy baselines.

Supporting arguments
- Scope and problem: The paper clearly frames visual servoing as one-step lookahead with learned feature dynamics and learns a task-specific distance metric via RL. This is a sensible and well-scoped formulation. The motivation to use pre-trained features for robustness and a lightweight predictive model for planning is reasonable and grounded in prior literature.

- Placement: Related work is broadly covered across IBVS/PBVS, deep RL, and video prediction. However, the empirical comparisons omit strong off-policy baselines (e.g., DDPG, NAF, SAC) that are more appropriate references for sample efficiency than TRPO. The claim of “two orders of magnitude” improvement relative to “standard model-free deep RL” is not adequately supported.

- Methodological soundness:
  - Dynamics: The locally connected bilinear model over feature maps is a plausible approximation for short-horizon changes under camera motion. The choice to keep feature extractors fixed and train only dynamics is methodologically clean and sample-efficient.
  - Q-learning: Designing the linear Q-approximator to align with the servo objective is elegant, and the α, b scaling trick is a nice engineering detail for stability. However, the learning still relies on a small validation set to pick parameters after only S=2 sampling iterations, risking overfitting.
  - Fairness of baselines: IBVS baselines are partly favorable (ground-truth depth and, in one variant, car dynamics), yet remain slow or fragile. The end-to-end TRPO baselines are known to be sample-inefficient and their underperformance is unsurprising. No comparison to model-based RL variants (e.g., MPC over learned dynamics) is presented.

- Empirical support and rigor:
  - All results are in a single synthetic environment. The authors argue for generalization to “novel cars,” but the distribution shift is small and there is no test of robustness to substantial lighting, weather, or camera perturbations. No hardware results are provided.
  - The RL sample claim (20 trajectories) excludes the 10k triplets used to train dynamics—which also require data collection and engineering a hand-coded exploration policy. It’s unclear how performance trades off with dynamics dataset size.
  - Statistical reporting lacks confidence intervals or standard errors. Hyperparameters are selected using 10 validation trajectories; no cross-validation or held-out random seeds are reported. Figures allude to averages over 100 trajectories but without variance bars.

- Novelty and significance:
  - The core pieces (pre-trained CNN features, bilinear dynamics, fitted Q-iteration) are well known. The main novelty lies in composing them for servoing with a specially structured Q-approximation and channel weighting. This is incremental rather than a substantial conceptual advance.
  - The approach’s practical significance would be better supported by real-world tests or at least stronger domain randomization and robustness evaluation.

Suggestions for improvement (not required for the decision)
- Evaluation breadth and rigor:
  - Include off-policy deep RL baselines (DDPG, SAC/TD3-style) with similar observation modalities (pixels and VGG features), and a model-based MPC baseline using the same bilinear dynamics (with receding horizon) to disentangle the benefit of the Q-weighting.
  - Report confidence intervals over multiple seeds and provide learning curves vs. number of RL trajectories and vs. dynamics data size. Show sensitivity to dynamics model errors.
  - Add ablations: no dynamics (pure servo on current features), different feature backbones (e.g., ResNet, self-supervised features), weighting learned via supervised signals (e.g., attention or mask), locally vs fully connected bilinear dynamics for semantic features (even if approximated via group sparsity).
  - Strengthen generalization tests with heavier domain randomization: lighting, occlusions, weather, camera intrinsics/extrinsics perturbations.

- Real-world validation:
  - A small-scale hardware demonstration (even with safety constraints), or at least sim-to-real transfer with moderate adaptation, would significantly increase confidence in the practical utility.

- Clarity and fairness:
  - Clarify what is counted in the “20 trajectories” claim—does it include dynamics training? If not, present a wall-clock and total sample accounting for both phases.
  - Justify using modified VGG with removed pooling/dilation and pre-trained weights; discuss whether fine-tuning was explored and why not.
  - Provide runtime breakdown and evaluate at comparable control frequencies across methods (IBVS vs proposed) to ensure fair performance comparisons.

Questions for the authors
1. Does the “two orders of magnitude” sample-efficiency claim include the 10k dynamics training samples? If not, please provide total sample counts and wall-clock for all phases.
2. Why were off-policy baselines (DDPG/TD3/SAC) omitted? Can you include them, and if not, justify why TRPO is a representative comparator for sample efficiency?
3. How sensitive is performance to the accuracy and capacity of the bilinear dynamics? What happens if you reduce the dynamics data, or if you train a purely linear dynamics?
4. Can you report variance across multiple seeds and include confidence intervals on all main metrics?
5. How robust is the approach to camera calibration errors, lighting changes, and occlusions not seen during training? Any experiments with domain randomization?
6. How is overfitting controlled when selecting weights using only 10 validation trajectories? Did you observe degradation when evaluating on fresh test sets with different initializations?
7. Have you tried fine-tuning the feature extractor or using alternative feature backbones? What is the computational cost–performance trade-off?
