Summary and contributions
The paper tackles visual servoing—specifically target following—by combining (i) pre-trained convolutional features (modified VGG), (ii) a learned predictive feature dynamics model (bilinear, locally connected), and (iii) a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure that learns per-channel, multi-scale feature weights and control penalties. The key idea is to execute an extremely simple one-step lookahead controller that minimizes the weighted Euclidean distance between predicted next-step features and a goal feature map, while using RL to assign weights that prioritize task-relevant features and scales. The authors derive a Q-function approximator that is linear in these weights, making FQI efficient and stable. On a synthetic quadcopter car-following benchmark (CitySim3D), they show: (1) learned VGG features with bilinear dynamics outperform raw pixels and hand-engineered IBVS (ORB, tracker-based), (2) significant sample-efficiency vs end-to-end model-free methods (TRPO), requiring only about 20 RL trajectories (with separate supervised dynamics training), and (3) generalization across novel cars for intermediate VGG layers. Code, simulator, and videos are released.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Well-motivated, simple, and novel integration of pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, and an analytically structured Q-function enabling very sample-efficient policy improvement.
- Thorough empirical study, including ablations across feature layers and comparisons to classical IBVS and modern RL baselines, supports the main claims of robustness and sample efficiency.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem and motivation: The paper clearly addresses the challenge that visual servoing requires robust, discriminative features and accurate feature Jacobians, which are typically hand-engineered and brittle. Leveraging pre-trained deep features exploits visual invariances without expensive task-specific data, while learning short-horizon feature dynamics suffices for a greedy servo that is fast. The need to focus on relevant features (target vs distractors) motivates learning feature weights with RL; the particular Q-approximation ties directly to the servo objective, ensuring consistency between learning and control. This is well situated relative to IBVS/PBVS, photometric servoing, video prediction, and model-free deep RL.

2) Technical soundness: 
- Bilinear locally connected dynamics is a reasonable and efficient choice: it matches the structure of convolutional feature maps, is linear in controls (allowing exact linearization), and can model local spatial interactions with action-dependent terms; biases allow action-independent changes (e.g., moving objects).
- The Q-function design is principled: with Q linear in weights and a policy that minimizes Q, the argmin action is invariant to positive scaling and bias; exploiting this to initialize α and b stabilizes FQI and reduces optimization difficulty. The non-negativity constraint on weights is sensible for interpretability and regularization.
- The training protocols are clear: dynamics trained supervised on 10k pairs; RL learns weights in 2 sampling iterations of 10 trajectories each, with regularization and validation-based model selection.

3) Empirical support:
- Ablations across VGG layers show a sweet spot (conv4_3), with a plausible explanation that deeper layers (conv5_3) are too spatially invariant. This aligns with the servoing need for spatially resolved features.
- Comparisons: the proposed method substantially outperforms raw-pixel servoing (less robust to occlusions/distractors), IBVS with ORB features (matching errors), and even tracker-based IBVS (C-COT) while being much faster. Versus TRPO: with only 20 RL trajectories (FQI) the method achieves lower cost than TRPO even with 50 iterations and far more data; TRPO using the same feature representation learns slowly and underperforms.
- Generalization: performance on unseen cars remains strong for mid-level VGG features, suggesting the pre-trained features/dynamics add useful invariances.

Limitations and concerns (do not outweigh acceptance):
- All experiments are in a synthetic environment; there’s no real-robot validation. While code and a simulator are positive, the sim-only setting limits claims about real-world robustness.
- The RL “sample-efficiency” is for the weighting stage only; supervised dynamics learning uses 100 trajectories/10k samples collected with a hand-crafted policy close to the task. This is reasonable but should be more clearly contextualized when comparing to model-free baselines.
- The cost function for RL leverages privileged ground-truth pose to compute per-step costs, which may not be available in real settings; although cost is used only for training/evaluation, this may affect the practicality of deployment without additional instrumentation.
- Baseline coverage: no model-based MPC baseline using the learned dynamics for multi-step planning; would clarify whether one-step lookahead with learned weights suffices vs short-horizon MPC.
- Analysis of learned weights (e.g., sparsity, channel selection across scales) and robustness to heavy occlusions/distractors could be expanded.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify the data budget breakdown: supervised dynamics data vs RL data, and provide sensitivity curves for both (how do 5k vs 10k dynamics samples, and 10 vs 20 vs 40 RL trajectories, affect performance?).
- Add a short-horizon MPC baseline using the same bilinear dynamics to assess the benefit of one-step greedy control vs planning.
- Discuss/measure uncertainty in the dynamics model, or regularization for compounding model errors; even a simple ensemble or dropout-based uncertainty could inform control.
- Include a qualitative/quantitative analysis of feature weights: which layers/channels are emphasized? Do weights transfer across targets?
- Evaluate more severe distractor/occlusion settings and lighting/viewpoint shifts to stress generalization.
- Provide timing breakdown and consider faster backbones (e.g., MobileNet) to show tradeoffs.
- If possible, include a small real-world validation (even a table-top setup or a drone simulation-to-real pilot).

Questions for the authors
1) Cost function: During RL, you use ground-truth pose to compute costs and terminations. How sensitive is FQI to noise or proxy costs that do not require pose (e.g., using image-space proxy penalties or an auxiliary learned cost)?
2) Data efficiency: How does performance scale with (a) fewer supervised dynamics samples, and (b) fewer RL trajectories? Is 20 RL trajectories near the knee of the curve?
3) Generalization: When switching to entirely new target classes (not cars), do the same learned dynamics and weighting procedure transfer, or do you need to relearn weights only? Any evidence on cross-task transfer?
4) Weights: Are the learned weights sparse or concentrated on certain channels/scales? Do they differ substantially between seen vs novel cars?
5) Planning: Did you try short-horizon MPC with your bilinear dynamics, and if so, how did it compare in cost and compute?
6) Fairness of baselines: TRPO policies ingest raw pixels; did you also try giving TRPO the same VGG features as inputs with a comparable policy class? You mention worse results—can you elaborate on why?
7) Real-world deployment: What would be required to remove privileged information (pose for cost) and to close the sim-to-real gap (e.g., domain randomization, real data for dynamics fine-tuning)?
