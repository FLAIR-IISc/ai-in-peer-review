This paper proposes a visual servoing method that combines pre-trained visual features (VGG network), a bilinear model for feature dynamics, and reinforcement learning (fitted Q-iteration) to enable fast adaptation to new targets. The core idea is to learn a policy that minimizes the distance between predicted and target features, leveraging the robustness of pre-trained features and the efficiency of fitted Q-iteration for weight optimization. The method is demonstrated in a synthetic car following benchmark, showing improvements over pixel-based and hand-designed feature approaches, and superior sample efficiency compared to standard deep RL.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper addresses a challenging problem – sample-efficient visual servoing – and proposes a technically sound and novel approach. The combination of pre-trained features, a bilinear dynamics model, and fitted Q-iteration is well-motivated and effectively demonstrated through experiments.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Novelty:** The approach of combining pre-trained features with learned dynamics and fitted Q-iteration for visual servoing is a compelling and novel contribution. While each component individually exists in the literature, their specific integration for this task is a significant step forward.  The use of a bilinear dynamics model, despite its simplicity, is well-justified and provides a computationally tractable means for prediction.
2. **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a clear and logical progression from problem definition to proposed solution. The theoretical underpinnings of the fitted Q-iteration algorithm are well-explained, and the linearization of the bilinear dynamics is justified.  The experimental setup appears rigorous, including a comparison to relevant baselines and ablation studies. The use of a bilinear model for dynamics prediction, and the subsequent efficiency gains in the Q-iteration process appear well-reasoned.
3. **Empirical Support:** The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The performance gains over baseline methods, particularly in terms of sample efficiency, are substantial and highlight the benefits of the approach. The included visualizations and analysis (e.g., Figure 5, Table 1) further support the claims.  The comparison against more complex deep RL approaches (TRPO) demonstrates a clear advantage in sample complexity.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Bilinear Model Justification:** While the bilinear model is justified, it would be helpful to discuss its limitations more explicitly. For example, what are the scenarios where the bilinear assumption might break down, and how might this affect performance?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions some hyperparameter settings, but a more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the method to these parameters (e.g., learning rate, regularization coefficient, exploration noise) would be valuable.
* **Generalizability Discussion:** The experiments focus on a synthetic environment. While the results are promising, a discussion of the potential challenges of applying this approach to real-world scenarios (e.g., sensor noise, lighting variations, inaccurate dynamics models) would strengthen the paper.
* **Computational Cost:** A brief discussion of the computational cost of the method, both during training and inference, compared to the baseline methods would be useful.
* **Ablation Study Details:** The ablation studies could be expanded with a more granular breakdown of the contributions from each component. For example, what is the improvement gained solely from using pre-trained features versus including the learned dynamics model?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*  Could you elaborate on the choice of the VGG network and its specific layers (e.g., conv4_3) for feature extraction? Were other pre-trained networks (e.g., ResNet) considered, and if so, why was VGG preferred?
* The paper mentions that the bilinear model is trained with a hand-coded policy.  How sensitive is the learned dynamics model to the quality of the data collected by this initial policy?
*  What are the limitations of the bilinear dynamics model, and in what scenarios would it likely fail to accurately predict feature changes?
*  Regarding the regularization coefficient ν, how was this value selected, and what impact does it have on the learned weights and the overall performance of the system?
*  Could you provide more details on the implementation and resources required to reproduce the experiments, beyond the links to the code repository?  Specifically, what hardware was used for training and testing?
