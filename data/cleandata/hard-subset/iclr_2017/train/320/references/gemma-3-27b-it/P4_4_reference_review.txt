This paper proposes a visual servoing method combining pre-trained features (VGG network), a bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning (fitted Q-iteration) to achieve sample-efficient target following. The core idea is to learn weights for the most relevant visual features to build a robust servo, requiring minimal data for adaptation to new targets.

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1. **Novelty:** The combination of pre-trained features, a bilinear dynamics model, and fitted Q-iteration for visual servoing is a novel approach. While each component has been explored individually, their integration for sample-efficient learning in visual servoing appears to be a significant contribution.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates substantial improvements over conventional methods (pixel-based and hand-designed keypoints) and significantly outperforms standard deep reinforcement learning techniques in terms of sample efficiency on a complex synthetic car following benchmark. The quantitative and qualitative results (sample trajectories) strongly support the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the problem, the proposed solution, and the experimental results. The motivation for combining pre-trained features with learning is clearly articulated, addressing the limitations of traditional visual servoing methods relying on hand-engineered features.  The use of a bilinear model for dynamics prediction is a reasonable choice given its simplicity and computational efficiency. The authors convincingly demonstrate that leveraging existing knowledge from ImageNet classification improves performance and reduces the data requirements for learning a visual servo.

The experimental setup is well-defined, with comparisons to relevant baseline methods. The ablation studies, showing the impact of different feature representations and learning algorithms, provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of each component. The availability of the environment (CitySim3D) and code promotes reproducibility. The thorough discussion of limitations and potential future work further strengthens the paper.

The mathematical formulation of the Q-function approximation and the fitted Q-iteration algorithm is clear and concise. The linearization of the bilinear dynamics appears justified and simplifies the optimization process.  The provided supplementary material (videos of test executions) is helpful for visually assessing the performance of the proposed method.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Dynamics Model Complexity:** While the bilinear model offers simplicity, it might be limited in capturing complex dynamics. Discussing the potential benefits of exploring more complex dynamics models (e.g., neural networks) could be valuable.
*   **Generalization to Real-World Data:** The experiments are conducted in a synthetic environment. Addressing the potential challenges of transferring the learned policy to real-world scenarios, such as domain adaptation and sensor noise, would strengthen the paper.
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:** Providing a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost of the different components (feature extraction, dynamics prediction, Q-iteration) would be helpful for assessing the practicality of the approach.
*  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Briefly discuss the sensitivity of the results to the key hyperparameters used in the experiments (e.g., learning rate, regularization coefficient, exploration noise).

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind choosing the VGG network specifically? Were other pre-trained networks (e.g., ResNet) explored? If so, how did their performance compare?
2.  The paper mentions that the bilinear model’s accuracy is limited. How does this limitation affect the overall performance of the visual servo, and are there strategies to mitigate its impact?
3.  Regarding real-world deployment, what are the biggest anticipated challenges in transferring this approach to a physical robot with real sensors (e.g., camera noise, lighting variations, imperfect calibration)?
4.   What is the computational overhead introduced by the bilinear dynamics model compared to directly using the VGG features within the reinforcement learning loop without the dynamics model? A quantitative comparison would be helpful.
