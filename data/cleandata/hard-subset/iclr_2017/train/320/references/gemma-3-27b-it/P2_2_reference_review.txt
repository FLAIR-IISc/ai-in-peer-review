## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper:** Visual Servoing with Learned Features and Dynamics

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features from pre-trained networks (VGG), a learned bilinear model for visual dynamics prediction, and reinforcement learning (Fitted Q-Iteration). The core contribution lies in achieving sample-efficient learning of a visual servoing policy that can quickly adapt to new targets with minimal data. The authors demonstrate improved performance and significantly higher sample efficiency compared to conventional image-based approaches and standard deep RL algorithms in a synthetic car-following benchmark. They provide code and supplementary materials for reproducibility.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper addresses a relevant problem with a novel and well-motivated approach. The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating significant improvements in sample efficiency and performance compared to existing methods. 

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Problem Significance:** Visual servoing is a fundamental problem in robotics with broad applications. The need for sample-efficient adaptation, especially in scenarios with varying visual conditions and new targets, is a clear and practical challenge.
* **Novelty & Motivation:**  The combination of pre-trained features, learned dynamics, and Fitted Q-Iteration is a novel approach.  The motivation for leveraging transfer learning (pre-trained features) and a simplified dynamics model to reduce sample complexity is well articulated.  The use of a bilinear model for dynamics appears justifiable given the camera motion context.
* **Strong Experimental Validation:** The results presented in the paper are convincing.  The significant improvement in sample efficiency (two orders of magnitude over standard deep RL) is particularly noteworthy.  The comparison against image-based methods and TRPO is fair and supports the authors' claims.  The ablation studies (comparing different feature representations) provide valuable insights.  The clear presentation of results with tables and figures enhances understanding.
* **Reproducibility:** Providing code and supplementary videos is a major strength, fostering reproducibility and enabling further research.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Dynamics Model Limitations:** While the bilinear model simplifies the dynamics prediction, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of its limitations. Specifically, under what conditions might the bilinear assumption break down, and how might this affect performance? A brief analysis of the errors in the dynamics prediction would be helpful.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions the importance of regularization (ν) in the FQI algorithm. A more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the method to different hyperparameters would be valuable. Were any systematic hyperparameter sweeps conducted?
* **Generalization Discussion:** The authors report generalization to novel cars. It would be helpful to quantify this generalization ability more rigorously (e.g., with a separate test set of entirely unseen cars).  Also, discussing what characteristics of the cars might lead to better/worse generalization would be insightful.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention the speed of their method, a more formal comparison of computational cost (training and execution time) against the other methods would be useful.
* **Related Work:** The related work section is good but could be slightly expanded to include more recent work on dynamics modeling for control, especially approaches using neural networks.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Dynamics Error Analysis:** Could you provide some examples or a quantitative analysis of the errors made by the bilinear dynamics model? Are there specific situations where it performs poorly?
2.  **Hyperparameter Tuning:** How sensitive is the performance of the FQI algorithm to the regularization parameter ν? Did you perform a hyperparameter search, and if so, what were the results?
3. **Generalization Metrics:** Beyond the current evaluation with "novel cars," do you have any plans to explore the generalization capabilities of the method more systematically? Perhaps by testing on simulated environments with different textures, lighting conditions, or even completely different target objects?
4. **Computational Resources:** Can you provide more detailed information about the computational resources required to train and execute the proposed method, compared to the baseline algorithms? (e.g., GPU type, training time, inference speed).



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of visual servoing. The proposed approach is well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated, and has the potential to be impactful in robotics applications. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above.
