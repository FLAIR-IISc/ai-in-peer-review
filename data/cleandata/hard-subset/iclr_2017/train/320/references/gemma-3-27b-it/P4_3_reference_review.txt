This paper proposes a visual servoing method combining pre-trained features (VGG network), a bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning (fitted Q-iteration) for target following. The goal is to achieve sample-efficient learning, enabling quick adaptation to new targets. The approach aims to overcome limitations of traditional visual servoing relying on hand-designed features and models, and addresses the challenges of robustness to visual variations and identifying the target object amidst distractors.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper addresses an important problem in robotics – sample-efficient visual servoing – and proposes a technically sound and novel solution. The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1.  **Novelty:** The combination of pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, and fitted Q-iteration for visual servoing is novel. While each component has been explored individually, their integration within a single framework tailored for sample efficiency is a significant contribution. The use of a bilinear model to approximate visual dynamics is particularly relevant and efficient given the complexity of image data.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a clear and well-motivated methodology. The use of fitted Q-iteration is justified by its ability to handle continuous control spaces and its sample efficiency. The linearization of the bilinear dynamics simplifies the optimization process. The mathematical formulation and algorithmic details are presented sufficiently for reproducibility.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the synthetic car-following benchmark demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The substantial improvements over baseline methods (pixel-based, hand-designed keypoints, and standard RL algorithms) provide strong evidence for the efficacy of the method. The ablation studies comparing different feature representations and optimization techniques further strengthen the conclusions. The quantitative and qualitative results (sample trajectories) are convincing.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Dynamics Model Complexity:** While the bilinear model is efficient, exploring the limitations and potential improvements to its representational capacity would be valuable. A discussion of scenarios where the bilinear model might struggle would strengthen the work.
*   **Generalization to Real-World Scenarios:** The experiments are conducted in a simulated environment. Discussing the potential challenges and adaptations required for deployment in real-world scenarios (e.g., lighting variations, sensor noise, imperfect camera calibration) would be beneficial.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** An analysis of the sensitivity of the performance to different hyperparameters (e.g., regularization coefficient, learning rate) would provide insights into the robustness of the method.
*   **Computational Cost Breakdown:** A more detailed breakdown of the computational cost of each component of the pipeline (feature extraction, dynamics prediction, Q-iteration) would be helpful for assessing the scalability of the method.
*   **Clarification on Action Space:** The action space is described as "4-dimensional vectors of real numbers." Providing more details about the mapping between these numbers and the robot’s control signals (e.g., linear and angular velocities) would enhance understanding.

**Questions for Authors:**

1.  The bilinear dynamics model appears to be a key component. What is the sensitivity of the performance to the accuracy of this model? Specifically, how does performance degrade as the error in the dynamics prediction increases?
2.  The paper highlights the benefits of using pre-trained VGG features. Have you experimented with other pre-trained models (e.g., ResNet, EfficientNet) and, if so, how do the results compare?
3.  The regularization parameter ν plays a role in the FQI algorithm. How was this parameter chosen, and what is its impact on the stability and convergence of the learning process?
4.  The experiments are conducted in a synthetic environment. What steps would be necessary to adapt this method to a real-world robotic platform? Are there specific challenges that you anticipate encountering?
5.  While you show improvement over other RL methods, what is the computational cost (training time, inference time) compared to these methods? A more direct comparison would be valuable.
