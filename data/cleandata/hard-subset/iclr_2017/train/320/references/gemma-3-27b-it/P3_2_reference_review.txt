## ICLR 2024 Review: Learning Visual Servoing with Reinforcement Learning

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of visual servoing, aiming to learn robust control policies directly from visual inputs. The authors address the limitations of traditional approaches relying on hand-engineered features and dynamics models by proposing a method that combines pre-trained convolutional features (VGG), a learned bilinear dynamics model, and fitted Q-iteration for efficient policy learning. The core idea is to leverage the generalization capabilities of pre-trained features while using reinforcement learning to adapt the system to specific tasks, specifically target following, with a focus on sample efficiency. The authors demonstrate compelling results in a synthetic car-following benchmark, showcasing significant improvements in sample efficiency compared to standard deep reinforcement learning methods and outperforming approaches based on pixel intensities or hand-designed keypoints. The code and environment are publicly available, promoting reproducibility.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to visual servoing, demonstrating impressive results and offering a significant contribution to the field. The strong empirical validation and clear presentation of the method are particularly commendable.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The combination of pre-trained features, learned dynamics, and fitted Q-iteration is a compelling approach. The motivation for addressing the limitations of traditional visual servoing methods is clearly articulated, and the potential for sample-efficient learning is particularly valuable. The problem of quickly adapting to new targets is well-defined and practically relevant.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed bilinear dynamics model and the use of fitted Q-iteration are well-justified and appear technically sound. The linearization of the dynamics for efficient policy optimization is a clever and practical choice.  The explanation of the fitted Q-iteration algorithm is clear and concise.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are convincing. The performance improvements over baseline methods, including traditional visual servoing techniques and standard deep RL algorithms, are substantial. The comparison against different feature representations is insightful and supports the authors’ claims regarding the effectiveness of VGG features. The use of both seen and unseen cars for testing demonstrates generalization capabilities.
* **Reproducibility:** The public availability of the code and the simulation environment is a major strength, fostering reproducibility and enabling further research in this area.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Dynamics Model Complexity:** While the bilinear model is justified, a more detailed discussion of its limitations and potential alternatives (e.g., more complex neural network-based dynamics models) could strengthen the paper.  How does the performance scale with increasing complexity of the environment dynamics?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly those related to the fitted Q-iteration algorithm (e.g., regularization coefficient, learning rate).
* **Generalization to Real-World Scenarios:** The experiments are conducted in a synthetic environment.  Discussing potential challenges and future work related to transferring the learned policies to real-world scenarios (e.g., dealing with sensor noise, lighting variations, and imperfect dynamics models) would be valuable.
* **Ablation Study Details:** While ablation studies are presented, expanding the discussion of the individual contributions of each component (pre-trained features, dynamics model, fitted Q-iteration) would further clarify their importance.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the bilinear dynamics model:**  You mention that the bilinear model works well for short-term prediction.  What is the maximum prediction horizon that the model remains accurate enough to be useful for the visual servoing task?  Have you explored techniques to extend the prediction horizon (e.g., model predictive control)?
* **Regarding the choice of VGG features:**  Why did you choose to remove some max-pooling layers and replace them with dilated convolutions in the VGG network? What impact did this modification have on the performance of the visual servo?
* **Regarding Fitted Q-Iteration:**  How sensitive is the algorithm to the initial weight initialization? Did you experiment with different initialization strategies? What is the computational cost of the FQI iterations compared to training a standard deep RL agent end-to-end?
* **Regarding real-world transfer:**  What are the key challenges you foresee in transferring this approach to a real-world robotic system? What specific modifications or additions would be necessary to address these challenges?



Overall, this is a high-quality paper with significant potential impact. The authors have presented a well-reasoned and thoroughly validated approach to visual servoing that addresses a critical gap in the field. The minor revisions suggested above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its contribution.
