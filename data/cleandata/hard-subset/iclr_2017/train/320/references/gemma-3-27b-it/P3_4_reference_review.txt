## ICLR 2024 Review: Learning Visual Servoing with Reinforcement Learning

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of visual servoing, moving beyond traditional methods reliant on hand-engineered features and dynamics models. The authors propose a novel approach that combines pre-trained visual features (VGG network), a learned bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning (specifically fitted Q-iteration) to learn a visual servoing policy for target following. The key contribution lies in their sample efficiency – achieving effective servoing with only 20 training trajectories – and demonstrating improved performance over existing pixel-based and hand-designed feature approaches, as well as significant gains over standard deep RL algorithms. The paper is well-motivated, highlighting the limitations of traditional visual servoing and the potential of learning-based approaches.  The code and environment are publicly available, fostering reproducibility.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a strong contribution to the field of visual servoing, offering a compelling solution that addresses key limitations of existing methods. The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating superior performance and sample efficiency.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty & Motivation:** The combination of pre-trained features, learned dynamics, and fitted Q-iteration is a novel and well-justified approach. The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional visual servoing (reliance on hand-engineering, lack of robustness) and the potential benefits of learning.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments on the synthetic car following benchmark demonstrate a significant improvement over baseline methods, including image-pixel based approaches, hand-designed keypoints, and standard deep RL algorithms. The reported gains in sample efficiency are particularly impressive (two orders of magnitude improvement over standard deep RL).
* **Theoretical Justification:** The use of a bilinear model for dynamics prediction is grounded in prior work and provides a computationally tractable framework. The analytical Q-function approximator enables an efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm.
* **Reproducibility:** The public availability of the code and environment (CitySim3D) is a significant strength, facilitating verification and extension of the work.
* **Thorough Comparison:** The authors meticulously compare their method against a variety of baselines, providing a comprehensive evaluation of its performance.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Discussion of Bilinear Model Limitations:** While the bilinear model offers computational benefits, its limitations should be discussed more thoroughly. What types of dynamics are difficult to capture with a bilinear model?  How does this impact the performance of the visual servo in more complex scenarios?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the method to key hyperparameters (e.g., regularization coefficient ν, learning rate) would be valuable. Are there specific hyperparameter regimes where the algorithm performs significantly better or worse?
* **Generalization to More Complex Environments:** The experiments are conducted in a synthetic environment. While this allows for controlled evaluation, it would be beneficial to discuss the potential challenges of generalizing the approach to real-world scenarios with more complex lighting, occlusions, and dynamics.
* **Ablation Study on Feature Levels:**  The paper states that deeper VGG features generally perform better, but conv5_3 is an exception. A more in-depth ablation study exploring the performance trade-offs between different feature levels could provide further insights.  Why does conv5_3 perform worse?
* **Clarify Cost Function:** The cost function is briefly explained but could benefit from more detail. Specifically, how is the relative weighting of the different terms in the cost function determined?



**Questions for the Authors:**

1. **Bilinear Model Capacity:** Can you elaborate on the limitations of the bilinear model used for dynamics prediction? What types of scenarios or dynamic behaviors would likely lead to a breakdown in its performance?
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning:**  Were any specific strategies employed for hyperparameter tuning?  Is the performance particularly sensitive to the choice of the regularization coefficient (ν) in the fitted Q-iteration algorithm?
3. **Real-World Transfer:** What are the biggest anticipated challenges in transferring this approach to a real-world robot equipped with a camera?  Are there specific modifications or extensions that would be necessary to address these challenges?
4. **Feature Level Selection:** You mention that conv5_3 doesn't perform as well as the other feature layers. Is there a correlation between the spatial resolution and performance of the VGG feature layers? How does the receptive field of these different layers impact the success of the servoing policy?
5. **Cost Function Weighting:** Could you clarify how the weighting of the terms in the cost function (position error and terminal cost) was determined? Is this weighting learned alongside the feature weights, or is it fixed?



Overall, this is a well-written and compelling paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of visual servoing. The proposed approach is novel, the experimental results are convincing, and the code is publicly available. Addressing the minor feedback points and answering the questions above would further strengthen the paper.
