This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The central claim is that this combination allows for effective visual servoing with minimal data, enabling quick adaptation to new targets. The authors leverage pre-trained VGG features, a bilinear dynamics model, and fitted Q-iteration to achieve this. The paper presents results on a synthetic car following benchmark, demonstrating improvements over conventional and other deep reinforcement learning approaches.

**Reject.** While the core idea of combining pre-trained features, dynamics modeling, and reinforcement learning for visual servoing is promising, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor but significant flaws that, collectively, undermine confidence in the results and the overall rigor of the work. The presentation is often imprecise, lacking crucial details for reproducibility, and the experimental validation, while demonstrating improvements, feels insufficiently comprehensive given the number of design choices.

My primary concern stems from the lack of clarity and justification for several key methodological choices. The bilinear dynamics model, while theoretically appealing, is presented with insufficient explanation of its limitations and how these are addressed. Specifically, the locally connected variant is introduced with the rationale of reducing parameters, but the effectiveness of this sparsity constraint is not thoroughly evaluated. How sensitive are the results to the neighborhood size (nf x nf)? A clear ablation study demonstrating the impact of different model complexities (fully connected vs. locally connected) is missing. 

Furthermore, the reinforcement learning setup and hyperparameter selection lack sufficient detail. The cost function, while conceptually reasonable, is not fully explained. What is the rationale behind the specific weighting of different cost components? How was the discount factor (γ = 0.9) chosen? The description of the FQI algorithm is dense and assumes a level of familiarity that may not be universal. Crucially, the choice of the regularization coefficient (ν = 0.1) is not justified, and its impact on the learned weights is not analyzed. The iterative process of validation and parameter selection seems ad-hoc, and the criteria for choosing the "best" parameters from the validation set are vague.

The experimental evaluation is also lacking in robustness. The car following benchmark, while complex, is entirely synthetic. The generalization to real-world scenarios is not addressed, and the limitations of the simulation environment are not adequately discussed. The comparison with other methods is incomplete. While the authors demonstrate improvements over several baselines, the choice of baselines seems somewhat arbitrary. A comparison to state-of-the-art model-free reinforcement learning algorithms with more extensive hyperparameter tuning would strengthen the claims of sample efficiency. The presentation of results is also confusing. Tables are referenced without being immediately adjacent to the discussion, making it difficult to follow the findings. The varying number of sampling iterations for each method feels unfair.

Finally, the writing quality is uneven. Numerous minor grammatical errors and inconsistencies detract from the readability of the paper. The use of equation numbers and cross-references is sometimes inaccurate. The supplementary material (code, videos) is cited, but it is not clear if this material was thoroughly reviewed.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Reproducibility:** Provide a detailed description of the experimental setup, including all hyperparameters, network architectures, and training procedures. Release a complete, self-contained codebase.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct thorough ablation studies to evaluate the impact of different design choices, such as the bilinear model’s sparsity, the regularization coefficient in FQI, and the choice of features.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** Investigate the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings.
*   **Real-World Validation:** Explore the potential for transferring the learned policies to a real-world environment.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** Include a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art reinforcement learning algorithms.
*   **Clarity and Precision:** Revise the writing to improve clarity, conciseness, and precision. Pay attention to grammatical errors and inconsistencies.
*   **Detailed Cost Function Explanation:** Explain the rationale behind each term in the cost function and how it contributes to the overall objective.
*   **Justification for Hyperparameter Choices:** Provide a clear justification for the selection of each hyperparameter.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of neighborhood size (nf x nf) in the locally connected bilinear model?
2.  What is the rationale behind the specific weighting of the different cost components in the reinforcement learning setup?
3.  Why was the regularization coefficient (ν = 0.1) chosen, and how does it affect the learned weights?
4.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the iterative process used to select the best parameters from the validation set?
5.  What are the limitations of the synthetic car following benchmark, and how might these limitations affect the generalizability of the results to real-world scenarios?
