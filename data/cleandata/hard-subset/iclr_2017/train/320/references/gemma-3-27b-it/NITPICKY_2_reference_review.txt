This paper proposes a visual servoing method combining learned visual features (from VGG), a bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning (Fitted Q-Iteration) to enable rapid adaptation to new targets. The core idea is to learn a policy that minimizes the error between predicted future features and a target feature map, using a weighted sum of features across multiple scales. The authors demonstrate improvements in sample efficiency compared to model-free RL and conventional visual servoing approaches on a synthetic car-following benchmark.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a potentially interesting combination of techniques, the numerous minor flaws and lack of rigor in experimental evaluation overshadow the contributions. The presentation is overly detailed in some areas (e.g., extensive description of standard algorithms like FQI) and insufficiently detailed in others (e.g., hyperparameter settings and ablation studies). The claims of significant sample efficiency improvements are not convincingly substantiated.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1.  **Overly Detailed Background & Redundancy:** The introduction and related work sections are overly verbose, spending significant space describing well-known concepts (visual servoing, reinforcement learning) without providing sufficient nuance or critical analysis. The extensive description of FQI feels unnecessary given its standard nature. This makes it difficult to quickly grasp the core novel contribution.
2.  **Lack of Experimental Rigor & Ablation:** The experiments lack sufficient detail and rigor. Hyperparameter settings are not clearly reported, making reproducibility difficult. Crucially, there is a *complete* absence of ablation studies. How much does each component (pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, FQI, multi-scale features) contribute to the performance? It's impossible to assess the value of the proposed combination without knowing the individual impact of each element. For example, is FQI truly necessary, or would a simpler optimization strategy suffice?
3.  **Questionable Baselines & Comparisons:** The comparison to prior methods is not entirely fair. The deep RL baseline (CNN policy) is trained with significantly fewer samples than the proposed method, and the authors acknowledge it’s likely underperforming as a result. The IBVS baselines are also not fully optimized (e.g., the description of the dynamic component is brief and unclear). The claim of "more than two orders of magnitude" improvement over standard RL is inflated given the baseline’s limitations.
4.  **Bilinear Dynamics Justification:** The justification for the bilinear dynamics model relies on a reference (Censi & Murray, 2015) but doesn’t adequately explain *why* this model is suitable for the specific visual features used (VGG features). Is it truly capturing meaningful dynamics, or is it simply a convenient parametric form?  The choice of locally connected dynamics appears somewhat arbitrary; a more thorough exploration of different dynamics models is warranted.
5.  **Vague Cost Function:** The cost function described in Appendix B contains a potentially problematic term (T-t+1)ct if the episode terminates.  This essentially penalizes the agent more heavily for finishing early, which might not align with the desired behavior.
6. **Formatting & Notation:** There are minor inconsistencies in formatting and notation throughout the paper (e.g., inconsistent use of boldface for variables, ambiguous subscripts). While individually minor, they contribute to a sense of lack of polish.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Streamline Introduction & Related Work:** Focus on the core novelty of the work and provide a more concise and critical review of existing literature.
*   **Detailed Experimental Setup:** Provide *all* hyperparameter settings, training details, and hardware specifications.
*   **Comprehensive Ablation Studies:**  Systematically evaluate the contribution of each component of the proposed method.
*   **Stronger Baselines:**  Ensure that baseline methods are properly tuned and given a fair chance to perform well.
*   **Analysis of Failure Cases:**  Show examples of scenarios where the proposed method fails, and analyze the reasons for the failure.
*   **Clarify Bilinear Dynamics Justification:** Provide a more compelling argument for why the bilinear model is appropriate for the chosen features.
*   **Address Cost Function Issues:** Explain the rationale behind the cost function and ensure it incentivizes the desired behavior.
*   **Proofread Carefully:**  Eliminate formatting inconsistencies and typographical errors.




**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What specific design choices were made during the training of the CNN policy baseline, and why was it trained with significantly fewer samples than your proposed method?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the justification for using a bilinear dynamics model with VGG features?  What are the limitations of this approach?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of your method to the choice of the regularization coefficient ν in the FQI algorithm?  Did you perform a sensitivity analysis?
4.  Can you provide examples of failure cases where your method struggles, and explain why it fails in those scenarios?
5.  Could you elaborate on the choice of dilated convolutions in the VGG network (Figure 2)? What was the rationale for removing max-pooling layers?
