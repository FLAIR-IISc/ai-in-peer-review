This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features (from pre-trained VGG networks), a bilinear model for predicting feature dynamics, and reinforcement learning (Fitted Q-Iteration). The core contribution is a sample-efficient method to learn a visual servo policy using minimal data for a new target, leveraging the power of pre-trained features and a streamlined RL approach.

**Reject.** While the idea is genuinely interesting and the sample efficiency results are impressive, the paper feels somewhat under-developed and lacks sufficient critical analysis of its own findings. The substantial performance gains compared to baselines are presented with limited discussion of *why* this method succeeds where others fail, and the reliance on a synthetic environment limits confidence in real-world applicability.

The paper’s strength lies in its novelty. The combination of pre-trained features, a learned bilinear dynamics model, and a fitted Q-iteration approach for weight learning is a compelling direction. The reported 20-sample learning rate for a complex task is a significant achievement, and the improvement over deep RL baselines is noteworthy. The publicly available code and environment are a plus for reproducibility.

However, several areas warrant concern. Firstly, the synthetic environment, while well-defined, feels restrictive. Real-world visual data is far noisier and more complex. The paper provides limited discussion of how the method might generalize to such scenarios. The claim of improved robustness to visual variation (viewpoint, lighting, occlusions) needs stronger empirical support beyond the synthetic car following task. 

Secondly, the analysis of the feature dynamics is superficial. The ablation studies (comparing different VGG layers) are a good start, but the authors don't delve into *why* some layers perform better than others. What specific information captured by `conv4_3` is crucial for servoing?  Why does the deepest layer (`conv5_3`) perform worse? A deeper investigation into the learned dynamics would strengthen the paper.

Thirdly, the comparison to other methods is somewhat uneven. While TRPO is included, the experimental setup feels a bit rushed. The statement that the CNN policies needed careful initialization to converge raises questions about the robustness of those baselines. It’s not clear if the TRPO implementation was fully optimized, or if it was presented to emphasize the FQI results. The comparison to classical IBVS methods using ORB features is rather dismissive. While ORB may be less robust, the paper does not adequately address the computational cost/efficiency tradeoff.

Finally, while the fitted Q-iteration formulation is presented, the discussion around the regularization parameter (ν) and its impact on performance is minimal. A sensitivity analysis of this parameter would be beneficial.



**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Generalization:**  A discussion of potential failure modes and limitations is necessary. What types of scenes or target objects might cause this method to struggle?
*   **Dynamics Analysis:** Investigate the learned bilinear dynamics more thoroughly. Visualize the learned weights.  Can you interpret what the model is “learning” about how actions affect visual features?
*   **Robustness:** Perform experiments with added noise to the input images or disturbances to the robot’s dynamics to evaluate the robustness of the learned policy.
*   **Real-World Validation:** Even a small-scale experiment with a real robot (even in a controlled environment) would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Ablation Study:** Include an ablation study on the bilinear model. How much does the bilinear dynamics contribute to the performance, compared to directly using the pre-trained features in the RL loop?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What specific challenges do you anticipate when applying this method to real-world visual data, and what steps could be taken to address them?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of *why* the `conv4_3` features are particularly effective for visual servoing, based on your analysis of the learned dynamics?
3.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the regularization parameter (ν) in the fitted Q-iteration algorithm? Can you provide a plot of performance vs. ν?
4.   You mention the computational efficiency of your method. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of the inference time of your method vs. the TRPO and IBVS baselines?
