This paper proposes a visual servoing method combining pre-trained visual features (VGG), a bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning (Fitted Q-Iteration) for rapid adaptation to new target-following tasks. The core idea is to learn weights for the VGG features to focus on relevant visual information, enabling robust servoing with limited data. The authors demonstrate improvements over conventional methods and standard deep RL on a synthetic car-following benchmark.

**Reject.** While the problem addressed is well-defined and the proposed approach is conceptually interesting, the paper lacks sufficient validation and justification for its claims, particularly concerning the novelty and advantages of the proposed method. The reliance on a synthetic environment and the limited comparison to established baselines weaken the conclusions.

The primary concern is the lack of rigorous comparison to well-established visual servoing techniques. The paper briefly mentions IBVS with ORB and a C-COT tracker but the comparison feels cursory. A more thorough evaluation against robust, state-of-the-art IBVS methods, perhaps including approaches utilizing more modern feature descriptors, is crucial. Furthermore, the presented gains over “standard deep RL” are not convincing given the vast landscape of RL algorithms and their hyperparameter sensitivity. Simply stating improved sample efficiency without detailed comparison parameters and learning curves is insufficient.

The justification for the chosen architecture and components feels weak. Why a bilinear model specifically? While the authors mention prior work, a more detailed discussion of alternative dynamics models and a comparison of their performance would strengthen the argument. Similarly, the choice of VGG features feels somewhat arbitrary. Has experimentation been performed with other pre-trained models (e.g., ResNet, EfficientNet) to assess their suitability for this task? The ablation studies in the paper, while present, don't address these crucial aspects.

The reliance on a purely synthetic environment is a significant limitation. While CitySim3D is mentioned, the generalizability of the results to real-world scenarios is questionable. The lack of real-world evaluation casts doubt on the practical applicability of the proposed method. Synthetic data, by its nature, often lacks the complexities and noise present in real-world environments.

Finally, the presentation could be improved. The equations are presented without sufficient explanation of the involved variables, hindering understanding. More detailed explanations of the FQI implementation and hyperparameter choices are also needed.

**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

*   **Expand Baselines:** Include a more comprehensive comparison against established visual servoing techniques, including modern IBVS methods and robust feature descriptors.
*   **Real-World Evaluation:** Conduct experiments in a real-world environment to assess the generalizability of the approach.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform a more extensive ablation study to evaluate the contribution of each component of the proposed method (e.g., different pre-trained features, alternative dynamics models).
*   **Justification of Design Choices:** Provide a stronger justification for the chosen architecture and components. Why VGG? Why a bilinear model?
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** Improve the clarity of the equations and provide more detailed explanations of the implementation details.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Beyond the stated “substantial improvement,” can you quantify the computational cost (e.g., inference time, memory footprint) of your method compared to the IBVS and TRPO baselines?
2.  What specific modifications were made to the VGG network (replacing max-pooling with dilated convolutions)? What was the rationale behind this modification, and how does it affect performance?
3.  How does the performance of the method degrade when using significantly different target objects (e.g., completely different shapes, textures, or sizes)?
4.  Have you explored other reinforcement learning algorithms beyond Fitted Q-Iteration, and if so, why was FQI chosen for this specific application?
5.  What are the primary failure cases of the proposed method, and what steps could be taken to address these limitations?
