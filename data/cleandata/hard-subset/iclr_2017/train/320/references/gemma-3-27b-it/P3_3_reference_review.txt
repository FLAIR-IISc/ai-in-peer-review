## ICLR 2024 Review: Learning Visual Servoing with Reinforcement Learning

**1. Summary**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features (from pre-trained VGG networks), a bilinear model for predicting visual dynamics, and reinforcement learning (Fitted Q-Iteration). The key contribution is a sample-efficient method for learning a visual servo that adapts quickly to new targets, requiring significantly less data than traditional or standard deep RL approaches. The authors demonstrate strong performance on a synthetic car following benchmark, achieving substantial improvements over pixel-based and keypoint-based methods, and show a significant gain in sample efficiency compared to standard model-free deep RL.  The code and environment are publicly available, promoting reproducibility.

**2. Decision**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling approach to a difficult problem, achieves impressive results, and demonstrates a clear understanding of the relevant literature. The combination of pre-trained features, dynamics modeling, and sample-efficient RL is a particularly strong point. The provided code and environment are a significant plus.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Strong Motivation & Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional visual servoing techniques (reliance on hand-designed features and domain knowledge) and motivates the use of learning-based approaches. The focus on sample efficiency is particularly relevant for real-world robotic applications.
* **Novelty and Technical Soundness:** Combining pre-trained features with a learned bilinear dynamics model and Fitted Q-Iteration is a novel and well-reasoned approach. The bilinear dynamics model is a pragmatic choice, balancing complexity and accuracy. The theoretical justification for using Fitted Q-Iteration in this context is clear.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results are convincing. The improvements over baseline methods, particularly in sample efficiency, are substantial. The comparison with other RL algorithms (TRPO) further highlights the benefits of the proposed approach. The ablation studies (comparing different feature representations) are insightful. The inclusion of results on novel cars demonstrates a degree of generalization.
* **Reproducibility:** Making the code and environment publicly available is a significant strength, facilitating verification and future research.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and logically structured, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.



**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement)**

* **Discussion of Bilinear Model Limitations:** While the bilinear model is a reasonable choice, a more detailed discussion of its limitations would be beneficial. For example, are there scenarios where it breaks down? Could more complex dynamics models be explored in future work?
* **Sensitivity Analysis:** A brief sensitivity analysis of key hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, regularization coefficient, exploration noise) could provide valuable insights into the robustness of the approach.
* **Qualitative Results:** While quantitative results are strong, adding more qualitative visualizations of the servoing process (e.g., trajectories, feature activations) would enhance understanding.  Show examples of successful and failed servoing attempts.
* **Computational Cost:** Although the authors mention the faster runtime compared to other methods, a more detailed breakdown of the computational costs associated with each component (feature extraction, dynamics prediction, Q-iteration) would be useful.  How does the performance scale with image resolution and the size of the VGG network?
* **Generalization Beyond Cars:** While the car following benchmark is well-defined, discussing the potential for generalizing the approach to other target types (e.g., pedestrians, drones) would strengthen the paper. What modifications, if any, would be needed?

**5. Questions for the Authors**

* **Why was VGG chosen specifically?**  Were other pre-trained networks (e.g., ResNet) considered? What impact does the choice of pre-trained network have on performance?
* **The locally connected bilinear model enforces sparsity. How sensitive is the performance to the size of the local neighborhood (nf)?**  Did you perform any ablation studies to determine the optimal value of nf?
* **The cost function incorporates a discount factor (γ). How was this value selected, and how does it affect the learning process?**
* **The paper mentions that deeper VGG features (conv5_3) performed worse. Is this because they are too invariant, or are there other factors at play?**  Could this be mitigated by incorporating some form of attention mechanism?
* **Can you elaborate on the selection of the regularization coefficient (ν) in the Fitted Q-Iteration algorithm?**  What is the practical impact of varying this parameter?



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of visual servoing. The approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. Addressing the suggested feedback would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
