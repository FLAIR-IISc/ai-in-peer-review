This paper proposes a visual servoing method combining pre-trained visual features (VGG), a bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning (Fitted Q-Iteration) to enable fast adaptation to new targets. The core idea is to learn weights for the VGG features to focus on relevant aspects of the target, allowing robust target following with limited data.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper tackles an interesting and relevant problem – sample-efficient visual servoing – and demonstrates promising results in a synthetic environment, I find the presentation lacking in crucial details, and the novelty somewhat overstated. The experimental validation, while thorough in its comparisons, is limited to a single synthetic benchmark, raising concerns about real-world applicability. Furthermore, the benefits of the proposed approach aren’t always convincingly demonstrated *over* well-established baselines with appropriate hyperparameter tuning.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Insufficient Detail & Clarity:** The paper glosses over several important implementation details. For example, the precise form of the cost function used in the RL loop (Appendix B) and the reasoning behind its design aren’t fully elucidated. The choice of hyperparameters, particularly for the FQI algorithm, isn’t sufficiently justified. A more detailed description of the learning process—how the learning rate decayed, how the regularization strength was chosen—would be vital.  The locally connected bilinear model isn’t adequately motivated beyond reducing parameters, and its impact on performance relative to the fully connected version isn’t clear. The definition of the "local neighborhood" within the locally connected model (nf x nf) is introduced in Appendix A without sufficient context in the main paper.

2. **Limited Generalization & Benchmark:** The experiments are conducted solely in the CitySim3D environment. This raises significant concerns about the generalizability of the proposed method to more complex and realistic scenarios.  The synthetic nature of the data means many real-world challenges, like lighting variations, sensor noise, and complex background clutter, aren't addressed. While the paper claims improvement over standard deep RL, the comparison isn't entirely fair – the deep RL baselines are not tuned as aggressively as the proposed method.  A demonstration on even a simple real-world dataset would significantly strengthen the paper.

3. **Overstated Novelty:** The combination of pre-trained features, dynamics learning, and RL isn’t entirely novel. Existing work already explores aspects of this pipeline. The main contribution seems to be the specific fitted Q-iteration implementation tailored to this problem and the demonstration of sample efficiency. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently contrast its approach with existing fitted Q-iteration methods adapted to similar visual tasks. Is the bilinear dynamics model truly essential for the success of the FQI algorithm, or could a simpler model suffice?




**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component: pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics model, and FQI. For instance, what happens when using randomly initialized features, or a simpler dynamics model?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the method to different hyperparameters. Provide a learning curve showing performance as a function of training samples, and discuss the impact of the regularization parameter.
*   **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** Compare against more recent and sophisticated deep RL algorithms specifically designed for continuous control, and ensure they are properly tuned.  Consider comparing against imitation learning approaches as well.
*   **Real-World Validation:**  Even a small-scale experiment with a real robot and camera would significantly boost confidence in the method's practicality.
*   **Visualizations:** Include visualizations of the learned feature weights to provide insights into which features the algorithm deems important for servoing.
*   **Clarify the Relationship to Existing Work:** More thoroughly discuss related work on fitted Q-iteration and how the proposed method differs. 
*   **Expand the Discussion of Limitations:** Acknowledge the limitations of the synthetic benchmark and discuss potential challenges when applying the method to more complex environments.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Why was the bilinear model chosen over other dynamics models (e.g., neural networks)? What are the trade-offs between computational complexity and accuracy?
*   What is the justification for the specific cost function used in the RL loop? How would different cost functions affect the learning process?
*   How does the performance of the proposed method vary with the choice of pre-trained features (e.g., different layers of VGG, or features from other networks)?
*   Can you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process? What strategies were used to select the optimal values for the FQI parameters?
*   Have you considered using a different exploration strategy during the RL phase? How would a more sophisticated exploration strategy (e.g., using an intrinsic motivation signal) affect the sample efficiency of the algorithm?
