This paper proposes a visual servoing method that combines pre-trained visual features (VGG), a bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning (fitted Q-iteration) for target following. The core idea is to learn a servo policy that efficiently adapts to new targets using minimal data, leveraging the representational power of pre-trained networks while addressing robustness to visual variations and distractors. The authors demonstrate improvements over conventional image-based and hand-designed feature approaches on a synthetic car-following benchmark, with significant gains in sample efficiency compared to standard deep RL methods.

**Reject.** While the problem is well-motivated and the combination of techniques is interesting, the paper lacks the rigor and extensive validation needed to convince me of the claimed advancements. The improvements reported appear substantial, but are demonstrated solely on a synthetic benchmark, and the justification for design choices feels somewhat lacking. The reliance on a single, potentially narrow, benchmark raises concerns about generalizability.

The primary issue is the lack of comparison to well-established, robust visual servoing techniques *beyond* the basic image-pixel and hand-crafted feature baselines. The field has decades of accumulated knowledge regarding feature design and control strategies (e.g., 2D/3D feature tracking with robust estimators, interaction matrix control, disturbance observers), and a compelling paper should clearly demonstrate superiority over these, not just improvements on rudimentary alternatives. The authors mention IBVS, but the comparison is not comprehensive. A stronger comparison would involve adapting state-of-the-art IBVS methods to the same benchmark, including robust feature tracking and potentially even learning-based adaptations of those techniques. 

Furthermore, the ablation studies, while present, are not exhaustive enough. The impact of each component (pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, fitted Q-iteration) needs to be more clearly isolated and quantified. For instance, could a simpler reinforcement learning algorithm (e.g., PPO) achieve comparable performance with the same feature dynamics? The claim of a "two orders of magnitude" improvement in sample efficiency is bold and requires significantly more supporting evidence, ideally with a detailed comparison of learning curves and confidence intervals.

Finally, the paper lacks a thorough discussion of potential failure modes and limitations. What happens when the target undergoes significant and abrupt changes in appearance, or is heavily occluded? How sensitive is the method to the choice of pre-trained features? A more critical self-assessment would strengthen the paper considerably.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Benchmark Details:** Provide more details about the synthetic environment. What is the range of lighting conditions, viewpoints, and occlusions? How realistic are the dynamics of the car and the quadcopter?
*   **Bilinear Dynamics Justification:** The choice of a bilinear model is motivated by prior work, but a more detailed explanation of why this specific model is well-suited for this application would be helpful. Why not explore other dynamics models?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Explore the sensitivity of the method to the various hyperparameters (learning rates, regularization coefficients, exploration noise, etc.).
*   **Computational Cost:**  While the authors mention the speed of the policy, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of training and inference would be valuable.
*   **Generalization:** Discuss the limitations of the learned dynamics model and the potential for domain transfer. Would the learned model generalize to different cars or environments?
*   **Visualizations:** Include more visualizations of the learned features, the dynamics model, and the trajectories of the quadcopter. This would help to understand how the method works and why it succeeds or fails.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Have you compared your method to more sophisticated IBVS techniques (e.g., those incorporating robust feature tracking and adaptive control)? If not, why?
*   Can you provide a detailed comparison of the learning curves for your method and the baseline RL algorithms (e.g., PPO) on the same benchmark?
*   How sensitive is the performance of your method to the choice of pre-trained features? Have you experimented with other pre-trained networks (e.g., ResNet, Inception)?
*   What are the main failure modes of your method, and what steps could be taken to mitigate them?
*   How would you expect your method to perform on a real-world dataset with more realistic lighting conditions, viewpoints, and occlusions?
