This paper proposes a visual servoing method combining pre-trained features (VGG), a bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning (fitted Q-iteration) to achieve sample-efficient target following. The core idea is to learn weights for relevant visual features to robustly track a target despite variations in appearance and occlusions, requiring minimal training data. The method is evaluated on a synthetic car following benchmark, demonstrating improvement over pixel-based and hand-designed feature approaches, and significantly better sample efficiency than standard deep RL.

**Reject.** While the core idea of leveraging pre-trained features and a bilinear dynamics model for efficient visual servoing is promising, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet collectively substantial, flaws in presentation, methodological rigor, and experimental validation. The excessive detail devoted to describing relatively standard components (e.g., FQI) overshadows a thorough discussion of the novel contributions, and several questionable choices and inconsistencies undermine the validity of the conclusions.

The presentation is overly verbose and lacks conciseness. For instance, the introduction is excessively long and repeats information. Several sentences are awkwardly phrased or unnecessarily complex. The frequent citations, while demonstrating awareness of the field, often feel gratuitous and disrupt the flow of the text. 

Methodologically, the choice of a bilinear model, while justified, isn’t rigorously motivated beyond citing prior work. The impact of the dilated convolutions on the VGG network isn’t adequately explored. Furthermore, the hyperparameter choices (e.g., regularization coefficient ν in FQI, exploration noise) are not clearly justified or subjected to ablation studies. The cost function used for reinforcement learning is also not sufficiently explained in its connection to the servoing task – why specifically is the squared error in position used? 

The experimental evaluation, while presenting promising results, is limited. The synthetic environment, while convenient, lacks the complexity and realism of real-world scenarios. The comparison to other methods feels incomplete. TRPO and other RL algorithms are presented but the hyperparameter tuning for these algorithms is glossed over, making a fair comparison difficult.  The claim of “two orders of magnitude” improvement in sample efficiency is overstated; while substantial, it requires a more precise quantification beyond a qualitative statement. The sensitivity analysis to the number of sampling iterations (Figure 7) feels superficial and doesn't convincingly demonstrate the stability of the learned policy. The tables are difficult to interpret; information is spread across multiple tables that could be consolidated for clarity. The lack of statistical significance testing for the reported results is concerning.

Finally, the code and data availability is appreciated, but the reliance on external GitHub repositories without proper versioning raises reproducibility concerns.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Conciseness:** Trim the introduction and other sections to focus on the core contributions. Eliminate redundant explanations and unnecessary citations.
*   **Methodological Rigor:** Provide a more detailed justification for the bilinear dynamics model and the choice of hyperparameters. Conduct ablation studies to assess the sensitivity of the results to key parameters. Clarify the connection between the cost function and the desired servoing behavior.
*   **Experimental Validation:**  Consider evaluating the method in a more realistic simulation environment or, ideally, on real-world robotic platforms. Include a more thorough comparison to relevant baseline methods, with properly tuned hyperparameters. Report statistical significance tests for all experimental results.
*   **Clarity:** Restructure the tables and figures to improve readability. Clearly define all symbols and abbreviations.
*   **Reproducibility:** Provide specific commit hashes for the referenced GitHub repositories.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why was the bilinear model chosen over other dynamics models (e.g., neural networks)? What are the limitations of the bilinear model in this context?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the regularization coefficient ν in FQI? Was a grid search or other optimization method used to select this value?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the cost function used for reinforcement learning and its rationale?
4.  The paper mentions a "locally connected" bilinear model. How does this differ from the standard bilinear model, and what is the benefit of using this approach in terms of computational complexity and performance?
5.  Regarding the "two orders of magnitude" improvement in sample efficiency: can you quantify this claim more precisely (e.g., with a specific number of samples required to achieve a certain level of performance)?
6.  What steps were taken to ensure the reproducibility of the experiments, given the reliance on external GitHub repositories? Are specific commit hashes available?
