This paper proposes a visual servoing method combining pre-trained features (VGG), a bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning (Fitted Q-Iteration) to achieve sample-efficient target following. The core idea is to learn weights for relevant visual features to robustly track a target despite variations in appearance and occlusions. The authors demonstrate improvements over conventional methods and other deep reinforcement learning approaches on a synthetic car following benchmark.

**Reject.** While the problem addressed is well-motivated and relevant, the paper lacks sufficient evidence to support its claims of substantial improvement and sample efficiency. The experimental setup, while detailed, feels somewhat contrived, and the gains demonstrated don’t appear significant enough to justify the complexity of the proposed approach, especially given the reliance on pre-trained features. The improvements over strong baselines aren’t convincingly demonstrated, and the extensive reliance on a synthetic environment raises concerns about real-world applicability.

The primary concern lies in the comparative analysis. While the authors compare against several baselines, the parameter tuning and implementation details for those baselines aren’t thoroughly described, making it difficult to assess whether the observed improvements are truly attributable to the proposed method or to differences in implementation. For instance, the TRPO implementations are mentioned as requiring significantly more samples, but the specific hyperparameter choices and training procedures aren’t sufficiently detailed for a fair comparison. The claim of “more than two orders of magnitude” improvement in sample efficiency needs stronger justification—the reported gains, while positive, don’t seem that dramatic when considering the relatively simple task and synthetic environment. 

Furthermore, the benefits of the bilinear dynamics model are not clearly articulated. While it's motivated by prior work, its contribution to the overall performance is not isolated. Is the performance gain primarily driven by the feature weighting via RL, or does the dynamics model play a crucial role? Ablation studies directly comparing performance *without* the dynamics model would be valuable. The locally connected bilinear model is presented as a parameter-efficient alternative, but the advantages are not quantitatively demonstrated. 

The reliance on the CitySim3D environment is also a weakness. While useful for initial development, results in a simulated environment often don’t translate directly to real-world performance. A demonstration on a real-world dataset, even a limited one, would significantly strengthen the paper. The justification for selecting the specific cost function is also lacking. Why this particular formulation? How sensitive is the performance to changes in the cost function?

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Baseline Details:** Provide significantly more detail about the implementation and hyperparameter tuning for *all* baseline methods. This is crucial for a fair comparison.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to isolate the contributions of the bilinear dynamics model and the feature weighting scheme.
*   **Real-World Evaluation:** Include experiments on a real-world dataset, even if limited in scope.
*   **Cost Function Justification:** Provide a more thorough justification for the chosen cost function.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests for all performance comparisons.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** More explicitly discuss the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding its potential challenges in real-world scenarios.
* **Clarify 'Sample Efficiency':** Precisely define what is meant by sample efficiency in this context. Is it the number of trajectories, number of interactions, or something else?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What specific efforts were made to optimize the hyperparameters for the baseline methods to ensure a fair comparison? Please provide more detail on the training procedure (e.g., learning rates, batch sizes, number of iterations) for each baseline.
*   Could you provide a quantitative comparison of the computational cost (e.g., training time, inference time) for the proposed method versus the baseline methods?
*   How sensitive is the performance of the proposed method to the choice of pre-trained features? Would using features from a different network (e.g., ResNet) significantly impact the results?
*   What are the main challenges you anticipate when deploying this method in a real-world robotic system, and how might those challenges be addressed?
*   How does the performance of your approach degrade with increased levels of occlusion or more complex background clutter?
