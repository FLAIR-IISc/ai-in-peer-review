## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper Title:** Learning Visual Servoing with Reinforcement Learning

**Reviewer:** [Your Name]

**1. Summary of Claims**

This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features (from pre-trained networks), learned predictive dynamics, and reinforcement learning. The authors argue that this approach enables sample-efficient learning of visual servos, requiring significantly less data than existing methods, particularly when adapting to new targets. Their key contributions include: utilizing pre-trained VGG features, a bilinear model for visual dynamics prediction, and a sample-efficient Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn feature weights for robust servoing. The paper demonstrates strong results on a synthetic car following benchmark, outperforming pixel-based and hand-engineered feature approaches, as well as showing substantial gains in sample efficiency compared to standard deep RL algorithms.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting combination of techniques and shows promising empirical results, the presentation lacks sufficient detail and clarity to fully evaluate the validity and impact of the proposed approach. Specifically, the experimental setup and justification for design choices are insufficiently detailed, making it difficult to assess the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

*   **Lack of Detail in Experimental Setup:** The description of the experimental environment (CitySim3D) is brief, and critical parameters related to the simulator (physics, camera parameters, etc.) are missing. This makes it difficult to understand the complexity of the task and the challenges the algorithm faces. Crucially, details about the hand-coded policy used to generate the initial dynamics training data are scarce. How representative is this data of the operational space the RL policy explores?
*   **Justification of Design Choices:** Several design choices lack clear justification. For example, the choice of the bilinear model, the specific architecture of the VGG network modifications (replacing max-pooling with dilated convolutions), and the parameters of the FQI algorithm (regularization coefficient, learning rate) are not thoroughly explained. Why were these choices made over alternative approaches? 
*   **Limited Comparison:** While the authors compare their method to several baselines, the comparison feels somewhat superficial. The deep RL baselines are not described in sufficient detail, making it difficult to understand their performance characteristics. Further, the comparison with classical IBVS methods feels a bit disconnected; the advantages of a learned dynamics model *specifically* over those methods aren’t clearly highlighted.
*   **Overstated Claims of Sample Efficiency:** The claim of "two orders of magnitude" improvement in sample efficiency over standard deep RL is impactful and requires stronger justification. The precise details of the compared algorithms and the number of samples used in each experiment are critical for verifying this claim. The authors do not provide a detailed breakdown of sample complexity.
* **Reproducibility:** While code is available, the minimal description of the experimental setup makes replicating the results challenging.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement)**

*   **Expand Experimental Details:** Provide a more thorough description of the experimental environment, including simulator parameters, camera settings, and the details of the initial hand-coded policy.
*   **Justify Design Choices:** Explain the rationale behind key design choices, such as the choice of the bilinear model, the VGG network modifications, and the FQI parameters.
*   **Strengthen Baseline Comparison:** Provide a more detailed description of the baseline algorithms, including their hyperparameters and training procedures. Perform a more rigorous comparison, highlighting the specific advantages of the proposed approach over each baseline.
*   **Provide Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of each component of the proposed approach (pre-trained features, bilinear model, FQI algorithm).
*   **Analyze Failure Cases:** Discuss the limitations of the approach and analyze failure cases. What types of scenarios does the algorithm struggle with, and why?
*   **Clarify the Role of Dynamics Prediction:** While the approach uses dynamics prediction, it's unclear how crucial this component is. How does performance degrade when using a simpler dynamics model or no dynamics model at all?
* **Quantitative Analysis of Feature Weights:** It would be insightful to visualize the learned weights assigned to different features, to understand which features are most important for the servoing task.
*   **Address the Computational Cost:** The paper mentions the runtime of the method but does not discuss its scalability to more complex scenarios.

**5. Questions for Authors**

*   Could you provide more details about the hand-coded policy used to generate the data for training the visual dynamics model? What were the key design considerations and how representative is this data of the exploration space during RL training?
*   What is the sensitivity of the results to the choice of the bilinear model? Have you experimented with other dynamics models, such as recurrent neural networks?
*   Could you elaborate on the ablation studies performed, and what insights did you gain from them regarding the contribution of each component of the proposed approach?
*   What are the limitations of the current approach, and what directions for future research do you see as most promising?
*   How does the performance of your method scale with the complexity of the environment (e.g., more distractors, more challenging lighting conditions)?
*   What is the computational overhead of the bilinear dynamics prediction compared to a direct feature-based approach?



In summary, while the paper proposes an interesting approach to visual servoing, the lack of detail and justification prevents a positive assessment. Addressing the points above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase confidence in the validity and impact of the results.
