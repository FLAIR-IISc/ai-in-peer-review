This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features, a learned predictive dynamics model, and reinforcement learning. The central goal is to achieve sample-efficient learning of a visual servo that can quickly adapt to new targets. The approach leverages pre-trained features from a VGG network and uses a bilinear model to estimate feature dynamics, coupled with a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration procedure for weight selection.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The combination of leveraging pre-trained features with a dynamics model and reinforcement learning to achieve efficient visual servoing is a significant contribution. The experimental results demonstrate clear improvement over existing methods, particularly in sample efficiency, a crucial aspect for real-world robotic applications.

The paper is well-motivated. Visual servoing is a fundamental problem in robotics, and the limitations of traditional approaches relying on hand-designed features and models are clearly articulated. The motivation for leveraging the power of deep learning and reinforcement learning is strong, and the focus on sample efficiency is particularly relevant. The literature review is comprehensive and appropriately frames the work within the existing landscape.

The paper convincingly supports its claims. The experiments on the synthetic car following benchmark demonstrate substantial improvement over competing approaches, including those using pixel intensities, hand-designed keypoints, and standard deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The ablation studies and comparisons with different optimization methods (CEM, TRPO) strengthen the validity of the proposed approach. The performance gains, particularly the two orders of magnitude improvement in sample efficiency over standard deep RL, are impressive. The availability of code and supplementary videos further enhances reproducibility and allows for independent verification of the results. The detailed description of the experimental setup and hyperparameter settings adds to the rigor of the evaluation.

Here are some suggestions for improving the paper:

*   **Clarify the role of the bilinear model:** While the paper mentions the bilinear model, its connection to enabling efficient learning isn't fully articulated. A more intuitive explanation of *why* this model is beneficial for visual servoing, beyond just approximating dynamics, would be helpful.
*   **Discussion of limitations:** The paper focuses on a simulated environment. Discussing the potential challenges of transferring this approach to real-world scenarios (e.g., robustness to sensor noise, domain adaptation) would strengthen the paper.
*   **Ablation study on the VGG layers:** The results show performance varies depending on the VGG layer used. A more detailed analysis of *why* certain layers perform better than others would be valuable. Is it related to the level of abstraction, spatial resolution, or invariance?
*   **Hyperparameter sensitivity:**  A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings (e.g., learning rate, regularization strength) would give more confidence in the robustness of the approach.
*   **Expand on the cost function:** The cost function is mentioned briefly. Providing more details on its design choices, and perhaps exploring alternative cost functions, could be useful.

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   The paper states the bilinear model allows the visual servo to "predict how motion of the robot’s camera will affect the perceived feature values." Is this prediction used *solely* within the Q-learning framework, or is it also used in any other part of the control loop (e.g., for action selection)?
*   The choice of using VGG features pre-trained on ImageNet seems reasonable. Have you explored using features pre-trained on different datasets, or perhaps datasets more relevant to robotic perception, and if so, how did the performance compare?
*   The code availability is excellent. Is there any plan to release the trained dynamics models along with the code? This would facilitate further research and comparisons.
*   In the discussion of classical IBVS methods, you mention the importance of ground truth depth values. How sensitive is the proposed method to inaccuracies in depth estimation? Would the performance degrade significantly if depth estimation were imperfect?
*   You mention that deeper VGG features become less suitable for approximating Q-values. Is this because of the loss of spatial information, or is there another underlying reason?
