This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features (from pre-trained VGG networks), learned predictive dynamics (using a bilinear model), and reinforcement learning. The core idea is to leverage the representations learned on large datasets to enable fast adaptation to new targets with limited data. The authors propose a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration procedure to learn appropriate weights for the visual features, optimizing a simple servoing policy that minimizes the Euclidean distance between predicted and target features.

I recommend **rejecting** this paper. While the approach is promising and the idea of combining pre-trained features, dynamics learning, and RL is well-motivated, the paper lacks sufficient experimental validation and clarity regarding the impact of key design choices. The synthetic benchmark, while well-defined, feels insufficient to demonstrate broad applicability. More importantly, the results, while showing improvement over baselines, aren’t substantial enough to warrant acceptance given the complexity added by the proposed approach.

Here’s a breakdown of my reasoning:

**1. Limited Empirical Validation:** The experiments primarily focus on a single, synthetic environment (CitySim3D). While the authors compare against several baselines, the improvements achieved aren't overwhelming, and the generalizability of the approach to real-world scenarios is entirely unclear. The synthetic environment is relatively simple, and the conditions under which the approach excels (e.g., specific lighting, viewpoint variations) aren't clearly delineated. There is no evaluation on even a modest real-world dataset. A key claim of the paper is sample efficiency, yet the number of samples required (20 trajectories) isn't dramatically lower than what could be achieved with simpler methods, especially given the pre-trained features.

**2. Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper presents a complex system with several components. A crucial missing element is a thorough ablation study. What is the contribution of each component (pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, fitted Q-iteration)? How much does performance degrade if one component is removed or replaced? The authors mention testing different VGG layers, but a more systematic ablation across all components is needed to justify the complexity of the full system. For example, could a simple linear regression on the pre-trained features, trained with RL, achieve comparable performance?

**3. Unclear Justification for Design Choices:** Several design choices lack strong justification. The choice of a bilinear model for dynamics prediction, while motivated by prior work, isn't convincingly demonstrated to be superior to simpler alternatives (e.g., a linear model). Similarly, the fitted Q-iteration algorithm, while sample efficient, introduces its own complexities.  The authors don’t provide a comprehensive analysis of why this particular RL algorithm was selected or how its parameters were tuned. The regularisation parameter 'nu' in FQI is mentioned, but a sensitivity analysis is missing.

**4. Reporting of Results & Statistical Significance:** While the authors present results in the form of cost curves, there's a lack of statistical analysis to demonstrate the significance of the improvements. Error bars or statistical tests (e.g., t-tests) would be crucial to determine whether the observed differences are meaningful.



**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Real-World Experiments:** Include experiments on a real-world dataset, even if it's a small-scale one. This would significantly strengthen the paper and demonstrate the practical relevance of the approach.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform a detailed ablation study to evaluate the contribution of each component.
*   **Comparative Analysis:** Provide a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art methods, including model-free deep RL algorithms trained with larger datasets.
*   **Sensitivity Analysis:** Perform a sensitivity analysis on the key hyperparameters of the algorithm (e.g., regularization parameters, learning rates).
*   **Visualization of Dynamics:** Provide visualizations of the learned dynamics to help understand what the model is capturing and how it's being used for servoing.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** Explicitly discuss the limitations of the approach, such as its reliance on pre-trained features and its sensitivity to the quality of the dynamics model.
* **Clarify cost function:** Explain why the cost function is defined in this manner, and what its relationship to the task is.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What is the computational cost of the proposed method compared to the baseline methods, both during training and inference?
*   How sensitive is the performance of the method to the choice of pre-trained features? Have you explored other pre-trained networks (e.g., ResNet, Inception)?
*   What is the impact of the image resolution on the performance?
*   Why was the bilinear dynamics model chosen over simpler models like linear regression?
*   Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of why the fitted Q-iteration algorithm is particularly well-suited for this problem?
*   What are the biggest challenges you foresee when deploying this approach in a real-world scenario?
