This paper proposes a visual servoing method combining pre-trained visual features (VGG), a bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning (Fitted Q-Iteration) for rapid adaptation to new target following tasks. The core idea is to learn weights for the VGG features to focus on the relevant parts of the image, minimizing the distance between predicted and target features. The authors demonstrate promising results on a synthetic car following benchmark, achieving improved sample efficiency compared to standard deep RL and conventional visual servoing approaches.

**Reject.** While the idea of leveraging pre-trained features and a dynamics model for visual servoing is reasonable, the paper presents a relatively incremental contribution supported by limited experimentation and lacks sufficient detail to assess reproducibility. The significant gains claimed over other methods aren’t convincingly demonstrated, and the reliance on a synthetic environment raises concerns about generalizability.

The primary argument for rejection stems from the insufficient validation. The results are reported on a single, synthetic benchmark (CitySim3D). While the authors acknowledge the limitations of simulation, the lack of experiments on real-world data or more diverse simulated environments severely weakens the claims of practical applicability. The comparison to other deep RL methods is not entirely fair; the significantly improved sample efficiency is shown against methods not explicitly designed for the *transfer* or *adaptation* scenario the authors highlight as motivation. A more compelling comparison would be against meta-learning or few-shot learning algorithms specifically targeting rapid adaptation in reinforcement learning. Furthermore, the presented ablation studies (Fig. 6) are limited, particularly the comparison to TRPO. Running TRPO for only 2 iterations before comparing it to FQI seems insufficient to allow TRPO to demonstrate its potential. The comparison to IBVS methods relies on relatively simplistic implementations; a more robust implementation of IBVS, potentially incorporating learned features for feature selection, could offer a stronger baseline. 

Further, the methodological details are sometimes lacking. The specific regularization coefficient (ν) used in FQI and its sensitivity analysis are not clearly presented. The choice of the bilinear dynamics model, while motivated, doesn’t have an extensive justification, and its performance compared to other dynamics models (e.g., a simple linear model) isn’t thoroughly explored. The choice of the specific VGG layers used for feature extraction seems somewhat arbitrary and a more systematic exploration of different layers and combinations would strengthen the results. 

Finally, the supplementary materials (code and videos) are valuable but not sufficient to fully reproduce the experiments, as the environment setup and specific configurations are not fully documented.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Real-World Validation:** The most important improvement would be to demonstrate the method’s performance on real-world data, even with a limited setup.
*   **Broader Comparison:**  Compare against more relevant state-of-the-art algorithms for few-shot or transfer reinforcement learning.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more comprehensive ablation studies, exploring different choices for the dynamics model, VGG layers, regularization parameters, and RL algorithms.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the results to various hyperparameters, particularly the regularization coefficient in FQI.
*   **Reproducibility:** Provide detailed instructions for setting up the environment and running the code, ensuring full reproducibility of the results.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** Explore the theoretical properties of the proposed approach, such as convergence guarantees or sample complexity bounds.

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  What is the computational cost of the proposed method compared to the IBVS methods, specifically considering the feature extraction and Jacobian calculation?
2.  How was the initial policy for the FQI algorithm selected, and what impact does the initial policy have on the final performance?
3.  Have you considered using meta-learning techniques to further improve the sample efficiency and generalization ability of the method?
4.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the choice of the bilinear dynamics model and justify its appropriateness for this application? Specifically, how does this perform relative to simpler, fully connected models?
5.  Regarding the TRPO comparisons, could you provide the learning curves for TRPO with more iterations to better assess its potential performance?
