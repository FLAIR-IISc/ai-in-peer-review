This paper proposes a visual servoing method that combines pre-trained visual features (VGG network), a bilinear model for predicting feature dynamics, and reinforcement learning (Fitted Q-Iteration) for efficient learning of servoing policies. The core idea is to leverage the robustness of pre-trained features while quickly adapting to new targets using a sample-efficient RL approach. The method is demonstrated on a synthetic car following benchmark, showing significant improvements over pixel-based and hand-designed feature approaches, as well as substantial sample efficiency gains compared to standard deep RL.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents a promising approach and demonstrates encouraging results, the current presentation and justification fall short of establishing a truly convincing contribution for ICLR. The key weakness lies in the limited novelty of the combination of techniques and the lack of a strong comparative baseline to highlight the benefit of the proposed method.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** The individual components – pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, and Fitted Q-Iteration – are all well-established techniques. The primary contribution appears to be the *combination* of these, but the justification for *why* this specific combination is particularly effective is not sufficiently elaborated. The authors state the approach addresses robustness and sample efficiency, but don’t fully articulate why this combination is superior to, for instance, directly applying deep RL to the pre-trained features or employing a different RL algorithm.

2. **Limited Baselines:** The comparison to “standard deep reinforcement learning algorithms” is vague. While the authors claim a two-orders-of-magnitude improvement, it's unclear which algorithms are being compared against and with what hyperparameter tuning. Furthermore, the comparison to other visual servoing methods (IBVS, TRPO) is helpful, but it’s unclear *why* the proposed method outperforms them beyond a general statement about incorporating dynamics. The performance gains over TRPO, while notable, seem more attributable to the efficiency of FQI than inherent strengths of the method's architecture.  A more rigorous comparison with a state-of-the-art model-based RL algorithm adapted to the image input space would be beneficial.

3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** It's difficult to discern the contribution of each component. While results are shown with different feature levels (VGG conv1_2 to conv5_3), a more systematic ablation study – e.g., removing the bilinear dynamics, using a different RL algorithm – would clarify which parts of the pipeline are most crucial for the observed performance.

4. **Synthetic Environment:** While a synthetic environment allows for controlled experimentation, it's unclear how well the method would generalize to real-world scenarios with more complex visual variations, sensor noise, and unpredictable dynamics. The paper doesn’t address this potential limitation adequately.



**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

* **Clarify Novelty:** More precisely articulate what distinguishes this work from existing approaches. Is it the specific bilinear model, the choice of FQI, the integration of pre-trained features with dynamics learning, or a combination thereof?
* **Stronger Baselines:** Include a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art deep RL algorithms (e.g., PPO, SAC) adapted to the same task and input.  Specifically, reporting the sample efficiency of these baselines is crucial.
* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to quantify the contribution of each component of the proposed method.
* **Generalization Discussion:** Discuss the limitations of the synthetic environment and potential challenges in transferring the method to real-world applications. Could sim-to-real techniques be used?
* **Cost Function Rationale:** Provide more justification for the choice of the cost function used in the RL training process.
* **Visualizations:** Include more visualizations of the learned feature dynamics and the weighting assigned to different features. This would help in understanding the learned behavior of the system.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. What specific deep RL algorithms were used for the comparison and what hyperparameters were tuned for each?
2. Why was Fitted Q-Iteration chosen over other RL algorithms (e.g., PPO, SAC)? What are the specific advantages of FQI in this context?
3. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the bilinear model? Could a simpler dynamics model achieve comparable results?
4. Could you provide more insight into the features that are assigned the highest weights by the RL algorithm? Are these consistent across different targets?
5. What steps could be taken to improve the generalization of the method to real-world scenarios?
