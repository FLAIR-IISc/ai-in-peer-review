## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper:** Visual Servoing with Learned Dynamics and Fitted Q-Iteration

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features (from pre-trained VGG networks), a learned bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning via Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI). The key contribution is a sample-efficient approach to learning a visual servo, requiring significantly less data than traditional or standard deep RL methods. The authors demonstrate this on a synthetic car following benchmark, showcasing improvements over pixel-based and keypoint-based approaches, and demonstrating strong generalization to novel target objects.  The code and environment are publicly available, facilitating reproducibility.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated approach to a challenging problem in robotics. The results are compelling, particularly the demonstration of sample efficiency, and the publicly available resources are a significant plus.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Problem & Motivation:** The paper clearly identifies a crucial limitation of traditional visual servoing – the reliance on hand-engineered features and models – and proposes a learning-based solution to address this. The motivation for sample efficiency is also well-articulated, highlighting the practical need for quickly adapting to new targets.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The combination of pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics modeling, and FQI is a sensible and well-justified approach. The use of a bilinear model is a reasonable simplification for capturing dynamics in the visual space, and the FQI algorithm is appropriately applied for learning the optimal weighting of features. The linearization of the dynamics for efficient optimization is also well-explained.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are convincing. The performance gains over baseline methods (pixel-based, keypoint-based, and standard deep RL) are substantial, and the generalization to unseen cars further strengthens the claims. The ablation studies comparing different feature representations (VGG layers) provides valuable insight into the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The comparison with TRPO, highlighting the superior sample efficiency of FQI, is particularly compelling.
*   **Reproducibility:**  The public availability of the code and the CitySim3D environment are extremely valuable and significantly enhance the credibility of the work.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Discussion of Limitations:** While the results are strong, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the limitations.  For example, the method is evaluated in a simulated environment. How might the performance be affected by the realities of real-world image data (e.g., sensor noise, lighting changes, more complex occlusions)? The assumption of known camera parameters should also be explicitly addressed.
*   **Visualizations:**  While the paper includes example trajectories, more visualizations would be helpful.  Visualizing the learned feature weights (w and λ) could provide further insight into which features are most important for the servoing task.  Perhaps showing the weighted feature activations when tracking a target would be informative.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the method to key hyperparameters (e.g., the regularization coefficient ν in FQI, the learning rate for the dynamics model) would be beneficial.
*   **Connection to Related Work:** While the related work section is adequate, expanding the discussion of recent advances in model-based reinforcement learning and meta-learning for robotic control could further contextualize the contribution.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   The paper mentions that deeper VGG features (conv5\_3) performed worse. Could you elaborate on the hypothesis that this is due to a lack of spatial information? Is there any quantitative analysis to support this claim (e.g., analyzing the variance of activations across spatial locations)?
*   The bilinear dynamics model appears to be a key component of the approach. Have you explored other dynamics modeling techniques (e.g., recurrent neural networks) and compared their performance to the bilinear model?
*   In the real world, camera calibration is rarely perfect. How robust is the approach to inaccuracies in camera calibration? Would the method need to be modified to handle uncalibrated cameras?
*   The computation time for running the FQI is given. What is the end-to-end time for performing visual servoing with the learned policy?



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of visual servoing. The combination of learned features, dynamics modeling, and reinforcement learning is a promising direction, and the authors have convincingly demonstrated the effectiveness of their approach. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
