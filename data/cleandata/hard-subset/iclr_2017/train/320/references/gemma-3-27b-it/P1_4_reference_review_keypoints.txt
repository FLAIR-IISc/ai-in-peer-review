- Paper proposes visual servoing via pre-trained VGG features, a bilinear visual-dynamics model, and sample-efficient Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI).
- Authors claim large sample-efficiency gains (up to two orders of magnitude) over standard deep RL on a synthetic car-following benchmark.
- Recommendation: Reject due to insufficient detail and unclear presentation limiting evaluation of validity and impact.
- Experimental setup description is too brief; critical simulator parameters (physics, camera) are missing.
- Details about the hand-coded policy used to generate dynamics training data are scarce, leaving representativeness unclear.
- Key design choices (bilinear model, VGG modifications, FQI hyperparameters) lack justification and comparison to alternatives.
- Baseline comparisons are limited and described superficially, reducing confidence in relative performance claims.
- The strong claim of two orders-of-magnitude sample-efficiency improvement is inadequately substantiated without detailed sample complexity breakdowns.
- Reproducibility is hampered despite code availability because of missing experimental details.
- Suggestion: expand experimental details (simulator, camera, hand-coded policy) to aid reproducibility and clarity.
- Suggestion: provide rationale and comparisons for design choices (model architectures and FQI parameters).
- Suggestion: strengthen baseline descriptions and perform more rigorous comparative experiments.
- Suggestion: conduct ablation studies to quantify contributions of pre-trained features, bilinear model, and FQI.
- Suggestion: analyze failure cases and limitations, and show performance degradation with simpler or no dynamics model.
- Suggestion: visualize learned feature weights to interpret which features drive servoing performance.
- Suggestion: report and discuss computational cost and scalability to more complex scenarios.
- Questions for authors include details of the hand-coded policy, sensitivity to the bilinear model, ablation study insights, identified limitations and future directions, scalability to harder environments, and computational overhead of the bilinear predictor.
