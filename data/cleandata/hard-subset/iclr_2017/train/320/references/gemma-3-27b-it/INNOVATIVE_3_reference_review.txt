This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, and proposes a method combining learned visual features (from pre-trained VGG networks), a bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning (Fitted Q-Iteration) to achieve sample efficiency. The central claim is that by leveraging pre-trained features and learning a dynamics model, the robot can quickly adapt to new targets with minimal training data.

**Reject.** While the idea of combining pre-trained features, dynamics learning, and RL for visual servoing is intriguing, the paper feels somewhat underdeveloped and lacks the rigor expected for ICLR. The core novelty – the FQI approach – isn’t convincingly demonstrated to be significantly superior to other methods *beyond* the specific experimental setup, and the justification for design choices often feels intuitive rather than thoroughly investigated.

The primary concern lies with the experimental validation. The synthetic car-following environment, while well-defined, appears relatively constrained. The improvements over baselines (especially TRPO) are significant, but the authors don't deeply analyze *why* their method excels. Is the bilinear dynamics model truly crucial, or is the benefit derived primarily from the pre-trained features and the specific weighting learned by FQI? The ablation studies are insufficient to disentangle these effects. Moreover, the results on novel cars, while showing some generalization, are not overwhelmingly convincing and warrant further investigation. The discussion focuses heavily on computational efficiency, which is valuable, but doesn't compensate for a lack of detailed analysis of the *algorithmic* advantages.

Further supporting this rejection is the somewhat hand-wavy explanation of certain choices. The rationale for using dilated convolutions, while mentioned, isn’t fully explored in relation to the task. Similarly, the choice of the cost function and regularization parameters feels somewhat arbitrary.  The appendix provides some details, but a more systematic exploration of these parameters and their impact on performance would strengthen the paper. The comparisons to classical visual servoing methods, while included, feel a bit tacked on and don’t offer a nuanced discussion of the trade-offs. Finally, while the code release is commendable, the reliance on a custom environment (CitySim3D) makes reproducibility slightly more challenging for the broader community.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more thorough ablation studies. Specifically, test the performance *without* the bilinear dynamics model (i.e., directly applying FQI to the VGG features). This would isolate the contribution of the dynamics learning component. Also, explore different pre-trained feature backbones (e.g., ResNet) to assess the robustness of the approach.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Perform a sensitivity analysis of the key hyperparameters (learning rate, regularization coefficient, exploration noise) to demonstrate the robustness of the results.
*   **Generalization Analysis:** Expand the generalization experiments. Test the trained policies on more diverse sets of novel cars (e.g., cars with significantly different appearances, in different lighting conditions, and with more occlusions).
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Provide a more detailed theoretical justification for the choice of the bilinear dynamics model. Why is it expected to be a good approximation for visual features during camera motion?
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art RL:** Provide a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art model-free deep RL algorithms (e.g., PPO, SAC) *within the same experimental setup*.  The claim of "two orders of magnitude" improvement needs more robust evidence.
*   **Error Analysis:** Include a qualitative error analysis, showing examples of failures and identifying the underlying causes.
*  **Discussion of Limitations:** Explicitly discuss the limitations of the approach. What types of scenarios might cause the method to fail? How could the method be extended to handle more complex environments?



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The choice of the bilinear model seems crucial. Can you provide more justification for this choice? Have you experimented with other dynamics models (e.g., neural networks) and, if so, what were the results?
2.  The experiments are performed in a simulator. How do you anticipate the performance of this method to degrade in a real-world scenario with sensor noise, imperfect dynamics models, and unmodeled disturbances?
3.  The regularization parameter ν appears to be important. How was this value selected, and what is its impact on the learned weights?
4.  Could you clarify how the cost function is designed to encourage long-term planning? What are the trade-offs between using a short-horizon vs. a long-horizon cost function?
5. The paper states that the visual servo must be able to "pick out the target of interest". How does the FQI approach specifically address this challenge? Does the learned weighting scheme effectively suppress irrelevant features?
