This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features from pre-trained networks (VGG), a bilinear model for visual dynamics, and reinforcement learning (fitted Q-iteration). The core idea is to quickly adapt a visual servo to new targets using minimal data, leveraging the robustness of pre-trained features and efficient learning via RL. This is a valuable contribution as traditional visual servoing often relies on hand-engineered features and precise system models, limiting adaptability.

I recommend **rejecting** this paper. While the idea is promising and the experiments demonstrate improvements over baselines, significant concerns regarding clarity, reproducibility, and the robustness of the reported gains prevent me from accepting it at this time. The experimental setup, while appearing comprehensive, lacks sufficient detail to be reliably reproduced, and the reported performance gains over strong baselines feel somewhat overstated given the limited analysis provided.

Here's a breakdown of my reasoning:

**Supporting Arguments for Rejection:**

1. **Lack of Clarity & Detail in Experimental Setup:** The description of the synthetic environment (CitySim3D) is insufficient. While the code is available, the paper doesn't adequately explain crucial parameters like the specific road network used, the distribution of initial conditions, car dynamics, and importantly, the specific implementation of the “hand-coded policy” used to generate the training data for the dynamics model.  Without this, it’s difficult to assess the generality of the approach. The cost function (Appendix B) is also somewhat vague; how is the distance calculated, and why is the cost a function of the inverse depth?  These details significantly impact the interpretability and reproducibility of the results.

2. **Overstated Performance Gains & Insufficient Ablation:** The paper claims “substantial improvement” and “sample-efficiency… of more than two orders of magnitude” over other methods. However, the comparison to deep RL baselines is not fully convincing. The CNN policy used for comparison is described briefly, and it’s unclear if the hyperparameter search was comparable to that performed for the proposed method. A more rigorous comparison with well-tuned, modern deep RL algorithms (e.g., PPO, SAC) is needed. Furthermore, the ablation study (Figure 6) feels limited. While comparisons to different feature representations are helpful, a deeper analysis of the impact of the bilinear dynamics and the fitted Q-iteration components is lacking.  For example, how much does each contribute to the overall performance?

3. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** While sample trajectories are mentioned, the paper lacks a comprehensive analysis of *why* the method fails. Are there specific scenarios (e.g., extreme occlusions, fast-moving targets, unusual camera angles) where performance degrades significantly? Understanding the limitations of the approach is crucial. The discussion touches upon potential issues with the deepest VGG features, but this needs further investigation.

4. **Trust in Implementation & Code Availability:** While code is provided, the reliance on external packages and the complexity of the setup mean that verification is difficult without significant effort.  Greater transparency in the experimental setup and a more detailed explanation of the code base would strengthen the paper considerably.



**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

* **Environment Details:** Provide a much more detailed description of the CitySim3D environment, including network topology, car dynamics model, and the parameters used for generating training and test data.
* **Baseline Comparison:** Conduct a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art deep RL algorithms. Ensure fair hyperparameter tuning and report more detailed performance metrics (e.g., learning curves, standard deviations).
* **Ablation Study:** Perform a more extensive ablation study to isolate the contribution of each component (pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, fitted Q-iteration).
* **Failure Analysis:** Analyze failure cases and identify the limitations of the approach. Provide examples of scenarios where performance degrades and discuss potential solutions.
* **Cost Function Justification:** Clearly explain the rationale behind the cost function used in reinforcement learning.
* **Code Documentation:** Improve the documentation for the provided code to facilitate reproducibility.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed method, particularly in comparison to the baselines.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Can you provide more details about the hand-coded policy used to generate the data for training the dynamics model? What were its key features, and how was it designed?
* What specific hyperparameters were used for training the deep RL baselines? Can you share the hyperparameter search space and the resulting performance metrics?
* What is the sensitivity of the results to the regularization parameter (ν) in the fitted Q-iteration algorithm?
* Can you provide examples of failure cases and discuss potential ways to address them?
* Could you quantify the computational cost of your approach compared to the baseline methods, both in terms of training time and inference time?



In summary, while the core idea is interesting, the lack of clarity, insufficient detail, and overstated performance gains prevent me from recommending acceptance. Addressing the points raised above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
