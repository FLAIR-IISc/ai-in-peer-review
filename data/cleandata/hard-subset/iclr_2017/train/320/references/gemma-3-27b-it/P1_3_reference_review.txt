## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper Title:** Learning Visual Servoing with Reinforcement Learning and Predictive Dynamics

**Reviewer:** [Your Name]

**1. Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features from pre-trained convolutional neural networks (VGG), a bilinear model for predicting feature dynamics, and reinforcement learning (Fitted Q-Iteration). The key contribution lies in efficiently learning a visual servo policy using minimal data, achieving robustness to visual variations, and demonstrably outperforming existing methods in a complex synthetic environment. The authors propose a sample-efficient approach to learn feature weights via Q-iteration, enabling a simple yet effective servoing mechanism. The code and environment are publicly available, facilitating reproducibility.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper tackles a relevant problem in robotics, proposes a novel and well-motivated approach, and provides strong empirical evidence supporting its claims. The significant improvement in sample efficiency compared to existing methods is particularly compelling.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional visual servoing methods reliant on hand-engineered features and dynamics, motivating the need for a learning-based approach. The focus on sample efficiency for rapid adaptation to new targets is a practical and valuable goal.
* **Novelty & Technical Soundness:** The combination of pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics modeling, and Fitted Q-Iteration is a novel approach. The justification for the bilinear model based on previous work (Censi & Murray, 2015) is sound, and the simplified Q-function approximation seems well-reasoned.
* **Compelling Experimental Results:** The results presented demonstrate substantial improvements over baseline methods, including image-pixel based approaches, hand-designed keypoints, and standard deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The improvement in sample efficiency (two orders of magnitude) is especially noteworthy. The extensive comparison and ablation studies strengthen the findings.
* **Reproducibility:** The public availability of the code and environment (CitySim3D) is crucial for verifying the results and fostering further research.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

* **Bilinear Model Justification:** While the reference to Censi & Murray (2015) is helpful, a slightly more detailed explanation of *why* a bilinear model is suitable for approximating feature dynamics would be beneficial.  Perhaps a visual illustration demonstrating its effectiveness compared to a simpler linear model.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of key hyperparameters (e.g., regularization coefficient ν, learning rate for dynamics model) on the performance of the proposed method could be investigated and discussed. A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the robustness of the conclusions.
* **Generalization to Real-World Data:** The experiments are conducted in a synthetic environment. While this provides a controlled setting for evaluation, discussing the potential challenges and strategies for transferring the learned policies to real-world scenarios (e.g., domain adaptation, sim-to-real transfer) would enhance the paper's practical impact.
* **Visualizations of Learned Weights:** Providing visualizations of the learned feature weights (w) could offer insights into which features the algorithm prioritizes for servoing, adding to the interpretability of the results.
* **Discussion of Failure Cases:**  Briefly discussing observed failure cases or limitations of the approach would demonstrate a critical understanding of the method and its potential shortcomings.




**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions the deepest VGG feature (conv5_3) performed worse. Could you elaborate on potential reasons for this, beyond spatial invariance? Is it related to information loss due to the successive pooling layers, or something else?
2. The regularization coefficient (ν) seems important for the FQI algorithm. How was this value selected, and what effect did different values have on the learning process and final performance?
3.  How sensitive is the method to the initial state distribution during reinforcement learning? Does performance degrade significantly if the initial states are vastly different from the validation/test states?
4.  You mention the potential for speedups by using a less computationally demanding network than VGG. Have you explored alternative network architectures (e.g., MobileNet) for feature extraction and assessed the trade-off between performance and computational efficiency?
5. In the experiments, you utilize ground truth depth values for the IBVS methods. How would the performance of your proposed method compare if depth information was *not* available, and instead estimated from monocular vision?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of visual servoing. Addressing the suggested improvements will further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
