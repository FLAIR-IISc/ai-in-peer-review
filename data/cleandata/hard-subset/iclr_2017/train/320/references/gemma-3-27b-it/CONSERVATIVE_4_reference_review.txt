This paper investigates combining learned visual features, predictive dynamics, and reinforcement learning for visual servoing, specifically target following. The core idea is to leverage pre-trained features (VGG) to avoid manual feature engineering, learn a bilinear dynamics model to predict feature evolution under robot actions, and then use fitted Q-iteration to efficiently learn a control policy that minimizes distance to a target feature map. The authors demonstrate improvements in sample efficiency compared to standard deep reinforcement learning and conventional visual servoing approaches on a synthetic car following benchmark.

**Reject.** While the idea of combining pre-trained features with learned dynamics and RL is reasonable, the paper lacks sufficient rigor and comprehensive validation to justify its claims. The experimental setup, while well-defined, feels limited and the improvements demonstrated are not overwhelmingly convincing given the reliance on a synthetic environment. The relatively small gains over baselines, coupled with the novelty of the approach, necessitate more extensive evaluation.

The primary issue is the limited breadth of the experimental comparison. The baselines, while included, feel somewhat weak. Comparing against state-of-the-art deep RL methods *specifically* designed for continuous control (e.g., SAC, TD3) and visual servoing tasks, rather than generic TRPO, would be far more informative. The comparison to IBVS is also limited; the provided implementation does not seem to represent the current state-of-the-art in that field, and the claim that the tracker-based method is slow doesn't detract from its potentially higher performance. The authors claim significant sample efficiency gains, yet the number of samples used (20 trajectories for RL) remains quite low and does not definitively demonstrate scalability to more complex scenarios or targets. Furthermore, the ablation study (Table 2) is not presented with sufficient detail to fully understand the contribution of each component. For example, why does the deepest VGG feature (conv5_3) *decrease* performance?

The theoretical justification for the fitted Q-iteration approach, while present, is not deeply explored. The connection between the linear Q-function approximator and the servoing objective is stated but lacks a more detailed analysis of its properties. The regularization term (ν) in the Q-function optimization is introduced but its sensitivity is not thoroughly investigated. Finally, the assumption of a bilinear dynamics model feels somewhat limiting. While justified by prior work (Censi & Murray, 2015), a more comprehensive evaluation of its accuracy and impact on overall performance would be beneficial.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Expand Baseline Comparisons:** Include stronger deep RL baselines (SAC, TD3) and a more robust IBVS implementation utilizing recent advances in feature tracking and geometry estimation.
*   **Ablation Study Detail:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the ablation study, quantifying the contribution of each component (pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, Q-iteration) with statistical significance. Explore the impact of different pre-trained feature layers more thoroughly.
*   **Dynamics Model Evaluation:** Quantify the accuracy of the learned bilinear dynamics model. How well does it predict future feature states? Error analysis of the dynamics prediction could provide valuable insights.
*   **Regularization Sensitivity:** Conduct a sensitivity analysis of the regularization parameter (ν) in the Q-function optimization.
*   **Real-World Validation:** While the synthetic benchmark is a good starting point, demonstrating the approach in a real-world setting, even with limited data, would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** A more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly the reliance on the bilinear dynamics model and the potential for domain gap between simulation and reality, is needed.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why does using the deepest VGG feature (conv5_3) result in *worse* performance compared to shallower layers?
2.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of pre-trained feature network (e.g., ResNet, Inception)?
3.  What is the error associated with the learned bilinear dynamics model, and how does this error propagate through the servoing loop?
4.  Have you considered alternative dynamics models beyond the bilinear approximation, and if so, what were the trade-offs?
5.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed method compared to the baselines, including memory requirements and inference speed?
