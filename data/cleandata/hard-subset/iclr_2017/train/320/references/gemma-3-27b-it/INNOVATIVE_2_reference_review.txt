This paper tackles the challenging problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features (from pre-trained CNNs), learned predictive dynamics, and reinforcement learning. The key contribution lies in achieving sample efficiency – learning a visual servo with a surprisingly small amount of data (20 trajectories) for a new target. The authors propose a fitted Q-iteration approach to learn appropriate feature weights, operating on a bilinear dynamics model of the visual features.

**Reject.** While the idea is genuinely exciting and addresses a significant bottleneck in visual servoing—data efficiency—the paper feels somewhat under-developed and relies heavily on claims of improvement without sufficiently rigorous comparison to alternative approaches, particularly modern RL baselines. The potential of the approach is clear, but the validation lacks the depth needed to confidently accept this work.

The primary supporting argument for rejection is the limited scope and comparative analysis. While the paper demonstrates improvement over a conventional pixel-based approach and IBVS methods, the comparison to modern deep reinforcement learning algorithms (beyond mentioning its sample efficiency advantage) feels superficial. The authors claim a “two orders of magnitude” improvement over standard deep RL, but this is presented without detailed baselines using comparable architectures and training procedures. The reliance on a synthetic environment, while useful for initial validation, also limits the generalizability claims. The paper leans heavily on the novelty of the combination of techniques, but doesn’t fully demonstrate *why* this particular combination is so successful compared to alternatives. The ablation studies showing the contribution of different VGG layers are insightful, but a more thorough investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity and alternative dynamics models would strengthen the findings.

Further, while the fitted Q-iteration is presented as a key innovation for sample efficiency, the justification for its effectiveness isn’t entirely convincing. The paper mentions the linear approximator for the Q-function, but doesn’t fully elaborate on *why* this linearity is advantageous in this context. It would be beneficial to provide a more detailed theoretical or empirical justification for this design choice. The choice of cost function is also somewhat arbitrary and could benefit from a more detailed rationale.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Strengthen Baselines:** Include more robust comparisons to modern deep RL algorithms (e.g., PPO, SAC) trained with similar computational resources. This is crucial to substantiate the claim of a significant sample efficiency improvement.
*   **Real-World Validation:** Consider transferring the approach to a real-world robotic platform or a more realistic simulation environment. This would address concerns about the generalizability of the results.
*   **Dynamics Model Analysis:** Explore the impact of different dynamics models (e.g., more complex neural networks) on performance.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Conduct a more thorough sensitivity analysis of the key hyperparameters, particularly those related to the fitted Q-iteration.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Provide a more detailed theoretical or empirical justification for the choice of fitted Q-iteration and the linear Q-function approximation. Why is this specifically effective for visual servoing?
*   **Cost Function Rationale:** Elaborate on the choice of the cost function and its impact on learning.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost (training time, inference time) of your method versus the baselines, including the deep RL algorithms?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of your method to the choice of the pre-trained features? Have you explored using features from different architectures (e.g., ResNet) or trained on different datasets?
3.  What are the limitations of the bilinear dynamics model? Are there scenarios where it breaks down, and if so, how could it be improved?
4.  The paper states the dynamics models were trained with a hand-coded policy. How sensitive is the resulting policy to the quality of this initial policy? Could a more random initialization impact performance?
5.   Could you explain more intuitively why the simplicity of the servoing policy—minimizing Euclidean distance—is effective, especially in the presence of distractors and variations in target appearance?
