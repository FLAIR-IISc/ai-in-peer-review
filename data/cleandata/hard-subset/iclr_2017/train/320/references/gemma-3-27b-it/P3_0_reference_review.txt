## ICLR 2024 Review: Learning Visual Servoing with Reinforcement Learning

**1. Summary**

This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features (from pre-trained VGG networks), a bilinear model for predicting feature dynamics, and reinforcement learning (Fitted Q-Iteration). The core contribution is a sample-efficient method for quickly adapting a visual servo to new targets, requiring only 20 training trajectory samples. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over conventional pixel-based and keypoint-based approaches, as well as substantial gains in sample efficiency compared to standard deep RL methods on a synthetic car-following benchmark. The code and environment are publicly available, facilitating reproducibility.

**2. Decision**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper addresses an important problem in robotics – sample efficient adaptation of visual servoing – and presents a compelling solution. The results are strong, the approach is well-motivated, and the publicly available resources are a significant benefit to the community.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently outperforms baseline methods on the car-following benchmark, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The improvement in sample efficiency (two orders of magnitude over standard deep RL) is particularly noteworthy.  The ablation studies (comparing different feature representations) provide valuable insights into the importance of feature selection.
* **Sound Methodology:** The combination of pre-trained features, a bilinear dynamics model, and Fitted Q-Iteration is a logical and well-justified design choice. The use of a bilinear model is a pragmatic approach to capturing short-term visual dynamics and the choice of Fitted Q-Iteration addresses the sample efficiency issues associated with more general RL algorithms. The theoretical justification for the linear Q-function approximation simplifies the learning process.
* **Clear Motivation and Related Work:** The paper provides a comprehensive overview of existing visual servoing techniques and clearly articulates the limitations of these methods (reliance on hand-designed features, lack of robustness to variations). It effectively positions the proposed work within the existing literature, highlighting its novelty and contributions.
* **Reproducibility:**  The availability of both the environment (CitySim3D) and the code makes the work highly reproducible, which is crucial for scientific progress.  This is a significant strength.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement)**

* **Discussion of Bilinear Model Limitations:** While the bilinear model provides a good trade-off between simplicity and accuracy, a more in-depth discussion of its limitations, especially in more complex scenes with non-linear dynamics, would be valuable.  Are there scenarios where the linear approximation breaks down, and how could these be addressed?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions regularization (ν) but a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the performance to hyperparameter tuning (learning rates, regularization coefficients, exploration noise) would be helpful.  A table summarizing these experiments could add to the rigor of the work.
* **Generalization to Other Tasks:** The experiments focus solely on car following. While this provides a strong validation, a brief discussion of the potential applicability of the approach to other visual servoing tasks (e.g., grasping, object manipulation) would broaden the impact of the work.
* **Visualizations of Learned Weights:**  Visualizing the learned weights on the VGG features (e.g., by showing activation maps corresponding to high-weight channels) could provide further insight into which features are most important for the servoing task.
* **Comparison to More Recent RL Algorithms:** While the comparison to standard Deep RL is strong, briefly acknowledging and discussing more recent advancements in sample-efficient RL (e.g., offline RL, model-based RL) and why they may or may not be applicable to this setting would strengthen the related work section.



**5. Questions for the Authors**

* **Regarding the bilinear model:**  You mention that the model can handle moving objects to some extent due to the bias terms. Can you provide more examples of how well the model captures different types of object motion, and what limitations it has in this regard?
* **Regarding the choice of VGG features:** You state that deeper VGG features (conv5_3) performed worse. Was this consistently observed across different hyperparameter settings and initializations?  Could this be due to the loss of spatial information in the deeper layers, as you hypothesize?
* **Regarding computational cost:**  Could you provide a breakdown of the computational cost associated with each component of the pipeline (feature extraction, dynamics prediction, Q-iteration)? This would help to understand the overall efficiency of the approach.
* **Regarding the FQI algorithm:** How sensitive is the performance of the algorithm to the choice of the initialization for the weights θ(0)? Did you observe significant variability in results across different random seeds?



Overall, this is a well-written and technically sound paper with significant potential impact. I believe that addressing the minor points raised above would further strengthen the work and increase its value to the robotics community.
