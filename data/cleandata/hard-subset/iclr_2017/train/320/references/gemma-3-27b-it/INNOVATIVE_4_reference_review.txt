This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by cleverly combining pre-trained visual features (VGG), a bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning (fitted Q-iteration). The core contribution lies in demonstrating that robust visual servo control can be learned with surprisingly little data – just 20 training trajectories – by leveraging existing knowledge encoded in large-scale pre-trained networks and efficiently learning task-specific weights via RL. The focus on sample efficiency is a strong motivation, and the proposed method outperforms conventional approaches and standard deep RL algorithms in this regard.

**Reject.** While the idea is intriguing and the results promising, the paper feels somewhat under-developed and relies heavily on a synthetic benchmark. The novelty is significant, but the validation feels insufficient to warrant acceptance at this stage.

The primary reason for rejection is the limited scope of the experimental validation. The entire evaluation is conducted within a “CitySim3D” environment. While the authors provide code and videos, the generalizability of the results to real-world scenarios is completely unknown. The comparison to other methods is valuable, but the reliance on a single synthetic domain significantly weakens the conclusions. A demonstration, even a limited one, on real robot hardware or a more diverse set of simulated environments would dramatically strengthen the paper.

Secondly, the choice of hyperparameters and the sensitivity of the method to these choices aren’t sufficiently explored. The paper mentions regularization (ν) but doesn't offer extensive ablation studies exploring its impact, or the impact of other key parameters.  The justification for the specific architecture choices (e.g., the bilinear model, the specific layers of VGG) feels somewhat ad-hoc. The authors mention deeper VGG features performing worse, which is insightful, but a more detailed analysis explaining *why* would be beneficial.

Despite these concerns, the core idea – combining pre-trained features with sample-efficient RL for visual servoing – is genuinely novel and has the potential to be impactful. The fitted Q-iteration approach appears well-suited to this problem.  

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Real-World Validation:** Prioritize a demonstration on real hardware, or a more challenging and diverse set of simulators.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct thorough ablation studies to understand the sensitivity of the method to key hyperparameters (learning rates, regularization coefficients, network architecture choices, etc.).
*   **Error Analysis:** Provide a more detailed error analysis of failure cases.  What types of scenarios does the method struggle with, and why?
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Elaborate on the theoretical underpinnings of the bilinear dynamics model and its suitability for visual servoing.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** Expand the comparison to more recent and sophisticated RL algorithms, particularly those designed for sample efficiency.
*   **Generalization Ability:** Evaluate the method's ability to generalize to new target objects (cars) and environments beyond the training distribution.
*   **Computational Cost:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed method, especially compared to other approaches.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the computational overhead of training and running the bilinear dynamics model compared to directly using the features without it?
2.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of pre-trained features? Have you experimented with features from other networks (e.g., ResNet, EfficientNet)?
3.  Why did the deeper VGG features (conv5\_3) perform *worse* than the shallower ones? Is there a trade-off between invariance and discriminative power at play here?
4.  How does the method handle significant changes in lighting conditions or occlusions in a real-world setting?
5.  What is the expected performance degradation if the initial estimate of the car’s depth is inaccurate? Would the method benefit from incorporating depth estimation?
