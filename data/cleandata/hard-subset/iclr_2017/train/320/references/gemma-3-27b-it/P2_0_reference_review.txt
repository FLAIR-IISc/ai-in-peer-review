## Review of "Learning Visual Servoing with Reinforcement Learning"

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features from pre-trained convolutional neural networks (VGG), a bilinear model for predicting feature dynamics, and reinforcement learning (Fitted Q-Iteration) to efficiently learn a servoing policy. The authors motivate their approach by highlighting the limitations of traditional visual servoing methods relying on hand-designed features and domain knowledge. They demonstrate strong performance on a synthetic car following benchmark, achieving significant sample efficiency gains over other reinforcement learning approaches and outperforming traditional methods. The code and environment are publicly available, facilitating reproducibility.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to a difficult problem, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate its effectiveness. The significant improvement in sample efficiency, coupled with the accessibility of the code and environment, makes this a valuable contribution to the field.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The combination of pre-trained features, learned dynamics, and reinforcement learning is a compelling approach. The paper effectively argues for the need to move beyond hand-engineered features and leverage the power of deep learning for robust visual servoing.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The quantitative results in the experiments are impressive. The reported improvement in sample efficiency (two orders of magnitude) over standard deep RL algorithms is substantial. The comparison with traditional IBVS methods further solidifies the advantage of the proposed approach.
* **Reproducibility:**  The availability of the code and environment (CitySim3D) is a significant strength, enabling the community to easily reproduce and build upon the presented work.
* **Technical Soundness:** The use of a bilinear model for dynamics prediction appears justified and appropriate. The explanation of Fitted Q-Iteration is clear and the linearization of the dynamics simplifies the optimization process, contributing to the computational efficiency.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The paper includes comparisons against a range of baselines and ablation studies, providing a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **Discussion of Limitations:** While the results are promising, a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach would be beneficial.  For example, the method's performance in more complex, real-world scenarios with significant visual clutter and unpredictable target motion could be discussed.  How well does the learned dynamics model generalize to different target types or environments?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief analysis of the sensitivity of the performance to key hyperparameters (e.g., regularization coefficient ν, learning rate) would be useful.
* **Visualization of Learned Dynamics:** Providing visualizations of the learned bilinear dynamics model (e.g., showing how features change with different actions) could offer further insight into the learned behavior and help build intuition.
* **Clarification on Action Space:** A more detailed description of the action space (ranges, meaning of each dimension) would be helpful.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Generalization to Novel Targets:** The paper mentions generalization to novel cars. Could you provide more details on how the "novel car" set was constructed to ensure it represents a truly independent test set (e.g., were the novel cars completely unseen during training, or were they only unseen in the specific training trajectories)?
* **Real-World Applicability:**  Given the synthetic nature of the environment, what are the biggest challenges you foresee when deploying this approach to a real-world robotic platform?  What modifications or extensions would be necessary?
* **Dynamics Model Complexity:**  Why was the locally connected bilinear model chosen over the fully connected model, given its potential for reduced expressiveness? Was there a significant trade-off between model complexity and performance?
* **Choice of Reward Function:** The cost function is based on the position of the target.  Have you experimented with other reward signals (e.g., a reward for minimizing distance, or a reward for tracking the target for a prolonged period)?  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the reward function?



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of visual servoing.  Addressing the minor suggestions above would further strengthen the work and enhance its impact.
