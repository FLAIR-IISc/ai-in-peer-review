## Peer Review for ICLR 2024

**Paper Title:** Learning Visual Servoing with Reinforcement Learning and Pre-trained Features

**Reviewer:** [Your Name]

**1. Summary of Claims:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by leveraging the power of pre-trained visual features (VGG), learned feature dynamics (bilinear model), and reinforcement learning (Fitted Q-Iteration). The authors argue that combining these elements allows for efficient learning of visual servoing policies, requiring significantly less data compared to traditional methods or model-free deep RL approaches. They demonstrate this on a synthetic car following benchmark, showing improved performance and sample efficiency. The key contribution lies in the sample-efficient learning framework enabled by the bilinear dynamics model and the cleverly designed Q-learning approach for weighting features.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper addresses a significant problem with a novel and well-motivated approach. The results are compelling, demonstrating a substantial improvement in sample efficiency compared to existing methods. The clear presentation and availability of code/data further strengthens the contribution.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Novelty and Motivation:** The idea of combining pre-trained features, learned dynamics, and reinforcement learning for visual servoing is a strong one. The motivation for addressing the data efficiency problem in visual servoing is clear, particularly for adaptability to new tasks and targets.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments on the synthetic car following benchmark show a clear advantage of the proposed method over several baselines, including image-based methods, hand-designed features, and standard deep RL. The improvement in sample efficiency (two orders of magnitude) is particularly impressive.
*   **Sound Technical Approach:** The use of a bilinear model for dynamics prediction is well-justified and provides a tractable framework for servoing. The fitted Q-iteration algorithm effectively learns the feature weights, allowing the system to focus on relevant visual cues.
*   **Reproducibility:** Providing the code and dataset (CitySim3D) is excellent and encourages further research in this area.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

*   **Bilinear Model Justification:** While the authors mention the bilinear model being motivated by prior work (Censi & Murray, 2015), a slightly more detailed explanation of *why* this model is suitable for capturing the visual dynamics in this specific context would be helpful.  Specifically, what properties of camera motion and visual features make the bilinear assumption reasonable?
*   **Ablation Studies:** While the comparisons are good, more targeted ablation studies within the proposed framework would be valuable. For example, how does performance change when removing the bilinear dynamics model, or using a simpler weighting scheme in the Q-learning stage?
*   **Generalization Discussion:** The results show some generalization to novel cars, but the discussion on the limits of generalization could be expanded. What types of visual variations (e.g., lighting, occlusion, viewpoints) are the most challenging for the learned policy?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the performance to key hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, regularization coefficient) would be useful.
*   **Computational Cost:** Provide more specifics on the computational cost of training the model. While mentioned briefly, a clearer comparison of training time and inference speed to the baseline methods would be beneficial.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   The paper mentions using a locally connected bilinear model. Why was this chosen over a fully connected model, especially given the potential reduction in parameters? What impact does this have on performance?
*   How does the performance of the method scale with the complexity of the environment (e.g., number of distractors, visual clutter)?
*   The cost function includes a term for the distance to the target in image coordinates. Could you elaborate on the design choices for this cost function and how it encourages effective target following behavior?
*   In the experiments, you mention using the same initial states for both sets of cars for testing. Is there a risk of bias introduced by this? Would the conclusions change if initial states were randomized independently for each car set?
*   Can the method be extended to more complex robotic platforms with more degrees of freedom? What modifications would be necessary?



Overall, this is a well-written and compelling paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of visual servoing. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and supported by strong empirical results. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
