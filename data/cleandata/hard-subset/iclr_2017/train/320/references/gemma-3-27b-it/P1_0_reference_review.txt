## ICLR 2024 Review: Learning Visual Servoing with Reinforcement Learning

**1. Summary**

This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features from pre-trained networks (VGG), a learned bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning (Fitted Q-Iteration). The authors aim to create a sample-efficient visual servoing mechanism that can quickly adapt to new targets, addressing the limitations of traditional methods relying on hand-designed features and precise system models. They demonstrate strong performance on a synthetic car following benchmark, achieving significant improvements in sample efficiency compared to standard deep reinforcement learning and classical visual servoing techniques.  The core contribution lies in effectively leveraging pre-trained features *with* a sample-efficient RL algorithm that learns feature weighting for the servoing task.

**2. Decision**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and effective approach to a challenging problem, demonstrating strong empirical results and a clear improvement over existing methods.  The combination of pre-trained features, learned dynamics, and a clever use of fitted Q-iteration is well-motivated and successfully implemented.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results presented in Section 6 convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method. The significant improvement in sample efficiency (two orders of magnitude) over standard deep RL is particularly impressive. Comparisons to pixel-based and keypoint-based visual servoing further highlight the benefits of utilizing pre-trained features and the proposed learning framework.
* **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional visual servoing and the potential of leveraging pre-trained features. The rationale for using a bilinear dynamics model is well-explained, and the use of fitted Q-iteration for efficient weight learning is a compelling choice.
* **Novelty:** The combination of these elements – pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, and fitted Q-iteration – constitutes a novel approach to visual servoing.  The focus on sample efficiency, specifically for adapting to new targets, is a valuable contribution.
* **Reproducibility:** The availability of the code and the synthetic environment (CitySim3D) is a significant strength, promoting reproducibility and further research in this area.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement)**

* **Discussion of Limitations:**  While the results are strong in the simulated environment, a discussion of potential limitations and challenges in transferring this approach to real-world scenarios would be beneficial. Factors like lighting changes, sensor noise, and imperfect dynamics models should be addressed.
* **Ablation Study Detail:** The ablation study in Section 6.2 is good, but further breakdown of the impact of each component (e.g., bilinear model vs. a simpler dynamics model) could provide more granular insights.
* **Clarify Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief discussion regarding the sensitivity of the algorithm to hyperparameters (learning rate, regularization coefficient, etc.) would be helpful for potential users.
* **Visualizations:** Including more visualizations of the learned feature weights (e.g., heatmaps) could help understand which features the algorithm deems most important.
* **Dynamics Model Complexity:** The choice of a bilinear model is justified, but a brief comparison to other dynamics modeling approaches (e.g., neural networks) – even if preliminary – could strengthen the argument.



**5. Questions for the Authors**

* **Real-World Transfer:** What are the biggest challenges you anticipate in transferring this approach to a real-world robotics application, and what modifications might be necessary to address these challenges?
* **Feature Selection:** The paper mentions the VGG network weights are fixed. Have you experimented with fine-tuning the VGG network during the reinforcement learning phase, even with a small learning rate?  Would this potentially improve performance, and at what computational cost?
* **Computational Cost Breakdown:**  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost of each stage of the algorithm (dynamics learning, Q-learning iteration)? This would help assess the practicality of the approach for real-time applications.
* **Scaling to Higher Dimensionality:**  How well does this approach scale to higher-dimensional feature spaces (e.g., using deeper layers of the VGG network or more complex pre-trained models)? Are there any strategies to mitigate the curse of dimensionality?
* **Generalization to Different Targets:** The experiments focus on car following. How well does this approach generalize to tracking *different* types of targets with significantly different visual appearances?



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of visual servoing. The clear presentation, strong experimental results, and potential for real-world impact make it a valuable addition to the ICLR conference.  The requested revisions are minor and aimed at further strengthening the paper and providing additional insights for the research community.
