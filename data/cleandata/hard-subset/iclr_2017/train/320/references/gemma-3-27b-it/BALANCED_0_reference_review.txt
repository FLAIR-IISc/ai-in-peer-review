This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features from pre-trained networks (VGG), a bilinear model for predicting feature dynamics, and reinforcement learning via Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI). The core idea is to learn a visual servo policy efficiently, requiring minimal data for adaptation to new targets.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents a novel combination of techniques and demonstrates promising results on a synthetic benchmark, I believe the work falls short of making a significant contribution to the field at this time. The primary reason is a lack of convincing evidence of the benefits of the proposed approach *beyond* the specific experimental setup and chosen baseline comparisons. Furthermore, critical aspects of the methodology lack sufficient detail and justification, hindering reproducibility and understanding.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Generalization & Benchmark Concerns:** The experiments are conducted in a highly controlled synthetic environment (CitySim3D). While the authors attempt to address generalization by testing on novel cars, the environment itself remains synthetic, and the results don’t clearly demonstrate transferability to real-world scenarios. The performance gains over “standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms” are claimed to be substantial, but the algorithms chosen for comparison appear somewhat dated (e.g., only mentioning DQN, DDPG without recent advances like SAC, TD3).  A comparison against more contemporary RL baselines is crucial to accurately assess the sample efficiency of the proposed method. The comparison to IBVS methods, while helpful, also relies on specific parameter choices (ground truth depth) that may not always be available.

2. **Insufficient Detail and Justification:**  Several key design choices lack sufficient justification. For instance, the choice of the bilinear dynamics model isn’t thoroughly motivated beyond citing prior work.  Why is a bilinear model suitable for this specific application? What are the limitations of this model? The weighting scheme and the specific cost function used for reinforcement learning are described, but the rationale behind these choices remains unclear. The regularization parameter 'ν' in the FQI algorithm is mentioned but its value isn't thoroughly explored or justified.  A more detailed ablation study would be highly valuable. The selection of VGG layers and the removal of max-pooling layers also feels arbitrary without a clear explanation of how these modifications impact the learned features.

3. **Lack of Real-World Validation:** The absence of any real-world experiments is a significant drawback. While simulation is a useful starting point, the gap between simulation and reality is substantial, particularly for vision-based tasks. Demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach on real robot hardware with real-world targets is essential to establish its practical relevance.



**Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

*   **Ablation Study:** Conduct a thorough ablation study to investigate the contribution of each component of the proposed method.  Specifically, examine the impact of the pre-trained features, the bilinear dynamics model, and the FQI algorithm individually and in combination.
*   **Baseline Comparisons:** Include more recent and state-of-the-art reinforcement learning algorithms (e.g., SAC, TD3) as baselines.  Ensure a fair comparison by tuning the hyperparameters of the baselines appropriately.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:**  Investigate the sensitivity of the performance to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the regularization parameter 'ν' and the learning rates.
*   **Visualizations:** Provide more visualizations of the learned feature dynamics and the weighting scheme.  This would help to understand what the algorithm is learning and why it is effective.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** While not strictly required, providing some theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the FQI algorithm in this specific context would strengthen the paper.
*   **Real-World Experiments:** The most important improvement would be to demonstrate the performance of the proposed method on a real robot with real-world targets.
*   **Clarify Cost Function:** The cost function's reliance on target pose and its handling of the episode termination condition could benefit from further clarification. How sensitive is the approach to inaccuracies in estimated target pose?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why was the bilinear dynamics model chosen specifically? What alternatives were considered, and why were they rejected?
2.  The paper claims a significant improvement in sample efficiency compared to standard deep RL. How were the hyperparameters of the baseline algorithms tuned to ensure a fair comparison? Can you provide the specific hyperparameter settings used for each baseline?
3.  Can you elaborate on the rationale behind the cost function used for reinforcement learning? Why was this specific cost function chosen, and how does it relate to the overall goal of target following?
4.  What steps have been taken to address the potential domain gap between the synthetic environment and real-world scenarios?  Do you anticipate that the performance will degrade significantly when deployed on a real robot?
5.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the removal of max-pooling layers in the VGG network affects the learned features and the overall performance of the system?
