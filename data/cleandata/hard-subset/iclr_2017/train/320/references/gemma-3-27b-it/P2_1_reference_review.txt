## Review of "Learning Visual Servoing with Reinforcement Learning"

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features (from pre-trained VGG networks), a learned bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning (Fitted Q-Iteration). The core idea is to leverage the robustness of pre-trained features while quickly adapting the servoing policy to new targets with limited data. The authors demonstrate promising results on a synthetic car following benchmark, achieving significant sample efficiency improvements over existing methods, including model-free deep reinforcement learning and traditional visual servoing approaches. They provide a clear implementation and release code/data for reproducibility.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** – The paper presents a novel and effective approach to visual servoing that addresses a significant challenge: sample efficiency. The combination of pre-trained features, learned dynamics, and a tailored reinforcement learning algorithm is well-motivated and demonstrably outperforms existing methods in a challenging benchmark.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional visual servoing methods relying on hand-designed features and the need for learning-based approaches that can adapt quickly to new targets. The motivation for combining pre-trained features with reinforcement learning is sound.
*   **Novelty of Approach:** The proposed method is a compelling combination of techniques. The use of a bilinear dynamics model to predict visual feature changes is a sensible choice and allows for efficient policy learning.  Fitted Q-iteration, adapted for this specific problem, appears to be a key contributor to the sample efficiency.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are convincing. The reported performance improvements over baseline methods (image pixels, hand-designed keypoints, and standard deep RL) are substantial, particularly regarding sample efficiency.  The comparison to the tracker-based IBVS method is particularly insightful.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors have made their code and the environment publicly available, which significantly strengthens the paper. This is a major plus for the community.
*   **Well-structured and clear presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a logical flow from problem statement to experimental results.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Ablation Study Discussion:** While the paper presents comparisons to various baselines, a more detailed ablation study within the proposed method would be beneficial. For instance, how much does each component (pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, FQI) contribute to the overall performance? What’s the impact of different VGG layers?
*   **Generalization to Different Datasets:** The experiments are conducted on a synthetic dataset. It would be valuable to discuss the potential challenges and strategies for generalizing the approach to real-world scenarios and datasets.  Even a limited evaluation on a real-world dataset would strengthen the paper.
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:** The paper mentions the speed of the proposed approach compared to the tracker-based method. A more detailed analysis of the computational cost (e.g., training time, inference time) for different components of the method would be helpful.
*   **Further Explanation of FQI Adaptations:** The adaptations made to standard FQI for this problem (linear approximator for the Q-function, handling of weights) could be explained in more detail. Providing intuition behind these choices would increase understanding.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   The bilinear dynamics model assumes a specific relationship between actions and visual features. Could you discuss the limitations of this assumption in more complex environments with more intricate dynamics? Would more sophisticated dynamics models (e.g., recurrent neural networks) be a worthwhile direction for future research?
*   The ablation study mentions a drop in performance with the deepest VGG features (conv5\_3). You hypothesize this is due to lack of spatial information. Could you elaborate on this? Is it possible to combine the deeper features with spatial attention mechanisms to regain some of the lost information?
*   The choice of the cost function seems crucial. How sensitive is the performance to different cost function designs? Have you experimented with alternative cost functions that might encourage different behaviors?
*   Regarding the real-world generalization, what modifications would be necessary to adapt the method to handle lighting changes, occlusions, and viewpoint variations common in real-world scenes?
*   In the FQI algorithm, the regularization coefficient ν seems important for convergence. How was this value selected? Was a sensitivity analysis performed?



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of visual servoing. The clear presentation, strong experimental results, and emphasis on reproducibility make it a valuable addition to the ICLR conference. I am confident that this work will inspire further research in this area.
