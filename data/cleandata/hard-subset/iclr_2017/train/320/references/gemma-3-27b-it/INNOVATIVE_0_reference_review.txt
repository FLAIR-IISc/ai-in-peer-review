This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by cleverly combining learned visual features from pre-trained networks (VGG), a bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning via fitted Q-iteration. The core contribution lies in demonstrating a sample-efficient approach to learning visual servo policies, requiring only 20 training trajectories, a significant improvement over existing methods. The work is well-motivated, recognizing the limitations of hand-engineered features and the data inefficiency of standard RL.

**Reject.** While the idea is genuinely novel and promising, and the sample efficiency results are compelling, the presentation feels somewhat incomplete and lacks sufficient ablation/analysis to fully justify the claims. The improvements over baselines, while substantial, aren't explored in enough depth to ascertain *why* this method works so well.

The novelty of combining pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, and fitted Q-iteration is strong. This is a genuinely interesting direction, sidestepping the need for extensive data collection often associated with learning visual servo policies. The reported results – learning an effective servo with only 20 samples – are remarkable and suggest a substantial leap forward. The choice of FQI is also well-justified, enabling efficient learning in a continuous action space. The experimental setup with the CitySim3D environment appears reasonable.

However, the paper feels rushed in its presentation of results. The comparison to TRPO is brief, and the reasons for the significant performance gap aren’t thoroughly investigated. While the ablation study comparing different VGG layers is helpful, it doesn’t delve into the impact of the bilinear dynamics model itself. How much does the bilinear model contribute to the performance gains, and what are its limitations? The discussion of the locally connected dynamics is minimal. The choice of hyperparameters (e.g., regularization coefficient ν) isn't fully explained, and a sensitivity analysis would strengthen the claims. The reliance on a specific simulation environment (CitySim3D) also raises concerns about generalization to more complex, real-world scenarios. The paper acknowledges this somewhat, but further discussion of potential transferability would be valuable.  A more detailed explanation of the cost function (Appendix B) within the main body of the paper is needed for clarity. Finally, while code is available, the reproducibility could be improved with a more comprehensive set of instructions and dependencies.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Bilinear Dynamics:** A more detailed analysis of the bilinear dynamics model is needed. What are the limitations of this approach? How does its performance vary with different action spaces?
*   **TRPO Comparison:** A more thorough comparison with TRPO, including parameter tuning and exploration strategies, would be beneficial. Explain why TRPO struggles in this setting.
*   **Ablation Study:** Expand the ablation study to include variations in the FQI algorithm (e.g., different regularization strengths, learning rates).
*   **Generalization:** Discuss the potential for generalization to different environments and target objects. Would the approach require retraining the dynamics model or the Q-function?
*   **Real-World Implications:** Briefly discuss the potential challenges and opportunities for deploying this approach in real-world robotic systems.
* **Explain the choice of VGG.** Why VGG? Was there experimentation with other pre-trained models (e.g., ResNet)?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why the bilinear dynamics model works well in this setting? What properties of the visual features and the robot dynamics make this a suitable approximation?
2.  What steps were taken to tune the hyperparameters of the TRPO algorithm? Could you show a learning curve for TRPO that demonstrates its convergence behavior?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of the FQI algorithm to the choice of the regularization coefficient ν?
4.  Have you experimented with different pre-trained feature extractors? If so, what were the results?
5.  How would you expect this approach to perform in a more complex, real-world environment with more significant visual variations and uncertainties?
