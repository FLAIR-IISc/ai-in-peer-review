This paper proposes a visual servoing method combining pre-trained features (VGG network), a bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning (fitted Q-iteration). The goal is to learn a visual servo quickly with limited data, enabling adaptation to new targets. The method aims to address the limitations of traditional visual servoing relying on hand-designed features and dynamics, and the data inefficiency of standard deep reinforcement learning.

**Decision: Accept**

The primary reasons for acceptance are the compelling approach to sample-efficient visual servoing and the strong empirical results demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods. The combination of pre-trained features, a learned dynamics model, and efficient reinforcement learning is a valuable contribution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of combining pre-trained visual features with a bilinear dynamics model and sample-efficient reinforcement learning is novel. While each component exists in prior work, their integration for visual servoing is a significant contribution. The use of fitted Q-iteration in this context seems particularly well-suited to the problem.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is technically sound. The use of a bilinear model for dynamics prediction appears reasonable, and the justification for its choice is well-presented. The use of fitted Q-iteration provides a theoretically grounded framework for learning the servoing policy. The linearization of the dynamics for efficient optimization is a practical and sensible step.
* **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate substantial improvements over baseline methods, including conventional approaches (image pixels, keypoints) and standard deep reinforcement learning. The reported improvement in sample efficiency (two orders of magnitude) is particularly impressive and suggests the practical potential of the method. The generalization to novel cars is also a positive indication of the approach's robustness. The comparisons with TRPO, while complex to interpret given hyperparameter tuning, also suggest advantages for the proposed approach.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Dynamics Model Complexity:** While the bilinear model is a good starting point, a more detailed discussion on the limitations of this model and potential avenues for improvement (e.g., exploring more complex dynamics models) would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A sensitivity analysis of the key hyperparameters (e.g., regularization coefficient ν, learning rate) would strengthen the results.
* **Ablation Studies:** More detailed ablation studies could further isolate the contributions of each component (pre-trained features, dynamics model, reinforcement learning). For example, what happens when using random features instead of VGG features?
* **Computational Cost:** Provide more detail on the computational cost of the method, especially compared to the baseline methods. While the text mentions the runtime comparison with the tracker-based method, a more comprehensive analysis would be valuable.
* **Generalization to Other Environments:** The experiments are conducted in a synthetic environment. Discussing the potential challenges and considerations for transferring the method to real-world scenarios would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the choice of the VGG network and its specific layers (conv4\_3) for feature extraction? Were other networks or layers explored, and what were the results?
2.  The paper mentions a potential limitation of the bilinear dynamics model. Could you provide more details on the observed limitations and potential directions for future work to address them?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the regularization coefficient (ν) in the fitted Q-iteration?
4.  Could you provide more specifics about the setup of the TRPO baseline experiments? In particular, what hyperparameters were used and how were they tuned?
5.  What steps would need to be taken to adapt this method to a real-world robotics application, and what challenges do you anticipate?
