## Review for ICLR 2024 Submission

**1. Summary of Claims**

This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features (from pre-trained VGG networks), learned predictive dynamics, and reinforcement learning. The authors argue that leveraging pre-trained features allows for faster adaptation to new targets with limited data, overcoming the limitations of traditional methods that require hand-engineered features and domain knowledge. They propose a fitted Q-iteration approach to learn feature weights, enabling the servo to focus on relevant visual information and achieve robustness to distractors. The method is evaluated on a synthetic car following benchmark, demonstrating significant improvements in sample efficiency and performance compared to conventional and deep reinforcement learning baselines.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Accept.** This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to visual servoing. The combination of pre-trained features, learned dynamics, and sample-efficient reinforcement learning is compelling, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of this approach, particularly its impressive sample efficiency. The clarity of presentation and availability of code and videos further strengthen the submission.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The motivation for combining learned features with RL for visual servoing is well-articulated. Existing methods often rely on hand-crafted features or require substantial data for training. This work provides a promising alternative, leveraging transfer learning and efficient reinforcement learning techniques.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method appears technically sound. The use of a bilinear model for dynamics prediction is a reasonable choice, and the fitted Q-iteration approach for learning feature weights is well-justified.  The linearization of the dynamics for efficient optimization is a clever detail.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are compelling. The authors demonstrate substantial improvements over several baselines, including pixel-based and keypoint-based approaches, and state-of-the-art deep RL algorithms.  The 20-sample learning curve is particularly impressive. The ablation studies (comparison of different feature representations, optimization methods) further solidify the claims.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors provide links to their code and a simulation environment, making the work highly reproducible. Supplementary videos are also available, enhancing transparency and understanding.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement)**

* **Dynamics Model Limitations:** While the bilinear model performs well, a discussion of its limitations would be beneficial.  For example, how does it perform in more complex environments with non-rigid object motion or significant viewpoint changes? A brief analysis of failure cases would be valuable.
* **Generalization to Real-World Scenarios:** The experiments are conducted in a synthetic environment.  A discussion of potential challenges in transferring this method to real-world scenarios (e.g., lighting variations, sensor noise, imperfect dynamics models) would strengthen the paper.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, regularization coefficient, exploration noise) would be useful.
* **Computational Cost:** A more detailed breakdown of the computational cost of the different components of the system (feature extraction, dynamics prediction, Q-iteration) would be helpful for understanding its practical applicability.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

*   You mention that the deepest VGG feature (conv5_3) did not perform well. Is this consistent across different target objects within your environment, or does performance vary significantly depending on the target? If so, could this be related to the level of abstraction of these features?
*   The bilinear dynamics model appears to be a key component. Have you explored more complex dynamics models, such as recurrent neural networks, and if so, what were the trade-offs in terms of performance and computational cost?
*   Regarding real-world transfer, what specific adaptations (e.g., domain adaptation techniques, robust feature extraction) do you anticipate would be necessary to deploy this method on a physical robot?
*   In the FQI algorithm, you use a regularization term (ν).  How was this hyperparameter selected, and how sensitive are the results to its value? Could you provide more insight into how the regularization affects the learning process and feature weighting?



Overall, this is a well-written and impactful paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of visual servoing. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
