This paper proposes a visual servoing method combining pre-trained visual features (VGG), a bilinear model for feature dynamics prediction, and reinforcement learning (Fitted Q-Iteration) for weight optimization. The core idea is to learn a servo that quickly adapts to new targets by leveraging existing visual knowledge and efficiently learning the optimal feature weighting for the servoing task. The authors demonstrate improvements in sample efficiency compared to standard deep reinforcement learning and conventional visual servoing approaches on a synthetic car following benchmark.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a technically sound approach and achieves promising results, the sheer number of minor flaws and inconsistencies, alongside a lack of thorough ablation studies and a somewhat overstated novelty claim, necessitate a rejection. The paper feels overly polished in its presentation, masking underlying issues in rigor and experimental design.

My primary concerns stem from the following:

1. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The extensive comparison to other methods is commendable, but a critical component is missing: a thorough ablation study.  The authors compare various feature representations (pixel intensities, different VGG layers) but don't systematically analyze the *contribution* of each component of their proposed method. For example, what is the performance gain specifically from *using* the bilinear dynamics model versus a simpler direct feature prediction? How much of the improvement comes from the Fitted Q-Iteration compared to, say, a simpler gradient-based weight optimization strategy? Without this breakdown, it’s difficult to assess the true value of their combined approach. The claim of a two-orders-of-magnitude improvement in sample efficiency feels inflated without dissecting the contribution of each element.

2. **Overstated Novelty:** The paper positions its method as a significant departure from prior work. However, several components are well-established. Pre-trained features are commonly used in robotics, bilinear dynamics models exist (though perhaps not applied in this specific context), and Fitted Q-Iteration is a known RL technique.  The novelty lies in the *combination* of these elements, but the paper doesn't adequately highlight the challenges overcome by bringing them together or sufficiently contrast their approach with existing methods that utilize similar components independently. 

3. **Implementation Details and Reproducibility Concerns:** While code and environment are available, the descriptions of several key implementation choices are vague. For instance, the regularization coefficient ν in the FQI algorithm is mentioned but not justified; its optimal value seems determined empirically rather than through a principled approach. The parameters for the Gaussian exploration policy, the batch size used for TRPO, and the justification for the specific CNN architecture used for the baseline in TRPO all lack sufficient detail. The statement about needing to try multiple random seeds for TRPO due to non-convergence raises a red flag about the stability and sensitivity of their implementation.

4. **Minor Technical Inconsistencies/Omissions:** Equation (7) mentions a linearization point *ū* but doesn't clearly define how *ū* is chosen. The explanation of the locally connected bilinear model in Section 4.2 is unclear, particularly the meaning of "untied filter weights." The cost function in Appendix B, while reasonable, lacks justification for the specific weights assigned to different components. The explanation of the interaction matrix in the comparison to classical visual servoing in section 6.4 seems to assume the reader is intimately familiar with the literature.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Clarify the role of the bilinear model:** Explicitly state why a bilinear model is preferred over other dynamics modeling approaches (e.g., neural networks). Justify this choice based on computational complexity, data efficiency, or other relevant factors.
*   **Expand the Related Work section:** A more comprehensive discussion of recent advances in visual servoing using deep learning would contextualize the contributions more effectively.
*   **Address the limitations:** Acknowledge the limitations of the synthetic environment and discuss potential challenges when transferring the approach to real-world scenarios (e.g., lighting variations, sensor noise, imperfect dynamics models).
*   **Improve the clarity of equations and notations:** Ensure all variables and parameters are clearly defined and consistently used throughout the paper.
*   **Provide a more detailed analysis of the experimental results:** Include statistical significance tests to support the claims of improvement.


**Questions for the authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of the VGG network and the specific layers used for feature extraction? Were other pre-trained networks considered, and if so, why were they rejected?
2.  What is the computational cost of evaluating the bilinear dynamics model compared to a direct prediction using a small neural network?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of the FQI algorithm to the choice of the regularization coefficient ν?
4.  Can you elaborate on why the TRPO algorithm required multiple random initializations to converge? What steps were taken to mitigate this issue?
5.  What is the estimated real-time performance of the proposed visual servoing system, considering the computational cost of feature extraction and the control loop?
