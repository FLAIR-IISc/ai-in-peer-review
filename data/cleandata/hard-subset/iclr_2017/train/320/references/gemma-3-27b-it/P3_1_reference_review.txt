## ICLR 2024 Review: Learning Visual Servoing with Reinforcement Learning

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features from pre-trained convolutional neural networks (VGG), a bilinear model for predicting feature dynamics, and reinforcement learning (Fitted Q-Iteration). The key contribution is a sample-efficient method for learning a visual servo policy quickly, requiring only a small amount of data for new targets. The authors demonstrate strong performance in a simulated car following task, significantly outperforming conventional and model-free deep reinforcement learning approaches. The code and environment are publicly available, enhancing reproducibility.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. 

The paper addresses an important problem with a novel and well-motivated approach.  The experimental results demonstrate a significant improvement in sample efficiency compared to existing methods, which is a crucial advantage for real-world robotic applications. The combination of pre-trained features, dynamics learning, and Fitted Q-Iteration is a clever and effective strategy.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Motivation & Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional visual servoing techniques (reliance on hand-designed features and domain knowledge) and convincingly motivates the use of learning-based approaches. The focus on sample efficiency is particularly relevant for practical robotic systems.
* **Novelty & Technical Soundness:**  The combination of pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, and Fitted Q-Iteration is a novel and well-reasoned approach. The use of a bilinear model for dynamics prediction is particularly insightful given its simplicity and efficiency. The use of Fitted Q-iteration for efficient weight learning within the servoing loop is a smart design choice.
* **Compelling Experimental Results:** The results on the synthetic car following benchmark are very promising. The significant improvement in sample efficiency over both conventional and deep RL methods is a major strength. The comparison against baselines like pixel-based IBVS and TRPO is well-executed and supports the claims made by the authors.  The ablation studies help to understand the contribution of each component.
* **Reproducibility:** The release of code and the simulation environment is a significant contribution that greatly aids reproducibility and future research.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

* **Discussion of Limitations:** While the results are strong in simulation, the paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of potential limitations and challenges in transferring this approach to real-world scenarios.  Issues like lighting changes, sensor noise, and imperfect dynamics models could be addressed.
* **Bilinear Model Justification:**  The justification for using a bilinear model is reasonable, citing previous work. However, a brief exploration of *why* the bilinear assumption holds well for these features, or at least a discussion of its limitations, would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief sensitivity analysis of the key hyperparameters (e.g., regularization coefficient ν, learning rate for dynamics model) would increase confidence in the robustness of the approach.
* **Visualization of Learned Weights:**  Visualizing the learned feature weights could provide valuable insight into which features the algorithm prioritizes and how it is achieving robustness to distractors. This could be shown as heatmaps on the VGG feature maps.
* **Ablation on Dynamics Model:** While comparisons are made with and without reinforcement learning, more detail on how performance changes when the dynamics model is removed (i.e. just using the pre-trained features and RL) could be informative.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Real-World Transfer:**  What specific steps would be necessary to adapt this approach to a real-world robotic platform? What are the biggest challenges you anticipate in transferring the learned dynamics and policies?
* **Bilinear Model Assumption:** Can you elaborate on any potential drawbacks or limitations of the bilinear dynamics model?  Are there scenarios where it might break down?
* **Feature Selection:**  The paper mentions that deeper VGG features (conv5_3) performed worse.  Is there a theoretical reason why this might be the case, beyond simply losing spatial information?  Could techniques like attention mechanisms be used to mitigate this issue?
* **Scalability:** How does the computational cost of the Fitted Q-Iteration algorithm scale with the dimensionality of the feature space (number of channels in the VGG features)? What are the practical limits on the size of the feature space that can be effectively handled?
* **Cost Function Design:** The cost function seems to heavily rely on position error. Have you explored other cost functions that might encourage more robust or natural-looking servoing behaviors (e.g., incorporating velocity or acceleration terms)?



Overall, this is a well-written and compelling paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of visual servoing. The strong experimental results, coupled with the clear motivation and novel approach, make it a strong candidate for acceptance. The suggested revisions are minor and aimed at further strengthening the paper and addressing potential limitations.
