

1. Summary: The paper proposes a novel visual servoing method that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG network), a bilinear model for visual feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn feature weighting. The authors claim their approach can learn effective visual servoing mechanisms using minimal data (20 training trajectory samples), significantly outperforming conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and achieving more than two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency than standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The method leverages pre-trained features to avoid the need for large amounts of task-specific data while using reinforcement learning to optimize feature weights for robustness to visual variations, occlusions, and distractors.

2. Decision: Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to visual servoing by demonstrating how pre-trained features can be effectively combined with a bilinear dynamics model and a novel reinforcement learning approach to achieve significant sample efficiency gains. The technical approach is sound and well-motivated, and the claims are thoroughly supported by rigorous experiments.

3. Supporting arguments: The paper addresses a meaningful problem in visual servoing with a well-motivated approach that properly situates itself within the literature. The bilinear dynamics model is well-justified by prior work and the authors provide a clear mathematical formulation. The fitted Q-iteration approach for learning feature weights is technically sound and demonstrates clear advantages over alternative optimization methods. The experimental results are comprehensive, with multiple ablation studies comparing feature representations, weighting methods, and baselines. The performance gains are substantial and consistently demonstrated across multiple evaluation metrics, including generalization to novel cars. The sample efficiency claim is particularly strong, with the authors showing their method requires only 20 training trajectory samples versus thousands for standard deep RL approaches. The results are scientifically rigorous, with proper validation procedures, statistical comparisons, and detailed analysis of the method's strengths and limitations.

4. Additional feedback: The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding the specific characteristics of pre-trained features that make them suitable for visual servoing. A comparison of the method's performance with different pre-trained networks beyond VGG would help establish the generality of the approach. The authors should also provide more insight into the computational complexity of their method, especially regarding real-time performance considerations for practical deployment. The paper could strengthen its claims by including statistical significance testing for the reported performance differences.

5. Clarification questions:
- The paper mentions VGG conv5_3 features are not suitable for approximating Q-values, but does not provide detailed analysis of why this is the case. Could the authors elaborate on the specific characteristics of these features that make them less effective for visual servoing?
- How generalizable is the approach to other visual servoing tasks beyond car following? Could the same method be effectively applied to manipulation tasks with similar sample efficiency gains?
- The paper reports performance on a synthetic environment. What specific challenges would the method face in a real-world setting with more complex visual variations and sensor noise, and how might these be addressed?
