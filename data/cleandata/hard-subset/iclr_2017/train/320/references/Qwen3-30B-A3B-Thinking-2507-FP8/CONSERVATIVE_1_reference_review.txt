

# Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

The paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG network trained on ImageNet classification), learned bilinear dynamics models, and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning algorithm (fitted Q-iteration) to create a visual servoing mechanism that can be quickly adapted to new targets. The authors claim their method can learn effective visual servoing using just 20 training trajectory samples, substantially outperforming conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and demonstrating more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample efficiency compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The method is evaluated on a synthetic car-following benchmark.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes strong claims about sample efficiency and performance improvements but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support these claims with rigorous validation against established baselines in realistic conditions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary concerns with the paper are:

First, the sample efficiency claim is misleading. The paper states it uses "just 20 training trajectory samples for reinforcement learning," but the 10,000 samples used to train the dynamics models were generated using a hand-coded policy (not the final learned policy), and the 20 training trajectories for RL are collected with Gaussian noise added to the hand-coded policy. This is not a realistic scenario for a new target, as the hand-coded policy already has significant knowledge of the environment and target. In real-world applications, the initial policy would need to be learned from scratch, which would require substantially more data than the 20 samples claimed.

Second, the validation is insufficiently rigorous. The experiments are conducted exclusively in a synthetic environment (CitySim3D), which may not reflect real-world visual servoing challenges. The paper does not demonstrate the method's performance on real-world data or on more complex, challenging environments beyond the car-following benchmark. Without such validation, the claim of "robustness to visual variation, changes in viewing angle and appearance, and occlusions" remains unproven.

Third, the comparison to baselines is inadequate. The paper compares against a few classical visual servoing methods (IBVS with ORB features and tracker-based features) and a simple CNN policy, but fails to compare against more recent learning-based visual servoing methods that might have addressed similar challenges. For example, the paper does not compare against methods like those from [Lampe & Riedmiller, 2013] or [Sadeghzadeh et al., 2015], which are more directly related to the problem the authors claim to solve.

Fourth, the motivation for using ImageNet pre-trained features is weak. The paper assumes that features learned for object classification would be useful for visual servoing, but provides no theoretical or empirical justification for why these features would be particularly well-suited for the task. The authors claim that "the features learned by large convolutional neural networks on large image datasets, such as ImageNet classification, tend to be useful for a wide range of other visual tasks," but this is a general claim that hasn't been specifically validated for visual servoing in their paper.

Finally, the paper's claim of "more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample-efficiency" lacks context. It doesn't specify how many samples the baselines would require in the same environment, making the claim difficult to evaluate. The comparison to TRPO is made with different numbers of training trajectories (2 vs. 50 iterations), but the paper doesn't provide a clear baseline for how many samples would be needed for standard model-free RL to achieve comparable performance.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide a more realistic evaluation of sample efficiency by demonstrating how many samples would be needed to learn the initial policy from scratch (not using a hand-coded policy).

2. Extend the experiments to include real-world data or at least more complex synthetic environments that better capture real-world visual servoing challenges.

3. Include a more thorough comparison with recent learning-based visual servoing methods, not just classical approaches and simple CNN policies.

4. Provide theoretical justification or empirical evidence for why ImageNet features would be well-suited for visual servoing, rather than simply assuming it.

5. Clarify the sample efficiency claim by providing a direct comparison of the number of samples required by each method to achieve comparable performance in the same environment.

6. Address the concern that the VGG features might be too spatially invariant for visual servoing (as noted in Section 6.2) and provide a more detailed analysis of this limitation.

## Questions for Authors

1. How many samples would be required to learn the initial hand-coded policy (which is used to generate the 10,000 samples for dynamics training) from scratch, and how does this compare to the claimed 20 samples?

2. Can you provide evidence that the ImageNet features used in this work are indeed better suited for visual servoing than other feature representations, such as those learned specifically for visual servoing tasks?

3. How would the method perform in the presence of significant distractors or in environments with more complex dynamics than the car-following benchmark?

4. Have you considered using features learned specifically for visual servoing (rather than ImageNet features) to compare the relative benefits of using pre-trained features versus task-specific features?

5. Can you provide a more precise comparison of sample efficiency by specifying how many samples each method would require to achieve comparable performance in the same environment?
