

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims and contribution

The paper proposes a novel visual servoing method that combines pre-trained VGG features, bilinear dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to learn visual servoing mechanisms with minimal data. The authors claim their approach can learn effective visual servoing using just 20 training trajectory samples, with substantial improvements over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample-efficiency over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The core innovation is using pre-trained features from ImageNet classification, a bilinear model for visual dynamics, and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration procedure to automatically select weights for relevant visual features.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The key reasons for this decision are the ambiguity in the claimed sample efficiency (20 training trajectory samples) and insufficient theoretical justification for the key claim about the tractable linear approximator for the Q-function.

## 3. Supporting arguments

The paper's most significant flaw is the lack of clarity regarding the claimed "20 training trajectory samples" for reinforcement learning. In Section 6.1, the authors state they "ran the overall algorithm for only S = 2 sampling iterations" where "at every sampling iteration, the current policy was executed with Gaussian noise to gather data from 10 trajectories." This would result in 20 trajectories total, but the paper never clarifies whether these are the same trajectories used for all iterations or different trajectories. More importantly, the paper claims "just 20 training trajectory samples for reinforcement learning," but the 20 trajectories are used to gather data for the reinforcement learning process, not as the sole training samples for the model. This creates a significant discrepancy between the claim and the described methodology.

Additionally, the claim of "more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample-efficiency" lacks proper justification. The paper compares to TRPO, but doesn't specify how many samples TRPO required for comparable performance. The paper states "the policy learned with TRPO improves further in 50 iterations; however, the cost incurred by this policy is still about one and a half times the cost of our policy, despite using more than 100 times as many trajectories." This suggests TRPO used 100 times more trajectories than their method, but the paper doesn't state how many trajectories their method actually used (20, or 20 per iteration?).

The theoretical claim that the Q-function approximation "leads to an analytic and tractable linear approximator for the Q-function" is not properly justified. In Section 5.2, the authors state: "We choose a Q-value function approximator that can represent the servoing objective such that the greedy policy with respect to the Q-values results in the policy of Equation (3)." However, the Q-function they propose (Equation 5.2) is quadratic in the control variables (as shown in Equation 3), not linear. The paper claims it's linear in the weight parameters, which is true, but the connection to the "analytic and tractable" aspect is not clearly established.

The paper also contains several formatting inconsistencies that undermine its professionalism, including inconsistent citation styles (e.g., "Deng et al., 2009" vs "Deng et al.,? \Q2009\E" in references), inconsistent notation (e.g., "y (l) t,c" should be "y_t,c^(l)"), and references to figures that aren't visible in the provided text.

## 4. Additional feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Clarify the exact meaning of "20 training trajectory samples" and provide a clear breakdown of how many samples were used for each component of the method (feature dynamics training vs. reinforcement learning).

2. Provide a more rigorous theoretical justification for the claim about the "analytic and tractable linear approximator for the Q-function," including a derivation or proof.

3. Fix the formatting inconsistencies throughout the paper, including consistent citation style, notation, and figure references.

4. Provide a clearer comparison to baseline methods in terms of absolute sample counts, not just relative performance.

5. Include an ablation study to demonstrate the contribution of each component (pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, FQI).

## Questions for the authors

1. What exactly constitutes a "training trajectory sample" in the claim of "just 20 training trajectory samples for reinforcement learning"? Is this 20 trajectories total, or 20 samples per trajectory?

2. How many training trajectories did the TRPO baseline require to achieve comparable performance to the proposed method? The paper states "more than 100 times as many trajectories" but doesn't specify the absolute number.

3. Can the authors provide a derivation or proof for their claim that the Q-function approximation is "analytic and tractable," given that the servoing policy (Equation 3) is quadratic in the control variables?

4. The paper claims "VGG conv5 3" is not suitable for approximating Q-values, but the paper doesn't clearly define what "conv5 3" refers to. Is this the 5th convolutional layer with 3 channels, or something else?

5. The paper states that "the features that we use are the outputs of some of the intermediate convolutional layers," but doesn't specify which layers were used for each experiment. Could the authors clarify which specific layers were used for the experiments shown in Figure 5?

The paper presents an interesting approach to visual servoing, but the lack of clarity in key claims and insufficient theoretical justification prevent me from recommending acceptance for ICLR.
