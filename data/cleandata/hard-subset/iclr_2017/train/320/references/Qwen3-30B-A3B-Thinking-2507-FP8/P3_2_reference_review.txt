

# Peer Review: "Learning Visual Servoing with Pre-trained Features and Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary of Claims/Contribution

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in visual servoing: the need for hand-designed features and models that limit adaptability to new targets and require significant data. The authors propose a novel approach combining pre-trained visual features (from VGG network trained on ImageNet), a bilinear model for visual feature dynamics prediction, and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration procedure to learn optimal feature weights. Their key contribution is demonstrating that this combination enables visual servoing that can be learned with just 20 training trajectory samples, achieving substantial performance improvements over conventional approaches (image pixels or hand-designed keypoints) and over two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency than standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The method is evaluated on a synthetic car-following benchmark where it demonstrates robustness to visual variations, viewpoint changes, and occlusions.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

**Key reasons for acceptance:**
1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in visual servoing with a well-motivated, novel approach that bridges visual servoing, deep learning, and reinforcement learning.
2. The claims are well-supported by rigorous experimental evaluation across multiple baselines and feature representations.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's strength lies in its clear articulation of the problem and a well-designed solution that addresses the core limitation of existing visual servoing methods: their reliance on hand-engineered features and models that require significant data per new task.

The technical contribution is significant. The authors' insight that pre-trained features can be effectively combined with a simple bilinear dynamics model and a sample-efficient Q-learning procedure for feature weighting is novel and well-justified. The bilinear dynamics model is particularly well-motivated by prior work on visual dynamics, and the authors provide compelling evidence that this simple model suffices for visual servoing.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The comparison against multiple baselines—including classical IBVS with ORB features, C-COT tracker, end-to-end CNN policies trained with TRPO, and position-based methods—provides strong evidence for the method's superiority. The results consistently show that their approach outperforms alternatives, especially in terms of sample efficiency (20 trajectories vs. 500+ for TRPO).

The most compelling evidence is the sample efficiency claim: "improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms." Figure 6 provides clear evidence that the FQI approach with conv4_3 features achieves a cost of ~0.5 with just 2 sampling iterations (20 trajectories), while TRPO requires 50 iterations (500 trajectories) to achieve a cost of ~0.75 (1.5x worse with 100x more data).

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these improvements would enhance clarity and impact:

1. **Clarify the "two orders of magnitude" claim**: The paper states "more than two orders of magnitude" improvement in sample-efficiency, but the comparison is between 20 trajectories (FQI) and 500+ trajectories (TRPO), which is a factor of 25x. The authors should clarify whether they mean "more than one order of magnitude" (10x) or "more than two orders" (100x). The evidence supports the former, so the phrasing should be adjusted.

2. **Expand on the feature modification**: The paper mentions modifying VGG by removing max-pooling layers and using dilated convolutions, but doesn't fully explain why these modifications are necessary for visual servoing. A brief discussion of how these changes better preserve spatial information needed for servoing would strengthen the paper.

3. **Discuss limitations more explicitly**: While the synthetic benchmark is well-designed, the authors should more clearly acknowledge limitations for real-world applications (e.g., the need for a pre-trained VGG model, potential challenges with domain shift between simulation and reality).

4. **Clarify the data requirements**: The paper states "just 20 training trajectory samples for reinforcement learning," but the dynamics model was trained on 100 trajectories. It would be helpful to clarify the total data requirements (120 trajectories) versus just the policy learning data.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper mentions that VGG conv5_3 features perform worse than conv4_3 for visual servoing. Could the authors provide more insight into why this might be the case (e.g., excessive spatial invariance, loss of local detail)? Does this suggest a fundamental limitation in using features from deeper layers for servoing tasks?

2. The comparison with classical IBVS methods requires ground truth depth values. In real-world applications, depth is often unavailable. How would the authors' method compare to depth-free alternatives (e.g., using only pixel intensities), and what performance degradation would be expected?

3. The paper states the method generalizes to novel cars (Figure 5), but it's unclear how much generalization is achieved. Could the authors provide quantitative comparisons between performance on training cars versus novel cars?

4. The authors note that "VGG features conv1_2 through conv4_3 achieve some degree of generalization on the novel cars" but conv5_3 does not. Given that conv5_3 is typically the most semantically rich layer, why would this layer be less suitable for visual servoing? Is this a limitation of the bilinear dynamics model or a characteristic of the feature representation itself?

This paper makes a significant contribution to visual servoing by demonstrating how pre-trained features can be effectively leveraged with a simple, sample-efficient reinforcement learning approach. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and supported by compelling experimental evidence. With minor clarifications to the claims and additional discussion of limitations, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
