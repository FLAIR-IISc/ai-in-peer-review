

# ICLR Peer Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to the visual servoing literature by proposing a novel approach that combines pre-trained visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to achieve highly sample-efficient visual servoing. The authors address a critical limitation in existing visual servoing methods - the need for substantial data to learn robust controllers for new targets. Their key innovation is a method that uses pre-trained VGG features (from ImageNet classification) combined with a bilinear dynamics model and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration procedure to learn feature weights that enable robust visual servoing. The authors demonstrate that their method can learn effective visual servoing for a complex car-following task using just 20 training trajectory samples, achieving substantial improvements over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and demonstrating more than two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency than standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The work is well-motivated, well-executed, and makes a clear contribution to the field of vision-based robot control.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field, and the key reasons for acceptance are:
1. The problem of sample-efficient visual servoing for new targets is clearly identified and well-motivated.
2. The proposed approach is novel, well-justified, and demonstrates substantial improvements over existing methods in terms of sample efficiency and performance.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in addressing a meaningful problem with a well-motivated solution. The authors correctly identify that traditional visual servoing methods rely on hand-designed features and dynamics models, which require manual engineering for each new system and prevent exploitation of statistical regularities in the world. Their solution leverages the power of pre-trained features from large-scale image classification tasks, which have been shown to be useful for many visual tasks, while addressing the critical challenge of distinguishing the target from distractors through learned feature weights.

The method is technically sound and well-justified. The bilinear dynamics model for feature prediction is well-motivated by prior work (Censi & Murray, 2015), and the authors extend this to feature maps from pre-trained networks. The fitted Q-iteration approach for learning feature weights is particularly clever and efficient, as it directly optimizes for long-term performance without requiring complex multi-step planning. The theoretical foundation for the linearization of the bilinear dynamics and the Q-function approximation is solid.

The empirical results are compelling and well-documented. The authors conduct thorough experiments comparing their method against multiple baselines, including:
- Pixel-based visual servoing
- Hand-designed keypoint methods (ORB, SIFT, SURF)
- End-to-end deep RL (TRPO)
- Classical image-based visual servoing (IBVS)

The key results show that their method (using VGG conv4_3 features with FQI) achieves the best performance with minimal data. The sample efficiency claim is strongly supported - they demonstrate that their method can learn with just 20 training trajectories, while TRPO requires over 100 times more data to reach comparable performance. The ablation studies and generalization tests on novel cars further strengthen their claims.

The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and the supplementary materials (code, videos, environment) are excellent for reproducibility.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance it further:

1. **Clarify the relationship between the pre-trained features and the visual servoing task:** While the authors note that VGG features are pre-trained for classification, it would be helpful to explicitly discuss why these features are suitable for visual servoing (beyond just being "useful for other visual tasks"). What specific properties of these features make them advantageous for servoing (e.g., spatial invariance, discriminative power, etc.)?

2. **Provide more details on the limitations of the method:** The paper focuses on demonstrating the strengths of their approach, but it would be valuable to discuss potential limitations. For example, how does the method perform when the target object is not present in the training data? How sensitive is the performance to the choice of VGG layers? What happens when the target object undergoes drastic appearance changes not seen during training?

3. **Compare with more recent visual servoing methods:** The related work section covers relevant literature, but it might be worth briefly discussing how their method relates to or differs from more recent approaches like those using deep learning for visual servoing (e.g., the "Visual Dynamics" work by Watter et al. or "Embed to Control" by Mania et al.).

4. **Clarify the computational requirements:** While the paper mentions that their method runs at about 16Hz, it would be helpful to provide more details on the computational resources required (e.g., GPU vs. CPU, memory requirements) and how this compares to the baselines (particularly the C-COT tracker).

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper notes that VGG conv5_3 features perform worse than other layers. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case? Is it related to the spatial invariance of deeper features, as you hypothesize, or could there be other factors at play?

2. In Figure 6, the FQI method with conv4_3 features outperforms TRPO even with only 2 sampling iterations, while TRPO requires 50 iterations to match performance. How much of this difference is due to the inherent sample efficiency of FQI versus the fact that TRPO is trained for more iterations? Could the TRPO method reach comparable performance with fewer iterations if the hyperparameters were tuned more carefully?

3. The paper mentions that the feature weights are non-negative. What would happen if negative weights were allowed? Would this improve performance, or is there a fundamental reason why negative weights would be problematic for the servoing task?

4. The authors note that policies based on pixel intensities are susceptible to occlusions and distractors. How would their method perform in scenarios where the target object is completely occluded for a period of time, and how does it compare to other methods in such challenging situations?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the visual servoing literature. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated. The sample efficiency claims are well-supported, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. I recommend acceptance.
