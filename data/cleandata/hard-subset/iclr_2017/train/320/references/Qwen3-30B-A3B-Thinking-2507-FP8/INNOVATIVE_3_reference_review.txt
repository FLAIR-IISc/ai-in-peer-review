

# Review for ICLR

## Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing by combining pre-trained visual features (from ImageNet classification), learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to achieve sample-efficient visual servoing. The key contribution is demonstrating that pre-trained VGG features, when combined with a bilinear dynamics model and a fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn feature weights, can enable effective visual servoing with just 20 training trajectory samples. The authors show substantial improvements over conventional approaches (pixel-based or hand-designed keypoints) and demonstrate a sample-efficiency improvement of more than two orders of magnitude over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

## Decision
Accept. The paper makes a bold and novel contribution to visual servoing by demonstrating how pre-trained features can be effectively leveraged for sample-efficient learning, addressing a fundamental limitation in the field.

## Supporting Arguments
This paper has significant novelty that aligns with ICLR's emphasis on bold, new directions. The core idea—using pre-trained ImageNet features for visual servoing with minimal task-specific training data—is fundamentally different from existing approaches and represents a meaningful shift in how visual servoing can be approached. The paper successfully bridges the gap between the rich statistical regularities captured by large-scale pre-trained models and the specific requirements of visual servoing.

The paper's claims are well-supported by experiments. The authors demonstrate clear performance gains across multiple metrics, including sample efficiency (20 trajectories vs. hundreds or thousands for competitors), robustness to visual variation, and generalization to novel targets. The comparison to both traditional methods (IBVS with hand-crafted features) and modern learning-based approaches (TRPO) provides strong evidence for their claims.

The authors' approach is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature, with a clear identification of the limitations of both traditional visual servoing (reliance on hand-designed features) and existing learning-based approaches (data inefficiency). The paper's focus on sample efficiency for rapid adaptation to new targets is particularly relevant for practical robotic applications.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of why VGG features work well for visual servoing. While the authors state that these features "tend to be useful for a wide range of visual tasks," they could provide more specific evidence for this claim in the context of visual servoing.

2. The distinction between the features used in the dynamics model versus those used in the servoing policy is somewhat blurred. Clarifying this relationship would strengthen the paper's technical contribution.

3. The paper claims "more than two orders of magnitude" better sample efficiency but doesn't provide exact numbers for comparison. Including specific metrics (e.g., "our method required 20 trajectories while TRPO required 2000") would make the claim more concrete.

4. The discussion of why VGG conv5_3 features underperform compared to conv4_3 could be expanded. The authors hypothesize it's due to spatial invariance, but more evidence would strengthen this claim.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that VGG conv5_3 features are "not as suitable for approximating Q-values" - could you elaborate on why this might be the case? Is it related to the spatial invariance of deeper features, as you hypothesize?

2. The paper claims "substantial improvement over a conventional approach based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints" but doesn't provide specific quantitative comparisons. Could you include these numbers in the paper?

3. The paper mentions using "bilinear dynamics" but doesn't clearly differentiate this from previous work (e.g., Censi & Murray, 2015). Could you clarify the novelty of your bilinear model specifically for visual servoing?

4. The paper states that the dynamics model is trained with "10000 samples (corresponding to 100 trajectories)" but doesn't explain why this is sufficient. Could you elaborate on the relationship between the number of dynamics samples and the final visual servoing performance?

This paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating how pre-trained features can be leveraged for visual servoing with minimal task-specific data. The novelty of the approach, combined with strong empirical results, makes it a compelling contribution to the field. The paper's focus on sample efficiency aligns perfectly with the practical needs of robotic systems that must adapt quickly to new tasks.
