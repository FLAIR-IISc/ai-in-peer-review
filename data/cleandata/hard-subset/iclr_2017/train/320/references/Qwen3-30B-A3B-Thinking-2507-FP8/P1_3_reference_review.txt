

# Peer Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in visual servoing: the need for sample-efficient learning of robust visual servoing mechanisms that can adapt quickly to new target objects. The authors propose a novel approach that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG network trained on ImageNet), a learned bilinear dynamics model for predicting feature changes, and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning procedure to learn optimal feature weights. The key contribution is demonstrating that their method can learn an effective visual servo for a complex car-following task using only 20 training trajectory samples, achieving substantial improvements over conventional approaches (pixel-based and hand-designed features) and over two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency than standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The approach is theoretically grounded, computationally efficient (running at ~16Hz), and demonstrates strong generalization to novel targets while being robust to visual variations like viewpoint changes, lighting, and occlusions. The paper makes a compelling case that pre-trained deep features, when combined with the right learning framework, can effectively solve visual servoing problems without requiring manual feature engineering.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) The paper addresses a meaningful problem in robotics with clear practical implications, and (2) The claims are strongly supported by rigorous empirical evidence and thorough ablation studies that demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a compelling contribution to visual servoing by addressing two critical limitations of existing approaches: (1) the need for extensive manual feature engineering and (2) the high sample complexity of learning-based visual servoing methods. The authors correctly identify that while pre-trained deep features have shown promise in many vision tasks, their applicability to visual servoing has not been thoroughly explored.

The motivation is well-placed in the literature. The paper provides a comprehensive review of visual servoing methods, highlighting the limitations of hand-designed features and the data inefficiency of standard deep RL approaches. The authors correctly position their work as a bridge between these two paradigms, leveraging the best of both worlds.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous and well-designed. The authors conduct multiple ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of each component of their approach (pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, and the FQI learning procedure). They show strong performance on both seen and novel targets, which demonstrates generalization. The comparison to multiple baselines (pixel-based methods, hand-designed features, standard RL, and classical visual servoing) is thorough and provides convincing evidence for their claims. The sample efficiency claim (20 training samples vs. thousands for other methods) is particularly well-supported by the results in Figure 6 and Table 2.

The theoretical foundation is sound, with the authors deriving the linear approximation of the bilinear dynamics and showing how their Q-function approximation leads to an efficient FQI algorithm. The implementation details are sufficiently complete to be reproducible, and the authors provide code and environment details online.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong overall, there are a few areas that could be improved to further strengthen the contribution:

1. **Broader Context for Sample Efficiency**: The paper claims "improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms." It would be helpful to specify exactly which standard algorithms they're comparing to (e.g., DDPG, SAC, TRPO) and provide the exact number of samples required by those methods for comparable performance. This would make the claim more concrete.

2. **Limitations of the Approach**: The paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of limitations. For example, how does the approach handle extreme occlusions (e.g., the target car being completely blocked by another car or building)? How does it perform under significant lighting changes (e.g., moving from day to night)? The current evaluation only includes moderate variations.

3. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: While the paper mentions the speed (16Hz vs. 1Hz), a more detailed breakdown of the computational components (feature extraction, dynamics prediction, weight learning) would help readers understand the trade-offs.

4. **Generalizability to Other Tasks**: The evaluation is focused on car following. It would be valuable to briefly discuss or show preliminary results on how well the approach might generalize to other visual servoing tasks (e.g., object manipulation, navigation) without requiring significant re-tuning.

5. **Ablation of Feature Levels**: The paper mentions using multiple feature levels (conv1_2 through conv4_3) but doesn't clearly explain why these particular levels were chosen or how the optimal levels might be selected for different tasks. A brief discussion of this would be helpful.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help clarify and strengthen the paper, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1. In Table 2, the authors report that their method using conv4_3 features achieves an average cost of 2.12, while the TRPO method with the same features achieves 3.05. Given that TRPO was run for 50 sampling iterations (vs. 2 for FQI), could the authors provide the learning curve (cost vs. number of trajectories) for both methods to show how quickly the FQI method converges compared to TRPO?

2. The paper states that "the deepest feature representation, VGG conv5_3, is not as suitable for approximating Q-values" and hypothesizes this is because it's "too spatially invariant." Could the authors provide more evidence for this claim, such as visualization of the feature maps at different levels to demonstrate the spatial invariance difference?

3. The paper mentions that "the features that we use are the outputs of some of the intermediate convolutional layers, that have been downsampled to a 32×32 resolution." Could the authors clarify why they chose 32×32 specifically, and whether they explored other resolutions?

4. The paper states that their method "can learn an effective visual servo on a complex synthetic car following benchmark using just 20 training trajectory samples for reinforcement learning." Could the authors clarify whether these 20 samples were used solely for the RL training (FQI), or whether they included the data used to train the dynamics model (which was trained on 10,000 samples)? This is critical for understanding the true sample efficiency claim.

These questions would help clarify the contribution and strengthen the paper's claims. Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of visual servoing, and I recommend acceptance with these minor revisions.
