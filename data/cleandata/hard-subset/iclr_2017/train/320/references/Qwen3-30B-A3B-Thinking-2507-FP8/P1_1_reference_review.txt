

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Contribution

This paper addresses the challenge of learning visual servoing mechanisms with minimal data, enabling quick adaptation to new targets. The authors propose a novel approach that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG network trained on ImageNet), learned predictive dynamics models using a bilinear model, and sample-efficient reinforcement learning to learn optimal feature weights. Their method enables visual servoing that is robust to visual variations like viewpoint changes, lighting, and occlusions, while requiring only 20 training trajectory samples for reinforcement learning. The authors demonstrate substantial improvements over conventional approaches (using image pixels or hand-designed keypoints) and show more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample efficiency compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The paper is well-motivated, technically sound, and provides thorough empirical validation on a synthetic car-following benchmark.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of visual servoing by addressing a critical challenge: sample efficiency for learning visual servoing mechanisms. The approach is well-motivated and well-positioned within the literature, and the results strongly support the claims made.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

The paper's contribution is highly relevant to both computer vision and robotics communities. Visual servoing is a fundamental problem with applications in navigation, manipulation, and other robot control tasks, but existing methods typically require hand-designed features and models, limiting their flexibility and applicability. The authors correctly identify that leveraging pre-trained features from large-scale vision datasets could address this limitation.

The approach is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors thoroughly review prior work on visual servoing, learning for visual servoing, and predictive dynamics modeling, clearly positioning their work as a natural extension that addresses key limitations of existing methods. They correctly note that while prior work has explored learning for visual servoing, it has not leveraged state-of-the-art pre-trained features or achieved the sample efficiency demonstrated in this paper.

The experimental validation is rigorous and convincing. The authors conduct comprehensive experiments on a synthetic car-following benchmark, comparing their method against multiple baselines including:
- Pixel-based approaches
- Hand-designed keypoints (ORB, SIFT, SURF)
- End-to-end CNN policies
- Classical image-based visual servoing (IBVS) methods

The results consistently show their method outperforms alternatives, particularly in terms of sample efficiency. The ablation studies on different feature representations and weight optimization methods provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of their specific approach. The demonstration of generalization to novel cars (not seen during training) further strengthens their claims.

The method is technically sound, with clear explanations of the bilinear dynamics model and the fitted Q-iteration algorithm. The computational efficiency claim (16Hz vs 1Hz for C-COT) is well-supported by their implementation details.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, I have a few suggestions to further strengthen it:

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach. For instance, how would this method perform on real-world hardware with sensor noise, or on tasks with more complex dynamics than car following? A brief discussion of potential limitations would provide a more balanced view of the contribution.

2. The computational efficiency claim (16Hz vs 1Hz) could be clarified with more details about the hardware used for benchmarking and the specific components contributing to the speed difference (e.g., VGG feature extraction vs servoing policy).

3. The paper mentions that deeper VGG features (up to conv4_3) work better than the deepest features (conv5_3), but the explanation is somewhat brief. A more detailed analysis of why certain features work better would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

4. The paper could include a more explicit comparison to recent work on visual servoing using deep learning (e.g., methods that directly learn visual servoing policies from images without feature engineering).

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help me better understand the paper and assess its contribution, I would like to ask the authors the following questions:

1. The paper states that their method can learn an effective visual servo using just 20 training trajectory samples. Could you clarify how many samples are needed per target? Is it 20 samples total for all targets, or 20 samples per target?

2. The paper mentions that VGG conv5_3 features are not as suitable for approximating Q-values. Could you provide more insight into why this is the case, and whether this limitation is specific to this task or generalizable to other visual servoing tasks?

3. The paper claims more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample efficiency over standard model-free deep RL algorithms. Could you clarify the specific comparison point (e.g., how many samples did the baselines require to achieve similar performance)?

4. The paper constrains feature weights to be non-negative (w^(l)_c ≥ 0). What would happen if negative weights were allowed, and why did the authors choose to constrain the weights to be non-negative?

These questions would help me better understand the scope and limitations of the proposed method, and would be valuable for readers interested in applying or building upon this work.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of visual servoing. The problem is well-motivated, the approach is sound, and the results are convincing. I recommend acceptance for ICLR.
