

# Review

## Summary
The paper proposes a visual servoing method that combines pre-trained features from a VGG network (trained for ImageNet classification), a bilinear model for visual feature dynamics, and a fitted Q-iteration procedure to learn weights for relevant visual features. The authors claim their approach can learn effective visual servoing using just 20 training trajectory samples, with substantial improvement over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and an improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

## Decision
Reject. The paper's claims about sample-efficiency are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided, and the evaluation is limited to a synthetic benchmark that lacks real-world relevance.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's main claim of "more than two orders of magnitude" improvement in sample-efficiency over standard model-free deep RL algorithms is unsubstantiated. The authors compare against a limited set of baselines (hand-designed features, pixel-based methods, and classical visual servoing) but fail to provide concrete numbers or statistical significance tests to support their strong claim. Without clear evidence of how many samples standard model-free deep RL would require to achieve comparable performance, the "more than two orders of magnitude" claim remains unsubstantiated.

The evaluation is limited to a single synthetic car-following benchmark (CitySim3D), which doesn't demonstrate real-world applicability or generalization to diverse visual servoing tasks. The authors don't show how their method would perform on more complex or varied environments, which is essential for a robotics paper. The paper also doesn't adequately address the limitations of their approach in real-world scenarios with more complex visual variations.

The justification for using ImageNet features for visual servoing is weak. The authors assume these features are suitable for visual servoing because they work well for other vision tasks, but they don't provide theoretical justification or empirical evidence specifically for visual servoing. The paper fails to explain why these features would be better suited for visual servoing than other pre-trained features or hand-designed features.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art model-based reinforcement learning methods designed for sample efficiency (e.g., MB-MAML, Model-Based RL with Model-Based Planning), not just standard model-free methods.

2. The authors should provide statistical significance tests for their results to support the claim of "substantial improvement" over baselines.

3. The paper should clarify the exact meaning of "more than two orders of magnitude" improvement. Provide concrete numbers: how many samples does the proposed method require, and how many would standard model-free deep RL require to achieve comparable performance?

4. The authors should better justify why ImageNet features are suitable for visual servoing, rather than just assuming they are. Provide specific evidence from their experiments that demonstrates the advantage of these features for the visual servoing task.

5. The paper would be stronger if it included experiments on a more diverse set of tasks or real-world environments, not just the synthetic car-following benchmark.

## Questions for Authors
1. How many samples would be required by standard model-free deep RL methods (e.g., DDPG, SAC) to achieve comparable performance to your method on your benchmark? Without this information, the claim of "more than two orders of magnitude" improvement cannot be substantiated.

2. Can you provide evidence beyond your synthetic benchmark that ImageNet features generalize to other visual servoing tasks? What happens when you try to use these features for servoing to a target with significantly different appearance than those seen during training?

3. Have you compared your method against other model-based RL approaches designed for sample efficiency (e.g., MB-MAML, Model-Based RL with Model-Based Planning)? If not, why not, and how would your method compare?

4. How would your method perform in scenarios with significant visual variations (e.g., different lighting conditions, occlusions, or background clutter) that are common in real-world visual servoing applications?

5. The paper claims "the actual servoing mechanism in our approach is extremely simple, and simply seeks to minimize the Euclidean distance between the weighted feature values at the next time step and the target." How does this simple approach handle cases where the target is occluded or when multiple similar objects are present in the scene, which are common challenges in visual servoing?
