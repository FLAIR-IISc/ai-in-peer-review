

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG network trained on ImageNet), learned bilinear dynamics models for visual feature prediction, and sample-efficient reinforcement learning to learn visual servoing mechanisms. The key contribution is a method that can learn effective visual servoing policies for new targets using only 20 training trajectory samples, achieving substantial improvements over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and demonstrating more than two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency than standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The authors demonstrate their method on a complex synthetic car-following benchmark, showing robustness to visual variations, viewpoint changes, lighting conditions, and occlusions.

## 2. Decision

Accept.

Key reasons: The paper makes a technically sound contribution to visual servoing with strong empirical validation, and the approach is well-motivated within the literature. The method provides a meaningful improvement in sample efficiency, which is a critical requirement for practical visual servoing systems that need to adapt to new targets quickly.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper clearly identifies a significant limitation in existing visual servoing approaches: the need for extensive data to learn robust servoing mechanisms for new targets. The authors position their work as addressing this limitation by leveraging pre-trained features, learned dynamics, and sample-efficient reinforcement learning.

The technical approach is well-justified and builds logically on prior work. The bilinear dynamics model for visual feature prediction is a reasonable simplification that allows for efficient computation while capturing essential dynamics. The use of fitted Q-iteration to learn feature weights is theoretically sound and well-suited for the problem, as it directly optimizes for the long-term performance of the servoing policy.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors compare their method against multiple relevant baselines, including:
- Pixel-based visual servoing
- Hand-designed keypoint methods (ORB, SIFT, SURF)
- End-to-end CNN policies trained with TRPO
- Classical image-based visual servoing (IBVS) with hand-engineered features
- Position-based visual servoing with ground truth car poses

The results consistently show that the proposed method outperforms all baselines, particularly when using VGG conv4_3 features. The paper demonstrates a substantial improvement in sample efficiency (20 samples vs. thousands required by other methods) while maintaining robustness to visual variations. The ablation studies (Section 6.3) further validate the effectiveness of their specific approach to learning feature weights.

The paper provides sufficient detail about the experimental setup, including the CitySim3D environment, task specifications, and evaluation metrics, which enhances reproducibility. The results are statistically sound, with multiple trajectory rollouts reported for each method.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, some improvements could enhance its clarity and impact:

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of their approach. For example, how does the method perform when the target object is not present in the training set (beyond the novel cars tested)? What are the specific failure modes?

2. The paper mentions that VGG conv5_3 features perform worse than conv4_3, but doesn't provide sufficient explanation for why this is the case. A more detailed analysis of feature representations would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

3. The paper could better clarify the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to other baselines. While the paper notes that the tracker-based method runs at 1Hz vs. their method at 16Hz, a more detailed breakdown of computational requirements would be helpful for practical implementation considerations.

4. The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison to recent vision-based reinforcement learning methods that use pre-trained features (e.g., DBC, D4RL, etc.) to better position the contribution within the broader context of vision-based RL.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper mentions that "VGG conv5_3 features perform worse than conv4_3" but doesn't provide a thorough explanation. Could the authors elaborate on why deeper features might be less suitable for visual servoing, particularly regarding spatial information and invariance?

2. The paper demonstrates sample efficiency improvements over standard model-free deep RL algorithms. How would the performance of the proposed method compare to model-based RL approaches that also use pre-trained features (e.g., Dreamer, MB-MPO)?

3. The authors use a hand-coded policy to generate the dynamics training data. How would the performance of the visual servoing policy be affected if the dynamics model were trained on data collected from the policy being learned, rather than a hand-coded policy?

4. The paper mentions that "VGG features are not optimized for this task and their representation loses some information that is useful for servoing." Could the authors provide more specific examples of this information loss, and how their method compensates for it through the learned weighting mechanism?
