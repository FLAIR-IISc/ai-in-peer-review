

# Review for ICLR

## Summary of Contributions
This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to achieve sample-efficient adaptation to new targets. The authors propose using features from a VGG network trained on ImageNet classification, a bilinear model to predict feature dynamics, and a fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn optimal feature weights for servoing. The key contribution is demonstrating that this approach can learn effective visual servoing on a complex synthetic car-following benchmark using only 20 training trajectory samples, representing more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample efficiency over standard deep reinforcement learning methods. The paper makes a compelling case that standard deep features, originally trained for classification, can be effectively repurposed for visual servoing with minimal adaptation.

## Decision
Accept

## Key Reasons for Acceptance
The paper makes a significant contribution to visual servoing by addressing a critical problem—sample efficiency—in a novel and impactful way. The core innovation of combining pre-trained features with a bilinear dynamics model and a specialized fitted Q-iteration algorithm represents a bold approach that challenges conventional wisdom in visual servoing. The authors successfully demonstrate that this combination achieves remarkable sample efficiency (20 samples vs. thousands for alternatives) while maintaining robustness to visual variations, occlusions, and viewpoint changes.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, clearly situating its work within the visual servoing and reinforcement learning communities. It correctly identifies the limitations of existing approaches (hand-designed features, data-hungry learning methods) and provides a compelling solution that leverages the statistical regularities captured in large-scale pre-trained networks.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's novelty is substantial. While prior work has used deep features for visual tasks, this is the first to demonstrate their effective use for visual servoing with such extreme sample efficiency. The bilinear dynamics model for feature prediction is a clever simplification that enables the method to scale to high-dimensional features while maintaining computational tractability. The fitted Q-iteration approach for learning feature weights is particularly novel—it's not just a standard RL method but a specialized adaptation to the visual servoing problem that leads to the remarkable sample efficiency.

The empirical results are compelling and well-justified. The authors conduct thorough comparisons against multiple baselines (pixel-based methods, hand-designed keypoint methods, end-to-end CNN policies, and classical visual servoing), demonstrating consistent and significant improvements. The ablation studies showing the contribution of each component (pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, and feature weighting) are particularly strong. The sample efficiency claim is well-supported with the 20-sample benchmark, which is orders of magnitude better than standard deep RL methods requiring hundreds or thousands of samples.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of why VGG features work well for visual servoing when they're trained for classification. The authors hint at this but could better articulate the specific properties of these features that make them suitable for the servoing task.

2. The theoretical justification for the bilinear dynamics model could be strengthened. While the paper cites prior work (Censi & Murray, 2015), a brief explanation of why bilinear dynamics are appropriate for visual servoing would help readers understand the model's limitations and applicability.

3. The authors should clarify the computational cost of their method relative to the classical methods they compare against. While they mention the tracker-based method runs at 1Hz versus their method's 16Hz, a more detailed breakdown of computational requirements would help assess practicality.

4. The paper should discuss potential limitations of the approach. For example, how would the method perform with very different target objects not seen during training? What are the boundaries of the visual invariances that the pre-trained features provide?

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper claims the method can generalize to novel cars not seen during training. Could you provide more details about the specific challenges the method overcomes to achieve this generalization, and how it compares to the generalization capabilities of the classical methods?

2. The bilinear dynamics model is trained on a limited dataset (100 trajectories). How sensitive is the method to the quality and quantity of this dataset? Would a more extensive dataset for training the dynamics model lead to further improvements?

3. The paper mentions that VGG conv5_3 features perform worse than shallower layers. Could you elaborate on why deeper features might lack necessary spatial information for servoing, and whether there are specific architectural modifications to deeper features that might improve their suitability for visual servoing?
