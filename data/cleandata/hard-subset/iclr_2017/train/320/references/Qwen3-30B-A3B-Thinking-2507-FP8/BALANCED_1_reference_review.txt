

# Review of "Learning Visual Servoing with Pre-trained Features and Predictive Dynamics"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel visual servoing approach that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG-16 network), learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to enable quick adaptation to new targets with minimal data. The key contribution is a method that uses a bilinear model for visual feature dynamics and a fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn feature weights, allowing the visual servo to tolerate visual variations, viewpoint changes, and occlusions. The authors demonstrate significant performance improvements over conventional approaches using just 20 training trajectory samples for reinforcement learning.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes a strong claim about sample efficiency, but the experimental setup and comparisons have significant limitations that undermine the validity of this claim.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main claim of "just 20 training trajectory samples" is misleading. The authors train a visual dynamics model on 10,000 samples (100 trajectories) before the reinforcement learning phase, and only the RL phase uses 20 samples. This creates an inaccurate impression of sample efficiency, as the total data requirement for the full pipeline is much higher than claimed.

The comparison to standard model-free deep RL algorithms is flawed. The authors compare their method (using 20 samples for RL) to TRPO, but TRPO typically requires thousands of samples for reasonable performance, not 20. The paper does not provide a fair comparison with methods that are designed for sample efficiency at the same data scale.

The "novel cars" evaluation is problematic. The paper states they trained on 5 cars but test on 100 cars (with replacement) from the same set of cars. This means the test set contains cars seen during training, so it's not truly evaluating generalization to novel objects. The paper should provide a more rigorous evaluation with completely unseen cars.

The paper claims "improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude" over standard model-free deep RL. However, they don't provide a fair comparison—standard methods would typically require many more samples than the 20 they used, but not necessarily the same 20 samples.

The paper's method requires a hand-coded policy to collect the 10,000 samples for the dynamics model, which undermines the claim of being "easy and quick to adapt to new targets" since this data collection step is non-trivial and requires domain knowledge.

## 4. Additional Feedback

- Clarify the total data requirements for the full pipeline (including dynamics model training) rather than just highlighting the 20 samples for RL.
- Provide a more rigorous comparison to standard model-based RL algorithms that use similar data requirements (e.g., 10,000 samples for dynamics model and 20 for policy).
- Re-evaluate the "novel cars" test with truly unseen cars (not from the same set used for training).
- Include ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component (pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, Q-iteration).
- Better explain why VGG features (trained for classification) are suitable for visual servoing, rather than just assuming they are.
- Provide more details on the computational efficiency claims (16Hz vs 1Hz), including hardware specifications.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How many total samples are required for the full pipeline (dynamics model training + RL), and how does this compare to standard approaches that are designed for sample efficiency?

2. Can you provide a more detailed comparison with standard model-based RL algorithms that use similar data requirements (e.g., 10,000 samples for dynamics model and 20 for policy)?

3. How many unique cars were used for training vs. testing in the "novel cars" evaluation? The paper states "100 cars (with replacement) from one of the two sets of cars," which suggests the test set contains cars seen during training.

4. What is the expected performance when using the method with a different pre-trained feature extractor (e.g., ResNet) or with features trained specifically for visual servoing?

5. How would the method perform if the target object has very different visual characteristics from the training set (e.g., a different car model with very different appearance)?
