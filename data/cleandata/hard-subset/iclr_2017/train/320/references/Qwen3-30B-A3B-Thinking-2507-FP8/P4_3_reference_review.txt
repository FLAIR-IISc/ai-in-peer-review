

# Review

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions
The paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG network trained on ImageNet classification), learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to achieve sample-efficient learning of visual servoing mechanisms. The authors specifically address the challenge of learning visual servoing for target following tasks that can adapt quickly to new targets with minimal training data. Their method uses bilinear dynamics models to predict how visual features change with robot actions, and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn optimal weights for visual features that focus on relevant target information while ignoring distractors. The authors demonstrate that their method can learn effective visual servoing using only 20 training trajectory samples, showing substantial improvement over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and an improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a clear, well-motivated contribution to visual servoing that is both technically sound and empirically validated. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the problem of sample-efficient visual servoing is well-motivated and relevant to robotics applications; (2) the approach effectively combines pre-trained features, learned dynamics, and reinforcement learning in a novel way that addresses the core challenge; and (3) the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the claimed improvements in sample efficiency and performance.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision
The paper clearly identifies the limitations of existing visual servoing approaches (hand-designed features requiring domain knowledge, poor generalization to new targets, and data inefficiency) and positions their work as addressing these challenges. The motivation is well-placed in the literature, with comprehensive discussion of related work in both classical visual servoing and learning-based approaches.

The technical approach is sound: the bilinear dynamics model is well-justified by prior work (Censi & Murray, 2015), and the authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of their method. The FQI algorithm for learning feature weights is theoretically grounded and computationally efficient, which directly addresses the sample efficiency challenge.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors compare their method against multiple baselines (pixel-based, hand-designed features, end-to-end CNN policies, classical IBVS) on a synthetic car-following benchmark (CitySim3D) with both seen and novel cars. They demonstrate clear improvements in performance with minimal training data (20 trajectories), and provide ablation studies showing the importance of their key components. The results are statistically significant, with careful attention to reproducibility (publicly available code and environment).

The paper also provides detailed implementation details and computational efficiency analysis, showing their method runs at ~16Hz compared to TRPO (which requires ~100x more trajectories) and tracker-based IBVS (which runs at ~1Hz).

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
- The paper could provide more detailed analysis of why VGG conv5_3 features perform worse than conv4_3 for visual servoing. While the authors hypothesize it's due to excessive spatial invariance, a more thorough investigation of the feature representations (e.g., visualization of feature maps) would strengthen the argument.
- It would be helpful to see results on more challenging scenarios, such as when the target is partially occluded or when there are multiple distractor objects in the scene, beyond the sample trajectories shown.
- The computational efficiency analysis could be expanded to include a breakdown of time spent on different components (feature extraction, dynamics prediction, FQI updates) to better understand where speedups might be possible.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- Could you elaborate on why VGG conv5_3 features (which are more spatially invariant) perform worse than conv4_3 features for visual servoing? Is it primarily due to loss of spatial information, or are there other factors?
- How would the method perform when the target object is not present in ImageNet (e.g., a novel object category not seen during ImageNet training)?
- Could you provide more details on the computational breakdown of your method (e.g., time spent on feature extraction vs. dynamics prediction vs. FQI updates) to better understand where potential speedups could be implemented?
- How would the method scale to more complex environments with multiple moving objects or more complex dynamics beyond the car-following task?
