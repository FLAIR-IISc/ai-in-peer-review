

# Review of "Learning Visual Servoing with Pre-trained Features and Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper presents an innovative approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained deep features, learned dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to create a sample-efficient visual servoing mechanism. The authors address a critical limitation in traditional visual servoing methods: the need for hand-designed features and dynamics models that prevent exploitation of statistical regularities and require significant manual engineering for each new system. Their key contribution is a method that uses pre-trained VGG features (from ImageNet classification) with a bilinear dynamics model and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn visual servoing policies that require only 20 training trajectory samples. The approach demonstrates robustness to visual variations (viewpoint changes, lighting, occlusions) while achieving substantial improvements in sample efficiency (more than two orders of magnitude) over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning methods.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field with a well-motivated approach that effectively addresses a significant challenge in visual servoing. The method is novel, well-justified, and supported by rigorous empirical evidence demonstrating substantial improvements in sample efficiency and performance.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in addressing three critical aspects of the problem:

First, the motivation is exceptionally strong. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing visual servoing methods (reliance on hand-designed features, requirement for system-specific engineering) and establish a compelling case for why pre-trained deep features could be beneficial. They correctly identify that simply using pre-trained features is insufficient due to the need to distinguish target objects from distractors, and they propose a well-justified solution using reinforcement learning to learn feature weights.

Second, the experimental validation is comprehensive and scientifically rigorous. The authors conduct extensive comparisons against multiple baselines including:
- Pixel-based methods
- Hand-designed feature points (ORB, SIFT, SURF)
- Classical image-based visual servoing (IBVS)
- End-to-end CNN policies trained with TRPO
- Other reinforcement learning approaches (CEM, TRPO)

Their results consistently show that their method outperforms all baselines, particularly in sample efficiency. The claim that their method requires only 20 training trajectory samples (compared to 1000+ for TRPO-based methods) is well-supported by their experimental results. The ablation studies on feature representations (VGG conv1_2 through conv5_3) and weight optimization methods provide valuable insights into the design choices.

Third, the theoretical foundation is solid. The paper provides a clear explanation of why their approach works, including the linearization of the bilinear dynamics (Appendix A) and the Q-function approximation (Section 5.2). The authors correctly identify the challenge of handling multiple feature scales and channels, and they provide a principled solution through weighted feature combinations learned via reinforcement learning.

The paper's contribution is significant because it bridges several fields (visual servoing, deep learning, reinforcement learning) in a way that directly addresses a practical robotics challenge: the need for sample-efficient visual servoing that can quickly adapt to new targets without manual engineering.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance its impact:

1. **Broader Context for Sample Efficiency**: While the paper demonstrates impressive sample efficiency, it would be helpful to explicitly discuss the practical implications of this efficiency. How many minutes/hours of training time does the method save compared to baselines? How does this translate to real-world deployment scenarios?

2. **Limitations and Failure Cases**: The paper does an excellent job showing what works well, but a brief discussion of failure cases would strengthen the paper. For instance, are there specific visual conditions (extreme occlusions, unusual lighting) where the method struggles? What are the theoretical limits of the approach?

3. **Computational Efficiency**: The paper mentions that their method runs at 16Hz compared to 1Hz for the tracker-based method, but doesn't provide details on computational requirements. A brief analysis of the computational complexity (e.g., time per inference, memory requirements) would be valuable for practitioners.

4. **Generalization to Real-World Robots**: The experiments are conducted in a synthetic environment. While this is reasonable for initial validation, a brief discussion of the challenges in transferring this approach to real-world robots would strengthen the paper's impact.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims the method can learn an effective visual servo with just 20 training trajectory samples. Could you provide more details about these samples? Specifically, are these 20 trajectories from a single target, or do they include multiple targets? How diverse are these trajectories in terms of viewpoints and lighting conditions?

2. The paper shows that VGG conv5_3 features perform worse than shallower features. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case? Is it primarily due to excessive spatial invariance, or are there other factors at play?

3. The authors mention that their approach uses the ground truth depth values in the classical IBVS baselines. How would the performance of the IBVS methods change if they had to estimate depth from monocular images (a more realistic scenario)? Would this significantly impact the comparison?

4. The paper focuses on target following (car following in their benchmark). How would you expect this approach to generalize to other visual servoing tasks, such as manipulation (e.g., grasping an object)? What additional challenges would need to be addressed for such tasks?

Overall, this is a strong contribution that makes a clear, well-supported case for a novel approach to visual servoing. The paper addresses a significant challenge in robotics with a well-motivated and rigorously validated solution, and it demonstrates substantial improvements in sample efficiency that could have meaningful practical implications.
