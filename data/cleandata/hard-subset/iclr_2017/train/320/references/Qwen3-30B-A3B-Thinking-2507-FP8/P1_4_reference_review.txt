

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims/Contributions

This paper addresses a significant challenge in robotics: visual servoing that can quickly adapt to new targets with minimal data. The authors propose a novel approach that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG-16 trained on ImageNet), a bilinear model for predicting visual feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning method (fitted Q-iteration) to learn weights for the most relevant visual features. Their key contributions include:

1. A method to leverage pre-trained features for visual servoing without requiring large amounts of task-specific data
2. A bilinear dynamics model that efficiently predicts how visual features change with robot motion
3. A fitted Q-iteration algorithm that learns feature weights to maximize long-term reward
4. Empirical demonstration that their method achieves superior performance with minimal training data (just 20 trajectory samples) compared to baselines

The authors show that their approach substantially outperforms conventional methods based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and demonstrates more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample efficiency over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The paper makes a significant contribution to the visual servoing problem by addressing a key limitation (sample efficiency) that has been a barrier to applying learning-based methods in robotics. The approach is well-motivated, the experiments are thorough, and the results are compelling.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

The problem is clearly articulated and important for robotics: visual servoing that can quickly adapt to new targets with minimal data. Traditional methods rely on hand-designed features and models, which limits their applicability. Learning-based approaches have been proposed but typically require large amounts of data, making them impractical for flexible visual servoing that needs to be repurposed for new tasks quickly.

The approach is well-motivated and builds on strong foundations. The authors correctly identify that pre-trained features from large datasets like ImageNet can provide useful invariances to visual distractors. The bilinear dynamics model is a good fit for visual feature dynamics prediction, and the use of fitted Q-iteration to learn feature weights is well-justified as a sample-efficient method.

The empirical results are strong and well-presented. The authors compare against multiple baselines: pixel-based methods, hand-designed keypoints (ORB features), end-to-end CNN policies trained with TRPO, and classical visual servoing methods (IBVS with ORB or C-COT trackers). The results consistently show that their method outperforms baselines in terms of both performance and sample efficiency. The sample efficiency claim is particularly impressive, with their method requiring only 20 training trajectory samples compared to the thousands required by standard deep RL methods.

The paper also makes a valuable contribution to understanding why certain pre-trained features work better than others for visual servoing, which could inform future work in the field.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where it could be improved:

1. **Feature Layer Analysis**: The paper mentions that VGG conv4_3 features work best, while conv5_3 is not suitable. A more detailed explanation of why this is the case would be valuable. A visualization comparing the features at different layers would help readers understand the spatial information requirements for effective visual servoing.

2. **Computational Breakdown**: The paper states the method runs at about 16Hz but doesn't provide a detailed breakdown of the computational components. It would be helpful to know how much time is spent on VGG feature extraction versus the servoing policy computation, as this would inform practical implementation considerations.

3. **Real-World Applicability Discussion**: The paper focuses on a synthetic environment. A more detailed discussion of potential limitations when applying this method to real-world scenarios, including sim2real challenges, would strengthen the paper's impact.

4. **Standardization Details**: The paper mentions features are standardized with respect to the training set. Providing more details about the standardization procedure would help readers understand if this would be feasible in real-world settings with limited training data.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that the method can learn an effective visual servo using just 20 training trajectory samples. Can you provide more details about the distribution of these samples (e.g., how many unique cars were used, how much variation in initial positions and motions was included)?

2. The authors mention that VGG conv5_3 features are not as suitable for their method. Could you elaborate on why this is the case, and whether there are specific properties of the features at different layers that make them more or less suitable for visual servoing?

3. The paper uses a fixed cost function for reinforcement learning. How sensitive is the learned policy to changes in the cost function, and would the method require retraining for different task objectives?

4. The authors note that the method runs at about 16Hz. Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational time for each component (VGG feature extraction, dynamics prediction, weight optimization, etc.) to help readers understand the practical implementation requirements?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the visual servoing literature. The approach is well-motivated, the experiments are rigorous, and the results are compelling. The paper should be accepted for publication at ICLR.
