

# ICLR Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper addresses a significant challenge in visual servoing: the need for sample-efficient learning of visual servoing mechanisms that can adapt quickly to new targets. The authors propose a novel approach that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG), a bilinear model for visual feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration procedure to learn feature weights. The key contribution is demonstrating that visual servoing can be learned with just 20 training trajectory samples, achieving substantial improvements over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and showing more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample efficiency compared to standard model-free deep RL algorithms. The work is well-motivated and positions itself clearly within the visual servoing and reinforcement learning literature.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field with a well-motivated approach that is supported by strong empirical results. The sample efficiency claim is particularly compelling, as it addresses a fundamental limitation in visual servoing for practical robotics applications.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in addressing the three key questions:

**1. Specific problem tackled**: The paper clearly identifies the problem of needing sample-efficient visual servoing that can adapt quickly to new targets. This is a critical limitation in existing visual servoing methods that rely on hand-designed features and require extensive training data for each new target. The paper's focus on leveraging pre-trained features to reduce data requirements is well-justified and addresses a real-world limitation in robotics.

**2. Approach well-motivated**: The paper provides a thorough literature review that properly situates their work within the visual servoing and reinforcement learning communities. They correctly identify the limitations of both traditional visual servoing (relying on hand-designed features) and recent deep RL approaches (requiring large amounts of data). The motivation for using pre-trained features from ImageNet is strong, as they note these features have been shown to be useful for other visual tasks. The paper also properly acknowledges challenges with using pre-trained features (need to weight features appropriately) and develops a suitable solution with fitted Q-iteration.

**3. Claims supported by evidence**: The paper provides comprehensive experimental validation on a synthetic car-following benchmark. Key evidence supporting their claims includes:
- Figure 5 shows substantial performance improvements across multiple feature representations
- Figure 6 demonstrates the sample efficiency advantage (20 samples vs. thousands for TRPO)
- Table 1 provides visual evidence of the method's robustness to occlusions
- The ablation studies (Section 6.3) properly isolate the contribution of the fitted Q-iteration approach

The most compelling evidence is their claim of learning with just 20 training trajectory samples, which is supported by their experimental results and clearly documented in their methodology. The comparison against TRPO (which required over 100x more samples) strongly supports their sample efficiency claim.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong overall, I'd suggest a few improvements to enhance clarity and impact:

1. **Clarify the novelty**: The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of what is truly novel in their approach. While they mention combining pre-trained features with bilinear dynamics and fitted Q-iteration, it's not entirely clear how this differs from other recent work that uses pre-trained features with RL. A more focused comparison would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Address limitations**: The paper should discuss limitations of their approach. For instance, they note that deeper VGG features (conv5_3) performed worse, but don't fully explain why or under what conditions their method might fail. This would make the paper more balanced and helpful for practitioners.

3. **Real-world relevance**: While the synthetic environment is sufficient for initial validation, a brief discussion of how this approach might generalize to real-world scenarios (e.g., domain shift from simulation to reality) would strengthen the paper's practical relevance.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper uses VGG features trained for ImageNet classification, but doesn't explore whether other pre-trained feature extractors (e.g., ResNet, EfficientNet) might yield better results. Have you tested with other architectures?

2. The paper claims "robustness to moderate variation in the appearance of the target, including changes in viewpoint and lighting, as well as occlusions." Could you provide specific examples of these variations in your test scenarios, and quantify how much variation the method can handle before performance degrades significantly?

3. The paper states that the feature dynamics model is trained with 10,000 samples (100 trajectories), but the RL training only requires 20 samples. How does the data efficiency of the feature dynamics model compare to the RL component? Specifically, is the 10,000 samples for feature dynamics a bottleneck for the overall approach?

4. In Figure 6, the TRPO method with 50 iterations still performs worse than the FQI method with 2 iterations. This is impressive, but could you provide more insight into why FQI converges so much faster than TRPO for this specific problem? Is it the specific form of the Q-function approximation, or is it something about the problem structure?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of visual servoing. The sample efficiency claim is well-supported and addresses a critical limitation in the field. With minor improvements to clarify novelty and discuss limitations, this would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
