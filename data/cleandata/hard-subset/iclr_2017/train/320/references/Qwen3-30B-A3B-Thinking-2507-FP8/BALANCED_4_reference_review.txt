

# Review

## 1. Summary of Claims

This paper proposes a novel visual servoing approach that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG-16 trained on ImageNet), a bilinear model for visual feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning method (fitted Q-iteration) to learn visual servoing mechanisms. The authors claim their method can learn effective visual servoing on a complex synthetic car-following benchmark using only 20 training trajectory samples, achieving substantial improvements over conventional approaches (based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints) and demonstrating more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample efficiency compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The paper makes a compelling case for leveraging pre-trained vision features for visual servoing, which has been underexplored in the literature.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes an interesting contribution to visual servoing, but the evaluation is limited to a synthetic environment with no real-world validation, and the paper lacks sufficient theoretical grounding for the key claims. While the results are impressive in the synthetic setting, the lack of real-world validation and the limited scope of the experiments prevent me from supporting acceptance at ICLR.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's strengths include:
- A well-motivated approach that correctly identifies the limitations of traditional visual servoing methods (reliance on hand-designed features and models) and proposes a novel integration of pre-trained vision features with reinforcement learning.
- Strong empirical results in the synthetic environment, demonstrating impressive sample efficiency (20 training trajectories) and performance improvements over multiple baselines.
- The paper makes a clear contribution to the intersection of visual servoing and deep learning, which is timely and relevant.

However, the weaknesses are significant:
- The evaluation is limited to a synthetic environment (CitySim3D) with no real-world validation. Visual servoing is ultimately a robotics problem, and the true value of the approach would be demonstrated on physical robots. The lack of real-world experiments is a major limitation for a robotics-focused paper.
- The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of their approach. For instance, they don't discuss how their method would perform under more challenging real-world conditions (e.g., significant lighting changes, extreme occlusions, or varying camera resolutions).
- The theoretical justification for the bilinear dynamics model and the weighting approach is somewhat superficial. While they provide a linearization argument, they don't rigorously establish why their specific approach to feature weighting is optimal or how it generalizes to other visual servoing tasks.
- The comparison to prior work is somewhat limited. The paper doesn't adequately address how their approach compares to recent work on visual servoing with deep learning (e.g., methods that use end-to-end learning with vision transformers or other modern architectures).

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. **Include real-world experiments**: The most critical improvement would be to demonstrate the approach on a physical robot platform. Even a simple setup (like a camera on a robotic arm following a target object) would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

2. **Expand theoretical analysis**: Provide more rigorous justification for the choice of bilinear dynamics model and the feature weighting approach. A formal analysis of the conditions under which their method would outperform alternatives would be valuable.

3. **Address limitations**: Explicitly discuss the limitations of the approach, including how it would perform under more challenging conditions (e.g., severe occlusions, large viewpoint changes, or dynamic lighting conditions).

4. **Compare with more recent methods**: Include comparisons with recent visual servoing approaches that use deep learning, such as those based on vision transformers or other modern architectures.

5. **Clarify the role of pre-trained features**: More clearly explain why pre-trained features from ImageNet classification are suitable for visual servoing, rather than just stating that they are "useful for a wide range of other visual tasks."

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims that their method requires only 20 training trajectory samples, but doesn't provide details on how these samples were generated or whether they represent a diverse set of scenarios. Could you clarify the diversity of the training trajectories and whether they cover a wide range of initial conditions, target positions, and lighting conditions?

2. The paper compares against a "hand-coded policy" for generating training data for the dynamics model. How does the performance of the overall system depend on the quality of this initial policy? Would a more sophisticated initial policy lead to better overall performance?

3. The paper states that "VGG features are not optimized for this task and their representation loses some information that is useful for servoing." However, they also claim that VGG features work well for visual servoing. Could you clarify this apparent contradiction and explain which aspects of the VGG features are beneficial for visual servoing despite their lack of task-specific optimization?

4. The paper mentions that "the feature Jacobian used in IBVS assumes that the target points are static in the world frame. This is not true for a moving car." How does your method handle the fact that the car is moving, and how does it compare to the "ground truth car dynamics" variant of IBVS that they mention?
