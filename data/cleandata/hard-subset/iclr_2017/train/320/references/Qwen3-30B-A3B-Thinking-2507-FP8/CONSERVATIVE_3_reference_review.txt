

# Review

## Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a visual servoing method that combines pre-trained VGG features, a bilinear dynamics model, and a fitted Q-iteration approach for learning visual servoing mechanisms. The authors claim their method can learn effective visual servoing using just 20 training trajectory samples, achieves substantial improvements over conventional approaches (pixel-based and hand-designed keypoints), and demonstrates more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample-efficiency compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. They demonstrate results on a synthetic car-following benchmark using CitySim3D.

## Decision
Reject. The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support its strong claims about sample efficiency and performance. The experimental evaluation lacks proper context for the sample efficiency claims, the synthetic environment does not adequately represent real-world challenges, and the comparison to relevant baselines is incomplete.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's main weakness is its unsupported claim of "more than two orders of magnitude" sample efficiency improvement. The authors state they use "just 20 training trajectory samples" but fail to clarify how many environment interactions this entails (which is the standard metric for sample efficiency in RL). For instance, if each trajectory is 100 steps long, that's 2000 interactions, which may not represent a significant efficiency gain over standard RL methods. The paper doesn't specify what "standard" RL methods they're comparing against or how many samples those methods required.

The experimental evaluation is limited to a synthetic environment (CitySim3D) that lacks the complexity and variability of real-world visual servoing challenges. The paper doesn't demonstrate how their method handles lighting variations, occlusions, camera motion blur, or other common real-world challenges. The synthetic environment appears to be highly simplified, making it difficult to assess the method's real-world applicability.

The comparison to baseline methods is inadequate. The authors compare to pixel-based methods and hand-designed keypoints, but fail to compare to more recent learning-based visual servoing methods that might be more relevant. For example, they don't compare to methods like those in (Levine et al., 2016) or (Watter et al., 2015), which are directly related to learning-based visual servoing. The comparison to "standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms" is vague and unsubstantiated.

The paper also lacks sufficient ablation studies to determine which components of their method contribute most to the observed performance. Without understanding what specific aspects of their approach are responsible for the improvements, it's difficult to assess the novelty and validity of their claims.

## Additional Feedback
1. Clarify the sample efficiency claim by specifying the exact number of environment interactions required for your method versus standard RL baselines. Provide a direct comparison to recent learning-based visual servoing methods.

2. Expand the experimental evaluation to include more challenging scenarios that better represent real-world visual servoing problems, including lighting variations, occlusions, and camera motion blur.

3. Include ablation studies to determine the contribution of each component (pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, fitted Q-iteration) to the overall performance.

4. Provide more detailed analysis of why VGG features (trained for object classification) are suitable for visual servoing, rather than assuming this without evidence.

5. Discuss the limitations of your approach, particularly how it handles cases where the target object is partially occluded or when multiple similar objects are present in the scene.

## Questions for Authors
1. How many environment interactions (not just trajectories) does your method require to learn effective visual servoing, and how does this compare to standard model-free deep RL methods on the same task?

2. Why did you choose VGG features specifically, and what evidence do you have that these features (trained for object classification) are particularly well-suited for visual servoing, which is a different task?

3. Can you provide evidence that your method would work in real-world scenarios with lighting variations, occlusions, and other challenges not present in your synthetic environment?

4. What specific ablation studies have you performed to determine which components of your method are most important for the observed performance?

5. How does your method handle cases where the target object is partially occluded or when there are multiple similar objects in the scene?
