

# Review of "Learning Visual Servoing with Pre-trained Features and Predictive Dynamics"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contribution

The paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG-16), a bilinear model for visual feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning method to learn visual servoing mechanisms. The authors claim their method can learn effective visual servoing for target following tasks using only 20 training trajectory samples, demonstrating substantial improvement over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and achieving more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample-efficiency over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The approach is evaluated on a synthetic car-following benchmark where the method shows robustness to visual variations, changes in viewpoint, and occlusions.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes a significant claim about sample-efficiency (more than two orders of magnitude improvement over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms), but the empirical evidence provided does not support this claim. The paper also contains numerous technical inconsistencies and formatting issues that undermine its credibility.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary reason for rejection is the fundamental discrepancy between the paper's claims and the presented evidence. The paper claims "an improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms," but the actual results only show a 5x improvement (2 sampling iterations with 10 trajectories each vs. TRPO's 50 iterations with 10 trajectories each, for a total of 20 vs. 500 trajectories). This is a factor of 25, not 100 as claimed. The paper's statement of "more than two orders of magnitude" (100x) is incorrect and misrepresents the results.

Additional issues include:

- **Technical Inconsistencies**: The paper states "We can learn an effective visual servo on a complex synthetic car following benchmark using just 20 training trajectory samples for reinforcement learning." However, the actual training process uses 2 sampling iterations with 10 trajectories each (20 total), but the dynamics model was trained on a separate dataset of 10,000 samples. The paper does not clarify whether these 20 trajectories are the only samples used for RL training or if the dynamics model training data should be included in the "20 training trajectory samples" claim.

- **Formatting and Presentation Issues**: The references contain numerous formatting errors (e.g., "? \Q2006\E" instead of proper citation format). Equations have inconsistent spacing and formatting (e.g., equation (2)). The paper refers to figures (Figure 1, Figure 2) that are not included in the text, making it impossible to verify the claims about the model architecture.

- **Missing Technical Details**: The paper claims "VGG features conv1 2 through conv4 3 achieve some degree of generalization on the novel cars," but doesn't specify which specific layers were used for each experiment. The paper also doesn't explain how the multiple scales of features are combined in the servoing policy.

- **Methodological Ambiguity**: The paper claims the method "implicitly incorporates" car dynamics information, but it's not clear how this is achieved. The comparison with classical IBVS methods states that "the tracker-based policy runs significantly slower than our method," but the paper doesn't provide meaningful speed comparisons (e.g., milliseconds per control step) to support this claim.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Correct the sample-efficiency claim to accurately reflect the 5x improvement (not 100x) and provide a more precise comparison to baseline methods.

2. Provide complete details about the specific VGG layers used for each feature representation, including the exact network architecture modifications.

3. Fix all formatting issues in references and equations, and ensure all figures are properly referenced and included.

4. Clarify the data requirements by explicitly stating the total number of samples used for the entire learning process (dynamics model training + RL training), not just the RL samples.

5. Provide more detailed performance metrics (e.g., average time per control step) to support the claim about computational efficiency.

6. Address the discrepancy between the claim that "the actual servoing mechanism in our approach is extremely simple" and the complexity of the weighted multiscale feature representation in equation (3).

7. Include a more thorough discussion of why the VGG conv5 3 features perform worse than the others, as this is an important finding that the paper currently only hints at.

The paper presents an interesting approach to visual servoing that could be valuable to the community, but the significant discrepancies between the claims and the evidence, combined with the numerous technical and presentation issues, make it difficult to evaluate the work with confidence. With these issues addressed, the paper could be a strong contribution to the field.
