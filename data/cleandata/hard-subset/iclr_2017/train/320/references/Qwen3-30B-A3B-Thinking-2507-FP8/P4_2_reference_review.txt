

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel visual servoing method that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG network trained on ImageNet), a bilinear model for predicting visual feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn feature weights. The approach is designed to enable rapid adaptation to new targets with minimal data (just 20 training trajectories), while being robust to visual variations including viewpoint changes, lighting conditions, and occlusions. The authors demonstrate substantial improvements over conventional approaches (pixel-based or hand-designed keypoints) and standard deep reinforcement learning methods, with sample-efficiency improvements of more than two orders of magnitude.

## 2. Decision
Accept

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision
The paper clearly addresses a meaningful problem in visual servoing: the need for adaptable methods that can quickly learn to follow new targets without requiring extensive hand-engineering or large amounts of data. The authors have well-placed their work in the literature, correctly identifying limitations of existing approaches (hand-designed features, lack of generalization, high data requirements) and positioning their method as a solution that combines pre-trained features with learned dynamics and reinforcement learning.

The claims are rigorously supported by comprehensive experiments on a synthetic car-following benchmark (CitySim3D). The paper provides thorough comparisons against multiple strong baselines: pixel-based methods, hand-crafted features (ORB, SIFT, SURF), end-to-end CNN policies, and classical visual servoing methods. The results consistently show that the proposed method outperforms alternatives, with the VGG conv4_3 feature representation with FQI learning achieving the best performance (Figure 6). The paper also demonstrates generalization to novel cars (Figure 5) and provides clear evidence of sample-efficiency improvements (20 samples vs. hundreds or thousands for other methods).

The methodology is well-described with sufficient technical detail for reproducibility (code and environment available online). The computational efficiency of the method (16Hz vs. 1Hz for tracker-based method) is also well-documented. The paper makes a compelling case for the novelty and effectiveness of their approach.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper could provide more insight into why VGG conv5_3 features perform worse than earlier layers, as this could inform future work on feature selection for visual servoing.
- The authors might consider discussing how the method could be extended to handle more complex occlusions or dynamic environments beyond the synthetic benchmark.
- While the paper mentions the environment is available online, including the exact GitHub URL in the main text would improve accessibility for readers.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that "VGG features conv1_2 through conv4_3 achieve some degree of generalization on the novel cars" but "VGG conv5_3 is not as suitable for approximating Q-values." Can the authors provide more insight into why conv5_3 features, which are more spatially invariant, perform worse for this task? Is there a way to modify the feature representation to better capture the spatial information needed for servoing?

2. The paper mentions that the method uses "a relatively simple gradient-based servoing policy that uses one-step feature dynamics." How does this one-step lookahead policy handle situations where the optimal action requires planning multiple steps ahead? Could the authors provide an example where the one-step policy would fail and how the feature weighting helps in such cases?

3. The paper states that the method can learn using "just 20 training trajectory samples." How does this compare to the number of samples needed to train the feature dynamics models (which required 10,000 samples)? Does the paper provide a breakdown of the total data requirements for the full system, and how this compares to other methods that require more data overall?
