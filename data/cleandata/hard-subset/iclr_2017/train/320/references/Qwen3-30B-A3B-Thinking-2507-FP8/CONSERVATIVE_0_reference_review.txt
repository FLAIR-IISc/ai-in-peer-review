

# Review

## Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a visual servoing method that combines pre-trained VGG features, a bilinear predictive dynamics model, and reinforcement learning to learn visual servoing mechanisms with minimal data (20 training trajectories). The authors claim their approach achieves substantial improvement over conventional methods based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and demonstrates "more than two orders of magnitude" improvement in sample efficiency over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The method learns feature weights through a fitted Q-iteration procedure to optimize long-horizon performance.

## Decision
Reject. The paper makes strong claims about sample efficiency and performance improvements but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support these claims through rigorous evaluation and fair comparison.

## Supporting Arguments
The primary issue with this paper is the lack of rigorous evidence supporting the paper's most significant claim: "an improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms." The paper compares its method (using 2 sampling iterations) against TRPO with 2 and 50 iterations, but this is not a fair comparison. Standard RL algorithms typically require many more iterations to converge, not just 2 or 50. The paper fails to demonstrate that TRPO would require more than 200 iterations to reach comparable performance, which would be necessary to support the "two orders of magnitude" claim.

The evaluation is limited to a synthetic car-following benchmark (CitySim3D) without any real-world validation or comparison to more challenging tasks. The paper claims to address a "classic problem in robotics" but doesn't demonstrate how their method would generalize to more complex or realistic scenarios. The synthetic environment is too simplistic to validate the claim that the method can "be easy and quick to adapt to new targets."

The paper also lacks sufficient ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component. For example, it's unclear whether the bilinear dynamics model is necessary or whether the performance gains would be similar with simpler dynamics models. The paper doesn't address how sensitive the results are to the choice of VGG layer used for features, despite noting that "VGG conv5 3 is not as suitable for approximating Q-values."

The theoretical claims about the bilinear model and Q-function approximation are not thoroughly validated. The paper states that "the form of the servoing policy in our approach leads to an analytic and tractable linear approximator for the Q-function," but doesn't provide sufficient evidence that this approximation is valid or superior to alternatives.

## Additional Feedback
To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct a fair comparison of sample efficiency by running both their method and standard RL algorithms until convergence, rather than comparing at arbitrary iteration counts.

2. Expand evaluation beyond the synthetic car-following benchmark to include more challenging tasks or real-world validation, demonstrating the method's applicability to diverse visual servoing scenarios.

3. Include more thorough ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, weighted features).

4. Provide a more detailed discussion of the limitations of their approach and when it might fail.

5. Clarify the relationship between the bilinear dynamics model and the specific feature representations used, including why certain VGG layers work better than others.

## Clarifying Questions
1. How do you justify the "two orders of magnitude" claim when your comparison only uses 2 sampling iterations for your method versus 50 for TRPO? Would TRPO require 200+ iterations to reach comparable performance, and how do you know?

2. The paper claims VGG features are generally useful for visual tasks, but only tests them on one specific task (car following). Have you tested these features on other visual servoing tasks or robotics tasks to demonstrate their general utility?

3. The paper states "VGG conv5 3 is not as suitable for approximating Q-values" but doesn't explain why. Is there a principled way to select the appropriate feature layer for a given task, or is this purely empirical?
