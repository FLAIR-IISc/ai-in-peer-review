

# Peer Review: "Visual Servoing with Pre-Trained Features and Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a compelling approach to visual servoing that effectively combines pre-trained visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to achieve sample-efficient adaptation to new targets. The authors demonstrate that using VGG features (trained on ImageNet classification) as visual representations, combined with a bilinear model for predicting feature dynamics, and a fitted Q-iteration procedure to learn feature weights, enables effective visual servoing with minimal training data. The key contribution is a method that achieves robust visual servoing for a complex car-following task using just 20 training trajectory samples, showing substantial improvements over conventional pixel-based methods and hand-designed features, and a more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample efficiency compared to standard model-free deep RL algorithms. The paper is well-motivated, addresses a significant limitation in visual servoing (the need for manual feature engineering and large amounts of data), and makes a strong case for the utility of pre-trained features in this domain.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**. The paper makes a strong contribution to the visual servoing literature with a well-motivated approach that is thoroughly evaluated. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the method demonstrates exceptional sample efficiency with compelling empirical results on a challenging synthetic benchmark, and (2) the approach is well-placed within the literature, with careful comparison to relevant baselines and thorough ablation studies.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main strength is its convincing empirical demonstration of sample efficiency. The claim that their method can learn an effective visual servo using just 20 training trajectories is well-supported by the experimental results. The comparison to TRPO (which requires over 100x more trajectories) and the classical methods (IBVS with hand-designed features) is particularly compelling. The ablation studies (Section 6.2-6.4) effectively show that the combination of pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, and their weighted FQI approach is essential for the performance gains.

The methodological choices are well-justified. Using pre-trained VGG features is a natural extension of the observation that features from large-scale image classification tasks generalize well to other vision tasks. The bilinear dynamics model is well-motivated by prior work (Censi & Murray, 2015) and provides an efficient way to model feature dynamics without requiring a complex video prediction model. The fitted Q-iteration approach for learning feature weights is clever and computationally efficient, avoiding the need for complex neural network architectures or extensive exploration.

The paper is well-placed in the literature. The related work section correctly identifies the limitations of both traditional visual servoing methods (reliance on hand-designed features) and learning-based approaches (requiring large amounts of data). The comparison to both classical methods (IBVS/PBVS with ORB, SIFT, SURF) and modern RL approaches (TRPO, end-to-end CNN policies) is comprehensive and fair.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, I have a few suggestions to improve it:

1. **Real-world applicability**: The experiments are conducted in a synthetic environment. It would strengthen the paper to include a brief discussion of the challenges in transferring this approach to real-world systems, particularly regarding the gap between simulation and reality (domain shift).

2. **Bilinear model details**: The bilinear dynamics model is a key component, but the paper could benefit from more intuitive explanation of how the locally connected bilinear model works. A simple example of how the spatial neighborhood operation affects feature prediction would help readers better understand this novel contribution.

3. **Failure modes**: The paper demonstrates success but doesn't discuss failure cases. It would be valuable to include a brief analysis of when the approach fails (e.g., extreme occlusions, unusual lighting conditions, or objects with very different appearance from training data).

4. **Computational efficiency**: While the paper notes that the method runs at 16Hz, it would be helpful to compare this with the computational requirements of the baselines (especially the C-COT tracker running at 1Hz) to better understand the trade-offs.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In the synthetic environment, how much of the performance gain comes from the pre-trained features versus the bilinear dynamics model? Could you provide an ablation study that isolates these components?

2. The paper claims "robustness to visual variation, changes in viewing angle and appearance, and occlusions." What specific visual variations were tested, and how did the method perform on the most challenging cases (e.g., complete occlusions, extreme lighting changes)?

3. The paper uses a single VGG layer (conv4_3) for the best performance. Why does deeper feature extraction (conv5_3) perform worse? Is this due to excessive spatial invariance, or are there other factors?

4. The sample efficiency claim is impressive (20 trajectories), but could you clarify whether these 20 trajectories represent 20 unique car instances or 20 trajectories of a single car? This distinction matters for understanding the true sample efficiency.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to visual servoing and learning-based robotics. The method is well-motivated, well-executed, and well-evaluated, with compelling results that advance the state of the art in sample-efficient visual servoing. The paper should be accepted to ICLR.
