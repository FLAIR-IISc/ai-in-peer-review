

# Review for ICLR Submission

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained VGG features, bilinear dynamics models for visual feature prediction, and a fitted Q-iteration method to learn feature weights for robust visual servoing. The authors claim to achieve significant sample efficiency (20 training trajectories for reinforcement learning), substantial improvement over conventional pixel-based approaches and hand-designed keypoint methods, and more than two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency compared to standard model-free deep RL algorithms.

## 2. Decision
Reject

## 3. Supporting Arguments
While the paper addresses an important problem in visual servoing with a potentially valuable approach, I must reject it due to several significant methodological inconsistencies and overstatements that undermine the scientific rigor of the work.

The most critical issue is the paper's overstatement of sample efficiency gains. The authors claim "improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude" but their comparison to TRPO shows only a 25x improvement (500 vs 20 trajectories), not 100x. This is a fundamental misrepresentation of their results that significantly impacts the paper's contribution claim.

There are multiple inconsistent notations throughout the paper that severely hinder reproducibility and understanding:
- "VGG conv4 3" vs "VGG conv4_3" (with underscore)
- "y(l)·,c" used for spatial resolution but incorrectly interpreted as cardinality
- Inconsistent use of "VGG-16" vs "VGG network" without specification
- Inconsistent citation formats ("Bay et al\.,? \Q2006\E" vs proper APA style)

The paper makes misleading claims about simplicity: "the actual servoing mechanism in our approach is extremely simple" yet the algorithm involves multiple iterations of FQI with complex parameter updates. This misrepresentation is particularly problematic given the paper's emphasis on simplicity.

The paper fails to provide sufficient detail for reproducibility: which specific VGG layers were used, why they were chosen, and how the features were processed. The claim that "the features at level l of the multiscale pyramid are denoted as y(l)" is inconsistent with their later use of "y(l)t" to denote features at time t, creating significant confusion.

The paper also claims "we can learn an effective visual servo on a complex synthetic car following benchmark using just 20 training trajectory samples for reinforcement learning," but Section 6.1 states they used "S = 2 sampling iterations" with "each sampling iteration" gathering data from 10 trajectories. This suggests 20 trajectories total, but it's unclear if these were all used for reinforcement learning or if some were used for training the dynamics model. This ambiguity undermines the claim.

The paper's comparison to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms is misleading since they only compare to TRPO, not to a representative set of standard algorithms.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Correct all inconsistent notations and terminology (VGG conv4_3 with underscore, consistent feature notation)
2. Provide precise details about which VGG layers were used and why
3. Clarify the exact experimental setup (how many trajectories were used for dynamics training vs. reinforcement learning)
4. Correct the sample efficiency claim to accurately reflect the 25x improvement (not 100x)
5. Provide error bars and statistical significance for all results
6. Fix the equation typos (e.g., "w (l) c |y(l)·,c |" should be "w (l) c / |y(l)·,c|")
7. Replace the misleading "extremely simple" claim with an accurate description of the algorithm complexity
8. Provide a more representative comparison to standard model-free deep RL algorithms, not just TRPO

## Key Questions for Authors
1. What specific VGG layers were used (e.g., conv1_2, conv4_3) and why were these particular layers chosen for visual servoing?
2. Can you clarify the exact number of trajectories used for training the dynamics model versus those used for reinforcement learning?
3. How do you explain the discrepancy between your claimed two orders of magnitude improvement (100x) and the actual 25x improvement shown in your experiments?
4. Why did you remove max-pooling layers and replace subsequent convolutions with dilated convolutions? What evidence do you have that this modification is beneficial for visual servoing?
5. Can you provide the missing Table 4 (CNN policies with VGG features) to support your claim about VGG features performing worse without dynamics?
