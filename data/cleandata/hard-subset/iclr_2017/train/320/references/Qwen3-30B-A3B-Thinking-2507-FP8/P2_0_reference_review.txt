

# ICLR Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing that effectively combines pre-trained visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The authors address a key limitation in traditional visual servoing methods: the reliance on hand-designed features and domain-specific dynamics models that require extensive engineering for each new task. Their key contribution is a method that uses pre-trained VGG features (trained for ImageNet classification) with a bilinear model to predict visual feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn optimal feature weights for robust servoing. Crucially, they demonstrate that their approach can learn effective visual servoing policies with only 20 training trajectory samples, achieving substantial improvements over conventional methods and standard deep reinforcement learning approaches.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of visual servoing by demonstrating a practical method that significantly improves sample efficiency compared to existing approaches. The key reasons for acceptance are:
1) The paper provides a well-motivated solution to a well-established problem in robotics that has been largely overlooked in the deep learning community
2) The empirical results are scientifically rigorous and demonstrate clear, substantial improvements in sample efficiency (more than two orders of magnitude) over baseline methods

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in several areas that make it a strong candidate for acceptance:

First, the problem statement is well-motivated. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of traditional visual servoing methods that rely on hand-designed features and models, and they correctly identify that the features learned by large CNNs on ImageNet might be useful for visual servoing. This is a significant gap in the literature that they effectively address.

Second, the methodological contribution is both novel and well-executed. The authors combine several existing concepts (pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics models, and fitted Q-iteration) in a way that creates a synergistic solution. The bilinear dynamics model is particularly well-justified by prior work (Censi & Murray, 2015), and the use of fitted Q-iteration to learn feature weights is a clever solution to the problem of selecting relevant features.

Third, the empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors test their method on a complex synthetic car-following benchmark with multiple baselines including:
- Pixel-based servoing
- Hand-designed keypoint methods (ORB features)
- End-to-end CNN policies trained with TRPO
- Classical image-based visual servoing (IBVS) with various feature representations

The results consistently show that their method outperforms all baselines, with the most dramatic improvements in sample efficiency (20 training trajectories vs. hundreds or thousands required by other methods). The ablation studies (comparing different VGG layers, feature representations, and optimization methods) provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of their approach.

The paper also includes valuable implementation details, code availability, and supplementary videos, which enhances reproducibility and scientific rigor.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these improvements would further enhance its contribution:

1. **Theoretical Justification**: While the method works well empirically, the paper would benefit from a more formal analysis of why VGG features (trained for classification) are particularly suited for visual servoing. Why do these features generalize better than other representations?

2. **Real-World Validation**: The experiments are conducted in a synthetic environment. While this is understandable for a methodological contribution, the paper would be stronger if it included preliminary results from a real robot platform or at least a more realistic simulation that includes sensor noise, calibration errors, and other real-world challenges.

3. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: The paper mentions that their method runs at about 16Hz, but doesn't compare this to the computational requirements of the baselines. A more detailed analysis of the computational efficiency would help readers understand the practical trade-offs.

## 5. Questions for Authors

To help clarify my understanding and strengthen the paper:

1. The paper claims that "the features learned by large convolutional neural networks on large image datasets, such as ImageNet classification, tend to be useful for a wide range of other visual tasks." Could you provide more specific evidence or analysis showing why these features work better for visual servoing than features learned specifically for this task?

2. The paper states that "VGG features conv1 2 through conv4 3 achieve some degree of generalization on the novel cars." Why does VGG conv5 3 perform worse for this task, and is this limitation specific to visual servoing or would it affect other tasks as well?

3. The paper mentions that "the actual servoing mechanism in our approach is extremely simple, and simply seeks to minimize the Euclidean distance between the weighted feature values at the next time step and the target." How does this simple approach compare to more complex planning methods that might consider multiple steps into the future?

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of visual servoing by demonstrating a practical, sample-efficient approach that leverages existing deep learning techniques. The empirical results are robust and the methodological contribution is well-executed. The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and makes a clear case for its novelty and significance. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the ICLR conference.
