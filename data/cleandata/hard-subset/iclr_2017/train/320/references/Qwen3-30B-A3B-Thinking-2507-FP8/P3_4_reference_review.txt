

# ICLR Peer Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing that effectively combines pre-trained visual features, learned dynamics models, and sample-efficient reinforcement learning. The authors address a critical limitation in traditional visual servoing methods: the reliance on hand-designed features and dynamics models, which limits adaptability to new targets and requires substantial data. Their key contributions are:

- Leveraging pre-trained VGG features (from ImageNet classification) as a visual representation that captures relevant invariances for servoing
- Proposing a bilinear model for visual feature dynamics prediction, which is computationally efficient and can be trained with modest data
- Introducing a fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn feature weights that optimize long-horizon performance, rather than relying on hand-tuning
- Demonstrating that their method can learn effective visual servoing policies using only 20 training trajectory samples (over two orders of magnitude more sample-efficient than standard deep RL methods)
- Showing robust performance against visual variations (viewpoint changes, lighting, occlusions) and generalizing to novel targets

The approach is particularly compelling because it avoids the need for task-specific feature engineering and leverages the statistical regularities captured in large-scale pre-trained networks.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The key reasons for acceptance are:
1. The paper presents a well-motivated, technically sound approach that addresses a meaningful problem in robotics with clear empirical advantages over prior methods.
2. The sample efficiency claim is strongly supported by rigorous experimental comparisons that demonstrate clear performance gains with minimal training data.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution—combining pre-trained features with a bilinear dynamics model and efficient RL for visual servoing—is well-justified and well-executed. The authors provide strong empirical evidence to support their claims, with comprehensive experiments on a challenging synthetic car-following benchmark.

The most compelling evidence is in Section 6.3, where they compare their FQI approach to TRPO and other methods. Their method achieves superior performance after just 2 sampling iterations (using 20 trajectories), while TRPO requires over 100x more data (50 iterations with 100x more trajectories) to reach comparable performance. This demonstrates the claimed sample efficiency advantage.

The theoretical foundation is solid, with the linear approximation of the Q-function (Section 5.2) enabling the computationally efficient FQI implementation. The bilinear dynamics model (Section 4.2) is well-motivated by prior work (Censi & Murray, 2015) and appropriately adapted for visual servoing.

The experiments are well-designed to address the key claims:
- Generalization to novel targets (Section 6.2)
- Robustness to visual variations (Section 6.2, Table 1)
- Sample efficiency (Section 6.3)
- Comparison to both traditional methods (Section 6.4) and modern deep RL approaches (Section 6.4)

The only minor weakness is the lack of ablation studies on the specific contribution of the bilinear model versus other dynamics models, but this is not critical to their main claim.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper further, I recommend:

1. **Clarify the limitations of the pre-trained features**: The paper claims VGG features work well, but doesn't thoroughly address why certain feature layers (conv4_3) work better than others (conv5_3). A brief discussion of how the spatial invariance of deeper layers might affect servoing performance would be valuable.

2. **Include more qualitative analysis**: While the paper includes some sample trajectories (Table 1), additional visualizations showing how the weighted features differ across different scenes (e.g., with occlusions, different lighting) would better demonstrate why the feature weighting is necessary.

3. **Clarify the relationship to existing work**: The paper references "visual dynamics" work (e.g., Watter et al., 2015), but doesn't explicitly position their bilinear model as a simpler, more data-efficient alternative to more complex video prediction models. A brief comparison would help contextualize their contribution.

4. **Address potential scalability concerns**: The paper demonstrates success on a synthetic car-following task, but doesn't discuss how the approach might scale to more complex environments with multiple objects or more challenging dynamics. A brief discussion of potential challenges in real-world deployment would strengthen the paper.

## 5. Questions for Authors

To help me fully assess the paper, I'd like clarification on:

1. The authors claim VGG features "tend to be useful for a wide range of other visual tasks" (Section 1), but don't provide evidence specific to visual servoing. Could the authors provide a brief ablation showing performance differences between using VGG features versus random features or features from other pre-trained networks?

2. The paper states that "VGG features conv1_2 through conv4_3 achieve some degree of generalization on the novel cars" (Section 6.2), but doesn't explain why conv5_3 performs worse. Is this due to the spatial invariance of deeper features, or could it be related to the bilinear dynamics model's inability to capture long-range dependencies?

3. The paper claims their method is "robust to occlusions" (Section 6.2), but doesn't provide specific examples of how it handles occlusions compared to baselines. Could the authors include a few qualitative examples of successful servoing when the target is partially occluded?

4. The authors mention "the actual servoing mechanism in our approach is extremely simple, and simply seeks to minimize the Euclidean distance between the weighted feature values at the next time step and the target" (Section 1). How does this differ from traditional image-based visual servoing (IBVS) approaches that also minimize feature errors? What specific advantage does the weighted feature approach provide over standard IBVS?

This paper makes a strong contribution to the visual servoing literature with a well-motivated approach that demonstrates clear empirical advantages. With the minor revisions suggested above, it would be an excellent addition to ICLR.
