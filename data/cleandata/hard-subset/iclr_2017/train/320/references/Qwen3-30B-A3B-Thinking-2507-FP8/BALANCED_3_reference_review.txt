

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary
The paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG network trained on ImageNet), a bilinear dynamics model for predicting feature changes, and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning method (fitted Q-iteration) to learn feature weights. The authors claim their method achieves effective visual servoing with minimal training data (just 20 trajectories), outperforms conventional pixel-based approaches and hand-designed features, and demonstrates significant sample efficiency improvements over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The work is well-motivated and addresses a meaningful problem in visual servoing, particularly the challenge of making visual servoing robust to visual variations while requiring minimal task-specific data.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The key reasons for this decision are: (1) the problem addressed is not clearly positioned as a novel contribution within the visual servoing literature, and (2) the experimental claims of sample efficiency and robustness are not sufficiently supported by the evaluation methodology.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's core contribution is a combination of existing techniques (pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, and fitted Q-iteration) for visual servoing. While the combination is novel, the problem itself is not particularly new in the visual servoing literature. The paper does not clearly articulate how this specific combination advances the field beyond incremental improvements. Visual servoing has been studied for decades, and the paper fails to establish a compelling gap in the literature that necessitates this particular approach.

The experimental evaluation is limited to a synthetic car-following benchmark. While synthetic environments are common in robotics research, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that the method would generalize to real-world scenarios. The synthetic environment has limited visual variation (cars are similar, lighting is consistent), which makes it difficult to evaluate the claimed robustness to visual variations, occlusions, and viewpoint changes. The paper claims the method is robust to these variations, but the evaluation doesn't adequately test this claim.

Regarding sample efficiency, the paper claims to use "just 20 training trajectory samples for reinforcement learning" and an improvement of "more than two orders of magnitude" over standard model-free deep RL. However, the paper does not clarify whether these 20 trajectories are for the entire training process (including the dynamics model training) or just the RL component. The dynamics model is trained on 10,000 samples (100 trajectories), which is substantial and likely represents the majority of the data used. The paper fails to make it clear whether the 20 trajectories are the only data used for the RL component or if the dynamics model training data is included in the sample efficiency claim. This ambiguity significantly weakens the paper's central claim.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- Clarify the exact definition of "sample efficiency" in the paper. Is it the number of trajectories for the RL component only, or for the entire system? The paper should be precise about what counts as a "sample" in the efficiency claims.
- Include more diverse test cases in the synthetic environment to better demonstrate robustness to visual variations (e.g., different lighting, weather, more complex scenes with more distractors).
- Provide more detailed analysis of why deeper VGG features perform better or worse than shallower ones (e.g., VGG conv5_3 being too spatially invariant).
- Discuss limitations of the synthetic environment and how the approach might generalize to real-world scenarios.
- Consider adding ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, and RL component).

## 5. Questions for Authors
- Could you clarify whether the "20 training trajectory samples" mentioned in the abstract refers only to the RL component or includes the dynamics model training data? If it's only for RL, how many trajectories were used to train the dynamics model, and how does this factor into the overall sample efficiency claim?
- The paper shows results on novel cars during testing. Could you provide more details on the car models used for training versus testing? Are they significantly different in appearance, and if so, how does this demonstrate the robustness claim?
- The paper mentions that VGG conv5_3 features are not suitable for approximating Q-values. Could you provide more insight into why this might be the case, and how this relates to the spatial invariance of deeper features?
