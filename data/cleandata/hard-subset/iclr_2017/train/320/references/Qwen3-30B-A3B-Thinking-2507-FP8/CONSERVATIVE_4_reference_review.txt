

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims

The paper proposes a visual servoing approach that combines pre-trained VGG features, bilinear dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to learn effective visual servoing mechanisms with minimal data. The authors claim their method can learn effective target following using only 20 training trajectory samples, with substantial improvements over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints. They report a more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample efficiency compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The key innovation appears to be a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration procedure that automatically learns weights for visual features to make the servo robust to distractors.

## 2. Decision

Reject.

Key reasons: The paper's most significant claim—learning effective visual servoing with just 20 training trajectories—is not supported by the methodology or evidence presented, and the comparison to prior methods is insufficiently rigorous.

## 3. Supporting arguments

The paper's main claim of "learning an effective visual servo on a complex synthetic car following benchmark using just 20 training trajectory samples" is misleading and not well-supported. The authors state they use "20 training trajectory samples for reinforcement learning," but the experimental section reveals they use a separate dataset of 10,000 samples (100 trajectories) to train the visual dynamics model before applying FQI. This is a critical misrepresentation—the 20 trajectories are only for the FQI learning process, while the larger dataset is required for the visual dynamics model. The paper fails to clarify this distinction, making the "20 samples" claim appear significantly more impressive than it actually is.

The comparison to prior methods is also problematic. The paper claims "improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms," but does not provide clear baseline numbers for this comparison. The authors compare to TRPO and CEM, but without showing exactly how many samples the baselines required to achieve comparable performance. The paper states TRPO requires "more than 100 times as many trajectories," but doesn't provide the absolute numbers for either method.

Furthermore, the paper does not adequately address whether the proposed approach would generalize to real-world scenarios. The experiments are conducted entirely in a synthetic environment (CitySim3D), with no real-world validation. The paper claims "robustness to visual variation, changes in viewing angle and appearance, and occlusions," but the synthetic environment may not capture the full complexity of these challenges.

The theoretical justification for the bilinear model and Q-function approximation is superficial. The paper states "the form of the servoing policy in our approach leads to an analytic and tractable linear approximator for the Q-function," but provides no detailed analysis of why this is the case or how it differs from other approaches. The paper fails to demonstrate why the bilinear model is superior to other potential dynamics models.

## 4. Additional feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Clarify the exact data requirements for their method. If they're using 10,000 samples for the dynamics model and 20 for FQI, they should explicitly state this rather than making the misleading claim of "20 training trajectory samples."

2. Provide clear baseline numbers for the sample efficiency comparison. Show the absolute number of samples required by both the proposed method and baselines to achieve comparable performance.

3. Include real-world experiments or at least a thorough discussion of the limitations of the synthetic environment for real-world deployment.

4. Provide a more rigorous theoretical analysis of why the bilinear model and Q-function approximation work well for this problem.

5. Compare to more recent deep learning-based visual servoing approaches that might have better sample efficiency, to properly position their work in the current literature.

## Questions for authors

1. The paper states "we can learn an effective visual servo on a complex synthetic car following benchmark using just 20 training trajectory samples for reinforcement learning." Can you clarify exactly what these 20 samples represent in the context of the full learning process (including the 10,000 samples used for visual dynamics training)?

2. Can you provide the exact number of samples required by TRPO to achieve comparable performance to your method, rather than just stating "more than 100 times as many trajectories"?

3. How do you justify the claim that VGG features are suitable for visual servoing without any task-specific optimization? The paper notes that "VGG features are not optimized for this task and their representation loses some information that is useful for servoing," which seems contradictory to your main claim.

4. The paper claims "robustness to visual variation, changes in viewing angle and appearance, and occlusions," but the synthetic environment may not adequately capture these challenges. What specific evidence do you have that the method would generalize to real-world scenarios with these challenges?
