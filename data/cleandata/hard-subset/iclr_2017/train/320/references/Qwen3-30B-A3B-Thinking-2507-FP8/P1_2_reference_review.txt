

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper proposes a novel approach for visual servoing that combines pre-trained visual features, a bilinear dynamics model, and reinforcement learning to learn visual servoing mechanisms that can be quickly adapted to new targets with minimal data. The authors address a key limitation of existing visual servoing methods: most require hand-designed features and models, preventing exploitation of statistical regularities in the world and requiring manual engineering for each new system. 

The key contributions include:
- Leveraging pre-trained VGG features (trained for ImageNet classification) as visual features for visual servoing
- Proposing a bilinear dynamics model to predict how visual features change with robot motion
- Introducing a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn feature weights that maximize long-horizon reward
- Demonstrating the approach can learn effective visual servoing with just 20 training trajectory samples
- Showing substantial improvements over conventional approaches (pixel-based or hand-designed features) and more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample-efficiency over standard deep reinforcement learning algorithms

The method is evaluated on a complex synthetic car-following benchmark, with thorough comparisons to multiple baselines including pixel-based methods, hand-designed keypoints, end-to-end CNN policies, and classical visual servoing techniques.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The key reasons are:
1. The paper addresses a significant and well-motivated problem in robotics with a novel, well-justified approach that effectively leverages existing deep learning techniques
2. The experimental results are comprehensive, rigorously designed, and clearly demonstrate the claimed advantages of the proposed method

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a strong case for its approach. The authors correctly identify the limitations of current visual servoing methods and propose a solution that addresses these limitations. The theoretical foundation for the method is sound, with appropriate justification for the linearization of the bilinear dynamics and the Q-function approximation.

The experimental results are particularly compelling. The authors demonstrate that their method can learn effective visual servoing with just 20 training trajectory samples for reinforcement learning, which is a remarkable improvement over standard deep reinforcement learning methods that typically require thousands or tens of thousands of samples. The comparison to multiple baselines (pixel-based, hand-designed features, end-to-end CNN policies, classical visual servoing methods) is comprehensive and shows consistent advantages for the proposed method.

The ablation studies (comparing different feature representations, different optimization methods for feature weighting) provide strong evidence for the components of the proposed approach. The results on novel cars demonstrate that the method generalizes well to unseen targets, which is crucial for a practical visual servoing system.

The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and the experiments are well-described. The availability of code and supplementary materials (including videos) enhances reproducibility.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, I have a few suggestions to make it even better:

1. Clarify the total data requirements: The paper states the method uses "just 20 training trajectory samples for reinforcement learning," but the dynamics model was trained on 10,000 samples (100 trajectories). It would be helpful to explicitly state whether these 20 samples are in addition to the dynamics model training data or if they include it. This is critical for understanding the total data requirements.

2. Provide more details on the VGG feature modifications: The paper mentions "modified VGG network" with removed max-pooling layers and dilated convolutions. A more detailed explanation of these modifications (which layers were removed, how dilation rates were chosen) would improve reproducibility.

3. Discuss limitations more thoroughly: While the paper demonstrates good performance on the synthetic benchmark, it would be valuable to discuss potential limitations of the approach, such as performance when the target object is occluded for extended periods or when distractor objects have very similar appearance to the target.

4. Address sim2real transfer challenges: The paper uses a synthetic environment; discussing potential challenges in applying this approach to real-world scenarios and how the method might be adapted for real-world deployment would strengthen the paper.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. Could you clarify whether the 20 training trajectory samples mentioned in the abstract refer to samples for reinforcement learning only, or include the data used for training the dynamics model? If the former, this is a significant advantage; if the latter, the advantage is less impressive.

2. The paper mentions that the deepest VGG feature representation (conv5_3) performs poorly. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case, particularly regarding the spatial invariance hypothesis you mentioned?

3. How would your method perform when the target object has very similar appearance to distractor objects (e.g., different cars with similar color and shape)? Would the feature weighting mechanism be able to distinguish between them?

4. The paper states that most computation time is spent on VGG feature extraction. Would using a more efficient network (e.g., MobileNet) significantly improve the real-time performance, and how would that impact the method's performance?

5. The paper shows good generalization to novel cars in simulation. How would you expect the method to perform in a real-world setting where there might be significant domain shift from the training environment?

This is a strong paper with a well-motivated approach and compelling results. The proposed method represents a significant step toward sample-efficient visual servoing that can be quickly adapted to new targets, and I believe it would be a valuable contribution to the ICLR community.
