

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to achieve sample-efficient visual servoing. The authors demonstrate that by using VGG features pre-trained on ImageNet classification, a bilinear model for visual feature dynamics prediction, and a specialized fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn optimal feature weights, they can achieve effective visual servoing with minimal training data. The key contribution is the demonstration that their method can learn effective visual servoing for a complex car-following task using only 20 training trajectory samples, showing substantial improvements over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and achieving over two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency than standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a strong technical contribution with a well-motivated approach that is clearly positioned within the literature. The claims are thoroughly supported by comprehensive experimental validation on a well-defined benchmark.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses a significant problem in robotics - the need for visual servoing systems that can quickly adapt to new targets with minimal data. The authors provide a strong theoretical foundation for their approach by leveraging the bilinear dynamics model, which is well-motivated by prior work (Censi & Murray, 2015). The key innovation lies in the combination of pre-trained features with a specialized Q-learning method that learns optimal feature weights without requiring extensive training data.

The experimental results are scientifically rigorous and well-designed. The authors conduct comprehensive comparisons against multiple baselines, including:
- Conventional approaches using image pixels or hand-designed keypoints
- Standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms
- Classical image-based visual servoing methods (IBVS) with ORB features or visual trackers
- End-to-end CNN policies trained with TRPO

The results consistently show that the proposed method outperforms all baselines in terms of both task performance and sample efficiency. The paper provides sufficient detail about the experimental setup, including data collection procedures, evaluation metrics, and validation protocols. The synthetic car-following benchmark (CitySim3D) is well-suited for evaluating the core claims of the paper.

The paper's technical soundness is strong, with clear mathematical formulations of the bilinear dynamics model and the fitted Q-iteration algorithm. The authors demonstrate that their approach is not only more sample-efficient but also computationally efficient (16Hz execution speed compared to 1Hz for tracker-based methods).

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, the authors could:

1. Provide more detailed analysis of why certain VGG feature layers (conv4_3) work better than others (conv5_3), which would help readers understand the limitations and potential improvements of their approach.

2. Include a more thorough discussion of the computational complexity of the approach, especially for real-time applications, including breakdown of computation time between feature extraction, dynamics prediction, and Q-function optimization.

3. Address potential limitations when applying this method to real-world scenarios where the target object may differ significantly from ImageNet categories, and discuss how the approach might need to be adapted.

4. Include a brief discussion of the relationship between the quality of the pre-trained features and the performance of the visual servoing system, to help readers understand the requirements for feature selection.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper mentions that VGG conv5_3 features are less suitable for visual servoing than earlier layers. Could you provide more insight into why this is the case, and whether this limitation could be addressed with additional feature processing?

2. The paper demonstrates impressive sample efficiency (20 training trajectories), but how does this compare to the number of trajectories required for the dynamics model training? Could you provide the total data requirements for the entire learning process?

3. The synthetic environment (CitySim3D) is well-suited for the experiments, but what specific challenges would need to be addressed to adapt this approach to real-world visual servoing tasks?

4. The paper states that the locally connected bilinear model was not used for semantic features due to high dimensionality. Would using a more efficient model (e.g., with channel-wise reduction) make this approach feasible for semantic features, and if so, how might it impact performance?

5. The paper claims robustness to visual variations, occlusions, and viewpoint changes. Could you provide specific examples or analyses of the conditions under which the method fails, and how the authors would address these limitations?
