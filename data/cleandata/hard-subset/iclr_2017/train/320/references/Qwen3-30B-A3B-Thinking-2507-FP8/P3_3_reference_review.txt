

# Peer Review: "Learning Visual Servoing with Pre-trained Features and Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the visual servoing literature by proposing a sample-efficient approach that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG-16), learned bilinear dynamics models, and a novel reinforcement learning procedure to solve target-following tasks. The authors demonstrate that their method can learn effective visual servoing policies for a complex car-following task using only 20 training trajectory samples, achieving substantial improvements over conventional approaches (pixel-based, hand-designed keypoints) and standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The key innovation lies in using a weighted multiscale feature representation with a bilinear dynamics model, combined with a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration procedure to learn feature weights that maximize long-term performance.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by addressing a critical limitation of visual servoing (sample inefficiency) while maintaining robustness to visual variations. The method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated with compelling results.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

The paper's approach is well-placed in the literature. The authors correctly identify the limitations of both traditional visual servoing (reliance on hand-designed features and geometric models) and recent learning-based approaches (sample inefficiency). Their use of pre-trained ImageNet features is well-justified by prior work showing their utility for other vision tasks, and they properly acknowledge that these features require adaptation to the servoing task.

The empirical results strongly support the claims. The method achieves over two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency than standard deep RL approaches (20 vs. 2000+ trajectories) and significantly outperforms baselines on both seen and novel cars. The ablation studies (Table 2, Figure 6) provide clear evidence that the bilinear dynamics model, weighted feature representation, and FQI learning procedure are all necessary components of the approach. The comparison with classical IBVS/PBVS methods (Table 5, 6) is particularly convincing, showing that the learned approach can outperform carefully engineered methods while being more computationally efficient (16Hz vs. 1Hz for tracker-based methods).

The theoretical foundation is sound. The authors correctly identify that simply using pre-trained features is insufficient for robust visual servoing (due to distractor objects), and they address this by learning feature weights via a Q-iteration procedure that directly optimizes the long-term reward. The linearization of the bilinear dynamics (Appendix A) provides a theoretically grounded justification for their efficient optimization approach.

## 4. Additional Feedback and Questions

**Major Strengths:**
- The sample efficiency claim is well-supported and addresses a critical gap in the field.
- The combination of pre-trained features with learned dynamics and RL is novel and well-executed.
- The ablation studies and comparisons to both classical methods and modern RL approaches are comprehensive.

**Areas for Improvement:**
1. **Generalization to Real-World Scenarios:** The paper is evaluated exclusively on a synthetic CitySim3D environment. While this is reasonable for initial validation, the authors should discuss how their method might transfer to real-world scenarios, where lighting, occlusions, and dynamics are more complex. A brief discussion of potential failure modes in real-world settings would strengthen the paper.

2. **Computational Efficiency:** The paper mentions that the VGG feature extraction is the bottleneck (16Hz execution), but doesn't discuss potential speedups. The authors should consider discussing how they might use more efficient feature extractors (e.g., MobileNet) or model compression techniques to improve real-time performance.

3. **Theoretical Justification for Feature Selection:** While the paper demonstrates that VGG conv4_3 features work best, the theoretical justification for why certain feature layers are better suited for visual servoing is limited. A brief discussion of the spatial invariance properties of different feature layers and how they relate to servoing requirements would strengthen the methodological foundation.

**Questions for Authors:**
1. How would the method perform if the target object is significantly different in appearance from those seen during training (e.g., different car model, color, or texture)? The paper shows some generalization to novel cars, but the authors should clarify the limits of this generalization.

2. The paper uses ground truth depth values for the classical IBVS methods. How would the performance of the proposed method change if ground truth depth were unavailable (as would be the case in real-world applications)? Would the authors need to incorporate depth estimation into their framework?

3. The authors mention that deeper VGG features (conv5_3) perform worse. Could this be related to the spatial invariance properties of these features, and how might this inform feature selection for other visual servoing tasks?

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of visual servoing with a well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated approach. The sample efficiency claim is particularly compelling and addresses a critical limitation of current learning-based visual servoing methods. With minor revisions addressing the generalization discussion and computational considerations, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
