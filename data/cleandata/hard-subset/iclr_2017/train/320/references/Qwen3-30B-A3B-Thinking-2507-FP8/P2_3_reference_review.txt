

# ICLR Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper addresses a significant challenge in visual servoing: the need to learn effective visual servoing mechanisms quickly for new targets with minimal data. The authors propose a novel approach that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG network trained on ImageNet), a bilinear model for predicting feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to automatically weight relevant visual features. Their key contribution is demonstrating that this combination allows learning effective visual servoing policies with just 20 training trajectory samples, achieving substantial improvements over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and showing over two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

The paper makes a strong case that pre-trained features alone are insufficient for visual servoing (due to the need to identify relevant features for the specific task), and that their method of learning feature weights through Q-iteration provides a sample-efficient solution. The authors provide a thorough evaluation on a synthetic car-following benchmark, including ablation studies on different feature representations, comparisons to multiple baselines, and analysis of generalization to novel targets.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**. The paper makes a significant contribution to the visual servoing literature by addressing a critical practical limitation of existing approaches (the need for large amounts of task-specific data) through a well-motivated combination of techniques. The experimental results are compelling and provide strong evidence for their claims of sample efficiency.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Strong motivation and well-placed in literature**: The paper clearly identifies the limitations of traditional visual servoing (reliance on hand-designed features requiring domain knowledge) and recent deep learning approaches (requiring large amounts of data). They correctly position their work as a bridge between these two paradigms, leveraging the benefits of pre-trained features while addressing their limitations through a sample-efficient learning mechanism. The literature review is comprehensive and well-structured, demonstrating a clear understanding of the field.

**Rigorous empirical validation**: The experimental design is scientifically rigorous. The authors:
- Use a well-defined synthetic benchmark (CitySim3D) with clear metrics (average trajectory cost)
- Provide comprehensive comparisons across multiple baselines (pixel-based, hand-designed keypoints, end-to-end CNNs, classical IBVS/PBVS)
- Include ablation studies on feature representations (VGG conv1_2 to conv5_3)
- Evaluate generalization to novel cars (not seen during training)
- Compare sample efficiency (20 samples vs. standard RL requiring thousands)
- Report computational efficiency (16Hz vs. tracker-based methods at 1Hz)

The results consistently support their claims. For instance, Figure 5 shows their method (with VGG conv4_3 features) achieves significantly lower costs than all baselines, including the tracker-based method which is the closest competitor. Figure 6 demonstrates the dramatic sample efficiency improvement over TRPO (FQI achieves better performance with 2 sampling iterations vs. TRPO's 50 iterations, using 100x more trajectories).

**Theoretical soundness**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for their approach. They correctly identify that the weighted servoing objective can be represented as a linear function approximator for the Q-function, which enables the efficient FQI algorithm. The bilinear dynamics model is well-motivated by prior work (Censi & Murray, 2015) and their local connectivity variant is a reasonable simplification for high-dimensional features.

**Novelty**: While combining pre-trained features with dynamics learning isn't entirely novel, the specific combination of techniques (bilinear dynamics model + sample-efficient Q-learning for feature weighting) and the demonstrated sample efficiency are novel contributions that address a meaningful gap in the literature.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong overall, I have a few suggestions to enhance it:

1. **Clarify the connection between feature weights and visual invariance**: The paper claims their method handles "moderate variation in the appearance of the target, including changes in viewpoint and lighting, as well as occlusions," but the explanation of how the learned weights specifically contribute to this robustness could be strengthened. A brief qualitative analysis of which features are weighted more heavily during different types of visual variations would be valuable.

2. **Discuss limitations more thoroughly**: The experiments are all conducted in a synthetic environment. While this is reasonable for initial validation, the paper should more explicitly discuss the limitations of their approach for real-world deployment (e.g., domain shift from simulation to reality, potential failure cases with extreme occlusions).

3. **Provide more insight into the learned weights**: A small analysis of what the learned weights look like (e.g., which channels or spatial locations are weighted more heavily) would help readers understand what the method has learned and why it works.

4. **Clarify the relationship between feature resolution and performance**: The paper notes that VGG conv5_3 performs worse than conv4_3, but doesn't fully explain why (beyond "too spatially invariant"). A brief discussion of how feature resolution interacts with the dynamics model's ability to capture relevant visual changes would strengthen the analysis.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper mentions that "the features at each level are obtained from the features below through a downsampling operator d(y^(l-1)) = y^(l) that cuts the resolution in half." Could you clarify how the downsampling operator works in practice (e.g., max-pooling, average-pooling, or other method), and how this affects the information content at each scale?

2. In Figure 5, you show that the performance of VGG conv5_3 is worse than conv4_3. You hypothesize this is because "this feature might be too spatially invariant." Could you provide more specific evidence for this claim? For example, could you show a visualization of the feature maps at these two layers and compare their spatial structure?

3. The paper claims "improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms." This is a strong claim. Could you clarify exactly how this sample efficiency is measured (e.g., number of trajectories vs. number of samples) and what the specific comparison points are (e.g., how many trajectories does TRPO require to match FQI's performance with 20 trajectories)?

4. The paper uses a synthetic environment (CitySim3D) for all experiments. What are your thoughts on how this approach would transfer to a real-world scenario, and what additional challenges might arise? Do you have any plans for real-world validation?
