

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims/contribution

This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG network trained on ImageNet), learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to achieve sample-efficient visual servoing. The key contribution is demonstrating that visual servoing can be learned with minimal data (just 20 training trajectory samples) while maintaining robustness to visual variations like viewpoint changes, lighting, and occlusions. The authors propose a method where they use pre-trained features to avoid the need for large amounts of task-specific data, a bilinear model to predict feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration procedure to learn optimal feature weights. The method achieves substantial improvements over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and demonstrates more than two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency than standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

## 2. Decision

Accept

Key reasons for acceptance: The paper introduces a bold and novel approach to visual servoing by leveraging pre-trained features in a way that hasn't been explored before, combined with a clever reinforcement learning procedure that achieves unprecedented sample efficiency. The core idea of using pre-trained features for visual servoing with minimal data is highly innovative and addresses a significant practical limitation in the field.

## 3. Supporting arguments

This paper excels in novelty, which is my highest priority as an ICLR reviewer. The authors make a compelling case for why pre-trained features from ImageNet classification could be useful for visual servoing, a domain where such features haven't been previously applied. The key innovation is their fitted Q-iteration approach for learning feature weights, which enables the method to work with minimal data (20 training trajectories) while maintaining robustness to visual variations.

The paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. It thoroughly reviews the visual servoing literature, clearly identifying the gap in sample efficiency that their approach addresses. The authors properly acknowledge prior work on learning-based visual servoing but correctly point out that these methods require large amounts of data, making them impractical for quickly adapting to new targets.

The empirical results are strong and support the claims. The paper provides comprehensive comparisons against multiple baselines, including pixel-based methods, hand-designed keypoints, end-to-end CNN policies, and classical image-based visual servoing. The sample efficiency results are particularly impressive, showing that their method requires orders of magnitude fewer samples than standard RL approaches. The ablation studies on different feature representations and optimization methods further strengthen the claims.

While the experiments are conducted in a synthetic environment, the authors provide a clear rationale for this choice and the results are sufficiently compelling to warrant acceptance. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I'm willing to overlook the synthetic nature of the experiments given the significant contribution to the field.

## 4. Additional feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. A more detailed discussion of the limitations of the synthetic environment and how the method might generalize to real-world scenarios with more complex visual variations and sensor noise.

2. A brief comparison with more recent methods that also aim to improve sample efficiency in visual servoing, to better position the work within the most recent literature.

3. A more thorough analysis of the computational requirements, especially for real-time applications. The paper mentions that the method runs at ~16Hz, but it would be helpful to discuss the computational bottlenecks and potential for optimization.

4. A more detailed explanation of why deeper VGG features (conv5_3) perform worse than shallower ones (conv4_3) for visual servoing, as this is a key finding but not well-explained.

## Questions for the authors

1. How do you expect the method to perform in a real-world setting with more complex visual variations and sensor noise, and what specific challenges would need to be addressed for real-world deployment?

2. Have you considered using other pre-trained feature extractors (e.g., ResNet, EfficientNet) to see if the results generalize beyond VGG, and if so, how do they compare?

3. Can you provide more insight into why the deeper VGG features (conv5_3) perform worse than shallower ones for visual servoing? Is this related to their spatial invariance properties?

4. How would the method scale to more complex visual servoing tasks, such as manipulation rather than just car following, and what modifications would be necessary?

5. What are the most significant computational bottlenecks in the current implementation, and how might they be addressed to enable real-time operation on resource-constrained platforms?
