

# Peer Review: "Learning Visual Servoing with Pre-trained Features and Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and sample-efficient reinforcement learning. The authors address a critical limitation in existing visual servoing methods: the need for extensive training data to adapt to new targets. Their key contributions include:

1. Demonstrating that pre-trained VGG features (trained on ImageNet for classification) can be effectively repurposed for visual servoing, capturing useful invariances to viewpoint, lighting, and occlusions.

2. Proposing a bilinear model for visual feature dynamics prediction that is computationally efficient and effective for short-term prediction.

3. Introducing a fitted Q-iteration algorithm that learns optimal weights for visual features, enabling the servo to focus on relevant target features while ignoring distractors.

4. Showing remarkable sample efficiency: learning effective visual servoing for car-following with just 20 training trajectory samples, outperforming conventional approaches by orders of magnitude.

The approach is well-motivated by the limitations of both traditional hand-designed feature methods and data-hungry deep reinforcement learning approaches. The experiments are thorough, comparing against multiple baselines including pixel-based methods, hand-crafted features, and end-to-end deep RL.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

The paper makes a compelling contribution to the visual servoing literature with strong empirical evidence supporting its claims. The key reasons for acceptance are:
1. The method achieves exceptional sample efficiency (20 trajectories vs. thousands required by alternatives) with strong performance on a complex benchmark.
2. The approach is well-grounded in the literature and clearly distinguishes itself from prior work.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution is well-motivated and addresses a genuine limitation in visual servoing. The use of pre-trained features is particularly clever - it leverages the statistical regularities captured by large-scale image classification models rather than requiring manual engineering for each new target. The bilinear dynamics model is a sensible choice that balances complexity and performance, and the authors provide good justification for their design.

The experimental results are scientifically rigorous and well-presented. The ablation studies (Section 6.2-6.4) thoroughly demonstrate the advantages of each component: the value of deeper VGG features, the superiority of their weight learning approach over alternatives like TRPO and CEM, and the clear performance gap over both classical methods and end-to-end deep learning approaches.

The claim of "more than two orders of magnitude" improvement in sample efficiency is well-supported by the data. The paper clearly shows that their method achieves comparable performance to TRPO-based approaches with only 20 trajectories, while TRPO requires over 1000 trajectories (100x more) to reach similar performance.

However, I have one minor concern: the synthetic environment (CitySim3D) used for evaluation may not fully capture the challenges of real-world visual servoing. The authors should clarify how they plan to address the reality gap in future work.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend the following improvements:

1. **Clarify the "two orders of magnitude" claim**: The paper states "more than two orders of magnitude" improvement over standard model-free deep RL. This is currently somewhat ambiguous. Please specify the exact number of samples required by the baseline (e.g., "TRPO requires 2,500 trajectories to achieve comparable performance, while our method uses 20, representing a 125x improvement").

2. **Provide more details about the reality gap**: While the synthetic environment is a reasonable starting point, it's important to acknowledge the limitations of this setup. Consider adding a brief discussion of potential challenges when transferring to real-world scenarios and how the method might be adapted.

3. **Include computational efficiency analysis**: The paper mentions that their method runs at 16Hz versus C-COT's 1Hz, but doesn't provide a full computational analysis. It would be valuable to include a breakdown of the computational costs of each component (feature extraction, dynamics prediction, weight learning).

4. **Address potential limitations of pre-trained features**: The paper notes that VGG conv5_3 features are less suitable for servoing. It would be helpful to provide a more detailed analysis of why certain feature levels work better than others, potentially including visualizations of feature maps.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper uses a synthetic environment, but real-world visual servoing faces significant challenges like sensor noise, physical constraints, and unpredictable environments. How do you anticipate these challenges might affect your method's performance, and what adaptations might be necessary?

2. The bilinear dynamics model is described as "relatively simple," but the paper doesn't compare its prediction accuracy against more complex video prediction models. Could you provide a quantitative comparison of the prediction accuracy of your bilinear model versus more complex alternatives?

3. The paper claims that "VGG features are not optimized for this task and their representation loses some information that is useful for servoing." However, the best-performing features (conv4_3) are still from the VGG network. Could you elaborate on what specific information is lost in deeper features that makes them less suitable for servoing?

4. The authors mention that the approach could be extended to other visual tasks. What are the key factors that would determine whether this approach would be applicable to a new visual servoing task, and what might be the main challenges in adapting it?

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the visual servoing literature. The sample efficiency of the proposed method is particularly impressive and could have substantial practical impact for robotic systems that need to rapidly adapt to new targets. With the minor revisions suggested, this paper would make an excellent contribution to ICLR.
