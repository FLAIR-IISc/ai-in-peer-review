

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing by combining pre-trained visual features (from VGG network trained on ImageNet), learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The core contribution is a sample-efficient visual servoing mechanism that requires only 20 training trajectory samples to adapt to new targets. The authors propose using a bilinear model to predict feature dynamics and a fitted Q-iteration algorithm to automatically learn feature weights that maximize long-term reward. The method demonstrates substantial improvements over conventional approaches (pixel-based, hand-designed keypoints) and achieves more than two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency than standard model-free deep reinforcement learning methods. The approach is theoretically grounded in linear approximators for the Q-function and is computationally efficient during execution.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper introduces a bold and novel approach to visual servoing that significantly reduces the data requirements for learning effective servoing policies. The core idea of leveraging pre-trained features with a simple bilinear dynamics model and sample-efficient Q-learning is highly innovative and addresses a critical limitation in the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's key strength is its novel combination of techniques to solve a fundamental problem in robotics with minimal data requirements. The authors make a compelling case for why pre-trained ImageNet features can be effective for visual servoing despite the lack of task-specific training. The bilinear dynamics model is well-motivated and computationally efficient, avoiding the complexity of other video prediction models. Most significantly, the proposed fitted Q-iteration algorithm for learning feature weights is a clever and simple solution to a challenging problem that enables dramatic sample efficiency improvements.

The paper's claim of learning with just 20 training trajectory samples is particularly impressive and represents a significant leap forward in sample efficiency for visual servoing. The experimental results consistently support this claim across multiple baselines, showing clear improvements in both performance and sample efficiency. The comparison with classical visual servoing methods (IBVS with hand-crafted features) and modern deep RL approaches (TRPO) provides strong evidence for the method's superiority.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper's core contribution to be highly valuable. The method is simple yet effective, and the reduction in sample requirements represents a meaningful step toward more practical visual servoing systems that can adapt to new tasks quickly.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach. For instance, how does the method perform when the target object is not in the training set at all (zero-shot generalization)? The experiments show generalization to novel cars, but not to completely different object categories.
- Consider adding a brief analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to other approaches, especially regarding the feature extraction from VGG and the bilinear dynamics model.
- The paper claims "more than two orders of magnitude" improvement over standard model-free deep RL, but the paper doesn't explicitly state what the baseline method's sample efficiency was. A specific number would strengthen this claim.
- The synthetic environment (CitySim3D) is well-specified, but it would be helpful to have a more detailed discussion of how this environment relates to real-world visual servoing challenges.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that the deepest feature representation (VGG conv5_3) is "not as suitable for approximating Q-values" due to being "too spatially invariant." Could you elaborate on why this particular feature representation is problematic for visual servoing, and whether this limitation is specific to the bilinear dynamics model or a more general issue with feature representation for this task?

2. The paper mentions that using VGG features as inputs to a CNN policy performs worse than using pixel intensities. This is counterintuitive given the success of pre-trained features in many vision tasks. Could you provide more insight into why the pre-trained features don't transfer as well to a direct policy learning approach, and whether this is due to the specific task or the policy architecture?

3. The authors claim "more than two orders of magnitude" improvement in sample efficiency over standard model-free deep RL. Could you specify the exact sample counts for the baseline method (e.g., TRPO) to quantify this improvement more precisely?

4. The paper demonstrates effectiveness in a synthetic environment. What challenges would you anticipate when transferring this approach to real-world robot hardware, and what additional experiments would be needed to validate the method in such settings?
