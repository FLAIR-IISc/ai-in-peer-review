

# Review

## Summary of Contribution

This paper presents a bold and novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG trained on ImageNet), a bilinear model for visual feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning method (fitted Q-iteration) to learn visual servoing mechanisms. The core contribution is the demonstration that standard deep features, not optimized for visual servoing, can be effectively leveraged with a simple, analytically tractable servoing policy to achieve robust visual servoing with minimal training data. The authors show that their method can learn an effective visual servo on a complex synthetic car-following benchmark using just 20 training trajectory samples, demonstrating substantial improvements over conventional approaches (image pixels or hand-designed keypoints) and more than two orders of magnitude better sample-efficiency than standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

## Decision

Accept. The paper makes a significant conceptual contribution by demonstrating that pre-trained features can be effectively used for visual servoing with minimal data, and the experimental results strongly support the claims. The approach is novel, bold, and represents a meaningful shift in how visual servoing can be approached.

## Supporting Arguments

This paper stands out for its novel conceptual contribution. The idea of leveraging pre-trained ImageNet features (not optimized for visual servoing) combined with a simple, analytically tractable servoing mechanism represents a fundamentally different approach to visual servoing than existing methods. While prior work has used reinforcement learning for visual servoing, it typically required large amounts of data, and the use of pre-trained features with a bilinear dynamics model is genuinely novel.

The paper's key strength is its focus on sample efficiency, which is critical for real-world applications where data collection is expensive. The claim of learning from just 20 training trajectories is supported by strong empirical results that consistently outperform all baselines across multiple evaluation metrics. The comparison to both traditional visual servoing methods (IBVS with hand-designed features) and modern deep reinforcement learning approaches (TRPO) is thorough and convincing.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with a thoughtful discussion of related work that clearly identifies the gap it fills. The authors successfully demonstrate that their approach addresses the limitations of prior methods (reliance on hand-designed features, high data requirements) while achieving superior performance.

## Additional Feedback

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of why VGG features work well for visual servoing. While the authors note that these features capture "statistical regularities in the world," a brief analysis of which specific features or layers are most important for the servoing task would strengthen the conceptual contribution.

2. The synthetic environment is well-designed, but the paper could more explicitly discuss the potential challenges of transferring this approach to real-world settings, including the reality gap between simulation and reality. A brief discussion of potential adaptation strategies would be valuable.

3. The paper mentions that VGG conv5_3 features are less suitable for visual servoing, but doesn't provide a clear explanation. A short analysis of how the spatial invariance of different feature layers affects the servoing task would help readers understand this important observation.

4. The computational efficiency claim (16Hz vs. C-COT's 1Hz) is compelling but could be strengthened with a more detailed breakdown of the time spent on each component (feature extraction, dynamics prediction, servoing) to help readers understand where the speedup comes from.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that VGG conv5_3 features are less suitable for visual servoing. Could you provide more details on why this might be the case, particularly regarding the spatial invariance of this feature layer?

2. The authors claim "more than two orders of magnitude" improvement in sample-efficiency. Could you provide the exact numbers for the comparison with standard model-free deep RL algorithms to make this claim more concrete?

3. The paper assumes the target object is visible in the initial frame. How would the method need to be modified to handle cases where the target is initially occluded or not visible?

4. The authors mention that the method uses "a relatively simple gradient-based servoing policy." Could you elaborate on how this policy handles cases where the target is not visible or the robot is far from the target?
