

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG), a bilinear model for visual dynamics, and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning method (fitted Q-iteration) to learn visual servoing mechanisms. The authors demonstrate that their method can learn effective visual servoing on a complex synthetic car-following benchmark using only 20 training trajectory samples for reinforcement learning. They claim substantial improvements over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and a sample-efficiency improvement of more than two orders of magnitude over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The key contribution is the demonstration that pre-trained features can be effectively used for visual servoing with minimal data.

## 2. Decision

Reject. While the paper presents a promising approach with strong conceptual motivation, the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient rigor to support the claims made, particularly regarding sample-efficiency. The comparison to prior work is not sufficiently fair, and the results do not conclusively demonstrate the claimed improvements.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main claim of "improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude" is overstated based on the experimental setup. The authors compare their FQI method (using 2 sampling iterations with 10 trajectories each, totaling 20 trajectories) to TRPO (using 50 iterations with 4000 samples per iteration). However, the paper does not clarify whether these represent equivalent amounts of training data. The authors state that TRPO "used more than 100 times as many trajectories," but the exact comparison is unclear. A fair comparison would require using the same number of environment interactions for both methods, not just different numbers of iterations.

The paper claims to learn visual servoing using "just 20 training trajectory samples for reinforcement learning," but the dynamics model was trained on 10,000 samples generated by a hand-coded policy. The paper does not clearly distinguish between the data used for dynamics model training versus the RL data, which is crucial for understanding the true sample efficiency.

The paper lacks a critical baseline: a model-based RL method using the same pre-trained features but without the weighting mechanism. This would help isolate whether the contribution is the weighting mechanism or the use of pre-trained features.

The synthetic environment (CitySim3D) is well-defined, but the paper provides insufficient analysis of the realism of the simulation and how the results might translate to real-world applications. The paper doesn't address the significant gap between simulation and reality, which is critical for robotics applications.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- Clarify the exact number of samples used for each method and ensure fair comparison based on equivalent amounts of training data. For example, if FQI used 20 samples, TRPO should be compared with 20 samples as well, not 50 iterations with 4000 samples.

- Provide a more detailed analysis of why certain VGG layers (conv4_3) work better than others (conv5_3), including visualizations of feature maps and their discriminative properties. This would strengthen the theoretical motivation.

- Include ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the weighting mechanism versus the use of pre-trained features. Compare a model using pre-trained features without weighting to the full method.

- Discuss the limitations of the approach more thoroughly, particularly regarding the gap between simulation and reality. How might this approach need to be adapted for real-world deployment?

- Provide more detailed analysis of failure modes. The paper mentions that VGG conv5_3 features don't work as well, but doesn't explain why or how this limitation could be overcome.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states "improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude," but the comparison is made against TRPO using 50 iterations while FQI uses 2 iterations. Could you clarify how many environment interactions were used for each method (not just the number of iterations) to make the comparison fair?

2. The paper uses a synthetic environment with ground truth depth and car dynamics information. How would the method perform in a more realistic setting without access to this information, such as using only RGB images and no ground truth depth?

3. The authors claim VGG conv5_3 features are "too spatially invariant" for servoing. Can you provide more specific analysis of what properties of these features make them unsuitable, and how might these properties be addressed in future work?

4. The paper states the dynamics model was trained on 10,000 samples, but the RL component used only 20 samples. How does the overall sample efficiency (including the dynamics model training) compare to prior work that might also require a similar amount of data to train a dynamics model?
