

# Review of "Learning Visual Servoing with Pre-trained Features and Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes an approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained visual features (from VGG network), a bilinear model for predicting visual feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning method (FQI) to learn feature weights. The authors claim their method can learn effective visual servoing for target following using just 20 training trajectory samples, achieving substantial improvement over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, with more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample efficiency compared to standard model-free deep RL algorithms. The approach is evaluated on a synthetic car-following benchmark.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The key reasons for rejection are: (1) The paper's core contribution appears to be a relatively straightforward combination of existing techniques without sufficient novelty or theoretical insight, and (2) The experimental evaluation is limited to a single synthetic environment without sufficient evidence of generalizability or practical impact.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution is the integration of three well-established techniques: (1) pre-trained features from VGG (standard in vision tasks), (2) bilinear dynamics models (previously used in Censi & Murray, 2015), and (3) FQI for learning feature weights (a standard RL technique). While the combination is novel for visual servoing, the paper fails to establish why this specific integration provides meaningful new insights beyond the sum of its parts. The bilinear dynamics model is presented as an improvement over previous work, but the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate why a bilinear model is particularly well-suited for visual feature dynamics in servoing contexts.

The experimental evaluation is limited to a single synthetic car-following task with a controlled environment. While the results are promising for this specific benchmark, the paper provides no evidence of generalizability to other visual servoing tasks (e.g., manipulation, navigation in different environments) or real-world settings. The comparison with prior methods is also limited to older baselines (e.g., TRPO from 2015), while more recent state-of-the-art methods for visual servoing and RL (e.g., PPO, SAC) are not included, weakening the sample efficiency claim.

The paper claims "more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample-efficiency" over standard model-free deep RL algorithms. However, the comparison is against TRPO, which is not the current state-of-the-art for sample-efficient RL. The paper would need to compare against more recent algorithms (e.g., PPO, SAC) to properly establish this claim.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
- **Novelty Clarification**: The paper should more clearly articulate what makes this specific combination of techniques novel and why it's better than existing approaches. A more detailed discussion of the theoretical insights gained from combining these elements would strengthen the contribution.

- **Broader Evaluation**: The paper would benefit from additional experiments on different visual servoing tasks (e.g., manipulation, navigation in more complex environments) and potentially some real-world validation to demonstrate generalizability beyond the synthetic car-following benchmark.

- **Updated Baselines**: The comparison with RL methods should include more recent algorithms (PPO, SAC) to properly establish the sample-efficiency claim. The paper should also clarify how "sample-efficiency" is measured (e.g., number of environment interactions, wall-clock time, etc.).

- **Ablation Study**: An ablation study showing the contribution of each component (pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics, FQI) to the overall performance would help clarify the necessity of each part of the approach.

- **Theoretical Justification**: The paper would benefit from a more thorough theoretical justification for why the bilinear model is appropriate for visual feature dynamics in this context, rather than simply presenting it as an empirical choice.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- How would your approach generalize to visual servoing tasks that require more complex interactions with the environment (e.g., object manipulation rather than just following)?
- Can you provide evidence that the bilinear dynamics model is necessary for your approach, or would a simpler model (e.g., linear dynamics) work just as well?
- How does the approach handle cases where the target object is not present in the scene (e.g., if the car is occluded for an extended period)?
- Have you considered using more recent visual feature extractors (e.g., ResNet, ViT) instead of VGG, which might provide better features for visual servoing?
- Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of your approach compared to other methods, especially in terms of real-time performance requirements for robotics applications?
