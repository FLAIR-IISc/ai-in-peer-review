### **Review**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel method for visual servoing that combines several modern machine learning techniques. The core idea is to leverage features from a pre-trained VGG network, learn a predictive model of these features' dynamics under robot actions, and then use reinforcement learning to find an optimal servoing policy. The dynamics are modeled using a multiscale bilinear model, which allows for an efficient, analytical solution to the one-step servoing problem. The key contribution is a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm that learns to weight different feature channels and scales, effectively learning a task-specific distance metric that focuses on the target object and ignores distractors. The authors demonstrate on a simulated car-following task that their method can be trained with only 20 trajectories, significantly outperforming classical visual servoing methods and demonstrating orders-of-magnitude greater sample efficiency than standard deep reinforcement learning approaches.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The description of the core algorithmic contribution—the proposed Fitted Q-Iteration procedure—is critically underspecified and contains apparent flaws that make the method irreproducible and its correctness questionable.
2.  The paper suffers from a substantial number of minor yet significant issues, including notational inconsistencies, opaque methodological choices, and severe formatting errors, which collectively undermine confidence in the work's rigor.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

While the overall direction of combining pre-trained features, dynamics models, and RL is promising, the execution and presentation of the methodology are not up to the standard for publication.

**Major Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Correctness of the FQI Algorithm:** The FQI method in Section 5.3 is the paper's main novelty, but its description is deeply problematic.
    *   The two-step update process (Eq. 5 and 6) is poorly motivated. The claim that it "speed[s] up the optimization" by solving for a scalar `α` and bias `b` is hand-wavy. The optimization in Eq. (5) is non-convex due to the `min_u Q` term inside the squared error, which itself depends on `α` and `b`. The paper does not explain how this optimization is actually performed. Is the `min_u` term treated as a fixed target? This crucial detail is missing.
    *   Algorithm 1 contains what appear to be serious typographical errors. Line 3 specifies using the initial policy `π_θ(0)` for exploration in *every* sampling iteration, meaning the policy never improves its data collection strategy. Furthermore, line 8, `θ(0) ← θ(K)`, is either a no-op at the end of the inner loop or implies the weights are reset, which makes no sense. As written, the algorithm is either incorrect or incomplete.
    *   The weight parameters `w` and `λ` must be non-negative, as stated in Section 5.1. However, the optimization problems in Eq. (5) and (6) are presented as unconstrained least-squares problems. The mechanism for enforcing this critical non-negativity constraint is not mentioned in the main text, a significant omission for reproducibility.

*   **Insufficient Methodological Justification:**
    *   The paper proposes a "locally connected bilinear model" (Eq. 2), with the definition of the locally connected operator relegated to a dense footnote. There is no justification provided for why this complex, high-parameter model (with untied weights) is preferable to a standard convolutional operator (with tied weights), which would be more parameter-efficient and is a more common choice.
    *   The use of a bilinear model is motivated by Censi & Murray (2015), who showed it works well for pixel dynamics "under mild assumptions." The current paper fails to state what these assumptions are and whether they hold for the dynamics of abstract VGG features, which is a substantial generalization.

**Minor (but Numerous) Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** There are several inconsistencies that detract from clarity. For example, the squared L2 norm is written as `‖·‖^2` in Eq. (1) but as `‖·‖^2_2` in Eq. (3). The cardinality operator `|·|` in Eq. (3) is non-standard for denoting spatial resolution; `H × W` would be clearer.
*   **Poor Structure and Presentation:** Critical details are frequently buried in the appendix. For instance, the method for handling non-negativity constraints in the TRPO baseline (`√w`) is only mentioned in Appendix C.3. The policy parameterization for the TRPO baseline is confusing in the main text and only clarified in the appendix. Such details are essential for understanding the main experiments and should be in the paper's body.
*   **Formatting:** The bibliography is severely malformed, with artifacts such as `Bay et al\.,? \Q2006\E, Bay et al\.,` appearing for nearly every entry. This demonstrates a concerning lack of attention to detail in the manuscript's preparation.
*   **Understated Baseline Advantages:** The classical IBVS baselines are provided with ground truth depth for their interaction matrices and a ground truth bounding box for initializing the tracker. These are enormous advantages that are not available to the proposed method. While the paper's ability to outperform these baselines is impressive, the significance of this oracle information should be stated more prominently and discussed more thoroughly.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The highest priority should be to rewrite Section 5.3 and Algorithm 1 entirely. Provide a clear, step-by-step derivation for the two-stage FQI update. Explain precisely how the optimization problems are solved, including how non-negativity is enforced and how the `min_u` term is handled. The pseudocode must be corrected to show how the policy is updated between sampling iterations.
*   In Section 4.2, move the definition of the locally connected operator into the main text and provide a clear justification for its use over a standard convolutional model. A comparison of parameter counts would be helpful.
*   Perform a thorough pass to fix all notational inconsistencies and formatting errors, especially the reference list.
*   Strengthen the experimental analysis by being more upfront about the oracle information given to baselines and discussing the potential limitations of the approach, such as its reliance on a hand-coded policy for collecting dynamics data and the potential for model bias.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the exact optimization algorithm used to solve Equation (5)? Is the `min_u Q` term pre-computed and treated as a constant target for the least-squares fit? If so, what parameters are used for this pre-computation?
2.  Please provide the corrected pseudocode for Algorithm 1. Specifically, how is the exploration policy updated after each set of FQI iterations, and what is the correct weight update rule between sampling iterations `s`?
3.  How are the non-negativity constraints on the weights `θ` enforced when solving the least-squares problems in Equations (5) and (6)? Is it a constrained optimization, or is a re-parameterization trick used?
4.  What was the rationale for choosing a locally connected model for the dynamics over a standard convolutional model? Did you experiment with a convolutional model, and if so, how did it perform?
