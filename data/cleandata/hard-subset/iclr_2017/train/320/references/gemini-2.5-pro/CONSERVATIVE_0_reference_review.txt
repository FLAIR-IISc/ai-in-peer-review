### Review

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel method for visual servoing that combines several modern machine learning techniques. The core idea is to leverage features from a pre-trained convolutional neural network (VGG) as the visual representation, learn a predictive model of these features' dynamics, and then use reinforcement learning to learn an optimal policy for a target-following task. Specifically, the dynamics are modeled using a bilinear system, which captures the interaction between the current visual features and the robot's actions. To handle the high dimensionality of the CNN features and focus on task-relevant information, the authors introduce a sample-efficient Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) algorithm. This FQI variant learns to weight different feature channels and scales, effectively learning a task-specific distance metric. The final control policy is a simple, greedy action selection that minimizes the weighted, predicted one-step error to the target features. The authors evaluate their method on a complex, synthetic car-following task, demonstrating significant improvements in performance and a dramatic reduction in sample requirements (using only 20 trajectories) compared to standard model-free deep RL (TRPO) and classical visual servoing techniques.

**Decision: Reject**

Key reasons for this decision are:
1.  The methodological novelty relies on a combination of existing components, but the specific choices, particularly the bilinear dynamics model and the custom FQI algorithm, are not sufficiently justified against more established or simpler alternatives. The work lacks ablation studies to demonstrate that the complexity of these components is necessary.
2.  The empirical evaluation, while showing impressive results, suffers from significant weaknesses in the baseline comparisons. Specifically, the classical baselines are provided with ground truth information (depth) that is not available to the proposed method, making the comparison fundamentally unfair and casting doubt on the claimed superiority.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper presents an interesting integration of several powerful ideas, and the claimed sample efficiency is compelling. However, as a reviewer who values methodological soundness and rigorous evaluation, I find several aspects of the work unconvincing.

*   **Methodological Justification:** The approach is presented as a pipeline of three main components: pre-trained features, a bilinear dynamics model, and a custom FQI algorithm.
    *   **Bilinear Dynamics:** The choice of a bilinear model is motivated by a single citation and the intuition that it can model interactions. However, there is no empirical evidence provided to show that this specific model class is superior to a simpler, more standard linear model learned on the VGG features. Given that the VGG features are already highly non-linear, a linear dynamics model (i.e., `y_t+1 = A*y_t + B*u_t`) is a strong and conventional baseline that is conspicuously absent. Without an ablation study comparing linear vs. bilinear dynamics, it is unclear if the added complexity of the bilinear model contributes meaningfully to performance or if a simpler model would have sufficed.
    *   **Custom FQI Algorithm:** The proposed FQI algorithm introduces a two-step update process (Eqs. 5 and 6) where scaling and bias parameters (`alpha`, `b`) are first solved for jointly before updating the main weight vector `theta`. This is an unconventional modification to the standard FQI procedure. The paper provides no theoretical justification or intuitive explanation for why this two-step process is preferable to a standard FQI update. It appears to be a heuristic that worked for this problem, but without further analysis, its stability and general applicability are questionable. This departure from established algorithms requires stronger motivation and analysis than is currently provided.

*   **Empirical Evaluation and Baselines:** The strength of the paper rests heavily on its experimental results, but there are critical issues with the comparisons.
    *   **Unfair Comparison to Classical Methods:** The most significant flaw is the comparison with Image-Based Visual Servoing (IBVS). In Section 6.4, the paper states that for IBVS methods, "we provide the ground truth depth values of the feature points, which are used in the algorithm’s interaction matrix." This is a crucial piece of information that classical methods require but which the proposed learning-based method does not use. Providing a baseline with privileged, ground-truth information makes the comparison invalid. A fair comparison would require the IBVS method to rely on estimated depth or an adaptive Jacobian, which are standard techniques for unknown-depth scenarios. As presented, the results do not prove that the proposed method is better, only that it is better than a classical method given access to perfect depth information. The strong performance of the tracker-based IBVS, despite this handicap, suggests it remains a very competitive approach.
    *   **Scope of Validation:** The entire evaluation is conducted on a single, simulated car-following task. While the environment appears visually complex, grounding the paper's claims requires validation in more than one domain. The generalization demonstrated is only to novel car models within the same simulator and task structure. To be truly convincing, the method should be tested on at least one other distinct servoing task (e.g., manipulation, docking) to show that the approach itself is general, not just the trained model for this one task.
    *   **Sample Efficiency Claims:** The claim of learning from only 20 trajectories is impressive. However, the comparison to TRPO, which requires over 100 times more data, needs careful consideration. While the result is plausible given that the proposed method bakes in significant priors (VGG features, a structured dynamics model), the performance gap is so large that it raises questions about the hyperparameter tuning of the TRPO baseline. A more thorough exploration of the TRPO agent's architecture and learning parameters would be needed to be fully confident that it represents a strong, well-tuned model-free baseline.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   Could you please conduct an ablation study comparing the performance of your system with a simple linear dynamics model (`y_t+1 = A*y_t + B*u_t`) learned on the same VGG features? This would clarify the contribution of the bilinear model.
*   What is the intuition behind the two-step update in your FQI algorithm? Does it have any connection to other optimization techniques, and can you provide any analysis of its convergence properties compared to standard FQI?
*   To create a more rigorous comparison, would it be possible to re-run the IBVS baselines using an estimated depth, or a method that does not require depth, such as that of Jagersand et al. (1997)?
*   The paper mentions that the `conv5_3` features perform poorly, hypothesizing that they are too spatially invariant. This is an interesting finding. Have you tried using features from more modern architectures (e.g., ResNet), which may have different invariance properties?
*   How sensitive is the performance of the learned dynamics model to the quality of the data-collection policy used to generate the 100 trajectories for its training? Was this "hand-coded policy" carefully designed?
