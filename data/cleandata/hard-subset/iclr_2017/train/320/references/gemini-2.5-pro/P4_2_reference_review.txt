### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel method for learning visual servoing controllers that is both robust and highly sample-efficient. The core problem is to control a robot's camera to follow a specific target object, adapting quickly to new targets with minimal data. The authors' approach cleverly combines three key components: (1) pre-trained convolutional neural network features (from VGG) to provide a robust visual representation that is invariant to moderate changes in lighting, viewpoint, and occlusion; (2) a learned, per-channel bilinear dynamics model that predicts how these features will change in response to robot actions; and (3) a sample-efficient reinforcement learning algorithm based on fitted Q-iteration (FQI) to learn a task-specific distance metric.

The main contribution is the formulation of the learning problem. Instead of learning a complex policy or Q-function from scratch, the method employs a simple, analytic servoing policy that minimizes the one-step predicted error to a target feature map. The FQI algorithm is then used not to learn the policy directly, but to learn the channel-wise weights of the feature error metric. The Q-function approximator is specifically designed to be linear in these weights, which mirrors the structure of the policy's objective function. This makes the FQI updates computationally efficient and enables learning from a very small number of training trajectories. The effectiveness of the method is demonstrated on a challenging simulated quadcopter-car-following task, showing significant improvements in performance and a reduction in sample requirements of over two orders of magnitude compared to standard deep reinforcement learning baselines.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Sound Formulation:** The paper presents a novel synthesis of pre-trained features, learned dynamics, and reinforcement learning. The central technical contribution—designing a Q-function approximator whose greedy minimization corresponds exactly to the proposed servoing policy, thereby allowing FQI to learn the policy's metric weights—is both technically sound and insightful. This formulation elegantly sidesteps the high sample complexity typically associated with learning visuomotor control policies.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The claims of sample efficiency and performance are substantiated by a thorough experimental evaluation. The proposed method is shown to substantially outperform a comprehensive set of baselines, including policies based on raw pixels and hand-designed features, standard model-free deep RL (TRPO), and classical visual servoing methods. The ability to learn an effective policy from only 20 trajectories is a significant result that highlights the practical potential of the approach.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's strength lies in its well-motivated and technically sound approach. Traditional visual servoing methods often lack robustness to visual distractors, while modern deep RL methods are notoriously sample-inefficient. This work carves out a compelling middle ground.

The use of pre-trained VGG features is a well-justified choice for obtaining a rich, semi-invariant representation without requiring task-specific feature learning. The decision to model dynamics in this feature space with a relatively simple bilinear model is pragmatic, avoiding the data requirements of more complex video prediction models while being sufficient for one-step-ahead prediction for control.

The most significant contribution is the RL formulation in Section 5. By designing the Q-function `Q_theta(s, u)` to be a linear combination of the feature-wise squared errors, the authors ensure that `argmin_u Q_theta(s, u)` is equivalent to their servoing policy `pi(s)`. This allows the use of FQI, a data-efficient off-policy algorithm, to directly learn the parameters `theta` (the feature weights) that optimize for long-term reward. This is a much more constrained and lower-dimensional learning problem than learning a general-purpose Q-network or policy network, which explains the dramatic gains in sample efficiency.

The experimental results are convincing. Figure 6 clearly illustrates the primary claim: the proposed FQI-based method achieves a low cost using only 2 sampling iterations (20 trajectories), whereas TRPO requires 50 iterations (2000 trajectories) and still fails to reach the same performance level. The comparison against classical IBVS methods, including one augmented with a state-of-the-art visual tracker (C-COT), further demonstrates the benefits of the learned dynamics and feature weighting, as the proposed method performs better without needing ground-truth depth information or an external tracker. The ablation study on different feature layers in Figure 5 provides useful insight into which level of semantic abstraction is most suitable for this type of control task.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Clarity on FQI updates:** The motivation for the two-step update procedure in Section 5.3 (solving for scale `alpha` and bias `b` in Eq. 5 before solving for `theta` in Eq. 6) could be explained more intuitively. While technically described, a brief sentence on *why* this decomposition is helpful (e.g., "This stabilizes training by first finding the optimal scale and offset for the current weights before updating the weights themselves...") would improve readability.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of its limitations. For instance, the bilinear dynamics model might not be sufficient for systems with highly non-linear or discontinuous dynamics. The reliance on ImageNet pre-trained features might be a limitation if the target objects are from a domain with very different visual statistics (e.g., microscopic organisms, textureless industrial parts). A brief discussion on the potential sim-to-real gap would also be welcome.
*   **Cost Function:** The cost function described in Appendix B relies on the ground-truth 3D position of the target car to provide a reward signal during RL training. While this is standard practice in simulated RL, it would be beneficial to explicitly state in the main text that this ground-truth information is *only* used for the reward signal and is not an input to the policy, which operates on visual data alone.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   The performance of the dynamics model seems crucial. This model was trained on data collected from a hand-coded policy. How sensitive is the final performance of the servoing agent to the quality and diversity of the data used for training the dynamics model?
*   The cost function in Appendix B uses ground-truth state information (the car's 3D position) for calculating the cost/reward. Could the proposed FQI framework be used with a reward function defined purely in the visual feature space, for instance, based on the distance between current and target feature maps, to remove the dependency on ground-truth state for training?
*   The results in Figure 5 show a clear trend where performance improves with deeper VGG layers up to `conv4_3`, but then degrades for `conv5_3`. The paper hypothesizes this is due to excessive spatial invariance. Could you elaborate on this? Is it possible that `conv5_3` features are too abstract and lose fine-grained spatial information necessary for precise control, while `conv4_3` represents a "sweet spot" of semantic understanding and spatial localization?
