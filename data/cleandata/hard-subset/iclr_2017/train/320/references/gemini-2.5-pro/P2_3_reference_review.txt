### Review

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel and highly sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing controllers. The core problem it addresses is how to quickly adapt a robot to follow a new target object with minimal data, while being robust to visual distractors, occlusions, and changes in viewpoint. The authors propose a compelling three-part solution:
1.  **Leveraging Pre-trained Features:** Instead of learning visual representations from scratch, the method utilizes features extracted from a VGG network pre-trained on ImageNet. To make these features suitable for servoing, spatial information is preserved by replacing max-pooling layers with dilated convolutions.
2.  **Learning a Simple Dynamics Model:** A simple, computationally efficient bilinear model is learned to predict the one-step evolution of these visual features given a control action. This model is linear with respect to the controls, which enables an efficient, analytical solution for the servoing policy.
3.  **Sample-Efficient Policy Learning:** The key insight is to formulate the servoing objective as a weighted Euclidean distance in the feature space. The paper then frames the problem of learning these feature weights as a reinforcement learning task. By designing a Q-function approximator that is linear in these weights, the authors can employ a highly sample-efficient Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) algorithm to find the optimal weights that maximize long-term reward.

The primary claims are that this combination allows for learning an effective servoing policy from an extremely small amount of data (just 20 trajectories), substantially outperforming classical visual servoing methods and demonstrating a sample efficiency improvement of over two orders of magnitude compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms like TRPO.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that I recommend for acceptance. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Clever and Effective Problem Decomposition:** The paper cleverly breaks down the complex problem of visual servoing into three manageable, well-motivated sub-problems: feature extraction, dynamics modeling, and policy optimization. The proposed solution for each part is well-chosen and integrates into a cohesive and effective whole. The formulation of the weighted servoing objective as a linear Q-function, enabling the use of FQI, is a particularly elegant technical contribution that directly leads to the claimed sample efficiency.
2.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and rigorously executed. The authors provide a comprehensive comparison against a strong set of baselines, including classical robotics methods (IBVS with ORB and state-of-the-art trackers), standard model-free deep RL (end-to-end TRPO), and important ablations of their own method (different features, alternative weight optimization schemes). The results strongly support their central claim of superior sample efficiency and performance, providing clear evidence for the method's effectiveness.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper's approach successfully navigates the trade-offs between classical methods and end-to-end deep learning. Classical visual servoing relies on hand-engineered features that can be brittle, while end-to-end deep RL is notoriously data-hungry, making it impractical for tasks requiring quick adaptation to new targets. This paper finds a "sweet spot" by combining the strengths of multiple paradigms: the representational power of deep features, the sample efficiency of model-based control, and the optimality of reinforcement learning.

The choice of a bilinear dynamics model is a pragmatic and well-justified one. While more complex video prediction models exist, they are data-hungry themselves. The bilinear model is simple enough to be learned from a small dataset, and its linearity in the control inputs is the key property that makes the resulting servoing policy and Q-function optimization tractable and efficient.

The experimental results are the paper's strongest asset. Figure 6 is particularly compelling. It shows that the proposed FQI-based method achieves a low cost using only 2 sampling iterations (20 trajectories), whereas TRPO, a state-of-the-art model-free algorithm, requires 50 iterations (500 trajectories, assuming 10 trajectories per batch as with FQI, though the paper states 4000 samples/iteration for TRPO) and still fails to reach the same performance level. This is a dramatic and meaningful improvement in sample efficiency. The comparison with classical IBVS is also insightful; the proposed method outperforms even a tracker-based approach that is given ground truth depth information, highlighting the benefit of learning task-specific feature dynamics. The evaluation on both seen and novel cars demonstrates that the learned policy generalizes well.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, the following points could help improve it further. These are suggestions and not critical for my decision.

*   **Real-World Applicability:** The work is evaluated exclusively in simulation. While the simulation is complex, a discussion on the potential challenges of transferring this method to a real robotic platform would strengthen the paper. For instance, how robust would the learned bilinear dynamics be to unmodeled physical effects, camera noise, or latency in a real system?
*   **Clarity on "Learning the Distance Metric":** The paper states that it learns the "distance metric." This is slightly imprecise. The method uses a fixed L2 distance but learns the *weights* for each feature channel and scale. Rephrasing this to "learning feature weights for a servoing cost function" might be more accurate and avoid confusion with work on metric learning.
*   **Intuition on the FQI Update:** The two-step update in FQI (solving for `α` and `b` in Eq. 5 before updating `θ` in Eq. 6) is an interesting heuristic. A sentence or two providing more intuition on *why* this helps stabilize or accelerate the learning process would be beneficial for the reader. For example, it appears to separate the optimization of the overall scale and bias of the Q-function from the optimization of the relative feature weights.
*   **Computational Cost:** The paper rightly emphasizes sample efficiency. A brief comparison of the wall-clock *training time* for the FQI method (2 sampling iterations) versus TRPO (50+ iterations) would provide a more complete picture of the method's overall efficiency.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

*   How sensitive is the performance of the final policy to the quality and quantity of the data used to train the bilinear dynamics model? Was the 100-trajectory dataset a minimum requirement, or could a good dynamics model be learned with even less data?
*   The bilinear dynamics model seems well-suited for the relatively smooth motion of a quadcopter following a car. How do you expect this model to perform in scenarios with more complex, non-linear dynamics, such as avoiding a sudden obstacle or interacting with a deformable object?
*   In Section 6.2, it's noted that the deepest VGG features (conv5_3) perform worse, hypothesized to be due to excessive spatial invariance. Did you experiment with intermediate levels of feature modification (e.g., removing only the last max-pooling layer) to find an optimal trade-off between semantic abstraction and spatial precision?
