### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a method for learning visual servoing control policies in a highly sample-efficient manner. The key idea is to combine the strengths of pre-trained deep visual features, a learned local dynamics model, and a tailored reinforcement learning algorithm. The proposed approach first extracts multi-scale feature maps from a VGG network pre-trained on ImageNet, with modifications to preserve spatial information. It then learns a simple, per-channel bilinear model to predict the one-step evolution of these features given a control action. Finally, instead of learning a complex policy end-to-end, the method uses a simple servoing policy that minimizes a weighted Euclidean distance between the predicted next-state features and the target features. The crucial contribution is a novel fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm that learns the weights for each feature channel and scale. The Q-function is specifically formulated as a linear combination of these feature error terms, which allows the greedy servoing policy to be recovered directly by minimizing the Q-function and makes the FQI updates computationally efficient. The method is evaluated on a challenging simulated car-following task, where it demonstrates the ability to learn an effective policy from only 20 trajectories. It substantially outperforms classical visual servoing methods and standard deep reinforcement learning algorithms, showing an improvement in sample efficiency of over two orders of magnitude compared to the latter.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper proposes a novel and technically sound method that effectively combines pre-trained representations, model-learning, and reinforcement learning. The formulation of the Q-function to directly represent the cost of the servoing policy is an elegant approach that leads to a highly sample-efficient weight-learning algorithm.
2.  The experimental results are comprehensive and provide strong evidence for the central claims of the paper. The method's ability to learn a robust servoing policy from only 20 trajectories, significantly outperforming data-hungry deep RL baselines like TRPO, represents a substantial contribution to learning-based robot control.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper is well-structured, clear, and technically sound. The problem of sample-efficient visual servoing is well-motivated by highlighting the limitations of both classical, feature-engineered approaches and modern, end-to-end deep RL methods.

**Technical Soundness and Novelty:** The core novelty lies in the specific formulation of the FQI algorithm. By designing the Q-function approximator to be a linear function of the feature-channel error terms (the `phi` features in Section 5.2), the authors create a direct link between the learned value function and the parameters of the simple, model-based servoing policy. This allows the complex problem of policy learning to be reduced to learning a set of linear weights (`theta`). This is a significant departure from standard deep RL, which would typically use a generic neural network to represent the policy or Q-function. The resulting FQI algorithm is computationally efficient, as the updates in Equations (5) and (6) are quadratic optimization problems. This tailored approach is a well-founded and clever solution to the problem.

**Clarity:** The paper is written with exceptional clarity. The motivation, problem statement, and individual components of the method (visual features, dynamics model, RL-based weighting) are explained logically and sequentially. The connection between the servoing objective in Equation (3) and the Q-function in Section 5.2 is particularly well-articulated, making the core idea easy to grasp. Figures and algorithms effectively supplement the text.

**Empirical Rigor and Significance:** The experiments are thorough and convincingly support the paper's claims.
*   **Sample Efficiency:** The most significant result is the comparison with TRPO (Figure 6). The proposed method achieves superior performance using only 2 sampling iterations (20 trajectories), while TRPO shows minimal improvement and is far from converging. Even after 50 iterations (2000 trajectories), TRPO does not match the performance of the proposed method. The comparison with an end-to-end CNN policy trained with TRPO, which requires 500 iterations, further solidifies the claim of a >100x improvement in sample efficiency.
*   **Performance:** The method outperforms multiple strong baselines, including classical Image-Based Visual Servoing (IBVS) using hand-designed features (ORB) and a state-of-the-art visual tracker (C-COT). This demonstrates the value of using learned deep features and learning the feature dynamics.
*   **Ablation and Analysis:** The comparison of different VGG feature layers (Figure 5) provides useful insight, showing that mid-level features (conv4_3) offer the best trade-off between semantic information and spatial precision for this task. The testing on both seen and novel cars demonstrates that the learned models generalize well. The public release of code and the simulation environment is a commendable step towards reproducibility.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   The motivation for modifying the VGG network (removing max-pooling and using dilated convolutions) is sound, but could be stated more explicitly in Section 4.1. A sentence explaining that servoing requires precise spatial localization, which is hindered by the spatial invariance introduced by max-pooling, would immediately clarify the rationale for this design choice.
*   In Section 5.3, the paper introduces a two-step update within each FQI iteration (Eq. 5 and Eq. 6). The motivation for first solving for a scalar `alpha` and bias `b` before updating the full weight vector `theta` could be elaborated upon. While the text notes a similarity to policy evaluation, explaining that this step helps stabilize and accelerate the learning of the value function's scale could make the algorithm's design more transparent.
*   The cost function defined in Appendix B is critical for the RL training but is not mentioned in the main paper. While details can remain in the appendix, a brief, one-sentence conceptual description of the cost function (e.g., "The cost penalizes deviation from a target position and orientation in the camera frame...") in Section 5 would be helpful for the reader.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   The bilinear dynamics model is trained on a dataset collected with a hand-coded policy. How sensitive is the final performance to the data distribution used for training this dynamics model? For example, if the data was collected via a purely random policy, would the learned dynamics be sufficient for the FQI stage to succeed?
*   The method follows a two-stage approach: first, the dynamics model is trained to completion, and then its parameters are frozen while the RL algorithm learns the feature weights. Have you experimented with or considered an alternating optimization scheme where the dynamics model and the policy weights are fine-tuned jointly? What might be the potential benefits or drawbacks of such an approach?
*   The `min_u Q(s_t+1, u)` operation is performed inside the FQI updates. Given that the dynamics `f(y,u)` are linear in `u`, the Q-function is quadratic in `u`, meaning this minimization has an analytical solution. Could you confirm this is the implementation approach and comment on its computational cost within the overall FQI loop?
*   What do you foresee as the primary challenges in deploying this system on a physical robot? In particular, how might the sim-to-real gap affect the VGG features, and would the simple bilinear model be sufficient to capture the dynamics of a real-world system with more complex noise and physical effects?
