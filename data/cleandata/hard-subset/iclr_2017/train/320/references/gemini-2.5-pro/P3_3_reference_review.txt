### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel and sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing controllers, demonstrated on a simulated quadcopter car-following task. The core contribution is an elegant combination of three key ideas: 1) leveraging powerful, pre-trained visual features from a VGG network to avoid learning visual representations from scratch; 2) learning a simple, task-agnostic predictive dynamics model (a multiscale bilinear model) in this feature space to enable one-step lookahead; and 3) using a highly sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn a task-specific weighting over the features. This weighting effectively learns a distance metric that focuses on the target object and ignores distractors. The resulting policy minimizes the weighted, predicted feature error. The authors demonstrate that this approach can learn a robust servoing policy using only 20 trajectories, significantly outperforming classical image-based visual servoing (IBVS) methods and standard model-free deep reinforcement learning (RL) which requires orders of magnitude more data.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Novel and Effective Problem Formulation:** The paper provides a compelling solution to a significant problem: making visual servoing both robust and data-efficient. Instead of falling into the extremes of purely classical methods (brittle, require manual engineering) or end-to-end deep RL (data-hungry), it carves out a highly effective middle ground. The idea of using RL not to learn the entire policy, but to learn the *objective function* for a simpler, model-based controller is powerful and well-executed.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and well-designed. The proposed method is compared against a comprehensive set of strong and relevant baselines, including classical IBVS with hand-crafted features, IBVS with a state-of-the-art tracker (C-COT), and model-free RL (TRPO). The results, particularly the dramatic improvement in sample efficiency (outperforming TRPO with >100x less data), strongly support the paper's central claims. The provision of open-source code and a simulation environment adds significant value and supports reproducibility.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's strength lies in its intelligent decomposition of the visual servoing problem. The authors correctly identify that pre-trained CNN features (VGG) already capture rich semantic and spatial information, eliminating the need for a massive, task-specific dataset for representation learning. The choice of a bilinear model for dynamics is also well-justified; it is simple enough to be learned from a small dataset (100 trajectories) yet expressive enough to capture the local dynamics of feature maps under camera motion, as demonstrated by prior work.

The most critical component is the use of FQI to learn feature weights. Standard servoing would weight all feature channels equally, making it susceptible to distractors. By formulating the Q-function as a linear combination of the per-channel feature errors (Section 5.2), the authors create a direct link between the long-term value (Q-function) and the one-step servoing objective. This allows FQI to efficiently find weights `w` that prioritize features corresponding to the target object, effectively teaching the servo *what* to look at. This is a much lower-dimensional and better-structured learning problem than learning a complex, non-linear policy from pixels, which explains the remarkable sample efficiency.

The experimental results in Figure 6 are particularly persuasive. The fact that the proposed method, after only 2 sampling iterations (20 trajectories), substantially outperforms TRPO trained for 50 iterations (200,000 samples) is a standout result. Furthermore, the comparison with the C-COT tracker is important; while the tracker-based method performs well, the authors' method is better and significantly faster at runtime, which is crucial for real-time robotic control. The ablation study over different VGG layers provides useful insights, corroborating the hypothesis that intermediate-level features (like conv4_3) offer the best trade-off between semantic abstraction and spatial precision for this task.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, the following points could be clarified or expanded upon to further improve it:

*   **Real-World Applicability:** The work is validated entirely in simulation. While the simulation is complex, a discussion of the potential challenges in transferring this method to a real robotic platform would be valuable. For instance, the bilinear dynamics model might be too simple to capture unmodeled physical effects, camera noise, or system latency.
*   **Clarity on the FQI Update:** The two-step update within the FQI loop (Eq. 5 and 6) is interesting. The paper states this is done to "speed up the optimization," but the intuition could be clearer. Is this primarily a stability trick, or does it provably improve convergence? A more detailed explanation of why this specific formulation was chosen over a more standard FQI update would be helpful.
*   **Dependence on Dynamics Data:** The dynamics model is trained on data collected from a "hand-coded policy with some Gaussian noise." The quality of this initial data seems critical for the performance of the entire system. It would be useful to understand how sensitive the final servoing performance is to the quality and diversity of this initial dataset.
*   **Privileged Information for Reward:** The cost function for RL (Appendix B) uses ground-truth 3D positions of the car. While the final policy is purely vision-based, the learning signal itself relies on privileged information. This is standard practice in simulation but is an important detail that should be mentioned in the main body, as it has implications for applying the learning framework in the real world where such ground truth is unavailable.

### Questions for Authors

1.  Your analysis suggests that the `conv5_3` features perform worse because they might be "too spatially invariant." Have you considered analyzing the learned weights `w` for different layers? One might expect that for deeper, more invariant features, the FQI would learn to weight only a few, very specific channels, whereas for earlier layers, the weights might be more distributed. Could this provide further evidence for your hypothesis?
2.  Could you elaborate on the practical challenges you foresee in deploying this system on a physical quadcopter? In particular, how robust do you expect the learned bilinear dynamics model to be to sim-to-real gaps in appearance and dynamics?
3.  How crucial is the specific "hand-coded" policy used for collecting the dynamics training data? For instance, if a purely random exploration policy was used instead, how much degradation in performance would you expect for the learned dynamics model and the final servoing policy?
