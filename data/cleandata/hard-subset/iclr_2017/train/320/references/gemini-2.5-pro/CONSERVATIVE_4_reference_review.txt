### Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel method for visual servoing that combines several learning-based components. The core idea is to leverage features from a pre-trained VGG network, learn a predictive model of these features' dynamics under robot actions, and then use reinforcement learning to find an optimal weighting of these features for a servoing controller. The feature dynamics are modeled using a bilinear system, which allows for an efficient, analytic solution for the control policy. To learn the feature weights, the authors propose a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm that directly optimizes the parameters of a Q-function structured to match the servoing objective. The method is evaluated on a synthetic car-following task using a simulated quadcopter, where it demonstrates significant sample efficiency improvements over a standard model-free deep RL algorithm (TRPO) and outperforms classical visual servoing methods based on hand-crafted keypoints.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Insufficient Comparison to Established Baselines:** The paper's primary claim of high sample efficiency is not rigorously substantiated due to a weak choice of baselines. The main deep RL comparison is with TRPO, a general-purpose model-free algorithm known for its high sample complexity in vision-based tasks. The comparison to classical Image-Based Visual Servoing (IBVS) is confounded by providing the baseline with ground-truth depth information, an unrealistic advantage that is not available to the proposed method.
2.  **Unjustified Methodological Complexity:** The proposed system is a complex pipeline involving pre-trained features, a specific type of learned dynamics model (bilinear), and a custom FQI algorithm. The necessity and benefit of each component are not clearly isolated or justified against simpler, more established alternatives. The evaluation is restricted to a single simulated environment, which is insufficient to validate the general applicability of such a complex approach.

### Supporting Arguments

**1. Weakness of Baselines:**

The central claim is that the proposed method is highly sample-efficient, learning an effective policy from only 20 trajectories. This claim is primarily supported by comparison to TRPO, which requires over 100 times more data to achieve inferior performance. However, this comparison is not compelling. It is widely recognized that model-free methods like TRPO are inefficient when learning from high-dimensional image inputs. A much stronger and more relevant baseline would be a modern model-based RL algorithm that also learns a dynamics model from images (e.g., PlaNet, Dreamer). These methods are designed for sample efficiency in visual control tasks and would provide a much more rigorous benchmark.

Furthermore, the paper uses 100 trajectories of a "hand-coded policy" to train the visual dynamics model. This is a significant amount of expert data. A crucial missing baseline is a simple behavioral cloning (imitation learning) approach trained on this same dataset. If behavioral cloning can achieve comparable performance, it would call into question the necessity of the complex FQI-based reinforcement learning component.

The comparison with classical IBVS methods is also problematic. The IBVS baselines are provided with ground-truth depth for the interaction matrix. This is a significant piece of information that is typically unavailable or noisy in real-world scenarios. In contrast, the proposed method learns its dynamics from images alone. This asymmetry makes the comparison difficult to interpret. While the proposed method outperforms the ORB-based approach, it is only marginally better than the tracker-based IBVS, which is given this considerable advantage. A fairer comparison would require the IBVS method to estimate depth or operate without it.

**2. Complexity and Limited Validation:**

The proposed method combines three distinct ideas: using pre-trained VGG features, modeling their dynamics with a bilinear model, and weighting them with a custom FQI procedure.

*   **Bilinear Dynamics:** The motivation for using a bilinear model is based on prior work for *pixel* dynamics. Its suitability for the abstract feature maps of a deep CNN is asserted but not deeply investigated. Is this simple linear approximation sufficient for the complex, non-linear dynamics of semantic features? How does it compare against other simple predictive models, such as a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) or a simple convolutional network? Without such an ablation, it's hard to judge if the bilinear model is a critical and well-justified choice.
*   **Custom FQI Algorithm:** The proposed FQI algorithm includes a two-step update process (Eq. 5 and 6) to first solve for a scaling factor and bias before updating the primary weights. The motivation for this specific procedure is not well-explained, and its theoretical properties (e.g., convergence) are not discussed. It is unclear how this method compares to a more standard FQI implementation.
*   **Single Environment Evaluation:** All experiments are conducted in a single, albeit visually rich, simulation. Claims of robustness to occlusions, viewpoint changes, and generalization to novel targets would be substantially stronger if demonstrated across multiple different tasks or environments, or ideally, on a physical robot. As it stands, the method's effectiveness has only been shown for one specific task (car following) in one specific simulator.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

*   **Strengthen Baselines:** I strongly recommend including a behavioral cloning baseline using the 100 trajectories available for dynamics model training. Additionally, comparing against a state-of-the-art model-based RL algorithm would provide a much more convincing measure of sample efficiency.
*   **Ablation Studies:** To justify the chosen methodology, it would be beneficial to see ablations on the dynamics model (e.g., bilinear vs. MLP predictor) and the FQI algorithm (proposed vs. standard FQI). This would help disentangle the contributions of each component.
*   **Fairer Classical Comparison:** The IBVS comparisons should be re-evaluated without providing ground truth depth to the baseline, for example by using a fixed-depth assumption or a simple depth estimator.
*   **Clarity on Data Usage:** The paper should be more transparent about the total data budget. The RL algorithm uses 20 trajectories, but the system first requires 100 trajectories to learn the dynamics. The total data requirement should be stated clearly when making claims about sample efficiency.

### Questions for Authors

1.  Could you provide the performance of a simple behavioral cloning policy trained on the 100 trajectories used to learn the dynamics model? This seems like a critical baseline to assess the value added by the reinforcement learning stage.
2.  What is the theoretical or empirical justification for the two-step update in your FQI algorithm (Algorithm 1, lines 5-7)? How does its performance and stability compare to a standard FQI implementation that directly minimizes the Bellman error for `θ` and `b` in one step?
3.  Why was TRPO chosen as the deep RL baseline over more recent and sample-efficient model-based RL methods that are designed for visual control problems?
4.  How does the performance of the learned bilinear dynamics model degrade when the robot executes trajectories that are statistically different from the 100 trajectories used for its training? Is the model robust to out-of-distribution actions and states?
