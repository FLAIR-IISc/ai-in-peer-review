### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a method for learning visual servoing control policies with high sample efficiency. The core idea is to combine visual features from a pre-trained convolutional neural network (VGG) with a learned, predictive model of the feature dynamics. The dynamics are represented by a simple bilinear model, which predicts the next feature map given the current one and the robot's action. To handle distractors and focus on the target, the authors propose learning a set of weights over the feature channels and scales. These weights are learned using a novel, customized Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) algorithm that leverages the specific structure of the control policy. The policy itself is a simple servo that minimizes the weighted one-step prediction error. The authors demonstrate their approach on a simulated quadcopter car-following task, showing significant improvements in performance and sample efficiency compared to classical visual servoing methods and standard model-free deep reinforcement learning.

### **Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting integration of several learning components, I am recommending rejection for two primary reasons:

1.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The claims of robustness and effectiveness are supported only by experiments in a single simulated environment. Visual servoing is an established field in robotics where validation on physical hardware is the standard for demonstrating practical utility. The absence of any real-world experiments makes it difficult to assess how the proposed method would cope with real-world complexities not fully captured in simulation.
2.  **Methodological Rigor:** The novel FQI algorithm, a key component of the contribution, is presented without sufficient theoretical justification. Its design appears heuristic, and its convergence properties and stability are not analyzed, which is a significant omission for a new learning algorithm.

### **Supporting Arguments**

**1. Lack of Real-World Experiments:**
The primary weakness of this work is its exclusive reliance on simulation. While the CitySim3D environment appears to be a reasonably complex benchmark, it cannot substitute for validation on a physical robot. Real-world visual servoing is challenged by issues such as imperfect camera calibration, motion blur, variable and harsh lighting conditions, and unpredictable delays in the control loop. The paper claims the method is robust to occlusions and appearance changes, but these claims are not fully convincing without being tested under real-world conditions. An established method, particularly in a field as applied as robotics, requires this level of validation to be considered a significant contribution. Without it, the work remains an interesting proof-of-concept in a controlled setting, but its applicability remains unproven.

**2. Justification for the Proposed FQI Algorithm:**
The proposed FQI algorithm (Algorithm 1) modifies the standard FQI procedure by introducing an intermediate optimization step (Eq. 5) to solve for a scaling factor `α` and a bias `b` before the main weight update (Eq. 6). The motivation given is to "speed up the optimization." However, this is not supported by any theoretical analysis. Standard FQI has known, if sometimes restrictive, convergence guarantees. It is unclear if the proposed modifications preserve these guarantees. This two-step update appears to be an empirically-driven heuristic. For a core part of the paper's contribution to be a new algorithm, a more rigorous treatment is expected, including a discussion of its stability and a clearer justification for why this specific formulation is superior to other FQI variants from a learning-theoretic perspective.

**3. Strength of Baselines:**
The paper compares against several baselines, but the framing could be stronger.
*   The classical Image-Based Visual Servoing (IBVS) method using ORB features performs very poorly. This may reflect the unsuitability of ORB for this specific task rather than a fundamental weakness of all classical methods.
*   The tracker-based (C-COT) baseline is much more competitive. While the proposed method outperforms it, the gap is not dramatic. Crucially, the IBVS methods were provided with ground truth depth for the interaction matrix. This is a significant advantage that would not be available in many real-world scenarios. It is unclear how a full system, including depth estimation, would compare.
*   The comparison with TRPO is compelling in terms of sample efficiency. However, the proposed method incorporates a great deal of structural knowledge (pre-trained features, a linearizable dynamics model, a structured Q-function), whereas TRPO is a general-purpose model-free method. The massive gain in sample efficiency is therefore expected and highlights the benefit of incorporating priors, but the performance comparison is between two very different classes of algorithm.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   The paper is well-written and structured. The motivation for combining pre-trained features with a simple, learned dynamics model is sound and addresses a clear gap between classical methods and data-hungry deep RL.
*   The analysis of which VGG layers provide the most useful features for this task is a valuable insight. The conclusion that extremely deep, invariant features (conv5_3) are less suitable than mid-level features for a spatial task like servoing is important for the community.
*   The choice of a bilinear model for the dynamics is pragmatic. Could you comment on its limitations? How would you expect it to perform on tasks with highly non-linear dynamics, such as tracking an agile or articulated object?
*   The dynamics model was trained using data from a hand-coded policy. How sensitive is the final performance to the quality of this data-gathering policy? For the method to be truly general, it would be ideal if the dynamics model could be learned from less structured, or even random, interaction data.
*   Could you clarify why the parameters `α` and `b` in Eq. 5 are solved for jointly for both the current and target Q-networks? This is an unusual formulation, and a more detailed explanation of the intuition behind it would be beneficial.
