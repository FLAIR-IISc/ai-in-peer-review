### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel and sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing policies. The core contribution is a well-designed framework that combines three key ideas: (1) leveraging powerful, general-purpose visual features from a pre-trained VGG network to avoid learning perception from scratch; (2) learning a simple, task-agnostic bilinear model to predict the short-term dynamics of these features in response to robot actions; and (3) using a highly customized and sample-efficient Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn task-specific weights over the features. This FQI method learns which features are most relevant for the task, effectively allowing the policy to focus on the target object and ignore distractors. The policy itself is a simple, greedy servoing mechanism that minimizes the weighted one-step predicted error to a target feature representation. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a complex, visually rich simulated car-following task, showing that their method can be trained with only 20 trajectories. It substantially outperforms classical visual servoing baselines and achieves over two orders of magnitude greater sample efficiency than standard deep reinforcement learning approaches like TRPO.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

1.  **Novel and Effective Method:** The paper proposes a novel and elegant synthesis of ideas from deep learning, dynamics modeling, and reinforcement learning to tackle the important problem of sample-efficient visual servoing. The specific design of the Q-function to match the structure of the servoing policy, enabling a linear parameterization and a highly efficient FQI algorithm, is a key technical contribution that directly leads to the method's impressive sample efficiency.
2.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive and well-executed. The method is compared against a strong and diverse set of baselines, including classical image-based visual servoing (IBVS), state-of-the-art visual trackers, and modern end-to-end deep RL. The results clearly demonstrate substantial improvements in both final performance and sample efficiency. The testing on both seen and novel cars also provides evidence of the method's ability to generalize.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this work lies in its thoughtful problem decomposition. Instead of attempting to solve the visual servoing problem with a single, monolithic end-to-end network, the authors break it down into perception, dynamics, and control, and use the most appropriate tool for each subproblem.

*   **Perception:** Using pre-trained VGG features is a well-motivated choice that provides a strong perceptual foundation without requiring vast amounts of task-specific data. The ablation study over different VGG layers (Figure 5) provides useful insights into which level of abstraction is most suitable for this task.
*   **Dynamics:** The choice of a bilinear model for feature dynamics is simple, efficient, and well-justified by prior work. Learning this model as a separate, supervised problem on a small dataset is a crucial element for sample efficiency.
*   **Control/Task-Specification:** The most significant contribution is the RL component. The insight to formulate the Q-function as a weighted sum of feature-space errors allows the problem of learning a policy to be reframed as learning a task-specific distance metric. The proposed FQI algorithm with its two-step update (first solving for scale and bias, then for the weights) is a clever adaptation that fits the problem structure perfectly and is demonstrably more efficient than general-purpose policy optimization methods like TRPO.

The experimental results strongly support the paper's claims. The comparison in Figure 6 is particularly compelling. The proposed FQI-based method achieves excellent performance with only 20 trajectories, whereas TRPO struggles with the same amount of data and still underperforms after using more than 100 times the data. Furthermore, the comparison to classical IBVS methods is very strong. The authors give these baselines a significant advantage by providing them with ground-truth depth information, yet their learning-based method is still competitive or superior, while also being computationally faster than the tracker-based approach. The provision of code and the simulation environment is a commendable step towards reproducibility.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Real-World Applicability:** The work is evaluated exclusively in simulation. While the simulation is visually complex, a discussion of the potential challenges in transferring this method to a real robotic platform would be valuable. For instance, how sensitive is the learned bilinear dynamics model to the sim-to-real gap?
*   **Analysis of Learned Weights:** The method learns weights `w` for each feature channel. Visualizing these weights or analyzing which channels receive high weights could provide fascinating insights into what the model learns to focus on. This could also help explain why certain feature layers (e.g., `conv4_3`) perform better than others and why `conv5_3` fails (e.g., by confirming the spatial invariance hypothesis).
*   **Dependence on Dynamics Data:** The dynamics model is trained on data from a hand-coded policy. The paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the overall system to the quality and coverage of this initial dataset. Does a "better" data collection policy lead to a better dynamics model and, consequently, a better final servoing policy?
*   **Cost Function:** The cost function for RL is based on ground-truth Cartesian coordinates of the target (Appendix B). While this is standard practice, it creates a slight disconnect with the policy's objective, which is to minimize weighted feature error. It would be interesting to discuss why this indirect optimization works so well and whether a cost function defined purely in feature space could also be effective.

### **Questions for Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential challenges of deploying this method on a real robotic platform? Specifically, do you foresee issues with the sim-to-real transfer of the pre-trained features or the learned dynamics model?
2.  The RL cost function uses ground-truth pose information. How critical is this? Have you experimented with a cost defined purely in feature space, such as `c_t = ||y* - y_t||_w`, using the currently learned weights `w`?
3.  How does the performance of the learned dynamics model and the final servoing policy depend on the initial data collection policy? For example, if the data were collected from a purely random policy instead of a hand-coded one, would the system still work effectively?
4.  Could you provide some analysis or visualization of the learned feature weights `w`? This could offer valuable intuition about which visual cues the Q-function learns to prioritize for the car-following task.
