### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents an elegant and effective approach to visual servoing that combines the strengths of pre-trained deep features, learned dynamics models, and sample-efficient reinforcement learning. The core problem it addresses is how to learn a robust visual servoing policy for a new target object with very little task-specific data. The key contributions are threefold:

1.  **Leveraging Pre-trained Features:** The method utilizes features from a VGG network pre-trained on ImageNet, modified with dilated convolutions to preserve spatial resolution. This provides a rich, semantic feature space without requiring vast amounts of data for the servoing task itself.
2.  **Learning Feature Dynamics:** Instead of assuming a known interaction matrix or learning a complex end-to-end policy, the authors learn a simple, multiscale bilinear model that predicts the one-step dynamics of the visual features in response to robot actions. This model is simple enough to be learned from a small dataset of random motions.
3.  **Sample-Efficient Policy Learning:** The central insight is to frame the policy as minimizing a weighted distance between predicted and target features. The paper proposes a novel Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn the per-channel and per-scale feature weights. By designing a Q-function approximator that is linear in these weights, the Bellman error minimization becomes a simple and efficient least-squares problem, leading to remarkable sample efficiency.

Empirically, the paper demonstrates on a challenging simulated car-following task that their method can learn an effective policy from just 20 training trajectories. This significantly outperforms classical visual servoing methods (based on ORB features or trackers) and is over two orders of magnitude more sample-efficient than standard deep RL algorithms like TRPO.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Exceptional Sample Efficiency:** The paper compellingly addresses one of the most significant barriers to applying reinforcement learning in robotics: the need for massive amounts of data. Learning a robust policy from only 20 trajectories is a standout result that makes the method practical for real-world applications where data collection is expensive and time-consuming.
2.  **Novel and Sound Methodological Combination:** The work is not just an application of existing techniques but a thoughtful integration of them. The specific formulation of the Q-function to match the structure of the servoing policy is the core technical novelty. This allows the power of RL (optimizing for long-term reward) to be harnessed via a very efficient, low-variance learning signal (solving a linear system), which is a significant departure from high-variance gradient-based policy optimization methods.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's claims are strongly supported by a well-designed set of experiments.

First, the core methodology is well-motivated. The choice of pre-trained VGG features is a standard and effective way to bootstrap perception. The bilinear dynamics model is a clever compromise: it is more powerful than a simple linear model (e.g., the classic interaction matrix) by capturing dependencies between state and action, yet simple enough (linear in controls) to be learned from little data and to allow for an analytic solution to the policy's optimization problem (Eq. 3).

The main strength lies in the FQI formulation for learning the feature weights. Standard deep RL methods would attempt to learn a complex, non-linear policy mapping features to actions, which is notoriously data-hungry. Instead, this paper fixes the policy structure to a simple, intuitive form (greedy minimization of feature error) and uses RL to learn the *metric* in the feature space. The insight to make the Q-function linear in the weights `θ` (Section 5.2) is what makes this tractable and efficient. The empirical results in Figure 6 clearly demonstrate the value of this approach: the FQI-based method achieves excellent performance with 20 trajectories (2 sampling iterations), while TRPO barely improves and requires over 100 times more data to approach, but not surpass, the performance of the proposed method.

The ablation studies and comparisons are also convincing. The paper shows that mid-level VGG features (e.g., `conv4_3`) outperform both raw pixels and the most abstract features (`conv5_3`), providing useful insight into the trade-off between semantic invariance and spatial precision for control tasks. The comparison against strong classical baselines, particularly Image-Based Visual Servoing (IBVS) with a state-of-the-art tracker (C-COT), is crucial. The proposed method's superior performance highlights the benefit of learning task-specific dynamics, even simple ones, over relying on generic geometric models.

Finally, the open-sourcing of the simulation environment and code is a commendable contribution to the community, enhancing the paper's reproducibility and impact.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, the following points could be addressed to further improve its clarity and impact:

*   **Sim-to-Real Transfer:** The primary limitation is the exclusive reliance on simulation. Visual servoing in the real world introduces challenges like imperfect camera calibration, lighting variations, and physical dynamics that are difficult to simulate perfectly. A discussion of the potential challenges in transferring this approach to a real robotic platform, or even a simple real-world proof-of-concept, would significantly strengthen the paper's claims of practical applicability.
*   **Static Goal Representation:** The goal is defined by a single feature map `y*` from an initial observation. This assumes the target's appearance remains relatively constant from the desired viewpoint. In longer-horizon tasks where the target's appearance might change substantially (e.g., a car turning a corner), this fixed goal could become problematic. It would be beneficial to discuss this limitation and potential extensions, such as updating the target features online.
*   **Clarity on FQI Algorithm:** The description of the two-step FQI update in Section 5.3 is quite dense. While technically sound, a more intuitive explanation for *why* this procedure is used would be helpful. For example, explaining that step 1 (Eq. 5) finds the optimal scaling and bias for the *current* policy's Q-function before using it as a target in step 2 (Eq. 6) could improve readability. This seems to be a form of policy evaluation stabilization, and framing it as such might help the reader.

### 5. Questions for Authors

1.  How does the system handle significant, non-rigid changes in the target's appearance or large rotations out-of-plane, given that the target feature `y*` is fixed? Have you experimented with scenarios where the initial view of the car (e.g., from the back) is very different from a view encountered later in the trajectory (e.g., from the side)?
2.  The dynamics model is trained on data from a hand-coded exploration policy. How sensitive is the performance of the final learned servoing policy to the quality and coverage of this initial data-gathering policy? Would a dynamics model trained on purely random actions perform substantially worse?
3.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost? While the C-COT tracker is noted as being slower at runtime (1Hz vs 16Hz), what is the offline training time for the proposed method (dynamics model training + FQI) compared to, for instance, the time taken to train the TRPO policies? This would provide a fuller picture of the method's overall efficiency.
