### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel and sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing policies, demonstrated on a simulated quadcopter car-following task. The core idea is to cleverly combine three powerful components: (1) rich visual features extracted from a pre-trained convolutional neural network (VGG), which avoids learning perception from scratch; (2) a simple but effective bilinear dynamics model learned in this feature space to predict the short-term consequences of actions; and (3) a reinforcement learning algorithm based on fitted Q-iteration (FQI) to learn a task-specific weighting over the feature channels and control inputs. This FQI formulation is particularly elegant, as it defines a Q-function whose greedy minimization directly yields the servoing control law, leading to an efficient, analytical update. The authors demonstrate that this approach can learn a robust servoing policy from only 20 trajectories, outperforming classical visual servoing methods and achieving more than a 100-fold improvement in sample efficiency over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms like TRPO.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper proposes a well-motivated and elegant framework that effectively integrates pre-trained representations, model-based control, and reinforcement learning. The specific formulation of the Q-function to learn weights for a feature-space servoing objective is a significant and clever contribution that leads to impressive sample efficiency.
2.  The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The method is benchmarked against a comprehensive set of strong and relevant baselines, including ablations, model-free deep RL, and classical visual servoing techniques. The results strongly support the central claims of the paper regarding performance and sample efficiency.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper has several notable strengths:

*   **Novelty and Elegance:** The primary contribution is the specific formulation that connects a model-based servoing policy to a Q-function. By defining the Q-function as a weighted sum of predicted feature errors (Equation in Sec 5.2), the authors create a direct link between the long-term value function (learned via RL) and the single-step, model-based control law (Equation 3). This avoids the need for a separate policy network and allows the weights to be learned efficiently using a linear solver within the FQI framework.
*   **Exceptional Sample Efficiency:** The results presented in Figure 6 are compelling. The ability to achieve strong performance with just 20 training trajectories, significantly outperforming TRPO even when it is given 100 times more data, is a major practical advantage for robotics, where data collection is often expensive and time-consuming. This result highlights the power of leveraging strong priors (pre-trained features, model structure) within a learning framework.
*   **Thorough and Rigorous Experiments:** The experimental design is sound. The authors compare their method not only to end-to-end RL and classical methods but also provide valuable ablation studies on the choice of visual features (Figure 5) and the method for learning the weights (Figure 6). Testing on both seen and novel cars demonstrates the generalization capabilities of the learned policies. The public release of the code and environment is also a commendable contribution to reproducibility.

The paper also has some weaknesses that should be addressed:

*   **Reliance on Privileged Information for Training:** The most significant weakness is the use of a cost function for RL training that relies on the ground truth 3D position of the target vehicle (Appendix B). This information would not be available in a real-world scenario, where the agent must operate from images alone. This dependency on "privileged information" during training undermines the premise of a purely vision-based learning system. While the final policy operates only on images, the learning process itself is not fully "end-to-end" from visual inputs.
*   **Simulation-Only Results:** All experiments are conducted in simulation. While the synthetic environment is visually rich, the paper does not address the well-known sim-to-real gap. A real-world deployment would introduce challenges such as camera noise, unpredictable lighting changes, and mismatches between the simple bilinear dynamics and real-world physics, which are not evaluated here.
*   **Limited Discussion of Model Limitations:** The bilinear dynamics model is chosen for its simplicity and efficiency. However, its limitations are not discussed. It is unclear how this approach would fare with more complex dynamics, such as following non-rigid objects, interacting with articulated robots, or in scenarios with more complex agent-environment interactions.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to be constructive suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not critical for my decision to accept.

*   It would significantly strengthen the paper to discuss the reliance on the ground truth cost function. A paragraph in the discussion acknowledging this limitation and suggesting potential solutions (e.g., using the learned feature distance `‖y* - yt‖` as a reward signal, or learning a separate reward function from images) would make the work more complete.
*   The analysis of why `conv5_3` features perform worse is brief. Visualizing the learned weights `w` for the different VGG layers could provide concrete insight. For instance, does the FQI algorithm learn to assign low weights to the highly invariant feature channels of the deeper layers?
*   A brief justification in the main text for modifying the VGG network with dilated convolutions (Sec 4.1) would be helpful, as this is an important architectural detail for preserving spatial information, which is key to the servoing task.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points to help finalize my assessment:

1.  Could you elaborate on the decision to use a cost function based on ground truth 3D position for training? Have you experimented with or can you comment on the expected performance if a purely vision-based cost were used, for example, by using the weighted feature-space error from the policy objective as a negative reward?
2.  The dynamics model is trained on a dataset collected with a hand-coded policy. How sensitive is the final performance to the quality and diversity of this initial dataset? For instance, if the initial policy provides poor coverage of the state-action space, does the learned dynamics model generalize poorly, leading to a suboptimal final policy?
3.  In Algorithm 1 (line 3), the dataset appears to be gathered using the initial policy `πθ(0)` in every sampling iteration `s`. Is this a typo? I would expect the data to be collected using the most recently learned policy from the previous sampling iteration to enable on-policy improvement.
