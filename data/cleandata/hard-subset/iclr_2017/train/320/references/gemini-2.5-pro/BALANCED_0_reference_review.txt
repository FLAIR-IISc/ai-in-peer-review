### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel, sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing policies, demonstrated on a complex simulated car-following task. The core idea is to cleverly combine three key components: (1) powerful, pre-trained visual features extracted from a VGG network to provide robustness to visual variations; (2) a simple, learned bilinear dynamics model that predicts how these features change in response to robot actions; and (3) a specialized Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) algorithm that learns to weight the importance of different feature channels and scales to optimize for long-term task success. The authors claim that this combination allows for learning an effective and generalizable visual servoing policy from an extremely small amount of data (20 trajectories). The experimental results convincingly show that the proposed method significantly outperforms classical visual servoing techniques based on hand-crafted features, as well as being over two orders of magnitude more sample-efficient than standard model-free deep reinforcement learning approaches like TRPO.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Exceptional Sample Efficiency:** The paper tackles a critical challenge in robotic learning: the need for large amounts of data. By structuring the problem to leverage pre-trained features and a simple dynamics model, the proposed FQI-based method achieves impressive performance with only 20 training trajectories. This is a significant practical contribution and a compelling demonstration of how model-based RL with good priors can drastically outperform generic model-free methods.
2.  **Strong and Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments in a challenging simulated environment. The authors provide convincing comparisons against multiple relevant baselines, including classical image-based visual servoing (IBVS), a state-of-the-art tracker-based method, and end-to-end deep RL. The inclusion of ablations on feature choice and learning algorithms, as well as testing for generalization to novel objects, adds significant weight to the results.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

**Strengths:**
*   **Elegant Problem Decomposition:** The method is a prime example of effective problem decomposition. Instead of learning an end-to-end policy from pixels to actions, the authors break the problem down into feature extraction (re-using a pre-trained network), dynamics learning (a simple, linear-in-action model), and control (learning weights for a quadratic objective). This structure is key to its sample efficiency and interpretability.
*   **Novel RL Formulation:** The formulation of the Q-function as a weighted sum of feature prediction errors is clever. It directly connects the learning objective of RL (maximizing long-term reward) to the control objective of the servoing policy (minimizing next-step feature error). The proposed two-step FQI algorithm, while heuristic, appears effective and tailored to this specific Q-function structure.
*   **High-Quality Experiments:** The experimental setup is rigorous. The use of a publicly available, high-fidelity simulator is a plus. The comparisons are fair and well-chosen, clearly positioning the proposed method's advantages. For instance, showing that TRPO needs >100x more data to approach (but not match) the performance of the proposed method is a powerful result. The finding that intermediate VGG layers (e.g., `conv4_3`) work best provides valuable insight for future work in this area.
*   **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written, with the method and experiments described clearly. The authors' commitment to open-sourcing their code and simulation environment is commendable and will be of great value to the community.

**Weaknesses:**
While the paper is strong, there are a few points that could be addressed to further improve it:
*   **Reliance on Privileged Information for Training:** The cost function used for reinforcement learning (Appendix B) relies on the ground-truth 3D position of the target car. While the final policy at test time uses only images, the training process requires this privileged information. This is a significant assumption that limits the method's direct applicability to real-world scenarios where such ground truth is unavailable. This limitation should be more prominently discussed in the main body of the paper.
*   **Simulation-Only Results:** The experiments are conducted exclusively in simulation. Visual servoing is an embodied robotics problem where real-world complexities like camera noise, calibration errors, and unpredictable lighting can pose significant challenges. A demonstration on a physical platform, even a simple one, would have made the paper's contributions substantially more impactful.
*   **Limited Discussion on Dynamics Model Scope:** The bilinear dynamics model is a core component and a major reason for the method's sample efficiency. However, its limitations are not discussed. It is well-suited for the relatively smooth dynamics of a quadrotor following a car, but its performance on tasks with more complex, non-rigid, or discontinuous dynamics is unclear. A brief discussion of the model's scope and potential failure modes would be beneficial.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following are suggestions aimed at further strengthening the paper and are not critical for my decision to accept.
*   It would be valuable to discuss potential avenues for removing the dependency on ground-truth pose for the reward signal. Could a reward function be learned via inverse reinforcement learning, or could a self-supervised objective (e.g., based on image similarity) be used instead? A short paragraph on this would strengthen the "future work" aspect.
*   The computational efficiency comparison against the C-COT tracker is a nice touch. To complete the picture, could you add the wall-clock training times for your FQI method versus the TRPO baselines? This would further highlight the overall efficiency of your approach.
*   In Section 5.3, the motivation for the two-step FQI update (first fitting `alpha` and `b`, then `theta`) could be briefly elaborated. Providing some intuition on why this decomposition is helpful (e.g., stabilizing learning, faster convergence) would improve reader understanding.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate it if you could address the following questions during the author response period to clarify my understanding.
1.  Could you elaborate on the importance of using ground-truth car position for the cost function during RL training? Have you experimented with alternative, image-based cost functions, and if so, how did they perform? How do you envision adapting the training process for a real-world scenario where this information is not available?
2.  The bilinear dynamics model is effective here. How do you expect it to perform in scenarios with more challenging dynamics, for example, if the target object were to move erratically or deform, or if the robot itself had more complex, non-linear dynamics?
3.  The two-step update in your FQI algorithm (Eq. 5 and 6) is an interesting design choice. What was the empirical or theoretical motivation behind this specific update rule compared to a more standard, single-step update of the Q-function parameters?
