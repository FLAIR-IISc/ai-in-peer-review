### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing controllers. The core idea is to combine the strengths of pre-trained deep visual features, a learned predictive dynamics model, and a targeted reinforcement learning algorithm. Specifically, the method leverages features from an ImageNet-pre-trained VGG network to provide a robust visual representation. It then learns a simple bilinear model to predict the one-step dynamics of these features in response to robot actions. The key contribution is a novel application of Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) to learn a set of weights over the different feature channels and scales. This FQI formulation is designed such that its corresponding greedy policy is a simple servoing controller that minimizes the weighted Euclidean distance between the predicted next-state features and the target features. This allows the system to automatically identify and prioritize the most task-relevant visual information. The authors demonstrate on a simulated quadcopter car-following task that their method can learn an effective policy from only 20 trajectories, significantly outperforming classical visual servoing techniques and showing a sample efficiency improvement of over two orders of magnitude compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning baselines.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a technically sound and novel combination of existing concepts (pre-trained features, learned dynamics, FQI) to address the important problem of sample-efficient learning for visual servoing. The formulation of a Q-function whose greedy policy corresponds directly to the weighted servoing objective is a clever and effective contribution.
2.  The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and provides strong evidence for the central claims of the paper. The method demonstrates a substantial improvement in performance and, most notably, a dramatic reduction in sample requirements (over 100x) compared to relevant deep reinforcement learning baselines in a complex simulated environment.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The methodology is well-motivated and technically sound. The paper identifies a clear gap between classical visual servoing methods, which often lack robustness to complex visual scenes, and modern deep RL methods, which are notoriously sample-inefficient. The proposed approach offers a compelling middle ground.

*   **Clarity and Structure:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The problem is clearly defined, and the motivation for each component of the proposed solution—pre-trained features for representation, a bilinear model for dynamics, and FQI for feature weighting—is well-articulated.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The core technical contribution is the design of the Q-function approximator (Section 5.2) to be linear in learnable weights that directly parameterize the servoing objective function (Equation 3). This creates a direct and efficient link between the long-term value estimation of RL and the parameters of the immediate control policy. The use of a simple bilinear dynamics model is appropriate for the one-step prediction required by the controller, and its linearity with respect to control inputs allows for an efficient, analytic solution to the policy's optimization problem.
*   **Novelty:** While the individual components are not new, their synthesis is novel and effective. The specific FQI algorithm designed to learn policy-objective weights, rather than a generic value function, is a significant contribution to model-based and sample-efficient RL for robotics.
*   **Empirical Rigor:** The experimental results are convincing. The ablation study comparing different feature layers from the VGG network (Figure 5) provides valuable insight. The primary claim of sample efficiency is strongly supported by the comparison with TRPO (Figure 6), where the proposed method achieves superior performance with only 2 sampling iterations (20 trajectories) compared to TRPO's 50 iterations (requiring thousands of trajectories). The comparisons to classical IBVS methods (with ORB features and a C-COT tracker) and a purely model-free end-to-end RL agent further situate the work and highlight its advantages in the tested domain.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions for strengthening the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   The experiments are conducted exclusively in simulation. While the simulation is complex, a discussion of the potential challenges and required adaptations for transferring this method to a real-world robotic platform would be a valuable addition. This could include topics like sim-to-real gap in visual features, system latencies, and noise in sensing and actuation.
*   The bilinear dynamics model is trained on a dataset collected by a "hand-coded policy." The performance of the final system may be sensitive to the quality and coverage of this initial dataset. It would be useful to discuss this dependence. For instance, how would the system perform if the dynamics were learned from a less-optimal or even random data collection policy?
*   The modified FQI algorithm (Equations 5 and 6) includes a non-standard intermediate step to fit scaling (`α`) and bias (`b`) parameters. While the motivation to "speed up the optimization" is stated, a more detailed intuition or justification for this specific update rule would improve clarity. For example, explaining its connection to a policy evaluation step would be helpful.
*   An empirical comparison between the proposed locally connected bilinear model and a fully connected version for the pixel-based baseline could provide a stronger justification for the architectural choice and quantify the performance trade-offs.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   The FQI algorithm introduces a two-step update within each iteration (Equations 5 and 6). Could you provide further intuition on why this specific update structure was chosen over a more standard FQI approach where the target network parameters are simply held fixed from the previous iteration? Have you performed any empirical comparison to a standard FQI implementation to quantify the benefits of this modification in terms of convergence or final performance?
*   How critical is the choice of the VGG network? Given the task's spatial nature, have you considered whether features from networks pre-trained on more spatially-oriented tasks, such as semantic segmentation or depth estimation, might offer further performance improvements?
*   The comparison to TRPO is a cornerstone of the sample efficiency claim. To ensure the comparison is as fair as possible, could you elaborate on the extent of hyperparameter tuning performed for the TRPO baseline, particularly regarding its network architecture, learning rate, and batch size?
