### Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel and highly sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing policies. The core contribution is a synergistic combination of three key components: (1) pre-trained deep visual features (from VGG) to provide a robust visual representation, (2) a learned, simple bilinear model to predict the short-term dynamics of these features in response to control actions, and (3) a customized fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn weights over the feature channels. The key insight is to structure the Q-function as a linear combination of the squared prediction errors of the dynamics model. This allows the greedy servoing policy—which minimizes the weighted one-step prediction error—to be recovered directly by minimizing the learned Q-function. This formulation makes the RL problem tractable and highly efficient. The authors demonstrate their approach on a challenging simulated car-following task, showing that it can learn an effective policy from just 20 trajectories. Empirically, the method significantly outperforms classical visual servoing techniques and standard model-free deep reinforcement learning approaches, showcasing a sample efficiency improvement of over two orders of magnitude compared to the latter.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The paper's main strength lies in its clever formulation of the Q-function. By designing the Q-function approximator to directly mirror the structure of the one-step lookahead servoing objective, the authors create a direct and efficient link between reinforcement learning and the control policy. This avoids the need for complex, black-box policy or Q-networks and instead learns an interpretable weighting over visual features, which is a more constrained and sample-efficient learning problem.

2.  **Impressive Empirical Results and Sample Efficiency:** The experimental results are comprehensive and convincingly support the paper's claims. The proposed method dramatically outperforms relevant and strong baselines, including end-to-end deep RL (TRPO) and classical image-based visual servoing (IBVS) with a state-of-the-art tracker. The most striking result is the massive improvement in sample efficiency: the method achieves strong performance with only 20 trajectories, while TRPO requires more than 100 times the data and still performs worse. This is a significant result for applying learning methods to real-world robotics, where data collection is expensive.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper effectively addresses a key challenge in robotics: how to build robust, perception-driven controllers without requiring massive amounts of task-specific training data. The approach is well-motivated by drawing from the strengths of different fields. Using pre-trained VGG features leverages large-scale vision datasets to provide a good perceptual foundation, bypassing the need to learn this from scratch. The choice of a bilinear dynamics model is well-justified; it is simple enough to be learned from a small dataset, yet its linearity with respect to the control action `u` makes the policy's optimization problem (`argmin_u Q(s,u)`) analytically solvable and computationally cheap.

The central technical contribution—the FQI-based learning of feature weights—is sound. The algorithm learns to automatically attend to the most relevant feature channels for the task (i.e., those corresponding to the target car) while ignoring distractors. The empirical evaluation clearly demonstrates the value of this learned weighting. The comparison in Figure 5 shows that while raw VGG features are useful, the performance varies significantly by layer. The FQI algorithm learns to best utilize these features. Figure 6 provides the most compelling evidence: the proposed FQI method vastly outperforms both an unweighted version and a policy trained with a state-of-the-art policy gradient method (TRPO) given the same small data budget. The comparison to classical IBVS methods is also important, as it shows the learned dynamics and feature weighting can outperform even methods supplied with a powerful object tracker. The generalization tests on novel cars further strengthen the claim of robustness.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to further strengthen the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Justification for the FQI Update Rule:** The two-step update within the FQI loop (Eq. 5 and 6), where a global scaling `alpha` and bias `b` are first fit before updating the weight vector `theta`, is presented as a heuristic to "speed up the optimization." While it clearly works well empirically, the paper would benefit from a more detailed justification or intuition. For instance, does this procedure approximate a form of natural gradient, or does it serve as a variance reduction technique by first anchoring the overall scale of the Q-values before refining their relative shape?
*   **Sensitivity to Dynamics Model Training Data:** The bilinear dynamics model is trained on a dataset generated by a hand-coded policy. The quality and performance of the final servoing policy likely depend on the quality of this dynamics model. It would be valuable to briefly discuss the sensitivity of the overall system to the data used for training the dynamics. For example, does the initial data need to cover a wide range of states and actions around the target for the model to be effective?
*   **Privileged Information in the Cost Function:** The cost function used for reinforcement learning (Appendix B) is based on the ground-truth 3D position of the car relative to the camera. This is standard practice in simulated RL experiments, but it represents privileged information not available from images alone. The policy itself only uses visual input, but the reward signal that shapes the policy does not. It would be good to acknowledge this limitation in the main text and perhaps briefly discuss how a purely image-based reward (e.g., from a learned discriminator or based on bounding box properties) might be incorporated in future work.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the intuition behind the two-step FQI update? Does solving for `alpha` and `b` first (Eq. 5) stabilize the subsequent update for `theta` (Eq. 6), perhaps by preventing the optimizer from making drastic changes to the overall magnitude of the Q-function?
2.  The results show that the `conv4_3` VGG layer works best. Have you experimented with combining features from multiple layers (e.g., concatenating feature maps from `conv3_3` and `conv4_3`)? Could this potentially provide a richer representation that combines mid-level details with more semantic information, possibly improving performance further?
