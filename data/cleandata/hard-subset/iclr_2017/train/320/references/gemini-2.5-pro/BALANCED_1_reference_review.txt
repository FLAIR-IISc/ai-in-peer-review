### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel and compelling method for learning visual servoing policies with high sample efficiency. The core idea is to combine the strengths of three distinct components: (1) powerful, pre-trained visual features from a VGG network to handle perception, (2) a simple, learned bilinear dynamics model to predict the short-term evolution of these features in response to actions, and (3) a reinforcement learning algorithm to optimize the control policy. Specifically, the authors frame the policy as a greedy minimization of a weighted, multi-scale feature distance, where the weights are learned using a specialized and highly efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm. The FQI formulation is particularly clever, as it learns a linear Q-function over features derived from the servoing objective itself, allowing for an analytic policy and stable, fast learning. The method is evaluated on a challenging simulated quadcopter car-following task, where it demonstrates remarkable performance. It significantly outperforms classical visual servoing methods and standard deep RL approaches, achieving strong results with as few as 20 training trajectories—an improvement of over two orders of magnitude in sample efficiency compared to model-free deep RL (TRPO).

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Effective Synthesis:** The paper proposes a well-motivated and elegant synthesis of pre-trained features, model-based control, and sample-efficient reinforcement learning. The specific formulation of using FQI to learn weights for a dynamics-based servoing objective is a novel and powerful contribution to the domain of learning-based robotic control.
2.  **Exceptional Sample Efficiency:** The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the primary claim of the paper: the ability to learn a robust visual servoing policy from a very small amount of data. The orders-of-magnitude improvement in sample complexity over a strong baseline like TRPO is a significant result and addresses a major bottleneck for applying RL to real-world robotics tasks.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The primary strength of this work lies in its thoughtful system design, which cleverly circumvents the major challenges of end-to-end learning for control. Instead of learning a complex policy from pixels to actions, which is notoriously data-hungry, the authors decompose the problem. Using pre-trained VGG features offloads the burden of learning a general visual representation. Learning a simple, linear-in-action dynamics model in this feature space is much more data-efficient than learning a full video prediction model. Finally, the reinforcement learning problem is reduced to learning a small set of weights for a well-structured policy, which is a much lower-dimensional and better-behaved optimization problem than training a large neural network policy.

The proposed FQI algorithm is tailored perfectly to this structure. By designing the Q-function to be linear in the weights and directly representative of the servoing cost, the policy `argmin_u Q(s,u)` can be solved analytically and efficiently. This avoids the instabilities and large data requirements of learning a general Q-network or policy network.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and provides strong support for the method's effectiveness. The authors compare against a comprehensive set of relevant baselines:
*   **Ablations on features:** The comparison between pixels and different layers of VGG features (Fig. 5) provides insight into which representations are most suitable.
*   **Ablations on learning:** The comparison of their FQI approach against TRPO and CEM for learning the weights (Fig. 6) clearly establishes the superiority of their chosen algorithm in terms of final performance and sample efficiency.
*   **Comparison to other paradigms:** The comparison against end-to-end deep RL (TRPO on a CNN policy) and classical image-based visual servoing (IBVS) with both keypoints and a state-of-the-art tracker situates the work in the broader literature and demonstrates a clear performance advantage.

The reported result of learning a robust policy from just 20 trajectories in a complex 3D environment with visual distractors and occlusions is impressive and highlights the practical potential of this approach for rapid adaptation to new tasks or targets.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, the following points could be addressed to further improve its clarity and impact. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from the decision to accept.

*   **Cost Function Dependency on Ground Truth:** The most significant point of clarification needed is regarding the cost function used for RL (Appendix B). It relies on the ground truth 3D position of the target car relative to the camera. While the final policy only uses images, the training signal is derived from this privileged information. This is a crucial detail that should be stated and justified in the main body of the paper (e.g., Section 5.3), as it makes the learning problem considerably easier. A discussion on how the method might perform with a purely image-based reward (e.g., one based on the feature distance `||y* - y_t||`) would strengthen the paper and make the setup more realistic.
*   **Limitations of the Bilinear Model:** The bilinear dynamics model is a key simplifying assumption. While it evidently works well for this task, the paper would benefit from a brief discussion of its limitations. In what scenarios might it fail? For instance, how would it handle highly non-linear dynamics or abrupt appearance changes not captured by the VGG features? This would provide better context for the applicability of the proposed method.
*   **Discussion on Sim-to-Real Transfer:** As the work is performed entirely in simulation, a short discussion on the potential challenges and strategies for transferring this system to a real robot would be valuable. For instance, would the dynamics model trained in simulation transfer? Would the VGG features be robust enough to the sim-to-real domain gap?

### 5. Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following questions in their rebuttal:

1.  Could you elaborate on the choice to use a cost function based on ground truth 3D position? What challenges did you encounter, if any, when attempting to use a purely image-based cost (e.g., feature-space distance), and how do you foresee the performance of your method changing if such a cost function were used instead?
2.  The dynamics model is trained on a dataset collected by a hand-coded policy. How sensitive is the performance of the final servoing policy to the quality and coverage of this initial dataset? For example, if the data collection policy was suboptimal or had a very different motion profile, would the learned dynamics model still be effective?
3.  The locally connected bilinear model includes action-independent bias terms intended to model visual changes like moving objects. Could you provide some analysis or intuition on how critical these terms were for successfully tracking the moving car in your experiments? Did you observe them capturing the car's independent motion as intended?
