This paper presents a method for learning visual servoing controllers by combining pre-trained deep features (from VGG), a learned predictive model of feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning algorithm. The core idea is to learn a simple bilinear model that predicts how visual features change in response to robot actions. A greedy servoing policy then minimizes the weighted Euclidean distance between the predicted features and a target feature representation. The key contribution is a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm that efficiently learns the optimal weights for different feature channels and scales, allowing the policy to focus on task-relevant visual information. The method is evaluated on a simulated car-following task, where it demonstrates significant improvements in sample efficiency and final performance compared to model-free deep RL (TRPO) and classical visual servoing baselines.

**Decision: Reject**

My primary reasons for this recommendation are:
1.  The experimental comparison to established visual servoing baselines is not rigorous. The classical methods are provided with ground-truth depth information, a significant advantage that is not typically available in practice and which undermines the claims of superior performance.
2.  The evaluation is confined to a single synthetic environment. The claims of robustness and real-world applicability are not supported by physical experiments, and the assumptions underlying the approach—particularly the adequacy of a simple bilinear dynamics model—may not hold in more complex, real-world scenarios.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The central claim of the paper is that its proposed combination of methods yields a visual servoing controller superior to conventional approaches. However, the experimental validation of this claim is unconvincing. In Section 6.4, the authors compare their method to Image-Based Visual Servoing (IBVS) using ORB features and features from a C-COT tracker. Critically, the paper states, "we provide the ground truth depth values of the feature points, which are used in the algorithm’s interaction matrix." This is a major flaw in the experimental design. The interaction matrix in classical IBVS is highly sensitive to depth, and providing perfect depth information circumvents one of the key challenges of the task. A fair comparison would require the baseline methods to operate under the same conditions as the proposed method, i.e., using depth estimated from vision or assuming a fixed depth. Without this, the reported performance gap is not meaningful.

Furthermore, the results in Appendix C.5 and C.6 reveal that classical methods, when given access to ground truth information (such as object pose or dynamics), can outperform the proposed method by orders of magnitude. While the authors' method does not use this information, this result suggests that the primary difficulty of the task may lie in state estimation rather than control. The proposed method conflates these two problems by learning a direct mapping from features to actions, but it is not clearly demonstrated that this approach is fundamentally better than a more traditional pipeline of object tracking followed by a classical controller, especially if the tracker is state-of-the-art. The comparison to the C-COT tracker-based method is the most relevant, and while the proposed method performs better, the performance gap is not overwhelming, and the argument for superiority rests partly on a reported speed advantage (16Hz vs 1Hz), which may be implementation-dependent.

The second major weakness is the complete reliance on a single, simulated environment. Visual servoing is an applied robotics problem, and claims of robustness to occlusions, viewpoint, and lighting changes must ultimately be validated on a physical system. Simulation allows for perfect state information, noise-free control, and simplified physics, which may mask failure modes of the proposed approach. The choice of a bilinear dynamics model, in particular, is a strong simplifying assumption. While motivated by prior work on pixel dynamics, its applicability to the dynamics of abstract VGG features is not thoroughly justified or tested. It is plausible that such a linear model is sufficient for the relatively simple dynamics of the simulated quadcopter but would fail to capture the more complex, non-linear effects present in real-world robotic systems with camera distortion, motion blur, and actuator delays.

Finally, while the proposed FQI algorithm for learning feature weights is clever, its novelty and generality are limited. The method is built around a very specific, linear Q-function approximator that directly mirrors the structure of the quadratic servoing policy. This makes the approach a highly specialized parameter tuning technique rather than a general reinforcement learning algorithm. The two-step update in Algorithm 1, where scale and bias parameters are fit before the main weight vector, seems ad-hoc and lacks theoretical justification. While it appears to work well empirically on this specific problem, its stability and performance in other contexts are unknown.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

*   To strengthen the paper, the comparison with classical IBVS methods should be repeated without providing ground-truth depth. For instance, a constant depth assumption could be used, or depth could be estimated with a monocular depth estimation network. This would constitute a much fairer and more compelling baseline.
*   The paper would be significantly improved by the inclusion of at least one real-world experiment, even a simple one. This would lend much-needed credibility to the claims of robustness and practical applicability.
*   A discussion on the limitations of the bilinear dynamics model is needed. In what scenarios would this model be expected to fail? How sensitive is the performance to unmodeled non-linearities in the feature dynamics?
*   The justification for the specific two-step optimization procedure within the FQI algorithm should be expanded. Is there a theoretical motivation for this approach, or is it a heuristic that was found to improve convergence?

**Questions for Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the decision to provide ground-truth depth to the classical IBVS baselines? How do these baselines perform under more realistic assumptions, such as estimated depth or a fixed-depth assumption for the interaction matrix calculation?
2.  The FQI algorithm is tightly coupled to the proposed quadratic policy structure. Could you comment on the generality of this method? Is the two-step update procedure (solving for `α, b` separately from `θ`) essential for stability, and can you provide more intuition for why this is beneficial?
3.  Given that the experiments are in simulation, what do you foresee as the primary challenges in deploying this system on a physical quadcopter? Specifically, how robust do you expect the learned bilinear dynamics model to be to real-world phenomena like camera non-idealities, motion blur, and aerodynamic effects?
