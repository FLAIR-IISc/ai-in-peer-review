### Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel and sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing policies, specifically for a target-following task. The approach cleverly combines three key components: (1) robust, pre-trained visual features extracted from a VGG network to handle visual variations; (2) a learned, task-agnostic bilinear model to predict the short-term dynamics of these features in response to robot actions; and (3) a highly sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn a task-specific policy. The policy itself is a simple, greedy controller that seeks to minimize the one-step weighted Euclidean distance between predicted features and target features. The main contribution lies in the FQI formulation, which learns the optimal weights for different feature channels and scales, effectively teaching the greedy controller to optimize for long-term rewards. The method is validated in a complex simulated quadcopter car-following environment, where it is shown to learn an effective policy from just 20 trajectories. It demonstrates substantial performance gains and over two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency compared to standard deep reinforcement learning baselines like TRPO, as well as outperforming classical visual servoing techniques.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

1.  **Novel and Effective Synthesis of Methods:** The paper presents a compelling and well-motivated synthesis of pre-trained features, model-based control, and sample-efficient reinforcement learning. The specific formulation of the Q-function to directly represent the servoing objective is a key technical insight that enables the use of a simple, linear FQI procedure. This provides a powerful solution to the challenging problem of learning adaptable visual servoing policies without requiring massive datasets.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evidence:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and provides strong support for the paper's claims. The method's remarkable sample efficiency (learning from only 20 trajectories) is convincingly demonstrated through direct comparison with TRPO, which fails to match its performance even with over 100 times more data. Furthermore, the comparisons against various feature representations and classical visual servoing baselines (including a state-of-the-art tracker) clearly establish the superiority of the proposed approach in the tested domain. The public release of the code and simulation environment is a significant plus for reproducibility.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The core strength of this work is its pragmatic and effective solution to a long-standing robotics problem. Visual servoing has traditionally been split between classical methods that are data-efficient but brittle to visual distractors, and modern end-to-end RL methods that are robust but notoriously data-hungry. This paper carves out a highly effective middle ground.

By leveraging pre-trained VGG features, the system inherits a powerful visual representation, bypassing the need to learn one from scratch. The choice of a simple bilinear dynamics model is well-justified and proves sufficient for one-step prediction, avoiding the complexities and data requirements of more general video prediction models.

The most elegant part of the method is the reinforcement learning component. Instead of learning a complex, black-box policy network, the authors learn simple weights for a pre-defined servoing controller. The insight to structure the Q-function approximator (Section 5.2) to mirror the servoing objective (Eq. 3) is what makes the FQI approach so efficient. It allows the Bellman updates to be framed as a simple, regularized least-squares problem, which is far more stable and data-efficient than gradient-based policy optimization methods. The results in Figure 6 are striking and represent a significant contribution to applying RL in robotics, where sample efficiency is paramount. The comprehensive ablation studies (e.g., comparing different VGG layers in Figure 5) and comparisons to strong baselines further strengthen the paper's conclusions.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help improve the clarity and positioning of the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

*   **Clarity on Data Usage:** The abstract and introduction emphasize learning from "just 20 training trajectory samples." While this is true for the final RL stage, the dynamics model itself was trained on 100 trajectories (10,000 data points). To improve transparency, it would be beneficial to more clearly distinguish between the one-time, task-agnostic data cost for learning the dynamics and the remarkably low, task-specific data cost for learning the policy weights.
*   **Use of Ground-Truth for Cost Signal:** The cost function used for RL (Appendix B) relies on the ground-truth 3D position of the car. This is a minor weakness in a paper focused on visual control, as the learning signal is not derived purely from pixels. A brief discussion on the possibility and potential challenges of using a purely image-based reward signal (e.g., based on the target's bounding box in the image) would strengthen the work.
*   **Generalization of the Dynamics Model:** The "general visual dynamics model" was trained specifically on data from a quadcopter following a car. A short discussion on the expected limits of this model's generalization would be valuable. For instance, would the same dynamics model be applicable to a ground robot or for tracking a non-rigid object like a person? This would help frame the scope of the current work and opportunities for future research.
*   **Details on Dynamics Model Used:** The paper proposes both a fully connected (per-channel) and a locally connected bilinear dynamics model. Appendix C.2 states the locally connected version was not used for VGG features due to memory issues. It should be clarified in the main text (Section 4.2 or 6) that the main VGG-based results use the fully connected per-channel variant, as this is a key implementation detail.

### Questions for the Authors

*   Could you provide some insight or visualization regarding the learned feature weights `w` for the best-performing `conv4_3` model? For example, which feature channels received the highest weights? This could offer valuable intuition about which visual patterns the learned servoing policy deems most important for tracking.
*   The FQI algorithm includes an intermediate step (Eq. 5) to solve for a scaling factor `α` and bias `b` before the main parameter update. What is the primary motivation for this step, and how crucial is it for the stability and speed of convergence compared to a more standard FQI update that would only perform the step in Eq. 6?
*   How robust is the overall system to errors in the learned dynamics model? For instance, if the dynamics were trained with less data or on a slightly different motion profile, how gracefully does the performance of the final servoing policy degrade?
