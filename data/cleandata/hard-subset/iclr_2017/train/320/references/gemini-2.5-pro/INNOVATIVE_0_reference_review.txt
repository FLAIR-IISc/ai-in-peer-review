### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel and highly effective method for learning visual servoing controllers with remarkable sample efficiency. The authors propose a principled framework that decomposes the problem into three synergistic components: 1) leveraging powerful, general-purpose visual representations from a pre-trained VGG network to handle visual complexity; 2) learning a simple, local predictive model of the feature dynamics (a bilinear model) to enable one-step planning; and 3) using a novel fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn a weighting over the feature channels. This third component is the core contribution, as it allows the system to use reinforcement learning not to learn a complex, end-to-end policy, but rather to learn an optimal "attentional" mechanism that prioritizes task-relevant features for the simple, underlying servoing controller. The result is a system that can learn a robust car-following policy in a complex 3D simulation from just 20 training trajectories, substantially outperforming classical visual servoing methods and standard deep RL approaches that require orders of magnitude more data.

### **Decision & Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This paper is a clear accept. It introduces a bold, conceptually novel, and highly promising direction for learning-based robotics. My decision is based on two key strengths:

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The paper's primary contribution is not just another application of deep learning to robotics, but a new way of thinking about the problem. The idea to use reinforcement learning to learn a *distance metric* (via feature weighting) for a simple, model-based controller is elegant and powerful. It sidesteps the immense sample complexity of end-to-end model-free RL while avoiding the brittleness of purely classical methods. This hybrid approach is a significant conceptual advance.
2.  **Exceptional Sample Efficiency and Strong Empirical Results:** The claims of data efficiency are not minor; they are dramatic. Learning a complex visuomotor skill from only 20 trajectories is a fantastic result. The authors provide a compelling suite of comparisons, demonstrating that their method is not only more sample-efficient but also achieves better final performance than well-established baselines like TRPO and classical IBVS, even when the latter are given access to privileged information like ground-truth depth.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strength lies in its intelligent decomposition of the visual servoing problem. Instead of treating the policy as a black box to be learned from scratch, the authors embed significant, well-motivated structural priors. The use of pre-trained VGG features is a well-established and sensible choice for bootstrapping visual understanding. The choice of a bilinear model for the dynamics is also well-justified; it is simple enough to be learned from a small amount of data while being sufficient for the one-step predictions required by the servoing policy.

The most innovative part of the work is the reinforcement learning formulation. By structuring the Q-function to be linear in the feature weights, the authors turn the problem of learning these weights into a simple, efficient regression problem that can be solved with fitted Q-iteration. This is a technically sound and clever piece of algorithm design that enables the entire system to work. It directly optimizes the feature representation for the long-term task objective, effectively learning to ignore distractors and focus on what matters for control.

The experiments convincingly support the paper's claims. The comparison across different VGG layers provides useful insight, suggesting that mid-level features strike the right balance between semantic understanding and spatial precision. The core result—outperforming TRPO by two orders of magnitude in sample efficiency—is striking and directly addresses one of the biggest bottlenecks in applying deep RL to robotics. Furthermore, beating a state-of-the-art visual tracker (C-COT) combined with a classical controller demonstrates the practical relevance and robustness of the learned approach.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, the following points could be addressed to improve it further. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from my recommendation to accept.

*   **Real-World Applicability:** The work is currently validated only in simulation. While the simulation appears reasonably complex, the ultimate test for any robotics paper is the real world. A brief discussion on the anticipated challenges of transferring this method to a physical robot (e.g., the sim-to-real gap for the learned dynamics model, latency issues) would strengthen the paper. Even a simple hardware demonstration would make the work's impact undeniable.
*   **Sensitivity to Initial Data:** The dynamics model is trained on data collected from a "hand-coded policy." The quality of this initial data seems crucial. It would be valuable to understand how sensitive the system's performance is to this policy. For instance, if the initial data does not contain sufficient exploratory actions, can the dynamics model still be learned effectively?
*   **Cost Function:** The cost function used for RL training relies on ground-truth 3D coordinates of the target vehicle. While this is acceptable for training in simulation, it relies on privileged information that would not be available in a real-world scenario without an external motion capture system. The paper would be more self-contained if it demonstrated that the weights could be learned with a cost function defined purely in the image space (e.g., from a pre-trained object detector, or even from the weighted feature error itself).

### **Questions for the Authors**

*   The bilinear dynamics model is simple and effective here. Could you elaborate on its potential limitations? For example, how do you expect it would perform in scenarios with highly non-linear dynamics, such as object articulations or sudden, drastic lighting changes that are not well-modeled as linear transformations in feature space?
*   In Section 5.3, you propose a two-step FQI update (first solving for a scale `α` and bias `b`, then for `θ`). This is an interesting deviation from standard FQI. Could you provide more intuition on why this particular update scheme proved more effective or stable than a more conventional single-step update for the Q-function parameters?
*   The comparison against classical IBVS is very strong, especially since you provide the baselines with ground-truth depth. Could you clarify whether your method uses or infers depth in any way, or does the learned dynamics model operating on 2D feature maps implicitly capture the necessary 3D effects for control?
