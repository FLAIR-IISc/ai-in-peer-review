### Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel method for learning visual servoing controllers with high sample efficiency. The core idea is to combine three main components: (1) pre-trained deep visual features from a VGG network to provide a robust visual representation, (2) a learned, multiscale bilinear model to predict the short-term dynamics of these features in response to control actions, and (3) a custom fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn weights for different feature channels and scales. The policy is defined as the action that minimizes a weighted one-step prediction error, which is made tractable by a specific Q-function parameterization. The authors demonstrate on a simulated car-following task that their method can learn an effective policy from only 20 trajectories, significantly outperforming conventional visual servoing methods and standard deep reinforcement learning approaches in terms of sample efficiency.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Significant Lack of Polish and Attention to Detail:** The manuscript is marred by a substantial number of formatting errors, notational ambiguities, and presentation flaws. Most notably, the reference section is completely malformed, rendering it almost unreadable. This pervasive sloppiness undermines confidence in the rigor of the work and falls well below the standard for publication at ICLR.
2.  **Insufficient Justification for Methodological Choices:** Several key components of the proposed method, particularly the custom two-step FQI update procedure, are presented without adequate motivation or justification. The rationale for this specific design is unclear, and its advantages over more standard approaches are not empirically or theoretically demonstrated.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

**1. Pervasive Presentation and Formatting Issues:**

*   **References:** The bibliography is in a state of disarray. Entries contain raw BibTeX artifacts like `\.,? \Q2006\E,`, extraneous author repetitions ("Bay et al., Bay et al."), and garbled text. This is not a minor cosmetic issue; it reflects a profound lack of care in the preparation of the manuscript and makes it difficult to verify and contextualize the cited work.
*   **Figures and Tables:** All figures (e.g., Fig. 5, Fig. 6) and tables lack proper, numbered captions. They instead use unnumbered titles, which is non-standard and hinders clarity. For instance, a reader cannot be referred to "Figure 5" in the text.
*   **Footnotes:** The use of footnotes for repository URLs (1, 2, 3) is unconventional. More importantly, critical model details, such as the mathematical definition of the locally connected operator (footnote 4), are relegated to a footnote in dense, poorly explained notation. This operator is a novel contribution of the paper and deserves a proper, clear explanation in the main text. The indexing `Wkh,kw,ih−kh,iw−kw` is particularly opaque without further explanation.
*   **Notational Ambiguity:** The policy in Algorithm 1, line 3 is specified as `N(πθ(0), σ^2exploration)`. This notation is ambiguous. Does `θ(0)` refer to the initial parameters before any training, or is it updated after each outer loop? The final line, `θ(0) ← θ(K)`, suggests the latter, but the notation `θ(0)` is then overloaded and confusing. This makes the algorithm difficult to reproduce from the description alone.

**2. Unclear Methodological Rationale:**

*   **Custom FQI Procedure:** The paper proposes a two-stage update within each FQI iteration (Eq. 5 and 6). First, a scalar `α` and bias `b` are fitted, and then the feature weights `θ` are updated. The motivation provided is "to speed up the optimization." This justification is insufficient. Why is this specific two-stage procedure better than a standard FQI update on the parameters `θ` and `b` directly? The claim of speed is not substantiated with a comparison. The procedure feels ad-hoc without a more rigorous argument or an ablation study comparing it to a simpler, standard FQI implementation.
*   **Misleading Terminology:** The servoing policy is repeatedly described as "gradient-based" (e.g., Section 3). However, the policy is derived from `argmin_u ||...||^2` (Eq. 1), which, given the linear dynamics, is a quadratic optimization problem with an analytical solution. This is an optimization-based or model-based policy, not a gradient-based one in the typical sense of policy gradient methods or gradient descent on the control inputs.
*   **Unsupported Claims:** In Section 4.2, the bias terms in the bilinear model are claimed to "allow to model action-independent visual changes, such as moving objects." The experiments only involve a single moving target car whose motion is correlated with the ego-vehicle's actions. There is no evidence presented that the model can handle independently moving distractor objects, making this claim speculative.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While my recommendation is to reject the paper in its current state, the core ideas are promising and the empirical results, particularly the sample efficiency, are impressive. I believe a revised version addressing the following points could be a strong submission.

*   **Thorough Proofreading and Reformatting:** The entire manuscript requires a meticulous pass to fix all formatting issues. The reference section must be completely rebuilt using a standard bibliography style. Figures and tables should be given proper, numbered captions.
*   **Clarify the Algorithm:** Algorithm 1 needs to be rewritten with unambiguous notation to make the training procedure perfectly clear. Specify precisely how the policy used for data collection in sampling iteration `s` relates to the parameters learned in iteration `s-1`.
*   **Strengthen Methodological Justification:** The rationale behind the two-step FQI update needs to be explained in detail. If it is indeed superior, provide an ablation study in the appendix comparing its convergence or final performance against a standard FQI update to empirically validate the design choice.
*   **Refine Claims:** Please moderate claims that are not directly supported by the experiments. For instance, rephrase the purpose of the action-independent dynamics term to more accurately reflect what has been demonstrated.
*   **Baseline Discussion:** The use of ground truth depth for the IBVS baselines is a major concession. While it strengthens your results, it would be valuable to discuss the expected performance of these baselines with a realistic depth estimation module to provide a fairer comparison against your RGB-only method.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the exact procedure in Algorithm 1? Specifically, for the data collection in sampling iteration `s > 1`, is the policy `π` parameterized by the initial weights `θ(0)` or the final weights `θ(K)` from the previous sampling iteration `s-1`?
2.  What is the empirical or theoretical motivation for the two-stage FQI update involving `α` and `b` (Eq. 5) before updating `θ` (Eq. 6)? How does this compare to a more standard approach of simply minimizing the Bellman error with respect to `θ` and `b` in a single optimization step, using a fixed target network?
3.  In Equation 3, the term `1/|y(l)·,c|` is used for normalization. Is `|·|` the cardinality of the spatial dimensions (i.e., height x width)? This notation is non-standard and could be clarified.
