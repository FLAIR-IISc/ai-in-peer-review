### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel and sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing policies. The core contribution is an elegant combination of three key components: (1) pre-trained convolutional features (from VGG) to provide a robust visual representation without requiring task-specific feature engineering; (2) a learned, simple bilinear dynamics model that predicts the one-step evolution of these features in response to robot actions; and (3) a specially-formulated fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm that learns to weight the feature channels and control inputs. The FQI procedure directly optimizes the parameters of a greedy servoing policy, allowing it to learn long-horizon behavior from a very small number of interactions. The authors demonstrate their method on a challenging simulated quadcopter car-following task, showing substantial improvements in performance and a dramatic reduction in sample complexity (over two orders of magnitude) compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms and classical visual servoing techniques.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Effective Method:** The paper presents a well-motivated and clever synthesis of transfer learning, model-based control, and reinforcement learning. The key insight of designing the Q-function approximator to have the same functional form as the servoing objective is particularly elegant. This allows the FQI algorithm to directly learn the parameters of the controller in a principled and highly sample-efficient manner, which is a significant contribution.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and convincingly support the paper's claims. The method is benchmarked against a comprehensive set of strong baselines, including end-to-end deep RL (TRPO), classical image-based visual servoing (IBVS) with hand-designed and tracker-based features, and ablations of the proposed approach. The reported results, especially the >100x improvement in sample efficiency over TRPO and superior performance against a state-of-the-art visual tracker (C-COT), are impressive and clearly demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed technique.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this work lies in its targeted approach to solving the sample-efficiency problem in visuomotor control. Instead of learning an end-to-end policy from scratch, which is notoriously data-hungry, the authors judiciously break the problem down. Using pre-trained VGG features is a well-established but effective strategy for bootstrapping perception. The choice of a bilinear dynamics model is also well-justified; it is simple enough to be learned from a small dataset (100 trajectories) yet expressive enough to capture the local dynamics of visual features under camera motion, as demonstrated by the strong performance of the final policy.

The most novel part of the method is the application of FQI. The authors formulate a linear Q-function approximator based on the squared error of the predicted features, which is the same objective the final servoing policy minimizes. This tight coupling between the Q-function and the policy allows for an analytic solution for the greedy action `argmin_u Q(s,u)`. Consequently, the Bellman error minimization becomes a simple least-squares problem to find the optimal feature weights `θ`. This avoids the complexities of training a separate neural network for the Q-function or policy and is the main reason for the method's remarkable sample efficiency. Learning an effective policy from just 20 trajectories (2 sampling iterations) in a complex 3D environment is a significant achievement.

The experimental results are compelling. The comparison in Figure 6 clearly shows that the proposed FQI-based method vastly outperforms TRPO given the same small data budget. Even after 50 sampling iterations (over 100 times more data), TRPO fails to match the performance of the proposed method, validating the sample-efficiency claims. Furthermore, the comparison against classical methods is strong. Outperforming IBVS with ORB features is expected, but the favorable comparison to a C-COT tracker—a state-of-the-art method, which was even provided with ground-truth depth information—highlights the robustness of the learned representation and dynamics. The paper's commitment to reproducibility, by providing open-source code and the simulation environment, further strengthens its contribution.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Real-World Applicability:** The experiments are conducted exclusively in simulation. While the simulation appears realistic, a discussion of the potential challenges in transferring this method to a real robotic platform would be valuable. For example, how would unmodeled dynamics, camera-to-actuator latency, or more complex visual noise affect the performance of the learned bilinear dynamics model?
*   **Target Specification and Adaptation:** The target is defined by a single goal image `x*` provided at the start of an episode. This assumes a static target appearance. It would be beneficial to discuss the limitations of this approach, for instance, when tracking objects that deform or undergo significant, non-rigid appearance changes over time.
*   **Training Data for Dynamics Model:** The dynamics model is trained on data collected from a "hand-coded policy." The performance of the final learned servoing policy could be sensitive to the quality and coverage of this initial dataset. A brief discussion on this sensitivity or how one might iteratively improve the dynamics model would strengthen the paper.
*   **Computational Cost:** While the policy inference is fast (16Hz), it would be useful to have a more explicit breakdown of the offline computation costs (training the dynamics model and running FQI) compared to the baselines.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of the learned policy to the data used for training the dynamics model? For instance, if the dynamics model was trained on trajectories that only explored a small region of the state-action space, would the FQI-trained policy still be effective and stable when exploring new regions?
2.  The paper notes that regularization was important for FQI to converge. Could you elaborate on the sensitivity to the regularization hyperparameter `ν`? Was the algorithm sensitive to the initialization of the weights `θ`?
3.  A key motivation is the ability to quickly adapt to new targets. The experiments show generalization to new car models. How well do you expect the method to generalize to tracking a new *category* of object (e.g., a person or another drone)? Would it be sufficient to only re-run the FQI stage with a few trajectories of the new target, or would the dynamics model also need to be retrained?
