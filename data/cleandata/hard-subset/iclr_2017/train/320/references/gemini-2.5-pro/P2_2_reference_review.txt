### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel and highly sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing policies, demonstrated on a complex simulated car-following task. The core contribution is an elegant synthesis of three key components: (1) robust visual representations extracted from a pre-trained convolutional neural network (VGG), modified to preserve spatial information; (2) a learned bilinear dynamics model that predicts how these visual features change in response to robot actions; and (3) a specialized Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) algorithm that learns to weight the importance of different feature channels and scales to optimize for a long-term task objective. By framing the control problem as minimizing a weighted, predicted feature error, the authors construct a Q-function approximator that is linear in its parameters. This structure allows for an extremely sample-efficient learning algorithm, reportedly learning an effective policy from just 20 trajectories, which is a significant improvement over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning approaches.

### **2. Recommendation: Accept**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are:

1.  **Elegant and Effective Formulation:** The paper's main strength lies in its clever formulation of the visual servoing problem. Instead of learning an end-to-end policy or a complex general-purpose Q-network, it learns a task-specific *metric* over pre-existing, powerful features. This decomposition—reusing features, learning simple dynamics, and using RL for weighting—is a well-motivated and highly effective strategy for achieving sample efficiency.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The claims of performance and sample efficiency are rigorously supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The proposed method significantly outperforms a wide range of relevant baselines, including classical visual servoing methods (IBVS with ORB and trackers), standard model-free deep RL (end-to-end TRPO), and ablations of their own method (e.g., using pixel features or different learning algorithms). The reported improvement of over two orders of magnitude in sample efficiency compared to TRPO is particularly impressive and impactful.

### **3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper makes a compelling case by successfully bridging the gap between classical robotics and modern deep learning.

*   **Intelligent Use of Pre-trained Features:** The authors rightly identify that learning visual representations from scratch is a major cause of sample inefficiency in deep RL. By leveraging features from a VGG network pre-trained on ImageNet, they inherit a rich, hierarchical representation of the visual world. Their modification to replace max-pooling with dilated convolutions is a crucial and well-justified step to retain the spatial precision necessary for a servoing task. The ablation study in Figure 5, which shows that intermediate-level features (`conv4_3`) work best, provides valuable insight into the trade-off between semantic abstraction and spatial invariance.

*   **Pragmatic Choice of Dynamics Model:** Rather than attempting to learn a complex, generative video prediction model, the authors opt for a simple bilinear model to capture the short-term feature dynamics. This is an excellent design choice. The model is simple enough to be learned from a small dataset (100 trajectories) yet powerful enough to provide the one-step lookahead needed for the servoing policy. The linearization of this model for control (Appendix A) is exact, leading to an efficient and analytic policy optimization step (Equation 3).

*   **Novel Reinforcement Learning Formulation:** The most significant contribution is the design of the Q-function and the associated FQI learning algorithm. By defining the Q-function as the weighted servoing objective (`Q(s,u) = φ(s,u)ᵀθ + b`), the authors create a direct link between the value function and the policy. The policy is simply the greedy action with respect to Q, `argmin_u Q(s,u)`. This allows them to learn the weights `θ` by solving a simple regularized least-squares problem to minimize the Bellman error, which is far more stable and sample-efficient than backpropagating gradients through a complex neural network, as is done in methods like DDPG or policy gradient. The comparison against TRPO in Figure 6 provides definitive evidence of this benefit.

*   **Thorough and Rigorous Experiments:** The evaluation is exemplary. The simulated environment is complex enough to feature distractors, occlusions, and varying viewpoints. The comparisons are well-chosen and cover the most relevant alternative paradigms:
    *   **Classical Methods:** The comparison to IBVS with ORB features and a state-of-the-art tracker (C-COT) situates the work within the traditional robotics literature and demonstrates a clear performance advantage.
    *   **Model-Free RL:** The comparison to an end-to-end TRPO policy highlights the enormous gains in sample efficiency achieved by the proposed structured approach.
    *   **Ablations:** Comparing different features (pixels, various VGG layers) and different learning algorithms (CEM, TRPO) on their own policy structure effectively isolates and validates each component of their design.

### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, the following points could help improve it further. These are suggestions and do not detract from my recommendation to accept.

*   **Clarity on FQI Updates:** The two-step update procedure in FQI (Equations 5 and 6) is central to the algorithm. While the text mentions it is to "speed up the optimization," a slightly more intuitive explanation of this process would be beneficial. For instance, explaining that solving for the scalar `α` and bias `b` first helps to properly scale and shift the target Q-values before fitting the main weight vector `θ` could enhance reader understanding.

*   **Discussion of Limitations:** The discussion section could be strengthened by explicitly addressing the limitations of the approach. For example:
    *   The bilinear dynamics model, while effective here, may struggle with highly non-linear dynamics (e.g., abrupt changes, contact).
    *   The method's performance relies on the quality of the pre-trained features. How might it perform on tasks with visual domains vastly different from ImageNet (e.g., thermal imagery, medical scans)?
    *   The experiments are conducted in simulation. A brief discussion of potential challenges in real-world deployment (e.g., camera calibration drift, latency, sim-to-real gap in dynamics) would add valuable context.

*   **Generalization of the Dynamics Model:** The dynamics model is trained once on data from multiple cars. It would be interesting to know if this general model is crucial for generalizing to novel cars, or if a dynamics model trained on just a single car could also suffice for following that same car. This would clarify whether the dynamics being learned are object-specific or more general to the robot's egomotion.

### **5. Questions for Authors**

*   The initial dataset for learning the dynamics model was collected using a "hand-coded policy." How sensitive is the final performance to the quality and coverage of this initial data? If this policy were significantly different or suboptimal, would the learned dynamics model still be effective?

*   The cost function used for RL (Appendix B) relies on ground-truth 3D position information from the simulator. This is a common and acceptable practice for training in simulation. However, how do you envision this approach being applied on a real robot where such ground-truth rewards are unavailable? Would this necessitate a separate reward-learning phase, or could a self-supervised objective based on feature distance serve as a proxy?

*   The results show that `conv4_3` features outperform deeper features like `conv5_3`. Have you considered using a combination of features from multiple layers (e.g., concatenating `conv3_3` and `conv4_3`) to potentially gain both the spatial detail of earlier layers and the semantic invariance of later ones?
