### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel and sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing controllers. The core problem addressed is how to quickly adapt a robot to follow a new visual target without requiring vast amounts of task-specific training data, a common limitation of standard deep reinforcement learning (RL) approaches. The authors propose a three-part solution:
1.  **Leveraging Pre-trained Features:** Instead of learning visual representations from scratch, the method utilizes features extracted from a VGG network pre-trained on ImageNet. To preserve crucial spatial information for servoing, the network architecture is modified by replacing some max-pooling layers with dilated convolutions.
2.  **Learning Feature Dynamics:** A simple, multiscale bilinear model is learned to predict the one-step evolution of these visual features given a control action. This model is linear in the controls, which allows for an efficient, analytical solution for the servoing policy.
3.  **Learning a Feature Metric with RL:** The key contribution is framing the servoing problem as minimizing a weighted Euclidean distance between the predicted next-step features and the target features. Instead of hand-tuning these weights, the authors propose a sample-efficient Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn them. They formulate a Q-function whose greedy minimization directly corresponds to the servoing control law, cleverly connecting a long-term reward signal (from RL) to the parameters of a simple, one-step predictive controller.

The method is evaluated on a complex simulated car-following task with a quadcopter. The results convincingly demonstrate that this approach significantly outperforms classical visual servoing methods (based on ORB features or trackers) and is over two orders of magnitude more sample-efficient than standard model-free deep RL (TRPO), requiring only 20 trajectories to learn an effective policy.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper presents a clever synthesis of pre-trained features, learned dynamics, and reinforcement learning. The core idea of using FQI not to learn a complex policy network, but to learn a low-dimensional weighting for a simple, model-based controller is both novel and highly effective at addressing the sample-efficiency problem in robotic learning.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The experimental setup is thorough and the results are compelling. The authors compare their method against a strong set of baselines, including classical robotics methods, model-free deep RL, and ablations of their own approach. The dramatic improvement in sample efficiency (20 trajectories vs. thousands for TRPO) is a significant result that strongly supports the paper's central claims.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's strength lies in its pragmatic and effective decomposition of the visual servoing problem. Instead of an end-to-end approach, it identifies which components can be transferred (visual features), which can be learned efficiently from unstructured data (the dynamics model), and which require task-specific reward signals (the feature weights).

The formulation of the Q-function in Section 5.2 is particularly insightful. By designing the Q-function approximator `Q(s, u) = φ(s, u)ᵀθ + b` to exactly match the structure of the servoing objective in Equation 3, the authors establish a direct link between the Bellman equation and the parameters of their controller. This allows FQI to directly optimize the controller's objective function to maximize long-term rewards, sidestepping the need to learn a separate, complex policy or value network. The proposed two-step FQI update (Eqs. 5 & 6) is an interesting optimization detail that appears to contribute to the algorithm's stability and speed.

The experimental results are persuasive. The comparison against IBVS with a state-of-the-art tracker (C-COT) is a strong baseline, and outperforming it highlights the benefit of learning task-specific dynamics, even simple ones. More importantly, the sample efficiency comparison with TRPO is stark. The ability to learn a robust controller from just 20 trajectories is a major step towards practical robotic systems that can be quickly repurposed for new tasks. The ablation study on different VGG layers provides useful insight, suggesting an optimal trade-off point between semantic abstraction and spatial precision (`conv4_3`). The generalization results on novel cars further strengthen the claim of robustness.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Clarity on FQI updates:** The description of the FQI algorithm in Section 5.3, particularly the two-step update, is quite dense. A few sentences explaining the intuition behind first solving for a global scaling factor `α` and bias `b` (Eq. 5) before updating the weights `θ` (Eq. 6) would improve readability. Is the motivation to stabilize the updates by decoupling the direction of the weight vector from its magnitude? Clarifying this would strengthen the section.
*   **Limitations of the Bilinear Model:** The paper is built on the assumption that a bilinear model can sufficiently capture the feature dynamics. While this is well-motivated by prior work for camera motion, the discussion could benefit from a more explicit acknowledgment of its limitations. For example, how would it handle scenes with multiple independently moving objects, or highly non-rigid deformations of the target? Acknowledging these boundaries would provide a more complete picture.
*   **Dependence on Initial Dynamics Data:** The dynamics model is trained once on a dataset collected via a hand-coded policy. It would be interesting to know how sensitive the final performance is to the quality of this initial data. Could the system be improved by iteratively collecting data with the learned policy and re-training the dynamics model?
*   **Cost Function in Main Paper:** The definition of the cost function is crucial for any RL problem. While understandable for space reasons, moving the details from Appendix B into the main paper (perhaps in a condensed form) would make the problem setup more self-contained.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  The proposed FQI algorithm uses a two-step update process (Eq. 5 and 6). How does this compare in terms of stability and convergence speed to a more standard FQI implementation that uses a periodically updated target network (i.e., `Q_target`)?
2.  The method relies on a fixed target feature map `y*` derived from a single initial observation `x*`. How robust is the system to significant changes in the target's appearance over a long trajectory that deviate from this initial view (e.g., a 180-degree change in viewpoint)?
3.  Given the impressive results in a high-fidelity simulator, what do you foresee as the main challenges in deploying this system on a physical quadcopter? In particular, how might factors like motion blur, camera auto-exposure, and system latencies affect the performance of the learned feature dynamics and the 10Hz control loop?
