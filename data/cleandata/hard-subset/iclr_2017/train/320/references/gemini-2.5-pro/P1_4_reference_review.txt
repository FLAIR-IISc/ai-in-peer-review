This paper proposes a novel and sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing controllers. The core idea is to combine the strengths of pre-trained deep visual features, a learned predictive dynamics model, and a reinforcement learning algorithm. Specifically, the method leverages features from a pre-trained VGG network to provide a robust visual representation. It then learns a simple, multiscale bilinear model to predict the one-step dynamics of these features in response to robot actions. The key contribution is in how these components are integrated for control: instead of learning a complex, end-to-end policy, the authors formulate a simple servoing policy that minimizes a weighted Euclidean distance between predicted and target features. The crucial feature weights, which determine which visual aspects are important for the task, are learned using a custom, highly efficient Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) algorithm. The authors demonstrate on a challenging simulated car-following task that their method can learn an effective policy using only 20 training trajectories, outperforming classical visual servoing methods and being over two orders of magnitude more sample-efficient than standard deep reinforcement learning approaches.

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  The paper presents a clever and well-motivated approach that effectively addresses the critical challenge of sample efficiency in learning-based robotics. By structuring the problem as learning weights for a simple policy rather than learning a policy from scratch, it makes the learning problem vastly more tractable.
2.  The experimental evaluation is extensive and convincing. The proposed method is compared against a strong and diverse set of baselines, including both classical and modern learning-based approaches. The results clearly and substantially support the primary claims of the paper regarding performance and sample efficiency.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The main strength of this work lies in its elegant problem formulation. The authors correctly identify that while pre-trained features provide a powerful representation and a simple dynamics model can be learned efficiently, the challenge is in identifying which of the many high-dimensional features are relevant to the task at hand. Their solution—to learn weights over feature channels and scales using reinforcement learning—is insightful.

The technical implementation of this idea is particularly strong. By designing a Q-function approximator that is linear in the feature weights and whose greedy minimization directly corresponds to the desired servoing policy (Equation 3), the authors create a direct link between long-term value (the Q-function) and the one-step control action. This enables the use of a very sample-efficient FQI procedure. The results speak for themselves: Figure 6 shows a dramatic improvement over a standard deep RL algorithm (TRPO) which, despite using over 100 times more data, fails to match the performance of the proposed FQI-based method. This is a significant result that highlights the power of incorporating domain knowledge and structure into the learning problem.

The empirical comparisons are thorough and fair. The paper not only shows improvement over pixel-based methods and end-to-end RL but also against strong classical baselines. The comparison against Image-Based Visual Servoing (IBVS) using a state-of-the-art visual tracker (C-COT) is particularly compelling. The proposed method not only achieves better performance but is also an order of magnitude faster at runtime, which is a crucial advantage for practical robotics applications. The ablation study on different VGG feature layers provides useful insights into which level of abstraction is most suitable for this type of control task.

Finally, the public release of the simulation environment and the code is a valuable contribution to the community, promoting reproducibility and enabling future research to build upon this work.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified or expanded upon to further improve it.

*   **Dynamics Model Training Data:** The paper states that the data for training the bilinear dynamics model was collected using a "hand-coded policy." This is a crucial detail. The quality and diversity of this data could significantly impact the learned model's ability to generalize to the states encountered by the final learned policy. It would be beneficial to provide more details on this policy and discuss the sensitivity of the overall system to this data collection strategy. For instance, would a purely random exploration policy suffice?
*   **Use of Ground Truth in Cost Function:** Appendix B reveals that the cost function used for RL training relies on the ground truth 3D position of the target car. While the final policy at execution time is purely visual, the learning signal itself is not. This is a reasonable choice for training in simulation, but it should be stated more explicitly in the main text (e.g., in Section 5) for clarity, as it is an important aspect of the experimental setup.
*   **FQI Algorithm Details:** The two-step update within the FQI loop (solving for scaling factor α and bias `b` separately in Eq. 5 before updating θ in Eq. 6) is an interesting variant. The paper mentions this is done to "speed up the optimization," but a bit more intuition or justification for this specific procedure would strengthen the presentation in Section 5.3.
*   **Real-World Transfer:** The work is evaluated exclusively in simulation. A short discussion on the potential challenges and prospects for transferring this method to a real robotic platform would be a valuable addition. For example, how robust would the simple bilinear dynamics model be to unmodeled physical effects or sensor noise in the real world?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the data collection process for the dynamics model? How critical is the specific hand-coded policy, and have you investigated how performance changes if this data is collected using a different strategy, such as a purely random policy?
2.  Your RL cost function relies on privileged, ground-truth information about the target's 3D position. Have you considered or experimented with a self-supervised approach where the cost is derived directly from the visual features, for instance, using the feature distance `||y* - y_t||` as a reward signal? How do you think this would impact the learning process and final performance?
3.  The proposed bilinear model is used for one-step prediction. Could you provide some insight into its performance on multi-step prediction? While not used by the final policy, understanding how quickly prediction error accumulates could help characterize the learned model's capabilities and limitations.
