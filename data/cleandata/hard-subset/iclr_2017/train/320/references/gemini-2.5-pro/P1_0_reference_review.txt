### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing for target following, aiming to develop a method that is both robust to visual distractors and highly sample-efficient. The authors propose a novel approach that synergistically combines three key components: (1) pre-trained deep visual features from a VGG network to provide a rich, semi-invariant representation of the visual scene; (2) a learned, multiscale bilinear dynamics model that predicts how these features will change in response to robot actions; and (3) a sample-efficient reinforcement learning algorithm based on Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) to learn optimal weightings for the features.

The core contribution is the formulation of the learning problem. Instead of learning a complex, end-to-end policy, the method uses a simple, structured servoing policy that minimizes a weighted Euclidean distance between predicted and target features. The reinforcement learning is then cleverly used only to learn the optimal weights for this distance metric. This constraint on the policy space allows for a Q-function that is linear in the weights to be learned, leading to a highly efficient and stable FQI procedure. The authors demonstrate their method on a challenging simulated quadcopter car-following task, showing that it can learn an effective policy from just 20 training trajectories. The results show substantial improvements in performance and a more than two-orders-of-magnitude improvement in sample efficiency compared to standard deep reinforcement learning (TRPO) and classical visual servoing baselines.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Novel and Effective Formulation:** The paper's main strength lies in its intelligent problem formulation. By constraining the policy class and using RL to learn the weights of a structured objective function rather than a generic policy network, the authors create a learning problem that is vastly more sample-efficient. The insight that this specific policy structure leads to a linear Q-function approximator, enabling a very efficient FQI update, is elegant and highly effective.

2.  **Compelling Empirical Results and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The paper provides strong evidence for its claims by comparing against a comprehensive set of relevant baselines, including pixel-based control, classical IBVS with hand-crafted features (ORB), state-of-the-art trackers (C-COT), and model-free deep RL (TRPO). The demonstrated sample efficiency improvement (learning from 20 trajectories vs. 500+ for TRPO) is significant and directly addresses a major bottleneck in applying deep RL to robotics. The inclusion of tests on both seen and novel cars effectively demonstrates the generalization capabilities of the learned system.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The proposed method represents a well-motivated middle ground between classical robotics and modern deep learning. The paper correctly identifies the limitations of both: classical visual servoing often relies on brittle, hand-engineered features, while end-to-end deep RL is notoriously data-hungry, making it impractical for tasks requiring quick adaptation. This work successfully leverages the strengths of each paradigm: the powerful representations from pre-trained CNNs, the predictive power of a simple learned dynamics model, and the optimization framework of RL.

The technical approach is sound. The use of pre-trained VGG features is a standard and effective technique for transfer learning in vision tasks. The choice of a bilinear model for feature dynamics is well-justified by prior work and offers a good trade-off between expressive power and model complexity, particularly since it allows for an exact linearization and efficient computation of the Jacobian (as detailed in Appendix A).

The most significant contribution is the RL formulation. By defining the policy as `argmin_u Q(s,u)` where `Q(s,u)` is linear in learnable feature weights `θ` (Section 5.2), the Bellman error minimization in FQI becomes a simple regularized least-squares problem. This is a stark contrast to the difficult non-convex optimization required for training deep Q-networks or policy gradients. This formulation is the primary driver behind the method's remarkable sample efficiency. The experiments in Section 6.3 and Figure 6 clearly illustrate this advantage. While TRPO (a state-of-the-art policy gradient method) struggles to make progress after 2 sampling iterations, the proposed FQI method achieves near-optimal performance. Even after 50 iterations (over 25x more data), TRPO still underperforms the FQI-based approach.

The paper also performs valuable ablation studies, such as comparing different VGG feature layers (Section 6.2, Figure 5). This analysis provides useful insights, suggesting that intermediate-level features (e.g., `conv4_3`) offer the best balance between semantic information and spatial resolution for this control task, while the most abstract features (`conv5_3`) may be too spatially invariant. These details strengthen the paper's conclusions and provide guidance for future work.

### 4. Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The paper is very well-written and the experiments are strong. The following points are intended as constructive feedback to further improve the paper and clarify some aspects.

*   **Clarity on Q-function Structure:** The link between the servoing policy in Equation (3) and the Q-function approximator in Section 5.2 is the crux of the method. While correct, it could be made more explicit for the reader. A brief sentence explaining that "we design a Q-function approximator whose greedy action selection *exactly* recovers our desired servoing policy" would help solidify this connection.

*   **Role of the Cost Function:** The cost function used for RL (Appendix B) relies on ground-truth 3D positions of the car, which are available in simulation. This is a potential weakness for real-world deployment. It would be beneficial to add a short discussion on how this method could be adapted to real-world scenarios where such a cost function is not available. Would it be possible to use a purely image-based cost, for example, based on the success of a tracker or a learned reward function?

*   **Bias in Dynamics Model Training:** The visual dynamics model is trained on a dataset collected by a hand-coded policy. This can introduce a distributional bias, as the model may not be accurate for state-action pairs far from the data-collection policy's distribution. Could the authors comment on whether this was an issue, and if an iterative approach (alternating between policy improvement and data collection/dynamics model refitting) might yield further benefits?

*   **FQI Convergence Trick:** The intermediate update step for `α` and `b` in Equation (5) is an interesting heuristic to stabilize and accelerate training. Could the authors provide a bit more intuition on why solving for a single scaling factor for the target Q-function is effective? Does it act as a form of trust region or adaptive learning rate for the Q-values themselves?

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a novel, well-motivated, and thoroughly evaluated approach that makes a significant contribution to sample-efficient learning for robotic control.
