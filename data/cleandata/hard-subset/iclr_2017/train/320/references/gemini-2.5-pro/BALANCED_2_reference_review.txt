### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel and highly sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing policies for target following tasks. The core idea is to cleverly combine the strengths of three distinct areas: (1) using robust visual features from a large, pre-trained convolutional neural network (VGG) to handle visual variations; (2) learning a simple, task-agnostic bilinear dynamics model to predict how these features change in response to robot actions; and (3) using a reinforcement learning algorithm to learn a task-specific policy. The key contribution lies in the formulation of the policy and the learning algorithm. The policy is a simple, greedy servoing controller that minimizes a weighted distance between predicted features and target features. The authors then frame the learning of these crucial feature weights as a Q-learning problem and develop a customized, computationally efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm to solve it. This approach effectively learns a distance metric in the feature space that is optimized for long-term task performance. The method is evaluated on a challenging synthetic car-following benchmark, where it demonstrates remarkable sample efficiency—learning an effective policy from just 20 trajectories—and significantly outperforms classical visual servoing techniques and standard model-free deep reinforcement learning approaches.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Novel and Effective Hybrid Approach:** The paper proposes a well-motivated and elegant method that synergistically combines pre-trained representations, model-based control, and model-free reinforcement learning. This hybrid design directly addresses the primary weaknesses of its constituent parts: it avoids the massive data requirements of end-to-end deep RL and the feature engineering and brittleness of classical visual servoing. The formulation of learning feature weights via a structured Q-function is the central, novel insight that makes this combination particularly effective.

2.  **Exceptional Sample Efficiency and Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are convincing and highlight the primary strength of the proposed method: its sample efficiency. Learning a competent visuomotor policy from only 20 trajectories is a significant result that is highly relevant for practical robotics applications where data collection is expensive. The paper supports this claim with a thorough empirical study that shows improvements of over two orders of magnitude in sample complexity compared to a strong deep RL baseline (TRPO), while also outperforming it and other relevant classical methods in final performance. The public release of the simulation environment and code further strengthens the contribution.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper has several notable strengths:

*   **Clarity and Motivation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The problem is clearly defined, and the motivation for each component of the proposed solution is thoroughly explained. The authors do an excellent job of placing their work within the context of existing literature, highlighting the gaps they aim to fill.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The design of the system is technically sound. Using pre-trained VGG features is a standard but effective choice. The choice of a bilinear dynamics model is well-justified by prior work and its linear relationship with the control inputs is cleverly exploited to create an analytically solvable and efficient policy. The core contribution—the custom FQI algorithm for learning feature weights—is well-designed for the chosen Q-function parameterization and proves highly effective.
*   **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive. The authors compare against a strong and diverse set of baselines, including different feature representations (pixels, various VGG layers), alternative learning algorithms (CEM, TRPO), end-to-end RL policies, and classical IBVS methods (using both ORB features and a state-of-the-art tracker). The inclusion of generalization tests on novel cars adds rigor to the evaluation. The results consistently and clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach, particularly in the low-data regime.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors have provided links to their code and the simulation environment, which is commendable and enables the community to build upon this work.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions for improvement and are not critical to the acceptance decision.

*   **Discussion on Real-World Transfer:** The experiments are conducted entirely in simulation. While the simulation is visually complex, a discussion on the challenges and potential solutions for transferring this method to a real robotic platform would strengthen the paper. For instance, how might the sim-to-real gap in visual appearance affect the fixed VGG features? Would the dynamics model, trained on data from a hand-coded policy, be robust enough to minor physical effects not present in the simulation?
*   **Reliance on Privileged Information for Training:** The cost function used for RL training (Appendix B) relies on the ground truth 3D position of the target vehicle. This is a common and acceptable practice in simulation, but it is an important detail. It would be beneficial to explicitly state in the main paper that this privileged information is required during the RL training phase (though not for execution) and briefly discuss how a cost or reward signal might be obtained in a real-world scenario where such ground truth is unavailable (e.g., using a learned reward model or a different proxy).
*   **Ablation of Dynamics Model:** The bilinear model is a key component. An ablation study comparing it to simpler (e.g., linear) or more complex (e.g., MLP-based) dynamics models could provide further insight into the method. This would help disentangle the performance gains from the specific choice of dynamics model versus the gains from the FQI-based feature weighting scheme.

### Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following questions in their rebuttal:

1.  **Sensitivity to Dynamics Model Accuracy:** How crucial is the accuracy of the learned dynamics model to the final policy's performance? Does the FQI algorithm show an ability to compensate for prediction errors by learning to down-weight features that are predicted less accurately?
2.  **Role of Multiscale Features:** The method utilizes a multiscale feature representation. Could you provide any analysis or intuition regarding the learned weights `w` across the different scales? For example, does the policy learn to rely more on coarse, global features when far from the target and fine-grained features when close?
3.  **Exploration Strategy:** The data collection for FQI uses the current deterministic policy plus Gaussian noise. Did you find this simple exploration strategy to be sufficient? How sensitive is the method's performance to the variance of the exploration noise, and did you experiment with more sophisticated exploration schemes?
4.  **Computational Cost Analysis:** You note that your method is faster at inference than the tracker-based baseline. Could you provide a more holistic comparison of the computational trade-offs, including the total training time for your method (dynamics model training + FQI) versus the end-to-end TRPO baseline, which requires significantly more interaction time but may have a different offline computation profile?
