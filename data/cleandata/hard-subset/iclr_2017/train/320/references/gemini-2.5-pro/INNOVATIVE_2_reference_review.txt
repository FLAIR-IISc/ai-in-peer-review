### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel and highly sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing policies. The core idea is a clever synthesis of three components: (1) leveraging robust visual features from a pre-trained VGG network to handle appearance variation, (2) learning a simple, task-agnostic bilinear model to predict the dynamics of these features in response to robot actions, and (3) using a specialized fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn a weighting over the feature channels and scales. This reinforcement learning component is the key contribution, as it learns a task-specific distance metric that allows the simple one-step predictive controller to achieve good long-horizon performance. The method effectively learns which visual features are most relevant for tracking a specific target, enabling it to ignore distractors. The authors demonstrate their approach on a complex simulated quadcopter car-following task, showing that it can learn an effective policy with only 20 trajectories, drastically outperforming standard deep RL methods in sample efficiency and surpassing classical visual servoing techniques.

### **Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that I am enthusiastic about accepting. My primary reasons are:
1.  **Significant Conceptual Novelty:** The paper introduces a bold and elegant framework that bridges classical model-based visual servoing with modern deep learning and reinforcement learning. The idea of using RL not to learn a policy from scratch, but to learn an optimal *weighting* for a feature-based predictive controller is a powerful new direction for sample-efficient robotics.
2.  **Exceptional Sample Efficiency:** The empirical results are striking. Achieving strong performance on a complex task with just 20 trajectories is a major achievement and provides compelling evidence for the efficacy of the proposed structured approach. The >100x improvement in sample efficiency over a strong model-free baseline like TRPO is a standout result that highlights the value of the paper's core ideas.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The strength of this work lies in its intelligent decomposition of the visual servoing problem. Instead of treating it as a monolithic, end-to-end learning problem that requires massive amounts of data, the authors leverage prior knowledge in a principled way.

First, using pre-trained VGG features is a well-motivated choice to provide a rich, semi-invariant representation without requiring task-specific feature learning. The modification to use dilated convolutions to preserve spatial resolution is a thoughtful detail that is critical for servoing.

Second, the choice of a bilinear dynamics model is a key simplifying assumption that makes the approach tractable. While more complex video prediction models exist, they are notoriously data-hungry. By assuming locally-linear dynamics in the feature space, the authors can learn a predictive model from a small dataset. This model is then sufficient for the one-step lookahead required by the servoing policy.

The most novel and impactful contribution, however, is the FQI-based feature weighting. The insight that the servoing objective is a weighted distance metric, and that these weights can be optimized to maximize long-term reward, is excellent. The authors construct a Q-function approximator whose structure directly mirrors the policy's objective function. This leads to an elegant and efficient FQI update rule that can directly solve for the optimal feature weights. This formulation is the primary driver of the method's remarkable sample efficiency, as it provides a much stronger learning signal and a more constrained optimization problem than end-to-end policy gradient methods.

The experiments convincingly support the paper's claims. The ablation study on different VGG layers is informative, and the comparison against a wide range of baselines—including pixel-based control, classical IBVS with ORB features, a state-of-the-art tracker, and end-to-end TRPO—is thorough. The proposed method consistently outperforms these alternatives, often by a large margin, especially when considering the amount of training data required. The ability of the learned policy to generalize to novel cars further strengthens the results.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions**

While I am strongly in favor of acceptance, the following points could help improve the paper further and are worth considering for a final version or future work. These are suggestions, not preconditions for acceptance.

*   **Real-World Applicability:** The paper's primary limitation is its evaluation in simulation only. While the simulation is non-trivial, a simple real-world demonstration, even on a tabletop robot arm, would significantly elevate the impact and convincingly demonstrate that the approach can handle real-world sensor noise and dynamics.
*   **Limitations of the Bilinear Model:** The bilinear dynamics model is a core assumption. It would be beneficial to include a short discussion on its potential limitations. In what scenarios might it fail? For instance, would it struggle with highly non-rigid objects, abrupt lighting changes, or dynamics that are not well-approximated as linear in the feature space?
*   **Cost Function:** The cost function used for RL (Appendix B) relies on the ground truth 3D position of the target, which may not be available in many real-world scenarios. Could the authors comment on how the framework might be adapted if only a weaker reward signal is available, such as one derived directly from images (e.g., "is the target centered and at the right scale?")?
*   **FQI Update Intuition:** The two-step update in the FQI algorithm (solving for scale `α` and bias `b` before updating the weight vector `θ`) is interesting. Could you provide more intuition on why this decomposition was chosen over a more standard FQI update? Does it improve stability or speed up convergence in practice?
