This paper presents a method for learning visual servoing controllers. The core idea is to combine visual features from a pre-trained convolutional neural network (VGG) with a learned, local dynamics model and a reinforcement learning algorithm. Specifically, the method learns a bilinear model to predict the one-step evolution of VGG feature maps given a control action. The servoing policy then greedily chooses the action that minimizes a weighted Euclidean distance between the predicted features and a target feature map. The main contribution lies in using a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn the weights for different feature channels and scales, which is intended to make the greedy one-step policy optimal for the long-horizon task. The authors demonstrate their approach on a simulated quadcopter car-following task, claiming substantial improvements in sample efficiency and final performance over both classical visual servoing methods and standard model-free deep reinforcement learning.

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The primary methodological novelty, a bespoke Fitted Q-Iteration algorithm, is not sufficiently justified or validated. The specific design choices, particularly the two-step update procedure, lack ablation studies to demonstrate their necessity or advantage over more standard techniques.
2.  The experimental comparison to baseline methods, while extensive, contains weaknesses that may overstate the proposed method's relative performance. The model-free deep RL baselines appear undertuned, and the comparison with classical methods in the main paper omits stronger variants of those baselines that are only discussed in the appendix.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper's approach of combining pre-trained features with a simple learned dynamics model is a reasonable direction. However, the rigor of the evaluation and the justification for the novel components do not meet the high standards required for publication.

My primary concern is with the validation of the proposed FQI algorithm in Section 5.3. The authors propose a non-standard, two-step update process (Eq. 5 and 6) where they first solve for a scaling factor `alpha` and bias `b` before updating the primary weights `theta`. This is motivated by a desire to "speed up the optimization," but this claim is not substantiated. There is no ablation study comparing this two-step update to a more conventional FQI update that directly optimizes the Bellman error for `theta` and `b` in a single step. Without this comparison, it is impossible to assess whether this added complexity is beneficial or even necessary. Is the performance of a standard FQI approach significantly worse? The stability and convergence of FQI are known to be sensitive to the choice of function approximator and hyperparameters; the introduction of a novel update scheme requires a more thorough theoretical or empirical justification than is provided.

Secondly, the strength of the paper's claims rests heavily on the empirical comparisons in Section 6, but these comparisons raise questions. The claim of being ">100x" more sample efficient than TRPO is a strong one. However, the performance of the TRPO baselines seems quite poor. Deep RL methods are notoriously difficult to tune, and the provided results are not sufficient to be confident that the TRPO baseline represents a fair and strong competitor. For example, the end-to-end CNN policy trained with TRPO achieves a cost of ~1700 after 500 iterations, which is still substantially worse than several of the authors' variants. It is plausible that with more extensive tuning of architectures and hyperparameters, the performance of model-free methods could be improved, narrowing the reported efficiency gap.

Furthermore, the comparison to classical Image-Based Visual Servoing (IBVS) is not presented as strongly as it could be. The main paper compares against IBVS using ORB features (which performs poorly) and a C-COT tracker (which performs well, but not as well as the proposed method). A critical piece of information is that these baselines are provided with ground-truth depth, a significant advantage. Even so, the authors note that the performance gap is partly due to the classical model's lack of target dynamics. The appendix (Table 5) then shows that providing ground truth car dynamics to the IBVS method dramatically improves its performance, making it highly competitive with the proposed method. A truly rigorous comparison would place this stronger, dynamics-aware classical baseline in the main results table, as it represents a more established approach for servoing to a moving target. By omitting it from the main text, the paper may give an inflated sense of its superiority over established techniques.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

*   The claim that the bilinear dynamics model can handle "moving objects, occlusions, and other complex phenomena" seems overstated. A bilinear model is fundamentally a linear system whose matrices are selected by the state. While it can model some action-dependent changes, its ability to represent complex, non-linear events like occlusions is limited. The method's robustness more likely stems from the invariance of the VGG features and the RL-tuned weighting that learns to ignore distractors, rather than from the predictive power of the dynamics model itself. The manuscript would be stronger if these claims were toned down or better supported.
*   The work is conducted entirely in simulation. While the simulation environment is reasonably complex, a discussion of potential challenges for real-world deployment would be welcome. For example, how would unmodeled dynamics, camera noise, or the sim-to-real gap in visual features affect the performance?
*   The initial dynamics model is trained on data collected from a hand-coded policy. The quality of this data could be critical. An analysis of the sensitivity of the final performance to the quality of this initial data-collection policy would be valuable.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide an ablation study that compares your proposed two-step FQI update scheme with a standard FQI implementation? This would help clarify the motivation and necessity for your novel algorithm.
2.  The appendix shows that an IBVS baseline with ground truth car dynamics is a very strong competitor. Could you elaborate on why this more appropriate classical baseline was not featured in the main paper's results and how you view your method's performance in light of this result?
3.  Could you provide more detail on the tuning of the TRPO baselines? Given their relatively poor performance, it would be helpful to understand what architectures and hyperparameters were explored to ensure the sample efficiency comparison is on solid ground.
