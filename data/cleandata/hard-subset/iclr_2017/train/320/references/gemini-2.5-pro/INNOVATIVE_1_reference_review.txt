### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel and highly sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing controllers. The core idea is to combine the power of pre-trained deep visual features (from VGG) with a simple, learned dynamics model and a specialized reinforcement learning algorithm. Instead of learning a complex, end-to-end policy, the authors propose a simple servoing policy that minimizes the weighted Euclidean distance between the predicted next-step features and the target features. The key contributions are twofold: (1) learning a simple bilinear dynamics model directly in the VGG feature space to predict the effect of actions, and (2) a specialized Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) algorithm that efficiently learns the optimal weights for different feature channels and scales. This FQI formulation is particularly elegant, as the Q-function is designed to match the structure of the servoing policy, enabling efficient optimization. The method is evaluated on a challenging simulated car-following task, where it demonstrates the ability to learn an effective policy from only 20 trajectories, outperforming standard deep RL methods by over two orders of magnitude in sample efficiency and also surpassing classical visual servoing baselines.

### **Decision & Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that presents a bold and compelling vision for sample-efficient robotic learning. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The paper introduces a significant new direction by intelligently decomposing the visual servoing problem. Instead of monolithic end-to-end learning, it leverages pre-trained features for perception, learns a simple local model for dynamics, and uses a highly efficient RL algorithm to learn a task-specific distance metric. The formulation of the policy and the corresponding Q-function is clever and principled, moving beyond brute-force function approximation.
2.  **Exceptional Sample Efficiency:** The demonstrated ability to learn a robust controller from just 20 trajectories is a dramatic improvement over standard methods and addresses one of the most significant barriers to applying deep RL in real-world robotics. The empirical results, showing a >100x improvement in data requirements over a strong baseline like TRPO, are striking and highlight the power of the proposed approach.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The strength of this work lies in its thoughtful synthesis of ideas from computer vision, dynamics modeling, and reinforcement learning. It eschews the common trend of simply applying larger models to more data. Instead, it injects structural priors that make learning tractable and efficient.

The core idea—that the policy should minimize a weighted distance in a powerful, pre-trained feature space—is very appealing. The subsequent steps follow logically: if the policy is defined this way, we need a model to predict `f(y,u)`, and we need a way to find the right weights `w`. The choice of a bilinear model for the dynamics is pragmatic; it is simple enough to be learned from little data while being expressive enough for the task, and its linear dependence on the control `u` makes the policy optimization tractable.

The most significant technical contribution is the FQI-based algorithm for learning the feature weights. By designing the Q-function approximator to mirror the policy's objective function, the authors create a tight link between value-function estimation and policy improvement. This leads to a sample-efficient learning rule that is far more effective than general-purpose policy gradient methods like TRPO for this problem structure. The results in Figure 6 are a powerful testament to this: the proposed method achieves a strong result with only 2 sampling iterations (20 trajectories), while TRPO barely improves and requires over 50 iterations (and far more samples per iteration) to reach a substantially worse result.

While the experiments are conducted in simulation, the environment is reasonably complex, featuring distractors, occlusions, and appearance variations. The fact that the method outperforms strong baselines like IBVS using a state-of-the-art tracker (C-COT)—especially when the baseline is given ground-truth depth information—further validates the strength of learning dynamics and feature relevance. For a paper focused on a novel learning methodology, the lack of real-world experiments is an acceptable limitation, as the core claims about sample efficiency and the validity of the framework are convincingly supported by the simulated results.

### **Additional Feedback & Questions**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

**Feedback:**
*   **Ablation on VGG modification:** The paper mentions modifying the VGG network with dilated convolutions to preserve spatial information, which is a sensible choice. It would be interesting to see an ablation or a brief comment on how a standard, off-the-shelf VGG network (with max-pooling) would have performed. This would help quantify the importance of this architectural modification.
*   **Intuition on Feature Layers:** The result that mid-level features (`conv4_3`) outperform both shallower (`pixels`) and deeper (`conv5_3`) features is insightful. The paper hypothesizes that `conv5_3` is too spatially invariant. This intuition could be strengthened, perhaps by visualizing the learned weights `w` to see if the model learns to ignore channels known to be more abstract and focus on those encoding more precise spatial relationships.
*   **Cost Function:** The cost function in Appendix B relies on ground-truth 3D position information from the simulator. This is a common practice for training in simulation, but it would strengthen the paper to briefly discuss how this approach could be adapted to real-world scenarios where such dense, privileged information is unavailable (e.g., using a cost derived from human demonstrations, sparse rewards, or a learned reward function).

**Questions for the Authors:**
1.  The bilinear dynamics models were trained on data from a hand-coded policy. How sensitive is the performance of the final servoing policy to the quality and coverage of this initial dataset? For instance, would a dynamics model trained on data from a random exploration policy be sufficient?
2.  The two-step update in the FQI inner loop (solving for a scaling factor `α` and bias `b` separately before updating `θ`) is an interesting design choice. Could you provide more intuition on why this decomposition is beneficial for the stability or speed of convergence compared to a more standard FQI update that solves for all parameters jointly?
3.  Could you clarify the computational cost of the method at runtime? The paper notes that C-COT runs at 1Hz while the proposed method runs at 16Hz. Is the forward pass through the VGG network the main bottleneck, and have you considered replacing VGG with a more lightweight, modern backbone to potentially achieve real-time performance?
