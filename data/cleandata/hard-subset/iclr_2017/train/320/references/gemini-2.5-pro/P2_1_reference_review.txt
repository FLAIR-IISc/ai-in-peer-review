### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper addresses the classic robotics problem of visual servoing, specifically for target following tasks. The authors propose a novel and sample-efficient method that combines three key ideas: (1) using robust visual features from a large, pre-trained convolutional neural network (VGG) to handle visual variations; (2) learning a simple, task-agnostic bilinear model to predict the one-step dynamics of these features in response to robot actions; and (3) using a specialized fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn a task-specific weighting over the features and control inputs. The core insight is that by structuring the servoing policy as a simple minimization of weighted, predicted feature error, the corresponding Q-function can be represented as a linear function of these weights. This allows the FQI procedure to solve for the optimal weights efficiently via a simple least-squares regression, leading to remarkable sample efficiency. The method is evaluated on a challenging synthetic car-following task, where it demonstrates significant improvements in performance and a >100x improvement in sample efficiency over standard model-free deep RL methods, while also outperforming classical visual servoing baselines.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation for Sample-Efficient Learning:** The paper's main strength lies in its elegant formulation that exploits the problem structure to achieve high sample efficiency. Instead of using a generic, black-box policy and value function, the authors define a simple, interpretable servoing policy and then design a Q-function approximator that perfectly matches this policy's structure. This turns the difficult problem of policy learning into a much simpler, data-efficient problem of learning a set of linear weights. This combination of pre-trained features, a simple learned dynamics model, and a structured RL approach is a compelling and effective method for a practical robotics problem.

2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental results are thorough and convincingly support the paper's claims. The authors provide extensive comparisons against a well-chosen set of baselines, including pixel-based methods, classical hand-engineered feature-based visual servoing (IBVS), a state-of-the-art tracker-based method, and standard model-free deep RL (TRPO). The results clearly show that the proposed method not only achieves superior performance but does so with vastly fewer training samples (20 trajectories vs. thousands for TRPO), highlighting the practical significance of their approach for tasks where data collection is expensive. The inclusion of generalization tests on novel cars further strengthens the evaluation.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and technically sound. The problem statement correctly identifies a key limitation of both classical visual servoing (brittleness, manual engineering) and modern deep RL (poor sample efficiency). The proposed solution is a well-reasoned synthesis of ideas from computer vision, dynamics modeling, and reinforcement learning.

The technical contribution is significant. The bilinear dynamics model is simple yet sufficient for the one-step prediction required by the servoing policy. The key innovation is in Section 5, where the Q-function is defined as a linear combination of the squared feature errors (Equation in 5.2). This allows the Bellman error minimization in the FQI algorithm to be framed as a regularized linear least-squares problem (Equations 5 and 6), which is stable and can be solved efficiently with very little data. This is a much more direct and data-efficient approach than backpropagating gradients through a complex neural network policy, as demonstrated by the stark contrast in performance with TRPO in Figure 6.

The experimental design is rigorous. The use of a publicly available simulator and the release of their own code promotes reproducibility. The ablation-like study in Figure 5, which compares different layers of VGG features, provides valuable insight into which representations are most suitable for this task and lends credence to their hypothesis about the trade-off between semantic abstraction and spatial precision. The comparison against C-COT, a strong tracker-based baseline, is particularly important, as it shows the proposed method is competitive with or superior to methods that are explicitly designed for tracking, while also being computationally faster.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, the following points could help improve it further:

1.  **Clarity on FQI Updates:** The two-step update process in Section 5.3 using the `(k-1/2)` notation is functional but could be explained more intuitively. A brief sentence motivating *why* this two-step process (first solving for a global scaling `α` and bias `b`, then refining `θ`) is beneficial—presumably for stability or faster convergence—would improve clarity.
2.  **Discussion on Sim-to-Real Transfer:** The work is evaluated exclusively in simulation. While this is appropriate for demonstrating the core method, a short discussion in Section 7 on the potential challenges and prospects for transferring this approach to a real robotic system would be valuable. For instance, how might unmodeled dynamics, camera noise, or motion blur affect the learned bilinear model?
3.  **Cost Function Dependency:** The cost function in Appendix B relies on ground-truth 3D positions of the target car. This is an oracle signal unavailable in most real-world scenarios. While perfectly acceptable for training in a simulator, the paper would be more complete if it discussed how this method might be adapted for real-world training, perhaps using a proxy cost from a learned reward model or self-supervision.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  The dynamics model is trained on a dataset generated by a "hand-coded policy." How sensitive is the performance of the final learned servoing policy to the quality and coverage of the data generated by this initial policy? Did you experiment with different data-gathering policies, and did it have a significant impact?
2.  Your hypothesis for the poor performance of VGG `conv5_3` features is that they are "too spatially invariant." This is plausible. Could you provide any further qualitative or quantitative evidence for this? For instance, would visualizing the feature weights `w` learned by FQI show that the model learns to ignore these deeper features?
3.  The bilinear dynamics model is trained once and then fixed. Have you considered fine-tuning the VGG feature extractor and/or the dynamics model jointly with the FQI weight updates? Do you foresee any benefits or stability challenges with such an end-to-end approach, given your focus on sample efficiency?
