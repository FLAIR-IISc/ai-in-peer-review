### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel and sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing policies, demonstrated on a challenging simulated car-following task. The approach effectively combines three key components: (1) pre-trained convolutional neural network features (from VGG) to provide a robust visual representation that is invariant to moderate visual distractors; (2) a learned bilinear model that predicts the short-term dynamics of these features in response to robot actions; and (3) a reinforcement learning algorithm based on fitted Q-iteration (FQI) that learns to weight the different feature channels and scales. This weighting allows the policy to focus on task-relevant information and ignore distractors. The final control policy is simple and efficient, seeking to minimize the weighted Euclidean distance between the predicted features of the next state and a target feature representation. The central claims are that this combination enables the learning of an effective visual servo from a very small amount of data (20 trajectories) and that it significantly outperforms both classical visual servoing techniques and standard model-free deep reinforcement learning methods in terms of performance and sample efficiency.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Sound Method:** The paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound framework that intelligently combines pre-trained representations, model-based control, and reinforcement learning. The core technical contribution—structuring the Q-function approximator to mirror the servoing policy's objective function, enabling a direct and efficient FQI learning procedure—is both novel and effective.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The claims of high sample efficiency and superior performance are convincingly supported by extensive experiments. The method demonstrates a significant performance gain over well-chosen baselines, including end-to-end deep RL and classical feature-based servoing, while requiring over two orders of magnitude less training data than standard deep RL approaches.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper excels in its clarity, technical soundness, and the novelty of its approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The problem is clearly defined, the proposed method is broken down into its constituent parts (features, dynamics, RL), and each part is explained with sufficient detail. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly, allowing for a straightforward assessment of the paper's claims.

**Technical Soundness:** The methodology is sound. The use of a bilinear model for dynamics is a reasonable choice, balancing expressive power with simplicity, which in turn allows for an exact linearization and an efficient analytical solution for the optimal action (Equation 3). The main technical insight is the formulation of the Q-function in Section 5.2. By designing the Q-function approximator to be linear in a set of weights applied to the squared feature errors, the greedy policy `argmin_u Q(s, u)` exactly recovers the desired servoing policy. This formulation enables the use of a computationally efficient FQI algorithm where the Bellman error minimization becomes a linear least-squares problem, contributing directly to the method's sample efficiency.

**Novelty and Significance:** While the individual components (pre-trained features, learned dynamics, FQI) are not new, their synthesis into a cohesive framework for sample-efficient visual servoing is novel and significant. The approach provides a compelling alternative to purely model-free deep RL methods, which are often impractical for robotics applications due to their high data requirements. By demonstrating that a complex visuomotor skill can be learned from only 20 trajectories, this work presents a major step towards making learned control policies more practical and adaptable for real-world robotic systems. The empirical results are strong: the method not only outperforms a TRPO baseline that uses 100 times more data but also surpasses classical methods that are given access to ground-truth information like feature depth. This highlights the power of combining rich, learned feature representations with task-specific fine-tuning.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions for strengthening the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Analysis of Learned Weights:** The paper states that learning feature weights helps distinguish the target from distractors. This claim could be strengthened with a qualitative analysis. For instance, visualizing some of the learned weights `w` or the resulting weighted feature-error maps could provide valuable insight into which features the policy learns to attend to.
*   **Ablation on Feature Extractor:** The authors use a modified VGG network with dilated convolutions to maintain spatial resolution. A brief comment or ablation study on the importance of this modification versus using a standard off-the-shelf VGG network would be informative for readers looking to apply this method.
*   **Comparison to IBVS with Car Dynamics:** In Appendix C.5, an experiment is run where classical IBVS is provided with ground-truth car dynamics. The proposed method still appears to outperform this strong baseline, especially on novel cars. Highlighting this result more prominently in the main text would further underscore the benefit of the learned feature representation over hand-designed keypoints, even when the classical method has an informational advantage.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

Your response to these questions would help clarify some aspects of the work and increase confidence in the assessment.

1.  The dynamics model is trained on data from a hand-coded policy. How sensitive is the final performance to the data-gathering policy used for training the dynamics? For example, would a purely random exploration policy yield a dynamics model of sufficient quality for the servoing task?
2.  The FQI algorithm (Algorithm 1) uses a two-step update within each iteration (lines 5-7), first solving for scaling and bias parameters (`alpha`, `b`) and then updating the weights `theta`. What is the motivation for this two-step process compared to a standard FQI update where the new `theta` is learned directly to minimize the Bellman error with a fixed target network?
3.  The cost function used for RL training relies on the ground-truth 3D position of the target car (Appendix B). How would this approach be adapted for a real-world scenario where this ground truth is not available? Could the RL objective be formulated directly in feature space (e.g., using `||y_t - y*||^2` as the cost), or would this create unforeseen challenges?
4.  In the TRPO baseline (Section 6.3), the policy network includes a matrix inverse operation. Did you encounter any numerical stability issues when training this network, and were any specific techniques required to ensure stable learning?
