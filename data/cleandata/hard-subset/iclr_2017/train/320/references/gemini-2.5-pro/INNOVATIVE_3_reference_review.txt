### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel and highly sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing controllers. The core idea is to synergistically combine three key components: (1) robust visual features extracted from a pre-trained deep network (VGG), (2) a simple, learned bilinear model to predict the one-step dynamics of these features, and (3) a specialized fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn a weighting over the features, effectively teaching the controller which parts of the visual input are relevant to the task. By structuring the problem this way, the authors frame the reinforcement learning component as learning the parameters of a Q-function that is linear in the feature weights. This leads to a computationally efficient and stable learning algorithm that requires remarkably little data. The method is evaluated on a challenging simulated car-following task, demonstrating substantial improvements in performance and a reduction in data requirements by over two orders of magnitude compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning approaches like TRPO, and outperforming classical visual servoing techniques.

### Decision: Accept

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It presents a bold, novel, and highly effective approach to a classic robotics problem. The two primary reasons for my decision are:

1.  **Significant Conceptual Novelty:** The paper carves out a compelling and underexplored middle ground between purely geometric, classical visual servoing and purely model-free, end-to-end deep reinforcement learning. The intelligent fusion of pre-trained representations, a simple learned dynamics model, and a structured, sample-efficient RL algorithm is a powerful and original concept that offers a new direction for learning-based robot control.
2.  **Exceptional Sample Efficiency:** The ability to learn a robust controller from only 20 trajectories is a standout result that directly addresses one of the most significant barriers to applying reinforcement learning in robotics. The clever formulation of the Q-function, which makes learning the feature weights a simple, regularized regression problem, is the key insight that enables this efficiency. This is not an incremental improvement; it is a categorical leap in data efficiency for this type of task.

### Supporting Arguments

The strength of this work lies in its thoughtful system design, where each component is chosen to address the weaknesses of the others. Using pre-trained VGG features elegantly sidesteps the need for massive datasets to learn robust visual representations from scratch. However, these generic features are not task-specific. The paper’s solution—learning a set of weights to identify the task-relevant features—is both intuitive and effective.

The choice of a bilinear dynamics model is another key strength. Instead of employing a complex and data-hungry video prediction model, the authors opt for a simpler model class that is well-suited to approximating the dynamics of visual features under camera motion. This model is simple enough to be learned from a small dataset, yet powerful enough to enable effective one-step predictive control.

The most innovative component is the FQI algorithm. By designing the servoing policy as the minimizer of a weighted feature error, the authors construct a corresponding Q-function that is linear in those weights. This transforms the notoriously difficult problem of general Q-function approximation into a series of simple, convex optimizations. This is a fantastic example of how injecting domain knowledge and structure into a learning problem can lead to dramatic gains in efficiency and stability over more generic, black-box approaches.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The comparisons against a variety of strong baselines—including end-to-end TRPO with raw pixels, TRPO with VGG features, and classical IBVS methods (even when supplied with ground-truth depth information)—clearly establish the superiority of the proposed approach. The generalization experiments on novel cars further validate the robustness of the learned controllers. The fact that the method outperforms TRPO even when TRPO is given 100 times more data (Figure 6) is a powerful testament to the method's effectiveness.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While my decision is a firm accept, the following points could help further strengthen the paper:

*   **Real-World Applicability:** The paper's primary limitation is its evaluation in simulation only. A brief discussion on the potential challenges of transferring this method to a real robot would be valuable. For instance, how might unmodeled physical effects (e.g., latency, camera noise, motion blur) impact the learned bilinear dynamics model? Acknowledging these challenges would make the work more complete.
*   **Feature Extractor Choice:** The method relies on the VGG network, which is now somewhat dated and computationally heavy. It would be interesting to see a comment on whether the framework is compatible with more modern and efficient backbones (e.g., MobileNets, ResNets). This could potentially improve the runtime (currently ~16Hz) and make the system more practical for real-time control on embedded hardware.
*   **Clarity on Baseline Advantages:** In the comparison to classical IBVS, the baselines are provided with ground-truth depth for the feature points. This is a very strong assumption and a significant piece of information that the proposed method does not require. I would recommend highlighting this point more explicitly, as it further underscores the strength and generality of your learning-based approach.

### Questions for the Authors

*   How sensitive is the final servoing performance to the quality of the learned bilinear dynamics model? For example, if the dynamics model were trained on significantly less data (e.g., from 10 trajectories instead of 100), does the performance gracefully degrade, or does it fail catastrophically?
*   The two-step update within each FQI iteration (Eq. 5 and 6) is an interesting technique. Could you provide more intuition on why you first solve for a global scaling factor `α` and bias `b` before updating the full weight vector `θ`? Is this step primarily for faster convergence, or does it also improve the stability of the FQI process?
*   Could you comment on the primary failure modes of your system? The results show many successful cases, but understanding when and why it fails would be very insightful. For example, does it fail when the target object undergoes extreme appearance changes not captured by the pre-trained features, or when its motion sharply deviates from the learned dynamics?
