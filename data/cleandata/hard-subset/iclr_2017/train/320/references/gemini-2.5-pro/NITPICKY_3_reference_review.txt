### Review

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a method for learning visual servoing control policies with high sample efficiency. The proposed approach leverages visual features from a pre-trained VGG network, learns a simple bilinear predictive model of the feature dynamics, and uses a reinforcement learning algorithm based on fitted Q-iteration (FQI) to learn a weighting over feature channels and scales. This weighting allows the controller to focus on task-relevant visual information and ignore distractors. The final policy performs a greedy one-step lookahead, minimizing the weighted Euclidean distance between the predicted features and the target features. The method is evaluated on a complex simulated car-following task, where it demonstrates substantial performance gains over conventional baselines and a sample efficiency improvement of over two orders of magnitude compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning.

**Decision: Reject**

**Key Reasons:**

1.  The manuscript is marred by a significant number of critical presentation flaws, including missing figures and tables, severe formatting errors in the bibliography, and inconsistent notation. These issues make it impossible to fully evaluate the empirical claims and suggest the paper was submitted in a premature state.
2.  The methodological description contains critical ambiguities and apparent contradictions, particularly concerning the exact architecture of the dynamics model used for the main experiments. This lack of clarity raises serious concerns about the work's reproducibility and scientific rigor.

**Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper, while built on a promising set of ideas, is not in a state suitable for publication at a top-tier conference. My decision is based on the following major issues:

*   **Missing Figures and Tables:** The paper repeatedly refers to figures (1-7) and tables (1-6) that are central to understanding the method and its results. For instance, Figure 1 is meant to provide an overview of the model, Figure 2 the VGG architecture modifications, and Figures 5 and 6 the primary quantitative results comparing different methods. Without these essential components, it is impossible to verify the experimental claims, assess the significance of the results, or properly understand the proposed architecture. This is a fatal flaw in the submission.

*   **Severe Formatting and Presentation Issues:**
    *   **Bibliography:** The reference list is improperly formatted, with clear BibTeX compilation errors throughout (e.g., `Bay et al\.,? \Q2006\E, Bay et al\.,`). This indicates a lack of care in the preparation of the manuscript.
    *   **Notation:** The notation is frequently unclear and non-standard. The `∗` operator for the locally connected model is defined in a footnote (Footnote 4), which is poor practice; its mathematical definition is also difficult to parse due to unclear indexing (`kh,kw,ih,iw`). The feature map cardinality `|y(l)·,c|` in Equation (3) is used without a clear definition of the `·` notation. In Algorithm 1, `θ(0)` is used both as the initial parameter set and as a variable updated at the end of the outer loop (`θ(0) ← θ(K)`), which is extremely confusing. Standard indexing (e.g., `θ_s`) should be used.
    *   **Footnotes:** The use of footnotes is inconsistent. Some are for URLs, while others contain critical definitions (Footnote 4) that belong in the main text.

*   **Methodological Ambiguity:**
    *   A major point of confusion lies in the description of the bilinear dynamics model. Section 4.2 introduces a computationally efficient "locally connected bilinear model." However, Appendix C.2 explicitly states, "We do not use the latter for the semantic features since they are too high-dimensional for the dynamics model to fit in memory." Since the paper's best results are achieved using semantic VGG features, it remains entirely unclear which dynamics model was actually used. If a fully connected model was used, the paper fails to explain how the stated memory issues were overcome. This contradiction is at the very core of the proposed method.
    *   The modification of the VGG network is a key component for obtaining spatially-aware features suitable for servoing. However, the description—"we remove some of the max-pooling layers and replace the convolutions that followed them with dilated convolutions"—is too vague for reproducibility. The authors must specify which layers were modified and with what dilation rates.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is not ready for acceptance, the underlying research direction is valuable. The combination of pre-trained features, simple dynamics models, and sample-efficient RL for learning a metric/weighting is a compelling paradigm for robotics. Should the authors choose to revise and resubmit, I would suggest the following:

*   **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The primary focus should be on a complete and unambiguous description of the method. This includes providing architectural details (VGG modifications, dynamics model structure), algorithm details (clear pseudocode), and a precise definition of all mathematical notation.
*   **Justification of Design Choices:** The novel two-step update in the FQI algorithm (Eq. 5 and 6) is introduced "to speed up the optimization." This claim should be substantiated, for example, with an ablation study comparing it to a more standard FQI update procedure.
*   **Transparency in Experiments:** The paper should be more upfront in the main text that the RL cost function (Appendix B) requires privileged ground-truth 3D position information from the simulator. Furthermore, the classical baselines are given strong oracular information (ground truth depth, bounding boxes, car dynamics). While this is acceptable for analysis, it should be clearly highlighted, and perhaps compared against versions of the baselines with more realistic sensory inputs (e.g., estimated depth).
*   **Thorough Proofreading:** A future version must be meticulously proofread to correct all formatting, grammatical, and typographical errors. All figures and tables must be included and properly referenced.

**Questions for the Authors**

To clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following:

1.  Which specific bilinear dynamics model (fully connected per channel, or locally connected) was used to obtain the main results with VGG `conv4_3` features presented in Section 6? If the fully connected model was used, how were the memory constraints described in Appendix C.2 addressed?
2.  Could you please provide the exact architectural details of the modified VGG network? Specifically, which max-pooling layers were removed, and what were the dilation rates of the subsequent convolutional layers?
3.  The cost function in Equation (9) depends on the ground-truth 3D position of the car. Was this privileged information used *only* for computing the scalar reward signal during RL training, or was it also accessible to any part of the policy or value function at test time?
