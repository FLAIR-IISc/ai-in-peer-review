Summary and contributions
The paper tackles visual servoing for target following by combining (i) pretrained deep visual features (modified VGG-16 conv layers), (ii) a learned short-horizon predictive dynamics model in feature space (a multiscale bilinear model), and (iii) a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure to learn feature weights that define the servoing objective. The control policy itself is a simple one-step lookahead that minimizes the predicted next-step feature error to a goal image, with per-channel, per-scale weights and quadratic control regularization. The key ideas are: (1) servoing in the space of robust CNN features rather than raw pixels or hand-crafted keypoints, (2) leveraging a structured bilinear dynamics model to predict feature evolution under camera motion, and (3) using a linear Q-function approximator aligned with the servo objective to enable efficient FQI that selects task-relevant channels and scales. On a realistic synthetic quadrotor–car following benchmark, the method achieves strong performance with very limited RL data (∼20 trajectories), substantially outperforming pixel-based servoing, classical IBVS with ORB or tracker points, and end-to-end deep RL baselines in sample efficiency. Code, simulator, and videos are released.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Sound, well-motivated integration of representation learning (via pretrained features), model learning (bilinear feature dynamics), and RL (Q-learning over feature weights), with a clear link between the Q-function and the execution-time servo policy that yields sample efficiency.
- Comprehensive empirical comparison on a challenging simulator with varied baselines; results substantiate claims of robustness and efficiency.

Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled?
- Visual servoing for target following with minimal task-specific data, emphasizing robustness to viewpoint change, appearance variation, and occlusions, and fast adaptation to new targets.

Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature?
- The need to move beyond hand-engineered features/Jacobians in classical IBVS/PBVS is well argued. Using pretrained CNN features for invariance is timely and supported by prior transfer learning work. Choosing bilinear dynamics is motivated by pixel motion models and offers a tractable, differentiable, and exact-in-control linearization for one-step optimization. The FQI angle is positioned relative to NFQ/regularized FQI, with the novel twist of a Q approximator whose greedy action coincides with the servo policy, yielding efficient learning of a high-dimensional weighting vector.

Do results support the claims?
Strengths:
- Ablations across VGG layers show a sensible trend: mid-level features (up to conv4_3) are best; conv5_3 is too invariant spatially. Pixel-based servoing degrades under occlusion/distractors, matching intuition.
- The proposed FQI learns effective weights in as few as two sampling iterations (20 trajectories), outperforming the same policy class with CEM-tuned weights and policies trained with TRPO given much more data. Learning curves corroborate sample efficiency.
- Comparisons to classical IBVS with ORB and tracker-derived points are fair and include depth/interaction matrix assumptions; tracker-based IBVS is competitive but slower and still behind the proposed method.
- Clear computational profile (∼16 Hz dominated by VGG feature extraction), and code/simulator availability improve reproducibility.

Limitations/concerns:
- All results are in simulation. While this is common, the core claims relate to robustness and sample efficiency for “quick adaptation,” which would be more compelling with at least limited real-world validation or sim-to-real analysis.
- The RL cost uses ground-truth target pose in the camera frame (Appendix B). This is acceptable in simulation but raises questions about how to obtain training signals in real deployment without external tracking.
- Dynamics learning relies on 10k triplets from a hand-coded policy; the “20 trajectory” headline applies to RL weight learning, not the dynamics. The overall data requirement (particularly for dynamics) should be discussed explicitly.
- Baseline coverage: end-to-end TRPO is included, but modern off-policy continuous-control methods (e.g., DDPG/NAF of the era) are not compared; TRPO policies are relatively small and may be under-optimized.
- The locally connected bilinear model is used for pixels; for semantic features, only channel-wise fully connected dynamics seem feasible due to memory constraints. A more detailed complexity trade-off and its impact on performance would help.
- Lack of analysis of the learned weights (e.g., which channels/scales are selected), stability properties, and sensitivity to dynamics/model mismatch.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not decisive)
- Clarify the data budget: separate counts for dynamics training and RL weight learning; report sensitivity to the amount/coverage of dynamics data and to model mismatch.
- Provide an ablation where the servo uses the same features but no learned dynamics (e.g., assuming identity or finite differencing) to isolate the value of the bilinear predictor.
- Include comparisons to DDPG/NAF (circa 2015–2016) for end-to-end and for learning the weighting parameters, given their sample efficiency claims.
- Add interpretability: visualize channels/scales with large learned weights; examine whether they align with car-specific filters and how they generalize to novel cars.
- Discuss the feasibility of obtaining training costs without ground-truth poses (self-supervised surrogates, weak supervision, or learned reward models), and any plans for sim-to-real transfer.
- Report robustness tests: larger viewpoint shifts, stronger occlusions, dynamic distractors, and domain shift (lighting/weather).
- Analyze the effect of the α,b “policy-independent parameter” fitting step on stability/convergence; provide multiple seeds and confidence intervals.
- Consider alternative backbones (shallower CNNs) to trade off speed vs performance and demonstrate that the approach is not tied to VGG.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to inaccuracies in the bilinear dynamics? If you degrade the dynamics model or reduce dynamics data, how does servoing and FQI training degrade?
- The cost uses ground-truth car pose. How would you obtain comparable training signals in the real world? Can your FQI operate with image-only costs (e.g., goal feature distance) without collapsing to distractors?
- What is the total sample budget including dynamics learning? Can FQI still succeed with fewer than 10k dynamics triplets or with dynamics trained on different objects?
- How do the learned weights distribute across channels and scales? Do they consistently emphasize certain layers or spatial locations corresponding to the car?
- Could you report performance and runtime with a lighter feature extractor (e.g., AlexNet/MobileNet) and discuss speed/accuracy trade-offs?
- Did you try off-policy actor-critic baselines (DDPG/NAF) for the same task and policy class? If so, how do their sample requirements and asymptotic performance compare?
- Any convergence guarantees or empirical stability diagnostics for the two-step α,b and θ updates in FQI? How critical is the non-negativity constraint and the regularization coefficient ν?
