Summary of contributions
- The paper addresses visual servoing for target following using learned visual features and learned feature dynamics combined with a simple greedy controller whose weights are trained by a sample‑efficient fitted Q‑iteration (FQI). The key idea is to servo in the space of pretrained deep features (modified VGG) rather than raw pixels or hand-engineered keypoints, and to predict feature evolution with a bilinear, locally connected dynamics model conditioned on robot actions.
- The servoing policy minimizes a weighted Euclidean distance between predicted next‑step features and a goal feature map; the weights over feature channels and scales, and control regularizers, are chosen to maximize long‑horizon return via an analytically tractable linear Q‑function approximator, enabling efficient FQI with very few trajectories.
- On a synthetic quadcopter–car following benchmark, the method learns effective policies using only ~20 training trajectories for RL, outperforms pixel-space and keypoint-based servoing, generalizes to novel cars, and is >100× more sample‑efficient than end‑to‑end model‑free deep RL baselines. The approach is computationally efficient at test time and robust to viewpoint changes and occlusions.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) A compelling and well-motivated integration of pretrained deep features, lightweight predictive models, and fitted Q‑iteration that delivers strong sample‑efficiency and robust performance; (2) Thorough empirical comparisons and ablations demonstrating clear advantages over classical IBVS and modern deep RL alternatives.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets fast adaptation of visual servoing to new targets with minimal per-target data. It is well placed in literature: classic IBVS assumes engineered, calibrated features; end‑to‑end deep RL is data‑hungry; recent video prediction methods are powerful but heavy. Leveraging pretrained CNN features plus a simple, physically motivated bilinear feature dynamics is a plausible middle ground. The need to learn weights that select task-relevant channels is well argued.
- Approach soundness: The locally connected bilinear model is a sensible approximation for feature flow under camera motion; the multiscale feature pyramid captures local/global cues. Casting the servo’s objective as a linear Q‑approximator in the weights, and using FQI with a closed-form inner minimization over actions, is elegant and contributes to sample efficiency. The observation that scaling/bias don’t change the greedy action and can be optimized jointly is neat and reduces instability typical of FQI.
- Empirical support: 
  - Strong ablations across VGG layers show best performance up to conv4_3 and degradation at conv5_3 due to excessive invariance, aligning with intuition.
  - Comparisons: (i) classical IBVS with ORB underperforms; (ii) tracker-driven IBVS is better but still worse than the proposed method, despite having GT depth and incurring high runtime; (iii) TRPO on the same dynamics achieves worse cost with vastly more samples; (iv) end‑to‑end CNN policies need many more samples and still underperform. 
  - Generalization to unseen cars is demonstrated and degrades reasonably for pixels/conv5_3, while conv1_2–conv4_3 maintain performance. 
  - Sample efficiency: only 2 sampling iterations × 10 trajectories each for FQI, with most gains after the first iteration.
- Rigor and clarity: The dynamics training and FQI procedures are described with sufficient detail (losses, optimizers, regularization, data collection), and code/simulator are released. The cost function is precisely specified. The linearization used to solve the control step is exact for the bilinear model, supporting computational claims.

Concerns and limitations
- Evaluation is entirely in simulation; applicability to real robots is not shown. Domain shift in feature statistics and dynamics may challenge the bilinear model and weight selection.
- The RL cost uses privileged simulator state (ground truth 3D target position). This is reasonable for benchmarking but limits immediate deployment where this supervision is unavailable; results may not reflect sample efficiency under purely vision-derived rewards.
- Baseline fairness: IBVS variants are given GT depth and sometimes GT dynamics, which should help them, but the tracker baseline’s 1 Hz speed is a strong practical limitation; a faster modern tracker or keypoint detector could narrow the gap. Conversely, end-to-end baselines were trained extensively, yet still underperform; more architecture tuning might change absolute numbers.
- Limited theoretical guarantees: While the Q‑approximation is linear and the inner argmin is tractable, convergence properties with the proposed α,b re-scaling step are empirical.

Feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not decisive)
- Real-world validation: Demonstrate transfer to a physical platform, or at least to a photorealistic simulator, and discuss sim‑to‑real issues (e.g., feature layer choice, fine‑tuning features, robustness to calibration).
- Reward design without privileged state: Show how to estimate or surrogate the cost from vision only (e.g., learned car detector/pose estimator) and the impact on sample efficiency.
- Ablations:
  - Quantify the contribution of the α,b re-scaling step in FQI (learning curves with/without).
  - Compare fully vs locally connected bilinear dynamics for semantic features if memory permits, or report parameter counts and runtime to justify the locality choice.
  - Analyze sparsity/selection in learned weights across channels/scales to interpret which features matter.
- Broaden baselines: Include a model-based MPC using learned feature dynamics over a short horizon; test a more lightweight backbone (e.g., AlexNet or ResNet with FPN-like features) to assess speed–performance tradeoffs.
- Robustness: Evaluate under stronger occlusions, lighting changes, and camera perturbations; report failure modes.
- Clarify computational profile: Break down per-frame latency (feature extraction vs control solve) and discuss possibilities for on-board deployment.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of VGG layer and the multiscale pyramid design? Did you try fine‑tuning the feature extractor with a small auxiliary loss for servoing?
- Can the proposed FQI learn effectively when the per-step cost is derived purely from vision (e.g., bounding box center/scale) rather than GT 3D pose? Any results with noisy reward signals?
- What is the parameter count and memory footprint of the locally connected bilinear model at each scale and channel? How does it scale with image resolution and action dimensions?
- How often does the learned weight vector become sparse across channels/scales? Do weights concentrate on semantically meaningful channels?
- Does planning for >1 step using the learned dynamics (e.g., 2–3 step lookahead) improve performance, or does the greedy one-step already saturate gains?
- How robust is the learned policy to modest camera calibration errors, latency, or dynamics mismatch?
- Could your fitted Q framework be extended to learn feature weights jointly with the dynamics (or limited fine‑tuning of the backbone) without losing sample efficiency?
