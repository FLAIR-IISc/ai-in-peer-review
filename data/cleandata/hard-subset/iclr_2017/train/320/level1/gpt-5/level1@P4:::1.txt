Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes a visual servoing approach that operates in the space of learned deep features (from a pre-trained, lightly modified VGG-16), rather than pixels or hand-crafted keypoints. The core servo policy performs one-step lookahead in feature space and chooses controls that minimize the predicted feature error to a goal image.
- A multiscale bilinear predictive model is learned to approximate feature dynamics under camera motion; a locally connected variant reduces parameters and yields efficient Jacobian computation needed by the controller.
- To make the servo robust to distractors and select the most relevant feature channels and scales, the paper introduces a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) scheme with a linear Q-function whose greedy policy matches the weighted servo objective. This yields an analytic and efficient learning procedure for the feature weights and control regularizers.
- Experiments on a synthetic quadcopter car-following benchmark show strong performance and robustness to viewpoint changes and occlusions, generalization to novel car instances, and large gains in sample efficiency over model-free deep RL (TRPO). Ablations compare feature layers, pixel vs. VGG features, different weighting/optimization schemes (FQI, TRPO, CEM), and classical IBVS baselines (ORB features and tracker-based).

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) The approach is technically sound and well-motivated, combining pre-trained features, a simple yet effective bilinear feature dynamics, and a tailored FQI that tightly matches the servo objective; (2) Clear empirical gains with strong sample efficiency and ablations that support the claims.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Visual servoing with hand-engineered features and interaction matrices is brittle and task-specific. Using pre-trained convolutional features to gain invariance while learning short-horizon predictive dynamics is well placed in the literature, and the bilinear assumption for feature-map evolution under camera motion is reasonable and computationally attractive.
- Methodological clarity and soundness:
  - The servo objective and its relation to a linear Q-function class are clearly specified; the greedy policy corresponds to minimizing a quadratic objective with closed-form or efficient solution due to exact linearization in controls (Appendix A).
  - The bilinear locally connected dynamics and multiscale features are described with sufficient detail for reproduction; training is supervised with ADAM, and Jacobians are efficiently computed via forward passes.
  - The FQI variant exploits scale invariance in θ and includes L2 regularization and non-negativity constraints, which improve stability. While no theoretical convergence guarantees are provided, the construction is standard within fitted Q-iteration and the linear approximator matches the control structure, reducing approximation error.
- Empirical evaluation:
  - The setup is rigorously described, with fixed train/validation/test splits, reporting over 100 test rollouts, and comparisons to multiple baselines. Code and environment are made available, improving reproducibility.
  - The ablations convincingly show that mid-level VGG features (conv1_2–conv4_3) outperform pixels and conv5_3, that the learned weighting is crucial (FQI > TRPO/CEM with the same dynamics), and that the approach is more sample efficient than end-to-end policies trained with TRPO.
  - Comparisons to classical IBVS (including tracker-based) are informative; the method runs substantially faster than tracker-based IBVS and is more robust than ORB-based IBVS. PBVS with ground-truth poses unsurprisingly performs best, serving as an upper bound.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect the recommendation)
- Clarify the use of privileged information: the RL cost uses ground-truth 3D car positions to shape the cost and termination. This is fine for simulation but should be discussed as a limitation and avenues for image-based surrogate costs in real settings (e.g., learned pose estimators or detection-based costs).
- Sample efficiency framing: the dynamics training uses 10k (100 trajectories) supervised transitions, and RL uses 20 trajectories. Please separate and report both budgets when comparing to model-free RL, and, if possible, provide a version where model-free RL gets the same total number of environment transitions.
- Statistical reporting: include standard deviations or confidence intervals on average costs, and significance where applicable.
- Generality: results are on a single synthetic domain. A small real-world validation (or sim-to-real sensitivity study) and/or a second task (e.g., following different object classes, manipulation-based servoing) would strengthen the case for generality.
- Feature choices: provide more analysis on why conv5_3 underperforms (quantify spatial invariance loss) and the effect of dilated-conv modifications to VGG. A small study on fine-tuning the feature extractor (even shallow layers) could be informative.
- Overfitting control: the number of trainable weights (w per channel and scale, λ per control) can be large. Discuss regularization (beyond L2), sparsity, or grouping strategies, and show weight distributions or stability across runs.
- Failure modes: add qualitative examples of failures (e.g., heavy occlusions, rapid viewpoint changes, moving distractors of the same class), and quantify robustness thresholds.
- Fairness in IBVS comparisons: you provide ground-truth depths and, in one variant, car dynamics to the IBVS baselines. Make explicit which variants are reported in main figures and ensure consistent resource assumptions.

Questions for the authors
1. Cost function: Does learning critically depend on access to ground-truth 3D positions to compute costs and early terminations? Have you tried purely image-based costs (e.g., bounding-box overlap to the goal image) and how does performance change?
2. Data efficiency: How does performance vary with (a) fewer/more supervised dynamics samples, and (b) fewer/more RL sampling iterations S or inner FQI iterations K? Any signs of overfitting when increasing θ dimensionality?
3. Dynamics model: How sensitive is the controller to model mismatch or larger control magnitudes that may violate the bilinear approximation? Do you re-linearize online or adapt the model?
4. Feature weighting: What is the dimensionality of θ for the best setting (conv4_3) and how do learned weights distribute across layers/channels and scales? Are weights sparse or broadly distributed?
5. Target specification: Beyond using the first-frame goal image, can the method follow a class exemplar unseen in the current scene? How does it disambiguate among multiple similar cars?
6. Generalization: How well does the learned policy transfer across camera intrinsics (FOV), height ranges, or lighting conditions not seen in training?
7. End-to-end learning: Did you attempt joint fine-tuning of the feature extractor with the dynamics and weights, perhaps with a small number of additional trajectories?
8. Runtime: Please report wall-clock training time for dynamics and FQI, and inference FPS on CPU/GPU. Can a lighter backbone (e.g., MobileNet) maintain performance with higher FPS?

Overall, the paper is clearly written, technically sound, and presents a novel and practical approach with convincing empirical support and open-source release.
