Summary of contributions
The paper tackles visual servoing for target following by replacing hand-engineered features and Jacobians with learned components: (i) fixed, pretrained multiscale VGG features (modified to preserve spatial detail), (ii) a bilinear predictive model of feature dynamics, and (iii) a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) scheme that learns per-channel, per-scale feature weights and control penalties so that a simple one-step lookahead servoing objective aligns with long-horizon returns. The approach yields an analytic Q-approximator linear in weights, enabling a sample-efficient FQI procedure. On a simulated quadcopter–car-following benchmark, the method achieves robust performance to viewpoint changes, appearance variation, and occlusions, outperforming pixel- or keypoint-based IBVS, end-to-end TRPO, and variants without learned weights, while using as few as 20 RL trajectories. Code, simulator, and videos are released.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong, well-motivated integration of pretrained features, learned dynamics, and a tailored RL objective that directly induces a practical, efficient controller. (2) Convincing empirical evidence and ablations showing large sample-efficiency gains and generalization to novel targets.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation are clear and well-situated in classic IBVS/PBVS and modern RL/video prediction literature; using pretrained features with a light-weight bilinear dynamics model is a principled way to leverage invariances without large data.
- The Q-parameterization that makes the greedy action recover the weighted one-step objective is elegant and leads to a stable, data-efficient FQI routine; the α, b re-scaling optimization is a nice practical twist.
- Empirical support is broad: comparisons across feature layers (conv1_2–conv5_3), with/without weights, against TRPO (2 and 50 iters), CEM, CNN end-to-end, ORB/IBVS, and tracker-based IBVS; generalization to unseen cars; runtime comparisons (≈16 Hz vs ≈1 Hz tracker).
- While experiments are in simulation and costs use ground-truth pose, the claims are limited to sample efficiency and robustness within that setting and are well supported.

Additional feedback (to help improve, not decisive)
- Provide confidence intervals and success metrics, not only average trajectory cost; include failure mode analysis (e.g., long occlusions, fast target maneuvers).
- Discuss sensitivity to the choice of VGG layer(s) and to the number of RL trajectories; include a small hyperparameter sweep.
- Clarify fairness: ensure baselines see comparable data budgets and feature access; report TRPO variance across seeds.
- Explore replacing ground-truth cost with self-supervised/learned proxies to facilitate real-world deployment; a small hardware demo would strengthen impact.
- Detail memory/computation of the locally connected bilinear model and scalability to higher resolutions/backbones.

Questions for the authors
- How is the ground-truth cost replaced in real settings without target pose? Can detection/tracking signals suffice, and how would that affect FQI?
- How sensitive is performance to the specific VGG layers and to mixing layers (e.g., learned layer selection)?
- What happens if weights are unconstrained (allow negative values)? Does non-negativity materially help stability?
- Can the same dynamics model transfer across object categories, or must it be retrained for new targets?
- How robust is the method to prolonged occlusions or target loss, and does the policy recover reliably?
- Could lighter backbones (e.g., MobileNet) retain performance and improve >16 Hz throughput?
- Are there failure cases where conv5_3’s invariances help (e.g., severe appearance shifts)?
