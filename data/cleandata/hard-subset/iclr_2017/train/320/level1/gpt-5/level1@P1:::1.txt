Summary of contributions
- The paper tackles visual servoing for target following by combining pre-trained deep visual features, learned predictive feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient RL procedure. The key idea is to servo in a learned feature space (VGG intermediate feature maps modified with dilations) rather than pixels or hand-engineered keypoints.
- A bilinear predictive model (with locally connected structure) is trained to predict next-step feature maps given current features and action, yielding an exact linearization in control for efficient optimization.
- A weighted multi-scale objective is used for servoing; the weights over channels and control regularization are learned via a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) scheme with a Q-approximator constructed to match the servoing objective. This makes the greedy policy with respect to Q coincide with the weighted one-step lookahead servo.
- Empirically, on a synthetic quadrotor car-following task (CitySim3D), the method achieves robust tracking across viewpoint changes and occlusions, generalizes to novel cars, substantially outperforms IBVS with keypoints or a tracker, and is >100x more sample-efficient than model-free deep RL baselines. Notably, only ~20 RL trajectories are used to learn weights; the feature dynamics model is trained from 100 trajectories (10k transitions).

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons: (1) Well-motivated and novel integration of pre-trained features, bilinear feature dynamics, and a tailored FQI that yields a compelling sample-efficiency advantage; (2) Careful empirical comparisons and ablations support the main claims, including generalization to novel objects and robustness to distractors.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets an important gap between traditional IBVS (requiring engineered features and Jacobians) and model-free deep RL (data hungry). Using pre-trained features is well grounded in transfer learning literature, and servoing in feature space is a natural, under-explored idea for control.
- Methodological soundness:
  - The bilinear, locally connected feature dynamics is justified by prior work on image-motion bilinearities and is computationally efficient; the exact linearization in controls enables a closed-form quadratic objective for the policy step.
  - The Q-approximator is constructed so that its greedy action recovers the servoing policy; the FQI variant that rescales α and bias for both current and target Q stabilizes training and exploits the structure of the approximator. Non-negativity constraints on weights are sensible.
  - The use of multi-scale features and per-channel weights is a practical mechanism for relevance selection without end-to-end training.
- Empirical support:
  - Comparisons across feature representations (pixels and VGG conv1_2 to conv5_3) show that mid-level features (up to conv4_3) are most effective; conv5_3 is too spatially invariant, which is plausible.
  - Comparisons of weight-learning methods (no weighting/CEM, TRPO with 2 and 50 iterations) show that the FQI approach reaches superior performance with two orders of magnitude fewer trajectories.
  - Baselines include classical IBVS with ORB and with a strong tracker (C-COT), end-to-end CNN policies with TRPO (pixels and VGG). The proposed approach outperforms these while running faster than tracking-based IBVS at test time. Ablations and training details are provided.
- Rigor and clarity: The paper is well written, provides algorithmic details, training regimes, and a public simulator/code. Results are averaged over 100 held-out test trajectories, with consistent sampling. The description of cost, dynamics training, and FQI is clear.

Caveats and limitations (impact decision but do not negate contributions)
- Evaluation is solely in a synthetic environment; no real-robot results or sim-to-real evidence. This limits external validity for ICLR.
- The RL cost uses privileged 3D car position from the simulator; while the policy itself uses only images, the reliance on non-image supervision during training may complicate real deployment.
- The FQI/Q-function is linear in weights, which may limit expressivity when more complex trade-offs are needed; nevertheless, it performs well here.
- Baselines for continuous control exclude other model-free algorithms popular at the time (e.g., DDPG); inclusion would strengthen claims about sample efficiency.
- The predictive model for semantic features is trained from 100 trajectories; although modest, this is non-trivial and the sensitivity to this dataset size is not explored.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Consider adding at least a small-scale real-robot experiment (or hardware-in-the-loop) to demonstrate feasibility and robustness to real-world nuisances (latency, calibration error, lighting).
- Clarify sensitivity to the amount of supervisory data: (a) number of trajectories for learning dynamics, (b) number for FQI. A figure showing performance vs data would be valuable.
- Provide confidence intervals/standard errors in plots and report variability across random seeds to quantify stability.
- Analyze which feature channels receive high weights to increase interpretability (e.g., are object-selective channels up-weighted?). A visualization could also indicate the spatial support used by the bilinear model.
- Evaluate alternative pre-trained backbones (ResNet, shallower nets) and the effect of fine-tuning the feature extractor jointly with dynamics on the available dataset.
- Explore reward signals that can be computed from images only (e.g., tracker-derived proxy or learned distance estimators), to reduce dependence on privileged state.
- Discuss the computational footprint of the locally connected bilinear model and possible compression/pruning; show timing breakdown.
- Include an ablation on the bias terms in the bilinear dynamics (handling of moving objects) and on the local neighborhood size.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is FQI to the non-negativity constraints on w and λ? Would allowing negative weights help discount distractor channels?
- The cost requires ground-truth 3D car pose. How would you train in the absence of this supervision? Have you tried image-only proxy costs?
- How robust is the method to mismatches between the goal image x* and the current viewpoint (scale/pose differences, lighting)? Does re-setting x* during a trajectory help?
- Can you report variance over seeds and confidence intervals for all main plots? How stable is the FQI optimization?
- Why was DDPG (or other off-policy model-free baselines) not included? Do you anticipate similar sample-efficiency gaps?
- What is the effect of the amount and diversity of dynamics training data (cars, backgrounds)? Does performance degrade gracefully with fewer than 10k transitions?
- Could the bilinear dynamics be extended to multi-step planning (e.g., short-horizon MPC) without sacrificing sample efficiency? Would that help in more challenging scenarios?
