## Review of "Learning Visual Servoing with Reinforcement Learning"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The core idea – leveraging pre-trained deep features and learning a servo policy in this feature space – is a compelling approach to address the limitations of traditional methods that rely on hand-engineered features and analytical models. The focus on sample efficiency, aiming for quick adaptation to new targets, is particularly valuable.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is well-motivated, addressing a significant limitation in the field, and the experimental results demonstrate a substantial improvement in sample efficiency compared to existing methods, including deep reinforcement learning. The improvement of two orders of magnitude in sample efficiency is a particularly strong result.

The paper clearly articulates the problem and positions the work within the existing literature on visual servoing and reinforcement learning. The proposed method is logically sound, building upon established techniques like fitted Q-iteration and bilinear dynamics modeling. The experimental setup, using a complex synthetic car following benchmark, is well-defined and allows for a fair comparison against baseline methods. The results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, showing improvements over pixel-based and hand-designed feature methods, and achieving strong generalization to novel targets. The inclusion of videos further strengthens the presentation of the results.

However, some aspects could be clarified. While the paper mentions the choice of VGG features, a more detailed discussion of *why* these features are particularly suitable for this task would be beneficial.  Furthermore, the impact of the regularization parameter (ν) in the fitted Q-iteration algorithm could be explored in more detail. How sensitive are the results to changes in this parameter?  Finally, the computational cost of the learned dynamics model compared to other approaches could be quantified. 

Specifically, I would like the authors to address the following:

*   Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind choosing VGG features specifically, and how their properties contribute to the success of the approach?
*   What is the sensitivity of the performance to the regularization parameter ν in the fitted Q-iteration algorithm?
*   What is the computational overhead of learning and using the bilinear dynamics model compared to directly using the features without a dynamics model?



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to visual servoing. Addressing the above points would further enhance the clarity and impact of the work.
