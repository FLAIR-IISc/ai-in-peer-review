## Review of "Learning Visual Servoing with Reinforcement Learning"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The central idea – leveraging pre-trained deep features and learning a servo policy in this feature space – is a compelling approach to address the limitations of traditional methods relying on hand-designed features and analytical models. The authors demonstrate a promising method for quickly adapting to new targets using a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is novel and well-motivated, addressing a significant issue in robotic control – the need for adaptable and data-efficient visual servoing. The experimental results, particularly the significant improvement in sample efficiency over standard deep RL methods, are impressive and convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. 

The paper effectively establishes the context of visual servoing and clearly articulates the shortcomings of existing techniques. The proposed method is logically presented, with a clear explanation of the bilinear dynamics model and the fitted Q-iteration algorithm. Crucially, the authors validate their approach on a complex synthetic car following benchmark and provide a thorough comparison to alternative methods, including both traditional and learning-based approaches. The substantial improvement over existing methods and the impressive sample efficiency achieved (using only 20 training trajectories) strongly support the claims made in the paper. The availability of code and videos further enhances the reproducibility and impact of the work.

However, several points could improve the paper. While the results are strong, a discussion of the limitations of the synthetic environment would be beneficial. How well would the approach generalize to real-world scenarios with more complex visual variations and sensor noise? The rationale for specific hyperparameter choices (e.g., regularization coefficient in FQI) could also be elaborated. Additionally, a more detailed explanation of the choice of VGG layers for feature extraction, and perhaps an ablation study exploring different layers, would be valuable. 

**Questions for the authors:**

*   How sensitive is the performance to the quality of the initial dynamics model? Does pre-training the dynamics model on a diverse dataset of trajectories significantly improve performance?
*   What are the computational costs associated with training and deploying the bilinear dynamics model and the fitted Q-iteration algorithm?
*   How robust is the system to variations in lighting conditions and occlusions beyond those present in the synthetic benchmark?
*   Could you provide more insight into why the deepest VGG feature (conv5\_3) performed worse than the others? Was this due to overfitting or a loss of relevant spatial information?
