This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The goal is to achieve quick adaptation to new targets with minimal data, moving away from manually engineered features and analytical dynamics models. The core contribution is a method that servos the camera in the space of learned visual features (from a pre-trained VGG network) using a bilinear predictive model and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to determine the most relevant features.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods, particularly in sample efficiency. The problem is important, and the proposed solution addresses a critical limitation of traditional visual servoing techniques – the need for extensive hand-engineering and domain expertise.

The paper is exceptionally well-structured and clearly articulates the motivation, related work, method, and experimental results. The authors convincingly demonstrate that leveraging pre-trained deep features, coupled with a learned dynamics model and reinforcement learning, can lead to robust and adaptable visual servoing. The improvement in sample efficiency – over two orders of magnitude compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning – is a particularly compelling result. The experiments on the synthetic car following benchmark are well-designed and the comparisons to baseline methods (image pixels, hand-designed keypoints, and other RL algorithms) are thorough and clearly presented. The accompanying videos further strengthen the findings. The mathematical derivations (linearization of dynamics, Q-function approximation) are clearly presented in the appendix. The ablation studies regarding feature choices and optimization methods are also insightful.

However, a few points could be addressed to further improve the paper:

*   **Generalization to Real-World Data:** While the synthetic benchmark is compelling, the paper would benefit from demonstrating the approach’s performance on real-world datasets or in a simulated environment with more realistic visual variations (e.g., lighting changes, cluttered backgrounds).
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the results to key hyperparameters (e.g., learning rates, regularization coefficients, network architecture) would be valuable.
*   **Computational Cost:** While the paper mentions the computational cost, a more detailed breakdown of the time required for training the dynamics model and the reinforcement learning phase would be helpful for potential users.
*   **Choice of Bilinear Model:** The authors mention exploring different variations of the bilinear model. It would be useful to provide a more comprehensive comparison of the performance of these different variants, and a justification for the final choice.
*   **FQI Iterations:** The choice of 2 sampling iterations in FQI seems low, given the 10 iterations within each. How was this number determined? Was it sufficient for convergence, or was it limited by computational resources?

I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  The bilinear model’s performance seems sensitive to the chosen features (e.g., conv5\_3 performing worse). Was any hyperparameter tuning performed specifically for the dynamics model in conjunction with each feature level, or were the parameters kept consistent across all levels?
2.  The regularization term (ν) in the FQI optimization is mentioned but its value isn’t explicitly stated in the main text. What value was used, and how sensitive were the results to different values of ν?
3.  The paper claims the method is sample-efficient. Can the authors provide a more intuitive explanation of *why* FQI contributes to this sample efficiency, beyond stating it's an efficient algorithm? Specifically, how does the analytic Q-function approximation play a role?
4.  The final paragraph mentions the potential for speedups in feature computation. Is there an estimate for the performance improvement that could be achieved by using a less computationally demanding network than VGG?
5. The car moves around the city following roads, how do you ensure the trajectories are diverse and prevent the training data from being biased towards certain road types or layouts?
