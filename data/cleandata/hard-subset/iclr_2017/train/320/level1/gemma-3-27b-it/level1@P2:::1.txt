This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing, combining learned visual features from pre-trained CNNs (VGG), a bilinear predictive dynamics model, and reinforcement learning (fitted Q-iteration). The core idea – leveraging the power of learned representations while maintaining sample efficiency – is compelling and addresses a key limitation of traditional visual servoing methods that rely on hand-engineered features and analytical models. The focus on quickly adapting to new targets with limited data is also a strong motivation.

**Decision: Accept**

The primary reason for acceptance is the significant improvement in sample efficiency demonstrated by the proposed method, exceeding existing deep reinforcement learning approaches by two orders of magnitude. This addresses a core challenge in applying RL to robotics. Secondly, the method demonstrates robust performance in a complex synthetic environment, outperforming conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results presented in Section 6 are convincing. The comparison against baseline methods (pixel-based servoing, ORB/tracker-based IBVS, TRPO) clearly illustrates the benefits of the proposed approach. The improvement in sample efficiency (20 trajectories for RL learning vs. the much larger sample requirements of TRPO) is particularly noteworthy. The validation and test sets help to demonstrate generalization, though the limited number of cars used in training warrants further investigation (addressed in feedback).
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper successfully motivates the use of pre-trained features by arguing their generalizability and invariance to visual variations. The bilinear dynamics model provides a tractable way to predict feature evolution, and the fitted Q-iteration efficiently learns appropriate feature weights. The combination of these components is a sensible approach to the problem.
3. **Technical Soundness:** The description of the bilinear dynamics model and the fitted Q-iteration algorithm is clear and well-defined. The linearization of the dynamics (Appendix A) and the details of the cost function (Appendix B) add to the credibility of the technical contribution. The inclusion of implementation details and publicly available code (and videos) is also a positive sign.
4. **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly defines the problem of visual servoing and highlights the limitations of existing methods, effectively setting the stage for the proposed solution.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not impacting the accept decision):**

1. **Generalization & Dataset Size:** While the paper demonstrates improvement over baselines, the number of "cars" used in the training set seems relatively small. It would be beneficial to evaluate the method on a larger and more diverse dataset of target objects to further assess its generalization capabilities. How does performance degrade as the number of novel targets increases significantly?
2. **Ablation Studies:** A more detailed ablation study could provide further insights into the contribution of each component of the proposed system. For example, what is the performance gain achieved by using the bilinear dynamics model compared to directly using the pre-trained features in the reinforcement learning loop? How much does each scale of the feature pyramid contribute?
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions regularization (ν) in the FQI algorithm. A discussion on the sensitivity of the method to different hyperparameter settings (learning rate, regularization strength, exploration noise) would be valuable.
4. **Real-World Applicability:** The experiments are conducted in a synthetic environment. While this allows for controlled evaluation, a discussion of the challenges and potential solutions for applying this approach to real-world scenarios (e.g., lighting variations, sensor noise, model inaccuracies) would strengthen the paper.
5. **Comparison to Other RL Algorithms:** Although TRPO is used as a comparison, a more thorough comparison to other sample-efficient RL algorithms (e.g., SAC, PPO) might be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the performance of the algorithm to the choice of pre-trained CNN architecture (e.g., ResNet, Inception)?
2. The paper mentions the use of a locally connected bilinear model. What is the impact of the neighborhood size (nf) on the performance and computational cost of the model?
3. Could you elaborate on the choice of the reward function (cost function) and its influence on the learned policy? Is the choice of this cost function critical for the method’s success?
4. In the real world, perfect depth information is often unavailable. How robust is the method to inaccuracies in depth estimation?
