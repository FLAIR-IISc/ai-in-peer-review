This paper tackles the challenging problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The core idea – to learn a visual servo policy operating in the space of learned features rather than raw pixels – is a compelling and potentially impactful direction for increasing the robustness and adaptability of visual servoing systems. The focus on sample efficiency, aiming for quick adaptation to new targets with minimal data, is also a significant contribution.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a promising approach and demonstrates encouraging results, the experimental validation and presentation lack sufficient rigor and clarity to justify acceptance at ICLR. Specifically, the comparison to baselines is not comprehensive enough, and the synthetic nature of the environment limits the generalizability of the findings. Furthermore, the presentation of the experimental results lacks sufficient detail and comparative analysis, making it difficult to fully assess the significance of the improvements reported.

The primary justification for rejection lies in the lack of convincing evidence demonstrating a substantial and generalizable advantage over existing approaches. The authors compare against relatively simple baselines (image pixel-based servoing, ORB features, and a basic tracker-based method) and claim a significant improvement. However, more sophisticated reinforcement learning baselines, or methods leveraging recent advancements in visual imitation learning, are not considered. Without such comparisons, it’s difficult to ascertain whether the reported gains stem from the specific combination of techniques proposed or simply from overcoming the limitations of the chosen baselines.

Secondly, the reliance on a highly-controlled, synthetic environment raises concerns about the real-world applicability of the learned policy. While the CitySim3D environment is described as "complex," it lacks the inherent complexities and noise present in real-world visual data. The transferability of the learned dynamics model and servoing policy to real-world scenarios remains an open question.

The approach itself, combining pre-trained features with a bilinear dynamics model and fitted Q-iteration, is well-motivated. Utilizing pre-trained features is a sensible strategy for leveraging existing knowledge and reducing data requirements. The bilinear model provides a computationally tractable way to approximate the visual dynamics, and fitted Q-iteration offers a sample-efficient reinforcement learning algorithm.  The use of sample-efficient RL is particularly attractive given the potential for reducing the amount of real-world data needed for training. The detailed explanation of the fitted Q-iteration process is a strength of the paper.

However, several aspects of the experimental setup and analysis require further scrutiny.  The choice of hyperparameters (e.g., regularization coefficient ν) seems somewhat arbitrary. The ablation studies are limited; while variations in feature representation are explored, a more thorough analysis of the impact of different components (e.g., the bilinear model, the weighting scheme) would be beneficial.  The visualization of the learned feature weights and the dynamics model's predictions is lacking.  Finally, the claim of a "two orders of magnitude" improvement in sample efficiency over standard model-free deep RL is unsubstantiated without a more direct comparison to established deep RL algorithms (e.g., DDPG, PPO) using comparable evaluation metrics.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **More Robust Baselines:** Include comparisons to stronger baseline methods, such as model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms (DDPG, PPO), and visual imitation learning approaches.
*   **Real-World Evaluation:** Consider a limited evaluation of the learned policy in a real-world setting, even if it requires simplifying the task. This would provide valuable insights into the generalizability of the approach.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform more comprehensive ablation studies to analyze the contribution of each component of the proposed approach.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings.
*   **Visualization:** Provide visualizations of the learned feature weights and the dynamics model's predictions to aid in understanding the learned behavior.
*   **Error Analysis:**  Conduct a detailed error analysis to identify the failure modes of the proposed approach and potential areas for improvement.
*   **Clarify Cost Function:** Provide a more intuitive explanation of the cost function used in reinforcement learning and its relation to the desired servoing behavior.
* **Explain the choice of a bilinear model:** Explain why the authors believed the assumption of a bilinear model would hold for this problem. Was it computationally motivated, or were there other reasons?

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  Why were specific baseline algorithms (e.g. DDPG, PPO, visual imitation learning) not included in the experimental comparison?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the regularization coefficient (ν) in the fitted Q-iteration algorithm?
3.  Can you provide some visualizations of the learned feature weights and the dynamics model's predictions?
4.  What are the main failure modes of the proposed approach, and what steps could be taken to address them?
5.  What assumptions are made that justify using a bilinear dynamics model, and how might violations of these assumptions affect performance?
6.  The results seem to indicate that deeper VGG features aren't always better. Could you elaborate on why the VGG conv5\_3 feature representation performed worse?
