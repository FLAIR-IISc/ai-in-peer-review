This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing that leverages learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The core idea is to learn a policy for controlling a robot’s camera to follow a target by operating in the space of learned visual features, rather than raw pixels or hand-designed keypoints. The authors demonstrate promising results in a synthetic car following benchmark, achieving significant sample efficiency gains compared to existing methods.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a compelling idea and demonstrates strong empirical results, the clarity of the method, particularly the connection between the learned dynamics and the reinforcement learning objective, is insufficient for a confident acceptance. The numerous equations and components require a clearer, more cohesive explanation. Furthermore, the experimental setup, while detailed, lacks a comprehensive ablation study isolating the contribution of each component (e.g., bilinear dynamics, feature weighting).

The paper's primary strength lies in its strong empirical results. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their approach outperforms both traditional visual servoing techniques (based on image pixels and hand-designed features) and standard deep reinforcement learning algorithms in terms of sample efficiency. The improvement of two orders of magnitude in sample efficiency is a significant achievement. The choice of a complex synthetic benchmark is also commendable, as it showcases the algorithm’s ability to handle realistic scenarios with visual variation. The availability of code and videos further strengthens the reproducibility of the results.

However, several aspects of the paper hinder a positive assessment. The description of the fitted Q-iteration algorithm is dense and difficult to follow. The jumps between sections are sometimes abrupt, and the rationale behind certain design choices (e.g., the specific form of the bilinear dynamics, the regularization term in the Q-function optimization) is not always clear. The connection between the learned feature dynamics and the subsequent RL training is not fully elucidated. Specifically, it's unclear how the learned dynamics improve the sample efficiency of the RL algorithm beyond providing a reasonable initial model. The ablation study is limited; while comparisons to other methods are presented, a more detailed analysis of the individual components of the proposed approach would be highly beneficial. The justification for using a bilinear model specifically, beyond citing prior work, is weak. A discussion of the limitations of this assumption (and potential alternatives) would strengthen the paper.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Clarity of the Fitted Q-Iteration:** Consider providing a simplified, step-by-step explanation of the fitted Q-iteration algorithm, possibly with pseudocode. Focus on clarifying the roles of α and b, and how they are updated.
*   **Dynamics Model Rationale:** Provide a more in-depth justification for the choice of a bilinear model. Discuss the assumptions inherent in this model and potential limitations. How does the locally connected bilinear model address potential issues with a fully connected one?
*   **Ablation Study:** Conduct a more comprehensive ablation study. Evaluate the performance of the algorithm with: 1) random feature weights, 2) without the learned dynamics model (i.e., direct RL on features), 3) different regularization strengths for the Q-function, and 4) alternative dynamics models.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance to the various hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, regularization coefficient, exploration noise).
*   **Generalization to Real-World Data:** While the synthetic benchmark is valuable, briefly discuss the potential challenges and considerations for deploying this approach in a real-world setting.
*   **Equation (7):** Explain what ū represents in more detail. Is it the current action?
*   **Cost Function:** Detail the precise values of the constants used in the cost function in Appendix B.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of how the learned feature dynamics contribute to the sample efficiency gains in the RL phase? What specifically about the dynamics model allows the agent to learn faster?
2.  Why was a bilinear model chosen for the feature dynamics, and what are its limitations in this context?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of the algorithm to the choice of the regularization coefficient (ν) in the Q-function optimization?
4.  Could you clarify the role of `ū` in Equation (7) and explain how it is chosen in practice?
5.  Are there plans to evaluate this approach on real-world datasets or robotic platforms? If so, what challenges do you anticipate?
