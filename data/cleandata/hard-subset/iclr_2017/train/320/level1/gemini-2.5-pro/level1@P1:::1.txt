### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel and highly sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing policies for target following. The core contribution is a framework that intelligently combines three key components: (1) robust visual representations extracted from a pre-trained convolutional neural network (VGG), (2) a simple, learned bilinear model to predict the dynamics of these features in response to robot actions, and (3) a reinforcement learning algorithm based on fitted Q-iteration (FQI) that learns to weight the importance of different features and scales to optimize for long-term task success. By framing the problem this way, the method avoids the brittleness of hand-engineered features used in classical visual servoing and the extreme sample-inefficiency of end-to-end deep reinforcement learning. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a challenging simulated quadcopter car-following task, showing that they can learn a robust policy from only 20 training trajectories. This represents a substantial improvement in performance over classical baselines and a greater than two-orders-of-magnitude improvement in sample efficiency over standard deep RL methods like TRPO.

### **Decision**

**Accept**. This is a strong paper with a clear and significant contribution.

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper proposes a novel and well-motivated synthesis of ideas from deep learning, model-based control, and reinforcement learning. The approach of using RL to learn a metric over pre-trained features for a simple, model-based controller is an elegant and effective way to achieve sample-efficient learning for a complex visuomotor task.
2.  The experimental results are thorough and convincingly support the paper's claims. The method is shown to be vastly more sample-efficient and to achieve better final performance than a comprehensive suite of strong baselines, including end-to-end deep RL and classical visual servoing techniques.

### **Supporting Arguments**

**Strengths:**
*   **Novelty and Significance:** The primary strength of this work lies in its intelligent problem decomposition. Instead of attempting to learn a complex, end-to-end policy from pixels to actions, the authors leverage the strengths of different techniques for different parts of the problem. Using pre-trained VGG features provides a powerful, invariant visual representation "for free." Learning a simple bilinear dynamics model in this feature space is far more tractable and data-efficient than learning dynamics in pixel space. Finally, using FQI to learn feature weights is a clever way to perform task-specific adaptation, enabling the policy to focus on the target object while ignoring distractors. This framework provides a compelling and practical recipe for sample-efficient robot learning.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and rigorous. The authors compare their method not only against end-to-end RL (TRPO) and classical methods (IBVS with ORB features and a state-of-the-art tracker), but also perform important ablations. They show the effect of using different feature layers from the VGG network, demonstrating that mid-level features perform best. They also compare their FQI-based weight learning against other optimization schemes (CEM, TRPO) on the same policy structure, directly validating the effectiveness of their proposed learning algorithm. The testing on both "seen" and "novel" cars provides evidence of the method's generalization capabilities.
*   **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The method is described clearly, and the motivation for each component is well-articulated. The authors' decision to release their code and the simulation environment is highly commendable, greatly increasing the impact and verifiability of their work.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Simulation-Only Results:** The experiments are conducted exclusively in a simulated environment. While the simulation is visually rich, the real world introduces challenges like sensor noise, motion blur, variable lighting conditions beyond what is simulated, and physical dynamics (e.g., wind) that are not accounted for. This is a common limitation but leaves the question of real-world applicability open.
*   **Reliance on Ground Truth for Cost:** The cost function used for reinforcement learning relies on the ground truth 3D position of the target vehicle (Appendix B). This is unavailable in most real-world scenarios, and the paper does not discuss how this supervision signal could be replaced with something more practical, such as a learned reward function or a signal from a target detector.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.
1.  **Discussion on Real-World Transfer:** The paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion section acknowledging the sim-to-real gap. For instance, how robust might the learned bilinear dynamics be to unmodeled physical effects or camera parameter variations? Discussing potential challenges and future work in this direction would add valuable context.
2.  **Dynamics Model Data Collection:** The dynamics model is trained on data from a "hand-coded policy." It would be helpful to provide a brief characterization of this policy. How critical is the quality or coverage of this initial data? Is the system's performance sensitive to it, or does a simple policy that generates reasonably diverse motion suffice?
3.  **FQI Algorithm Justification:** The modification to the standard FQI algorithm (the intermediate step to fit α and b) is an interesting heuristic. While the motivation is to "speed up the optimization," a little more intuition or a small ablation study comparing it to a more standard FQI update would provide stronger justification for this specific algorithmic choice.

### **Questions for the Authors**
1.  The choice of a bilinear dynamics model seems central to the method's success, particularly because it allows for an exact linearization (Appendix A). Could you comment on why this model class is a good fit for the dynamics of CNN features under ego-motion? Did you experiment with or consider other simple parametric models (e.g., a small MLP)?
2.  As mentioned, the cost function relies on simulator ground truth. How do you envision adapting your framework to a new real-world task where such a cost signal is not readily available? Could the cost be derived from a simple binary classifier (e.g., "target centered" vs. "not centered") or learned from a few demonstrations?
3.  The features from the VGG network are used in a fixed, off-the-shelf manner. Given the excellent sample efficiency, did you consider fine-tuning the last few layers of the VGG network as part of the learning process? Do you hypothesize this would improve performance, or would it be prone to overfitting with only 20 trajectories?
