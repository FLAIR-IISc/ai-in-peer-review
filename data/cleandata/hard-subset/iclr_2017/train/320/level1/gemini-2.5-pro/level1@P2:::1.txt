### **Summary**
This paper proposes a novel and highly sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing policies. The core problem is to control a robot (a quadcopter) to follow a target (a car) using only camera images. The authors make several key contributions. First, instead of using raw pixels or hand-designed features, they leverage robust visual representations from a pre-trained VGG network, which provides invariance to changes in lighting, viewpoint, and appearance. Second, they learn a simple but effective bilinear dynamics model that predicts how these features will change in response to the robot's actions. Third, and most crucially, they introduce a sample-efficient Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn a weighting over the different feature channels and scales. This allows the system to automatically identify which features are most relevant for tracking the target and ignore distractors in the scene. The final policy is a simple one-step planner that minimizes the weighted, predicted feature error. Through experiments in a challenging 3D simulation, the paper demonstrates that this approach can learn an effective servoing policy from just 20 training trajectories, outperforming classical visual servoing methods and achieving more than a 100-fold improvement in sample efficiency compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms like TRPO.

### **Decision: Accept**
I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are:
1.  **Novel and Effective Synthesis of Ideas:** The paper presents an elegant and well-motivated combination of pre-trained deep features, a learned dynamics model, and a custom reinforcement learning algorithm. The core idea of framing the feature selection problem as learning weights for a Q-function is insightful and leads to a highly efficient learning procedure.
2.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough and the results are compelling. The claimed improvement of two orders of magnitude in sample efficiency over standard deep RL baselines is significant and well-supported. The comparisons against a strong set of classical and learning-based methods convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach on the chosen task.

### **Supporting Arguments**
The paper is well-structured and makes a strong case for its proposed method.

**1. Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The work is well-positioned in the literature. It correctly identifies the key weaknesses of two dominant paradigms: classical visual servoing (brittle, hand-engineered features) and end-to-end deep RL (prohibitive sample complexity for many robotics tasks). The proposed approach provides a principled middle ground that harnesses the power of deep features without the need for massive online data collection. The formulation of the servoing policy as minimizing a weighted one-step prediction error is simple, intuitive, and ultimately proves effective.

**2. Technical Soundness:** The technical approach is sound and elegant.
*   The choice to use a simple bilinear model for dynamics in feature space, rather than a more complex generative model for video prediction, is a key design choice that enables sample efficiency.
*   The formulation of the Q-function as a linear approximator with respect to the feature weights (Section 5.2) is the paper's most significant technical insight. This structure allows the Bellman error minimization in FQI to be cast as a computationally efficient linear regression problem. The two-step update procedure within each FQI iteration (Eq. 5 and 6) is a clever technique to stabilize and accelerate the learning of the Q-function's scale and bias before updating the feature weights themselves.
*   The overall algorithm integrates these components cohesively, resulting in a system that can learn a complex behavior from very limited interaction data.

**3. Experimental Rigor:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and provides strong support for the paper's claims.
*   The ablation study comparing different feature representations (pixels vs. various VGG layers) clearly shows the benefit of using pre-trained semantic features and helps identify the most suitable level of abstraction (conv4_3).
*   The comparison against TRPO on the same policy structure (Figure 6, right) is a direct and fair comparison that isolates the benefit of the FQI-based learning algorithm, validating the sample efficiency claims.
*   The comparison against an end-to-end CNN policy trained with TRPO highlights the difficulty of learning such tasks from scratch and reinforces the value of the paper's hybrid approach.
*   The inclusion of strong classical baselines, particularly IBVS with a state-of-the-art C-COT tracker, is commendable. Outperforming this method demonstrates that the learned dynamics and feature weighting capture more relevant information for control than a generic tracker combined with an analytical motion model.
*   The evaluation of generalization to novel cars demonstrates that the system is not merely memorizing the training instances. The public release of the code and environment is a major plus for reproducibility and future research.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**
These points are suggestions for strengthening the paper and are not critical to my decision.
*   **Real-World Transfer:** The work is performed entirely in simulation. While this is perfectly acceptable for demonstrating the core methodology, a brief discussion in the conclusion about the potential challenges and required adaptations for real-world deployment would be valuable. For instance, how might the system cope with camera calibration errors, motion blur, or communication latency?
*   **Limitations of the Dynamics Model:** The bilinear dynamics model is effective here but has inherent limitations. It primarily models geometric transformations of features due to camera motion. A short discussion of its limitations—for example, in scenarios with highly deformable targets or complex articulated motion—and how the framework might be extended to handle them would be a welcome addition.
*   **Clarity on Policy-Q connection:** The connection between the servoing policy in Eq. (3) and the Q-function in Section 5.2 is central to the method. While it is stated that `πθ(st) = argminu Qθ,b(st,u)`, the motivation for this specific Q-function form could be introduced more explicitly in Section 5.1 to make the transition smoother for the reader.

### **Questions for the Authors**
Answering these questions in a rebuttal would strengthen my confidence in the assessment.
1.  **Dynamics Model Training Data:** The dynamics model is trained on data from a hand-coded policy and then fixed. Have you investigated if performance could be improved by iteratively re-training the dynamics model using data collected by the learned policy? This might help mitigate issues of distributional shift between the initial data collection policy and the final task policy.
2.  **Importance of Dilated Convolutions:** You mention modifying the VGG network with dilated convolutions to preserve spatial information. How crucial is this modification to the final performance? Have you compared this against using a standard VGG architecture and simply upsampling the lower-resolution feature maps?
3.  **Analysis of Learned Weights:** A key component of your method is learning the feature weights `w`. Have you performed any analysis or visualization of these learned weights? For instance, which channels in the `conv4_3` layer receive the highest weights, and can they be qualitatively interpreted as corresponding to car-specific features? Such an analysis would provide fascinating insight into what the model deems important for the task.
