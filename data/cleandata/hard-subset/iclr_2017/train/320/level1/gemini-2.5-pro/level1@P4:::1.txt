**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper addresses the problem of learning visual servoing policies with high sample efficiency, aiming to overcome the limitations of traditional methods that rely on hand-engineered features and models. The authors propose a method that combines three key components: (1) robust visual representations extracted from a pre-trained deep convolutional neural network (VGG), (2) a learned, multiscale bilinear model to predict the short-term dynamics of these features in response to robot actions, and (3) a reinforcement learning algorithm to determine the task-relevant features for control. The core contribution lies in the formulation of the control policy as a weighted minimization of predicted feature error, which leads to a Q-function approximator that is linear in the feature weights. This allows the use of a highly sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn which feature channels and scales are most important for the task, effectively isolating the target object from distractors. The authors demonstrate their approach on a complex simulated quadcopter car-following task, claiming significant improvements in performance and sample efficiency over classical visual servoing methods, as well as over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning approaches.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Sound Method:** The paper presents a novel and well-motivated synthesis of pre-trained features, learned dynamics, and reinforcement learning. The central technical contribution—structuring the policy and Q-function to enable a highly efficient, linear FQI update—is sound, elegant, and directly addresses the critical challenge of sample efficiency in robotic learning.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The claims are substantiated by a comprehensive set of experiments that systematically compare the proposed method against multiple relevant and strong baselines. The results convincingly demonstrate superior performance and a dramatic improvement in sample efficiency (over two orders of magnitude compared to a standard deep RL approach), providing robust evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed technique.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper's primary strength lies in its effective and pragmatic approach to a challenging robotics problem. Instead of attempting to learn a complex, end-to-end policy from scratch, the authors intelligently decompose the problem. By leveraging pre-trained VGG features, the method inherits a powerful, general-purpose visual representation, bypassing the need for vast amounts of task-specific data for representation learning.

The choice of a bilinear dynamics model is well-justified. It is expressive enough to capture the first-order effects of motion on the feature maps yet simple enough to be learned from a relatively small dataset (100 trajectories). This model-based component is crucial for planning and control.

The most significant contribution is the reinforcement learning formulation. The insight to design the servoing policy (Equation 3) such that its corresponding Q-function is linear in the learnable weights (Section 5.2) is technically sound and impactful. It transforms the difficult problem of learning a Q-function into a simple, regularized least-squares regression problem. This formulation is the key enabler of the method's remarkable sample efficiency, as demonstrated by the ability to learn a competent policy from only 20 trajectories. Standard model-free methods like TRPO require thousands of trajectories to achieve inferior performance, highlighting the strength of this model-based RL approach.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and provides strong support for the paper's claims. The ablation study on different feature layers (Figure 5) provides valuable insight into which level of semantic abstraction is most suitable for this task. The direct comparison against TRPO (Figure 6) quantifies the massive gains in sample efficiency. Furthermore, the comparisons to classical Image-Based Visual Servoing (IBVS) methods, including one augmented with a state-of-the-art visual tracker, ground the work within the established robotics literature and demonstrate its superior robustness and performance in a dynamic environment with visual distractors. The evaluation on both seen and novel cars confirms the generalization capabilities of the learned system.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions aimed at further strengthening the paper and are not critical factors in the decision.

*   **Clarity of FQI Algorithm:** The motivation for the two-step update in the FQI iteration (Equations 5 and 6) could be explained more intuitively. While technically described, a brief explanation of why separating the optimization of the scaling factor `α` and bias `b` from the main weights `θ` is beneficial (e.g., for stability or convergence speed) would improve clarity for the reader.
*   **Simulation-to-Reality Gap:** The work is evaluated entirely in simulation. A discussion of potential challenges in deploying this method on a physical robot would be a valuable addition. For instance, how might the performance of the learned bilinear dynamics model be affected by factors not present in the simulation, such as camera motion blur, hardware latencies, or unmodeled aerodynamic effects?
*   **Dynamics Model Training Data:** The dynamics model is trained on data collected from a hand-coded policy. While effective, this raises a question about the generality of the learned model. It would be useful to discuss the sensitivity of the system to the quality of this data-collection policy.
*   **Computational Cost:** The paper notes that the tracker-based baseline runs at 1Hz while the proposed method runs at 16Hz. It would be beneficial to provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost at inference time, particularly the time spent on feature extraction vs. the dynamics prediction and control calculation steps. This would help in assessing the real-time feasibility of the approach.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

*   The linearity of the Q-function approximator is key to the method's sample efficiency. Could you elaborate on the potential limitations of this choice? In what types of visual servoing tasks might this linear approximation be insufficient, and would it be possible to extend the FQI framework to handle a more expressive, non-linear Q-function approximator while retaining reasonable sample efficiency?
*   The system demonstrates impressive generalization to novel car models. Have you performed any experiments to test its robustness to more significant domain shifts, such as different lighting conditions (e.g., night), weather, or different background environments (e.g., rural vs. urban)?
*   How crucial is the choice of pre-trained network? Did you experiment with other architectures besides VGG (e.g., ResNet), and does the performance depend heavily on the features being trained on a large, diverse dataset like ImageNet?
