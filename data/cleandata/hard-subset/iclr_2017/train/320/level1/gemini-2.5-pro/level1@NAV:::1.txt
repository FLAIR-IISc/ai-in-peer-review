This paper presents a novel and highly sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing controllers. The key contribution is an elegant framework that combines the strengths of pre-trained visual representations, a learned dynamics model in feature space, and a tailored reinforcement learning algorithm. Specifically, the authors use features from a VGG network, learn a simple bilinear model to predict how these features change in response to robot actions, and then use a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn a weighting over the feature channels. This weighting allows the controller to focus on task-relevant visual information. The method is validated on a challenging simulated car-following task, demonstrating robust performance with significantly less training data than standard model-free deep RL approaches.

**Decision: Accept**

The primary reasons for my decision are the paper's well-motivated approach to achieving sample efficiency and the strong empirical results that convincingly support its claims.

The paper’s main strength lies in its principled decomposition of the visual servoing problem. Instead of learning a complex end-to-end policy, the authors leverage three distinct components: a powerful, pre-trained feature extractor; a simple, learnable local dynamics model; and a highly efficient RL algorithm for learning a final policy. This design circumvents the data-hungriness of both complex video prediction models and model-free deep RL. The choice of a bilinear dynamics model is particularly clever, as it allows for an analytic policy and a structured Q-function approximator, which in turn enables the highly sample-efficient FQI procedure. The experimental results are comprehensive, providing comparisons against strong baselines from both classical robotics (IBVS) and modern deep RL (TRPO). The reported two-orders-of-magnitude improvement in sample efficiency over TRPO is a significant result and clearly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed model-based feature-space control approach.

I have some additional feedback aimed at further improving the paper. While the synthetic experiments are compelling, a simple real-world demonstration would greatly strengthen the claims of robustness. The intuition behind the two-step update in the FQI algorithm (solving for scaling factor α before the full weights θ) could also be explained more explicitly.

I would also appreciate clarification on the following questions:
1.  How sensitive is the performance of the learned dynamics model to the data collection policy used to generate its training set?
2.  The Q-function approximation is linear in the feature prediction errors. Could you comment on the limitations of this functional form for tasks with more complex, non-quadratic cost structures?
