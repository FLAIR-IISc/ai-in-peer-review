

# Peer Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contribution

This paper presents a compelling approach to visual servoing that effectively combines learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to create a system that can quickly adapt to new targets with minimal data. The authors address a significant limitation in traditional visual servoing approaches that rely on manually designed features and analytical dynamics models. Their key contribution is a method that uses pre-trained deep features (from VGG) in conjunction with a bilinear predictive model to learn a visual servo that is robust to visual variation, changes in viewpoint, and occlusions. The authors demonstrate that their approach can learn an effective visual servo on a complex synthetic car-following benchmark using just 20 training trajectory samples, achieving substantial improvement over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and showing more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample efficiency over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The method is elegant in its simplicity, using a weighted multiscale feature representation with a one-step lookahead policy optimized via a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm.

## 2. Decision

Accept

Key reasons: The paper addresses an important problem in robotics with a well-motivated and scientifically rigorous approach. The empirical results strongly support the claims of sample efficiency and performance improvements over multiple baselines. The method is both theoretically sound and practically effective, with clear demonstration of the benefits of combining pre-trained features with learned dynamics and reinforcement learning.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is exceptionally well-positioned in the literature. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing visual servoing approaches and justify their choice of using pre-trained features (VGG) based on prior work showing their usefulness for other vision tasks. The motivation for learning feature weights via reinforcement learning rather than hand-engineering is particularly strong, as it addresses a key challenge in visual servoing where the same features may contain irrelevant information.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and rigorous. The authors compare against multiple relevant baselines including traditional visual servoing methods (IBVS with hand-crafted features), end-to-end deep RL approaches, and other learning-based methods. The results consistently demonstrate that their approach outperforms all baselines in terms of both performance and sample efficiency. The ablation studies provide valuable insights into why certain design choices (like feature layers and weighting methods) work better than others.

The sample efficiency claim is particularly impressive and well-supported. Learning an effective visual servo with just 20 training trajectories is a significant achievement in the context of reinforcement learning for visual servoing. The authors provide clear evidence that their method achieves more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample efficiency over standard deep RL methods, which is a major contribution to the field.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide more detailed discussion of the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding how it would perform with more severe occlusions or in more complex real-world environments beyond the synthetic car-following benchmark.

2. Include a more thorough analysis of why certain feature layers (conv1_2 through conv4_3) work better than others, especially why the deepest layer (conv5_3) is less suitable. This would help readers understand the underlying principles of feature selection for visual servoing.

3. Clarify the computational trade-offs of their method. While they note their method runs at 16Hz compared to the tracker-based method's 1Hz, they should discuss the potential for further speed improvements (e.g., using a more efficient feature extractor than VGG).

4. Consider adding a brief discussion of how their method might be extended to handle multiple target objects or more complex manipulation tasks beyond car following.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that deeper VGG features (conv5_3) are not suitable for approximating Q-values, hypothesizing it might be too spatially invariant. Could you provide more specific analysis of what aspects of the feature representation make it unsuitable for visual servoing? Is this a fundamental limitation of the feature space or could it be mitigated with additional processing?

2. In the experiments, the authors use a synthetic environment with ground truth depth values for classical IBVS methods. In real-world applications, depth would need to be estimated from the camera feed. How would the performance of their method compare to classical IBVS when depth is estimated rather than provided?

3. The paper demonstrates robustness to occlusions in the synthetic environment. Could you share specific examples of occlusion scenarios in the supplementary videos that demonstrate the limits of this robustness? This would help readers better understand the practical applicability of the approach.

4. The authors note that the proposed method is more sample-efficient than standard deep RL approaches. How does the sample efficiency compare to other model-based reinforcement learning methods that also use dynamics models, such as MBPO or PETS?
