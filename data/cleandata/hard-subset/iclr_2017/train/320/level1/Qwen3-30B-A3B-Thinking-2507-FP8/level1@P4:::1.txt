

# Review for ICLR_2017

1. Summary of contributions:
The paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained visual features (from a VGG network), a bilinear predictive dynamics model, and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning algorithm (Fitted Q-Iteration). The authors demonstrate that standard deep features from ImageNet classification can be effectively used for visual servoing, with the key innovation being a method to learn optimal weights for these features to focus on relevant target objects while ignoring distractors. The approach enables learning an effective visual servo for target following using only 20 training trajectory samples, showing substantial improvement over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and achieving more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample efficiency compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The method is shown to be robust to visual variations, changes in viewing angle, appearance, and occlusions.

2. Decision:
Accept. The paper makes a clear contribution to a significant problem in robotics with strong empirical support.

3. Supporting arguments:
The paper addresses a well-motivated problem in visual servoing that has been limited by reliance on manually designed features and dynamics models. The authors provide a thorough literature review that clearly positions their work within the field, highlighting the limitations of prior approaches and justifying their use of pre-trained features and reinforcement learning.

The experimental results are scientifically rigorous and well-supported. The authors demonstrate clear improvements over multiple baselines, including:
- Pixel-based visual servoing (which is susceptible to occlusions)
- Hand-designed keypoints (ORB features)
- End-to-end deep reinforcement learning (TRPO)
- Traditional visual servoing methods (IBVS with ORB or C-COT tracker)

The sample efficiency claim is particularly well-supported, with the authors showing that their method requires only 20 training trajectories while other methods require hundreds or thousands. The ablation studies comparing different feature representations and optimization methods provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of their approach. The experiments are conducted in a well-defined synthetic car-following benchmark with consistent evaluation protocols across all methods.

4. Additional feedback:
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of why VGG conv5_3 features performed worse than other layers. A deeper exploration of the spatial invariance properties of this feature layer would strengthen the discussion of feature selection.
- While the paper demonstrates robustness to occlusions, providing specific quantitative analysis of performance degradation under severe occlusion scenarios would further support this claim.
- The computational efficiency analysis could be expanded to include a more detailed breakdown of time spent on feature extraction versus servoing, particularly for different network architectures.
- The paper mentions that the approach works with "any pre-trained features" but doesn't provide examples of other feature sets (beyond VGG) that might be applicable. Including a brief discussion of alternative feature sources would broaden the contribution.

5. Questions for authors:
- The paper reports that VGG conv5_3 features performed worse than other layers. What specific properties of this feature layer might be causing this issue, and how could the method be adapted to better handle such features?
- The paper claims robustness to occlusions but doesn't provide detailed analysis of how performance degrades with increasing occlusion. Could the authors provide quantitative results showing performance at different occlusion levels?
- The paper states that the approach can learn with "just 20 training trajectory samples" - how does this number scale with more complex environments or tasks, and what is the minimum number of samples required for reliable performance across different target objects?
