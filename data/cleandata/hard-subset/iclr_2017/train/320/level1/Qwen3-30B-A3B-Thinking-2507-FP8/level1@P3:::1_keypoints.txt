- Paper proposes a novel visual-servoing method combining pre-trained VGG features, a bilinear predictive dynamics model, and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration RL algorithm.  
- Method automatically learns weights for relevant visual features to minimize predicted vs target feature error.  
- Demonstrates strong sample efficiency (effective with ~20 training trajectories) and >100× better sample efficiency than standard model-free deep RL on a car-following task.  
- Empirical results show superiority over pixel-based methods, hand-designed keypoints, end-to-end CNN policies, and classical IBVS.  
- Technical approach is well-motivated: leverages Imagenet-pretrained features and a bilinear dynamics model justified by prior work.  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive with multiple baselines, ablation studies, and tests on seen and novel cars for generalization.  
- Practical advantages include real-time operation (16 Hz vs tracker’s 1 Hz) and demonstrated robustness to visual variations and occlusions.  
- Reviewer recommends acceptance, citing significant contribution and strong empirical support.  
- Request for more details on the synthetic environment complexity (number of car models, city layouts, and scene objects during training/testing).  
- Suggestion to analyze other pre-trained feature extractors (e.g., ResNet, Inception) to test generality beyond VGG.  
- Ask to clarify impact of VGG modifications (removed max-pooling, dilated convolutions) vs using vanilla pre-trained features.  
- Recommend a more thorough discussion of limitations, including performance on targets with very different appearance and severe partial occlusions.  
- Question: why VGG conv5_3 features were less suitable—too spatially invariant or other properties?  
- Question: clarify total data required including how many trajectories were used to train the dynamics model.  
- Question: provide specific occlusion scenarios tested and quantitative performance under those conditions.  
- Question: how the method would scale to more complex tasks, multiple targets, or different target object classes (e.g., people or other vehicles).
