

# Review for ICLR_2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a compelling approach to visual servoing that effectively combines learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to create a sample-efficient visual servoing mechanism. The authors propose a novel method that uses pre-trained deep features (from VGG network) and a bilinear predictive model to learn visual servoing without requiring manual feature engineering or extensive application-specific model development. The key innovation is the use of a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn optimal feature weights, enabling the system to adapt quickly to new targets with minimal data (only 20 training trajectory samples). The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves robust visual servoing that is resilient to visual variations, changes in viewing angle, appearance, and occlusions, while significantly outperforming conventional methods based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and provides strong empirical evidence for the claims.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper addresses a significant and timely problem in robotics (sample-efficient visual servoing) with a novel and well-justified approach that combines multiple techniques in a novel way.
2. The empirical results demonstrate substantial improvements in sample efficiency (more than two orders of magnitude) over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms, which is a major contribution to the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a strong case for its approach. The problem of visual servoing is well-motivated, with a clear explanation of the limitations of current approaches (reliance on manual feature engineering and analytical models) and how the authors' method overcomes these limitations. The paper is well-placed in the literature, with thorough related work that accurately positions their contribution.

The empirical evaluation is rigorous and comprehensive. The authors compare their method against multiple strong baselines, including:
- Conventional approaches based on image pixels
- Hand-designed keypoints (ORB)
- State-of-the-art visual trackers (C-COT)
- End-to-end deep reinforcement learning (TRPO)
- Classical image-based visual servoing (IBVS)

The results consistently demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms all baselines, particularly in terms of sample efficiency. The claim of learning an effective visual servo with just 20 training trajectory samples is impressive and well-supported by the experiments. The ablation studies (e.g., comparing different feature representations, different optimization methods) provide strong evidence for the specific contributions of the proposed approach.

The theoretical justification for the bilinear dynamics model and the Q-function approximation is sound, and the paper provides sufficient mathematical detail to understand the approach. The implementation details are thorough, with clear descriptions of the environment, training procedures, and evaluation metrics.

The paper's contribution is both practically significant (enabling quick adaptation to new targets) and theoretically interesting (sample-efficient reinforcement learning for visual servoing). The claimed improvements in sample efficiency are particularly noteworthy, as this is a major challenge in applying reinforcement learning to robotics.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these suggestions would further improve it:

1. The paper mentions that VGG conv5_3 features perform worse than other VGG layers. A more detailed explanation of why this might be the case would be helpful. Is it due to excessive spatial invariance? Does the paper's approach to feature extraction (removing max-pooling layers) make conv5_3 more or less suitable for visual servoing?

2. The paper claims that their method is robust to occlusions, but the experiments don't explicitly test severe occlusions (e.g., when the car is completely covered by another object). A more thorough evaluation of the method's robustness to occlusions would strengthen the claims.

3. The paper states that their method runs at about 16Hz, while the tracker-based method runs at about 1Hz. It would be helpful to provide more details about the hardware used for these speed comparisons, as the computational speed of deep learning methods can vary significantly across different hardware platforms.

4. The paper mentions that they use a modified VGG network with dilated convolutions, but it's not clear how this modification specifically helps with visual servoing. A more detailed explanation of why this modification is necessary would improve the paper.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper mentions that the deepest feature representation (VGG conv5_3) is not as suitable for approximating Q-values. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case? Is it because conv5_3 features are too spatially invariant, or is there another factor at play?

2. The paper states that "VGG features are not optimized for this task and their representation loses some information that is useful for servoing." Could you provide more specific examples of what information is lost, and how the bilinear dynamics model compensates for this loss?

3. The paper claims substantial improvement over conventional approaches, but it's not entirely clear how much improvement is due to the learned feature weights versus the bilinear dynamics model. Could you provide an ablation study showing the contribution of each component to the overall performance?

4. The paper mentions that the feature dynamics model is trained using a hand-coded policy. How does the quality of the hand-coded policy affect the performance of the final visual servo? Would the method still work with a very poor hand-coded policy?
