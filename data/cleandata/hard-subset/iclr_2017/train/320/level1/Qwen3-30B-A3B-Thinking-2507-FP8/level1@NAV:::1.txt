

This paper presents an interesting approach to visual servoing that combines learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to achieve sample-efficient adaptation to new targets. The authors demonstrate how standard deep features from pre-trained networks (VGG) can be used with a bilinear predictive model to learn robust visual servoing mechanisms, requiring only 20 training trajectory samples. They show substantial improvements over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and achieve more than two orders of magnitude better sample-efficiency compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a strong case for the problem's importance and provides a well-motivated approach that builds effectively on prior work in visual servoing, feature learning, and reinforcement learning. The experimental results are scientifically rigorous, with careful comparisons to multiple baselines across both seen and novel targets, and the sample-efficiency claim is compellingly supported.

The paper's key strength is its clear demonstration of how to combine pre-trained features with a simple, interpretable servoing mechanism that learns to weight features appropriately through a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm. The method's computational efficiency (16Hz execution speed) and robustness to visual variations, occlusions, and viewpoint changes are particularly impressive.

For improvement, I suggest clarifying the limitations of the synthetic environment and providing more concrete discussion of how this approach might generalize to real-world scenarios. The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of when the feature weighting mechanism fails and how it might be improved for particularly challenging visual variations.

I would like to ask the authors: How would the approach perform with significantly more complex occlusions that completely hide the target object for extended periods? Also, could you provide more details about the specific VGG layers used for feature extraction and why those particular layers were chosen over others?
