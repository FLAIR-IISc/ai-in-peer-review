

# Peer Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained deep features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to create a system capable of learning effective visual servoing mechanisms with minimal data. The authors propose using features from a pre-trained VGG network (trained for ImageNet classification) combined with a bilinear predictive model to enable robust visual servoing that handles visual variations, viewpoint changes, and occlusions. The key innovation is a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm that automatically learns weights for the most relevant visual features, allowing the system to minimize the error between predicted and target feature values. The method demonstrates impressive sample efficiency, requiring only 20 training trajectories to learn an effective visual servo for a complex car-following task, significantly outperforming conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints and achieving more than two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency than standard model-free deep RL algorithms.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to visual servoing by demonstrating how to learn a visual servoing mechanism with minimal data, addressing a critical limitation in the field. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and the claims are strongly supported by empirical evidence.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution—learning visual servoing with minimal data—addresses a fundamental limitation in traditional visual servoing approaches that rely on manually designed features and analytical dynamics models. The authors properly position their work within the literature, acknowledging that prior learning-based approaches have required large numbers of samples and haven't leveraged state-of-the-art deep features. Their approach is well-motivated by the observation that pre-trained features from large vision datasets like ImageNet have proven useful for many visual tasks, and they provide a compelling rationale for why these features might be suitable for visual servoing.

The technical approach is strong and well-executed. The use of a bilinear dynamics model is well-justified by prior work, and the fitted Q-iteration algorithm for learning feature weights is clever and efficient. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with multiple baselines (pixel-based, hand-designed features, end-to-end CNN policies, classical IBVS), thorough ablation studies, and evaluation on both seen and novel cars to test generalization. The sample efficiency claim (2 orders of magnitude better than standard model-free deep RL) is well-supported by the experiments, with the authors showing that their method achieves good performance with just 20 training trajectories, while TRPO-based methods require over 100 times more trajectories to achieve comparable performance.

The paper also demonstrates practical advantages, with the method running at 16Hz compared to the tracker-based method's 1Hz, and providing a clear demonstration of robustness to occlusions and visual variations that the pixel-based approach fails to handle.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, consider the following:

- Provide more details about the complexity of the synthetic car-following environment. How many different car models and city layouts were used? How many objects were present in the scene during training and testing?

- Include a more systematic analysis of how different pre-trained feature extractors (e.g., ResNet, Inception) might affect performance. The choice of VGG features is somewhat arbitrary, and it would be valuable to understand if the method generalizes to other feature extractors.

- Clarify the impact of the VGG modifications (removing max-pooling layers and using dilated convolutions). How much of the performance gain is due to these modifications versus simply using pre-trained features?

- Discuss the limitations of the approach more thoroughly. For example, how does the method perform when the target object is significantly different from the training objects (e.g., a car with a completely different appearance)? How does the method handle cases where the target object is partially occluded by other objects in the scene?

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that VGG conv5_3 features are less suitable for the task. Could you provide more insight into why this is the case? Is it due to the feature being too spatially invariant, or are there other properties of these features that make them less effective for visual servoing?

2. The paper mentions that the method uses "20 training trajectory samples" for reinforcement learning. Could you clarify the total amount of data required for the entire training process, including the data needed for the dynamics model training? How many trajectories were used to train the dynamics model?

3. The authors state that the method is robust to occlusions, but the paper doesn't provide specific examples of how the method handles challenging occlusion scenarios (e.g., when the target car is partially obscured by buildings or other vehicles). Could you provide more details about the occlusion scenarios tested and the method's performance in those cases?

4. How would the method scale to more complex tasks with multiple target objects or when the target object is not a car (e.g., a person or a different type of vehicle)? Have you considered testing the approach on more diverse target objects?
