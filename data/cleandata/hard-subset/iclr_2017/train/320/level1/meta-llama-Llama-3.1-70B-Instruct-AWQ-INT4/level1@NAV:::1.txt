**Summary of the Paper's Contribution**

This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing by combining learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The authors demonstrate that standard deep features, in this case, taken from a model trained for object classification, can be used together with a bilinear predictive model to learn an effective visual servo that is robust to visual variation, changes in viewing angle and appearance, and occlusions. The approach is evaluated on a complex synthetic car following benchmark, showing substantial improvement over conventional approaches and an improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

**Decision: Accept**

**Reasons for Acceptance**

1. **Well-motivated approach**: The paper tackles a specific problem in visual servoing, namely, the need for manual design of visual features and dynamics models, which limits the applicability of visual servoing approaches. The authors propose a well-motivated approach that combines learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning.
2. **Strong experimental results**: The paper presents strong experimental results on a complex synthetic car following benchmark, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach in terms of performance and sample efficiency.

**Supporting Arguments**

1. **Technical soundness**: The paper presents a technically sound approach to visual servoing, combining learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method and its components.
2. **Comparative evaluation**: The paper presents a comprehensive comparative evaluation of the proposed approach against other methods, including classical image-based visual servoing, position-based visual servoing, and model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The results demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach in terms of performance and sample efficiency.
