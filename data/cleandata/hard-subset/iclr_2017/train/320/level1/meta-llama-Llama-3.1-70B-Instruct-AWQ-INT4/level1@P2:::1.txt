**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing, which involves learning visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to learn visual servoing mechanisms. The authors focus on target following and demonstrate that standard deep features, combined with a bilinear predictive model, can be used to learn an effective visual servo that is robust to visual variation, changes in viewing angle and appearance, and occlusions. The approach is evaluated on a complex synthetic car following benchmark and shows substantial improvement over conventional approaches.

**Decision**

Based on the reviewing criteria, I decide to **accept** this paper. The two key reasons for this choice are:

1. The paper tackles a well-defined and relevant problem in the field of robotics, specifically visual servoing, and proposes a novel approach that combines learning visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning.
2. The approach is well-motivated and grounded in the literature, and the authors provide a clear and thorough evaluation of their method on a complex synthetic car following benchmark.

**Supporting arguments**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear structure and concise explanations of the proposed approach. The authors provide a thorough review of the literature on visual servoing and clearly motivate their approach as a novel combination of learning visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning.

The evaluation of the approach is comprehensive and convincing, with a range of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on a complex synthetic car following benchmark. The authors also provide a detailed comparison with other approaches, including classical image-based visual servoing and deep reinforcement learning methods.

The use of a bilinear predictive model to learn the dynamics of the visual features is a key contribution of the paper, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how this model is used in the visual servoing framework. The use of fitted Q-iteration to learn the weights of the features is also a novel aspect of the approach, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how this method is used to optimize the servoing policy.
