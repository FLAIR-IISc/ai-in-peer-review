This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing that combines learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to learn visual servoing mechanisms. The authors demonstrate that standard deep features, in their case taken from a model trained for object classification, can be used together with a bilinear predictive model to learn an effective visual servo that is robust to visual variation, changes in viewing angle and appearance, and occlusions.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper addresses a specific question/problem of visual servoing, which is a classic problem in robotics. The approach is well-motivated, and the authors provide a thorough review of related work. The paper supports its claims through extensive experiments on a complex synthetic car following benchmark, demonstrating substantial improvement over conventional approaches and model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

**Supporting arguments for the decision:**

1. The paper tackles a specific problem in visual servoing, which is a well-defined and relevant research question. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of standard visual servoing approaches and the benefits of using learned visual features and predictive dynamics models.
2. The approach is well-motivated, and the authors provide a thorough review of related work in visual servoing, reinforcement learning, and deep learning. They clearly explain how their approach builds upon and improves existing methods.
3. The experimental evaluation is extensive and well-designed, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach on a complex synthetic car following benchmark. The authors provide a thorough comparison with other methods, including model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms and classical image-based visual servoing.

**Additional feedback to improve the paper:**

1. While the paper provides a thorough review of related work, it would be helpful to include a more detailed comparison with other deep learning-based approaches to visual servoing, such as those using end-to-end learning or imitation learning.
2. The authors could provide more insight into the choice of hyperparameters and the sensitivity of the results to these choices. For example, how does the performance of the algorithm change with different values of the regularization coefficient or the number of sampling iterations?
3. The paper could benefit from more visualizations of the learned features and the servoing process. This would help to provide a better understanding of how the algorithm works and how it achieves its results.

**Questions to the authors:**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their approach to more complex scenarios, such as multi-object tracking or servoing in dynamic environments?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the computational efficiency of their approach, including the time complexity of the algorithm and the memory requirements?
3. How do the authors plan to address the issue of overfitting, which is a common problem in deep learning-based approaches?
