This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, and proposes a method that combines learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The motivation is strong: traditional visual servoing relies on hand-engineered features and models, limiting generalization. The authors aim to create a more adaptable system that requires less data for new targets.

I recommend **rejecting** the paper. While the idea is promising, and the experiments show improvements over baseline methods, the paper lacks sufficient clarity and rigor to confidently assess the contribution. Specifically, the experimental setup feels undertheorized, the ablation studies are incomplete, and the improvement over the baselines feels somewhat overstated given the complexity added.

The central argument is that pre-trained deep features, combined with a bilinear dynamics model and sample-efficient reinforcement learning (Fitted Q-iteration), allow for effective visual servoing with minimal data. The authors demonstrate this on a synthetic car-following benchmark. However, several issues weaken this argument.

First, the details of the environment and the training data generation are not sufficiently clear. While the code is available, simply stating that the hand-coded policy navigates the quadcopter “around” the car with Gaussian noise is insufficient. The specific parameters of this noise and the characteristics of the car’s motion are crucial and need to be reported. How representative is the training data of the test data? Is the distribution of car motions and initial conditions the same? Without this, it's difficult to assess the generalization ability.

Second, the comparison to baselines isn’t entirely convincing. The chosen baselines (classical IBVS with ORB, tracker-based IBVS, and TRPO policies) are not all directly comparable in terms of complexity or data efficiency. The TRPO baselines, for example, are trained end-to-end, meaning they have more freedom to learn representations optimized for the task, potentially leveling the playing field. The authors claim the improvement over TRPO is significant (one and a half times the cost), but this seems relatively small considering the additional complexity of the proposed method and the use of pre-trained features. A more thorough ablation study, isolating the contribution of each component (learned dynamics, FQI, feature weighting) would be beneficial.

Third, the choice of the bilinear dynamics model, while justified, is not thoroughly explored. Why is a bilinear model preferred over other alternatives, such as a neural network? The justification hinges on simplicity and tractability for FQI, but the trade-off with potentially reduced accuracy is not discussed. Furthermore, the performance of different scales of VGG features is presented but the reasoning for why deeper features eventually degrade performance is a bit hand-wavy ("too spatially invariant"). A more detailed analysis of the learned dynamics would strengthen the paper.

Finally, the paper is a bit verbose and could benefit from more concise writing. The frequent reiteration of the motivation and problem statement adds little value.



Here are some suggestions for improvement:

*   **Provide more details about the experimental setup:** Clearly describe the environment, the training data generation process, and the parameters used for the hand-coded policy.
*   **Strengthen the baseline comparisons:** Include more competitive baselines, such as other state-of-the-art reinforcement learning algorithms specifically designed for continuous control.
*   **Conduct a more thorough ablation study:** Evaluate the contribution of each component of the proposed method (learned dynamics, FQI, feature weighting).
*   **Analyze the learned dynamics:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the learned bilinear model, including visualizations of the learned parameters and a discussion of its limitations.
*   **Address the spatial invariance issue:** Investigate why deeper VGG features degrade performance.
*   **Clarify the significance of the improvements:** Frame the performance gains more precisely and discuss their practical implications.
*   **Streamline the writing:** Focus on conveying the key ideas in a concise and clear manner.

Questions for the authors:

*   Can you provide more details about the distribution of car motions and initial conditions in the training and test datasets?
*   How sensitive is the performance of the proposed method to the parameters of the hand-coded policy used to generate the training data?
*   What is the computational cost of the bilinear dynamics model compared to other alternatives, such as a neural network?
*   Why does the performance degrade when using deeper VGG features? Is it purely due to spatial invariance, or are there other factors at play?
*   Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational time for each component of the algorithm (feature extraction, dynamics prediction, reinforcement learning)?
