## Review of "Learning Visual Servoing with Reinforcement Learning"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, and proposes a novel approach that combines learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The central claim is that this combination allows for quick adaptation to new targets with minimal training data, overcoming the limitations of traditional methods reliant on manual feature engineering and analytical models. The paper convincingly demonstrates this by leveraging pre-trained deep features and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is well-motivated, addresses a significant problem in robotics, and is supported by strong experimental results, particularly the impressive sample efficiency achieved (20 training trajectories). The improvement over conventional and standard deep RL approaches is substantial and clearly presented. 

The strength of this work lies in its holistic approach. The authors intelligently combine existing strengths – pre-trained features capturing general visual representations, a bilinear dynamics model providing a predictive element, and reinforcement learning for adaptation – into a cohesive framework. The use of fitted Q-iteration is a clever choice, leading to computational efficiency and demonstrating superior performance. The synthetic car following benchmark provides a challenging and relevant test environment. The ablation studies, comparing different feature representations and optimization methods, add further rigor.

However, the paper could be strengthened by addressing a few points. While the synthetic environment is well-chosen, demonstrating the approach on real-world data would significantly increase its impact.  The discussion of the limitations of the bilinear dynamics model could be expanded.  Specifically, under what conditions might this model break down and how might this be addressed?  Additionally, providing a more intuitive explanation for why deeper VGG features beyond a certain layer *hurt* performance would be valuable. 

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors: 
1. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of pre-trained features?  Were other pre-trained models (e.g., ResNet) explored?
2. The regularization parameter (ν) in the FQI algorithm – how was this value selected, and is there a sensitivity analysis regarding its impact on performance?
3. Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the bilinear dynamics model compared to a direct end-to-end learned policy, considering inference time in a real-time control setting?
