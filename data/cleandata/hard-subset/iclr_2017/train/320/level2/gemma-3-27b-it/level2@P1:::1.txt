This paper tackles the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, aiming to combine learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to achieve quick adaptation to new targets with limited data. The approach focuses on servoing in the space of learned visual features (from a pre-trained VGG network) and uses a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn appropriate feature weights.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The core idea of leveraging pre-trained features and combining them with learned dynamics and reinforcement learning is compelling, and the results presented demonstrate a significant improvement in sample efficiency compared to existing methods, particularly standard deep reinforcement learning. The demonstrated ability to learn an effective visual servo with only 20 trajectory samples is a strong contribution.

The paper is well-motivated. Visual servoing is a classic problem, and the limitations of traditional approaches (manual feature engineering, reliance on accurate dynamics models) are clearly articulated. The authors effectively position their work within the existing literature, highlighting the challenges of applying deep learning to this domain, specifically the need for sample efficiency. The combination of pre-trained features, bilinear dynamics modeling, and fitted Q-iteration is presented as a logical solution to these challenges. 

The paper provides strong support for its claims. The experimental results on the synthetic car following benchmark are convincing. The authors provide a thorough comparison to several baselines, including a conventional approach based on image pixels and hand-designed keypoints, as well as standard deep reinforcement learning algorithms. The quantitative results clearly demonstrate the superior performance of their approach, particularly in terms of sample efficiency. The ablation studies examining different feature representations and optimization methods further strengthen the validity of the findings. The inclusion of qualitative results (sample trajectories) adds further insight. The provided code and videos facilitate reproducibility and understanding.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not impacting the decision):**

*   **Clarity of the Bilinear Dynamics:** While the explanation of the bilinear dynamics model is present, it could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of *why* a bilinear model is suitable for representing the feature dynamics. Briefly discussing the connection to the Jacobian and its simplification in this context would be helpful.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions regularization (ν), but doesn’t elaborate on its sensitivity. A brief discussion of how the performance changes with different values of ν would add robustness to the findings.
*   **Generalization to More Complex Scenarios:** The experiments are conducted in a synthetic environment. While this allows for controlled comparisons, it would be valuable to briefly discuss the potential challenges and considerations for applying this approach to real-world scenarios (e.g., more complex visual variations, imperfect dynamics models).
*   **Fitted Q-Iteration Details:** A more detailed explanation of the fitted Q-iteration algorithm's implementation, specifically how the linear system is solved (in Equation 5), would be beneficial.
*   **Appendix References:** Several references are mentioned as being in the appendix but are not easily accessible in the submitted version. Please ensure all references are readily available.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why the deepest VGG feature representation (conv5\_3) performed worse than the shallower ones? Was it due to a loss of spatial information or other factors?
2.  The paper mentions that the feature dynamics are trained with a hand-coded policy. How sensitive is the performance to the quality of this hand-coded policy and the data generated from it?
3.  How would the approach scale to higher-dimensional state spaces or more complex control tasks? Are there any potential bottlenecks or limitations?
4.  In the discussion of the related work, you mention using a "locally connected bilinear model". Could you provide some intuition as to why the sparse connections are helpful? Is this purely for computational efficiency, or does it provide regularization benefits as well?
