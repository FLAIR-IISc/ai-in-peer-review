This paper addresses the problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, and proposes a method combining learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The core idea is to perform servoing in the space of learned visual features (from a pre-trained VGG network) rather than directly on pixels or hand-designed keypoints. A bilinear predictive model estimates feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm learns feature weights for the servoing task. The work demonstrates strong performance on a synthetic car following benchmark, achieving substantial improvement over conventional approaches and significantly better sample efficiency than standard deep reinforcement learning.

The decision is to **accept** with minor revisions. The paper tackles a relevant problem in robotics, proposes a novel and well-motivated approach, and supports its claims with compelling experimental results. The improvement in sample efficiency over existing methods is particularly significant.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method. The motivation for combining learned features, predictive dynamics, and reinforcement learning is clearly articulated. The experimental setup is well-defined, and the comparisons to baseline methods are thorough. The inclusion of a video demonstration is also a strong positive. 

However, several areas could be improved. First, the choice of the bilinear model for predicting visual dynamics feels somewhat arbitrary. While the paper provides some justification, a more detailed exploration of alternative dynamics models, even if briefly, would strengthen the argument. Second, the explanation of the fitted Q-iteration algorithm could be more accessible. While the mathematical formulation is presented, a more intuitive explanation of how the algorithm selects the most relevant features would be beneficial. Third, the hyperparameter sensitivity of the proposed approach is not fully investigated. Reporting how performance changes with different regularization coefficients (ν) or learning rates would offer valuable insights. Finally, while the synthetic environment provides a good initial evaluation, demonstrating the method's effectiveness in a real-world setting, even with limited data, would significantly increase its impact. 

Specific questions for the authors:

*   Regarding the bilinear model: Were other dynamics models considered? If so, why were they rejected? What are the limitations of the bilinear model, and how might they be addressed in future work?
*   Regarding the fitted Q-iteration: Could the feature weighting learned by the algorithm be interpreted or visualized in any way? For example, can we understand *which* features are being prioritized for different targets or scenarios?
*   Regarding the regularization parameter ν: How sensitive is the performance to the choice of ν?  A simple ablation study showing results with different values of ν would be helpful.
*   Regarding real-world applicability: Are there any plans to evaluate this method on a real robot platform? What challenges are anticipated when transferring the method to a real-world environment?
