**Summary of the Paper**

The authors present a novel approach to visual servoing that combines learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The goal is to design algorithms that can learn a visual servo using low amounts of data of the target in question, enabling quick adaptation to new targets. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a complex synthetic car following benchmark, achieving substantial improvement over conventional approaches and showing an improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem they are trying to solve. The approach is novel and combines multiple techniques from computer vision and reinforcement learning. The experiments are thorough, and the results are impressive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Supporting Arguments**

1. **Well-motivated approach**: The authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of traditional visual servoing approaches and the benefits of using learned visual features and predictive dynamics models. They also provide a thorough review of related work, highlighting the contributions of their approach.
2. **Effective combination of techniques**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of combining learned visual features, learned predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The use of pre-trained features, bilinear predictive models, and fitted Q-iteration allows for efficient and effective learning of visual servoing mechanisms.
3. **Impressive experimental results**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a complex synthetic car following benchmark, achieving substantial improvement over conventional approaches and showing an improvement in sample-efficiency of more than two orders of magnitude over standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

**Additional Feedback**

1. **Clarity and organization**: The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. However, some sections, such as the related work section, could be more concise.
2. **Experimental evaluation**: While the authors provide a thorough experimental evaluation, it would be beneficial to include more details about the experimental setup, such as the specific parameters used for the algorithms and the computational resources required.
3. **Real-world applications**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a synthetic benchmark. It would be beneficial to discuss the potential real-world applications of their approach and the challenges that need to be addressed to deploy their system in real-world scenarios.

**Questions for the Authors**

1. Can you provide more details about the computational resources required to train and deploy your system?
2. How do you plan to address the challenges of deploying your system in real-world scenarios, such as dealing with varying lighting conditions, occlusions, and other sources of noise?
3. Can you discuss the potential applications of your approach beyond visual servoing, such as in areas like robotics, autonomous vehicles, or surveillance?
