This paper presents a novel and sample-efficient method for visual servoing by combining pre-trained deep visual features, a learned predictive dynamics model, and reinforcement learning. The core contribution is an elegant approach that learns to servo a camera by minimizing a weighted distance in the feature space of a pre-trained VGG network. A key component is the use of a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn the feature weights, which effectively selects the most task-relevant features and enables a simple, greedy servoing policy to achieve strong long-term performance. The method is shown to learn an effective target-following behavior from only 20 training trajectories, a significant improvement in sample efficiency.

I recommend **Acceptance** for this paper. The primary reasons are: 1) The work proposes a clever and well-motivated synthesis of deep learning, dynamics modeling, and reinforcement learning that directly addresses the critical challenge of sample efficiency in robotic learning. 2) The claims are strongly supported by a comprehensive set of experiments that show substantial improvements over both classical visual servoing techniques and standard deep reinforcement learning baselines.

The paper successfully tackles a well-defined problem: the brittleness and engineering-heavy nature of classical visual servoing and the prohibitive data requirements of model-free deep RL. The proposed solution is compelling; rather than learning a complex policy from scratch, it leverages the rich representations of pre-trained networks and focuses the learning problem on two simpler parts: a linear dynamics model in feature space and a weighting over those features. This design choice directly leads to the impressive sample efficiency demonstrated in the experiments. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing against multiple relevant alternatives including end-to-end RL (TRPO), classical IBVS, and ablations of the proposed method. The reported improvement of over two orders of magnitude in sample efficiency compared to TRPO is a significant result.

For future improvement, I would suggest the following:
*   The paper could benefit from a discussion on the limitations of using features from a network pre-trained on a generic classification task (ImageNet). How might performance be affected when servoing to targets that are out-of-distribution for this dataset?
*   While the learned bilinear dynamics model is key to the method's efficiency, its simplicity is also a potential limitation. A brief discussion on scenarios with more complex, non-linear dynamics where this model might fall short would strengthen the paper.

I would like to ask the authors the following questions to clarify my understanding:
1.  The cost function used for RL training appears to rely on the ground truth 3D position of the target. Could you clarify this point and discuss the feasibility of your approach if such privileged information were unavailable during training?
2.  Could you elaborate on the failure modes of the system? For example, how does the policy behave when the target is fully occluded for an extended period?
