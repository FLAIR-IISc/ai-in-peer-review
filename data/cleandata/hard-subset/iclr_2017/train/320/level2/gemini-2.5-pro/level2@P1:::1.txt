**Summary**

This paper presents a novel and highly sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing controllers. The core problem is to follow a target object using visual input, which is a classic challenge in robotics. The authors cleverly combine three key ideas: (1) using robust visual features from a pre-trained deep network (VGG) to handle visual variations, (2) learning a simple, local bilinear model to predict the dynamics of these features in response to robot actions, and (3) using a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn which feature channels are most relevant for the task. This final step is crucial, as it allows the controller to focus on the target object and ignore distractors. The policy itself is a simple one-step lookahead that minimizes the weighted distance to a target feature representation. The authors demonstrate their approach on a challenging simulated car-following task, showing that it can learn an effective policy from only 20 training trajectories. This represents a significant improvement in sample efficiency (over two orders of magnitude) compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning and also outperforms classical visual servoing techniques.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that I recommend for acceptance. My decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  The proposed method is a well-motivated and elegant synthesis of deep learning, model-based control, and reinforcement learning. The design choice of a Q-function that is linear in the feature weights is particularly clever, as it enables a very efficient FQI learning procedure.
2.  The experimental results are excellent. The demonstrated sample efficiency is a significant contribution, addressing a major bottleneck for applying reinforcement learning to real-world robotics. The comparisons to strong and relevant baselines are thorough and convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main strength lies in its thoughtful approach to combining different learning paradigms to create a practical and effective system. Instead of relying on a single, monolithic end-to-end model which is often data-hungry, the authors decompose the problem intelligently. They leverage the powerful, pre-existing knowledge in ImageNet-trained features for perception, learn a simple and data-efficient model for local dynamics, and use a targeted, sample-efficient RL algorithm to learn the final control policy. This decomposition is a key reason for the method's success.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and rigorous. The choice of baselines is excellent, including both modern deep RL methods (TRPO) and established classical approaches (IBVS with ORB and a state-of-the-art tracker). The fact that the proposed method outperforms TRPO by such a large margin in terms of sample efficiency (requiring less than 1% of the data to achieve better performance) is a standout result. The ablation study comparing different VGG feature layers provides useful insight, and the generalization test on novel cars demonstrates the robustness of the learned policies. The qualitative results in Table 1, which show resilience to occlusions and distractors, further strengthen the paper's claims.

Finally, the problem being addressed is of high relevance to both the robotics and reinforcement learning communities. Developing methods that can be learned quickly from minimal interaction is critical for building autonomous systems that can adapt to new tasks and environments. This work provides a compelling and promising direction toward achieving that goal.

**Additional Feedback**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper further and are not critical for my decision.

*   **Real-world applicability:** The work is performed entirely in simulation. A discussion of the potential challenges in transferring this method to a physical robot would be a valuable addition. For instance, how might unmodeled effects like camera latency, motion blur, or actuator inaccuracies affect the performance of the learned bilinear dynamics model?
*   **Target representation:** The target is defined by a single static image `x*`. It would be interesting to discuss how the method might be extended to handle targets that can deform or whose appearance changes significantly during a trajectory, beyond the invariance already captured by the VGG features.
*   **Cost function:** The cost function used for RL training (Appendix B) relies on the ground truth 3D position of the target car. This is privileged information that would not be available in many real-world scenarios. While perfectly acceptable for this study, it would be beneficial to discuss how the feature weights could be learned with a sparser reward signal (e.g., a binary success/failure signal, or a cost based purely on image-space metrics like tracker confidence).

**Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points during the author response period:

1.  Regarding the cost function in Appendix B, could you confirm that the ground truth car position is used only during the FQI training phase and not by the policy at test time? Could you comment on the feasibility of learning the feature weights if this ground truth information were replaced with a less informative, image-based cost?
2.  The dynamics model is trained on data collected from a hand-coded policy. How sensitive is the performance of the final servoing policy to the quality and coverage of the data collected for training the dynamics model?
3.  The results show that `conv5_3` features perform worse than `conv4_3`, with the hypothesis being that they are "too spatially invariant". This is an interesting finding. Could you elaborate on this? Did you observe anything in the learned weights `w` for `conv5_3` that would support this hypothesis (e.g., were the weights noisier or less sparse)?
