### Summary

This paper proposes a novel and highly sample-efficient method for learning visual servoing policies. The core problem is to control a robot to follow a target object using only camera images, with the ability to quickly adapt to new targets. The authors' approach cleverly combines three key ideas: (1) using robust visual features from a pre-trained deep network (VGG) to avoid learning perception from scratch; (2) learning a simple, task-agnostic bilinear dynamics model that predicts how these features change in response to robot actions; and (3) using a reinforcement learning algorithm based on Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) to learn a set of weights over the features. This weighting effectively learns a task-specific distance metric, allowing the policy to focus on features relevant to the target and ignore distractors. The method is evaluated on a challenging simulated car-following task, where it learns a successful policy from only 20 training trajectories. The experiments show that it substantially outperforms classical visual servoing methods as well as standard model-free deep RL, demonstrating a sample efficiency improvement of over two orders of magnitude compared to the latter.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **High Sample Efficiency and Strong Performance:** The paper convincingly tackles the critical problem of sample efficiency in learning visuomotor control. The proposed method achieves excellent performance on a complex task with only 20 trajectories, an improvement of more than 100x over a strong deep RL baseline (TRPO). This is a significant result that could make learning-based control more practical for real-world robotics.
2.  **Novel and Sound Methodological Combination:** The approach presents an elegant and well-motivated synthesis of pre-trained visual representations, model-based learning, and reinforcement learning. The specific formulation of the Q-function as a linear model of the feature-space prediction error is particularly clever, as it leads to a simple and efficient FQI learning procedure. This synergy of ideas is novel and provides a powerful framework for this class of problems.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's main claims are strongly supported by a thorough and well-designed set of experiments.

*   **Strength of the Approach:** The core contribution is the framework for learning a weighted, one-step lookahead policy in a pre-trained feature space. By separating the problem into learning a general dynamics model and then quickly learning task-specific feature weights, the method avoids the immense data requirements of end-to-end learning. The specific choice of a linear Q-function approximator whose greedy policy is exactly the desired servoing controller is a key insight that makes the FQI algorithm highly effective and computationally efficient.
*   **Rigorous Empirical Evaluation:** The authors compare their method against a comprehensive set of relevant baselines. This includes ablations of their own method (different features, different optimization schemes like CEM and TRPO), classical robotics approaches (IBVS with ORB features), and modern strong competitors (IBVS with a state-of-the-art tracker, end-to-end deep RL). The proposed method consistently outperforms these baselines, often by a large margin. Showing superior performance against IBVS supplied with ground-truth depth information is particularly impressive.
*   **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The methodology is explained clearly, and the motivations for design choices are well-articulated. The authors have also released their code and the simulation environment, which is a major strength that enables verification and extension of their work.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended as constructive feedback to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to the acceptance decision.

*   **Discussion on Real-World Transfer:** The experiments are conducted in a high-fidelity simulator. It would strengthen the paper to include a brief discussion on the potential challenges of transferring this method to a real robotic platform. For example, how might the bilinear dynamics model cope with real-world complexities not present in the simulation, such as camera noise, motion blur, and variable latencies?
*   **Visualization of Learned Weights:** The paper hypothesizes that `conv5_3` features are too spatially invariant for this task. This is an interesting insight. A visualization of the learned feature weights (`w_c^(l)`) for a successful policy (e.g., using `conv4_3` features) could provide concrete evidence for what the model learns to prioritize. For instance, are certain channels corresponding to "car-like" features consistently given high weight? This would add significant intuition.
*   **Intuition for FQI Update:** The two-step update within each FQI iteration (Eq. 5 and 6) is a key part of the algorithm. While the text states this is to "speed up optimization," a bit more intuition would be helpful. For example, explaining that the first step (Eq. 5) can be seen as a policy evaluation step that finds the correct global scale and offset of the Q-function for the current policy before the main update (Eq. 6) would clarify its purpose.

### Questions for Authors

To aid my understanding, I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1.  The cost function for RL (Appendix B) depends on the ground-truth 3D position of the target car. Could you clarify if this ground-truth information is used *only* during the RL training phase to compute the cost `c_t`, while the policy at test time operates purely on visual input (`x_t` and `x_*`)?
2.  The dynamics model is trained on a dataset of 100 trajectories, while the RL policy weights are learned using only 20 trajectories. Are the 20 trajectories for RL collected separately from the 100 used for the dynamics model, or are they a subset?
3.  The approach relies heavily on the quality of the VGG features. How crucial is the ImageNet pre-training? Have you considered how the system might perform with features from a smaller, more efficient network (e.g., MobileNet) or one trained on a different large-scale task like semantic segmentation?
