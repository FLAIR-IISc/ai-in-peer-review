### Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a method for learning visual servoing control policies with high sample efficiency. The core idea is to combine the strengths of pre-trained deep visual features, a learned predictive model of feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning algorithm. Specifically, the method leverages features from an ImageNet-trained VGG network, learns a simple bilinear model to predict how these features change in response to robot actions, and uses a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) algorithm to learn a set of weights that prioritize the most task-relevant features. The policy then servos by minimizing the weighted one-step predicted error to a target feature representation. The work is evaluated on a challenging simulated quadcopter-based car following task, demonstrating significant improvements in performance and sample efficiency (over 100x) compared to model-free deep reinforcement learning baselines and classical visual servoing techniques.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a clever and effective synthesis of techniques from computer vision, robotics, and reinforcement learning. The formulation of the servoing objective as a linear Q-function, where FQI learns the feature weights, is a novel contribution that directly addresses the sample inefficiency of many deep RL approaches to visuomotor control.
2.  The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The proposed method is compared against a strong and diverse set of baselines, including end-to-end deep RL, classical robotics methods using hand-crafted features, and state-of-the-art visual trackers. The results clearly demonstrate substantial improvements in both final performance and data efficiency, which are well-supported by the provided data and analyses.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The work is well-motivated, tackling the key limitations of classical visual servoing (reliance on hand-engineered features and models) and standard deep RL (poor sample efficiency). The approach is technically sound. Using pre-trained features provides a strong perceptual prior, avoiding the need to learn visual representations from scratch. The choice of a bilinear model for the dynamics is well-justified; it is simple enough to be learned from a small amount of data while being expressive enough for short-term prediction, and its linearity with respect to controls allows for an efficient policy optimization step.

The most significant contribution is the reinforcement learning formulation. By structuring the Q-function to directly represent the weighted servoing objective, the problem of learning a good policy is elegantly reduced to learning a set of linear weights. This enables the use of the highly sample-efficient FQI algorithm. The results in Figure 6 are particularly striking, showing that the proposed FQI-based method achieves a strong result with only 20 trajectories, while a state-of-the-art model-free method like TRPO requires over 100 times more data to reach an inferior level of performance.

The empirical rigor is a major strength. The simulation environment is reasonably complex, featuring distractors, occlusions, and varied target appearances. The ablation studies on different feature types (pixels vs. VGG layers) and different optimization methods (CEM, TRPO, FQI) effectively validate the paper's design choices. The generalization test on novel, unseen cars further strengthens the claim of robustness. The public release of the simulation environment and code is also a valuable contribution to the community, enhancing reproducibility.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended as constructive suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Scope of the Dynamics Model:** The bilinear dynamics model is a key component. A brief discussion on its potential limitations would be beneficial. For instance, in scenarios with highly non-linear dynamics or abrupt changes not captured in the training data, this model might falter. Clarifying the assumptions and expected failure modes would help to better frame the scope and applicability of the proposed method.
*   **Analysis of Feature Choice:** The finding that intermediate VGG features (conv4_3) outperform both shallower and deeper features is interesting. The paper hypothesizes this is due to a trade-off between spatial precision and invariance. Expanding this analysis slightly could provide valuable insights for future work on feature selection for control tasks.
*   **Transfer to Real Systems:** While the simulation is compelling, a short discussion on the potential challenges of transferring this system to a real robotic platform would be a welcome addition. This could include topics like the sim-to-real gap in visual appearance and dynamics, and the real-time constraints of running the VGG network and control optimization on-board a physical robot.

### Questions for Clarification

Answering these questions would help clarify some details of the work.

1.  The cost function used for reinforcement learning, as described in Appendix B, relies on the 3D position of the target car relative to the camera. Could the authors confirm that this ground truth information is used exclusively for computing the reward signal during the training of the feature weights, and is not required by the policy at test time?
2.  The dynamics models are trained using data collected from a hand-coded policy. How sensitive is the performance of the final system to the quality or trajectory distribution of this data collection policy? For instance, would a dynamics model trained on a purely random exploration policy be sufficient?
3.  The proposed FQI algorithm includes a novel intermediate step (Equation 5) to solve for a scaling factor `α` and bias `b` before the main weight update. This appears to be a key element for stable learning. Could more intuition be provided on why this two-step update is superior to a more standard FQI update that solves for all parameters simultaneously?
