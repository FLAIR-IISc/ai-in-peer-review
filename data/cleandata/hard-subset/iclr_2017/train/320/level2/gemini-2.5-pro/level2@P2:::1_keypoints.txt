- Summary: Proposes a sample-efficient visual servoing method combining pre-trained VGG features, a bilinear dynamics model, and Fitted Q-Iteration to learn task-specific feature weights; succeeds on a simulated car-following task using only 20 trajectories and outperforms strong baselines.

- Decision: Accept.

- Key reason 1: Extremely high sample efficiency and strong performance—learns successful policies from 20 trajectories and improves sample efficiency by >100× over TRPO.

- Key reason 2: Novel and sound methodological synthesis—combines pre-trained visual features, a simple bilinear dynamics model, and a linear Q-function over feature prediction errors for efficient FQI.

- Supporting argument 1: Strength of approach—separating dynamics learning and task-specific weight learning yields a simple greedy servoing controller and efficient optimization.

- Supporting argument 2: Rigorous empirical evaluation—comprehensive baselines and ablations show consistent, often large, gains, including versus IBVS with ground-truth depth.

- Supporting argument 3: Clarity and reproducibility—the paper is well-written and the authors released code and simulation environment.

- Feedback 1: Add discussion on challenges and considerations for real-world transfer (camera noise, motion blur, latencies, etc.).

- Feedback 2: Provide visualizations of learned feature weights to show which channels (e.g., car-like features) are prioritized.

- Feedback 3: Give more intuition for the two-step FQI update (Eqs. 5–6), explaining the role of the intermediate scaling/offset step.

- Question 1: Confirm whether ground-truth 3D target positions are used only to compute RL training cost and not available at test time (policy uses only visual inputs).

- Question 2: Clarify whether the 20 trajectories used for RL are separate from or a subset of the 100 trajectories used to train the dynamics model.

- Question 3: How crucial is ImageNet pre-training for VGG features, and how would the method perform with smaller networks (e.g., MobileNet) or features trained on other tasks (e.g., segmentation)?
