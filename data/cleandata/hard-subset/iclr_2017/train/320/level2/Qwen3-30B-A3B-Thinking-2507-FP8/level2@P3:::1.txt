

The paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing by combining learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning to create a system that can learn effective visual servoing mechanisms with minimal data. The authors propose using pre-trained VGG features for visual representation, a bilinear model for predicting feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to learn optimal feature weights. This combination enables the system to tolerate visual variations, changes in viewpoint, and occlusions while requiring only 20 training trajectory samples for reinforcement learning. The method demonstrates substantial improvements over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and achieves more than two orders of magnitude improvement in sample-efficiency compared to standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

Accept. The work addresses a significant limitation in visual servoing (reliance on manual feature engineering) with a well-motivated and innovative approach that effectively combines existing techniques in a novel way. The experimental results provide strong evidence for the method's effectiveness and sample efficiency.

The paper's key strengths include a clear problem statement, thorough literature review that situates the work within the visual servoing and reinforcement learning literature, and comprehensive experiments demonstrating the method's superiority over multiple baselines. The use of pre-trained features from ImageNet classification for visual servoing is particularly well-justified, and the authors provide a compelling argument for why standard deep features can be effective for this task despite their original purpose being classification. The bilinear dynamics model is well-motivated by prior work and allows for efficient prediction of visual feature changes. The fitted Q-iteration approach for learning feature weights is innovative in its simplicity and effectiveness, and the sample efficiency results are particularly impressive given the complexity of the task.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against multiple baselines including classical image-based visual servoing with hand-designed features, end-to-end CNN policies, and standard model-free reinforcement learning. The ablation studies on different feature representations and optimization methods provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the authors' approach. The synthetic car following benchmark is well-designed to test the method's ability to handle visual variations, occlusions, and changes in viewpoint.

The paper could benefit from additional discussion about the generalizability of the approach to real-world scenarios. While the synthetic environment demonstrates impressive performance, it would be helpful to understand how the method might scale to more complex environments with greater visual variation or dynamic obstacles. The computational requirements for deploying this system on real hardware versus the simulation should also be clarified.

The authors should provide more detail on why the VGG network required modification (removing max-pooling layers and using dilated convolutions) for their visual servoing task, and how these modifications specifically impact the feature representations used for servoing. While the paper mentions that the deepest VGG features (conv5_3) are less suitable due to excessive spatial invariance, it would be valuable to have more detailed analysis of the feature representations and their relationship to servoing performance.

The paper states the method achieves "robustness to visual variation, changes in viewing angle and appearance, and occlusions," but the experiments primarily focus on the car following task in a controlled environment. It would strengthen the paper to include more specific examples or analysis of how the method handles these specific challenges, particularly occlusions. The paper could also benefit from a more detailed comparison of the computational efficiency of the proposed method versus classical visual servoing approaches, including the trade-offs between speed and performance.

The paper demonstrates a significant contribution to the field of visual servoing by providing a data-efficient method that combines the best aspects of learned representations, predictive modeling, and reinforcement learning. The results are scientifically rigorous and the approach is well-justified by both theoretical arguments and empirical evidence.
