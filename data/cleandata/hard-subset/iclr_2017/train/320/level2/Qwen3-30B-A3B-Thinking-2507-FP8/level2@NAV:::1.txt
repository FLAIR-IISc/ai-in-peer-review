

This paper presents a compelling approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained visual features, bilinear predictive dynamics models, and sample-efficient reinforcement learning. The authors claim that their method can learn effective visual servoing mechanisms using only 20 training trajectory samples, significantly outperforming conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints, and achieving more than two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency than standard model-free deep reinforcement learning algorithms.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear, well-supported contribution to the field of visual servoing. The experimental design is thorough and well-motivated, with comprehensive comparisons against multiple relevant baselines including pixel-based methods, hand-designed feature approaches (ORB, SURF), end-to-end CNN policies, and classical visual servoing techniques. The results consistently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method, particularly in terms of sample efficiency, with the FQI-based approach requiring only 20 training trajectories compared to hundreds or thousands needed by other methods. The use of pre-trained VGG features for visual servoing is well-justified and represents a significant practical advantage.

The paper is well-written and demonstrates a strong understanding of the visual servoing literature. The method is clearly explained, and the experimental details are sufficient for reproducibility. The authors correctly acknowledge the limitation of using a synthetic environment, though this is common for initial algorithm development.

For improvement, the authors might consider adding a brief discussion on how to handle cases where the pre-trained features might not generalize well to novel objects (e.g., objects with very different appearance from ImageNet). It would also be valuable to provide a more detailed comparison of inference speed between the proposed method and the tracker-based baseline (mentioned as running at 16Hz vs. 1Hz). Additionally, while the paper mentions the importance of feature resolution, a more systematic exploration of how different feature scales contribute to performance would strengthen the analysis.

The key strength of this work is its practical significance - the ability to learn visual servoing with minimal data directly addresses a major limitation of current approaches in robotics applications.
