

# Review

This paper proposes a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained deep features (VGG), bilinear predictive dynamics models, and sample-efficient reinforcement learning. The authors claim to achieve effective visual servoing for target following using only 20 training trajectory samples, demonstrating substantial improvements over conventional pixel-based or hand-designed keypoint approaches, and showing more than two orders of magnitude better sample efficiency than standard model-free deep reinforcement learning methods.

I recommend **acceptance** for this paper. The work addresses a meaningful problem in robotics (visual servoing) with a well-motivated approach that effectively combines several promising directions in computer vision and reinforcement learning. The technical contributions are significant, and the experimental results are compelling.

The paper makes a strong case for the novelty of using pre-trained features for visual servoing. The authors properly situate their work in the literature, acknowledging the limitations of traditional visual servoing methods that rely on manually designed features and dynamics models. Their approach of using VGG features as a starting point for visual servoing is well-justified by prior work showing these features are useful for other visual tasks.

The key technical contribution is the sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm for learning feature weights. The paper provides sufficient evidence to support the claim of high sample efficiency (20 training trajectories), with thorough comparisons against multiple baselines including end-to-end CNN policies, classical IBVS methods, and TRPO. The ablation studies showing the superiority of the FQI approach over CEM and TRPO are particularly convincing.

The experiments are well-designed and conducted in a challenging synthetic environment (CitySim3D) with appropriate evaluation metrics. The authors demonstrate robustness to visual variation, changes in viewpoint, and occlusions, which are critical requirements for practical visual servoing. The comparison with classical methods (ORB features, C-COT tracker) provides valuable context for the proposed approach.

However, I have a few concerns that should be addressed before final acceptance:

1. The claim of "more than two orders of magnitude" improvement in sample efficiency needs more precise quantification. The paper states this claim but doesn't provide exact numbers for comparison with standard model-free RL methods (e.g., DQN, TRPO). Please add specific numbers to clarify this claim.

2. The paper doesn't adequately explain why the deepest VGG feature (conv5_3) performs worse than shallower features (conv1_2 to conv4_3). This is a significant observation that could help understand the limitations of the approach. A brief discussion of this phenomenon would strengthen the paper.

3. While the synthetic environment is well-suited for controlled experiments, the paper should acknowledge the gap between simulation and real-world deployment. A short discussion of potential challenges in real-world application would be valuable.

4. The computational efficiency claim (16Hz execution rate) is promising, but the paper doesn't compare this with other methods' computational requirements. A brief comparison of computational complexity across methods would help contextualize the efficiency gains.

5. The paper mentions "the actual servoing mechanism in our approach is extremely simple" but doesn't explicitly discuss the computational complexity of the servoing policy at execution time. A brief analysis of the time complexity would be helpful.

To improve the paper, I recommend:

1. Adding a table that quantifies the exact sample efficiency improvements (e.g., "Our method requires 20 trajectories while TRPO requires 1500 trajectories").
2. Including a brief discussion of why conv5_3 features underperform compared to shallower layers.
3. Adding a short paragraph on the limitations of the simulation-to-real gap and potential approaches to address it.
4. Including a comparison of computational requirements (inference time and memory usage) across the different methods.

Questions for authors:
1. Could you provide the exact numbers to quantify the "more than two orders of magnitude" improvement in sample efficiency?
2. What specific characteristics of conv5_3 features make them less suitable for visual servoing compared to shallower layers?
3. How would the approach handle situations where the target object is completely occluded (beyond the partial occlusions tested in the paper)?

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of visual servoing. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and experimentally validated. With minor clarifications and additions, this paper would make a valuable contribution to ICLR 2017.
