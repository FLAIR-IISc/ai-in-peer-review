

Summary: The paper presents a novel approach to visual servoing that combines pre-trained deep visual features, learned predictive dynamics, and reinforcement learning. The key innovation is using a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration algorithm to automatically select weights for visual features, enabling robust servoing to targets despite visual variations, changes in viewing angle, and occlusions. The authors demonstrate their method on a complex synthetic car-following benchmark, showing that they can learn an effective visual servo using just 20 training trajectory samples, with substantial improvements over conventional approaches based on image pixels or hand-designed keypoints.

Decision: I recommend acceptance. The paper addresses a significant limitation in current visual servoing approaches with a well-motivated and technically sound solution. The sample efficiency claim (20 training samples vs. >1000 for standard RL) is particularly compelling and addresses a key practical challenge in robotics.

Supporting arguments: The paper makes a strong case for the importance of learning visual features and dynamics for visual servoing, rather than relying on manually designed features and models. The technical approach is well-founded, building on solid prior work on bilinear dynamics models and fitted Q-iteration. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with multiple ablation studies comparing feature representations, weight optimization methods, and baselines. The results are convincing: the method significantly outperforms conventional approaches, demonstrates generalization to novel targets, and achieves remarkable sample efficiency (more than two orders of magnitude better than standard model-free RL). The use of pre-trained VGG features for visual servoing is particularly insightful, and the authors provide a thoughtful explanation for why certain feature layers work better than others.

Additional feedback: The paper is well-written and technically sound, but I have a few suggestions to further improve it. First, a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach would be valuable - for example, how the method would perform with more complex visual scenarios beyond the car-following benchmark (e.g., multiple interacting objects, extreme occlusions). Second, while the authors discuss why VGG conv4_3 features work best, a more systematic analysis of how feature layer depth relates to servoing performance would strengthen the paper. Third, including computational complexity analysis (e.g., inference time, memory requirements) would be helpful for practitioners considering real-world implementation.

Questions for authors: 
1. How would the method perform on real-world robot hardware where image quality and dynamics may differ from simulation? 
2. What is the impact of using different pre-trained feature extractors beyond VGG (e.g., ResNet, Inception)? 
3. Could the approach be extended to handle multiple target objects simultaneously, or would this require significant architectural changes?

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of visual servoing, with a well-motivated approach that combines several promising techniques. The sample efficiency claim is particularly impressive and addresses a key practical challenge in robotics. The authors have provided thorough experimental validation, and the code and dataset availability will facilitate reproducibility. This work has strong potential to influence future research in robot control and visual servoing.
