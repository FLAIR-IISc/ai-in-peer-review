## Review of "Learning Visual Servoing with Reinforcement Learning"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of visual servoing, specifically target following, by combining learned visual features, predictive dynamics models, and reinforcement learning. The core idea – leveraging pre-trained deep features and learning a servo policy in this feature space – is a promising approach to address the limitations of traditional methods reliant on manual feature engineering and analytical dynamics. The authors demonstrate strong results on a synthetic car following benchmark, achieving significant sample efficiency gains compared to existing deep reinforcement learning techniques and outperforming conventional approaches. 

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is well-motivated, the experimental results are compelling, and the paper addresses a significant challenge in robotics. The substantial improvement in sample efficiency, particularly the two orders of magnitude gain over standard deep RL, is a key strength. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the proposed method. The use of a bilinear predictive model and fitted Q-iteration is a clever combination, allowing for efficient learning of the servo policy. The ablation studies comparing different feature representations and optimization methods provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The inclusion of videos further enhances the understanding and reproducibility of the work.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. While the synthetic environment provides a good initial evaluation, it would be beneficial to see results on real-world datasets or even a simple real-robot experiment to demonstrate the robustness and generalizability of the approach. The discussion of the limitations is somewhat brief; a more detailed analysis of potential failure cases and challenges in real-world deployment would be valuable. Additionally, the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., regularization coefficient ν) could be better justified. 

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors:

*   How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the pre-trained feature network (VGG in this case)? Have you experimented with other architectures (e.g., ResNet)?
*   The paper mentions the potential for speedups in computation. Could you elaborate on specific strategies to reduce the computational cost of using the VGG network?
*   What are the primary factors limiting the transferability of the learned policy to new environments or target objects?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and effective approach to visual servoing. Addressing the minor suggestions above would further strengthen the contribution and impact of this work.
