Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles visual servoing for target following by learning to servo in the space of pretrained deep visual features instead of raw pixels or hand-crafted keypoints. The core idea is to combine (i) frozen, multiscale VGG features modified to preserve spatial information, (ii) a learned, sample-efficient bilinear predictive model of feature dynamics conditioned on control, and (iii) a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure that learns nonnegative weights over feature channels and scales (and control regularization) so that a simple one-step lookahead servoing objective aligns with long-horizon returns.
- The servoing policy is the analytical minimizer of a quadratic objective that penalizes predicted next-step feature distance to a per-episode goal image, weighted by the learned feature weights; the bilinear dynamics ensure the objective is convex in controls. The Q-function approximator is linear in these weights, enabling efficient FQI with a practical trick to jointly optimize a global scale and bias that are policy-independent.
- Experiments on a synthetic quadcopter–car-following benchmark show: (a) strong improvements over pixel-based servoing and classical IBVS with ORB features; (b) better sample efficiency (two orders of magnitude) than model-free deep RL (TRPO) for comparable policies; (c) robustness to appearance variation and occlusions; and (d) generalization to previously unseen cars. Code, environment, and videos are provided.

Decision: Accept (weak accept)
Key reasons:
- Well-motivated integration of pretrained features, simple yet effective bilinear predictive dynamics, and an FQI scheme tailored to the servoing objective yields substantial empirical gains with very modest RL data.
- Careful ablations and comparisons (pixels vs multiple VGG layers; with/without weights; FQI vs TRPO/CEM; classical IBVS) support the central claim of sample-efficient, robust visual servoing in feature space.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Visual servoing is fundamental and often brittle with hand-crafted features, especially under appearance changes and occlusions. Leveraging large-scale pretrained visual features while learning action-conditioned feature dynamics is a compelling direction.
- Position in literature: The paper is well-situated relative to IBVS/PBVS, model-free deep RL for visuomotor control, neural video prediction, and bilinear motion models. The choice to exploit VGG features (with pooling modifications) is justified by the need for spatial sensitivity; the bilinear model is motivated by camera-motion-induced image transformations.
- Method design: By constraining the policy to a one-step predictive minimization with learned feature weights, the authors achieve analytic control updates, a linear Q approximator, and a stable, sample-efficient fitted Q procedure. The α, b re-scaling step is a sensible practical enhancement that respects the argmin invariances of the policy.
- Empirical support: 
  - Clear ablations across VGG layers reveal that conv1_2–conv4_3 are effective; conv5_3 appears too invariant spatially, which aligns with intuition.
  - FQI-learned weights outperform no-weighting and TRPO-learned weights using the same dynamics; importantly, comparable performance with only 20 trajectories versus thousands for TRPO demonstrates the sample-efficiency claim.
  - Comparisons against classical IBVS with ORB features and tracker-based corners are fair and informative; the tracker baseline is competitive but slower and still behind the proposed method in this setup. PBVS with ground-truth poses serves as an upper bound (and is stronger, as expected).
  - Robustness to occlusions/distractors is qualitatively illustrated; generalization to novel cars is quantitatively reported.
- Rigor and clarity: The problem formulation, dynamics training, policy optimization, and Q-learning procedure are clearly described, with implementation details and computational considerations. Releasing code and environment strengthens reproducibility.

Additional feedback and questions (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Real-world transfer: All results are in simulation with costs defined via ground-truth pose. Please discuss how reward/cost would be obtained in the real world (e.g., proxy costs from detection/tracking, or self-supervision using relative pose estimates), and any preliminary evidence for sim-to-real transfer. Even a small hardware demo with fixed cameras would substantially strengthen the paper.
- Baselines: Including DDPG or NFQ-like baselines (given the linear Q-structure) would round out the comparison to sample-efficient model-free methods. Clarify whether the TRPO baseline policy class was tuned for capacity; providing learning curves with standard deviations and hyperparameter ranges would help assess fairness.
- Data efficiency: The RL component is very sample-efficient, but the dynamics model is trained on 10k transitions. Please report sensitivity to the amount and diversity of dynamics data, and whether dynamics and weights can be adapted online with minimal extra data.
- Task generality: The car-following setup is well-motivated. Could you comment on applicability to manipulation tasks (e.g., reaching/grasping) or to different camera intrinsics/poses without re-training dynamics? How sensitive is performance to the goal image being the first frame versus a canonical view?
- Policy expressivity: The policy is myopic but uses learned weights to align the one-step objective with long-term goals. Can you quantify how much performance degrades if dynamics are imperfect, and whether limited multi-step rollouts/MPC with the same bilinear model helps? A brief experiment with a short horizon (e.g., 3–5 steps) could be insightful.
- Feature choices: It would be useful to report results with other pretrained backbones (e.g., ResNet) and to visualize learned per-channel weights to understand which semantic features are emphasized. Do the learned weights transfer across targets (e.g., from one car to another) or require re-learning per target category?
- Occlusions and distractors: Provide quantitative stress tests (occlusion ratios, lighting shifts) and failure cases to better characterize robustness limits.
- Technical clarifications:
  - Are weights constrained to be nonnegative throughout FQI? Any risk of degenerate solutions focusing on trivial channels?
  - Please include computational costs for feature extraction and control solve across scales; mention nf and neighborhood sizes used in locally connected dynamics.
  - The Bellman targets depend on min_u Q for the next state. Since Q is linear in θ, the inner minimization yields the same closed-form control as in execution; clarifying this connection in Section 5.3 would aid readers.

Overall, this is a thoughtful and practical contribution that advances sample-efficient visual servoing by judiciously combining modern vision features, simple predictive models, and fitted Q-learning. With added discussion on real-world deployment and broader baselines, it would be even stronger.
