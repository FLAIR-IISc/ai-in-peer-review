1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a visual servoing method that combines (i) pretrained convolutional features (modified VGG) to provide semantically meaningful, spatially aware representations, (ii) a multiscale bilinear predictive model of feature dynamics to anticipate how actions change features, and (iii) a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) scheme that learns nonnegative feature/channel/scale weights so that a simple one-step lookahead controller minimizes a long-horizon objective. The key claims are: (a) robust, target-directed servoing via weighted deep features is more effective than pixels or hand-crafted keypoints; (b) learning is sample-efficient (about 20 trajectories) compared to model-free RL; and (c) the approach generalizes to novel targets and handles occlusions and appearance/viewpoint changes. Code, simulator, and videos are provided.

2. Decision: Accept
Primary reasons: (1) Strong empirical evidence of substantial sample-efficiency and performance gains over well-chosen baselines; (2) A clean, well-motivated integration of pretrained features, simple predictive dynamics, and an analytically tractable Q-approximation that yields a practical controller.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation are clear; the method is well-situated in visual servoing, video prediction, and RL literature. Using pretrained features to amortize visual invariances is sensible and novel in this servoing context.
- Technical soundness: bilinear locally connected dynamics with exact linearization for control is coherent; the linear-in-parameters Q approximator and the α,b normalization trick in FQI are well-justified and computationally efficient.
- Experimental rigor: ablations across VGG layers, comparisons to pixels, CEM/TRPO weight learning, end-to-end TRPO, and classical IBVS with ORB/C-COT. Fixed test sets and open resources improve credibility. Reported runtime advantages are practical.
- Limitations: results are in a synthetic car-following benchmark; statistical significance and robustness analyses are limited; cost uses simulator-derived target pose. Generalization beyond target following and sim-to-real remains to be shown.

4. Suggestions to improve (constructive, not decisive)
- Provide statistical tests and variance across seeds; report success rates and time-to-capture in addition to aggregate cost.
- Quantify robustness to occlusions, viewpoint, and distractors (controlled ablations), and sensitivity to dynamics/model mis-specification.
- Add failure mode analysis and stability guarantees or empirical convergence regions.
- Explore other backbones (e.g., lighter encoders) and feature adaptation with small finetuning while retaining sample efficiency.
- Include preliminary real-world experiments or domain randomization to assess sim-to-real transfer.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of VGG layer and multiscale weights dimensionality? Any overfitting signs in θ?
- Does the learned weighting concentrate on channels known to activate for cars? Any interpretability analysis?
- How does performance degrade with dynamics model errors or under unmodeled target motion?
- Can the approach handle multiple candidate targets or target switches online?
- What is the wall-clock training time vs. TRPO baselines, including feature computation?
- Have you tried modest finetuning of the feature extractor on servoing data to improve spatial fidelity?
- How does the method perform on other servoing tasks (manipulation, homing) without redesign?
