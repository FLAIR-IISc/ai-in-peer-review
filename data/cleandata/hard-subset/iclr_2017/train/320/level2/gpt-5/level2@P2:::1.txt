Summary and contributions
The paper tackles learned visual servoing for target following by combining (i) fixed, pre-trained deep visual features (modified VGG-16 layers), (ii) a learned, multiscale bilinear feature-dynamics model that predicts how feature maps evolve under robot actions, and (iii) a sample-efficient fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure that learns non-negative weights over feature channels and scales so that a simple one-step lookahead servo controller aligns predicted next-frame features to a goal image. The Q-function is designed to be linear in the weights so that the greedy policy coincides with the weighted servo objective; an efficient FQI variant jointly re-scales parameters and learns a bias to stabilize Bellman error minimization. On a synthetic quadrotor-car following benchmark (CitySim3D), the method learns an effective servo using only 20 RL trajectories (after training dynamics on 100 trajectories), outperforms pixel-based and keypoint-based servoing, generalizes to novel cars better than several baselines, and is >100× more sample efficient than end-to-end TRPO. Code, simulator, and videos are provided. The approach runs at ~16 Hz and is robust in qualitative sequences to pose changes and occlusions.

Decision: Accept (weak accept)
Key reasons: (1) A simple but well-motivated and effective integration of pre-trained features, bilinear feature dynamics, and a tailored FQI that yields strong sample efficiency and performance against meaningful baselines; (2) Clear empirical evidence that learned feature weights and dynamics deliver robustness and generalization superior to pixel/keypoint IBVS and far more data-efficient than model-free deep RL.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses the brittleness and engineering burden of classical visual servoing (requiring hand-designed features and Jacobians), and the sample inefficiency of model-free deep RL. Using pre-trained CNN features is well-motivated by transfer literature, and a bilinear model is a reasonable choice for short-horizon feature evolution under camera motion.
- Method soundness: The servo acts by minimizing a predicted feature distance to the goal with control regularization. Because the bilinear dynamics are linear in control, the Jacobian is cheap to compute and the one-step optimization is efficient. The Q-approximation matches the servo objective exactly, so the policy induced by Q-minimization is the proposed controller. The FQI variant that jointly optimizes a global scale and bias before weight updates is sensible and improves stability.
- Experimental support: 
  - Ablations over feature layers show that mid-level VGG features (up to conv4_3) are best, while conv5_3 is too spatially invariant—consistent with intuition.
  - Policies with VGG features generalize better to novel cars than pixels, and significantly outperform IBVS with ORB and a strong tracker (C-COT) baseline in both cost and runtime. Providing IBVS with ground-truth depth makes this a fair and slightly favorable comparator to classical methods.
  - Compared to TRPO: the FQI-weighted servo surpasses TRPO-trained weighted controllers after 2 iterations and remains better even after 50 TRPO iterations, at >100× fewer trajectories. End-to-end CNN policies on pixels require large data and still underperform.
- Position in literature: The paper situates itself relative to photometric servoing, model-based video prediction, and batch RL (regularized and neural FQI). The design choices and comparisons read as well-informed and timely for ICLR.

Weaknesses and concerns (not fatal)
- Evaluation limited to a synthetic simulator; no real-world demonstration or transfer from sim to real. Claims of robustness to occlusion and lighting are primarily qualitative; quantitative robustness studies are limited.
- The method benefits from large-scale pretraining (VGG on ImageNet), whereas end-to-end baselines do not—this should be acknowledged more explicitly when discussing “sample efficiency.”
- The cost for RL uses ground-truth car pose in the camera frame. While acceptable in simulation, this limits immediate applicability to real-world learning unless a proxy is available.
- The generality beyond target following is not empirically explored; the approach likely applies, but broader tasks would strengthen significance.
- Some algorithmic details are terse (e.g., non-negative constrained optimization for θ, b; regularization sensitivity), and there is no theoretical stability or convergence guarantee for closed-loop control.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide quantitative robustness experiments: e.g., stratify test rollouts by occlusion severity, viewpoint change, and lighting to support robustness claims.
- Offer more analysis of the learned weights: sparsity patterns across layers/channels, sensitivity to ν, and per-scale contributions to performance. Visualizing salient feature maps would improve interpretability.
- Expand ablations on dynamics: fully vs locally connected models on semantic features (if feasible), effect of neighborhood size, and impact of dynamics dataset size (e.g., 25/50/100 trajectories).
- Compare against a simple model predictive controller that rolls out the bilinear dynamics for a short horizon (2–5 steps) to verify the value of the Q-weighted one-step lookahead versus short-horizon planning.
- Clarify fairness in sample-efficiency comparisons by explicitly separating (i) RL trajectories, (ii) dynamics data, and (iii) reliance on external pretraining. Consider a baseline that fine-tunes VGG on the servoing data (even modestly) to test if adapting features helps.
- Discuss how the RL cost could be instantiated on real robots (e.g., using a detector/pose estimator or human-annotated goal alignment) and the impact of noise in the cost.
- Report compute: GPU/CPU, per-step latency decomposition (VGG vs optimization), and any batching tricks used.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the amount/coverage of dynamics training data? Does generalization to novel cars degrade significantly with fewer dynamics trajectories?
- What solver did you use for the non-negative least squares in FQI (θ ≥ 0), and how does ν affect stability/performance? Any issues with local minima?
- Can you quantify robustness to occlusion (e.g., occlusion fraction vs cost) and to background distractors not seen during dynamics training?
- Have you tried other pre-trained backbones (e.g., ResNet) or fine-tuning the feature extractor with an auxiliary objective to better preserve spatial detail for servoing?
- How does performance change if the goal image differs from the initial frame (e.g., different pose/appearance), or if the target undergoes appearance changes mid-episode?
- Could you provide results with a short-horizon MPC using your bilinear model, to verify the advantage of the learned weights versus planning depth?
- What failure modes did you observe (e.g., large depth errors, feature aliasing), and how often does the servo lose the target?
