Summary
The paper tackles visual servoing for target following by combining pre-trained deep visual features, learned predictive feature dynamics, and a sample-efficient reinforcement learning procedure. The central idea is to servo in a learned feature space rather than pixels or hand-crafted keypoints, using a bilinear feature-dynamics model conditioned on control to predict next-step features. A weighted, one-step lookahead controller then selects actions by minimizing a weighted feature distance plus control regularization. The weights are learned with a fitted Q-iteration (FQI) procedure using a linear Q-function whose greedy policy recovers the servo objective, yielding efficient policy evaluation/improvement without training an additional function approximator. Experiments in a 3D city simulator with a quadcopter following cars show that using VGG-based features (notably conv4_3) with locally connected bilinear dynamics and learned weights substantially outperforms variants based on pixels or hand-designed keypoints, generalizes to novel cars, and achieves strong sample efficiency: dynamics are learned from 10k triplets and the policy weights from only 20 trajectories for reinforcement learning. Code, environment, and videos are provided.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) A clear, well-motivated integration of representation learning, predictive modeling, and RL that yields a simple, efficient, and effective servoing mechanism; (2) Strong empirical performance and sample efficiency, with ablations and comparisons that substantiate claims, and open-sourced artifacts that support reproducibility.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Visual servoing often depends on hand-crafted features and interaction models, limiting robustness and portability. Using pre-trained CNN features addresses invariance needs with minimal task-specific data, while learning feature dynamics provides action-conditional prediction needed for control. The paper situates the approach relative to IBVS/PBVS, deep RL, and video prediction work, and emphasizes the need for sample efficiency when adapting to new targets.
- Methodological novelty: 
  - Servoing in multi-scale CNN feature space with a locally connected bilinear dynamics model that is linear in controls, allowing analytic Jacobians and exact linearization for action selection.
  - A linear Q-function approximator whose greedy minimizer coincides with the weighted servo objective, enabling a simple, stable fitted Q-iteration procedure with only nonnegativity constraints and a scaling/bias calibration step.
- Empirical support:
  - Dynamics learned from 10k (xt, ut, xt+1) samples; weights learned with only S=2 sampling iterations of 10 trajectories each (≈20 trajectories).
  - VGG conv1_2–conv4_3 outperform pixels and conv5_3, including on novel cars, supporting the claim that mid-level features provide spatially informative yet robust representations.
  - The proposed FQI-based weighting clearly outperforms no-weighting, CEM, and TRPO after comparable sampling; even after 50 TRPO iterations (4000 samples/iter), performance lags while using >100× more trajectories.
  - Comparisons to classical IBVS with ORB points and with a state-of-the-art tracker (C-COT) plus GT depth show substantial gains; runtime is ~16 Hz vs ~1 Hz for C-COT-based control.
- Rigor and clarity: The paper clearly defines the MDP, the cost function used for learning, the dynamics learning objective, the servo objective, and the FQI updates. Appendices provide practical details (exact cost, linearization, training setup), and ablations across feature layers and learning algorithms strengthen the empirical case.

Areas for improvement (to help strengthen the work; not all essential for acceptance)
- Real-world validation: All experiments are in simulation. Even a small-scale real-robot study (with modest domain shift and occlusions) would significantly increase impact and credibility.
- Privileged cost signal: The RL cost depends on ground-truth target pose in the camera frame (Appendix B). This is acceptable for training in sim, but limits direct transfer. Evaluating learning with image-only surrogates (e.g., feature-space distance to a provided goal image, keypoint/tracker-based proxies, or learned cost) would be valuable and closer to deployable settings.
- Accounting of sample efficiency: Claims emphasize “20 trajectories for RL,” while dynamics learning uses 10k samples (≈100 trajectories). Presenting total data requirements and a sensitivity curve vs. dynamics data size would make the efficiency story more complete.
- Analysis of dynamics accuracy and role: Include quantitative prediction metrics (e.g., one-step feature prediction error by layer/scale) and a study correlating dynamics quality with control success. An ablation replacing the learned dynamics with an oracle or with a simple constant-velocity flow field would clarify the contribution of bilinear dynamics.
- Generality of features: Reporting results with a different backbone (e.g., ResNet) or with light task-specific fine-tuning could test robustness to the choice of pre-trained features and possibly improve performance/speed.
- Robustness details: More discussion and quantitative evaluation of failure modes: heavy occlusion, abrupt target maneuvers, target absence, distractors visually similar to the target, and target leaving FOV.

Questions for clarification
- Cost and supervision: How sensitive is weight learning to the privileged cost? When replacing the cost with an image-only proxy (e.g., distance in the same feature space to the goal observation), how does performance change?
- Target specification: The method assumes a goal observation x*. How is x* obtained in practice for new targets, and what happens if the initial view differs markedly from test-time views (e.g., side vs. rear of car)?
- Dynamics data collection: The hand-coded policy that generates dynamics data keeps the target centered. How does performance degrade if dynamics data are collected under broader distributions (e.g., larger off-target views), or with fewer than 10k samples?
- Stability and convergence: The FQI variant uses nonnegativity constraints, regularization, and α/b calibration. Are there theoretical properties (e.g., contraction under certain conditions) or empirical signs of instability under different ν or γ?
- Computational footprint: What hardware was used to report 16 Hz? Is the runtime dominated by feature extraction, and would a lighter backbone preserve performance-speed tradeoffs?
- Multi-object scenes: When similar distractors exist, do the learned weights consistently focus on channels that localize the true target, and can attention maps or channel-wise visualizations be provided to interpret learned weights?

Overall, this is a well-written, thoughtfully designed contribution that advances sample-efficient visual servoing by leveraging learned features and dynamics with a principled RL objective. Despite simulation-only evaluation and reliance on privileged costs for training, the core approach and empirical evidence justify acceptance.
