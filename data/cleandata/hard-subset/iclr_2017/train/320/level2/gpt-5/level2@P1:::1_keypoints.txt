- Paper proposes visual servoing by learning in pretrained deep feature space instead of pixels or hand-crafted keypoints.
- Core components: frozen multiscale VGG features (spatially preserved), a learned bilinear action-conditioned feature dynamics model, and fitted Q-iteration (FQI) to learn nonnegative per-channel/scale weights.
- Servoing policy is the analytic minimizer of a quadratic one-step objective; bilinear dynamics make the objective convex in controls.
- Q-function is linear in the learned feature weights, enabling efficient FQI and a practical global scale/bias re-scaling trick.
- Experiments on a synthetic quadcopter–car-following benchmark show large gains over pixel-based servoing and IBVS with ORB features.
- Method is far more sample-efficient than model-free RL (two orders of magnitude vs TRPO) for comparable policies.
- Demonstrated robustness to appearance variation, occlusions, and generalization to unseen cars; code and videos released.
- Decision: weak accept.
- Key reason: well-motivated integration of pretrained features, simple bilinear dynamics, and tailored FQI yields strong empirical gains with little RL data.
- Thorough ablations and comparisons (VGG layers, weighting, FQI vs TRPO/CEM, classical IBVS) support the central claims.
- Problem significance: addresses brittleness of hand-crafted features under appearance changes and occlusions by leveraging pretrained features and learned dynamics.
- Method design: constraining policy to one-step predictive minimization yields analytic control updates, linear Q, and stable, sample-efficient FQI.
- Empirical detail: conv1_2–conv4_3 VGG layers are effective; conv5_3 is too spatially invariant as expected.
- FQI-learned weights outperform no-weighting and TRPO-learned weights, matching performance with far fewer trajectories.
- Comparisons to classical IBVS and tracker baselines are fair; PBVS with ground-truth poses serves as a stronger upper bound.
- Robustness claims are qualitatively illustrated; generalization results are quantitatively reported.
- Rigor and clarity: clear problem formulation, dynamics training, policy optimization, and Q-learning procedure; implementation details and code release aid reproducibility.
- Concern: all results are in simulation with costs from ground-truth pose — real-world reward acquisition and sim-to-real transfer need discussion or demos.
- Baseline coverage: suggest including DDPG or NFQ-like baselines and clarifying TRPO tuning and learning-curve variability for fairness.
- Data efficiency: dynamics trained on 10k transitions — report sensitivity to dynamics data size and potential for online adaptation.
- Task generality: ask about applicability to manipulation tasks, different camera intrinsics/poses without retraining, and sensitivity to goal-image choice.
- Policy expressivity: the policy is myopic; quantify degradation under imperfect dynamics and test short-horizon MPC/rollouts.
- Feature choices: recommend results with other backbones (e.g., ResNet) and visualization of learned per-channel weights and transferability across targets.
- Occlusion robustness: request quantitative stress tests (occlusion ratios, lighting shifts) and explicit failure cases.
- Technical clarifications requested: confirm nonnegativity constraint on weights, risk of degenerate solutions, computational costs for feature extraction/control solve, and neighborhood sizes for locally connected dynamics.
- Clarify the Bellman target computation and the connection between closed-form inner minimization and execution policy.
