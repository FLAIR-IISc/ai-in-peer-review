## Review for ICLR 2017 Submission

This paper presents a novel knowledge distillation framework that focuses on minimizing the difference in feature map statistics between a teacher and student network. Specifically, the authors propose matching the sum of statistics – such as squared activations or L<sub>p</sub> norms – across feature maps, demonstrating empirical improvements over a FitNet-style distillation baseline on several computer vision datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   The proposed methods are evaluated on a diverse set of computer vision datasets, demonstrating reasonable generalizability.
*   The paper is generally well-written and relatively easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Architectural Limitation:** The proposed method appears to be specifically tailored to convolutional neural networks. The applicability to other architectures, such as recurrent neural networks or transformers, is not discussed and may be limited.
*   **Misleading Terminology:** The use of “attention” is somewhat misleading. The technique does not involve attention mechanisms in the typical sense, but rather the distillation of aggregated statistics (e.g., summed squared activations, L<sub>p</sub> norms) of hidden feature maps. This discrepancy creates confusion.
*   **Disjoint Gradient-Based Transfer:** The inclusion of a gradient-based attention transfer method feels disjointed from the core contribution. It is presented as a separate idea, and crucially, is *not* compared directly to the primary “attention-based” (statistics-matching) transfer, nor is it used in conjunction with it. This parallel exploration lacks clear motivation and does not significantly strengthen the overall paper.
*   **Computational Cost & Notation Clarity:** The computation of the induced 2-norm in equation (2) raises concerns. Given that Q is a matrix in ℝ<sup>H x W</sup>, its induced 2-norm corresponds to its largest singular value, which is computationally expensive to calculate. The authors should clarify whether they intended to use the Frobenius norm instead, as this would be a more efficient and plausible approach. A clearer explanation of this computation is crucial.

**Overall Assessment:**

The proposed distillation method demonstrably performs well in practice, achieving improved results compared to the established FitNet baseline. However, the paper suffers from organizational issues and a lack of clarity in its notation and terminology. The tangential inclusion of the gradient-based transfer method detracts from the focus of the primary contribution. 

**Recommendations:**

The authors should address the following points to strengthen the paper:

*   **Clarify the scope:** Explicitly state the architectural limitations of the proposed method.
*   **Revise terminology:** Avoid using the term “attention” and instead describe the method as feature statistic matching or a similar, more accurate descriptor.
*   **Streamline the presentation:** Either integrate the gradient-based transfer method more directly into the core framework with a comparative analysis, or remove it to improve the paper’s focus.
*   **Clarify the 2-norm computation:** Provide a detailed explanation of how the induced 2-norm in equation (2) is computed, and confirm whether the Frobenius norm was actually intended.  If the singular value decomposition is used, justify its computational feasibility.
*   **Consider broader impact:** Briefly discuss potential limitations or negative societal impacts of improved model compression and efficiency.



Addressing these concerns would substantially improve the clarity, rigor, and overall impact of this work. I believe that with these revisions, this paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the knowledge distillation literature.
