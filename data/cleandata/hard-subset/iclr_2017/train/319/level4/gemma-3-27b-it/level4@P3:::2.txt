This paper explores knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks by aligning attention maps – defined as spatial statistics of activations or gradients – from a teacher network to a student network. The authors propose several methods for transferring this attention information and demonstrate consistent performance improvements across various datasets and network architectures. The code and models are publicly available, which is commendable.

**Reject**

My primary concerns relate to the somewhat misleading terminology and the lack of a cohesive narrative connecting the different proposed methods. While the empirical results are promising, the presentation lacks clarity and rigor in several aspects, hindering a confident assessment of the contributions.

The paper claims to tackle the problem of transferring *attention* from a teacher to a student network to improve performance. However, the proposed methods do not operate on attention mechanisms in the traditional sense (e.g., those used in transformers). Instead, they focus on aligning statistics (sum, max, p-norms) of feature map activations. Calling this "attention transfer" is, therefore, a misnomer and obscures the core contribution, which is a novel form of feature-level knowledge distillation. This terminology issue permeates the paper and impacts the clarity of the motivation and experimental analysis.

Furthermore, the inclusion of gradient-based attention transfer feels disjointed. While the idea of aligning gradients is potentially interesting, it’s presented as a parallel approach without clear integration with the activation-based methods. The experimental comparison is limited, and there's no exploration of combining the two techniques. This separation weakens the overall narrative and suggests a lack of cohesive direction.

Finally, the computational complexity of the proposed loss function (eq. 2) raises concerns. The paper states using the induced 2-norm of the attention maps, which involves singular value decomposition and is computationally expensive, especially for high-resolution feature maps. It's unclear whether this cost was considered during experimentation, and the authors should clarify if they actually used the induced 2-norm or a more efficient approximation like the Frobenius norm. The ambiguity here needs to be addressed to understand the practical feasibility of the method.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Terminology:** Reframe the paper to avoid the term "attention transfer." Instead, focus on “feature map alignment” or “activation-based knowledge distillation.”
*   **Cohesion:** Integrate the gradient-based method more effectively. Explore combining it with activation-based transfer, and provide a more thorough comparative analysis. Justify its inclusion within the overarching theme of the paper.
*   **Computational Cost:** Clarify the computation of the norm in eq. (2). If the induced 2-norm is used, provide evidence that its computational cost is manageable. If a different norm is used, explicitly state it.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more extensive ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components of the proposed method. For example, analyze the impact of using different activation statistics (sum, max, p-norms).
*   **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are encouraging, a theoretical analysis of why aligning feature map statistics improves performance would strengthen the paper.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the results to the β parameter and provide guidelines for its selection.
*   **Expand Discussion of Related Work:** The related work section is thorough, but could benefit from a more focused discussion on existing feature-based knowledge distillation techniques and how the proposed method differs.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you clarify the specific computational cost associated with calculating the induced 2-norm in eq. (2)? If it is computationally expensive, did you consider using the Frobenius norm or another approximation?
2.  Why did you choose to present the gradient-based attention transfer as a separate approach rather than integrating it with the activation-based methods? Are there fundamental differences that preclude a unified framework?
3.  Do you believe the observed performance improvements are solely due to aligning feature map statistics, or do you hypothesize that other factors (e.g., regularization effects) play a role?
4.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the β parameter? Can you provide some guidance on selecting an appropriate value for different datasets and network architectures?
5.  Could you elaborate on the potential limitations of the proposed method, especially concerning its applicability to non-convolutional architectures?
