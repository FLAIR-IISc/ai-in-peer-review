The paper presents a knowledge distillation framework that minimizes the difference between statistics of feature maps in a teacher and student network. The authors empirically demonstrate that their proposed methods outperform a FitNets-style distillation baseline.

**1. Summary**

This paper investigates knowledge transfer from a powerful teacher convolutional neural network to a student network by transferring attention information. The authors define attention as spatial maps derived from both activations and gradients, and propose several methods for transferring this information to improve the student’s performance. They demonstrate consistent improvements across a variety of datasets and CNN architectures, showing that attention-based transfer outperforms full activation transfer and can be effectively combined with knowledge distillation. The authors also explore gradient-based attention transfer as a complementary approach.

**2. Decision**

I recommend **rejecting** this paper. While the empirical results demonstrate improvements, the conceptual clarity and the justification for some of the presented methods are insufficient. The paper feels somewhat disjointed, presenting multiple ideas without a strong unifying thread and lacking thorough comparison between them.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

My primary concern lies with the somewhat misleading use of “attention” terminology. The proposed method primarily focuses on distilling statistical properties (sum of absolute values, or p-norms) of activations within feature maps, rather than directly transferring attention mechanisms as commonly understood in the literature (e.g., spatial weighting of input features). This is not necessarily a flaw, but the framing creates an expectation that is not fully met.

Secondly, the inclusion of gradient-based attention transfer feels disconnected from the core contribution. While the method is presented, its performance is not comprehensively compared to the activation-based transfer methods, and the rationale for exploring it alongside the main approach is not clearly articulated. It appears as a parallel idea that doesn’t significantly strengthen the overall paper.

Finally, the mathematical notation in equation (2) is unclear and potentially incorrect. The induced 2-norm of a matrix is computationally expensive to calculate, and it is likely the authors intended to use the Frobenius norm. This ambiguity raises concerns about the practical implementation and computational cost of the proposed loss function.

The experimental evaluation is reasonably thorough, covering various datasets and architectures. However, the ablation studies (e.g., Table 2) are limited and do not provide sufficient insight into the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings or attention mapping functions. 

**4. Additional Feedback**

The following points are suggestions for improvement and do not directly influence my rejection decision:

*   **Clarify the terminology:** Consider re-framing the work to emphasize the distillation of feature map statistics, rather than “attention transfer”. This would improve conceptual clarity and avoid potential misinterpretations.
*   **Strengthen the connection between methods:** Either integrate the gradient-based attention transfer more fully into the main narrative, or provide a stronger justification for presenting it as a separate exploration. A direct comparison with the activation-based methods is crucial.
*   **Clarify the mathematical notation:** Explicitly state whether the 2-norm in equation (2) refers to the induced norm or the Frobenius norm. If it's the former, discuss the computational implications and potential approximations.
*   **Expand ablation studies:** Investigate the impact of different hyperparameters (e.g., β) and attention mapping functions on performance.
*   **Discuss limitations:** Acknowledge the limitations of the approach, such as its potential dependence on convolutional architectures.
*   **Explain the lack of KD success on ImageNet:** Further investigation into why KD failed on ImageNet would be beneficial, given its success on CIFAR.



**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Regarding equation (2), could you please confirm whether you are using the induced 2-norm or the Frobenius norm? If the former, what approximations or optimizations are employed to address the computational cost?
*   Why was the gradient-based attention transfer explored as a separate method, and what motivates its inclusion alongside the activation-based transfer?
*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why knowledge distillation did not improve performance on ImageNet, despite working well on CIFAR?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of the β parameter in equation (2)? Have you explored different strategies for setting or tuning this parameter?
*   Could you elaborate on the computational overhead associated with calculating and applying the attention transfer losses, particularly for large-scale datasets like ImageNet?
