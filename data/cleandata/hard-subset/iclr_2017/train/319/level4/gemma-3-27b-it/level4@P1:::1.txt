The paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer by focusing on the transfer of attention maps between convolutional neural networks. The central idea – leveraging attention mechanisms to guide student network learning – is well-motivated, grounded in theories of human visual attention, and positions itself effectively within the existing literature on knowledge distillation and attention visualization. The proposal of both activation-based and gradient-based attention maps, and the exploration of different transfer methods, demonstrate a thoughtful investigation of the concept.

I recommend **rejecting** the paper in its current form. While the core idea is promising, the experimental results, particularly on larger datasets like ImageNet, do not demonstrate a sufficiently compelling advantage over existing techniques, such as knowledge distillation, to warrant acceptance. The improvements observed are often marginal and lack robust statistical significance.

My decision is primarily based on the limited and sometimes ambiguous empirical evidence. While the CIFAR experiments show consistent improvements, the conditions are relatively constrained. The ImageNet results are presented with a caveat of incomplete hyperparameter tuning, and the claim of being “the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet” feels overstated given the limitations acknowledged by the authors. Furthermore, the presentation of results across different tables and the appendix makes it difficult to obtain a clear and concise understanding of the overall performance gains. Specifically, the comparisons with Knowledge Distillation (KD) are not always direct or fully comprehensive, hindering a fair evaluation of the proposed method's benefits. The conclusion states KD struggles on ImageNet when combined with AT but this is not well substantiated in the experimental section.

Here are some suggestions to improve the paper:

*   **Expand the ImageNet Experiments:** Dedicate more effort to thoroughly exploring the hyperparameters on ImageNet. A comprehensive comparison with KD, including different temperature settings and loss weights, is crucial.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance for all reported improvements. This will strengthen the validity of the conclusions.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform more detailed ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components of the proposed method (e.g., different attention mapping functions, different loss weights).
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** Consolidate the results into fewer, more comprehensive tables. Clearly highlight the key findings and provide a more direct comparison with baseline methods.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** Acknowledge the limitations of the proposed approach more explicitly. For instance, the computational cost of the second backpropagation step in gradient-based attention transfer could be discussed.
*   **Explain the lack of KD success:** Explain more thoroughly why KD performs poorly in the ImageNet setting, especially given its success on CIFAR. Is this due to architectural differences, hyperparameter sensitivity, or other factors?

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   In section 4.2.2, you mention that KD struggles on ImageNet. Was this observed consistently across multiple runs, and what specific hyperparameters were tested?
*   Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost associated with the gradient-based attention transfer method compared to standard training or knowledge distillation?
*   Regarding the choice of l2 normalization for attention maps, did you explore other normalization techniques (e.g., l1 normalization) and how did they affect performance?
*   What is the sensitivity of performance to the β parameter in Equation 2, and how was this parameter chosen in the experiments?
*   Could you elaborate on the reasons why you chose to focus on the last two groups of residual blocks for attention transfer in the ImageNet experiments? Would transferring attention from earlier layers improve performance?
