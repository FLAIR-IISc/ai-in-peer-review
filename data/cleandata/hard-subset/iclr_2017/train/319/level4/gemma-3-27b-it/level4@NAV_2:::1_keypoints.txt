- Paper studies transferring attention (activation- and gradient-based maps) from teacher to student CNNs to improve performance.  
- Approach is well-motivated (human vs. artificial attention) and grounded in KD/feature-visualization literature; methods are clearly explained and code is released.  
- Recommendation: reject in current form due to insufficient magnitude of improvements on large-scale data and sparse experimental presentation.  
- Empirically shows consistent improvements across datasets/architectures; CIFAR results and ablations on attention mapping functions are encouraging.  
- Inclusion of gradient-based attention transfer alongside activation-based methods broadens the contribution.  
- ImageNet gains are modest (~1.1% top-1) and hyperparameter tuning there was limited.  
- Claim of being the “first” method applied successfully on ImageNet is overstated; novelty lies in transferring attention specifically, not in transfer per se.  
- Major improvement needed: expand ImageNet experiments (more hyperparameters, varied teacher/student architectures, and combinations with KD).  
- Need a more detailed, quantitative comparison with standard knowledge distillation to clarify benefits of attention transfer over KD.  
- Add error analysis to identify failure cases and settings where attention transfer is ineffective.  
- Clarify and justify the impact of l2-normalization on attention maps and why it is crucial.  
- Provide more visual attention-map examples, especially for ImageNet, to aid interpretation.  
- Reviewer question: report exact KD settings on ImageNet (temperature, alpha, architectures) and explain why KD didn’t yield positive results.  
- Reviewer question: quantify computational overhead of attention transfer losses versus standard training or KD.  
- Reviewer question: report sensitivity to β (weighting of attention loss) and how β was selected.  
- Reviewer question: discuss potential applications beyond accuracy improvements, e.g., interpretability or detecting teacher biases.
