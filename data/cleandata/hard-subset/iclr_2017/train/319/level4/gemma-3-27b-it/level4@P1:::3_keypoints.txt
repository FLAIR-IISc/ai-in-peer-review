- Proposes attention transfer by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps alongside classification loss and optional KD.  
- Defines activation-based attention maps (sum/max of |features|^p) and a gradient-based attention (loss derivative wrt inputs).  
- Evaluated on CIFAR, CUB/Scenes, and ImageNet; attention transfer consistently improves student test performance.  
- Student networks still underperform the teacher across experiments.  
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks have stronger spatial correlation between objects and attention maps lacks quantitative evidence.  
- Hyperparameter selection (notably β) is unclear; impact of β is not analyzed.  
- Suggestion to report the teacher’s training and validation loss (e.g., in Fig. 7(b)) for clearer comparison.  
- Experiments do not clearly compare pros and cons of different attention map types or give guidance on when to use each.  
- No demonstration that attention transfer yields performance beyond the teacher; practical benefits (size/speed-up) are not quantified.  
- Decision: Reject — improvements are incremental and novelty is limited relative to prior work (e.g., FitNets).  
- Primary rejection reason: student never surpasses teacher, weakening the claim of practical impact.  
- Secondary rejection reason: lack of clarity on which attention mechanism is optimal for different scenarios.  
- Recommend quantitative analysis of the claimed correlation between accuracy and attention-map spatial alignment.  
- Recommend a comprehensive ablation study varying β to assess sensitivity.  
- Recommend including teacher loss curves to better show training dynamics.  
- Recommend a summary table of strengths/weaknesses for each attention-map type.  
- Recommend experiments where teacher and student share the same architecture to isolate attention-transfer effects.  
- Recommend speed-up analysis comparing training/inference time and model size for student vs. teacher.  
- Questions for authors: provide quantitative evidence for the correlation claim, justify β and its sensitivity, explain persistent student underperformance, recommend attention-map choices per dataset/architecture, and report results for same-architecture teacher/student.
