- Proposes attention transfer by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps across layers, combined with classification loss and optional knowledge distillation.
- Defines activation-based attention maps (sum of |features|^p and max of features^p) and introduces a gradient-based attention from loss derivatives w.r.t. input.
- Evaluates methods on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet and shows attention transfer consistently improves student test performance.
- Despite improvements, student networks still consistently underperform their teacher networks.
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks exhibit greater spatial correlation between object and attention map is supported visually (Figure 4) but lacks quantitative validation.
- Hyperparameter selection (particularly the impact of beta) is not explained; a sensitivity analysis is requested.
- Suggests reporting the teacher’s training and validation loss in Figure 7(b) for comparison.
- Experiments do not clearly compare the advantages and disadvantages of the different attention-map types explored.
- Recommends characterizing runtime/speed-up benefits from the student’s smaller parameter count and assessing benefit when student and teacher share the same architecture.
- Paper is clearly written and well motivated.
- Attention transfer yields consistent improvement over training the student alone.
- Main drawbacks: students still lag behind teachers in performance.
- Unclear which attention mechanism is optimal for given scenarios.
- Contribution is somewhat incremental relative to prior work such as FitNets.
