This paper investigates attention transfer between a teacher and a student network. Attention transfer is achieved by minimizing the L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps at multiple layers, in addition to minimizing the classification loss and, optionally, employing a knowledge distillation term. The authors define several activation-based attentions – the sum of absolute feature values raised to the power *p*, and the maximum of values raised to the power *p* – and also propose a gradient-based attention derived from the derivative of the loss with respect to the input. They evaluate these approaches on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet, demonstrating that attention transfer improves the test performance of the student network. However, the student networks consistently underperform the teacher network, even with attention transfer.

Several remarks and questions arise:

* In section 3, the authors state that networks with higher accuracy exhibit greater spatial correlation between the object and the attention map. While Figure 4 is compelling, quantitative results supporting this claim would be beneficial.
* How were the hyperparameter values chosen, and what is the impact of $\beta$? A sensitivity analysis would provide valuable insight.
* It would be helpful to report the teacher’s training and validation loss in Figure 7(b).
* The experiments do not clearly delineate the pros and cons of the different attention maps explored.
* Although attention transfer does not yield student performance exceeding that of the teacher, the student networks possess fewer parameters. Characterizing the resulting speed-up would be valuable. Furthermore, if the student and teacher networks share the same architecture, is there still a benefit to employing attention transfer?

In summary:

**Pros:**
* Clearly written and well motivated.
* Consistent improvement of the student network's performance with attention transfer compared to training the student network alone.

**Cons:**
* Student networks consistently achieve lower performance than the teacher models.
* The optimal choice of attention map remains unclear – it is not obvious which attention mechanism is best suited for a given scenario.
* The novelty is somewhat incremental relative to existing work such as FitNets.
