This paper explores attention transfer as a method for improving the performance of student convolutional neural networks by leveraging the attention mechanisms of more powerful teacher networks. The core idea is to minimize the distance between attention maps generated by the teacher and student networks, alongside the standard classification loss, and optionally incorporating knowledge distillation. The authors define both activation-based (using sum or max of absolute feature values raised to a power) and gradient-based attention mechanisms, and demonstrate improvements across a variety of datasets – CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet.

**Decision: Reject**

The paper presents a reasonable approach to knowledge transfer, but the improvements demonstrated are incremental and do not convincingly justify the complexity added by the attention transfer mechanism. While consistent gains are shown, the student networks consistently underperform the teacher networks, even *with* attention transfer, raising questions about the practical benefit. The novelty feels somewhat limited in the context of existing work like FitNets and knowledge distillation.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Incremental Improvement:** While the paper demonstrates consistent, statistically relevant improvements with attention transfer, the magnitude of these improvements is relatively small. On many tasks, the gains are a few percentage points, which may not be substantial enough to warrant the added complexity of implementing and tuning the attention transfer process. The gains are not transformative, and the student networks consistently remain below the performance of the teacher.

2. **Teacher Outperformance:** A key issue is that the student networks, even after attention transfer, do not reach the performance level of the teacher networks. This indicates that attention transfer is not fully capturing the knowledge embedded within the teacher, and suggests limitations to the proposed approach. The paper does not sufficiently address why this is the case. Furthermore, the benefits of reduced parameters (mentioned briefly) aren’t quantified in terms of speedup or efficiency gains, weakening the justification for using a smaller student network.

3. **Lack of Clear Guidance on Attention Map Selection:** The paper explores multiple attention map definitions (sum-based, max-based, gradient-based) but provides limited guidance on which method performs best under different circumstances. The comparison between attention map types is not conclusive, and the practical implications for users are unclear. The discussion feels descriptive rather than providing actionable recommendations.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

* **Quantitative Validation of Attention Correlation:** The claim that higher-accuracy networks exhibit stronger spatial correlation between objects and attention maps (Section 3) is supported by Figure 4, but would be significantly strengthened by quantitative results (e.g., correlation coefficients, IoU scores).
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** A more thorough analysis of the impact of the hyperparameter β (in Eq. 2) is needed.  How sensitive are the results to different values of β?  A sensitivity analysis would help understand the robustness of the approach.
* **Complete Results in Figure 7(b):** Please include the teacher training and validation loss curves in Figure 7(b) for a complete comparison of training dynamics.
* **Detailed Comparison of Attention Maps:** A more systematic and in-depth comparison of the strengths and weaknesses of each attention map definition (activation-based vs. gradient-based, different power values) is needed.  Presenting ablation studies would be beneficial.
* **Analysis with Identical Architectures:** Investigate the benefit of attention transfer when the student and teacher networks have the *same* architecture and depth. This would isolate the effect of attention transfer from architectural differences and provide a clearer understanding of its value.
* **Computational Efficiency:** Quantify the speedup achieved by using a smaller student network, even if it performs slightly worse than the teacher. This would help assess the trade-off between accuracy and efficiency.



**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why the student networks consistently underperform the teacher networks, even with attention transfer?
* What is the computational cost of the attention transfer process itself, and how does it compare to the cost of standard training or knowledge distillation?
* Can you provide a more definitive guideline on which attention map definition is most appropriate for different tasks or network architectures?
* Were any attempts made to explore attention transfer in other domains, such as object detection or semantic segmentation? If so, what were the results?
* What are the limitations of this approach, and what future research directions could address these limitations?
