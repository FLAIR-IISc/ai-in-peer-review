## Review for ICLR 2017 Submission

This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer, focusing on the transfer of attention maps rather than traditional activation-based methods. The core idea – leveraging attentional mechanisms to guide learning in a student network – is compelling and represents a potentially valuable contribution to the field. Transferring attention maps offers a potentially more targeted and interpretable form of knowledge transfer than directly mimicking activations.

The motivation behind the work is clear: while knowledge distillation has proven effective, it can sometimes be limited by the complexity of directly matching activations across networks. By focusing on *where* the teacher network is looking, rather than *what* it is processing, this paper aims to provide a more efficient and effective transfer mechanism.

However, while the concept is promising, the experimental results presented do not yet demonstrate a substantial advantage over baseline knowledge distillation techniques. The improvements observed, particularly on the datasets examined, are modest. While statistically significant improvements are reported in some cases, the magnitude of these gains raises questions about the practical impact of the proposed method. 

My primary concern lies with the Imagenet experiments (Section 4.2.2). The reported results here are particularly limited, and a more comprehensive evaluation on this benchmark is crucial. Imagenet represents a standard and widely-used testbed for knowledge transfer, and a more thorough investigation – potentially involving different student and teacher architectures, varying distillation temperatures, and exploring the effect of combining attention transfer with traditional distillation – is needed to convincingly demonstrate the efficacy of the approach at scale.  Specifically, it would be helpful to see results comparing to stronger distillation baselines, and an ablation study isolating the impact of attention transfer *specifically* on Imagenet performance. 

The presentation of the results could also be improved. While the tables are clear, a visual comparison of the attention maps themselves – showing how the student network’s attention aligns with the teacher's after applying the proposed transfer method – would significantly strengthen the analysis and provide further intuition into the learning process. Including examples demonstrating successful and unsuccessful transfer cases would be particularly valuable.

The paper is well-written and the methodology is clearly explained. The ablation studies performed in Section 4.2.1 are helpful in understanding the individual contributions of different components of the proposed approach.  The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, accurately positioning the contribution within the existing literature.

**Strengths:**

*   Novel and intuitive approach to knowledge transfer.
*   Clear and well-written paper.
*   Useful ablation studies to analyze the method’s components.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Modest performance gains compared to knowledge distillation baselines.
*   Insufficient experimentation on the Imagenet dataset.
*   Lack of visual examples of attention map transfer.



**Recommendation:**

I currently lean towards a weak reject. The core idea has merit, but the experimental validation is not strong enough to justify acceptance at this time. I would be willing to reconsider a higher score if the authors significantly expand the Imagenet experiments (Section 4.2.2), providing a more thorough analysis and demonstrating a more compelling improvement over existing knowledge transfer techniques. Specifically, addressing the points raised regarding stronger baselines and ablation studies would be vital.
