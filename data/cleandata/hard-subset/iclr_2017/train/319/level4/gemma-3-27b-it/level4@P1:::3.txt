This paper proposes to investigate attention transfer between a teacher and a student network. 
Attention transfer is performed by minimizing the L2 distance between the teacher and student attention maps at different layers, in addition to minimizing the classification loss and, optionally, a knowledge distillation term.
The authors define several activation-based attention mechanisms (sum of absolute feature values raised to the power *p*, or max of values raised to the power *p*) and also propose a gradient-based attention approach (derivative of the loss with respect to inputs). 
They evaluate their approaches on several datasets (CIFAR, CUB/Scenes, ImageNet), demonstrating that attention transfer helps improve the student network’s test performance. However, the student networks’ performance remains lower than the teacher’s, even with attention transfer.

A few remarks/questions:
- In section 3, the authors claim that networks with higher accuracy exhibit a stronger spatial correlation between objects and attention maps. While Figure 4 is compelling, quantitative results supporting this claim would be beneficial.
- How were the hyperparameter values chosen? It would be helpful to see the impact of β.
- It would be valuable to report the teacher’s training and validation loss in Figure 7(b).
- The experiments do not clearly delineate the pros and cons of the different attention maps.
- Attention transfer does not achieve results better than the teacher model. However, the student networks have fewer parameters. It would be interesting to characterize the corresponding speed-up. If the same architecture is used for both the student and the teacher, is there still a benefit to attention transfer?

In summary:
**Decision: Reject.** While the paper presents a technically sound approach and demonstrates consistent improvements, the gains are incremental, and the student networks consistently underperform the teacher. The novelty, while present, feels somewhat limited in the context of existing work like FitNets.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary reason for rejection is the fact that the student network never surpasses the performance of the teacher network, despite benefiting from the teacher's attention information. Although the paper acknowledges this, the limited performance gap is a significant concern. While a smaller model size and potential speed-up are mentioned, these are not thoroughly investigated or quantified, weakening the practical impact of the proposed method.

The second reason for rejection stems from a lack of clarity regarding the optimal attention map for different scenarios. The paper explores several attention mechanisms, but fails to provide definitive guidance on which approach is most suitable for specific datasets or network architectures. This makes it difficult to readily apply the proposed techniques.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Quantitative Correlation:** The claim regarding the correlation between network accuracy and attention map spatial correlation would be strengthened by quantitative analysis.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A more comprehensive ablation study varying β would provide valuable insight into the sensitivity of the method.
*   **Teacher Loss:** Including the teacher's training and validation loss in Figure 7(b) would allow for a clearer comparison of the training dynamics.
*   **Attention Map Comparison:** A table summarizing the strengths and weaknesses of each attention map type would improve clarity.
*   **Architectural Impact:** Exploring the benefits of attention transfer when using the same architecture for both teacher and student would help isolate the impact of the attention mechanism itself.
*   **Speed-up Analysis**: A proper analysis comparing the training/inference speed and model size of student and teacher network is needed.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide quantitative evidence to support the claim that higher-accuracy networks have stronger spatial correlation between objects and attention maps?
*   What considerations guided the choice of β, and how sensitive are the results to changes in this parameter?
*   Why does the student network consistently underperform the teacher network, even with attention transfer?
*   Can you provide specific recommendations on which attention map type is most suitable for different datasets or network architectures?
*   Have you explored the benefits of attention transfer when using the same architecture for both teacher and student networks? If so, what were the results?
