This paper investigates attention transfer between a teacher and a student network. 
Attention transfer is achieved by minimizing the L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps across different layers, alongside the classification loss, and optionally incorporating a knowledge distillation term.
The authors define several activation-based attention mechanisms – specifically, the sum of absolute feature values raised to the power *p*, and the maximum of values raised to the power *p* – and also propose a gradient-based attention mechanism derived from the derivative of the loss with respect to the input.
They evaluate these approaches on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet, demonstrating that attention transfer improves the student network’s test performance. However, the student networks consistently underperform the teacher network, even when utilizing attention transfer.

Several remarks and questions arise:
- In Section 3, the authors state that networks with higher accuracy exhibit greater spatial correlation between the object and the attention map. While Figure 4 is visually suggestive, quantitative results supporting this claim would be beneficial.
- The paper would benefit from a discussion of the hyperparameter selection process, and an analysis of the impact of the weighting factor β.
- Reporting the teacher network’s training and validation loss alongside the student network’s loss in Figure 7(b) would provide valuable context.
- The experimental results do not clearly delineate the advantages and disadvantages of the different attention maps.
- Although attention transfer does not enable the student network to surpass the teacher network in performance, the student network has fewer parameters. Characterizing the resulting speed-up would be valuable. Furthermore, if the student and teacher networks share the same architecture, is there still a benefit to employing attention transfer?

In summary:
Pros:
- The paper is clearly written and well-motivated.
- Attention transfer consistently improves student performance compared to a student network trained without it.
Cons:
- Student networks consistently achieve lower performance than the teacher models.
- The optimal choice of attention mechanism for specific scenarios remains unclear.
- The novelty of this work is somewhat incremental compared to previous work such as FitNets.
