## Review of “Attention Transfer”

This paper investigates the transfer of knowledge between convolutional neural networks by focusing on attention mechanisms. The authors propose transferring attention maps – specifically, activation-based and gradient-based maps – from a powerful teacher network to a student network to improve the student’s performance. They present several novel methods for defining and transferring attention, demonstrating improvements across a variety of datasets and network architectures. The code and models are publicly available, which is a significant positive aspect.

**Reject.** While the idea of attention transfer is interesting and potentially valuable, the reported gains over existing knowledge distillation techniques, particularly on the larger datasets, are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance at this time. The paper feels somewhat exploratory, and lacks a compelling demonstration of the method’s superiority.

The paper tackles the question of how attention differs within artificial vision systems and whether this information can be leveraged to improve network performance. This is a relevant and interesting problem, given the importance of attention in both human and artificial vision. The approach is well-motivated by observations in psychology and prior work on knowledge distillation and attention mechanisms in neural networks. The paper appropriately situates itself within the existing literature, referencing relevant work on attention, knowledge distillation, and visualization techniques.

The claims of improvement are supported by a range of experiments on CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet. However, the magnitude of the improvements on ImageNet appears modest, and the experimental section feels somewhat incomplete. While improvements are consistently observed, the analysis lacks depth. For example, the ablation studies on the different activation-based attention mapping functions (Table 2) are useful, but a more thorough investigation into the optimal weighting (β) for the attention transfer loss would be beneficial. Furthermore, the authors acknowledge the lack of strong results combining attention transfer with knowledge distillation on ImageNet, and this weakens the overall impact of the paper. The implementation details are clearly presented, and the availability of code promotes reproducibility.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **ImageNet Experiments:** The ImageNet results are currently the weakest part of the paper. Expanding the ImageNet experiments with more thorough hyperparameter tuning, different network architectures (beyond ResNet-18/34), and a more extensive comparison with state-of-the-art knowledge distillation methods would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The effect of different layers for attention transfer (e.g., shallow vs. deep layers) could be explored more thoroughly. Which layers yield the most significant improvements, and why?
*   **Visualization of Attention Maps:** While the paper mentions visualizing attention maps, including more qualitative examples (e.g., side-by-side comparisons of teacher and student attention maps) in the main text would help readers better understand the impact of attention transfer.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** A more in-depth discussion of *why* attention transfer works could be valuable. Is it simply regularizing the student network, or is there a more fundamental mechanism at play?
* **Gradient-based attention:** The results for gradient-based attention are limited to a small setup. The authors should elaborate on the difficulties of applying it to more complex scenarios and offer insights into potential solutions.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   You mention the difficulty of applying gradient-based attention transfer with batch normalization. Could you elaborate on the specific challenges and potential workarounds?
*   What is the computational overhead of attention transfer compared to standard training or knowledge distillation?
*   The β parameter in Eq. 2 seems crucial. How sensitive are the results to its value, and is there a principled way to determine an optimal value?
*   In the ImageNet experiments, you state that KD did not improve performance. Did you explore different temperature settings for KD, or different loss weighting schemes?
* Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why you believe attention transfer is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet?
