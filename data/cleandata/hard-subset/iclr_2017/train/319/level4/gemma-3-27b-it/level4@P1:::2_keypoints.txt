- Proposes distilling teacher→student by matching spatial “attention” maps derived from activations and gradients, claiming consistent improvements over a FitNets-style baseline.  
- Empirically shows attention-based transfer often outperforms full activation transfer and can complement standard knowledge distillation.  
- Recommends rejection due to insufficient conceptual clarity and a disjointed presentation of multiple ideas without a unifying thread.  
- Argues the term “attention” is misleading because the method primarily distills feature-map statistics (p-norms/sums), not attention mechanisms as commonly understood.  
- Finds the inclusion of gradient-based attention transfer disconnected and inadequately compared against activation-based methods.  
- Flags ambiguity in equation (2): unclear whether the induced 2-norm or Frobenius norm is intended, raising computational and implementation concerns.  
- Notes experiments cover multiple datasets and architectures but criticizes limited ablation studies and insufficient sensitivity analysis (e.g., hyperparameters, attention mappings).  
- Suggests reframing the work to emphasize distillation of feature-map statistics rather than “attention transfer.”  
- Recommends either better integrating or more strongly justifying the gradient-based transfer and directly comparing it to activation-based methods.  
- Requests explicit clarification of the norm used in equation (2) and discussion of computational implications if the induced 2-norm is intended.  
- Advises expanding ablation studies to examine hyperparameter (β) sensitivity and different attention mapping functions.  
- Encourages discussion of limitations, such as dependence on convolutional architectures.  
- Urges investigation and explanation for why knowledge distillation failed on ImageNet despite success on CIFAR.  
- Asks for details on computational overhead of computing/applying attention transfer losses for large-scale datasets like ImageNet.
