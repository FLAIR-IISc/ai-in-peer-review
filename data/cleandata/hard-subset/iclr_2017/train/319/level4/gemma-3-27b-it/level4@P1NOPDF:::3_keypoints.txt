- Paper studies attention transfer (AT) by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps plus classification loss, optionally with KD.
- Authors define activation-based attentions (sum or max of |features|^p) and a gradient-based attention from loss w.r.t. input.
- Evaluated on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet, showing AT consistently improves student test performance.
- Despite AT, student networks consistently perform worse than the teacher.
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks show greater spatial correlation between object and attention map; Figure 4 is visual but lacks quantitative support.
- Hyperparameter selection is under-described; analysis of the impact of β is requested.
- Figure 7(b) should report the teacher’s training and validation loss for context.
- Experiments do not clearly compare pros and cons of the different attention maps explored.
- Need to quantify runtime/parameter trade-offs: how much speed-up does the smaller student gain?
- Question whether AT provides benefits when student and teacher share the same architecture.
- Paper is clearly written and well-motivated.
- AT consistently improves student performance over training the student alone.
- Main weaknesses: students still underperform teachers, unclear best attention mechanism, and novelty is incremental relative to FitNets.
