- Proposes attention transfer methods (activation- and gradient-based) for student CNNs to mimic teacher attention maps.  
- Demonstrates consistent performance gains across CIFAR, CUB, and ImageNet and multiple architectures.  
- Provides code and models, enhancing reproducibility and impact.  
- Paper is well-motivated and positioned within knowledge distillation, feature visualization, and compression literature.  
- Ablation studies and attention-map visualizations support that students learn teacher-like focus.  
- Improvements on large datasets (e.g., ImageNet) are modest and do not close the gap with teachers—primary reason for rejection.  
- Novelty is incremental relative to existing distillation and feature-transfer work.  
- Request to quantitatively validate the claim that stronger networks have higher spatial correlation between objects and attention maps (beyond Figure 4).  
- Ask for sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameter β and its impact on performance.  
- Suggest reporting teacher training/validation loss alongside student results (e.g., in Figure 7b) for clearer comparison.  
- Recommend a systematic comparison of attention mapping functions, detailing pros/cons and use-case guidance.  
- Advise characterizing computational trade-offs, specifically speed-up from using smaller students versus performance loss.  
- Encourage exploring whether attention transfer benefits remain when student and teacher share the same architecture and how speed-up compares to gains.
