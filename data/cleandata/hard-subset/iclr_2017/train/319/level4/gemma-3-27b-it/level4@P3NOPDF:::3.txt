## Review of “Attention Transfer”

This paper investigates attention transfer as a method for improving the performance of student networks by leveraging knowledge from pre-trained teacher networks. The core idea is to minimize the L2 distance between attention maps of the teacher and student networks across multiple layers, alongside the standard classification loss and an optional knowledge distillation term. The authors introduce several novel attention mechanisms – activation-based attentions defined using sums and maxima of feature values raised to a power *p*, and a gradient-based attention derived from the gradient of the loss with respect to the input. Extensive experiments on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet datasets demonstrate that attention transfer consistently improves the student network’s performance, although it generally remains below that of the teacher.

**Strengths:**

The paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and presents a conceptually simple yet effective approach to knowledge transfer. The motivation for transferring attention, rather than just activations, is compelling, and the exploration of different attention mechanisms is valuable. The consistent performance improvement of the student networks with attention transfer, compared to training without it, is a positive result. The experimental setup is comprehensive, covering a variety of datasets and network architectures.

**Weaknesses:**

While attention transfer demonstrably benefits the student network, a significant limitation is that the student consistently underperforms the teacher, even *with* attention transfer. This raises questions about the overall practical benefit of the proposed approach. Furthermore, the paper lacks a clear guide for selecting the appropriate attention mechanism; the experiments do not convincingly demonstrate which attention type is superior in different scenarios. The novelty, while present, feels somewhat incremental, building upon existing work in knowledge distillation, particularly FitNets. 

**Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Spatial Correlation & Accuracy:** In Section 3, the authors claim a correlation between network accuracy and spatial correlation between the object and attention map. Figure 4 provides visual support for this claim, but quantitative results would significantly strengthen this argument. Providing a correlation coefficient or similar metric would be highly beneficial.

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of the hyperparameter β (controlling the weight of the attention transfer loss) is not adequately explored.  A sensitivity analysis, showing performance variations with different values of β, would be valuable. Understanding how β influences the balance between classification accuracy and attention alignment is important.

* **Teacher Training Details:** Figure 7(b) presents student training curves.  Including the training and validation loss curves for the *teacher* network in the same figure would provide crucial context for evaluating the student’s performance relative to its teacher and assessing the effectiveness of the knowledge transfer.

* **Attention Map Comparison:** The experiments show performance results for different attention maps. However, it’s unclear what the pros and cons of each approach are. A more detailed analysis, potentially involving visualization of attention maps for different layers and examples, could help elucidate the strengths and weaknesses of each attention mechanism.  Are there specific cases where one attention type consistently outperforms others?

* **Speed-Up Analysis & Architectural Impact:** The student networks have fewer parameters than the teacher.  The paper should characterize the resulting speed-up in inference time. More importantly, if the student and teacher networks have *identical* architectures, is there still a measurable benefit to using attention transfer? If so, quantifying this benefit (e.g., reduction in training time to reach a certain accuracy) is crucial. 

* **Comparison to FitNets:** The paper acknowledges FitNets as related work.  A more direct comparison, perhaps including a reproduction of FitNets results on the same datasets using identical architectures, would better position the contribution of this work.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a reasonable approach to improving student network performance through attention transfer. The idea is well-motivated, the implementation appears sound, and the experimental results demonstrate a consistent, though moderate, improvement. However, the fact that the student never surpasses the teacher's performance, combined with the lack of clear guidance on attention mechanism selection, and the incremental nature of the novelty, limit the overall impact of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend this paper be accepted with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should address the questions and suggestions outlined above regarding: (1) quantitative analysis of spatial correlation, (2) hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, (3) inclusion of teacher training curves, (4) a detailed comparison of attention maps, and (5) a discussion of speed-up and performance with identical student/teacher architectures. Addressing these points would strengthen the paper’s conclusions and clarify its contribution to the field.
