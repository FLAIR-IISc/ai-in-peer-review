This paper introduces a modified knowledge distillation framework that minimizes the difference between aggregated statistics—such as the sum of activations—across feature maps of teacher and student networks. The authors demonstrate empirically that their approach outperforms a standard FitNet-style distillation baseline.

Pros:
+ The proposed methods were evaluated on a variety of computer vision datasets.
+ The paper is generally well-written.

Cons:
- The method appears to be limited to convolutional neural network architectures.
- The use of “attention” terminology is misleading; the method primarily distills aggregated statistics (e.g., the summed squared values or Lp norms) of activations within hidden feature maps.
- The inclusion of gradient-based attention transfer feels somewhat disjointed. The gradient-based methods are not directly compared to, nor combined with, the “attention-based” transfer mechanism, and appear as a separate, less impactful idea.
- The computation of the induced 2-norms in equation (2) is unclear. Given that Q is a matrix in ℝ<sup>H×W</sup>, its induced 2-norm corresponds to its largest singular value, which may be computationally expensive. It is possible the authors intended to use the Frobenius norm instead?

Overall, the proposed distillation method performs well in practice, but the paper suffers from organizational issues and occasional notational ambiguity.
