- Core idea: minimize L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps across layers, combined with classification loss and optional distillation.

- Proposes novel attention mechanisms: activation-based attentions (sums/maxima of features^p) and a gradient-based attention from loss w.r.t. input.

- Empirical finding: attention transfer consistently improves student performance on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet, but students remain worse than teachers.

- Strength: clear, well-motivated, simple and effective approach with broad experimental coverage.

- Strength: valuable exploration of different attention mechanisms.

- Weakness: student never surpasses teacher, questioning practical benefit.

- Weakness: insufficient guidance on selecting among attention mechanisms; experiments don’t identify a consistently superior type.

- Weakness: novelty feels incremental, building on prior distillation work (e.g., FitNets).

- Ask: provide quantitative analysis (e.g., correlation coefficient) supporting claimed spatial correlation between object and attention map.

- Ask: include sensitivity analysis for hyperparameter β to show its effect on performance and attention alignment.

- Ask: add teacher training/validation loss curves alongside student curves for context.

- Ask: provide more detailed comparison/visualization of attention maps across layers and examples to clarify pros/cons of each attention type.

- Ask: report inference speed-up from smaller student models and evaluate whether attention transfer helps when student and teacher share identical architectures (e.g., faster convergence).

- Ask: include a direct comparison or reproduction of FitNets under identical settings to better position contributions.

- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions pending the above analyses to strengthen claims and practicality.
