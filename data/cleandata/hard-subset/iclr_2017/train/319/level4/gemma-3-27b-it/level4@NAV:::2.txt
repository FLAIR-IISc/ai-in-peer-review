This paper introduces a novel knowledge distillation approach focused on minimizing the discrepancy in feature map statistics—specifically, the sum of statistics—between teacher and student networks. The authors provide empirical evidence demonstrating performance gains over a standard FitNet distillation baseline.

**Strengths:**

*   The proposed methods were evaluated across a diverse set of computer vision datasets.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clear in its presentation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The current formulation appears constrained to convolutional neural network architectures.
*   The use of “attention” terminology is potentially misleading. The method focuses on distilling aggregate statistics (e.g., summed squared values or Lp norms) of activations within feature maps, rather than attention mechanisms. 
*   The inclusion of gradient-based attention transfer feels somewhat disjointed. The paper does not offer a direct comparison to, or integration with, the primary “attention-based” transfer method, leading to a sense of an underdeveloped, parallel idea that provides limited incremental benefit.
*   The computation of the induced 2-norm in equation (2) requires clarification. Given that Q is a matrix in ℝ<sup>H x W</sup>, its induced 2-norm corresponds to its largest singular value, which may be computationally prohibitive. The authors should confirm if the intended norm is, in fact, the Frobenius norm.

**Overall:**

The proposed distillation method demonstrates practical effectiveness. However, the paper would benefit from improved organization and greater clarity in its notation and terminology.
