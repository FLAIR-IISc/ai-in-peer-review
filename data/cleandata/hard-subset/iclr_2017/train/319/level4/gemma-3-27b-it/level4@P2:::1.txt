This paper investigates knowledge transfer via attention maps in convolutional neural networks. The authors propose transferring information from a powerful “teacher” network to a “student” network by encouraging the student to mimic the teacher’s attention maps, defined using both activation-based and gradient-based methods. The core idea – leveraging attention as a means of knowledge transfer – is well-motivated and aligns with the growing understanding of attention mechanisms in both human and artificial vision. The paper presents several novel methods for transferring attention and demonstrates consistent improvements across a variety of datasets and network architectures. Code and models are publicly available, facilitating reproducibility.

**Reject.** While the idea is promising, the experimental results, particularly on the larger ImageNet dataset, do not demonstrate a sufficiently substantial or convincing improvement to warrant acceptance at this time. The gains reported appear modest relative to the complexity introduced, and the ImageNet results feel particularly underdeveloped.

The paper’s main strength lies in its exploration of attention as a transfer mechanism. The authors clearly articulate the motivation, drawing parallels between human attention and the potential for leveraging attentional information in neural networks. The proposed methods – activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer – are well-defined and the experimental setup is reasonably comprehensive, covering a range of datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet). The ablation studies on CIFAR, comparing different attention mapping functions and contrasting attention transfer with full activation transfer, are particularly insightful. The observation that attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer is a valuable finding.

However, the magnitude of the improvements reported, especially on ImageNet, is limited. While statistically significant improvements are noted, the practical impact appears relatively small. The ImageNet section feels incomplete; the authors acknowledge limited hyperparameter tuning and a lack of comparison with knowledge distillation *in that setting*, which significantly weakens the claim of a novel contribution. Furthermore, the discussion surrounding the reasons for the lack of success of KD on ImageNet is speculative and could benefit from deeper investigation. The appendix offers a great deal of detail, but could be more directly integrated into the main text to improve clarity and flow. 

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not assessment):**

*   **Expand the ImageNet experiments:** A more thorough exploration of hyperparameters, a direct comparison with knowledge distillation *on ImageNet*, and potentially experiments with different teacher-student architectures would substantially strengthen the paper.
*   **Clarify the computational cost:** While the authors mention minimal computational cost when combining attention transfer with knowledge distillation, a more detailed analysis of the computational overhead associated with the attention transfer process itself would be helpful.
*   **Investigate different normalization strategies:** The choice of l2-normalization is mentioned as important. Exploring the impact of other normalization techniques (e.g., l1-normalization) might yield further improvements.
*   **Discussion of Failure Cases:** Including a discussion of scenarios where attention transfer *doesn’t* work well and potential reasons for the failure would demonstrate a nuanced understanding of the approach.
*   **Streamline the presentation:** The paper is quite dense. Consider using more figures and tables to visually represent key findings and simplify complex concepts. Some sections of the related work could be condensed.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on why knowledge distillation did not perform well on ImageNet in your experiments? What specific hyperparameter settings were tried?
*   What is the computational cost overhead of adding attention transfer losses compared to training without them (e.g., in terms of training time per epoch)?
*   Did you observe any significant differences in the effectiveness of attention transfer based on the type of dataset (e.g., fine-grained vs. general object recognition)?
*   Have you experimented with transferring attention from intermediate layers of the teacher network to multiple layers of the student network simultaneously? If so, what were the results?
*   Could you provide a more intuitive explanation for why transferring attention maps is more effective than transferring full activations?
