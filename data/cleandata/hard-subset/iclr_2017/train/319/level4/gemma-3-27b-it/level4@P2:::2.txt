This paper introduces a knowledge transfer approach for convolutional neural networks based on aligning attention maps between a teacher and student network. The core idea is to minimize the difference in spatial attention, defined as statistics of feature map activations (sum, max, or p-norm), between the two networks. The authors demonstrate improvements across several datasets and architectures, including comparisons to full activation transfer and a gradient-based attention transfer method.

**Decision: Reject.** While the experimental results are promising and demonstrate consistent improvements, the paper suffers from conceptual clarity issues and a somewhat disjointed presentation. The core contribution, while potentially valuable, is obscured by imprecise terminology and a lack of focused comparison of different components.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Imprecise Terminology & Conceptual Clarity:** The use of the term "attention transfer" is misleading. The method primarily focuses on matching statistics of feature map activations – a form of knowledge distillation – rather than explicitly modeling or transferring attentional mechanisms in the sense commonly understood in the literature. This imprecision weakens the narrative and makes it harder to assess the novelty of the contribution. The work does not demonstrate the student network *attending* to the same regions as the teacher; it simply encourages similar activation patterns.

2. **Disjointed Presentation & Unclear Motivation:** The inclusion of gradient-based attention transfer feels somewhat disconnected from the main contribution. It’s presented as a parallel idea, with limited integration or comparative analysis with the activation-based approach. The motivation for exploring gradient-based methods isn’t strongly articulated, and the results presented are less compelling than those for activation-based transfer.

3. **Computational Concerns & Notational Ambiguity:** The paper mentions minimizing the induced 2-norm of the attention maps (Q) in equation (2). Computing the induced 2-norm (largest singular value) of a potentially large matrix (H x W) is computationally expensive. It is unclear if this is indeed the intended norm or if the authors meant the Frobenius norm, which is significantly cheaper to compute. Clarification on this point is crucial.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Refocus the Narrative:** Reframe the work explicitly as a novel knowledge distillation technique utilizing feature map statistics, rather than “attention transfer.” This will create a clearer and more accurate understanding of the contribution.
*   **Streamline the Presentation:** Consider removing the gradient-based attention transfer section, or significantly integrate it with the activation-based approach, with a clear comparative analysis. Justification for its inclusion is needed.
*   **Clarify the Norm:** Explicitly state which norm is used in equation (2) (induced 2-norm or Frobenius norm) and justify its choice in terms of computational efficiency and theoretical properties.
*   **Expand Ablation Studies:** Conduct more thorough ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components (e.g., different attention mapping functions, KD vs. AT).
*   **Discuss Limitations:** Acknowledge the potential limitations of the approach, such as its current reliance on convolutional architectures and its sensitivity to hyperparameter settings.
* **Explain the choice of β:** Explain how β is determined and its impact on performance.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you clarify the exact computation of the 2-norm used in equation (2)? Is it the induced 2-norm (largest singular value) or the Frobenius norm? If it is the induced 2-norm, can you comment on the computational cost and potential approximations?
*   Why was the gradient-based attention transfer explored as a separate line of work rather than integrated into the core activation-based approach? What are the key differences in their underlying mechanisms?
*   Given that the method relies on matching feature map statistics, how do you differentiate it from other forms of feature-based knowledge distillation? What specific advantages does this approach offer?
*   Have you explored the performance of this method with architectures beyond convolutional neural networks (e.g., Transformers)? If so, what were the results?
*   How sensitive is the performance to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., β, the power ‘p’ in the activation-based maps)?
