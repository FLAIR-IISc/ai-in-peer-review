- Proposes transferring knowledge between CNNs by aligning attention maps (spatial statistics of activations/gradients) and reports consistent empirical improvements; code/models released.  
- Recommendation: Reject.  
- Primary concern: terminology is misleading—“attention transfer” mischaracterizes methods that align activation statistics rather than true attention mechanisms.  
- Lack of a cohesive narrative tying the different proposed methods together; presentation lacks clarity and rigor.  
- Gradient-based attention transfer feels disjoint from activation-based methods and is not integrated or combined in experiments.  
- Experimental comparisons are limited; no exploration of combining approaches or deeper comparative analysis.  
- Unclear computational cost: eq. (2) uses induced 2-norm (SVD) which may be expensive for high-resolution maps; unclear if a cheaper approximation (e.g., Frobenius norm) was used.  
- Needs explicit clarification on whether the induced 2-norm was computed and how computational cost was managed.  
- Suggest reframing terminology to “feature map alignment” or “activation-based knowledge distillation.”  
- Recommend integrating gradient-based method with activation-based transfer and performing more extensive ablation studies (sum, max, p-norms).  
- Suggest providing theoretical justification for why aligning feature map statistics improves performance.  
- Recommend investigating hyperparameter sensitivity (especially β) and offering guidelines for selection.  
- Advise expanding related-work discussion to better situate this method among feature-based distillation techniques.  
- Key questions for authors include: actual cost of induced 2-norm, rationale for separating gradient and activation methods, whether gains stem solely from statistic alignment or other effects, β sensitivity, and applicability to non-convolutional architectures.
