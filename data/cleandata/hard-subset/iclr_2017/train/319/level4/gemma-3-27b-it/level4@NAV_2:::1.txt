This paper investigates the transfer of knowledge between convolutional neural networks by focusing on attention mechanisms. The authors propose transferring attention maps – specifically activation-based and gradient-based maps – from a teacher network to a student network to improve performance. This is a compelling approach, grounded in the analogy between human and artificial attention, and well-motivated within the existing literature on knowledge distillation and feature visualization. The proposed methods are clearly explained and the code release is a valuable contribution.

I recommend **rejecting** the paper in its current form. While the core idea is sound and the work is generally well-executed, the magnitude of the performance improvements demonstrated, particularly on larger datasets like ImageNet, is not substantial enough to warrant acceptance at a competitive conference. Additionally, the experimental results are presented somewhat sparsely, lacking detailed ablation studies or error analysis.

The paper adequately demonstrates the viability of attention transfer, showing consistent improvements across several datasets and network architectures. The results on CIFAR are encouraging, and the ablation studies comparing different attention mapping functions are insightful. The exploration of gradient-based attention transfer, alongside activation-based methods, adds to the breadth of the work. However, the improvements reported on ImageNet (1.1% top-1 accuracy) are relatively modest, and the authors acknowledge limited hyperparameter tuning in this setting. The claim of being the “first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet” feels overstated given the existing body of work on knowledge distillation; the novelty lies in *how* knowledge is transferred (via attention), not simply that transfer occurs.

To improve the paper, I suggest the following:

*   **Expand the ImageNet experiments:** A more thorough investigation of attention transfer on ImageNet is crucial. This should include a wider range of hyperparameters, different teacher/student architectures, and potentially exploring attention transfer in conjunction with other techniques like knowledge distillation more extensively.
*   **Provide a more detailed comparison with knowledge distillation:** The paper mentions knowledge distillation but doesn’t present a comprehensive comparison. A clear delineation of the benefits of attention transfer *over* standard knowledge distillation would strengthen the contribution.
*   **Include error analysis:** Analyze cases where attention transfer fails to improve performance. Are there specific types of images or network architectures where the method is less effective? This would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approach.
*   **Clarify the impact of normalization:** The importance of l2-normalization of attention maps is mentioned, but a more detailed explanation of *why* this is crucial would be beneficial.
*   **Provide more visual examples:** While some attention maps are shown, including more examples, especially for the ImageNet experiments, would help readers better understand the transferred attention patterns.

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   In Section 4.2.2, you mention not being able to achieve positive results with KD on ImageNet. Could you elaborate on the specific settings used (temperature, alpha, architecture) and why you suspect it didn't work?
*   What is the computational overhead of implementing the attention transfer losses compared to standard training or knowledge distillation?
*   Did you observe any sensitivity to the choice of β (the weighting factor for the attention transfer loss)? How was β determined?
*   Could you discuss the potential applications of this attention transfer technique beyond improving network accuracy, such as interpretability or identifying biases in the teacher network?
