This paper explores attention transfer as a method for improving student network performance by aligning attention maps with those of a teacher network. This alignment is achieved by minimizing the L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps across layers, alongside standard classification loss and, optionally, a knowledge distillation loss.

The authors introduce a set of attention mechanisms, including those based on activations (sum or max of absolute feature values raised to a power *p*) and one derived from gradients of the loss with respect to inputs. They demonstrate the effectiveness of these attention transfer techniques on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet datasets, showing performance gains for the student networks. Notably, while attention transfer improves student performance, it does not allow the student to surpass the accuracy of the teacher network.

Several points warrant further consideration:
- The authors state in Section 3 that higher accuracy networks exhibit stronger spatial correlation between objects and their attention maps. While Figure 4 provides visual support, quantitative results validating this claim would be beneficial.
- The rationale behind the selection of hyperparameter values, particularly the influence of $\beta$, could be clarified. An ablation study exploring its impact would be valuable.
- Including the teacher network's training and validation loss in Figure 7(b) would provide a more complete comparison.
- The experimental results do not clearly delineate the advantages and disadvantages of the different attention map types explored.
- While attention transfer does not yield student performance exceeding that of the teacher, the student networks possess fewer parameters. Quantifying the resulting speed-up would be insightful. Furthermore, investigating whether attention transfer provides any advantage when maintaining identical architectures for both student and teacher would be of interest.

In summary:
Pros:
- The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and effectively motivated.
- Attention transfer consistently improves student network performance compared to training the student network independently. 

Cons:
- Student networks consistently underperform the teacher networks, even with attention transfer.
- The optimal choice of attention mechanism for different scenarios remains unclear.
- The novelty of the approach is somewhat incremental in relation to existing work such as FitNets.
