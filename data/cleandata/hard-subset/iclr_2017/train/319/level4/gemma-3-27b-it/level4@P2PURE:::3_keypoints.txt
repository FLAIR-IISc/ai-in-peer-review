- Paper studies attention transfer from teacher to student by minimizing L2 distance between their attention maps across layers alongside classification loss (optionally with knowledge distillation).
- Authors define activation-based attention mechanisms (sum of |features|^p and max of features^p) and propose a gradient-based attention from d(loss)/d(input).
- Evaluations on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet show attention transfer improves student test performance but students still underperform teachers.
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks show greater spatial correlation between object and attention map is visually suggested (Figure 4) but lacks quantitative backing.
- Paper lacks discussion of hyperparameter selection and analysis of the impact of the attention-weighting factor β.
- Suggestion to report teacher training/validation loss alongside student loss (e.g., in Figure 7(b)) for better context.
- Experimental results do not clearly distinguish the advantages and disadvantages of the different attention-map formulations.
- Although students have fewer parameters, the paper does not quantify resulting speed-up or runtime benefits.
- Unclear whether attention transfer provides benefits when student and teacher share the same architecture.
- Pros: paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and attention transfer consistently improves student performance over baseline.
- Cons: students consistently perform worse than teachers, the best attention mechanism per scenario is unclear, and novelty is incremental compared to prior work like FitNets.
