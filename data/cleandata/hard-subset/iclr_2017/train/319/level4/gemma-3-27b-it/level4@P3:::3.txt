## Review for ICLR 2017 Submission

This paper investigates attention transfer as a method for improving the performance of student convolutional neural networks by mimicking the attention maps of a powerful teacher network. The authors propose and evaluate several methods for defining and transferring attention, demonstrating consistent improvements across a variety of datasets and network architectures. The core idea is well-motivated, connecting to both human visual attention and recent work in knowledge distillation.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents a solid investigation and demonstrates positive results, the improvements achieved, particularly on the larger datasets, are somewhat modest and don't fully justify the complexity added by the attention transfer mechanism. Additionally, the analysis of *which* attention mechanism works best in various scenarios lacks sufficient depth to provide clear guidance for practitioners.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper clearly articulates the problem and places it within the existing literature on attention mechanisms and knowledge distillation. The proposed methods for defining activation-based and gradient-based attention maps are logical and well-explained. The experimental setup is comprehensive, covering several datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and network architectures (NIN, ResNet). The consistent improvements observed when transferring attention, especially in the CIFAR experiments, suggest a valid contribution. 

However, the gains on the larger datasets (CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) are less substantial. While statistically significant, a 1.1% improvement on ImageNet (Table 5) doesn’t represent a breakthrough. More critically, the student networks consistently underperform the teacher, even with attention transfer. The authors acknowledge this, but don’t fully address it beyond noting parameter differences. A key question is whether the benefits of attention transfer outweigh the performance gap relative to simply using a larger, well-trained teacher network. 

The paper lacks a thorough comparative analysis of the different attention mapping functions. While the authors explore variations (Fsum, Fpsm, Fpmax), Table 2 provides limited insight, and the discussion remains largely descriptive. A more systematic evaluation, perhaps with ablation studies, would help identify the optimal attention mechanism for different tasks and network architectures. The claim that activation-based attention transfer is the "first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" feels overstated, given the extensive body of work on knowledge distillation.

**Additional Feedback:**

The paper is generally well-written and clear. However, several minor improvements could enhance readability and impact.

*   **Quantitative Validation of Correlation:** The authors claim a correlation between accuracy and spatial attention alignment (Section 3). While Figure 4 is visually compelling, providing quantitative results (e.g., correlation coefficients) would strengthen this claim.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A more detailed discussion of the hyperparameter $\beta$ and its impact on performance would be valuable. A sensitivity analysis showing the performance variations with different $\beta$ values would improve the robustness of the findings.
*   **Complete Results in Figure 7(b):** Including the teacher's train and validation loss in Figure 7(b) would provide a more comprehensive comparison and context for the student's performance.
*   **Speed-Accuracy Tradeoff:** Characterizing the speedup achieved by using a smaller student network is important. Given that the student network has fewer parameters, how does the inference time compare to the teacher?
*   **Same Architecture Experiment:** Investigating the benefits of attention transfer when the student and teacher networks have *identical* architectures would be insightful. This would isolate the effect of attention transfer from architectural differences.
*   **Clarify novelty claim:** The claim about being the first knowledge transfer method on ImageNet should be carefully re-examined and potentially toned down.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why the student networks consistently underperform the teacher, even with attention transfer? Is there a theoretical limit to the performance improvement achievable through this method?
2.  What criteria should a practitioner use to select the most appropriate attention mapping function (Fsum, Fpsm, Fpmax) for a given task and network architecture?
3.  Have you explored any techniques to mitigate the performance gap between the student and teacher networks, such as increasing the student’s capacity or using more sophisticated training schedules?
4.  Can you comment on the computational cost of calculating and applying the attention transfer loss during training? Does this overhead significantly impact training time?
5.  Regarding the gradient-based attention transfer, what considerations were made when dealing with batch normalization during the second backpropagation step, and how does this affect the results?
