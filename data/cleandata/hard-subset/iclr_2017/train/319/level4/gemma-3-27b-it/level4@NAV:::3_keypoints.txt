- Paper proposes attention transfer: align student and teacher attention maps by minimizing L2 distance across layers alongside classification (and optional distillation) loss.
- Introduces attention mechanisms: activation-based maps (sum/max of |features|^p) and a gradient-based attention map.
- Demonstrates student performance improvements on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet, but students never exceed teacher accuracy.
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks show stronger spatial correlation between objects and attention maps is supported visually but lacks quantitative validation.
- Rationale and sensitivity for hyperparameter β are unclear; an ablation study on β is needed.
- Figure 7(b) should include the teacher’s training/validation loss for a more complete comparison.
- Experiments do not clearly compare advantages/disadvantages of the different attention map types or identify when each is best.
- Paper should quantify runtime/speed-up benefits from smaller student networks and test attention transfer when student and teacher share identical architectures.
- Pros: well-written, clearly motivated, and consistently improves student performance versus training alone.
- Cons: students still underperform teachers, optimal attention mechanism is unclear, and novelty is incremental relative to prior work like FitNets.
