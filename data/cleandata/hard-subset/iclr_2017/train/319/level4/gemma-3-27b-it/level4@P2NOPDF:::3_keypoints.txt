- Proposes attention transfer by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps alongside classification loss, optionally with knowledge distillation.  
- Evaluates multiple attention map types: activation-based (sum-of-abs, max^p) and gradient-based (loss w.r.t. input).  
- Experiments on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet show attention transfer improves student performance versus training without attention.  
- Student networks still consistently underperform the teacher, even with attention transfer.  
- Claim of correlation between higher accuracy and better spatial correspondence (Fig. 4) needs quantitative validation, not just visuals.  
- Paper lacks detailed discussion of hyperparameter selection, especially the weighting factor β; an ablation study on β is recommended.  
- Figure 7(b) should include the teacher’s training and validation loss curves to contextualize the student’s learning trajectory.  
- Experimental results do not clearly compare which attention map types work best in which scenarios; provide a comparative analysis.  
- Quantify the computational speedup from smaller student models (runtime/throughput), since students have fewer parameters.  
- Test attention transfer when student and teacher share the same architecture to isolate the effect of attention transfer from capacity differences.  
- Strength: well-written, clearly motivated, and presents a logical approach to using attention for knowledge transfer.  
- Strength: attention transfer consistently improves student performance over baseline without attention.  
- Weakness: students still do not match teacher performance.  
- Weakness: no guidance on selecting the most appropriate attention map per task/dataset.  
- Weakness: novelty is incremental, building on prior distillation work like FitNets.
