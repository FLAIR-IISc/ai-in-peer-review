This paper investigates attention transfer between a teacher and a student network. Attention transfer is achieved by minimizing the L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps at different layers, in addition to minimizing the classification loss, and optionally including a knowledge distillation term. The authors define several activation-based attentions – specifically, the sum of absolute feature values raised to the power *p*, or the maximum of values raised to the power *p* – and also propose a gradient-based attention, derived from the derivative of the loss with respect to the input. They evaluate their approaches on several datasets (CIFAR, CUB/Scene, ImageNet), demonstrating that attention transfer helps improve the student network’s test performance. However, the student networks consistently perform worse than the teacher network, even with attention transfer.

Several remarks and questions arise from this work:

*   In Section 3, the authors claim that networks with higher accuracy exhibit greater spatial correlation between the object and the attention map. While Figure 4 is visually compelling, quantitative results supporting this claim would be beneficial.
*   Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter selection process? It would be valuable to see an analysis of the impact of β.
*   Reporting the teacher network’s training and validation loss in Figure 7(b) would provide helpful context.
*   The experiments do not clearly delineate the pros and cons of the different attention maps explored.
*   Although attention transfer (AT) does not enable the student network to surpass the teacher’s performance, the student networks have fewer parameters. It would be interesting to characterize the resulting speed-up. Furthermore, if the student and teacher networks share the same architecture, is there still a benefit to employing attention transfer?

In summary:

**Pros:**

*   Clearly written and well-motivated.
*   Consistent improvement of the student network’s performance when using attention transfer, compared to training the student network in isolation.

**Cons:**

*   Student networks consistently underperform compared to the teacher model.
*   It remains unclear which attention mechanism is most appropriate for different scenarios.
*   The novelty of this work is somewhat incremental relative to FitNets.
