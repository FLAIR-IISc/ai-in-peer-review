- Paper studies transferring teacher attention maps to student CNNs, showing consistent but modest improvements across datasets and architectures.
- Decision: Reject.
- Main criticism: Improvements on larger datasets (CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) are small and don’t justify added complexity (e.g., 1.1% on ImageNet).
- Student networks still underperform the teacher even after attention transfer; authors don’t sufficiently address this gap beyond parameter differences.
- Unclear whether attention transfer benefits outweigh simply using a larger teacher model.
- Analysis of which attention mapping (Fsum, Fp-sum, Fp-max) works best is shallow; Table 2 and discussion are largely descriptive.
- Claim that activation-based attention transfer is the first KD method applied on ImageNet is overstated and should be toned down.
- Strengths: clear motivation, logical definition of activation- and gradient-based attention maps, comprehensive experimental setup across datasets and architectures.
- Request quantitative validation of correlation between accuracy and spatial attention alignment (e.g., correlation coefficients).
- Ask for hyperparameter sensitivity analysis for β to demonstrate robustness of results.
- Suggest including teacher’s train/validation loss in Figure 7(b) for complete comparison.
- Recommend reporting speed–accuracy tradeoff (inference time) given student’s fewer parameters.
- Propose experiments with identical student and teacher architectures to isolate attention transfer effects.
- Questions for authors: explain persistent student underperformance; criteria for choosing attention mapping functions; techniques to mitigate student–teacher gap; computational cost/overhead of attention transfer; handling batch normalization during gradient-based transfer.
