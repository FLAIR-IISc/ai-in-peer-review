This paper investigates attention transfer from a teacher network to a student network as a regularization technique. The core idea is to minimize the L2 distance between attention maps of the teacher and student networks across multiple layers, alongside the standard classification loss, and optionally incorporating a knowledge distillation term. The authors explore several methods for generating attention maps, including those based on feature activations (using sum-of-absolute-values and maximum values raised to a power *p*) and gradient information (the derivative of the loss with respect to the input). Their experiments on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet demonstrate that attention transfer generally improves the performance of student networks. However, the student networks consistently underperform the teacher network, even when utilizing attention transfer. 

Here are several points for the authors to consider:

In Section 3, the authors assert a correlation between higher network accuracy and greater spatial correspondence between the object of interest and the attention map. While Figure 4 provides a visually compelling illustration of this relationship, quantitative results supporting this claim would strengthen the argument. 

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter selection, particularly the influence of the weighting factor, β. Providing an ablation study demonstrating the sensitivity of results to different β values would be valuable. 

Figure 7(b) currently presents the student’s performance during attention transfer. Including the teacher’s training and validation loss curves in this figure would offer a clearer comparison and context for the student’s learning trajectory.

The experimental results do not clearly delineate the strengths and weaknesses of the different attention map types explored. A comparative analysis, outlining scenarios where specific attention mechanisms perform best, would improve the practical utility of the work.

Although attention transfer does not enable the student network to surpass the teacher’s performance, the student networks typically have fewer parameters. Characterizing the resulting computational speedup would be a valuable contribution. Furthermore, if the student and teacher networks share the same architecture, does attention transfer still provide a noticeable benefit? Exploring this scenario would help isolate the effect of attention transfer itself, independent of model capacity.

**Summary:**

**Strengths:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly motivated, presenting a logical approach to leveraging attention mechanisms for knowledge transfer.
*   Attention transfer consistently improves the student network’s performance compared to training the student network without attention.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Student networks consistently achieve lower performance than the teacher network, even with attention transfer.
*   The paper lacks guidance on selecting the most appropriate attention map for a given task or dataset.
*   The novelty of the approach is somewhat incremental, building upon existing work in knowledge distillation like FitNets.
