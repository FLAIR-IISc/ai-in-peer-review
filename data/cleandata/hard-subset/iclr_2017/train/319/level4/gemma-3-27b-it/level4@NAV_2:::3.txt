This paper investigates attention transfer as a mechanism for improving the performance of student convolutional neural networks by mimicking the attention maps of a more powerful teacher network. The authors propose several methods for defining and transferring attention, including both activation-based and gradient-based approaches, and demonstrate consistent performance gains across a variety of datasets and network architectures. The availability of code and models further enhances the reproducibility and impact of this work.

Reject. While the paper presents a thorough investigation and demonstrates positive results, the improvements achieved, particularly on larger datasets like ImageNet, are relatively modest and do not fully close the performance gap with the teacher network. The novelty, while present, feels somewhat incremental given existing work in knowledge distillation and feature transfer.

The paper effectively tackles the question of whether attention information can be successfully transferred from a teacher to a student network to improve performance. The approach is well-motivated, grounded in the understanding of attention mechanisms in both human and artificial vision systems, and appropriately positioned within the existing literature on knowledge distillation, feature visualization, and network compression. The authors build upon established concepts and offer novel ways to leverage attention maps for knowledge transfer. 

The paper provides empirical support for its claims through a comprehensive series of experiments. The results on CIFAR, CUB, and ImageNet demonstrate consistent, statistically significant improvements in student network performance with attention transfer. The ablation studies exploring different attention mapping functions and transfer strategies (activation-based vs. gradient-based, with and without knowledge distillation) are particularly valuable in understanding the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The visualization of attention maps further supports the claim that student networks are indeed learning to focus on similar regions as their teachers.

Several points could further strengthen the paper. It would be beneficial to quantitatively validate the claim that stronger networks exhibit higher spatial correlation between objects and attention maps (currently supported only by Figure 4). A more detailed analysis of the impact of the hyperparameter β on performance would also be helpful. Reporting the teacher’s training and validation loss alongside the student's results (e.g., in Figure 7b) would provide a clearer comparison. A more systematic comparison of the different attention mapping functions, outlining their respective strengths and weaknesses, would aid practitioners in selecting the most appropriate method for their specific application. Finally, characterizing the computational trade-offs – specifically, the speed-up achieved by using smaller student networks – would add further value. It's worth exploring whether attention transfer provides a benefit *even when* the student and teacher networks have the same architecture, beyond simply reducing model size. 

I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  In Section 3, you state that stronger networks have higher spatial correlation between objects and attention maps. Could you provide quantitative results to support this observation?
2.  What is the sensitivity of the results to the hyperparameter β? A sensitivity analysis demonstrating its impact on performance would be valuable.
3.  Could you provide the teacher’s training and validation loss alongside the student’s results in Figure 7b to provide a more direct comparison?
4.  What are the key pros and cons of each attention mapping function explored in the paper? Under what circumstances would you recommend using one over another?
5.  If the student and teacher networks have identical architectures, does attention transfer still yield a performance improvement? If so, how does the speed-up compare to the performance gain?
