The paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer. I find the idea of transferring attention maps, rather than activations, particularly appealing. However, the experimental results do not demonstrate a substantial improvement over knowledge distillation alone, and I believe further experimentation is needed, specifically within the ImageNet setting. I would be inclined to reconsider the score if the authors were to expand on Section 4.2.2.

The core contribution lies in the proposition that attention maps encapsulate richer, more transferable information than raw activations. This is a compelling hypothesis, as attention mechanisms inherently highlight salient features relevant to the task at hand. The authors argue this allows for more effective knowledge transfer from a teacher network to a student network. 

The methodology appears sound. The approach of distilling attention maps alongside, or instead of, activations is logically presented and the implementation details seem clearly articulated. The use of different attention mechanisms (e.g., spatial attention, channel attention) adds to the flexibility and potential of the proposed method. 

However, the empirical evaluation, while thorough across several datasets (CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, SVHN), feels somewhat limited in its impact. The gains observed when incorporating attention map transfer are consistently modest when compared to standard knowledge distillation. While statistically significant improvements are reported in some cases, the magnitude of these improvements doesn't fully justify the added complexity of the proposed approach. 

My primary concern rests with the ImageNet experiments. The results presented in this section are particularly underwhelming, showing minimal benefit from attention map transfer. Given the scale and importance of ImageNet as a benchmark for knowledge transfer, a more substantial demonstration of efficacy here is crucial. I would strongly encourage the authors to investigate this further, perhaps by exploring different network architectures, hyperparameter settings, or attention mechanisms specifically tailored for ImageNet.

Specifically, Section 4.2.2, which discusses the impact of different weighting schemes for the attention and feature distillation losses, feels underdeveloped. The analysis provided is brief and doesn’t offer much insight into *why* certain weighting schemes perform better than others. A more detailed ablation study exploring the sensitivity of the method to these hyperparameters would be highly beneficial. Further, exploring alternative loss functions beyond the standard L2 loss for attention map distillation might yield better results.

Beyond these points, the paper is well-written and clearly presented. The related work section provides a good overview of existing knowledge transfer techniques. The code is available, which is always a positive aspect. 

In summary, the paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable idea. However, the experimental results, particularly on ImageNet, need to be more convincing to fully demonstrate the benefits of attention map transfer. Addressing the concerns raised regarding Section 4.2.2 with a more comprehensive analysis would significantly strengthen the paper. I believe the work has merit, but currently falls short of making a substantial contribution to the field.
