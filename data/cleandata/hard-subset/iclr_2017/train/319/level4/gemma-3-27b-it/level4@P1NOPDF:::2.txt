The paper presents a modified knowledge distillation framework that minimizes the difference in the sum of statistics – across a feature map – between a teacher and a student network. The authors empirically demonstrate that their proposed methods outperform a FitNet-style distillation baseline.

**Pros:**

* The authors evaluated the proposed methods on a variety of computer vision datasets.
* The paper is generally well-written.

**Cons:**

* The method appears to be limited to convolutional architectures.
* The use of “attention” terminology is misleading. The proposed method essentially distills the summed squared values (or other statistics, e.g., summed Lp norm) of activations within a hidden feature map.
* The gradient-based attention transfer feels somewhat disconnected from the core contribution. The proposed gradient-based methods are not directly compared to, nor used in conjunction with, the “attention-based” transfer. It reads as a parallel idea added to the paper that doesn’t significantly add value.
* The computation of the induced 2-norms in equation (2) is unclear. Given that Q is a matrix ∈ ℝ<sup>H x W</sup>, its induced 2-norm corresponds to its largest singular value. This seems computationally expensive. Is it possible the authors actually intended to use the Frobenius norm?

**Overall:**

The proposed distillation method performs well in practice. However, the paper suffers from some organizational issues and unclear notation. While the results are promising, improvements to clarity and focus would strengthen the contribution.
