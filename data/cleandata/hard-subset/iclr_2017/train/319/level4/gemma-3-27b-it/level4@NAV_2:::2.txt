The paper presents a novel knowledge transfer framework that aims to improve the performance of a student convolutional neural network by minimizing the difference between spatial attention maps derived from a teacher network. These attention maps are based on statistics computed across feature maps – specifically, sums and maximums of absolute activations, potentially raised to a power. The authors demonstrate empirical improvements across several computer vision datasets and network architectures.

Reject. While the paper presents a promising approach and demonstrates positive results, several organizational and technical clarity issues hinder a strong recommendation for acceptance. The core contribution feels diluted by tangential explorations, and key methodological details require further clarification.

The paper’s central idea – transferring knowledge via attention map matching – is well-motivated within the context of recent work on knowledge distillation and the understanding of attention mechanisms in neural networks. The experimental evaluation across a range of datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet) is commendable, demonstrating the robustness of the proposed approach. The provided code and models further enhance reproducibility. However, the framing of the method as “attention transfer” is potentially misleading. The approach essentially distills statistics of feature map activations, rather than directly transferring attention in the sense of mimicking attention weights derived from biologically plausible mechanisms. Furthermore, the inclusion of gradient-based attention transfer feels disjointed from the main contribution. It's unclear why it was included and how it relates to the activation-based approach, and it receives comparatively less attention in the results. The computational cost of the proposed loss function (Eq. 2) is also a concern. The use of the spectral norm (induced 2-norm) for the attention maps appears computationally expensive, and it's unclear if this was intended – the Frobenius norm would be a more practical alternative.

To improve the paper, I suggest the following: 
1.  Clarify the terminology: Reframe the method to more accurately reflect its nature as feature map statistic distillation, rather than "attention transfer."
2.  Streamline the presentation: Focus the paper on the activation-based attention transfer method, as it appears to be the core contribution. Consider moving the gradient-based approach to a future work or shortening its presentation significantly.
3.  Clarify the loss function: Explicitly state whether the 2-norm in Eq. (2) refers to the spectral norm or the Frobenius norm. If it’s the spectral norm, provide justification for its computational cost. 
4.  Provide more detailed ablation studies: Investigate the impact of different attention mapping functions (sum, max, p-norms) more thoroughly. 
5.  Investigate the effect of different layers for attention transfer.
6.  Discuss the limitations of the approach: Address potential challenges when applying this method to different architectures beyond convolutional networks.

I have the following questions for the authors:
1.  Could you elaborate on the choice of the spectral norm for the attention maps in Eq. (2)? What are the practical implications of this choice in terms of computational cost, and have you considered using the Frobenius norm as an alternative?
2.  In the gradient-based attention transfer section, you mention the challenges of incorporating batch normalization during the second backpropagation step. Could you provide more detail on these challenges and potential solutions?
3.  You observed that KD didn’t perform well on ImageNet, while it did on CIFAR. Could you elaborate on the potential reasons for this discrepancy?
4.  What is the sensitivity of the results to the hyperparameter β? Did you perform a hyperparameter search to find the optimal value?
