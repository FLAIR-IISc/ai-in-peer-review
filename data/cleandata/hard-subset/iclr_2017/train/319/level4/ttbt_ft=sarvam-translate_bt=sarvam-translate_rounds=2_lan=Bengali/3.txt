This paper aims to discuss the transfer of attention within teacher-student networks.

Attention transfer is achieved by reducing the l2 distance within the attention maps at different levels. Simultaneously, the amount of classification loss and optionally the knowledge extraction term are also reduced.

The authors have defined several activation-based attention measures (sum of absolute attribute values to the power of p or maximum value of values to the power of p). They have also proposed a gradient-based attention measure (derivative of loss with respect to input).

They have evaluated their method on several datasets such as CIFAR, Cub/Scene, and Imagenet, which shows that attention transfer helps improve student network test performance. However, student networks perform worse than teachers despite having attention.

A few comments/questions:
- In section 3, the authors claim that high-precision networks have a high spatial relationship between the object and the attention map. Although Figure 4 is interesting, it would be good to have quantitative results that could also show this.
- It would be good to see how you selected the hyperparameters values and to see the effect of β.
- It would be good to provide a report on teacher training and validation loss in Figure 7 b).
- The advantages/disadvantages of different attention maps are not clear from the experiment.
- It does not give better results than teachers. However, student networks have fewer parameters. It would be interesting to identify the corresponding speed-up. Is there any advantage to attention transfer if you keep the same architecture between student and teacher?

In summary:
Advantages:
- Clearly written and well-motivated.
- Continuous improvement of the student with attention compared to the student alone.

Disadvantages:
- Students have worse performance than the teacher model.
- It is not clear in which case which attention to use.
- Slightly more novelty compared to FitNet.
