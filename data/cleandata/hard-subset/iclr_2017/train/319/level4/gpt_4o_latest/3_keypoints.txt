- Paper proposes attention transfer by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps across layers, plus classification loss and optional distillation term.  
- Introduces activation-based attention maps (sum/max of |features|^p) and a gradient-based attention map (loss derivative w.r.t. inputs).  
- Evaluated on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet; attention transfer improves student test performance but students still underperform teachers.  
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks show stronger spatial correlation between object and attention map is supported qualitatively (Fig.4) but lacks quantitative evidence.  
- Hyperparameter selection (including impact of beta) is not explained or analyzed.  
- Figure 7(b) should report teacher training and validation losses for comparison.  
- Experiments do not clearly compare advantages and disadvantages of the different attention mechanisms.  
- No analysis of computational trade-offs or speed-up for smaller student networks versus teacher models.  
- Unclear whether attention transfer benefits when teacher and student share the same architecture.  
- Strength: paper is clearly written and well-motivated.  
- Strength: method consistently improves student performance over training alone.  
- Weakness: students remain worse than teachers despite attention transfer.  
- Weakness: unclear which attention mechanism suits which scenario.  
- Weakness: novelty is incremental relative to FitNet.
