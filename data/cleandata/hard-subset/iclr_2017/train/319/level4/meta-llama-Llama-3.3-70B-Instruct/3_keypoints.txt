- Paper studies attention transfer by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps alongside classification loss (and optional knowledge distillation).
- Introduces activation-based attention mechanisms (sum of |features|^p or max of features^p) and a gradient-based attention derived from loss w.r.t. inputs.
- Evaluated on CIFAR, CUB, Scene, and ImageNet and shows attention transfer consistently improves student performance over baseline students.
- Despite improvements, student networks still underperform their teacher counterparts across experiments.
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks show higher spatial correlation between objects and attention maps is supported visually (Fig.4) but lacks quantitative evidence.
- Hyperparameter selection process is unclear, and the impact of the attention-loss weight β is not analyzed.
- Figure 7b should report the teacher’s train and validation loss to provide additional context.
- Experiments do not clearly delineate the relative advantages and disadvantages of the different attention map variants.
- Authors do not quantify runtime or speed-up benefits from using smaller student models despite lower parameter counts.
- It is not investigated whether attention transfer provides benefits when student and teacher architectures are identical.
- Writing and motivation are clear and the method consistently improves student accuracy — listed as strengths.
- Main weaknesses: students remain worse than teachers, guidance on which attention mechanism to use is unclear, and novelty is incremental relative to prior work like FitNet.
