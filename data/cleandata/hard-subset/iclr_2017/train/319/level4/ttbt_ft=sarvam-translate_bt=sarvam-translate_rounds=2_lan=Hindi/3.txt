This paper proposes investigating attention transfer between a teacher and a student network.

Attention transfer is achieved by reducing the l2 distance between the teacher/student attention maps at different layers, minimizing classification loss, and optionally with the term "knowledge distillation."

The authors define several activation-based attention methods (sum of full-facility values p increases or sum of facility values p maximizes). They also propose a gradient-based attention method (derivative of loss with respect to input).

They evaluate their approach on several datasets (CIFAR, Cub/Scene, Imagenet), showing that attention transfer helps improve student network test performance. However, student networks perform worst than the teacher, even with attention.

Some comments/questions:
- In Section 3, the authors claim that there is a high spatial correlation between the object and the attention map in the high-precision network. While Figure 4 is compelling, it would be good to show that there are also quantitative results.
- It would be good to see how you chose the hyperparameters, and what the effect of β is.
- It would be good to report the teacher training and validation loss in Figure 7 b).
- From the experiments, it is not clear what the advantages/disadvantages of the different attention maps are.
- AT does not give better results than the teacher. However, the student network has fewer parameters. It would be interesting to identify the relevant speed-up. If you have the same architecture between student and teacher, is there any benefit to attention transfer?

In summary:
Pros:
- Clearly written and well-motivated.
- Consistent improvement in student with attention compared to student alone.
Cons:
- Students' performance is worst than the teacher model.
- It is not clear which attention to use in which case?
- Some degree of incremental novelty compared to FitNet.
