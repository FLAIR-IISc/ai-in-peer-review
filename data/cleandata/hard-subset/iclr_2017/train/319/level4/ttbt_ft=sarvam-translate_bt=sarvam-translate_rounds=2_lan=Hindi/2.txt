The paper presented here presents a modified knowledge distillation framework that reduces the difference in the sum of statistics in a feature map between the teacher and student networks. The authors empirically demonstrated that the proposed methods perform better than the FitNet style distillation baseline.

Pros:
+ The authors evaluated the proposed methods on various computer vision datasets.
+ The paper is generally well-written.

Cons:
- This method seems limited to convolutional architecture.
- The focus terminology is misleading in the paper. The proposed method actually attempts to distill the sum squares of activations (or other statistics such as the sum LP criterion) in a hidden feature map.
- The gradient-based attention transfer seems inconsistent. The proposed gradient-based methods are never directly compared to "attention-based" transfers, nor are they used jointly. It seems that a parallel idea has been added to the paper that does not add much value.
- It is also unclear how the induced 2-norm is calculated in equation (2). Q is a matrix \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W} whose induced 2-norm is its largest single value. It seems computationally expensive to calculate such a cost function. Is it possible that the authors actually mean the Frobenius criterion?

Overall, the proposed distillation method works well in practice, but the paper has some organizational problems and unclear notations.
