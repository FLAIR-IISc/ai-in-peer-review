This paper aims to discuss the transfer of attention within a teacher-student network.

Attention transfer is achieved by reducing the l2 distance within the attention map at different levels. Simultaneously, the amount of classification loss and optionally the knowledge extraction term are also reduced.

The authors define several activation-based attention measures (sum of absolute feature values to the power of p or maximum value of values to the power of p). They also propose a gradient-based attention measure (derivative of loss with respect to input).

They evaluated their method on several datasets such as CIFAR, Cub/Scene, and Imagenet, demonstrating that attention transfer helps improve the performance of student network tests. However, student networks perform worse than teachers despite having attention.

A few comments/questions:
- In section 3, the authors claim that high-precision networks have a high spatial relationship between objects and attention maps. Although Figure 4 is interesting, it would be good to have quantitative results that can show this as well.
- It would be good to see how you selected the hyperparameters values, and to see the impact of β.
- It would be good to provide a report on teacher training and validation loss in Figure 7 b).
- The advantages/disadvantages of different attention maps are not clear from the experiment.
- AT does not give better results than teachers. However, student networks have fewer parameters. It would be interesting to identify the corresponding speed-up. If you keep the same architecture between student and teacher, is there any advantage to attention transfer?

In summary:
Advantages:
- Clearly written and well-inspired.
- Continuous improvement of student with attention compared to only student.

Disadvantages:
- Students have worse performance than the teacher model.
- It is not clear which attention to use in which case.
- Slightly more novelty compared to Fitnet.
