### **Summary**

This paper introduces Attention Transfer (AT), a method for transferring knowledge from a pre-trained "teacher" network to a smaller "student" network. The core mechanism involves training the student to mimic the teacher's "attention maps." This is achieved by adding a loss term that minimizes the L2 distance between the teacher's and student's attention maps at corresponding layers. This AT loss is combined with the standard classification loss and, optionally, a traditional knowledge distillation term. The authors propose several ways to define these attention maps, including activation-based methods (e.g., the sum or max of absolute feature values raised to a power) and a gradient-based method (the derivative of the loss with respect to the inputs).

The method is evaluated across several image classification benchmarks, including CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, and ImageNet. The results consistently demonstrate that Attention Transfer improves the performance of the student network compared to training it from scratch or using knowledge distillation alone. However, in the experiments shown, the student models do not surpass the performance of their respective teacher networks.

### **Strengths**

*   **Clarity and Motivation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for using attention as a mechanism for knowledge transfer—guiding the student on *where* to look—is intuitive and compelling.
*   **Consistent Empirical Gains:** The experimental results are consistent across multiple datasets and network architectures. This demonstrates that AT is a generally effective technique for improving the training of smaller student networks, providing a clear boost over baseline student models.
*   **Novelty of Concept:** The idea of transferring "attention" is an interesting and novel extension to the knowledge distillation literature, moving beyond simply matching output logits or intermediate feature representations.

### **Weaknesses**

*   **Performance Relative to Teacher:** A primary limitation is that while AT improves student performance, it does not enable the student to match or exceed the teacher's accuracy. Since the main motivation for model compression is to achieve a favorable trade-off between performance and efficiency, the paper would be significantly strengthened by quantifying this trade-off. Reporting metrics like inference speed-up, parameter reduction, or FLOPs for the student networks relative to their teachers would provide a clearer picture of the practical benefits.
*   **Guidance on Attention Map Choice:** The paper introduces several types of attention maps but provides limited analysis or intuition on their respective advantages and disadvantages. The experiments do not offer a clear conclusion as to when one type of attention map (e.g., activation-based vs. gradient-based) should be preferred over another. A more in-depth analysis would help readers understand the properties of each and make informed choices for new applications.
*   **Incremental Novelty:** The novelty could be seen as somewhat incremental when compared to prior work like FitNets, which also regularizes training by matching intermediate representations. While attention maps are a different form of intermediate knowledge, the overall framework of minimizing a distance metric between teacher and student hidden layers is similar. A more detailed discussion on the conceptual advantages of transferring attention over transferring raw feature maps would be beneficial.

### **Questions and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Quantitative Analysis of Attention:** In Section 3, the authors claim that networks with higher accuracy exhibit stronger spatial correlation between their attention maps and the object's location. While Figure 4 provides a compelling visual illustration, this claim would be much stronger if supported by quantitative metrics (e.g., pointing game accuracy or intersection-over-union between attention maps and ground-truth bounding boxes).
2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The manuscript would benefit from a discussion on hyperparameter selection, particularly the sensitivity to the AT loss weight, β. An ablation study showing the impact of β on the final student performance would be valuable for practitioners.
3.  **Analysis of Different Attention Types:** Could the authors provide more insight into the pros and cons of the different attention map definitions? For instance, is gradient-based attention more computationally expensive but more effective? When might an activation-based sum be preferable to a max?
4.  **Teacher Performance in Figures:** For a more direct comparison in Figure 7(b), I suggest including the teacher network's training and validation loss curves.
5.  **AT for Regularization:** An interesting experiment would be to apply AT in a scenario where the student and teacher have identical architectures. Would AT act as a form of regularization and lead to a better final model? This could help isolate the "knowledge transfer" effect of AT from the effect of model compression.
