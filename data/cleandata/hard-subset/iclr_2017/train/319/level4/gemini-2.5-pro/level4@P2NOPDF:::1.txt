This paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge transfer, where a compact student network is trained to mimic the behavior of a larger teacher network. The central idea is to transfer knowledge via spatial attention maps. This is a departure from traditional knowledge distillation, which typically focuses on matching logits or intermediate feature activations.

The primary strength of this work lies in its core concept. The motivation to use attention maps as the medium for knowledge transfer is intuitive and compelling. By forcing the student network to focus on the same salient image regions as the powerful teacher network, the method provides a structured and semantically rich form of guidance. This is an elegant alternative to matching raw activations, which can be noisy and less interpretable.

However, the main weakness of the paper is in its experimental validation. The results presented do not demonstrate a significant performance advantage over existing knowledge distillation baselines. The improvements are marginal, which raises questions about the practical benefits of the proposed method given its added complexity.

Furthermore, the empirical evaluation on the large-scale ImageNet dataset (Section 4.2.2) feels preliminary and lacks depth. To substantiate the claims of the method's effectiveness and generalizability, a more comprehensive set of experiments is necessary. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including a more thorough analysis on this dataset, perhaps with different network architectures or more extensive comparisons against competing methods.

In summary, while the paper presents an interesting and well-motivated idea, the current empirical evidence is not strong enough to fully support its claims. My assessment is therefore conditional. I would be willing to reconsider my score if the authors can provide a more robust and extended experimental validation, particularly for the ImageNet results discussed in Section 4.2.2, during the author response period.
