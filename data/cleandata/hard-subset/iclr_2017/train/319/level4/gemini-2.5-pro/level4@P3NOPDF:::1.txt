This paper proposes a novel method for knowledge transfer, designed to train a compact and fast "student" network by leveraging the knowledge from a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The central idea is to transfer knowledge via spatial attention maps. Instead of matching the raw feature activations or the final softened logits, the student network is trained to mimic the attention maps generated by the teacher network.

**Strengths:**

The core contribution of the paper—transferring knowledge through attention maps—is novel and highly intuitive. The concept of teaching a student network *where* to look is a compelling alternative to simply matching feature representations. Attention maps provide a high-level, semantic abstraction of the teacher's inference process, which could be a more flexible and potent form of supervision for the student than low-level features. This approach represents a promising and interesting direction for research in model compression and knowledge distillation.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

While the proposed method is conceptually appealing, the empirical evidence presented in the paper is not yet fully convincing. My primary concerns are as follows:

1.  **Marginal Performance Gains:** The experimental results show that the proposed attention transfer method offers only a small improvement over standard knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015). Given the added complexity of calculating and aligning attention maps, the practical benefit seems limited based on the current results. It would be beneficial to have a clearer understanding of the scenarios where attention transfer significantly outperforms other methods.

2.  **Insufficient Large-Scale Experiments:** The evaluation on the ImageNet dataset, presented in Section 4.2.2, feels preliminary and is not extensive enough to demonstrate the method's effectiveness and scalability. Large-scale validation is critical for any knowledge transfer technique, and the current paper lacks the comprehensive results needed to draw strong conclusions. To make a compelling case, this section requires a more thorough investigation, potentially including different teacher-student architecture pairs, more complete training runs, and comparisons against a wider array of state-of-the-art knowledge transfer methods beyond just knowledge distillation.

**Recommendation:**

In its current form, the paper presents an interesting idea but lacks the strong empirical support necessary for acceptance. The limited performance gains and, more importantly, the preliminary nature of the ImageNet experiments are significant weaknesses.

I would be willing to reconsider my score if the authors can provide a substantially extended version of Section 4.2.2 during the author response period. A more thorough and convincing set of experiments on ImageNet that clearly demonstrates the value of attention transfer would significantly strengthen the paper and address my main concerns. A qualitative analysis, such as visualizing the teacher and student attention maps to show successful knowledge transfer, would also be a valuable addition.
