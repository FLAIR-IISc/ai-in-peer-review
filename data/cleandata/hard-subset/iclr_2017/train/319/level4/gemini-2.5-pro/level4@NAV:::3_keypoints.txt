- Proposes attention transfer by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps at selected layers.  
- Attention transfer loss is combined with classification loss and sometimes knowledge distillation.  
- Defines several attention map types: activation-based (sum/max of |features|^p) and a gradient-based map (loss derivative w.r.t. inputs).  
- Evaluated on CIFAR, CUB/Scene Recognition, and ImageNet datasets.  
- Attention transfer consistently improves student test performance over a baseline.  
- Student models with AT do not reach or exceed teacher performance.  
- Claim that attention maps correlate with object locations is only qualitatively supported; needs quantitative evidence.  
- Hyperparameter selection (notably the β coefficient) is not detailed; ablation/sensitivity analysis is needed.  
- Suggests adding teacher training/validation loss curves in Fig. 7b for baseline comparison.  
- Experimental results do not clarify relative advantages or recommended use cases for different attention map formulations.  
- Important missing analysis: quantify computational speed-up from smaller students and test AT when student and teacher architectures are identical.  
- Pros: well-written and clearly motivated; method gives consistent student gains over baseline.  
- Cons: students still underperform teachers, unclear guidance on attention types, and novelty seems incremental compared to prior work like FitNets.
