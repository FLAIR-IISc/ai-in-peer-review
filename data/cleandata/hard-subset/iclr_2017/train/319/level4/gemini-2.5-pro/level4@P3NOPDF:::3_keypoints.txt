- Proposes Attention Transfer (AT): train a smaller student to mimic teacher attention maps by adding an L2 loss between their attention maps at multiple layers.
- Defines several attention map types: activation-based (sum/max of absolute activations) and gradient-based (derivative of loss w.r.t. input).
- Evaluated on CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, Stanford Scene, and ImageNet, showing consistent student improvements over training from scratch.
- Students improve with AT but do not surpass teacher performance.
- Strength: clear motivation and presentation; intuitive idea that teachers can guide students where to focus.
- Strength: consistent and significant performance gains across datasets and architectures, suggesting general applicability.
- Weakness: lacks quantitative support for the claim that higher-accuracy networks have attention maps more correlated with objects (e.g., no IoU or similar metrics).
- Weakness: no hyperparameter analysis, particularly for the weighting factor β balancing AT and classification loss.
- Weakness: experimental comparisons lack teacher training/validation curves, hindering interpretation of student learning dynamics.
- Weakness: insufficient guidance on choosing attention-map formulations and no clear conclusion on which type is best.
- Weakness: practical utility not fully characterized — no quantification of computational savings (parameters, FLOPs, speed) and no control where student and teacher share architecture to test regularization effects.
- Weakness/novelty: method is similar to prior hint-based approaches like FitNets; novelty is limited to using attention maps and needs clearer positioning against prior work.
