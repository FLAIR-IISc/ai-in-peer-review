This paper presents a modified knowledge distillation framework that trains a student network to mimic statistics of a teacher network's feature maps. Specifically, the method minimizes the difference between summary statistics (e.g., the sum of squared activations) computed across the spatial dimensions of feature maps from the teacher and the student. The authors provide empirical evidence showing that the proposed method outperforms a FitNet-style distillation baseline.

**Pros:**
+ The proposed methods are evaluated on a variety of computer vision datasets.
+ The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

**Cons:**
- The method, in its current form, appears to be limited to convolutional architectures, as it relies on spatial feature maps.

- The use of the term "attention" is potentially misleading. The proposed method distills a summary statistic of the activations across a hidden feature map (e.g., the sum of squares or the sum of absolute values). While this captures the spatial distribution of activation magnitudes, it differs from the more common definition of attention as a mechanism for re-weighting features. A more precise term might be "feature statistic matching."

- The section on gradient-based attention transfer feels disconnected from the main contribution. This gradient-based method is neither compared directly with nor used in conjunction with the activation-based transfer. It reads like a parallel idea that was added to the paper, and its contribution to the overall framework is not clearly justified.

- The notation in Equation (2) is unclear. The equation uses the induced 2-norm of the feature map Q, which is a matrix in ℝ^(H×W). The induced 2-norm (or spectral norm) is the largest singular value of the matrix, and computing this repeatedly during training would be computationally expensive. Could the authors clarify if they intended to use a different, more efficient norm, such as the Frobenius norm?

**Overall:**
The proposed distillation method demonstrates strong empirical performance. However, the paper suffers from some organizational issues and unclear notation. The misleading "attention" terminology, the unclear definition of the norm in the loss function, and the inclusion of a tangential gradient-based method detract from the paper's clarity. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the submission.
