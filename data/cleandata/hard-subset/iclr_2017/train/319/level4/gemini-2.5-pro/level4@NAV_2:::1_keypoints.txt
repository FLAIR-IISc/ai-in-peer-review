- Proposes Attention Transfer (AT) to improve student CNNs by forcing them to mimic teacher spatial attention maps.
- Introduces two attention definitions: activation-based (channel summaries) and gradient-based (loss gradient w.r.t. input).
- Evaluates AT on multiple architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN) and datasets (CIFAR, CUB, MIT Scenes, ImageNet).
- Shows AT improves student performance and can be combined with Knowledge Distillation (KD).
- Recommendation: Reject.
- Primary reason 1: ImageNet experiments are incomplete and insufficient to validate scalability.
- Primary reason 2: Performance gains over strong baselines like KD are often marginal, limiting practical significance.
- ImageNet concerns: only two transfer losses used, no hyperparameter tuning, and KD baseline not trained to completion.
- CIFAR/MIT results: AT consistently beats student baseline but only marginally outperforms KD (example: WRN-16-2/WRN-16-1 KD 8.15% vs AT 7.93%); on MIT Scenes AT matches KD.
- Positive comments: central idea is intuitive, paper is clearly written, and exploration of attention map variants (F_sum^p, F_max^p) is valuable.
- Major revision needed: complete ImageNet evaluation (apply losses to all residual groups, tune hyperparameters, fully train KD baseline).
- Suggest deeper analysis explaining why attention maps may transfer more effectively than full activations (e.g., potential information bottleneck).
- Gradient-based AT is only tested on NIN without batch norm; recommend extending to modern architectures like ResNets despite implementation challenges.
- Requested clarification 1: quantify computational overhead of activation- and gradient-based AT versus standard training and KD.
- Requested clarification 2: provide ablation showing impact of normalizing vectorized attention maps.
- Requested clarification 3: explain why sum-based attention outperforms max-based (intuition for aggregating across channels).
