This work investigates the concept of transferring attention from a teacher model to a student network. The transfer mechanism is implemented by minimizing the L2 distance between the attention maps of the teacher and student networks at selected layers. This attention transfer loss is combined with the primary classification loss and, in some configurations, a knowledge distillation term. The authors define several types of attention maps, including activation-based forms (e.g., the sum or maximum of absolute feature values raised to a power p) and a gradient-based form (the derivative of the loss with respect to the input features).

The methodology is evaluated across several datasets (CIFAR, CUB/Scene Recognition, ImageNet). The results indicate that attention transfer consistently enhances the test performance of the student network compared to a baseline. Nevertheless, the final student models do not achieve performance levels equivalent to or exceeding the teacher model.

A few remarks and questions:
-   The claim in Section 3, suggesting a stronger spatial correlation between attention maps and object locations for more accurate networks, is supported qualitatively by Figure 4. Providing quantitative evidence to substantiate this claim would strengthen the paper.
-   The selection process for hyperparameters is not detailed. An ablation study or sensitivity analysis, particularly on the impact of the $\beta$ coefficient, would be beneficial for understanding its role.
-   For completeness, including the teacher's training and validation loss curves in Figure 7b) would provide a useful baseline for comparison.
-   The experimental results do not clearly delineate the relative advantages and disadvantages of the different proposed attention map formulations. It remains unclear when one type should be preferred over another.
-   Given that Attention Transfer (AT) does not surpass the teacher's performance, a key motivation is model compression. It would be valuable to quantify the computational speed-up or inference time reduction associated with the smaller student models. Furthermore, an important control experiment is missing: what is the effect of AT when the student and teacher have identical architectures? Does it provide any benefit in that scenario?

In summary:

Pros:
-   The paper is well-written, with clear motivation for the proposed approach.
-   The method demonstrates consistent performance gains for the student network over a standard baseline.

Cons:
-   The student models, even after attention transfer, do not match the performance of the teacher models.
-   There is a lack of clarity regarding the specific use cases or advantages of each proposed attention type.
-   The novelty of the work appears somewhat incremental when compared to prior methods like FitNets.
