- Introduces Attention Transfer (AT), forcing a student CNN to mimic a teacher's spatial attention maps to improve performance.
- Proposes two attention map definitions: activation-based (summary statistics across channels) and gradient-based (sensitivity of output to input).
- Provides extensive empirical evidence that AT consistently improves student performance across datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet).
- Demonstrates effectiveness across architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN) and teacher-student pairs.
- Shows AT can outperform full-activation transfer (FitNets) and can be combined with traditional knowledge distillation.
- Decision: Accept.
- Novel, intuitive contribution: shifting distillation from matching full feature maps to matching where the network “looks.”
- Strong experimental validation, including ImageNet results and ablation studies on attention formulations.
- Claims that AT may be the first successful intermediate-representation transfer method applied to ImageNet.
- Paper is well-written, clearly structured, and methods are explained in sufficient detail.
- Student still lags behind teacher; practical utility would be clearer with efficiency metrics (parameters, FLOPs, inference speed).
- Limited analysis of relative pros/cons of different attention map formulations; choice remains largely empirical.
- Suggestions: add quantitative metrics for attention map quality (e.g., pointing game/IoU), analyze sensitivity to hyperparameter β, and include performance-vs-efficiency comparisons.
- Recommend adding teacher training/validation curves to visualizations for reference.
- Questions for authors: guidance on choosing attention map variants (F_sum^p vs F_max^p, activation vs gradient) and whether AT helps when student and teacher share the same architecture (acts as regularizer).
