This paper investigates the concept of attention transfer (AT) between a larger, pre-trained "teacher" network and a smaller "student" network. The proposed method works by minimizing the L2 distance between the teacher and student attention maps at various layers. This attention transfer loss is added to the standard classification loss and an optional knowledge distillation term.

The authors define several types of attention maps. These include activation-based attention, calculated as the sum or max of the absolute values of feature activations raised to a power p, and a gradient-based attention, defined as the derivative of the loss with respect to the input. The authors evaluate their approach on several datasets (CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, Scene, and ImageNet), demonstrating that attention transfer consistently improves the student network's test performance. However, it is noted that the student network, even with AT, performs worse than the teacher network.

**Remarks and Questions:**

*   In Section 3, the authors claim that networks with higher accuracy exhibit a stronger spatial correlation between the object of interest and the attention map. While Figure 4 provides compelling qualitative evidence, it would be beneficial to see quantitative results to support this claim.
*   Could the authors elaborate on the selection process for hyperparameters? Specifically, an analysis showing the impact of the $\beta$ parameter on performance would be valuable for understanding the method's sensitivity.
*   It would be helpful to include the teacher's training and validation loss curves in Figure 7b) for a more direct comparison of training dynamics.
*   The experimental results do not clearly delineate the relative pros and cons of the different proposed attention map types. Further analysis on when to prefer one type of attention map over another would strengthen the paper's practical contribution.
*   While AT does not lead to student performance that surpasses the teacher, the student networks have fewer parameters. It would be insightful to quantify the corresponding computational speed-up (e.g., in FLOPs or inference time) to better characterize the trade-off between accuracy and efficiency.
*   A key question remains: is there any benefit to applying attention transfer when the student and teacher networks share the same architecture? This experiment could help isolate the effect of AT as a regularizer or optimization aid, separate from its role in model compression.

**Summary:**

**Pros:**
*   The paper is clearly written and the motivation for attention transfer is well-argued.
*   The proposed method consistently improves the performance of student networks compared to training them from scratch.

**Cons:**
*   The student models, even with attention transfer, do not match the performance of the larger teacher models.
*   The paper lacks a clear guideline on which type of attention map is most suitable for different scenarios or architectures.
*   The novelty of the work appears somewhat incremental when compared to prior knowledge distillation methods like FitNet.
