- Proposes Attention Transfer (AT): minimize L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps at multiple layers, combined with classification loss and optional distillation.  
- Defines several activation-based attention maps (sum/max of absolute activations to power p) and a gradient-based attention map (loss derivative w.r.t. input).  
- Evaluated on CIFAR, CUB-200, Stanford Scene, and ImageNet; AT consistently improves student performance over baseline.  
- Despite improvements, student networks remain worse than teachers even with AT.  
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks have stronger spatial correlation between object and attention map is supported visually but lacks quantitative evidence.  
- Paper lacks analysis of hyperparameter selection, notably the impact of the attention transfer weight β.  
- Figure 7(b) should also report teacher training/validation loss for a fuller comparison.  
- Experiments do not clarify the relative advantages or context-specific suitability of the different attention map types.  
- Missing characterization of the computational trade-off: report speed-up or efficiency gains from using smaller student models.  
- No experiment testing AT when student and teacher share the same architecture to isolate AT effects from model compression.  
- Paper is clearly written with well-explained motivation.  
- Method provides consistent empirical gains but appears incrementally novel relative to prior work like FitNets.
