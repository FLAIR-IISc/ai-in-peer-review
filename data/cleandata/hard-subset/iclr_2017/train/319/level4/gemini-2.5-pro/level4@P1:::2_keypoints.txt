- Proposes "Attention Transfer" (AT) to train a student network to mimic teacher attention maps, with two types: activation-based and gradient-based.  
- Activation-based attention: primary method computing a spatial map by summarizing activations across channels (e.g., sum of squares).  
- Gradient-based attention: secondary method using the loss gradient w.r.t. the input as a sensitivity map.  
- Extensive experiments on CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet and architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN) show activation-based AT consistently improves student accuracy.  
- AT outperforms a FitNet-style full-activation transfer baseline and is claimed as the first successful distillation on ImageNet.  
- Reviewer decision: Weak Accept — method is simple and effective but presentation issues weaken the paper.  
- Positive: Strong empirical results and evidence that compact feature-map statistic distillation can be better than full feature transfer.  
- Negative: The term "attention" is misleading since the method is a post-hoc summary statistic, not a conventional learnable attention mechanism.  
- Negative: Gradient-based AT is underdeveloped and feels disconnected from the main activation-based contribution, creating a disjointed narrative.  
- Notation issue: Equation (2) ambiguity — clarify that ‖Q‖2 denotes the standard Euclidean (L2) norm of the vectorized map.  
- Suggestion: Focus the main paper on activation-based transfer, move gradient-based experiments to an appendix or future work.  
- Suggestion: Reconsider naming (avoid "attention") and discuss applicability to non-spatial architectures like Vision Transformers.  
- Questions for authors: Confirm norm interpretation in Eq.(2) and explain why gradient-based experiments used NIN without batch norm and how batch norm complicates the second backpropagation.
