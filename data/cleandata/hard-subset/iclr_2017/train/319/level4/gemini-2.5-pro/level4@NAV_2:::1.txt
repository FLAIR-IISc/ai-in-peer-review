This paper proposes a novel knowledge transfer mechanism, termed Attention Transfer (AT), designed to improve the performance of a smaller "student" convolutional neural network (CNN) by leveraging the knowledge of a larger "teacher" CNN. The core contribution is the idea of compelling the student network to mimic the spatial attention maps of the teacher. The authors introduce two distinct methods for defining these attention maps: an activation-based approach, which summarizes activations across channels, and a gradient-based approach, which uses the gradient of the loss with respect to the input. The efficacy of these methods is demonstrated through a series of experiments on various architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN) and datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet), showing that Attention Transfer can improve student performance and can be effectively combined with existing techniques like Knowledge Distillation.

I am recommending to **Reject** this paper at this time, with two primary reasons for my decision. First, the experimental validation on the large-scale ImageNet dataset is preliminary and lacks the thoroughness required to substantiate the method's scalability and general effectiveness. Second, while the results on smaller datasets are positive, the improvements over strong baselines like Knowledge Distillation are often marginal, questioning the practical significance of the proposed method.

My supporting arguments are as follows:

1.  **Incomplete ImageNet Experiments:** The ImageNet benchmark is crucial for validating knowledge transfer methods. The authors acknowledge in Section 4.2.2 that their experiments are not fully developed: they used only two transfer losses instead of applying them to all relevant network blocks, and they "did not have time to tune any hyperparameters." While the reported 1.1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18 is promising, the incomplete experimental setup diminishes the confidence in this result. Furthermore, the claim that Knowledge Distillation (KD) struggles on ImageNet is not fully supported, as the authors state they "did not train until the end due to lack of computational resources." A comprehensive and complete comparison against baselines on ImageNet is necessary to make a compelling case for the method's value.

2.  **Marginal Performance Gains:** On the CIFAR datasets (Table 1), Attention Transfer consistently outperforms the student baseline. However, its advantage over Knowledge Distillation is often small (e.g., on WRN-16-2/WRN-16-1, KD achieves 8.15% error while AT achieves 7.93%). While the combination of AT+KD yields the best results, the incremental gain provided by AT is modest. On the MIT Scenes dataset (Table 4), the paper reports that AT performs "as well as KD," not better. This suggests that the unique benefits of transferring attention, as opposed to output distributions, may not be universally significant across all tasks.

I believe the paper can be significantly improved, and I offer the following feedback not as a condition of acceptance, but to help strengthen future revisions:

*   The central idea of transferring attention is intuitive and well-motivated. The paper is clearly written, and the exploration of different attention map formulations (e.g., `F_sum^p`, `F_max^p`) is a valuable contribution.
*   The most critical area for improvement is to complete the ImageNet experiments. This should include adding transfer losses across all residual groups, conducting proper hyperparameter tuning, and running the KD baseline to completion to allow for a fair and direct comparison.
*   The paper states that attention maps carry "more important for transfer than full activations." This is an interesting finding. The work could be strengthened by providing more intuition or analysis as to why this might be the case. For example, do attention maps act as a form of information bottleneck that filters out noisy or redundant feature information?
*   The gradient-based AT experiments are limited to a non-standard setting (NIN without batch normalization). Broadening the evaluation of this interesting approach to modern architectures like ResNets would increase its impact, despite the implementation challenges noted by the authors.

To help clarify my understanding and strengthen my assessment, I would appreciate it if the authors could answer the following questions:

*   Could you quantify the computational overhead of activation-based and gradient-based Attention Transfer relative to standard training and to a Knowledge Distillation pipeline?
*   You found that normalizing the vectorized attention maps before computing the loss was crucial for success. Could you share results from an ablation study where this normalization is omitted to demonstrate its impact?
*   In Section 4.1.1, your results show that sum-based attention functions outperform max-based ones. What is your intuition for why aggregating information across all channels (sum) is a better signal for transfer than using the most dominant channel activation (max) at each spatial location?
