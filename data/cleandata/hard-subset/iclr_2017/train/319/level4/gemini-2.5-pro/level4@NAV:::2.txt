This work introduces a novel knowledge distillation approach designed to align the statistical properties of feature maps between teacher and student models. Specifically, the proposed loss function minimizes the discrepancy between the summed statistics of their respective feature activations. Through empirical evaluation, the authors show that their method surpasses the performance of FitNet-style distillation baselines.

Pros:
+ The proposed method is thoroughly validated across multiple computer vision datasets.
+ The manuscript is generally clear and well-written.

Cons:
- The applicability of the proposed technique appears to be confined to convolutional neural networks.
- The use of the term "attention" is potentially confusing. The core mechanism involves distilling summary statistics (e.g., the sum of squared values or other L_p norms) of the entire feature map, rather than what is conventionally understood as an attention mechanism.
- The section on gradient-based attention transfer feels disconnected from the main contribution. This gradient-based approach is not directly compared with, nor integrated into, the primary activation-based transfer method. It reads as a separate concept that dilutes the paper's focus without demonstrating significant additional value.
- The computation of the induced 2-norm in Equation (2) requires clarification. For a matrix Q of size HxW, the induced 2-norm corresponds to its largest singular value, which can be computationally intensive to calculate within a loss function. The authors should clarify if they are perhaps using the Frobenius norm instead, which would be more computationally tractable.

In summary, while the proposed distillation technique demonstrates strong empirical performance, the paper suffers from some structural weaknesses and a lack of clarity in its mathematical notation.
