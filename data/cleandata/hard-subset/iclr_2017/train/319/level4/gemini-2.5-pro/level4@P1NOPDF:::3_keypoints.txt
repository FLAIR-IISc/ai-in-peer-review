- Paper studies attention transfer (AT) from a large pre-trained teacher to a smaller student by minimizing L2 distance between attention maps across layers.  
- AT loss is combined with standard classification loss and an optional knowledge distillation term.  
- Authors define activation-based attention maps (sum/max of absolute activations^p) and a gradient-based attention map (loss derivative w.r.t. input).  
- Experiments on CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, Scene, and ImageNet show AT consistently improves student test performance.  
- Despite improvements, student networks with AT still perform worse than the teacher.  
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks show stronger spatial correlation between object and attention map is supported qualitatively but lacks quantitative evidence.  
- Hyperparameter selection (notably the β weighting AT) is not analyzed; sensitivity study is requested.  
- Figure 7b) should include teacher training/validation loss curves for clearer training-dynamics comparison.  
- Paper does not clearly compare pros/cons of the different attention map types or give guidance on when to prefer each.  
- Suggest reporting computational speed-up (FLOPs or inference time) to quantify the accuracy-efficiency trade-off from using smaller students.  
- Recommend an experiment where teacher and student share the same architecture to test whether AT acts as a regularizer/optimizer independent of compression.  
- Pros: clear writing, well-motivated, and AT consistently improves students over training from scratch.  
- Cons: students still lag behind teachers, unclear guidance on attention-map choice, and novelty appears incremental relative to prior distillation methods like FitNet.
