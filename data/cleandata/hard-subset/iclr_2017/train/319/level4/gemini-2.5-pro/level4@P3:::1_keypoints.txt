- Introduces Attention Transfer (AT) to train a student network to mimic a teacher's spatial attention maps.  
- Proposes two attention-map definitions: activation-based (channel-magnitude summaries) and gradient-based (sensitivity to inputs).  
- Demonstrates AT improves student performance on CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and shows a preliminary ImageNet result.  
- Shows AT combines well with Knowledge Distillation (KD) and that AT+KD yields the best results.  
- Finds transferring 2D attention maps outperforms transferring full activation tensors (FitNets-style).  
- Decision: Weak Accept — clear, compelling idea but incomplete evidence for large-scale ImageNet claims.  
- Novelty: Spatial attention as a focused, intuitive medium for knowledge transfer versus high-dimensional feature transfer.  
- Main weakness: ImageNet experiment is preliminary, under-tuned, and applied only to the last two residual groups.  
- CIFAR and transfer-learning experiments are consistently positive and convincingly support AT's effectiveness.  
- Gradient-based AT is underdeveloped, limited by batch-normalization complications and evaluated only on a non-standard NIN setup.  
- Reviewer requests hyperparameter-sensitivity analysis for the transfer loss weight β.  
- Reviewer asks for intuition/explanation on why sum-based attention (especially p=2) outperforms max-based variants.  
- Requests updated ImageNet results applying AT to all four residual groups and minimal hyperparameter tuning.  
- Asks for clarification on technical challenges of performing the second backpropagation step through batch-normalized layers.  
- Seeks deeper intuition why attention maps transfer better than full activations—possibly due to regularization forcing the student to learn its own features.
