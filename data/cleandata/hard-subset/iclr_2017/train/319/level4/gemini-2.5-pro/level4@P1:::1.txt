### Summary

This paper introduces Attention Transfer (AT), a novel method for knowledge transfer between neural networks. The core idea is to train a "student" network to mimic the spatial attention maps of a more powerful "teacher" network. The authors define two types of attention maps: activation-based maps, which summarize the activations across channels at each spatial location, and gradient-based maps, which capture the sensitivity of the output to changes in the input. The proposed method is presented as a form of hint-based training, analogous to FitNets, but using these compact attention maps instead of full feature maps. The authors conduct experiments across a variety of datasets (CIFAR-10/100, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN), demonstrating that Attention Transfer consistently improves the student network's performance, can be combined with Knowledge Distillation (KD), and is more effective than transferring full activation tensors.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Weak Reject

My decision is based on two primary concerns:

1.  **Marginal Performance Gains:** While the proposed method shows consistent improvements, the performance gains over standard Knowledge Distillation (KD) are often incremental. In some cases, AT alone does not outperform KD, though their combination generally yields the best results.
2.  **Preliminary Large-Scale Experiments:** The experiments on ImageNet, which are critical for validating the scalability and general applicability of the method, are presented as preliminary. The authors explicitly state that they did not have time to tune hyperparameters or run the full experiment, which weakens the support for the paper's main claims.

I would be willing to reconsider my score if the authors can provide more complete and thoroughly tuned results for the ImageNet experiments in their rebuttal.

### Supporting Arguments

The central idea of using attention as a medium for knowledge transfer is intuitive and compelling. The finding that transferring these summarized maps is more effective than transferring entire activation tensors (as in FitNets) is a particularly interesting contribution. However, the experimental support, while broad, is not uniformly strong.

On CIFAR-10 (Table 1), the combination of AT and KD consistently produces the best results. However, the added benefit of AT on top of a strong KD baseline is relatively small. For instance, in the WRN-40-2 to WRN-16-2 transfer, KD alone improves the baseline from 5.92% to 5.37% error, while adding AT only reduces it further to 5.20%. In this same setting, AT by itself (5.48%) is less effective than KD. This raises the question of the practical significance of the proposed method when simpler techniques like KD achieve most of the gains.

The most significant concern is the state of the ImageNet experiments (Section 4.2.2). The reported 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement for ResNet-18 is promising. However, the authors' admission that they "did not have time to tune any hyperparameters" and that they plan to "update the paper with losses on all 4 groups of residual blocks" makes it difficult to fairly assess this result. ImageNet is the standard benchmark for demonstrating that a method scales, and a preliminary, untuned result is insufficient evidence. Furthermore, the paper claims that KD fails to work in their ImageNet setup, which is a strong claim used to position AT as a uniquely successful method for large-scale transfer. This claim requires more substantial evidence and detailed reporting of the experimental setup for KD to be convincing.

### Additional Feedback and Questions

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the paper and are not the primary basis for my decision.

*   **Strengths to Emphasize:** The comparison against full-activation transfer is a highlight of the paper. The insight that a compressed representation of network focus (the attention map) is a better transfer target than the full, high-dimensional activation tensor is valuable and should be emphasized.
*   **Gradient-based AT:** The gradient-based attention transfer method is an interesting idea, but the experiments are confined to a simple setting (NIN on CIFAR without batch normalization). Expanding on this section with more analysis or application to more modern architectures would strengthen the paper.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** An ablation study on the `β` coefficient and the choice of the exponent `p` in the `F^p_sum` function would be beneficial for understanding the method's sensitivity and providing guidance for future users.

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following questions in their rebuttal:

1.  **ImageNet Results:** Have you had the opportunity to complete the planned ImageNet experiments with attention losses on all residual groups and with tuned hyperparameters? Updated results could significantly impact the assessment of this work.
2.  **Knowledge Distillation on ImageNet:** You report that KD performed poorly on ImageNet in your experiments. Could you provide more details about the KD configuration you used (e.g., temperature, `α` value)? This is a critical baseline, and its failure is an important part of your motivation, so understanding the setup is essential.
3.  **Task-Dependent Efficacy:** In your transfer learning experiments (Table 4), AT provides a much larger benefit than KD on the CUB fine-grained dataset, but a similar benefit on the Scenes dataset. You speculate this is due to the importance of intermediate attention for fine-grained tasks. Could you elaborate on this? Does this suggest that AT is particularly well-suited for a specific class of problems?
4.  **Why Attention Maps?:** Do you have any further intuition on why transferring summarized attention maps is more effective than transferring the full activation tensors? Is it possible that forcing a direct match of full activations over-constrains the student network's optimization process?
