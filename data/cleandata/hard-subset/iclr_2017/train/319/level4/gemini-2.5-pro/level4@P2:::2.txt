### Summary

This paper introduces a novel knowledge transfer technique called "attention transfer" (AT), designed to improve the performance of a smaller "student" convolutional neural network (CNN) by leveraging guidance from a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The core contribution is the idea of defining and transferring "attention maps," which are 2D spatial summaries of a network's internal activations. The authors propose two main methods for creating these maps: one based on aggregating activation statistics across channels (activation-based AT) and another based on input gradients (gradient-based AT). Through a comprehensive set of experiments on datasets including CIFAR, CUB, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, the authors demonstrate that forcing a student network to mimic the teacher's attention maps consistently improves its accuracy. Notably, the proposed activation-based AT is shown to be more effective than transferring full activation tensors (as in FitNets) and is successfully applied to the large-scale ImageNet dataset.

### Decision: Reject

While the empirical results are promising and suggest an effective knowledge transfer method, I recommend rejection due to two key issues:
1.  The use of the term "attention" is misleading, as the proposed mechanism is a form of feature statistic distillation rather than attention in the widely accepted sense.
2.  The paper's narrative is weakened by presenting two distinct and loosely connected ideas (activation-based and gradient-based transfer), with the latter being significantly less developed and evaluated.

### Supporting Arguments

1.  **Misleading Terminology:** The central concept of the paper is framed around "attention," but the mechanism described does not align with the common understanding of attention in the deep learning literature (e.g., the weighted-sum mechanisms pioneered in machine translation or self-attention in Transformers). The proposed activation-based "attention map" is a post-hoc summary of activations, generated by computing a simple statistic (e.g., sum of squares) across the channel dimension of a feature map. While this map effectively highlights spatially salient regions, it is not a learned, dynamic mechanism that modulates information flow within the network. Calling this "attention" overloads the term and creates a conceptual disconnect with established work. A more accurate description would be "spatial activation statistic transfer" or "activation summary distillation." This terminological choice obscures the paper's true contribution, which is an effective and lightweight method for feature distillation.

2.  **Lack of Cohesion and Focus:** The paper introduces two separate methods: activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer. These two approaches are treated almost as independent works within the same paper. The activation-based method is thoroughly explored across multiple datasets and architectures, forming the core strength of the paper. In contrast, the gradient-based method is evaluated only on CIFAR-10, using a specific network configuration without batch normalization, and its connection to the main activation-based results is not clearly established. The two methods are not combined, nor are they compared in a controlled, convincing manner. This division makes the paper feel unfocused. The paper would be significantly stronger if it concentrated solely on the well-validated activation-based transfer, presenting a more cohesive and compelling narrative. The gradient-based section, in its current form, feels like a preliminary experiment that detracts from the main message.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the manuscript and are not primary reasons for the rejection decision.

*   **Clarification on Notation in Equation (2):** The equation for the attention transfer loss is `|| Q_S/||Q_S||_2 - Q_T/||Q_T||_2 ||_p`. Here, `Q` is defined as the *vectorized* attention map. Could you please confirm that `||.||_2` refers to the standard vector L2 (Euclidean) norm? The distinction is important, as for a non-vectorized map, `||.||_2` could be misinterpreted as the matrix-induced 2-norm (spectral norm), which is computationally very different. A brief clarification would enhance the paper's precision.

*   **Comparison to Full Activation Transfer:** The paper shows that AT outperforms "FitNets-style hints using l2 losses on full activations." This is an interesting result. However, FitNets used a regressor with additional learnable parameters. The experiments here seem to omit these parameters ("without 1x1 convolutions"). Could the authors clarify if the comparison is fair? It seems plausible that transferring a low-dimensional summary (the attention map) is an easier optimization target for the student than matching a high-dimensional tensor, which might explain the performance gap.

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions that the loss weight `β` is set based on the size of the attention map. Could you provide more insight into the sensitivity of the results to this hyperparameter? A brief ablation study or discussion on how this value was chosen would strengthen the experimental validation.

*   **Generalizability:** The work is situated entirely within convolutional architectures. Have the authors considered if this principle of transferring activation summaries could be applied to other architectures, such as Vision Transformers, where attention maps are an explicit and integral part of the model? A discussion on this could broaden the potential impact of the work.
