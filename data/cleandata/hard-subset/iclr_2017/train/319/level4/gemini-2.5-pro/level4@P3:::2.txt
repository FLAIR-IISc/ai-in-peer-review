### **Summary**

This paper introduces a novel knowledge transfer method called Attention Transfer (AT), designed to improve the performance of a smaller "student" convolutional neural network (CNN) by leveraging the knowledge of a larger, more powerful "teacher" CNN. The core idea is to compel the student network to mimic the "attention maps" of the teacher. The authors define these attention maps as spatial summaries of activation patterns within intermediate layers, proposing two main types: activation-based (derived from statistics like the sum of squared activations across channels) and gradient-based (derived from the gradient of the loss with respect to the input). The paper provides extensive empirical evidence across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, NIN) to demonstrate that Attention Transfer consistently improves student network accuracy. Notably, the method is shown to be more effective than transferring full activation tensors (à la FitNets) and is successfully applied to the large-scale ImageNet dataset.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The proposed method, while conceptually simple, is demonstrated to be highly effective. The paper presents compelling and thorough empirical results across a variety of standard benchmarks, showing consistent performance improvements for the student network. The success on ImageNet is particularly significant, as many knowledge transfer methods have struggled to show clear benefits on this scale.
2.  The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of knowledge distillation by identifying a new and effective form of "hint" for student training. The finding that summarized attention maps can be more beneficial for knowledge transfer than the full activation tensors is an interesting and non-obvious result that warrants further investigation.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The primary strength of this work lies in its comprehensive experimental validation.
*   **Consistent Improvements:** Across all experiments, from CIFAR-10 to ImageNet, Attention Transfer provides a notable boost in accuracy. For instance, on ImageNet, training a ResNet-18 student with a ResNet-34 teacher yields a significant 1.1% improvement in top-1 accuracy (Table 5). This demonstrates the robustness and general applicability of the approach.
*   **Strong Baselines:** The authors compare their method against relevant baselines. In Section 4.1.2, they show that AT provides a much better final accuracy than a FitNets-style approach that transfers the full activation tensors. This comparison strengthens their claim that the "attention map" abstraction is a more effective form of knowledge to transfer.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The paper includes useful ablations, such as the comparison of different attention mapping functions (Table 2), which shows that sum-based functions generally outperform max-based ones. This provides practical guidance for implementing the method.
*   **Novelty in Application:** The authors claim that this is one of the first knowledge transfer methods to be successfully applied to ImageNet. Given the difficulty of demonstrating gains from knowledge distillation on large-scale, complex datasets, this is a noteworthy achievement and a valuable contribution to the community.

While the paper is strong, there are some weaknesses in its presentation and conceptual framing that slightly detract from its impact.
*   **"Attention" Terminology:** The use of the term "attention" is potentially confusing. In much of the contemporary literature (e.g., in NLP and for tasks like image captioning), "attention" refers to a learned, dynamic mechanism that weights or selects information during a network's forward pass. Here, it is used to describe a post-hoc static summary of activations. While related to the concept of saliency, this distinction is important. Clarifying this positioning relative to other attention mechanisms would strengthen the paper.
*   **Cohesion of Gradient-Based Method:** The gradient-based attention transfer method feels somewhat disconnected from the main activation-based approach. It is only evaluated on CIFAR-10 under a specific, simplified setting (e.g., no batch normalization) and is never integrated with or directly compared to the activation-based method on the main, stronger baselines. This section reads more like a preliminary exploration of a separate idea rather than a fully integrated part of the proposed framework.

Despite these minor issues, the strength and breadth of the empirical results for activation-based attention transfer are convincing and make a solid case for acceptance.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions aimed at further improving the paper and are not central to my decision.
*   The paper is generally well-written, but the notation in Equation (2) could be slightly clarified. Specifying that `Q` is a vector and that `||Q||_2` is the standard L2 (Euclidean) vector norm would remove any potential ambiguity with matrix norms.
*   The influence of the hyperparameter `β` is important for balancing the standard classification loss and the attention transfer loss. The paper provides a rule of thumb for setting it, but a small sensitivity analysis or further discussion on how `β` was chosen would be beneficial for reproducibility and understanding.
*   The argument that the method is limited to convolutional architectures is a minor one, as CNNs are the focus. However, a brief discussion on whether the core idea—transferring statistical summaries of activations—could be generalized to other architectures (e.g., Transformers, RNNs) might broaden the paper's conceptual impact.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points to help solidify my understanding of the work:
1.  The results comparing Attention Transfer to full activation transfer (Section 4.1.2) are very interesting. What is your intuition for why transferring a summarized statistic (the attention map) is more effective for training a student than transferring the full, richer information contained in the entire activation tensor? Does this suggest that the full activation contains noisy or difficult-to-mimic information that hinders the student's learning?
2.  The experiments for gradient-based attention transfer were conducted under specific settings (e.g., without batch normalization). Could you comment on the challenges of applying this method more broadly? In particular, how does the required second backpropagation step interact with components like batch normalization, where statistics are computed across a batch?
3.  In Equation (2), could you please confirm that `QjS` and `QjT` are vectors, `||Q||_2` refers to the standard vector L2-norm, and the outer `||.||_p` (with p=2 in experiments) is the Euclidean distance between the two L2-normalized vectors? A brief clarification in the text would be helpful.
