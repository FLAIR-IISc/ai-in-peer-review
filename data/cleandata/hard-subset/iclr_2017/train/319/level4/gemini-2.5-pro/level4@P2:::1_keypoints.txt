- Introduces Attention Transfer (AT): train a student to mimic teacher spatial attention maps (activation- and gradient-based) for knowledge transfer.
- Activation-based maps: summarize channel activations (e.g., sum^p or max^p); gradient-based maps: sensitivity of output to input.
- Experiments on CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet across multiple architectures show AT improves student accuracy and complements Knowledge Distillation (KD).
- AT outperforms transferring full activation tensors (F-ActT) in reported experiments.
- Decision: Weak Accept.
- Strength: Novel, intuitive idea focusing on “where” a model attends rather than raw features or outputs.
- Strength: Thorough exploration of activation-based mappings; sum-based functions empirically better than max-based.
- Strength: Strong CIFAR-10 results; AT+KD gives best performance there.
- Strength: Fine-tuning on CUB-200 and MIT Scenes shows practical transfer-learning utility, narrowing ResNet-18 vs ResNet-34 gap.
- Weakness: ImageNet validation is preliminary—limited tuning and incomplete layer-wise losses undermine large-scale claims.
- Weakness: Claim that AT is first successful ImageNet transfer is unsupported due to inconclusive KD comparison and incomplete KD training.
- Weakness: Gradient-based AT evaluated only on simplified setups (NIN without BN or cropping), leaving applicability to modern architectures uncertain.
- Requested clarifications: why sum-based outperforms max-based, effects of l2-normalization on training, motivation and scaling for β hyperparameter.
- Requested action: complete ImageNet experiments (apply losses to all groups, tune β) and provide a properly tuned KD baseline for fair comparison.
