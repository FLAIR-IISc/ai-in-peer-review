This paper proposes a method for transferring knowledge from a teacher to a student network via attention transfer (AT). The method works by minimizing the L2 distance between the teacher's and student's attention maps at various layers. This attention transfer loss is added to the standard classification loss and an optional knowledge distillation term. The authors define several types of attention maps: activation-based maps are generated from the sum or max of absolute feature values raised to a power p, while gradient-based maps are derived from the derivative of the loss with respect to the input features. The approach is evaluated on CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, a scene classification dataset, and ImageNet. The results demonstrate that attention transfer consistently improves the performance of the student network compared to a baseline trained without AT. However, a key finding is that even with attention transfer, the student network's performance remains below that of the teacher network.

**Questions and Remarks:**

*   In Section 3, the authors claim that networks with higher accuracy exhibit a higher spatial correlation between the object and the corresponding attention map. While Figure 4 provides compelling qualitative evidence for this, it would be beneficial to include quantitative results to substantiate this claim.
*   The paper does not detail the process for selecting hyperparameter values. An ablation study showing the impact of the AT loss weight, $\beta$, would strengthen the paper by providing insight into the method's sensitivity.
*   In Figure 7(b), reporting the teacher's training and validation loss curves alongside the student's would provide a more complete and informative comparison.
*   The experiments do not clearly delineate the pros and cons of the different proposed attention maps (e.g., activation-based vs. gradient-based). Guidance on which type of attention is preferable under different circumstances would be a valuable addition.
*   While AT does not enable the student to surpass the teacher, the student networks are smaller and have fewer parameters. It would be valuable to characterize the corresponding computational speed-up. Furthermore, it would be insightful to see the results of an experiment where the student and teacher have identical architectures. This would help clarify whether the benefit of AT is purely for model compression or if it also offers a more general regularization benefit.

**Summary:**

**Pros:**
*   The paper is clearly written and the motivation for the work is well-explained.
*   The proposed Attention Transfer method consistently improves the performance of student networks compared to training them in isolation.

**Cons:**
*   Even with attention transfer, the student models underperform compared to their teacher counterparts.
*   The paper lacks a clear analysis or guidance on when to use each proposed type of attention map.
*   The novelty of the method appears somewhat incremental when compared to prior work like FitNets.
