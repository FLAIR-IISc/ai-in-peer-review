### Review

**1. Summary**

This paper introduces a novel knowledge transfer technique called "Attention Transfer" (AT). The core idea is to improve a smaller "student" network's performance by training it to mimic the "attention maps" of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The authors define two types of attention maps. The primary method, activation-based attention, computes a spatial map by applying a summary statistic (e.g., sum of squared values) across the channel dimension of a convolutional feature map. The secondary method, gradient-based attention, uses the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image as a sensitivity map. The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN), demonstrating that the proposed activation-based transfer method consistently improves student network accuracy. Notably, the method is shown to outperform a FitNet-style baseline that transfers full activation tensors and is presented as the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on the ImageNet dataset.

**2. Decision**

I recommend a **Weak Accept**. The paper introduces a simple and effective knowledge distillation method that yields strong empirical results. However, the presentation is weakened by potentially misleading terminology and a lack of focus in its narrative.

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Positive:** The proposed activation-based transfer method is empirically strong. It consistently improves performance across various datasets and architectures and outperforms a relevant baseline (transferring full activations). The successful application to ImageNet is a significant result.
2.  **Negative:** The paper's central claims are undermined by two main presentation issues. First, the use of the term "attention" is confusing, as the method does not employ a conventional attention mechanism but rather distills a post-hoc summary statistic of activations. Second, the inclusion of gradient-based attention transfer feels disconnected from the primary contribution, creating a disjointed narrative.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The main strength of this paper lies in its empirical results. The authors provide compelling evidence that forcing a student network to mimic the summary statistics of a teacher's feature maps is an effective form of regularization and knowledge transfer. The comparison in Table 1, where Attention Transfer (AT) significantly outperforms full-activation transfer (F-ActT), is a powerful argument for the proposed approach. The fact that distilling this compact representation is more beneficial than distilling the full feature map is an interesting finding. Furthermore, the successful results on large-scale datasets like ImageNet, where other distillation methods have reportedly struggled, is a noteworthy contribution.

However, the paper suffers from clarity and organizational issues that detract from its impact.

*   **Misleading Terminology:** The term "attention" typically refers to mechanisms, often learnable, that re-weight features to focus on salient parts of an input (e.g., Bahdanau et al., 2014; Xu et al., 2015). The proposed method, however, calculates a fixed, post-hoc spatial summary of activations (e.g., the sum of squares across channels). While this map may correlate with what the network "focuses on," framing it as "attention" creates confusion with a large and distinct body of literature. A more precise description would be "feature map statistic distillation" or similar. This is not merely a semantic issue; it affects the paper's positioning and could mislead future work.

*   **Disjointed Narrative:** The paper introduces two distinct methods: activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer. However, these two ideas are never compared directly under the same conditions, nor are they integrated. The gradient-based experiments are confined to a specific setup on CIFAR-10 (NIN without batch norm) and feel like a separate, less-developed project appended to the main paper. This distracts from the core contribution of activation-based transfer, which is thoroughly evaluated and well-supported. The paper would be stronger and more focused if it centered exclusively on the activation-based method.

*   **Unclear Notation:** In Equation (2), the norm used for the loss calculation could be specified more clearly. While `Q` is defined as a *vectorized* map, making the `‖.‖2` norm likely the standard Euclidean norm, the notation can be ambiguous. Explicitly stating this would prevent any potential confusion with matrix norms (e.g., Frobenius or induced 2-norm).

**4. Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   To improve the paper's focus, I would suggest reframing the work to concentrate solely on the activation-based transfer method. The gradient-based method could be moved to an appendix or reserved for a future publication, which would allow for a more cohesive and impactful main paper.
*   I strongly encourage the authors to reconsider the "attention" terminology. Using a more descriptive name would improve clarity and accurately position the work within the knowledge distillation literature.
*   The proposed method seems inherently tied to the spatial structure of convolutional feature maps. Could you comment on its applicability to other architectures, such as Vision Transformers, where the notion of a spatial activation map is less direct?

Questions to clarify my understanding:
1.  In Equation (2), `Q` is the vectorized form of a 2D spatial map. Can you please confirm that `‖Q‖2` denotes the standard L2 (Euclidean) norm of this vector? A brief clarification in the text would be helpful.
2.  Why was the evaluation of the gradient-based method limited to a NIN architecture without batch normalization? You mention that it is unclear how batch norm should behave during the second backpropagation step. Could you elaborate on this challenge? Understanding this limitation would provide valuable context.
