- Proposes a novel knowledge transfer method that trains a compact student by mimicking teacher spatial attention maps rather than feature activations or logits.  
- The idea of teaching the student "where to look" via attention maps is intuitive and offers a high-level, semantic form of supervision.  
- Attention maps may be a more flexible and potent supervisory signal than low-level feature matching.  
- Empirical gains are marginal over standard knowledge distillation, raising questions about practical benefit given added complexity.  
- The ImageNet evaluation is preliminary and insufficient to demonstrate scalability and real-world effectiveness.  
- Needs more extensive large-scale experiments: varied teacher–student pairs, full training runs, and comparisons to a wider set of state-of-the-art methods.  
- Authors should clarify scenarios where attention transfer clearly outperforms alternatives.  
- Recommend adding qualitative analyses (e.g., visualizing teacher vs. student attention maps) to show successful transfer.  
- Current recommendation: interesting idea but reject unless Section 4.2.2 is substantially extended; reviewer may reconsider with stronger ImageNet experiments and analyses.
