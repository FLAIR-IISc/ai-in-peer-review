This paper proposes a method for transferring attention between a teacher and a student network, termed Attention Transfer (AT). The method minimizes the L2 distance between the teacher's and student's attention maps at various layers. This attention transfer loss is combined with the standard classification loss and an optional knowledge distillation term. The authors define several types of attention maps based on activations (e.g., the sum or max of absolute feature values raised to a power p), as well as a gradient-based attention map (the derivative of the loss with respect to the input). The method is evaluated on several datasets (CIFAR, CUB-200, Stanford Scene, ImageNet), where it is shown to consistently improve the performance of the student network. However, even with attention transfer, the student network's performance remains below that of the teacher.

**Remarks and Questions:**

1.  In Section 3, the authors claim that networks with higher accuracy exhibit a stronger spatial correlation between the object and the attention map. While Figure 4 provides a compelling visual, it would be beneficial to support this claim with quantitative results.

2.  The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of how hyperparameter values were chosen. Specifically, an analysis of the impact of the attention transfer loss weight, $\beta$, would be valuable.

3.  In Figure 7(b), it would be helpful to also report the training and validation loss of the teacher network for a more complete comparison.

4.  The experimental results do not clearly delineate the pros and cons of the different proposed attention maps. It would be useful to understand under what circumstances one type of attention map is preferable to another.

5.  While AT does not lead to a student model that outperforms the teacher, the student networks are smaller and have fewer parameters. It would be interesting to see this trade-off characterized by reporting the corresponding computational speed-up. A related question is whether there is any benefit to attention transfer when the student and teacher have the same architecture. This experiment could help isolate the effect of AT from the effect of model compression.

**Summary:**

**Pros:**
*   The paper is clearly written and the motivation for the work is well-explained.
*   The proposed method consistently improves the performance of the student network over a baseline trained without attention transfer.

**Cons:**
*   The student models do not achieve the performance of the teacher models, even with attention transfer.
*   The paper lacks guidance on which type of attention map is most suitable for a given scenario.
*   The novelty of the proposed method appears somewhat incremental when compared to prior work like FitNets.
