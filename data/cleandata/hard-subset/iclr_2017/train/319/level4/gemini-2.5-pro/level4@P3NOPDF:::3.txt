This paper proposes a method for knowledge distillation called Attention Transfer (AT), where a smaller "student" network is trained to mimic the attention maps of a larger, pre-trained "teacher" network. The core idea is to use attention as a way for the teacher to provide hints, guiding the student on where to focus in an image. This is implemented by adding a loss term that minimizes the L2 distance between the teacher's and student's attention maps at various layers. The authors define several types of attention maps, including activation-based maps (derived from the sum or max of absolute feature activations) and a gradient-based map (derived from the derivative of the loss with respect to the input). The method is evaluated on several image classification benchmarks, including CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, Stanford Scene, and ImageNet. The results consistently show that Attention Transfer improves the performance of the student network compared to training it from scratch, though the student models do not surpass the performance of their teachers.

**Strengths**

*   The paper is clearly written and the proposed method is well-motivated. The intuition that a student network can learn more effectively by being guided on "where to look" by a more powerful teacher is compelling and easy to follow.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a consistent and significant improvement in the performance of student networks across a variety of datasets and network architectures. This suggests that Attention Transfer is a generally applicable and effective technique for knowledge distillation.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

*   **Quantitative support for claims:** In Section 3, the authors claim that networks with higher accuracy have attention maps that show a higher spatial correlation with the object of interest. While the examples in Figure 4 are visually persuasive, this central claim would be substantially strengthened by quantitative evidence. For example, reporting metrics like IoU between attention maps and ground-truth object bounding boxes for models of varying accuracy would provide more rigorous support.
*   **Hyperparameter analysis:** The paper does not include an analysis of its key hyperparameters. In particular, the impact of the weighting factor β, which balances the AT loss against the standard classification loss, is not explored. An ablation study showing how performance varies with different values of β would provide valuable insight into the method's sensitivity and offer practical guidance for tuning.
*   **Clarity of experimental comparison:** In Figure 7(b), the training and validation loss curves for the student are shown. Including the corresponding curves for the teacher network would provide a crucial baseline, making it easier to interpret the student's learning dynamics and the effect of the AT loss.
*   **Guidance on attention map selection:** The paper introduces several different formulations for attention maps but provides little discussion on their respective pros and cons. The experiments do not offer a clear conclusion on which type of attention is most effective or under what circumstances one should be preferred over others. A more thorough analysis of the differences between activation-based and gradient-based attention would be a valuable addition.
*   **Practical utility and scope:** A key limitation highlighted by the results is that the student network, even with AT, always performs worse than the teacher. While this is expected when compressing a model, the practical trade-off is not characterized. It would be beneficial to quantify the computational savings (e.g., reduction in parameters, FLOPs, or inference speed-up) achieved by the student models to better contextualize the performance drop. Furthermore, it is unclear if AT offers benefits beyond model compression. An important control experiment would be to apply AT where the student and teacher have the identical architecture. This would help determine if the method acts as a general-purpose regularizer or if its benefits are specific to the teacher-student compression paradigm.
*   **Novelty:** The proposed method is conceptually similar to previous hint-based training methods like FitNets, which also transfer knowledge from intermediate layers. The novelty of this work lies in using *attention* as the medium for hints. The paper could be improved by more clearly positioning itself relative to FitNets and discussing the specific advantages of transferring spatial attention maps over transferring the full feature maps.

**Summary**

Overall, this paper presents an interesting and intuitive method for knowledge distillation. The strengths lie in the clarity of the presentation and the consistent performance improvements demonstrated in the experiments. However, the paper could be significantly improved by providing more quantitative backing for its claims, conducting a sensitivity analysis of key hyperparameters, offering a clearer comparison between the different types of attention proposed, and better characterizing the practical utility and scope of the method, particularly in relation to prior work like FitNets.
