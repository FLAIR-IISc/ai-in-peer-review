This paper introduces a novel knowledge transfer mechanism, termed Attention Transfer (AT), designed to improve the performance of a compact "student" network by leveraging guidance from a more powerful "teacher" network. The central idea is to compel the student network to mimic the spatial attention maps of the teacher. The authors' contributions are multi-fold: they formalize attention transfer as a new form of knowledge distillation; they propose and define two distinct types of spatial attention maps, one based on activations and the other on gradients; and they conduct an extensive set of experiments across diverse datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and network architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN). These experiments demonstrate that the proposed AT method consistently improves student model performance and can be effectively combined with existing knowledge distillation techniques.

**Decision: Accept**

My recommendation is to accept this paper. The primary reason is the compelling and consistent empirical evidence showing that Attention Transfer is a practical and effective method for model compression and knowledge transfer. The improvements are demonstrated across a variety of architectures and datasets, including a notable success on the large-scale ImageNet benchmark. My main reservation is that the conceptual novelty over prior hint-based knowledge transfer methods could be more strongly articulated.

**Supporting Arguments**

The key strength of this work lies in its thorough empirical validation. The authors convincingly show that AT helps close the performance gap between smaller student models and larger teacher models in various settings. For instance, the 1.1% top-1 accuracy gain for a ResNet-18 on ImageNet is a significant result, especially given the authors' note that other distillation methods have struggled on this benchmark. The experiment comparing AT to transferring full activations (F-ActT) is particularly insightful, as it supports the central claim that attention maps represent a more distilled and potent form of transferable knowledge than the raw feature maps themselves.

However, while the student models improve, they do not surpass their teachers. This is expected in a student-teacher framework, but the paper would benefit from a more explicit characterization of the efficiency gains (e.g., reduction in parameters, FLOPs, or inference time) to better frame the trade-off between performance and model size. Additionally, the method's core mechanism—minimizing an L2 distance between intermediate representations—is conceptually similar to hint-based methods like FitNets. While the paper successfully demonstrates that attention maps are a superior form of "hint," a clearer discussion of the conceptual advance would strengthen the paper's positioning.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions aimed at further strengthening the manuscript and are not critical to my decision:
*   **Guidance on Attention Map Selection:** The paper introduces several activation-based maps (sum-based, max-based) and a gradient-based one. The experiments provide some comparison, but a more in-depth discussion on the relative advantages and disadvantages of each would be highly beneficial for practitioners. For example, providing intuition on when a gradient-based map might be preferable to an activation-based one would enhance the paper's practical impact.
*   **Comparative Visualizations:** In Figure 7, including the teacher network's training and validation curves would provide a clearer visual reference for the performance gap that Attention Transfer helps to bridge.

**Questions for Authors**

I have a few questions that would help clarify my understanding and increase my confidence in the assessment:
*   You motivate the work in Section 3.1 with the claim that higher-accuracy networks exhibit a stronger spatial correlation between their attention maps and the objects of interest. Figure 4 provides a strong qualitative example. Have you explored any quantitative analysis to validate this motivating hypothesis?
*   Could you elaborate on the process for selecting the hyperparameter $\beta$? The results appear robust, but an ablation study on the sensitivity of the final performance to the choice of $\beta$ would be informative.
*   The experiments are framed in the context of a larger teacher and a smaller student. Have you investigated the effect of Attention Transfer when the student and teacher have identical architectures? I wonder if AT could serve as a regularization technique, where a network learns from a better-trained version of itself or an ensemble of its peers.
