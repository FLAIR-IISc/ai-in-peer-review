### Review

#### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel and intuitive method for knowledge transfer between neural networks, termed "Attention Transfer" (AT). The core idea is to improve a smaller "student" network's performance by training it to mimic the spatial attention maps of a more powerful "teacher" network. The authors propose two distinct ways to define these attention maps: (1) activation-based maps, which are derived by summarizing activation statistics (e.g., sum of absolute values powered by p) across the channel dimension of a convolutional layer, and (2) gradient-based maps, which capture the sensitivity of the network's output to changes in the input image. The efficacy of this approach is demonstrated through a comprehensive set of experiments across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet) and various architectures, including Network-in-Network and modern residual networks (ResNet, WRN). Key findings indicate that Attention Transfer consistently improves student network accuracy, can be effectively combined with existing knowledge distillation techniques, and appears to be more effective than transferring full, un-processed activation tensors.

#### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Weak Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Novelty and Intuition:** The central contribution—using spatial attention maps as the medium for knowledge transfer—is a compelling and novel idea. It moves beyond transferring raw feature maps (as in FitNets) or output distributions (as in Knowledge Distillation) and instead proposes to transfer a more distilled form of information about *where* a powerful model focuses its computational resources. This is a well-motivated and promising direction for research in model compression and knowledge transfer.
2.  **Incomplete Large-Scale Validation:** While the results on CIFAR and other smaller datasets are strong and encouraging, the experimental validation on ImageNet feels preliminary. The authors report a promising 1.1% top-1 accuracy gain but explicitly state that hyperparameters were not tuned and that transfer losses were not applied to all possible layers. Furthermore, the comparison to Knowledge Distillation on ImageNet is inconclusive, undermining the claim that AT is the first successful knowledge transfer method on this scale.

#### Supporting Arguments

**Strengths:**
*   The concept of transferring attention is well-grounded. The paper provides a clear motivation, connecting artificial attention to human visual perception and positioning the work effectively within the existing literature on knowledge distillation and network visualization.
*   The paper thoroughly explores the idea of activation-based attention, proposing several mapping functions (`F_sum^p`, `F_max^p`) and empirically demonstrating that sum-based functions perform better (Table 2).
*   The results on CIFAR-10 are particularly strong. Table 1 clearly shows that AT provides a significant boost over the baseline and that combining it with Knowledge Distillation (AT+KD) yields the best performance. The direct comparison against transferring full activations (F-ActT) provides convincing evidence that the proposed attention maps are a more effective carrier of transferable knowledge.
*   The fine-tuning experiments on CUB-200 and MIT Scenes demonstrate the practical utility of AT in a transfer learning setting, effectively closing the performance gap between a ResNet-18 student and a ResNet-34 teacher.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The primary weakness lies in the ImageNet experiments presented in Section 4.2.2. The authors acknowledge that the experiment was not exhaustive ("we did not have time to tune any hyperparameters," "plan to update the paper with losses on all 4 groups"). While the reported improvement is positive, the preliminary nature of this result makes it difficult to assess the true potential and robustness of the method on large-scale tasks.
*   The paper's claim that AT is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is not sufficiently supported. The authors dismiss Knowledge Distillation (KD) on ImageNet based on their own experiment where it "hurts convergence" and was not trained to completion. This is insufficient evidence to claim superiority, as the failure could be due to suboptimal hyperparameters or implementation details rather than a fundamental flaw in KD for large-scale tasks. A more rigorous and complete comparison is needed to substantiate such a strong claim.
*   The exploration of gradient-based attention transfer is limited. The experiments were conducted on a simplified setup (NIN without batch normalization or random cropping on CIFAR-10). While the results are interesting, particularly the effectiveness of the symmetry norm, the method's applicability to modern, state-of-the-art architectures remains an open question.

#### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   **Further Analysis of Attention Maps:** In Table 2, sum-based attention maps outperform max-based ones. Could you provide more intuition on why this might be the case? Does this suggest that transferring information about the *density* of multiple feature activations at a spatial location is more valuable than transferring information about the single *strongest* feature activation?
*   **Importance of Normalization:** You mention that l2-normalizing the vectorized attention maps before computing the loss is "important for the success of the student training." This is a crucial detail. Could you elaborate on what happens without this normalization? Does training become unstable, converge slower, or lead to a significantly worse final accuracy? An ablation study on this point would strengthen the paper.
*   **Hyperparameter β:** The description for setting the loss weight `β` is "we set it to 10^3 divided by number of elements in attention map and batch size for each layer." This heuristic is a bit opaque. Could you clarify the motivation for this formulation? Does it serve to balance the magnitude of gradients from the attention loss and the primary cross-entropy loss?
*   **Completing the ImageNet Experiment:** I strongly encourage you to complete the ImageNet experiments for the final version of the paper. Adding the transfer losses to the remaining groups and performing at least a light hyperparameter search for `β` would significantly strengthen the paper's main claims. A fair comparison with a properly tuned Knowledge Distillation baseline on ImageNet would be invaluable for the community.
