- Paper proposes Attention Transfer (AT), a knowledge transfer method that trains a compact student to mimic spatial attention maps of a larger teacher.
- Contributions: formalizes AT as a knowledge distillation variant, defines activation- and gradient-based spatial attention maps, and provides extensive experiments across datasets and architectures.
- Empirical claim: AT consistently improves student performance and complements existing distillation methods.
- Decision: Accept — recommendation based on compelling and consistent empirical evidence.
- Key empirical strength: thorough validation across CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet, NIN, ResNet, and WRN, including a notable 1.1% top-1 ImageNet gain.
- Insightful experiment: comparing AT to full-activation transfer shows attention maps are a more effective, compact form of transferable knowledge.
- Limitation: Students improve but do not surpass teachers; paper lacks explicit efficiency metrics (parameters, FLOPs, inference time) to quantify trade-offs.
- Conceptual concern: core mechanism (minimizing L2 between representations) resembles hint-based methods (e.g., FitNets); novelty over prior work could be more clearly argued.
- Suggestion: provide deeper guidance on selecting among activation-based (sum, max) and gradient-based attention maps, with practical trade-offs.
- Suggestion: include teacher training/validation curves in visualizations (e.g., Fig. 7) for clearer performance-gap context.
- Question: have you performed quantitative analysis validating the claimed correlation between higher-accuracy networks’ attention maps and object locations?
- Question: please clarify hyperparameter β selection and present ablation on sensitivity to β.
- Question: have you tried AT when student and teacher share the same architecture to assess AT as a regularizer or peer-ensemble teacher?
