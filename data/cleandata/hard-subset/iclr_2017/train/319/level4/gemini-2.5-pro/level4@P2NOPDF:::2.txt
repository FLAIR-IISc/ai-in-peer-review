This paper presents a novel knowledge distillation framework, termed Attention Transfer (AT), designed to transfer knowledge from a teacher to a student network. The core idea is to encourage the student to mimic the teacher's spatial "attention maps," which are defined as summary statistics (e.g., the sum of squared activations) computed from feature maps. By minimizing the discrepancy between the teacher's and student's attention maps, the student learns to focus on similar spatial regions. The authors provide strong empirical evidence showing that the proposed method outperforms hint-based distillation baselines like FitNet across several computer vision datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
*   The claims are supported by extensive empirical evaluations on a variety of computer vision tasks and datasets, including CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet. The consistent outperformance over the FitNet baseline is compelling.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Misleading Terminology:** The use of the term "attention" throughout the paper is potentially misleading. In modern deep learning literature, attention typically refers to mechanisms that re-weight or select features dynamically. The proposed method, however, does not involve such a mechanism. Instead, it distills knowledge by matching summary statistics of activation maps (e.g., the sum of squares or the L_p-norm). While these maps may implicitly encode where the network is "focusing," labeling this as "attention transfer" overstates the connection to established attention mechanisms and could cause confusion. A more precise term like "activation map statistic transfer" would better describe the method.

*   **Clarity of Notation in Eq. (2):** There is an ambiguity in Equation (2) regarding the computation of the induced 2-norm of the spatial attention map Q, where Q is a matrix in R^{H x W}. The induced 2-norm of a matrix corresponds to its largest singular value, which can be computationally expensive to compute and differentiate through during training. It seems more likely that the authors intended to use the Frobenius norm, which is computationally simpler and more common in such contexts. Clarification on this point is necessary.

*   **Disconnected Gradient-Based Method:** The paper introduces a second, gradient-based attention transfer method that feels disconnected from the main activation-based approach. This gradient-based technique is never directly compared against the activation-based method, nor are they used in conjunction. Its inclusion feels tangential and disrupts the paper's narrative focus. Without a clear connection or comparative analysis, this section seems to add complexity without providing significant value to the core contribution.

*   **Limited Applicability:** The proposed method, by its reliance on spatial feature maps, appears to be primarily applicable to convolutional architectures. It is unclear how this approach would generalize to other architectures like Transformers or MLPs that lack a direct spatial grid structure in their hidden representations. The authors should acknowledge this limitation or discuss potential adaptations for non-convolutional models.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper introduces a simple yet effective technique for knowledge distillation that demonstrates strong empirical performance. The core idea of matching spatial activation statistics is intuitive and well-validated by the experiments. However, the paper's overall quality is hampered by several key issues. The central weakness is the misleading use of "attention" to describe the proposed mechanism. Furthermore, the paper suffers from unclear notation in its core equations and a disjointed presentation of a secondary, gradient-based method. While the empirical results are promising, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing these points to improve clarity, precision, and narrative cohesion.
