### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel and intuitive method for knowledge transfer between neural networks, termed "Attention Transfer" (AT). The core idea is to train a "student" network to mimic the spatial attention maps of a more powerful "teacher" network. The authors propose two distinct methods for defining these attention maps: (1) activation-based maps, which summarize the magnitude of activations across channels at each spatial location, and (2) gradient-based maps, which represent the sensitivity of the network's output to its input. Through a comprehensive set of experiments on CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, the authors demonstrate that this technique consistently improves the performance of the student network. They show that AT can be effectively combined with existing methods like Knowledge Distillation (KD) and that it is more effective than transferring entire activation tensors, as done in methods like FitNets.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Weak Accept

This is a well-written paper with a clear and compelling core idea. The primary reasons for this recommendation are:

1.  **Novelty and Motivation:** The proposal to use spatial attention as the medium for knowledge transfer is novel, intuitive, and well-motivated. It provides a more focused and potentially more efficient form of guidance for the student network compared to transferring entire high-dimensional feature maps.
2.  **Preliminary but Incomplete Evidence:** While the experiments on smaller datasets (CIFAR) and in the transfer learning setting (CUB, Scenes) are convincing, the key experiment on ImageNet feels preliminary. The authors themselves note that it was not fully tuned, which weakens the claim of the method's effectiveness on large-scale problems.

I would be willing to raise my score to a full "Accept" if the authors can provide more complete results for the ImageNet experiment, as this would substantially strengthen the paper's claims.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The main strength of the paper is its central idea. Forcing a student network to focus on the same spatial regions as a powerful teacher is a very sensible approach to distillation. The paper does an excellent job of formalizing this idea into two concrete methods (activation-based and gradient-based) and designing a loss function to implement it. The empirical results largely support the value of this idea.

*   **Activation-based vs. Full-activation Transfer:** The comparison in Table 1 against "F-ActT" (FitNets-style transfer) is particularly strong. The finding that transferring a summarized 2D attention map yields significantly better results than transferring the full activation tensor is an important contribution. It suggests that *what* to focus on is more valuable information to transfer than the raw feature representations themselves.

*   **CIFAR Experiments:** The results on CIFAR are consistently positive. Attention Transfer provides a clear boost over the baseline student network and also outperforms Knowledge Distillation (KD) when used in isolation. The combination of AT+KD yields the best results, demonstrating that the two methods are complementary.

*   **Weakness in ImageNet Experiment:** The primary weakness lies in Section 4.2.2. The reported 1.1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18 is promising. However, the authors state they "did not have time to tune any hyperparameters" and only applied the transfer loss to the last two residual groups. This makes the result feel more like a proof-of-concept than a conclusive demonstration. Given the claim that this is potentially the "first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," the supporting evidence needs to be more robust. Training with losses on all four groups and some minimal hyperparameter tuning would make the results much more convincing.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors**

The following points are meant to help improve the paper and are not necessarily part of the decision assessment. Addressing the questions would help clarify my understanding and strengthen my confidence in the work.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Gradient-based AT:** The section on gradient-based attention transfer is interesting but feels underdeveloped compared to the activation-based section. The experiments are limited to a non-standard NIN setup on CIFAR due to complexities with batch normalization. While understandable, it would strengthen the paper to either expand on this section with more analysis or to frame it more as preliminary work and focus the main narrative on the more thoroughly-tested activation-based approach.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions that the loss weight `β` is set based on the size of the attention map. It would be helpful to include a brief analysis or discussion on the sensitivity of the model to this hyperparameter. Does performance vary significantly with different values of `β`?
*   **Clarity on `p` in Attention Maps:** In Table 2, you explore different values of `p` for `F_psum` and `F_pmax`. The text settles on `F_2sum` for simplicity. It would be beneficial to add a sentence or two of intuition about why sum-based functions outperform max-based ones, and why `p=2` might be a good default choice.

**Questions for Authors:**

1.  **Expanded ImageNet Results:** As my main reservation is the preliminary nature of the ImageNet experiment, my most important question is: have you been able to complete the planned experiment of applying attention transfer losses to all four residual groups for the ResNet-18/ResNet-34 pair? If so, could you share the updated results? This would be the most critical piece of additional evidence to strengthen the paper.
2.  **Challenges with Gradient-based AT:** Could you elaborate on the specific technical challenges of implementing the second backpropagation step through layers with batch normalization? Is the primary difficulty related to how batch statistics are computed and used, or something else?
3.  **Why Attention Maps over Activations?:** The result that attention maps are better for transfer than full activations is very interesting. Do you have a deeper intuition for why this is the case? Is it because the spatial summary acts as a form of regularization, forcing the student to learn its own feature representations while still matching the teacher's focus?
