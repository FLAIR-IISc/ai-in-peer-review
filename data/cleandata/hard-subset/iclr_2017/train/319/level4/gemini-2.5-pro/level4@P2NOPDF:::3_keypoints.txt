- Introduces Attention Transfer (AT): train a student to mimic teacher attention maps via an L2 attention loss combined with classification (and optional distillation) loss.  
- Proposes multiple attention map definitions: activation-based (sum/max of absolute activations^p) and gradient-based (loss derivative w.r.t. inputs).  
- Evaluated on CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, and ImageNet; AT consistently improves student performance over training from scratch or distillation alone.  
- Students improve but do not match or exceed teacher accuracy in presented experiments.  
- Strength: clear motivation and well-written presentation explaining attention as guiding where to look.  
- Strength: consistent empirical gains across datasets and architectures, showing general effectiveness.  
- Strength: conceptually novel extension to distillation by transferring attention rather than only logits/features.  
- Weakness: lacks quantification of performance-vs-efficiency trade-offs (e.g., speed-up, parameter/FLOPs reduction).  
- Weakness: limited analysis and guidance on when to choose different attention map types or their relative advantages.  
- Weakness: novelty may be incremental compared to FitNets; needs stronger argument for advantages of attention over raw feature matching.  
- Suggestion: provide quantitative evaluation of attention alignment (e.g., pointing game accuracy or IoU with bounding boxes).  
- Suggestion: include an ablation on AT loss weight β to show hyperparameter sensitivity.  
- Suggestion: analyze computational cost and effectiveness trade-offs between attention types (e.g., gradient- vs activation-based).  
- Suggestion: show teacher training/validation loss curves in figures for direct comparison.  
- Suggestion: test AT when student and teacher share the same architecture to assess regularization vs knowledge transfer effects.
