This paper proposes a novel knowledge distillation method, termed Attention Transfer (AT), designed to improve the training of a student network by leveraging knowledge from a larger, pre-trained teacher network. The core idea is to compel the student's feature maps to mimic the teacher's by minimizing the difference between their "attention maps." These attention maps are derived by summing statistics (such as the absolute value or the square) of the activations across the spatial dimensions of a feature map. The authors conduct experiments on several computer vision datasets, demonstrating that their proposed method outperforms a strong baseline, FitNet.

### Strengths

*   The proposed method is thoroughly evaluated on a range of computer vision datasets, including CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and the STL-10 dataset. The empirical results are promising, consistently showing an improvement over the FitNet distillation baseline.
*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, clearly outlining the problem of knowledge distillation and the motivation for the proposed approach.

### Weaknesses

*   **Misleading Terminology:** The use of the term "attention" is potentially misleading. In contemporary machine learning literature, "attention" typically refers to mechanisms that compute a weighted sum of values, where the weights are dynamically computed based on a query-key-value relationship (e.g., self-attention). The method proposed here, however, defines an "attention map" as a summary statistic (e.g., the sum of squared activations or the L_p-norm) across the spatial dimensions of a feature map. While this map can be interpreted as highlighting "important" spatial regions, it is a much simpler, static transformation of the feature map itself. This choice of terminology could cause confusion and misrepresent the method's novelty relative to established attention mechanisms.

*   **Limited Scope:** The proposed method, which operates on spatial feature maps, seems inherently tied to convolutional architectures. It is unclear how this concept of spatial "attention" would generalize to other architectures, such as Transformers or MLPs, which are becoming increasingly prevalent. A discussion on the potential limitations or extensions to other model families would strengthen the paper.

*   **Disjointed Presentation of Gradient-Based Transfer:** The introduction of gradient-based attention transfer feels disjointed from the main contribution of the paper. This method is presented as an alternative but is never directly compared against the activation-based attention transfer, nor is there an exploration of combining the two approaches. This makes it difficult to assess its relative merit or understand its relationship to the primary method. It reads like a separate idea included in the paper without sufficient integration or comparative analysis, which weakens the paper's focus.

### Questions for the Authors

A significant point of confusion lies in Equation (2), which uses the induced 2-norm (||Q||_2) for the attention maps. For a matrix Q ∈ ℝ^(H×W), the induced 2-norm is its largest singular value, the computation of which can be prohibitively expensive to perform at every training step. Could the authors clarify if this is indeed what they implemented? Or was another matrix norm, such as the more computationally tractable Frobenius norm, used in practice? Clarifying this notational ambiguity is crucial for the reproducibility and proper understanding of the method.

### Overall Assessment

In summary, this paper introduces a simple yet effective technique for knowledge distillation that shows strong empirical performance. However, the work is weakened by several issues. The terminology used ("attention") is potentially confusing and overstates the connection to modern attention mechanisms. The paper's narrative is somewhat fractured by the inclusion of a seemingly disconnected gradient-based method. Finally, a critical lack of clarity regarding the norm used in the loss function (Equation 2) hinders reproducibility. While the core idea is promising and the results are solid, the paper would be significantly improved by addressing these issues of terminology, organization, and notational precision.
