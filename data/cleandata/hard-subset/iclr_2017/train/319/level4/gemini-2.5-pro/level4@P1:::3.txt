### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel knowledge transfer method called "attention transfer" (AT). The core idea is that a smaller "student" network can be trained more effectively by forcing it to mimic the "attention maps" of a more powerful, pre-trained "teacher" network. The authors propose two main ways to define these attention maps: an activation-based approach, which summarizes feature activations across channels at a given layer, and a gradient-based approach, which measures the sensitivity of the output loss with respect to the input. The proposed attention transfer loss, which minimizes the L2 distance between the teacher's and student's normalized attention maps, is added to the standard classification loss. The authors present a comprehensive set of experiments across various datasets (CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN), demonstrating that attention transfer consistently improves the performance of the student network, both on its own and in combination with knowledge distillation.

### Decision: Accept

#### Key Reasons:

1.  The paper presents a well-motivated and intuitive method for knowledge transfer that is simple to implement. The core idea of using attention maps as a vehicle for transferring knowledge is novel and provides a new perspective compared to existing methods that focus on logits (Knowledge Distillation) or full feature maps (FitNets).
2.  The experimental results are thorough and compelling. The method provides consistent performance improvements for the student network across a wide range of tasks and architectures. Notably, the paper demonstrates a successful application of this knowledge transfer technique on the large-scale ImageNet dataset, a setting where other methods have reportedly struggled.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this work lies in its clear motivation and strong empirical backing. The authors convincingly argue that the spatial areas a network "focuses on" contain valuable information that can guide the training of a less powerful model. The proposed methods for defining attention maps are simple and grounded in prior work on network visualization.

The experimental evaluation is extensive. The consistent gains shown in Table 1 for various student/teacher pairings on CIFAR are significant. The paper's finding that attention transfer outperforms transferring full activation maps (a FitNet-style approach) is a key result, suggesting that summarizing intermediate features into attention maps is a more effective form of knowledge transfer than simply regressing on the raw features. The successful application to fine-tuning tasks (CUB, Scenes) and, most impressively, to ImageNet training, validates the general applicability and effectiveness of the proposed method.

While the approach is effective, the conceptual novelty could be seen as incremental when compared to methods like FitNets, which also transfer knowledge from intermediate layers. However, the specific contribution of formulating this transfer in terms of *attention* and demonstrating its superiority to full-feature transfer is a valuable one. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, and the authors have commendably released their code, which will benefit the community.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to further strengthen the paper. They did not factor heavily into my decision, which is based on the current strengths of the work.

**Clarifications and Suggestions:**

*   **Quantitative Correlation of Attention:** In Section 3, you claim that networks with higher accuracy exhibit stronger spatial correlation between their attention maps and the object of interest. While Figure 4 provides a compelling visual illustration of this, the argument would be much stronger if supported by quantitative evidence. Could you compute a metric (e.g., IoU between the attention map and a ground-truth bounding box) to formally demonstrate this correlation?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The loss is balanced by a hyperparameter $\beta$. The paper provides some detail on how it was set, but an analysis of the method's sensitivity to this parameter would be beneficial. A plot showing student performance as a function of different $\beta$ values for a key experiment would help readers understand its importance.
*   **Analysis of Attention Map Types:** The paper proposes several different activation-based attention functions (`F_sum`, `F_max`, with different powers `p`) as well as a gradient-based one. The experiments mostly focus on `F^2_sum`. It would be very helpful to include a more detailed discussion or analysis of the pros and cons of these different definitions. Is there a guiding principle for when one might be preferred over another?
*   **Quantifying Efficiency Gains:** The goal of training a smaller student network is typically to reduce computational cost (e.g., memory, FLOPs, inference time). While the paper shows the student's accuracy improves, it does not explicitly characterize the efficiency trade-off. It would strengthen the paper to report the reduction in parameters and/or inference speed-up of the student model relative to the teacher, alongside the accuracy gap.
*   **Visualizing Teacher Performance:** In Figure 7, it would be helpful to also plot the training and validation curves for the teacher model. This would provide a clearer context for the student's performance and visually show how much of the accuracy gap is closed by the proposed method.
*   **Same-Architecture Transfer:** The experiments briefly touch upon using AT between networks of the same architecture (e.g., WRN-16-2 teacher, WRN-16-1 student). Could you elaborate on the benefits of AT in a scenario where the student and teacher have the *exact same* architecture and depth? Does it primarily act as a regularizer or speed up convergence in this case?
