This paper presents a modified knowledge distillation framework that minimizes the difference between summary statistics of feature maps from the teacher and student networks. The authors empirically demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms a FitNet-style distillation baseline.

**Pros:**
+ The authors evaluated their proposed method on a variety of computer vision datasets.
+ The paper is generally well-written.

**Cons:**
- The proposed method appears to be limited to convolutional architectures.
- The use of the term "attention" is misleading. The proposed method distills summary statistics (e.g., the sum of squared activations or the sum of L_p norms) computed over the spatial dimensions of a feature map, which is distinct from conventional attention mechanisms.
- The gradient-based attention transfer method seems out of place. It is presented as a separate idea but is never directly compared with, or used in conjunction with, the activation-based transfer method. Consequently, it feels like a parallel contribution that is not well-integrated into the main narrative of the paper, and its added value is unclear.
- The computation of the induced 2-norm in Eq. (2) is unclear. For a matrix Q ∈ ℝ^(H × W), the induced 2-norm is its largest singular value. Calculating this during training would be computationally expensive. It is possible the authors meant to use the Frobenius norm instead; clarification on this point is needed.

**Overall:**
In summary, while the proposed distillation method shows strong empirical performance, the paper suffers from some organizational issues and unclear notation.
