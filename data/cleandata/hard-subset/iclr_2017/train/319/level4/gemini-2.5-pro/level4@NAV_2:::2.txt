This paper proposes a novel knowledge transfer mechanism, termed "attention transfer," designed to improve the performance of a smaller "student" convolutional neural network (CNN) by leveraging a more powerful "teacher" network. The core idea is to compel the student network to mimic the spatial attention maps of the teacher. The authors define two types of attention maps: activation-based, which are derived from summary statistics of feature map activations (e.g., sum of absolute values), and gradient-based, which are derived from the gradient of the loss with respect to the input. The paper's primary contribution is demonstrating through extensive experiments that this form of knowledge transfer provides significant and consistent performance improvements across various datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and CNN architectures.

I recommend accepting this paper. The proposed activation-based attention transfer is a simple yet demonstrably effective method for knowledge distillation that achieves strong empirical results, and the paper's findings represent a valuable contribution to the field. However, the presentation could be strengthened by clarifying the terminology and better integrating the different proposed methods.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1.  The primary strength of this work lies in its compelling empirical results. The proposed activation-based attention transfer (AT) consistently outperforms baseline student models and, importantly, shows improvements over a FitNet-style approach that transfers full activation tensors. The success of AT on the large-scale ImageNet dataset is particularly noteworthy, as the authors point out that other knowledge distillation methods have struggled in this setting. This demonstrates the practical utility and effectiveness of transferring summarized spatial information over complete feature maps.

2.  My main reservation stems from the presentation and framing. The use of the term "attention" is potentially misleading. The activation-based method is more accurately described as distilling summary statistics (e.g., the sum of squared activations) across the channel dimension of feature maps. While this serves as an effective proxy for spatial importance, it differs significantly from conventional attention mechanisms (e.g., in Transformers or recurrent models) that learn to re-weight features. This choice of terminology could create confusion.

3.  Furthermore, the gradient-based attention transfer section feels disconnected from the main activation-based contribution. The two approaches are presented in parallel but are never directly compared or combined. This makes the gradient-based method appear as a secondary, less-developed idea that detracts from the paper's central narrative and focus on the highly successful activation-based technique.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors:**

The following points are meant to help improve the manuscript and are not central to my decision.

*   The proposed method appears to be tailored to convolutional architectures where spatial feature maps are explicit. It would be beneficial to include a short discussion on how, or if, these ideas could be extended to other architectures like Vision Transformers, where attention has a different and more explicit definition.
*   In Equation (2), the notation for the loss `‖ QjS / ‖QjS‖2 − QjT / ‖QjT ‖2 ‖p` is slightly ambiguous. Since `Q` is defined as the vectorized form of the spatial map, `‖.‖2` presumably refers to the standard L2 (Euclidean) vector norm. Could you confirm this is the case and not, for example, a matrix norm which would be computationally different? A brief clarification in the text would be helpful.
*   Could you provide more intuition or a sensitivity analysis for the hyperparameter `β`? The paper mentions a rule for setting it, but understanding how sensitive the final performance is to this choice would be valuable for others seeking to replicate or build upon your work.
