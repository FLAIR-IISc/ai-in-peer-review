- Proposes a novel knowledge-transfer method where a compact student mimics a larger teacher via spatial attention maps rather than logits or raw features.
- Departure from traditional distillation: uses attention maps as the medium for transfer.
- Strength: motivation is intuitive and compelling — forcing student to attend to the teacher’s salient image regions provides structured, semantically rich guidance.
- Advantage: attention-based transfer is an elegant alternative to matching raw activations, which can be noisy and less interpretable.
- Main weakness: experimental validation is weak — reported improvements over existing distillation baselines are marginal.
- Practical concern: modest gains question the benefit of the added methodological complexity.
- ImageNet evaluation (Section 4.2.2) is preliminary and lacks depth.
- Needed: more comprehensive experiments (e.g., varied architectures and more extensive baselines/comparisons) to substantiate claims and generalizability.
- Overall: interesting and well-motivated idea but insufficient empirical evidence; reviewer would reconsider if authors provide stronger, extended experimental validation, especially on ImageNet.
