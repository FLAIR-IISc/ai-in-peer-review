- Introduces Attention Transfer (AT) to train a student network to mimic a teacher's intermediate spatial attention maps in addition to final predictions.  
- Proposes two attention-map definitions: activation-based (statistics of conv activations) and gradient-based (loss gradient wrt input).  
- Evaluated on CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet, showing consistent student improvements; notable +1.1% top-1 on ImageNet ResNet-18.  
- AT outperforms transferring full activation tensors (FitNets-style hints), suggesting attention maps are a more effective abstraction.  
- Decision: Weak Accept.  
- Strength: simple, intuitive method with consistent, tangible gains across datasets and architectures, including large-scale ImageNet.  
- Novelty is modest: conceptually incremental relative to FitNets (still using intermediate teacher representations).  
- Lacks deeper analysis of method properties and practical trade-offs (e.g., computational cost vs. accuracy gain).  
- Paper offers multiple activation-based attention functions but gives little guidance on choosing between them; Table 2 comparisons are limited.  
- Central motivation—that better networks have attention maps better correlated with object locations—is supported only qualitatively (Fig. 4), without quantitative analysis.  
- Missing evaluation of practical benefits: no characterization of inference speed-up, FLOPs, or memory reduction for the student vs. teacher.  
- Need ablation on hyperparameter β to show sensitivity and improve reproducibility.  
- Would be informative to test AT when teacher and student share the same architecture to see if AT acts as a regularizer vs. standard KD.  
- Gradient-based AT is less developed: experiments limited to a non-standard CIFAR-10 setting and unclear how it scales to modern architectures.  
- Figure clarity: Fig. 7(b) should include teacher training/validation curves for comparison.  
- Recommend adding a table/discussion quantifying computational benefits (FLOPs, inference time) alongside accuracy trade-offs to boost practical impact.
