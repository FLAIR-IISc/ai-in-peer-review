- Proposes Attention Transfer (AT): minimize L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps added to classification loss (optionally with distillation).
- Defines activation-based (sum/max of feature magnitudes^p) and gradient-based attention maps.
- Evaluated on CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, a scene dataset, and ImageNet; AT consistently improves student performance versus baseline.
- Key empirical finding: students with AT still underperform their teachers.
- Request quantitative validation of the claim that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps (Figure 4 is qualitative).
- Paper lacks hyperparameter selection details; an ablation on AT loss weight β is needed to show sensitivity.
- Suggests plotting teacher training/validation loss alongside student’s in Figure 7(b) for a fuller comparison.
- Experiments do not clearly compare trade-offs between attention-map types; guidance on when to use activation- vs gradient-based maps is missing.
- Recommends reporting computational speed-up for smaller student models and testing AT with identical teacher/student architectures to separate compression vs regularization effects.
- Pros: clear writing, well-explained motivation, and consistent student improvements from AT.
- Cons: students still worse than teachers, no guidance on attention map choice, and perceived incremental novelty relative to prior work (e.g., FitNets).
