This paper proposes a modified knowledge distillation framework that minimizes the difference between summary statistics of teacher and student feature maps. The authors provide empirical results showing that their method outperforms a FitNet-style baseline.

Pros:
+ The method is evaluated on a variety of computer vision datasets.
+ The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

Cons:
- The method's applicability appears limited to convolutional architectures, and its extension to other types of networks is not discussed.
- The use of the term 'attention' is misleading. The method distills summary statistics of activation maps (e.g., the sum of squared activations or the sum of other L_p norms), which does not align with the common definition of an attention mechanism.
- The section on gradient-based attention transfer feels disconnected from the main contribution. This gradient-based method is never directly compared with, nor used in conjunction with, the activation-based transfer, making its value to the overall paper unclear.
- There is an ambiguity in Equation (2) regarding the computation of the induced 2-norm. For a feature map matrix Q of size HxW, the induced 2-norm is its largest singular value, which is computationally expensive to calculate during training. The authors should clarify if this is the intended implementation or if they are instead using a more computationally tractable alternative, such as the Frobenius norm.

Overall, while the proposed distillation method demonstrates strong empirical performance, the paper's presentation is weakened by organizational issues, potentially misleading terminology, and unclear notation.
