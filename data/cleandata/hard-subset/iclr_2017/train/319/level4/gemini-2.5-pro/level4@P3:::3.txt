### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel knowledge transfer technique called "Attention Transfer" (AT), designed to improve the performance of a smaller "student" convolutional neural network (CNN) by leveraging the knowledge from a larger, more powerful "teacher" CNN. The core idea is to compel the student network to mimic the *attention maps* of the teacher. The authors propose two distinct methods for defining these spatial attention maps: 1) activation-based maps, derived from summary statistics (e.g., sum or max of powered absolute values) of feature activations across channels, and 2) gradient-based maps, which represent the sensitivity of the network's output to its input. The paper provides extensive empirical evidence showing that this method consistently improves the student network's performance across a variety of image classification datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet) and modern architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN). Notably, the authors demonstrate that transferring these compressed attention maps can be more effective than transferring the full, dense activation tensors (as in FitNets) and that AT can be successfully combined with traditional knowledge distillation.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Effective Knowledge Transfer Mechanism:** The paper's central proposal—using spatial attention maps as the medium for knowledge transfer—is an intuitive, novel, and well-motivated contribution to the field of knowledge distillation. It shifts the focus from mimicking entire representations to mimicking a more abstract concept of "where the network looks."
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The authors provide convincing and extensive experimental results across multiple datasets, including the challenging ImageNet benchmark. The consistent performance improvements, coupled with ablation studies on different attention map formulations and a direct comparison showing superiority over full-activation transfer, strongly support the paper's claims and demonstrate the method's effectiveness and generality.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this work lies in its elegant conceptual shift and the rigorous experiments that validate it. While knowledge distillation methods using intermediate "hints" exist (e.g., FitNets), this paper redefines the hint not as the full feature map, but as a summary of spatial importance. This is a compelling idea, as it suggests that teaching a student network *what* to focus on is a more efficient and effective strategy than forcing it to replicate the teacher's exact internal computations.

The empirical results are a standout feature. The consistent gains observed on CIFAR-10/100 across various teacher-student pairs are significant. More impressively, the method shows strong performance in transfer learning scenarios (CUB, Scenes) and provides a notable accuracy boost on ImageNet (ResNet-18 student with a ResNet-34 teacher). The authors' claim that this may be the first successful application of an intermediate representation transfer method to ImageNet is a significant result. The finding that AT provides a better final accuracy improvement than full-activation transfer (FitNet-style) lends strong support to the hypothesis that attention maps are a more potent signal for distillation than raw feature tensors.

The paper is also well-written, clearly structured, and the proposed methods are explained with sufficient detail. The inclusion of both activation-based and gradient-based attention definitions broadens the scope and provides alternative approaches to the same core problem.

While the work is strong, there are areas where the analysis could be deepened. The student network, even with Attention Transfer, does not surpass the performance of the teacher model. This is expected, but the practical utility of model compression would be clearer if the performance gains were contextualized with efficiency metrics (e.g., parameter reduction, FLOPs, or inference speed-up). Furthermore, the paper introduces several different formulations for attention maps but provides limited analysis on their relative pros and cons, leaving the choice of which map to use somewhat empirical. Despite these points, the core contribution is solid and the experimental backing is thorough.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions aimed at strengthening the paper further and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Quantitative Analysis of Attention Maps:** In Section 3, the paper makes the qualitative claim that networks with higher accuracy produce attention maps that correlate better with object locations (supported by Figure 4). This is a powerful argument that could be made even stronger with quantitative evidence. If possible, consider computing a metric (e.g., pointing game accuracy or IoU with bounding boxes on a dataset like CUB) to show a numerical correlation between model accuracy and attention map quality.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The loss is balanced by a hyperparameter `β`. A brief analysis or discussion of the sensitivity to this parameter would be beneficial for reproducibility and for practitioners looking to apply this method.
*   **Characterizing Practical Trade-offs:** The primary motivation for using a student network is efficiency. The paper would have a greater practical impact if it included a discussion of the performance-vs-efficiency trade-off. For instance, a table or plot showing Test Error vs. Number of Parameters (or FLOPs/Inference Time) for the baseline student, the AT-improved student, and the teacher would clearly illustrate the practical value of the proposed method.
*   **Visualizations:** In Figure 7, including the teacher's training and validation loss/error curves would provide a useful reference point and better illustrate the performance gap that Attention Transfer helps to close.

### **Questions for the Authors**

*   Could you provide more intuition or prescriptive guidance on choosing between the different attention mapping functions (e.g., `F_sum^p` vs. `F_max^p`, or activation-based vs. gradient-based)? For instance, are certain maps better suited for specific architectures or tasks (e.g., fine-grained vs. general classification)?
*   The experiments show that AT is beneficial when transferring knowledge from a larger teacher to a smaller student. Have you explored the effect of AT when the student and teacher have the same architecture? Does it act as a regularizer in this setting and still provide a performance benefit?
