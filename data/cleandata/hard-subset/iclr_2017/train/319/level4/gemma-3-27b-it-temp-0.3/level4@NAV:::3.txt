This paper explores attention transfer techniques to improve the performance of student networks by aligning their attention maps with those of a pre-trained teacher network. Attention transfer is achieved by minimizing the L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps across layers, alongside standard classification loss and an optional knowledge distillation loss. The authors introduce several novel attention map definitions, including activation-based attentions (utilizing sums or maxima of absolute feature values raised to a power *p*) and a gradient-based attention (derived from the gradient of the loss with respect to the input). 

The proposed methods are evaluated on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet datasets, demonstrating that attention transfer generally leads to improved test performance in student networks. However, the student networks consistently underperform the teacher network, even when employing attention transfer. 

Several points warrant further consideration:
- The authors assert in Section 3 a correlation between network accuracy and spatial correlation between objects and attention maps. While Figure 4 provides visual support, quantitative results would strengthen this claim.
- The rationale behind the chosen hyperparameter values would be valuable; specifically, an analysis of the impact of the $\beta$ parameter would be insightful.
- Including the teacher network’s training and validation loss in Figure 7(b) would provide a more complete comparison.
- The experimental results do not clearly delineate the advantages and disadvantages of the different attention map types.
- While attention transfer does not surpass the teacher’s performance, the student networks benefit from reduced parameter counts. Characterizing the resulting computational speed-up would be beneficial. Furthermore, investigating whether attention transfer provides any advantage when the student and teacher networks share the same architecture would be of interest.

In summary:
Strengths:
- The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and effectively motivated.
- Attention transfer consistently improves student network performance compared to training the student network independently.
Weaknesses:
- Student networks consistently achieve lower performance than the teacher network.
- The optimal choice of attention map for different scenarios remains unclear.
- The novelty of the approach is somewhat incremental compared to existing work such as FitNets.
