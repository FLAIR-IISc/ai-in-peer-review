This paper introduces a novel knowledge distillation framework focused on minimizing the discrepancy in feature map statistics – specifically, the sum of statistics – between teacher and student networks. The authors provide empirical evidence demonstrating performance gains over a standard FitNet distillation baseline.

**Strengths:**
+ The proposed methods were evaluated across a diverse set of computer vision datasets.
+ The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented.

**Weaknesses:**
- The current implementation appears constrained to convolutional neural network architectures.
- The use of “attention” terminology is potentially misleading. The method focuses on distilling aggregate statistics (e.g., summed squared values or Lp norms) of feature map activations, rather than attention mechanisms in the traditional sense.
- The inclusion of gradient-based attention transfer feels somewhat disjointed. This approach is presented independently and not directly compared to, or combined with, the primary “attention-based” transfer method, and its contribution to the overall work is unclear.
- The computation of the induced 2-norm in equation (2) requires clarification. Given that Q is a matrix in ℝ<sup>H x W</sup>, its induced 2-norm corresponds to its largest singular value, which could be computationally prohibitive. The authors should clarify whether the Frobenius norm was intended instead.

**Overall:** The proposed distillation method demonstrates practical effectiveness. However, the paper would benefit from improved organization and greater clarity in its notation and terminology.
