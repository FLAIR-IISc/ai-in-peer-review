This paper proposes investigating attention transfer between a teacher and a student network.

Attention transfer is achieved by reducing the l2 distance between the teacher/student attention maps at different layers, in addition to minimizing classification loss and optionally a knowledge distillation term.

The authors define several activation-based attentions (absolute convenience values sum p increases or values sum p maximize). They also propose a gradient-based attention (derivative of the loss with respect to the input).

They evaluate their approach on several datasets (CIFAR, Cub/Scene, Imagenet), showing that attention transfer helps improve student network test performance. However, student networks perform worst than the teacher, even with attention.

Some comments/questions:
- In Section 3, the authors claim that high-accuracy networks have high spatial correlation between the object and the attention map. While Figure 4 is compelling, it would be good to show that there are also quantitative results.
- It would be good to see how you chose the hyperparameters, what the effect of β is.
- It would be good to report the teacher training and validation loss in Figure 7 b).
- From the experiments, it is not clear what the advantages/disadvantages of the different attention maps are.
- AT does not lead to better results than the teacher. However, student networks have fewer parameters. It would be interesting to identify the corresponding speed-up. If you keep the same architecture between student and teacher, is there any benefit to attention transfer?

In summary:
Pros:
- Clearly written and well-motivated.
- Consistent improvement in student with attention compared to student alone.
Cons:
- Students' performance is worst than the teacher model.
- It is not clear which attention to use in which case?
- Some degree of incremental novelty compared to Fitnet.
