This paper introduces a modified knowledge distillation framework, which aims to minimize the discrepancy in the sum of statistics across feature maps between the teacher and student networks. The authors provide empirical evidence that their proposed method outperforms the FitNet-style distillation baseline.

Strengths:
+ The authors conducted a comprehensive evaluation of their proposed method on a variety of computer vision datasets.
+ The paper is generally well-written and clear in its presentation.

Weaknesses:
- The proposed method appears to be specifically tailored to convolutional architectures, limiting its broader applicability.
- The terminology surrounding attention is misleading, as the method primarily focuses on distilling the sum of squared (or other) statistics of activations in hidden feature maps, rather than true attention mechanisms.
- The inclusion of gradient-based attention transfer seems unnecessary and disjointed from the rest of the paper. This concept is not compared to or combined with the "attention-based" transfer method, which diminishes its value.
- The computation of the induced 2-norm in Eq. (2) is unclear. Given that Q is a matrix in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W}, its induced 2-norm corresponds to its largest singular value. However, calculating this cost function may be computationally expensive. It is possible that the authors intended to use the Frobenius norm instead.

Overall, the proposed distillation method demonstrates promising results in practice. However, the paper suffers from organizational issues and unclear notation, which detract from its overall impact.
