The paper presents a modified knowledge distillation framework that minimizes the difference of the sum of statistics across a feature map between the teacher and the student network. The authors empirically demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the FitNet-style distillation baseline.

The paper has several strengths. Firstly, the authors evaluate the proposed methods on various computer vision datasets, showcasing their effectiveness in different settings. Secondly, the paper is generally well-written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and understand the proposed approach.

However, there are some limitations and issues with the paper. Firstly, the method seems to be limited to convolutional architectures, and it is unclear how it can be extended to other types of neural networks. Secondly, the use of the term "attention" in the paper is misleading. The proposed method does not really capture attention in the classical sense but rather tries to distill the summed squared (or other statistics, e.g., summed lp norm) of activations in a hidden feature map. A more accurate description of the method would be beneficial.

Furthermore, the gradient-based attention transfer seems out-of-place in the paper. The proposed gradient-based methods are never compared directly to nor used jointly with the "attention-based" transfer. It appears to be a parallel idea added to the paper that does not seem to add much value. The authors should either integrate this idea more cohesively into the paper or remove it altogether.

Additionally, the notation in the paper could be improved. Specifically, it is unclear how the induced 2-norms in eq. (2) are computed. Q is a matrix ∈ ℝᴴˣᵂ, whose induced 2-norm is its largest singular value. Computing such a cost function seems computationally expensive. It is possible that the authors really mean the Frobenius norm, which would be a more computationally efficient choice.

Overall, the proposed distillation method works well in practice, but the paper has some organization issues and unclear notation that need to be addressed.

Decision: Accept with minor revisions.

Reasons for decision:

* The paper presents a novel and effective approach to knowledge distillation.
* The authors provide a thorough evaluation of the proposed method on various datasets.

Minor revisions:

* Clarify the notation in eq. (2) and specify whether the induced 2-norm is the largest singular value or the Frobenius norm.
* Remove or integrate the gradient-based attention transfer idea more cohesively into the paper.
* Provide a more accurate description of the proposed method, avoiding the misleading use of the term "attention".

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on how the induced 2-norms in eq. (2) are computed?
* How do you plan to extend the proposed method to other types of neural networks?
* Can you provide more insights into why the gradient-based attention transfer idea was included in the paper, and how it relates to the main contribution of the paper?
