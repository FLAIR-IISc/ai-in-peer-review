The paper presents a modified knowledge distillation framework that minimizes the difference of the sum of statistics across a feature map between the teacher and student network. The authors empirically demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform the FitNet style distillation baseline.

The paper has several strengths. The authors evaluate the proposed methods on various computer vision datasets, showcasing their effectiveness in different scenarios. Additionally, the paper is generally well-written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and understand their proposed approach.

However, there are some limitations and areas for improvement. Firstly, the method seems to be limited to convolutional architectures, which may restrict its applicability to other network types. Furthermore, the use of the term "attention" in the paper is misleading. The proposed method does not truly capture attention but rather distills the summed squared (or other statistics, e.g., summed lp norm) of activations in a hidden feature map.

Another concern is the inclusion of the gradient-based attention transfer method, which appears to be a separate idea that does not add significant value to the paper. The proposed gradient-based methods are not directly compared to nor used jointly with the "attention-based" transfer, making it seem like an afterthought.

Lastly, the notation in the paper could be improved. Specifically, the induced 2-norms in eq. (2) are not clearly defined. Q is a matrix in R^{H x W} whose induced 2-norm is its largest singular value. Computing such a cost function seems computationally expensive. It is possible that the authors actually mean the Frobenius norm, which would be a more computationally efficient choice.

Overall, the proposed distillation method works well in practice, but the paper suffers from some organization issues and unclear notation. Addressing these concerns could strengthen the paper and make it more impactful.

Decision: Reject (with potential for resubmission after revisions)

Reasons for rejection:

1. Limited applicability: The method is currently limited to convolutional architectures, which may restrict its applicability to other network types.
2. Misleading terminology: The use of the term "attention" is misleading, as the proposed method does not truly capture attention.
3. Organization issues: The inclusion of the gradient-based attention transfer method seems like an afterthought and does not add significant value to the paper.

Additional feedback:

* Consider rephrasing the paper to focus on the distillation of feature map statistics rather than attention.
* Provide a clear definition of the induced 2-norms in eq. (2) and consider using the Frobenius norm instead.
* Evaluate the proposed method on non-convolutional architectures to demonstrate its broader applicability.
* Consider removing the gradient-based attention transfer method or integrating it more seamlessly into the paper.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide a clear definition of the induced 2-norms in eq. (2) and explain why you chose this specific norm?
* How do you plan to extend the proposed method to non-convolutional architectures?
* Can you provide more insight into why the gradient-based attention transfer method was included in the paper and how it relates to the main contribution?
