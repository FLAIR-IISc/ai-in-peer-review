The paper proposes a modified knowledge distillation framework that minimizes the difference in the sum of statistics across feature maps between the teacher and student networks. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by empirically showing that it outperforms the FitNet-style distillation baseline.

The paper has several strengths. Firstly, the authors evaluate their proposed method on various computer vision datasets, providing a comprehensive understanding of its performance. Additionally, the paper is generally well-written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments.

However, there are several limitations and areas for improvement. One major concern is that the method appears to be limited to convolutional architectures, which restricts its applicability to other network types. Furthermore, the use of the term "attention" in the paper is misleading, as the proposed method primarily focuses on distilling the sum of squared (or other statistics, such as the sum of Lp norms) of activations in a hidden feature map.

Another issue is the inclusion of gradient-based attention transfer, which seems out of place in the paper. The authors do not directly compare this approach to the "attention-based" transfer nor do they use them jointly, making it unclear what value this addition brings to the paper. It appears to be a parallel idea that was added but not fully integrated into the main narrative.

Additionally, the notation and computation of the induced 2-norm in equation (2) are unclear. Specifically, Q is a matrix in R^{H x W}, and its induced 2-norm is its largest singular value. Computing this cost function seems computationally expensive. It is possible that the authors intended to use the Frobenius norm instead?

In summary, while the proposed distillation method demonstrates promising results in practice, the paper suffers from organizational issues and unclear notation. Addressing these concerns would strengthen the paper and improve its overall clarity.
