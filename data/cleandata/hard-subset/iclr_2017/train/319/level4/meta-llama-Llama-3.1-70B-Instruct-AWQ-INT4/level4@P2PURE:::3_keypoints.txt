- Proposes attention transfer by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps, plus classification loss and optional knowledge distillation term.  
- Defines activation-based attentions (sum of |features|^p and max of features^p) and a gradient-based attention (d loss / d input).  
- Evaluates on CIFAR, CUB, Scene, and ImageNet; attention transfer improves student test performance but students still underperform teachers.  
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between object and attention map (Figure 4) lacks quantitative results.  
- Hyperparameter selection is not detailed, particularly the impact of β; needs analysis of hyperparameter effects.  
- Figure 7b should report the teacher’s training and validation loss for fuller context.  
- Experiments do not clearly delineate pros and cons of different attention maps; requires a comparative analysis of strengths/weaknesses.  
- Notes potential practical benefit from smaller student models (speed-up); recommends characterizing speed-up and testing same-architecture teacher–student scenarios.  
- Paper is clearly written and well-motivated.  
- Results consistently show attention transfer improves student performance relative to student alone.  
- Student networks still perform worse than teacher models (major drawback).  
- It is unclear which attention map is preferable for which tasks or settings.  
- The approach is somewhat incremental in novelty compared to FitNet.
