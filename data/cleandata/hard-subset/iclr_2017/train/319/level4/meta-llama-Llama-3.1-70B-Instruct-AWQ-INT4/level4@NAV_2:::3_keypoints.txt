- Proposes attention transfer by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps across layers, alongside classification loss and optional knowledge distillation.  
- Defines activation-based attentions (sum/max of |features|^p) and a gradient-based attention (∂Loss/∂inputs).  
- Evaluated on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet; attention transfer consistently improves student test performance but students still underperform teachers.  
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks show higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps; Figure 4 supports this but lacks quantitative evidence.  
- Hyperparameter selection (e.g., impact of β) is unclear and should be analyzed.  
- Suggests reporting teacher train and validation loss in Figure 7b for completeness.  
- Experiments do not clearly show pros/cons or guidance for choosing among different attention map types.  
- Attention transfer does not exceed teacher performance; since students have fewer parameters, recommend reporting speed-up and testing same-architecture transfers.  
- Pros: paper is clearly written and well-motivated.  
- Pros: shows consistent improvement of students compared to students trained without attention.  
- Cons: students remain worse than teacher models.  
- Cons: no clear recommendation which attention formulation to use in which scenario.  
- Cons: novelty is somewhat incremental relative to FitNet.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Supporting argument: thorough multi-dataset evaluation demonstrating consistent student gains, but needs quantitative claims and hyperparameter clarification.  
- Additional feedback: include computational cost comparisons to other transfer methods and explore applications to tasks like object detection or weakly-supervised localization.
