This paper proposes to investigate attention transfers between a teacher and a student network. Attention transfer is performed by minimizing the l2 distance between the teacher/student attention maps at different layers, in addition to minimizing the classification loss and optionally a knowledge distillation term.

The authors define several activation-based attentions (sum of absolute feature values raised at the power p or max of values raised at the power p). They also propose a gradient-based attention (derivative of the loss w.r.t. inputs). They evaluate their approaches on several datasets (CIFAR, CUB/Scene, ImageNet) showing that attention transfers do help improve the student network's test performance. However, the student networks perform worse than the teacher, even with attention.

Few remarks/questions:

* In Section 3, the authors claim that networks with higher accuracy have a higher spatial correlation between the object and the attention map. While Figure 4 is compelling, it would be nice to have quantitative results showing that as well.
* How did you choose the hyperparameter values? It would be nice to see what is the impact of β.
* It would be nice to report teacher train and validation loss in Figure 7(b).
* From the experiments, it is not clear what the pros and cons of the different attention maps are.
* AT does not lead to better results than the teacher. However, the student networks have fewer parameters. It would be interesting to characterize the corresponding speed-up. If you keep the same architecture between the student and the teacher, is there any benefit to the attention transfer?

In summary:

Pros:
- Clearly written and well-motivated.
- Consistent improvement of the student with attention compared to the student alone.

Cons:
- Students have worse performances than the teacher models.
- It is not clear which attention to use in which case?
- Somewhat incremental novelty relatively to FitNets.

Decision: Reject.

Reasons for the decision:

* The paper's main contribution is the proposal of attention transfer methods, which is somewhat incremental to the existing knowledge distillation methods.
* The student networks do not outperform the teacher networks, even with attention transfer.
* The paper lacks a clear analysis of the pros and cons of different attention maps and their impact on the student network's performance.

Additional feedback:

* The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of the attention maps and their relationship to the network's performance.
* The authors could explore the application of attention transfer to other tasks, such as object detection or segmentation.
* The paper could be improved by providing more details on the implementation, such as the choice of hyperparameters and the training procedure.
