- Paper proposes attention transfer by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps alongside classification loss (and optional KD term).
- Defines several activation-based attentions (sum of |features|^p, max of features^p) and a gradient-based attention (dL/dinput).
- Evaluates on CIFAR, CUB, Scene, and ImageNet; attention transfer consistently improves student vs. student-alone but students still underperform teachers.
- Figure 4 suggests higher-accuracy nets have higher spatial correlation between object and attention map, but quantitative evidence is missing.
- Hyperparameter selection (especially β) is not described; impact of varying β is not explored.
- Teacher training/validation losses are not reported in Figure 7(b), limiting result interpretation.
- Experiments lack clear analysis of pros and cons for different attention map types.
- No characterization of runtime/parameter speed-up for smaller students; unclear practical trade-offs.
- Unclear whether attention transfer helps when student and teacher share the same architecture.
- Paper is clearly written and well-motivated.
- Authors consistently show student improvements with attention transfer compared to student-only baselines.
- Main weaknesses: students still worse than teachers, unclear guidance on which attention map to use, and novelty is incremental relative to FitNet.
