This paper proposes an investigation into attention transfer between a teacher and a student network, exploring the potential benefits of transferring attention maps from the teacher to the student. The attention transfer is achieved by minimizing the L2 distance between the teacher and student attention maps at different layers, in addition to minimizing the classification loss and optionally a knowledge distillation term.

The authors define several activation-based attention mechanisms, including the sum of absolute feature values raised to the power of p and the maximum of values raised to the power of p. They also propose a gradient-based attention mechanism, which is based on the derivative of the loss with respect to the inputs. These attention mechanisms are evaluated on several datasets, including CIFAR, CUB, Scene, and ImageNet, demonstrating that attention transfer can improve the test performance of the student network.

However, the results also show that the student network performs worse than the teacher network, even with attention transfer. This raises several questions and concerns. Firstly, in Section 3, the authors claim that networks with higher accuracy tend to have a higher spatial correlation between the object and the attention map. While Figure 4 provides some visual evidence for this claim, it would be beneficial to include quantitative results to support this assertion.

Furthermore, it is unclear how the hyperparameter values were chosen, and it would be informative to see the impact of varying the value of β. Additionally, including the teacher's training and validation loss in Figure 7(b) would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the results.

The experiments also do not clearly elucidate the pros and cons of the different attention maps. It is essential to understand the strengths and weaknesses of each attention mechanism to determine when to use each one. Moreover, since attention transfer does not lead to better results than the teacher network, it is crucial to characterize the corresponding speed-up in terms of computational resources. If the same architecture is used for both the student and teacher networks, is there any benefit to using attention transfer?

In summary, the paper has several strengths, including clear writing and a well-motivated concept. The consistent improvement of the student network with attention transfer compared to the student alone is also a notable finding. However, there are some limitations, including the fact that the student networks perform worse than the teacher models. Additionally, it is unclear which attention mechanism to use in which scenario, and the novelty of the approach is somewhat incremental compared to FitNet.
