- Proposes attention transfer by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps, alongside classification loss and optional knowledge distillation.  
- Defines activation-based attention maps (sum or max of |features|^p) and a gradient-based attention map (derivative of loss w.r.t. inputs).  
- Evaluates on CIFAR, CUB, Scene, and ImageNet; attention transfer improves student test performance but students still underperform teachers.  
- Section 3 claim that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps needs quantitative evidence beyond Figure 4.  
- The choice and tuning of hyperparameter β are not discussed; its impact on results is unknown.  
- Suggestion to report the teacher’s training and validation loss (e.g., in Figure 7b) for fuller context.  
- Experiments do not clearly compare pros and cons of different attention map types, leaving practitioners unsure which to use.  
- Although students have fewer parameters, the paper does not quantify speed-up or runtime benefits from model compression.  
- Unclear whether attention transfer provides benefits when student and teacher share the same architecture.  
- Pros: paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and attention consistently improves student performance over baseline.  
- Cons: students remain worse than teachers, attention-map choice is unclear, and novelty is incremental relative to FitNet.
