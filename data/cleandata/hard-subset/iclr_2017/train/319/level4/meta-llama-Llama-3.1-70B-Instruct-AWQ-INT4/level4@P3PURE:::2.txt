The paper presents a modified knowledge distillation framework that minimizes the difference of the sum of statistics across feature maps between the teacher and student networks. The authors empirically demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms the FitNet-style distillation baseline.

The paper has several strengths. The authors evaluate the proposed method on various computer vision datasets, providing a thorough assessment of its performance. Additionally, the paper is generally well-written, making it easy to follow.

However, there are several weaknesses that detract from the paper's overall quality. Firstly, the method appears to be limited to convolutional architectures, which may restrict its applicability to other types of neural networks. Furthermore, the use of the term "attention" in the paper is misleading, as the proposed method simply distills the summed squared (or other statistics, e.g., summed Lp norm) of activations in a hidden feature map.

The inclusion of gradient-based attention transfer in the paper seems out of place. The proposed gradient-based methods are not compared directly to, nor used jointly with, the "attention-based" transfer, which makes it seem like a parallel idea that does not add much value to the paper.

Additionally, the notation in the paper could be improved. Specifically, it is unclear how the induced 2-norm in Equation (2) is computed. Q is a matrix in ℝ^{H × W}, whose induced 2-norm is its largest singular value. Computing such a cost function seems computationally expensive. It is possible that the authors actually mean the Frobenius norm, which would be more computationally efficient.

Overall, while the proposed distillation method appears to work well in practice, the paper suffers from some organization issues and unclear notation, which detract from its overall impact.
