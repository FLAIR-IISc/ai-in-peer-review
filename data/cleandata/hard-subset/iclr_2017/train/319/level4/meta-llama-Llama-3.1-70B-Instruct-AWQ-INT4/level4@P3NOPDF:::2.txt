The paper presents a modified knowledge distillation framework that minimizes the difference of the sum of statistics across feature maps between the teacher and student networks. The authors empirically demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms the FitNet-style distillation baseline.

The paper has several strengths. Firstly, the authors evaluate the proposed method on various computer vision datasets, providing a comprehensive understanding of its performance. Additionally, the paper is generally well-written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments.

However, there are several limitations and areas for improvement. One major concern is that the method appears to be limited to convolutional architectures, which may restrict its applicability to other network types. Furthermore, the use of the term "attention" in the paper is misleading, as the proposed method actually distills the summed squared (or other statistics, e.g., summed Lp norm) of activations in a hidden feature map. This terminology may cause confusion among readers.

Another issue is the inclusion of gradient-based attention transfer, which seems out-of-place in the paper. The proposed gradient-based methods are not directly compared to nor used jointly with the "attention-based" transfer, making it unclear what value they add to the overall framework. It appears to be a parallel idea that was added to the paper without sufficient integration or justification.

Additionally, the notation and computational aspects of the method could be improved. Specifically, it is unclear how the induced 2-norm in equation (2) is computed. Given that Q is a matrix in ℝ^{H × W}, its induced 2-norm is indeed its largest singular value. However, computing this cost function may be computationally expensive. It is possible that the authors actually mean the Frobenius norm, which would be a more efficient and common choice.

Overall, the proposed distillation method demonstrates promising results in practice, but the paper suffers from organization issues and unclear notation. Addressing these concerns could strengthen the paper and provide a clearer understanding of the method's contributions and limitations.
