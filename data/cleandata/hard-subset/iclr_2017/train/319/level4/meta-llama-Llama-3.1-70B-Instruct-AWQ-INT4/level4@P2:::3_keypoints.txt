- Paper proposes attention transfer (AT) by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps alongside classification loss and optional KD term.  
- Defines activation-based attentions (sum or max of feature magnitudes raised to power p) and a gradient-based attention (loss derivative w.r.t. inputs).  
- Evaluated on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet; AT consistently improves student performance but students still underperform teachers.  
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks show greater spatial correlation between object and attention map; Figure 4 is compelling but lacks quantitative support.  
- Hyperparameter selection (notably β) is unclear; impact of β on results should be shown.  
- Figure 7b should report teacher train and validation loss for completeness.  
- Experiments do not clearly delineate pros and cons of the different attention map variants.  
- AT improves smaller student models, but speed-up (parameter/compute tradeoff) from smaller students is not characterized.  
- Unclear if AT provides benefits when teacher and student share the same architecture.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Reasons: novel AT method for knowledge transfer; thorough evaluation across datasets.  
- Supporting: paper is well-written and motivated; experiments show consistent student improvement with AT; provides analysis of attention map variants.  
- Requested additions: quantitative spatial-correlation results, β sensitivity analysis, teacher loss plots, detailed comparison of attention types, speed-up characterization, and experiments with identical architectures.
