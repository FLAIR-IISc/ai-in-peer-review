This paper proposes to investigate attention transfers between a teacher and a student network. Attention transfer is performed by minimizing the l2 distance between the teacher/student attention maps at different layers, in addition to minimizing the classification loss and optionally a knowledge distillation term.

The authors define several activation-based attentions (sum of absolute feature values raised at the power p or max of values raised at the power p) and propose a gradient-based attention (derivative of the Loss w.r.t. inputs). They evaluate their approaches on several datasets (CIFAR, CUB/Scene, ImageNet) showing that attention transfers do help improve the student network's test performance. However, the student networks perform worse than the teacher, even with attention.

The paper is clearly written and well-motivated, and the authors demonstrate consistent improvement of the student with attention compared to the student alone. However, there are some limitations and unclear aspects of the work.

Firstly, while the authors claim that networks with higher accuracy have a higher spatial correlation between the object and the attention map, it would be nice to have quantitative results to support this claim. Figure 4 is compelling, but more rigorous evaluation is needed.

Secondly, the choice of hyperparameter values, particularly β, is not well-explained. It would be helpful to see the impact of varying β on the results.

Thirdly, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the pros and cons of different attention maps. The authors present several options, but it is unclear which one to use in which case.

Lastly, while the student networks have fewer parameters, they do not achieve better results than the teacher. It would be interesting to characterize the corresponding speed-up. If the same architecture is kept between the student and the teacher, is there any benefit to the attention transfer?

To further improve the paper, I would suggest addressing these points and providing more quantitative results to support the claims. Additionally, it would be helpful to discuss the novelty of the approach relative to FitNets and other knowledge distillation methods.

Decision: Accept with minor revisions.

Supporting arguments for the decision:

* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
* The authors demonstrate consistent improvement of the student with attention compared to the student alone.
* The approach is novel and has the potential to improve knowledge distillation.

However, the paper needs to address the limitations mentioned above to strengthen the results and provide more insights into the proposed approach.

Additional feedback:

* Consider adding more quantitative results to support the claims, particularly regarding the spatial correlation between the object and the attention map.
* Provide more details on the choice of hyperparameter values and their impact on the results.
* Discuss the pros and cons of different attention maps and provide guidelines on when to use each one.
* Characterize the speed-up achieved by the student networks and discuss the benefits of attention transfer when the same architecture is kept between the student and the teacher.
* Address the novelty of the approach relative to FitNets and other knowledge distillation methods.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more quantitative results to support the claim that networks with higher accuracy have a higher spatial correlation between the object and the attention map?
* How did you choose the hyperparameter values, particularly β?
* Can you provide more details on the pros and cons of different attention maps and guidelines on when to use each one?
* How does the student network's performance change when the same architecture is kept between the student and the teacher?
* Can you discuss the novelty of the approach relative to FitNets and other knowledge distillation methods?
