This paper proposes an investigation into attention transfers between a teacher and a student network. The attention transfer is performed by minimizing the L2 distance between the teacher and student attention maps at different layers, in addition to minimizing the classification loss and optionally a knowledge distillation term.

The authors define several activation-based attentions, including the sum of absolute feature values raised to the power of p and the maximum of values raised to the power of p. They also propose a gradient-based attention, which is the derivative of the loss with respect to the inputs. The authors evaluate their approaches on several datasets, including CIFAR, CUB, Scene, and ImageNet, demonstrating that attention transfers can improve the test performance of the student network. However, the student networks still perform worse than the teacher, even with attention.

A few remarks and questions arise from the paper:

- In Section 3, the authors claim that networks with higher accuracy have a higher spatial correlation between the object and the attention map. While Figure 4 is compelling, it would be beneficial to provide quantitative results to support this claim.
- The authors do not discuss how they chose the hyperparameter values, and it would be interesting to see the impact of β on the results.
- Reporting the teacher's training and validation loss in Figure 7b would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the results.
- The experiments do not clearly elucidate the pros and cons of the different attention maps, making it difficult to determine which attention map to use in a given scenario.
- Although attention transfer (AT) does not lead to better results than the teacher, the student networks have fewer parameters. It would be interesting to characterize the corresponding speed-up. Furthermore, if the same architecture is maintained between the student and the teacher, is there any benefit to the attention transfer?

In summary, the paper has several strengths and weaknesses:

Pros:
- The paper is clearly written and well-motivated.
- The results consistently demonstrate an improvement in the student's performance with attention compared to the student alone.

Cons:
- The student networks still perform worse than the teacher models.
- It is unclear which attention map to use in a given scenario.
- The novelty of the paper is somewhat incremental relative to Fitnet.
