This paper proposes an investigation into attention transfers between a teacher and a student network. The authors employ attention transfer by minimizing the L2 distance between the teacher and student attention maps at various layers, in addition to minimizing the classification loss and optionally incorporating a knowledge distillation term.

The authors define several activation-based attentions, including the sum of absolute feature values raised to the power of p and the maximum of values raised to the power of p. They also propose a gradient-based attention, which is derived from the loss with respect to the inputs.

The authors evaluate their approaches on several datasets, including CIFAR, CUB, Scene, and ImageNet, demonstrating that attention transfer does improve the student network's test performance. However, the student networks consistently perform worse than the teacher, even with attention transfer.

Several remarks and questions arise from the paper:

1. In Section 3, the authors claim that networks with higher accuracy exhibit a higher spatial correlation between the object and the attention map. While Figure 4 is compelling, quantitative results would be beneficial in supporting this claim.

2. The authors do not provide details on how they chose the hyperparameter values. It would be informative to see the impact of β on the results.

3. Reporting the teacher's training and validation loss in Figure 7b would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the results.

4. The experiments do not clearly outline the pros and cons of the different attention maps. A more in-depth analysis would be beneficial in understanding the strengths and weaknesses of each attention map.

5. Although attention transfer (AT) does not lead to better results than the teacher, the student networks have fewer parameters. It would be interesting to characterize the corresponding speed-up. Additionally, if the architecture is kept the same between the student and teacher, is there any benefit to attention transfer?

In summary, the paper has several strengths and weaknesses:

Pros:

* The paper is clearly written and well-motivated.
* The student network consistently shows improvement with attention compared to the student alone.

Cons:

* The student networks perform worse than the teacher models.
* It is unclear which attention to use in which case.
* The novelty of the paper is somewhat incremental compared to FitNet.

Overall, the paper provides a well-written and well-motivated investigation into attention transfers between teacher and student networks. However, there are several areas that require further clarification and analysis to strengthen the paper's contributions.
