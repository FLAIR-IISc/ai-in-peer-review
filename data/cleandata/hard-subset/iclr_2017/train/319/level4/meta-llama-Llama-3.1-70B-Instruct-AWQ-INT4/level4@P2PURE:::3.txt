This paper proposes an investigation into attention transfers between a teacher and a student network. The authors perform attention transfer by minimizing the L2 distance between the teacher and student attention maps at different layers, in addition to minimizing the classification loss and optionally a knowledge distillation term.

The authors define several activation-based attentions, including the sum of absolute feature values raised to the power of p and the maximum of values raised to the power of p. They also propose a gradient-based attention, which is the derivative of the loss with respect to the inputs.

The authors evaluate their approaches on several datasets, including CIFAR, CUB, Scene, and ImageNet, and demonstrate that attention transfers can improve the test performance of the student network. However, even with attention transfer, the student networks perform worse than the teacher networks.

Several remarks and questions arise from this work:

* In Section 3, the authors claim that networks with higher accuracy have a higher spatial correlation between the object and the attention map. While Figure 4 is compelling, it would be beneficial to include quantitative results to support this claim.
* The authors do not provide details on how they chose the hyperparameter values, particularly the impact of β. It would be helpful to include an analysis of the effect of these hyperparameters on the results.
* Reporting the teacher's training and validation loss in Figure 7b would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the results.
* The experiments do not clearly elucidate the pros and cons of the different attention maps. A more detailed analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of each attention map would be beneficial.
* Although attention transfer does not lead to better results than the teacher, the student networks have fewer parameters, which could result in a speed-up. It would be interesting to characterize this speed-up and investigate whether there are any benefits to attention transfer when the student and teacher have the same architecture.

In summary, the paper has several strengths, including:

Pros:
* The paper is clearly written and well-motivated.
* The results consistently show an improvement in the student's performance with attention transfer compared to the student alone.

However, there are also several weaknesses:

Cons:
* The student networks perform worse than the teacher models.
* It is unclear which attention map to use in which scenario.
* The novelty of the approach is somewhat incremental compared to FitNet.
