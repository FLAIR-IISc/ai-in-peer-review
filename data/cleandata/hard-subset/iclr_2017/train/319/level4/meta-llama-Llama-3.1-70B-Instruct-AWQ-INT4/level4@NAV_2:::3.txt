This paper proposes to investigate attention transfers between a teacher and a student network. Attention transfer is performed by minimizing the l2 distance between the teacher/student attention maps at different layers, in addition to minimizing the classification loss and optionally a knowledge distillation term. 

The authors define several activation-based attentions (sum of absolute feature values raised at the power p or max of values raised at the power p) and propose a gradient-based attention (derivative of the Loss w.r.t. inputs). They evaluate their approaches on several datasets (CIFAR, Cub/Scene, Imagenet) showing that attention transfers do help improve the student network test performance. However, the student networks perform worse than the teacher, even with attention.

Few remarks/questions:
- In section 3, the authors claim that networks with higher accuracy have a higher spatial correlation between the object and the attention map. While Figure 4 is compelling, it would be nice to have quantitative results showing that as well.
- How did you choose the hyperparameter values? It would be nice to see what is the impact of β.
- It would be nice to report teacher train and validation loss in Figure 7 b).
- From the experiments, it is not clear what the pros/cons of the different attention maps are.
- AT does not lead to better results than the teacher. However, the student networks have fewer parameters. It would be interesting to characterize the corresponding speed-up. If you keep the same architecture between the student and the teacher, is there any benefit to the attention transfer?

In summary:
Pros:
- Clearly written and well-motivated.
- Consistent improvement of the student with attention compared to the student alone.

Cons:
- Students have worse performances than the teacher models.
- It is not clear which attention to use in which case.
- Somewhat incremental novelty relatively to Fitnet.

Decision: Accept (with minor revisions).

Supporting arguments for the decision:
The paper presents a clear and well-motivated approach to attention transfer between teacher and student networks. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their methods on several datasets, demonstrating consistent improvements in student network performance. However, there are some minor issues that need to be addressed, such as providing quantitative results to support the claim in section 3, and clarifying the choice of hyperparameter values.

Additional feedback:
To further improve the paper, the authors could consider providing more details on the computational cost of their approach, particularly in comparison to other knowledge transfer methods. Additionally, it would be interesting to explore the application of attention transfer to other tasks, such as object detection or weakly-supervised localization, as mentioned in the conclusion.
