- Proposes attention transfer by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps, alongside classification loss and optional knowledge distillation.
- Defines activation-based attention (sum or max of feature values raised to power p) and a gradient-based attention (dLoss/dInput).
- Evaluated on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet; attention transfer consistently improves student vs student alone.
- Students still perform worse than teachers even with attention transfer.
- Paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and shows consistent empirical improvement.
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between object and attention map lacks quantitative support (Figure 4 is suggestive but insufficient).
- Hyperparameter choices, especially β, are not well-justified; need analysis of varying β’s impact.
- Insufficient analysis of pros and cons of different attention maps and no guidelines on when to use each type.
- Need characterization of runtime/speed-up tradeoffs for smaller student networks and assessment of benefits when student and teacher share the same architecture.
- Discuss novelty relative to FitNets and other knowledge distillation methods more clearly.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on addressing the above limitations.
- Specific questions for authors: provide quantitative correlation results, explain β selection, detail pros/cons and usage guidelines for attention maps, report student performance when architectures match, and clarify novelty vs prior KD work.
