- Paper studies attention transfer from teacher to student by minimizing L2 distance between attention maps, plus classification loss and optional KD term.  
- Defines activation-based attentions (sum of |features|^p and max of features^p) and a gradient-based attention from loss wrt inputs.  
- Evaluates on CIFAR, CUB, Scene, and ImageNet, showing attention transfer improves student test performance.  
- Despite AT gains, student networks still consistently underperform teachers.  
- Claim: higher-accuracy networks show higher spatial correlation between object and attention map; Figure 4 is compelling but lacks quantitative support.  
- Hyperparameter selection (e.g., β) is not detailed; impact of β on results should be reported.  
- Figure 7b lacks teacher training/validation loss curves for fuller result interpretation.  
- Experiments do not clearly compare pros and cons of different attention map types; deeper analysis of strengths/weaknesses is needed.  
- The paper does not analyze runtime/compute benefits: students have fewer parameters but speed-up characterization is missing.  
- Unclear if attention transfer helps when student and teacher share the same architecture.  
- Paper is clearly written and well-motivated, and students consistently benefit from attention compared to training alone.  
- Main weaknesses: students remain worse than teachers, unclear guidance on which attention to use, and novelty is incremental relative to FitNet.  
- Overall: solid investigation into attention transfer but needs more quantitative analyses, hyperparameter details, and comparative insights to strengthen contributions.
