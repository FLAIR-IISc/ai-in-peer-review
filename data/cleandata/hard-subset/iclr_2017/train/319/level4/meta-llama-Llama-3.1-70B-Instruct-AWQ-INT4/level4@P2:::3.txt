This paper proposes to investigate attention transfers between a teacher and a student network. Attention transfer is performed by minimising the l2 distance between the teacher/student attention maps at different layers, in addition to minimising the classification loss and optionally a knowledge distillation term.

Authors define several activation based attentions (sum of absolute feature values raise at the power p or max of values raised at the power p). They also propose a gradient based attention (derivative of the Loss w.r.t. inputs). 

They evaluate their approaches on several datasets (CIFAR, Cub/Scene, Imagenet) showing that attention transfers  does help improving the student network test performance.  However, the student networks performs worst than the teacher, even with attention.

Few remarks/questions:
- in section 3 authors  claim that networks with higher accuracy have a higher spatial correlation between the object and the attention map. While Figure 4 is compelling, it would be nice to have quantitative results showing that as well.
- how did you choose the hyperparameter values, it would be nice to see what is the impact of $\beta$.
- it would be nice to report teacher train and validation loss in Figure 7 b)
- from the experiments, it is not clear what at the pros/cons of the different attention maps
- AT does not lead to better result than the teacher. However, the student networks have less parameters. It would be interesting to characterise the corresponding speed-up. If you keep the same architecture between the student and the teacher, is there any benefit to the attention transfer?

Decision: Accept

Reasons:
1. The paper proposes a novel method for transferring knowledge from a teacher network to a student network, using attention maps.
2. The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method on several datasets, including CIFAR, Cub/Scene, and Imagenet.

Supporting arguments:
- The paper is well-written and well-motivated, with a clear explanation of the proposed method and its advantages.
- The experimental results show consistent improvement in the student network's performance when using attention transfer, compared to not using it.
- The paper provides a good analysis of the different attention maps and their pros and cons.

Additional feedback:
- It would be nice to see more quantitative results to support the claim that networks with higher accuracy have a higher spatial correlation between the object and the attention map.
- The impact of the hyperparameter $\beta$ on the results could be explored further.
- Reporting teacher train and validation loss in Figure 7 b) would provide a more complete picture of the results.
- A more detailed analysis of the pros and cons of the different attention maps would be helpful.
- Characterizing the speed-up achieved by using attention transfer with student networks having less parameters would be interesting.
- Exploring the benefit of attention transfer when keeping the same architecture between the student and teacher would be a good direction for future work.

Questions to the authors:
- Can you provide more quantitative results to support the claim that networks with higher accuracy have a higher spatial correlation between the object and the attention map?
- How did you choose the hyperparameter values, and what is the impact of $\beta$ on the results?
- Can you report teacher train and validation loss in Figure 7 b)?
- Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the pros and cons of the different attention maps?
- Can you characterize the speed-up achieved by using attention transfer with student networks having less parameters?
- Can you explore the benefit of attention transfer when keeping the same architecture between the student and teacher?
