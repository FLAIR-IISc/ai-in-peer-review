- Proposes attention transfer by minimizing L2 distance between teacher/student attention maps, plus classification loss and optional knowledge distillation.  
- Defines activation-based attentions (sum/max of features to the power p) and a gradient-based attention (loss derivative w.r.t. inputs).  
- Evaluated on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet; attention transfer consistently improves students versus student-only baselines but students remain worse than teachers.  
- Requests quantitative evidence (beyond Figure 4) that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between object and attention map.  
- Asks how hyperparameters were chosen and requests analysis of the impact of the β weight.  
- Suggests reporting teacher train and validation loss in Figure 7(b).  
- Notes experiments do not clarify the relative pros and cons of the different attention map types.  
- Suggests characterizing runtime/parameter speed-up and asking if attention transfer helps when student and teacher share the same architecture.  
- Pros: clearly written, well-motivated, consistent student improvement with attention.  
- Cons: students still underperform teachers; unclear guidance on which attention to use; incremental novelty relative to FitNets.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Reasons for decision: contribution seen as incremental, students do not beat teachers, and the paper lacks clear analysis of different attention maps.  
- Additional feedback: add deeper analysis of attention maps-performance relationship, explore other tasks (detection/segmentation), and provide more implementation/hyperparameter details.
