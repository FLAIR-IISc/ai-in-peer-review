This paper proposes an investigation into attention transfers between a teacher and a student network. The authors perform attention transfer by minimizing the L2 distance between the teacher and student attention maps at different layers, in addition to minimizing the classification loss and optionally a knowledge distillation term.

The authors define several activation-based attentions, including the sum of absolute feature values raised to the power of p and the maximum of values raised to the power of p. They also propose a gradient-based attention, which is calculated as the derivative of the loss with respect to the inputs.

The authors evaluate their approaches on several datasets, including CIFAR, CUB, Scene, and ImageNet, demonstrating that attention transfers can improve the test performance of the student network. However, even with attention transfer, the student networks perform worse than the teacher.

Several remarks and questions arise from the paper:

- In Section 3, the authors claim that networks with higher accuracy tend to have a higher spatial correlation between the object and the attention map. While Figure 4 is compelling, it would be beneficial to include quantitative results to support this claim.
- The authors do not provide information on how they chose the hyperparameter values, particularly β. It would be interesting to see the impact of varying β on the results.
- Reporting the teacher's training and validation loss in Figure 7(b) would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the results.
- The experiments do not clearly outline the pros and cons of the different attention maps. A more detailed analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of each attention map would be beneficial.
- Although attention transfer does not lead to better results than the teacher, the student networks have fewer parameters. It would be interesting to characterize the corresponding speed-up. Furthermore, if the same architecture is maintained between the student and teacher, is there any benefit to using attention transfer?

In summary, the paper has several strengths and weaknesses:

Pros:
- The paper is clearly written and well-motivated.
- The authors consistently demonstrate improvement in the student network's performance with attention transfer compared to the student alone.

Cons:
- The student networks perform worse than the teacher models.
- It is unclear which attention map to use in which scenario.
- The novelty of the paper is somewhat incremental compared to FitNet.
