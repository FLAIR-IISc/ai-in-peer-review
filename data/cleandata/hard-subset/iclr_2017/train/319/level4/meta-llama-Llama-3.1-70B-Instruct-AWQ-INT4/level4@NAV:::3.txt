This paper explores the concept of attention transfer between a teacher and a student network, with the goal of enhancing the student's performance. 
The attention transfer is achieved by minimizing the L2 distance between the teacher and student attention maps across different layers, in conjunction with minimizing the classification loss and an optional knowledge distillation term. 
The authors introduce various activation-based attention mechanisms, including the sum of absolute feature values raised to the power of p and the maximum of values raised to the power of p. Additionally, they propose a gradient-based attention approach, which is based on the derivative of the loss with respect to the inputs. 
The authors evaluate their methods on several datasets, including CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet, and demonstrate that attention transfer can improve the student network's test performance. However, the student networks consistently underperform the teacher networks, even with attention transfer. 

Some observations and questions arise:
- While the authors claim in Section 3 that networks with higher accuracy exhibit a higher spatial correlation between objects and attention maps, quantitative results supporting this assertion would be beneficial. Figure 4 is compelling, but additional data would strengthen this argument.
- The authors' choice of hyperparameter values is not explicitly stated, and an analysis of the impact of the β parameter would be valuable.
- Including the teacher's training and validation loss in Figure 7b would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the results.
- The experiments do not clearly elucidate the advantages and disadvantages of the different attention maps.
- Although attention transfer does not yield better results than the teacher, the student networks have fewer parameters. Characterizing the corresponding speed-up would be interesting. If the same architecture is maintained between the student and teacher, is there any benefit to attention transfer?

In summary:
Strengths:
- The paper is well-written and effectively motivated.
- Consistent improvement in the student's performance with attention transfer compared to the student alone is demonstrated.

Weaknesses:
- The student networks consistently underperform the teacher models.
- The choice of attention map and its application is not clearly defined.
- The novelty of the approach is somewhat incremental compared to FitNet.
