- Proposal: Investigates attention transfer by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps alongside classification loss and optional knowledge distillation.

- Attention mechanisms: Defines activation-based maps (sum/max of |features|^p) and a gradient-based map (derivative of loss w.r.t. inputs).

- Evaluation: Tests on CIFAR, CUB, Scene, and ImageNet and shows attention transfer improves student performance versus student alone.

- Limitation: Student networks remain worse than teacher networks even with attention transfer.

- Evidence gap: Claim that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between object and attention map is supported visually but lacks quantitative validation.

- Hyperparameters: Choice of hyperparameter values (e.g., β) and sensitivity analysis are unclear and not reported.

- Missing plots: Teacher’s training and validation loss should be included in Figure 7(b) for fuller interpretation.

- Comparative analysis: Experiments do not clearly elucidate the pros and cons or selection criteria for different attention mechanisms.

- Practical benefit: Need characterization of computational speed-up and resource trade-offs, especially when student and teacher architectures are identical.

- Novelty: Approach is well-motivated and clearly written but appears incremental compared to prior work like FitNet.

- Strengths: Clear writing, good motivation, and consistent improvement of students over baseline students.

- Final judgment: Overall moderate contribution (rating 6) with reviewer confidence 4.
