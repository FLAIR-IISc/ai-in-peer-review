- Proposes attention transfer (AT): align teacher and student spatial attention maps with an L2 loss alongside classification loss and optional KD.
- Defines activation-based attention maps (sum/max after powering activations with p) and a gradient-based saliency map.
- Experiments on CIFAR, CUB/Scenes, and ImageNet show consistent student accuracy gains with AT, though students remain worse than teachers.
- Motivation is clear: transferring spatial attention as inductive bias is intuitive and well-justified.
- Method is simple and architecture-agnostic: adding an L2 penalty between attention maps is easy to implement.
- Empirical evaluation is broad across datasets and architectures, showing consistent improvements over no-AT baselines.
- AT complements knowledge distillation; combining them appears beneficial.
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks have greater attention-object correlation is mainly supported qualitatively and needs quantitative evidence.
- Hyperparameter choices (beta weight, exponent p) and their sensitivity are under-specified and not systematically explored.
- Unclear guidance on when to use activation- vs. gradient-based attention or sum vs. max aggregation; experiments don’t yield actionable rules.
- Paper lacks compute/parameter/speed-accuracy trade-off analysis comparing smaller students to teachers.
- Novelty is incremental relative to FitNets/hint methods; positioning and empirical comparisons should be sharpened.
- Request: quantify attention-object correlation (localization accuracy, pointing-game, IoU, CAM correlation) and show AT’s effect on it.
- Request: provide hyperparameter sensitivity curves for beta and p, training stability notes, and layer-wise weighting/normalization effects.
- Request: include teacher training/validation curves to compare learning dynamics under AT.
- Request: report parameter counts, FLOPs, wall-clock times, and AT training/inference overhead to quantify practical trade-offs.
- Request: evaluate AT when teacher and student share architecture to see benefits beyond compression and compare to pure KD.
- Request: ablate attention definitions across layers/datasets, analyze failure cases (e.g., noisy gradient attention), and test normalization fixes.
- Request: strengthen baselines and ablations versus FitNets/KD to clarify what AT uniquely transfers and demonstrate additive benefits.
- Minor: clarify which layers are used for AT and their weighting.
- Minor: specify preprocessing for gradient-based attention (smoothing, absolute values, normalization).
- Overall: well-written and effective idea with consistent gains, but needs quantitative attention-accuracy links, hyperparameter guidance, speed/accuracy analysis, and clearer positioning vs prior work.
