Summary
This paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a simple, effective form of knowledge distillation for convolutional neural networks. The key idea is to define spatial attention maps for intermediate CNN layers—either activation-based (e.g., channel-wise sums of absolute activations or their powers) or gradient-based (input sensitivity maps)—and to train a student to match the teacher’s attention maps. The authors explore several activation-based mapping functions, use l2-normalized attention vectors, and place AT losses at multiple layers, optionally combining AT with standard knowledge distillation (KD). Empirically, the paper reports consistent gains on CIFAR-10 with ResNets and WRNs, fine-tuning gains on CUB and MIT Scenes, and notable improvements on ImageNet (ResNet-18 student with a ResNet-34 teacher). The method also outperforms FitNets-style full-activation regression, and code is released.

Decision: Accept
- The approach is simple, well-motivated, and delivers consistent, meaningful improvements across datasets and architectures, including ImageNet.
- The paper provides clear empirical evidence that aligning spatial attention is more effective than regressing full activations and is complementary to KD.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and contribution: The paper targets whether spatial “attention” information from a teacher can be used to guide a student in CNNs. It operationalizes attention as spatial summaries of internal representations or input gradients, and introduces a practical loss to align student and teacher attention maps. This is a clear, focused extension of knowledge transfer ideas.
- Positioning and motivation: The work is well placed in two literatures: (a) visualization/attention in CNNs (saliency, CAM/Grad-CAM, guided backprop), and (b) knowledge distillation/teacher-student training. The choice to transfer spatial summaries (rather than full activations) is motivated and tested, with ablations over mapping functions (sum vs max, powers p).
- Evidence and rigor: Results cover multiple architectures and settings. On CIFAR, AT reliably improves WRN students, and combining AT with KD helps further in many cases. AT beats full-activation regression both in final accuracy and convergence speed. On fine-tuning tasks (CUB, Scenes), AT narrows the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34. On ImageNet, AT improves ResNet-18 by 1.1% top-1 without exhaustive tuning. These findings support the claims. The importance of l2-normalization of attention maps and placing losses across multiple groups are noted and empirically supported.

That said, the paper has two issues that do not undermine acceptance but merit revision:
- Terminology: Calling the activation statistics “attention” is somewhat broad; they are spatial summaries of feature maps. The paper should clarify this to avoid confusion with class-discriminative attention (e.g., CAM/Grad-CAM).
- Organization: The gradient-based AT section feels detached. It is evaluated only on CIFAR with a thin NIN (no BN), is not directly compared or combined with activation-based AT in the same setting, and the practical overhead of double backprop is not discussed. Integrating or streamlining this part would improve focus.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not central to my decision)
- Clarify norms and notation: In Eq. (2), QjS = vec(F(AjS)) and QjT are vectors. Please state explicitly that the norms are vector p-norms (with p = 2 in experiments), i.e., equivalent to the Frobenius norm on the H×W map after vectorization—not the matrix operator 2-norm (largest singular value). This will prevent confusion.
- Terminology: Consider framing the activation-based maps as “spatial activation summaries” or “attention proxies,” and contrast them with class-discriminative attention (CAM/Grad-CAM). You already cite related methods—an explicit positioning would help.
- Gradient-based AT: Either integrate it more tightly (e.g., direct head-to-head with activation-based AT under identical settings; a joint loss combining both; report compute overhead) or move it to an appendix. The current presentation feels like a parallel idea.
- Compute and implementation details: Report the extra training time/memory for AT (number of added losses/layers), and for gradient-based AT (double backprop cost). Clarify how attention maps are interpolated when spatial sizes differ, and why l2-normalization is essential.
- Hyperparameters and sensitivity: You scale β by map size and batch size and sometimes decay it when combining with KD. Please include a brief sensitivity analysis (β, choice of p, l1 vs l2 normalization), and discuss stability.
- KD comparisons: On ImageNet, KD reportedly struggled. It would be helpful to report KD with tuned temperature/α and, if possible, a same-architecture teacher/student to match prior observations. This will strengthen the claim that AT is the first successful transfer method on ImageNet.
- Scope and limitations: The method is currently tailored to CNNs relying on spatial structure. Please discuss applicability to non-convolutional architectures or tasks without spatial grids, and to detection/segmentation where attention may matter even more.

Questions for the authors
1) Norms in Eq. (2): Please confirm that the norms are vector p-norms after vectorization (i.e., Frobenius on the map), not matrix operator norms. If so, state this explicitly to avoid confusion.
2) Layer matching and interpolation: How exactly do you align teacher and student layers when depths differ, and how do you up/down-sample attention maps? Any artifacts from interpolation?
3) Importance of normalization: You emphasize l2-normalizing attention maps before computing the loss. Can you quantify the degradation without normalization and explain why normalization helps optimization?
4) Hyperparameters: How sensitive are gains to β, the power p in Fpsum/Fpmax, and the choice of l1 vs l2 normalization? Any default recommendations?
5) Combining AT with KD: On which datasets/settings is AT+KD reliably better than AT alone? On ImageNet, is KD’s failure due to teacher/student mismatch, suboptimal temperatures/α, or optimization dynamics?
6) Gradient-based AT: What is the training-time overhead (and memory) of double backprop in your implementations? Did you attempt a joint loss combining activation-based and gradient-based AT, and if so, with what effect?
7) Computational budget: For ImageNet, you used only two AT losses and no hyperparameter tuning. Do more AT locations/losses yield further gains? What are the best practices you recommend?
8) Generality: Have you tried AT on detection/segmentation or on non-convolutional backbones? Any anticipated challenges or modifications?

Overall, this is a solid and practical contribution to knowledge transfer with convincing empirical results. With clearer terminology, notation, and a tighter treatment of the gradient-based component, the paper would be even stronger.
