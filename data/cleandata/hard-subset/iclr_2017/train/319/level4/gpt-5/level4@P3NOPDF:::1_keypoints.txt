- Proposes distilling teacher inductive biases by aligning student attention maps instead of raw activations, arguing attention is compact and semantically meaningful.  
- Idea is simple, easy to integrate into existing pipelines, and compatible with standard knowledge distillation (KD).  
- Conceptual novelty: transferring attention distributions may better capture "where to look" than feature vectors.  
- Practicality: method appears straightforward to implement and combine with KD.  
- Clear motivation: paper convincingly argues why attention can be a better knowledge carrier than activations.  
- Limited empirical gains: reported improvements over strong KD baselines are modest and not clearly consistent.  
- ImageNet evaluation is underdeveloped and too brief to support large-scale claims.  
- Missing ablations: lacking systematic studies on which layers to transfer, attention definitions, loss-weight/temperature sensitivity, and additive benefit over KD.  
- Baseline fairness unclear: unclear whether KD baselines were tuned comparably and whether gains exceed run variance.  
- Overhead not quantified: computational and memory costs of computing/matching attention maps are not reported.  
- Clarify attention construction: need exact definitions, normalization, statistics used, and gradient-flow behavior.  
- Clarify layer pairing: explain how teacher–student layer pairs are chosen and weighted, and whether principles guide selections.  
- Clarify KD interaction: report how KD and AT loss weights are chosen and whether their signals conflict.  
- Robustness to architecture mismatch: need results for teachers and students with different depths/topologies.  
- Suggest expanding teacher–student pairs: include multiple and cross-family transfers (e.g., ResNet→MobileNet).  
- Suggest stronger, fair baselines: perform careful hyperparameter sweeps for KD and feature-based distillation baselines.  
- Demonstrate additive gains: show AT improves upon best-tuned KD and characterize regimes where it helps most.  
- Ablation recommendations: compare attention definitions, vary matched layers/count, and study loss weighting/schedules.  
- Statistical rigor: report mean±std over multiple runs, confidence intervals, and possibly significance tests with training curves.  
- Cost and scalability: quantify extra compute/memory, wall-clock overhead, and effects on training stability and inference.  
- Qualitative analysis: provide visualizations of teacher vs. student attention maps to illustrate transfer effects.  
- Generalization tests: evaluate under data shifts or related tasks to show broader utility.  
- Overall: idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but current empirical evidence is insufficient; stronger ImageNet experiments, ablations, and cost analyses could change the evaluation.
