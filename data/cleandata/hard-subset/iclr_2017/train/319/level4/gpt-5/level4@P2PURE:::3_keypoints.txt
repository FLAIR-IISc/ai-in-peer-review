- Proposes attention transfer by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps at multiple layers, alongside classification loss and optional knowledge distillation.  
- Introduces activation-based attention mechanisms (e.g., sum of |activations|^p, max of activations^p) and a gradient-based attention (loss gradient w.r.t. inputs).  
- Experiments on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet show attention transfer consistently improves student test performance, but students still underperform teachers.  
- Claims higher-accuracy networks exhibit higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps; Figure 4 is suggestive but lacks quantitative evidence.  
- Hyperparameter selection is unclear; sensitivity analysis for the attention weight beta is not reported.  
- Requests reporting of the teacher’s training and validation loss in Figure 7b.  
- Experimental results do not clarify the relative pros and cons of the different attention mechanisms.  
- Since students have fewer parameters, the paper should quantify speed-up and clarify whether attention transfer helps when teacher and student architectures are identical.  
- Pros: clearly written, well motivated, and yields consistent improvement over student alone.  
- Cons: students remain worse than teachers, guidance on which attention mechanism to use is lacking, and novelty seems incremental relative to FitNet.
