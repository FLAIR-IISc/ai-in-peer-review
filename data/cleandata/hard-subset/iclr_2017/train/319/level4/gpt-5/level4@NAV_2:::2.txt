1. Summary of contributions
- The paper introduces a variant of knowledge transfer that constrains a student CNN to match spatial “attention” maps derived from a teacher’s intermediate feature activations. The attention maps are defined via simple channel-wise reductions of absolute activations (e.g., sum, sum of powers, or max of powers) and are compared after l2-normalization. The authors also explore a gradient-based formulation using double backpropagation and an optional flip-invariance regularizer.
- Empirically, the proposed activation-based transfer improves student performance consistently across multiple datasets and architectures, and it outperforms a FitNets-style full activation regression baseline. The method can be combined with standard knowledge distillation. The paper is generally clear and readable.

2. Decision: Accept (borderline)
Key reasons:
- Strong, consistent empirical gains over reasonable baselines across several computer vision benchmarks.
- A simple, practical mechanism that appears robust across network families and datasets.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem: The work targets the question of how to transfer where a teacher “focuses” spatially inside a CNN to help a student learn better. The formulation is operationalized as matching spatial maps computed from internal activations (or input gradients).
- Motivation and related work: The framing fits within the knowledge distillation literature and connects to attention visualization methods. That said, the use of “attention” here can be confusing because the maps are statistics of hidden activations (or gradients) rather than a learned attention mechanism; clearer positioning would help.
- Empirical support and rigor: The activation-based transfer shows consistent improvements and beats a FitNets-style regression of full activations, which is a solid and relevant baseline. However, the gradient-based transfer section feels orthogonal: it is not directly compared against the activation-based variant under matched conditions nor integrated with it, which weakens the narrative around its added value.
- Technical clarity: The notation around the norm in the attention-matching loss (Eq. 2) is ambiguous. As written, using the induced 2-norm on an H×W map would imply computing a spectral norm, which is expensive; it seems more likely the intended metric is the l2 norm of the vectorized map (equivalently a Frobenius norm on the matrix), but this should be unambiguous.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all items impact the accept decision)
- Terminology: Consider toning down or clarifying the use of “attention.” Explicitly state that the method matches spatial summaries of internal activations (or input sensitivities), which aids transfer, and relate this to attention visualization rather than learned attention mechanisms.
- Organization: Either (a) more tightly integrate the gradient-based variant—provide controlled comparisons to activation-based transfer and/or a combined variant—or (b) move it to an appendix as an exploratory direction to keep the main narrative focused.
- Notation and norms: Precisely define the normalization and distance in Eq. (2). If you use vectorization followed by an l2 distance (equivalently Frobenius on the 2D map), state it explicitly and avoid “induced 2-norm” phrasing that suggests a spectral norm.
- Scope: Note explicitly that the method targets CNNs with spatial feature maps; comment on whether and how the idea could extend to non-convolutional architectures.
- Ablations: While you do compare different pooling functions (sum vs. max, varying p), a brief sensitivity analysis of β and of which layers/groups to apply the loss (and the computational overhead) would strengthen the empirical section.

Questions for the authors
- Eq. (2) norms: Do you compute the l2 distance between l2-normalized, vectorized attention maps (i.e., Frobenius on the H×W maps after normalization)? If so, please clarify the notation and confirm there is no spectral norm computation.
- Gradient-based vs activation-based: Under matched architectures and training settings, how does gradient-based transfer compare directly to activation-based transfer? Do they combine additively if used together?
- Terminology: Could you justify the use of “attention” versus “spatial activation statistics” more explicitly, and discuss how your maps relate to existing attention visualization methods?
- Scope: Is the method applicable beyond convolutional backbones (e.g., to models without explicit spatial feature maps)? If not, please state the limitation clearly.
- Hyperparameters: How sensitive are results to β, the choice of p in the mapping functions, and the specific layers where the transfer loss is applied?
- Overheads: What is the training-time overhead of activation-based transfer and of the gradient-based variant (with double backprop), and how does this scale with model size and number of transfer points?
