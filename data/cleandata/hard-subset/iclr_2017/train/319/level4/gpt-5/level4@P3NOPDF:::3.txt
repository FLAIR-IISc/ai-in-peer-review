Summary:
The paper studies transferring “attention” from a high-capacity teacher to a smaller student network. Attention transfer (AT) augments the student’s training objective with an L2 loss that aligns student and teacher attention maps at selected layers, in addition to the standard classification loss and, optionally, a knowledge-distillation term. The authors define several activation-based attention maps (e.g., spatial maps obtained by aggregating channel activations via sum or max after raising magnitudes to a power p) and a gradient-based attention map (saliency via the derivative of the loss with respect to the input). Experiments on CIFAR, CUB/Scenes, and ImageNet indicate that AT consistently improves student accuracy over training without AT, though students still underperform their teachers.

Strengths:
- Clear problem formulation and motivation: using spatial attention as a vehicle for transferring inductive biases from teacher to student is intuitive and well justified.
- Simple, architecture-agnostic mechanism: adding an L2 penalty between attention maps is easy to implement and integrate with existing training pipelines.
- Broad empirical evaluation: results on multiple datasets and architectures show consistent gains for students over their no-AT baselines.
- Complementary to knowledge distillation: the combination of AT with distillation is natural and appears beneficial.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- The paper claims that higher-accuracy networks exhibit greater spatial correlation between object regions and their attention maps (Section 3), but the evidence is primarily qualitative (Figure 4). A quantitative analysis would strengthen this claim.
- Hyperparameter selection is under-specified. In particular, the choice and sensitivity of beta (weight on the AT loss) and the exponent p are not systematically explored.
- It is unclear when to prefer activation-based versus gradient-based attention, and which aggregation (sum vs. max) works best under which conditions. The current experiments do not distill actionable guidance.
- AT improves students but does not surpass teachers. The paper does not characterize the compute and parameter savings achieved by smaller students or provide a speed/accuracy trade-off.
- Relative to FitNets and related hint-based methods, the novelty is somewhat incremental. The positioning could be sharpened by clearer conceptual distinctions and stronger empirical comparisons.

Requests for additional analysis:
- Quantify attention-object correlation: Report metrics such as localization accuracy, pointing-game scores, IoU with ground-truth boxes/segments, or correlation with class-activation maps. Show that this correlation increases with model accuracy and that AT increases the student’s correlation.
- Hyperparameter study: Provide sensitivity curves for beta and p across at least one CIFAR and one ImageNet setup. Include training stability observations (e.g., does large beta hinder convergence?) and the effect of layer-wise weighting or normalization of attention maps.
- Teacher diagnostics: In Figure 7(b), include teacher training and validation curves for direct comparison, making clear how student/teacher learning dynamics differ when AT is applied.
- Practical trade-offs: Report parameter counts, FLOPs, and wall-clock training/inference times for teacher and student models. Quantify the speed-up obtained by the smaller student and relate it to accuracy loss. Evaluate memory/compute overhead of AT during training (extra forward/backward passes for attention).
- Architectural breadth: Explore whether AT provides benefits when teacher and student share the same architecture (capacity matched). If so, how does AT compare to pure KD? This would clarify whether AT is useful beyond model compression.
- Pros/cons of different attention definitions: Provide a comparative table or ablations indicating which attention type works best for shallow vs. deep layers, small vs. large datasets, or specific architectures. Analyze failure cases (e.g., when gradient-based attention is noisy) and whether normalization mitigates scale mismatches across layers.
- Relation to FitNets and KD: Include stronger baselines and ablations controlling for capacity and loss weights. Clearly articulate what AT transfers that FitNets or KD alone do not, and demonstrate additive benefits under matched settings.

Minor comments:
- Clarify which layers are used for AT and how their contributions are weighted.
- Specify preprocessing for gradient-based attention (e.g., smoothing, absolute values, normalization) to ensure stability.

Overall assessment:
This is a clearly written and well-motivated paper that introduces a simple and effective mechanism for transferring spatial inductive biases from teacher to student. The empirical improvements over student baselines are consistent and practically relevant. However, the paper would be stronger with (1) quantitative evidence linking attention quality to accuracy, (2) clearer guidance on choosing among attention definitions and hyperparameters, (3) a thorough speed/accuracy analysis highlighting the practical benefits of smaller students, and (4) sharper positioning and comparisons to prior hint/distillation methods. Addressing these points would significantly improve both the scientific rigor and the practical impact of the work.
