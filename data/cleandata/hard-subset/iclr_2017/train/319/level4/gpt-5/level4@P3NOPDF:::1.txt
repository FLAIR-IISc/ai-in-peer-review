Summary
The paper proposes a knowledge transfer method that distills a teacher’s inductive biases by aligning student “attention maps” rather than raw activations. This idea is appealing: attention maps provide a compact, task-relevant summary of where the network focuses, and may be more architecture-agnostic and semantically meaningful than feature activations. The method is simple, compatible with standard knowledge distillation (KD), and potentially broadly applicable.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Transferring attention distributions rather than feature vectors is a natural and intuitive step that may better capture “where to look” information from a teacher.
- Practicality: The approach appears easy to integrate with existing training pipelines and can be combined with KD.
- Clarity of motivation: The paper clearly argues why attention may be a better knowledge carrier than activations.

Weaknesses and empirical concerns
- Limited empirical gains: Across the reported experiments, improvements over strong KD baselines appear modest. It is hard to conclude that attention transfer (AT) consistently yields a meaningful advantage over KD alone.
- ImageNet evaluation is underdeveloped: Section 4.2.2 is too brief to support strong claims on large-scale settings. A single or limited configuration on ImageNet is insufficient to demonstrate generality.
- Missing ablations and analysis: The paper would benefit from systematic studies on (i) which layers’ attentions to transfer, (ii) different attention definitions, (iii) sensitivity to loss weights and temperatures, and (iv) the additive benefit of AT when combined with KD under fair tuning.
- Baseline fairness and significance: It is unclear whether KD baselines were tuned as carefully as the proposed method, and whether reported gains exceed variance. Confidence intervals or repeated runs are not reported.
- Overhead and practicality: The computational/memory overhead of computing and matching attention maps is not quantified.

Requested clarifications
- Attention map construction: Please clarify the exact definition(s) used, normalization, and whether gradients flow through the teacher or only the student. Are maps based on channel-wise sums, squared activations, or other statistics?
- Layer selection and weighting: How are teacher-student layer pairs chosen and weighted? Is there a principled criterion (e.g., matching receptive fields) or a heuristic?
- Interaction with KD: When combining KD and AT, how are loss weights selected? Do the two signals ever conflict, and if so, how is that mitigated?
- Robustness to architecture mismatch: How well does AT work when teacher and student have very different depths or topologies?

Suggestions for improvement (especially for Section 4.2.2 on ImageNet)
- Expand teacher–student pairs: Include multiple pairs (e.g., deeper-to-shallower ResNets, cross-family transfers such as ResNet→MobileNet/ShuffleNet). Show that AT is not tailored to a specific pair.
- Stronger and fair baselines: Include well-tuned KD (temperature, loss weight) and feature-based distillation baselines. Report results with careful hyperparameter sweeps for all methods.
- Combine AT with KD: Demonstrate additive gains relative to the best-tuned KD baseline and analyze regimes where AT helps most (e.g., high compression ratios).
- Ablations:
  - Attention definitions: Compare different map constructions (e.g., sum vs. squared-sum across channels; spatial vs. channel attention).
  - Layer choices: Vary which layers are matched and how many; analyze diminishing returns and compute–memory trade-offs.
  - Loss weighting and schedules: Study sensitivity and provide recommended defaults.
- Statistical rigor: Report mean ± standard deviation over multiple runs and, if possible, significance tests. Include training curves and convergence behavior.
- Cost and scalability: Quantify extra compute/memory, wall-clock overhead, and any effect on training stability. Discuss inference-time cost (expected to be negligible).
- Qualitative analysis: Provide visualizations of teacher vs. student attention maps to illustrate what is being transferred and when it improves or hurts performance.
- Generalization tests: If feasible, assess transfer under data shifts or on related tasks (e.g., fine-grained classification) to demonstrate broader utility.

Overall assessment
The central idea—transferring attention maps instead of activations—is interesting and potentially impactful. However, the current empirical evidence does not convincingly show consistent advantages over well-tuned KD, especially at ImageNet scale. Strengthening Section 4.2.2 with broader and more rigorous experiments, along with clearer ablations and cost analyses, would substantially improve the paper and could change my evaluation.

I would be inclined to update my score if the authors expand the ImageNet experiments as suggested, demonstrate clear and statistically significant gains over strong baselines, and provide the requested ablations and analysis to support the claimed benefits of attention transfer.
