This paper studies transferring attention between a teacher and a student network.
The transfer is implemented by minimizing the L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps at multiple layers, in addition to minimizing the classification loss and, optionally, a knowledge distillation term.
The authors define several activation-based attentions (sum of absolute feature values raised to the power p, or max of values raised to the power p). They also propose a gradient-based attention (the derivative of the loss with respect to the inputs).
They evaluate their methods on several datasets (CIFAR, CUB/Scene, ImageNet), showing that attention transfer improves the student network’s test performance. However, even with attention, the student models still perform worse than the teacher.

Few remarks/questions:
- In Section 3, the authors claim that networks with higher accuracy exhibit stronger spatial correlation between the object and the attention map. While Figure 4 is compelling, quantitative evidence would strengthen the claim.
- How were the hyperparameters selected? It would be helpful to show the impact of β.
- It would be useful to report the teacher’s training and validation loss in Figure 7(b).
- From the experiments, the pros/cons of the different attention maps are not clearly delineated.
- AT does not surpass the teacher. Since the student networks have fewer parameters, it would be informative to characterize the corresponding speed-up. If the student and teacher share the same architecture, is there any benefit to attention transfer?

In summary:
Pros:
- Clearly written and well motivated.
- Consistent improvement of the student with attention compared to the student alone.
Cons:
- Students have worse performance than the teacher models.
- Unclear which attention variant to use in which scenario.
- Somewhat incremental novelty relative to FitNet.
