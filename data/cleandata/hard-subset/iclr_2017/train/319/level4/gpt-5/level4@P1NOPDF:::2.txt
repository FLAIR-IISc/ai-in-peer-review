Summary:
The paper proposes a modified knowledge distillation framework that minimizes the discrepancy between the teacher and student by matching aggregated statistics over feature maps. Concretely, it transfers information by aligning the sum of statistics (e.g., summed squared activations or summed lp norms) across hidden feature maps. Empirically, the method outperforms a FitNet-style distillation baseline on several computer vision benchmarks.

Pros:
- The authors evaluate the proposed approach on multiple computer vision datasets.
- The paper is generally well written.

Cons:
- The method appears restricted to convolutional architectures. Because it relies on spatial feature maps and aggregated spatial statistics, it is unclear how it would extend to non-convolutional or sequence models without such structure.
- The use of the term “attention” is misleading. The method essentially distills aggregated activation magnitudes (e.g., sums of squared activations or lp norms) from hidden feature maps, rather than learning or transferring attention mechanisms in the common sense (e.g., learned attention weights or distributions).
- The gradient-based attention transfer component feels out of place. It is neither directly compared to the proposed “attention-based” transfer nor combined with it in a joint setting. As presented, it reads as a parallel idea that is insufficiently integrated and does not clearly add value to the main contribution.
- The definition and computation of the induced 2-norm in Eq. (2) are unclear. If Q ∈ R^{H×W}, its induced 2-norm is the largest singular value, which would be computationally expensive to evaluate within the loss at training time. Did the authors instead intend to use the Frobenius norm (i.e., the l2 norm of all entries), which is much cheaper and more common in such contexts? Clarifying this point is important for both reproducibility and understanding the computational cost.

Overall assessment:
The proposed distillation method works well empirically and is a sensible way to transfer spatial activation statistics from teacher to student, yielding improvements over a FitNet-style baseline. However, the paper would benefit from clearer terminology (avoid calling aggregated activation norms “attention”), tighter organization and integration of the gradient-based component, and precise, unambiguous notation—especially for norms and their computational implications in the loss. Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the paper.
