1) Summary of contributions
The paper studies how to transfer “where to look” information from a teacher CNN to a student CNN. It defines attention for CNNs as spatial maps derived either from activations (e.g., sum or max of absolute feature values raised to a power p) or from gradients (input saliency). The core idea is to train the student with an additional loss that encourages its attention maps at selected layers to match the teacher’s (using an l2 distance between normalized maps), optionally combined with knowledge distillation on softened outputs. The authors show:
- Several activation-based attention mappings, and a gradient-based variant that uses double backpropagation, plus a symmetry regularizer (horizontal flip consistency).
- Consistent gains across CIFAR-10/100, transfer learning on CUB and MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, with code released.
- Empirically, activation-based attention transfer (AT) improves accuracy over the student baseline and often outperforms distilling full activations, and can be combined with KD.
- On ImageNet, AT improves ResNet-18 when using ResNet-34 as teacher, suggesting scalability.

2) Decision
Weak accept. The approach is simple, well-motivated, and yields consistent empirical improvements across architectures and datasets. While not always surpassing the teacher (expected in compression), the method offers a practical, model-agnostic mechanism to transfer supervisory signals beyond logits.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem: Can attention information from a strong teacher improve a smaller or shallower student?
- Motivation and placement: The paper connects to human and neural attention, saliency/CAM/Grad-CAM visualization, knowledge distillation, and FitNets. It fills a gap by explicitly transferring spatial attention rather than logits or full activations, offering a lightweight, architecture-agnostic signal.
- Claims and evidence: The authors present clear definitions of activation- and gradient-based attention and specify how to place losses across layers. Results show consistent gains on CIFAR with multiple WRN settings; AT beats full activation regression in final accuracy while still accelerating convergence. On CUB and Scenes, AT narrows the ResNet-18 vs. ResNet-34 gap; on ImageNet, AT yields ~1% top-1 improvement on ResNet-18 with minimal tuning. Ablations compare attention mappings (sum vs. max; varying p), layer placement, and normalization. Gradient-based AT and a symmetry regularizer also help on CIFAR, though with higher computational cost and more limited experiments.
- Rigor: The method is precisely described and easy to reproduce; ablations are helpful. Some aspects could be more thorough: quantitative correlation between attention quality and accuracy, more systematic hyperparameter sensitivity (especially beta), and stronger statistical reporting (e.g., multiple runs on large-scale experiments).

4) Suggestions and questions for the authors (to improve the paper; not all affect my decision)
- Quantify “better attention”: You state that higher-accuracy networks show higher spatial correlation between attention and objects. Please add quantitative metrics (e.g., pointing game, IoU with bounding boxes when available, or correlation with CAM/Grad-CAM) to substantiate Figure 4.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Provide a sensitivity analysis for beta and the normalization choice (l1 vs. l2). How does performance vary with p in the activation maps? Are there robust defaults?
- Pros/cons of attention variants: Clarify when to prefer activation-based vs. gradient-based AT. For example, activation-based AT seems simpler and more stable (and better than full-activation regression) while gradient-based AT is costlier (double backprop) but can regularize (via symmetry). A concise guidance table or discussion would help practitioners.
- Teacher–student settings: Since students remain weaker than teachers (as expected for compression), please characterize the speed and memory benefits (parameters, FLOPs, wall-clock inference) of the improved students. Also report whether AT brings benefits when teacher and student share the same architecture (beyond convergence speed), especially at larger scale (ImageNet).
- Layer selection: Expand ablations on which layers/groups to match, and whether matching more layers always helps. Are there diminishing returns or over-regularization effects?
- Normalization: You emphasize the importance of normalizing attention maps. Show an ablation confirming the effect of no normalization, l1, and l2 normalization.
- Statistical reporting: Where feasible, report mean and standard deviation over multiple runs, especially for CIFAR and key ImageNet results, to assess robustness.
- KD on ImageNet: You note KD struggled for ResNet-18/34 with default hyperparameters. Can you include a brief hyperparameter sweep or a note on temperature/alpha choices to rule out tuning issues?
- Gradient-based AT details: Clarify how batch normalization is handled in the second backprop step and provide computational overhead estimates vs. activation-based AT.
- Visualization: Include more side-by-side student vs. teacher attention maps before and after AT to qualitatively support claims, especially for fine-grained datasets where mid-level attention may be critical.

Questions
- How sensitive are AT gains to mismatches in spatial resolution between teacher and student, and which interpolation strategies work best?
- Does AT primarily help hard classes or specific failure modes? Any class-wise or calibration analysis?
- Can AT be combined with label smoothing or other regularizers without conflict?
- For the symmetry regularizer, does it generalize to other transformations (e.g., rotations), and does it interact with data augmentation?

Overall, the paper is clear and well positioned, with practical impact. The proposed attention transfer is simple, broadly applicable, and empirically effective, warranting a weak accept.
