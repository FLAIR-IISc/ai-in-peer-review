1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes transferring “attention” from a teacher CNN to a student CNN as a form of knowledge transfer. The authors define attention as spatial maps derived either from activations (activation-based attention) or from input gradients (gradient-based attention), and add explicit losses that encourage the student’s attention maps to match those of the teacher. They explore multiple attention mappings (sum, powered sum, max), emphasize the importance of normalization, and show that attention transfer (AT) can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD). Empirically, they report consistent gains on CIFAR, fine-grained (CUB), and scenes, and show an ImageNet case (ResNet-18 student, ResNet-34 teacher) with a +1.1% top-1 improvement. They also show that activation-based AT outperforms full-activation regression and that gradient-based AT can be implemented via double backpropagation with some regularization benefits. The idea is simple, well-motivated, and potentially broadly useful.

2. Decision
Reject.
Key reasons:
- The empirical improvements over strong distillation baselines are modest/inconclusive; the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that AT provides a clear advantage over KD.
- The ImageNet evidence is too limited. Section 4.2.2 needs more thorough experiments and comparisons to substantiate the claimed contribution at scale.

3. Supporting arguments
- Comparisons to KD: While AT often helps and can be combined with KD on CIFAR, the gains relative to KD alone are not convincingly larger or clearly robust across settings. In some cases, KD performs similarly, and the paper does not provide strong statistical evidence that AT is consistently superior to KD.
- ImageNet evaluation: The ImageNet study uses only two AT losses (on the last two groups), no tuning, and lacks a thorough KD comparison under matched training budgets. The claim that KD “struggles” on ImageNet is not sufficient to establish AT’s advantage; stronger, side-by-side baselines are needed (e.g., KD with tuned temperature/α, FitNets-style hints, and full-activation transfer), ideally with confidence intervals and equalized compute.
- Gradient-based AT: The gradient-based method is evaluated only on CIFAR with a thin NIN without batch normalization, under a restricted training setup, with noted uncertainty about batch-norm behavior in second backprop. This limits the strength of conclusions about gradient-based AT’s practical value.
- Scope of ablations: Although the paper evaluates several activation mappings and shows that sum-based variants work best, broader ablations (e.g., sensitivity to β schedules, layer placement, p values, normalization choices, and interpolation across mismatched spatial sizes) would clarify when and why AT helps.
- Reporting: The ImageNet section states intent to add losses to all four groups but does not report results. Given the centrality of ImageNet-scale validation to the paper’s claim, the current evidence is insufficient.

4. Additional feedback to help improve the paper (not all are part of the decision)
- Strengthen Section 4.2.2:
  - Add AT losses to all residual groups; report the effect of each placement.
  - Provide tuned KD baselines (temperatures, α, label-smoothing), FitNets-style hints, and full-activation regression under matched training budgets.
  - Report multiple runs with variance/confidence intervals.
  - Include training curves and compute/memory overhead for AT vs KD.
- Clarify normalization and weighting:
  - Justify the specific l2 normalization and β schedule; include sensitivity analyses.
  - Discuss how attention map interpolation affects performance when spatial sizes differ.
- Gradient-based AT:
  - Resolve batch normalization handling for the second backpropagation.
  - Evaluate on architectures with BN and on at least one large-scale setting or a large-resolution task.
- Qualitative analysis:
  - Provide systematic qualitative comparisons of student vs teacher attention before/after AT across datasets, and relate changes to accuracy gains.
- Reproducibility:
  - Detail hyperparameters for each experiment, including temperature, α, β per layer, schedules, and data augmentation.

Questions for the authors
- On ImageNet, how does AT compare to a well-tuned KD baseline with temperature/α sweeps and label smoothing, under matched compute and the same data augmentation?
- What is the impact of placing AT losses on each residual group individually versus all groups together on ImageNet? Does AT still help if applied only to early or only to mid-level layers?
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of attention mapping (Fsum vs F^2sum vs F^pmax) and to the exponent p on large-scale models?
- What is the training-time and memory overhead of AT relative to KD and to full-activation transfer, especially when placing multiple AT losses?
- For gradient-based AT, how should batch normalization be handled in the second backpropagation? Have you tried fixing BN stats/parameters during the second pass, and what are the trade-offs?
- Can you provide error bars across multiple runs for CIFAR and ImageNet to assess the statistical significance of AT’s gains?
- Do the AT-induced gains persist when the student and teacher have identical architectures and capacities, or when the student is wider but shallower (e.g., WRN variants)?

I would consider updating my score if the authors significantly extend and strengthen Section 4.2.2 with the above comparisons and analyses.
