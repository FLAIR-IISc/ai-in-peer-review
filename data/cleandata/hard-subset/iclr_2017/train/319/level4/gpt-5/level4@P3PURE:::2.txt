The paper presents a modified knowledge distillation framework that minimizes the discrepancy between teacher and student by matching sums of statistics over feature maps. The authors empirically demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform a FitNet-style distillation baseline.

Pros:
- The authors evaluate the proposed methods on multiple computer vision datasets.
- The paper is generally well written.

Cons:
- The method appears to be limited to convolutional architectures.
- The “attention” terminology is misleading. The method effectively distills the sum of squared activations (or other statistics, e.g., a summed lp norm) over hidden feature maps.
- The gradient-based attention transfer component feels out of place. It is neither directly compared to nor used jointly with the “attention-based” transfer, and reads as a parallel idea that does not add much value.
- It is unclear how the induced 2-norm in Eq. (2) is computed. For Q ∈ R^{H×W}, the induced 2-norm is the largest singular value, which would be computationally expensive to use as an objective. Is it possible the authors meant the Frobenius norm?

Overall, the proposed distillation method works well in practice, but the paper has organizational issues and unclear notation.
