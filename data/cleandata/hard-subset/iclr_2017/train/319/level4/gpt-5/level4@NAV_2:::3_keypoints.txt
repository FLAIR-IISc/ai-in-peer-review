- Investigates transferring “where to look” from a high-capacity teacher CNN to a smaller student by aligning spatial attention maps from activations or input gradients.  
- Proposes channel-aggregation attention mappings (e.g., sums/max of |A|^p) and uses input-gradient-based attention as an alternative.  
- Trains the student with classification loss plus an L2 attention-alignment loss, optionally combined with knowledge distillation.  
- Evaluated on CIFAR-10/100, transfer to CUB and MIT Scenes, and ImageNet across architectures; attention transfer consistently improves students.  
- Reports activation-based AT outperforms regressing full activations; gradient-based AT and a flip-symmetry regularizer provide extra CIFAR gains; code/models released.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main rejection reason: novelty is incremental relative to prior hint/feature transfer and knowledge distillation methods.  
- Empirical evidence missing key quantitative analyses and practical guidance (no quantitative validation of attention–accuracy correlation, limited hyperparameter sensitivity, unclear attention-definition trade-offs).  
- Motivation and literature placement are clear, but the paper doesn’t convincingly justify why spatial attention summaries are fundamentally preferable to full-feature matching.  
- Support for claims is partial: students improve, but claims like “better models have better attention” are only shown qualitatively.  
- Insufficient analysis of attention loss weight (beta), layer selection, attention mapping choices, and normalization, hindering reproducibility and recommendations.  
- The student never surpasses the teacher and the paper lacks characterization of practical resource/efficiency benefits (speed, FLOPs, latency, memory).  
- No distilled, actionable guidance on choosing attention mappings (sum vs max, exponent p) from experiments.  
- Suggestion: provide quantitative evidence linking attention maps to ground-truth object regions (IoU, pointing game, energy-in-box) across models.  
- Suggestion: include sensitivity studies for beta and scaling across datasets/architectures and recommend practical defaults.  
- Suggestion: summarize guidance on attention definitions (when to prefer sum vs max and which p) in a concise table.  
- Suggestion: report teacher training/validation curves and accuracy to contextualize student–teacher gaps and KD behavior.  
- Suggestion: quantify student efficiency benefits (wall-clock speed, FLOPs, memory, latency) corresponding to accuracy trade-offs.  
- Suggestion: clarify benefits when teacher and student share architecture/depth and whether AT helps beyond KD on large-scale data.  
- Suggestion: expand implementation guidance for gradient-based AT with batch normalization and the second backprop pass.  
- Questions: can you quantify the attention-quality vs accuracy claim (mass inside GT boxes, by how much)?  
- Questions: how were beta and other hyperparameters chosen and how sensitive are results; are there robust defaults?  
- Questions: what criteria guide choosing Fsum, F^2sum, F^pmax and p; when did max-based maps help?  
- Questions: add teacher curves for Fig.7b, report cases where AT hurts, effect of architectural mismatch, impact of other augmentations/symmetry constraints, and end-to-end throughput/latency for students.
