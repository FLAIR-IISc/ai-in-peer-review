- Paper proposes transferring “where to look” by matching spatial attention maps from teacher to student to improve student CNN accuracy.
- Defines two attention map types: activation-based (channel aggregation like sum/max with exponent) and gradient-based (input sensitivity via loss gradients).
- Introduces simple losses that align student attention maps to l2-normalized teacher maps at selected layers.
- Activation-based AT is easy to implement, low-overhead, and compatible with knowledge distillation (KD).
- Empirical gains shown across CIFAR-10/100 (ResNets/Wide ResNets), ImageNet (ResNet-18 student, ResNet-34 teacher), and transfer to CUB-200 and MIT Indoor Scenes.
- Activation-based AT outperforms full feature regression and often complements or surpasses KD.
- Gradient-based AT relates to double backpropagation with a symmetry regularizer and yields improvements on CIFAR.
- Decision: Accept — simple, well-motivated, broadly useful method with strong empirical evidence including ImageNet.
- Strong evidence that spatial aggregation of activations is a more effective transfer signal than regressing full activations.
- Paper is well-situated between model compression/distillation and saliency/attention visualization literature (e.g., CAM, Grad-CAM).
- Thorough CIFAR ablations: map definitions (sum vs max, exponent choices), layer selection, comparisons to KD and feature regression.
- Gradient-based AT feels somewhat orthogonal and is not fully compared head-to-head with activation-based AT under identical protocols.
- Terminology caveat: “attention” here means spatial activation/gradient statistics, not learned parametric attention; should be clarified.
- Notation ambiguity: Eq. (2) lacks explicit definition of vec(·) and the norm; clarification needed on per-sample and per-layer normalization.
- Suggest clarifying that after l2-normalizing, l2 distance is equivalent to 2(1−cosine) and consider reporting cosine loss.
- Recommend integrating gradient-based results with activation-based comparisons and testing their combination.
- Request reporting of computational overhead (wall-clock, memory) for activation- and gradient-based AT, especially with multiple loss sites.
- Recommend more ablations on exponent p, sum vs max, layer choices, normalization type (l1 vs l2), and β scaling/scheduling.
- KD interactions: KD reportedly harms convergence on ImageNet for ResNet-18/34; authors should sweep KD hyperparameters and report KD+AT results or explain the issue.
- Suggest stating scope explicitly (targets CNNs with spatial feature maps) and discuss extensions to non-convolutional models or tasks (detection/segmentation).
- Presentation fixes: unify notation, tighten references, and consider moving some gradient-based material to appendix if not central.
- Question: Is ||·|| in Eq. (2) the Euclidean/Frobenius norm over the vectorized H×W map, and did you try cosine loss explicitly?
- Question: How are β values chosen and scheduled across layers, and is the provided scaling crucial for new architectures?
- Question: What is the runtime and memory overhead per training step for activation-based AT and the extra cost for gradient-based second backprop?
- Question: How is batch normalization handled in the second backprop for gradient-based AT (BN stats/affine updates)?
- Question: Can you report combined activation- and gradient-based AT results on CIFAR and larger datasets?
- Question: Why does KD hinder convergence on ImageNet for ResNet-18/34; were different temperatures/α or calibration methods explored?
- Question: How sensitive are results to exponent p and sum vs max aggregation, and do preferences generalize across datasets?
- Question: How robust is AT to teacher quality—does a marginally better or worse teacher help or harm the student?
- Question: Any qualitative failure modes where attention alignment causes the student to focus on spurious regions?
