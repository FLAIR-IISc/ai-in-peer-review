- Proposes guiding student networks by matching teacher attention maps instead of regressing full activations, focusing on where the network attends.  
- Recommendation: Reject.  
- Main reasons for rejection: reported gains over standard knowledge distillation (KD) are modest and the ImageNet evaluation is underdeveloped.  
- Experiments do not convincingly show a clear advantage over strong KD baselines; improvements are limited.  
- ImageNet section (4.2.2) is too brief and lacks breadth and detailed analysis to demonstrate robustness and generality.  
- Suggest expanding ImageNet experiments with multiple teacher–student pairs, stronger baselines, and careful hyperparameter tuning.  
- Recommend direct, apples-to-apples comparisons against KD with clear reporting of absolute and relative gains.  
- Ask for ablations to identify when attention maps help most (loss placement, attention variants, training schedules).  
- Request reporting of statistical variability (multiple runs) and training dynamics to verify consistency of improvements.  
- Advise quantifying computational overhead and training stability relative to KD to clarify practical trade-offs.  
- Reviewer would reconsider decision if ImageNet results are substantially strengthened.  
- Question: Can the authors expand ImageNet experiments to isolate attention transfer benefits under matched hyperparameters and budgets?  
- Question: Across datasets, how often and under what conditions does attention transfer meaningfully outperform KD?  
- Question: Which attention map configurations and layer choices drive gains, and how sensitive are results to these choices?  
- Question: How does computational cost and training stability compare to KD in large-scale settings (memory, extra passes)?  
- Question: If gains are mainly in convergence speed, can authors provide learning curves and evidence of wall-clock advantage?
