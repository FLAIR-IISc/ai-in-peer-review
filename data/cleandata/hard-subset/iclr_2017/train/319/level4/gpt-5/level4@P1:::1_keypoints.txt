- Paper proposes attention transfer (AT): match spatial attention maps (activation- or gradient-based) from teacher to student via explicit losses.  
- Defines multiple attention mappings (sum, powered sum, max), emphasizes normalization, and combines AT with knowledge distillation (KD).  
- Empirical results show consistent gains on CIFAR, CUB, scenes, and +1.1% top-1 on ImageNet (ResNet-18 student, ResNet-34 teacher).  
- Activation-based AT outperforms full-activation regression; gradient-based AT can be implemented via double backprop with regularization benefits.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main reason: empirical improvements over strong KD baselines are modest and inconclusive.  
- Main reason: ImageNet evidence is too limited and insufficiently compared to tuned KD or other baselines.  
- Comparisons to KD: AT’s gains are not convincingly larger or robust; KD often performs similarly and statistical significance is lacking.  
- ImageNet evaluation: only two AT losses used, no tuning, lacks matched training-budget KD baselines, and omits reported results for proposed losses on all groups.  
- Gradient-based AT: evaluated only on CIFAR with a thin NIN without BN and restricted setup, leaving practical value unclear.  
- Ablations are limited: need more sensitivity analyses (β schedules, layer placement, p values, normalization, interpolation across spatial sizes).  
- Reporting gaps: missing multiple runs, confidence intervals, training curves, and compute/memory overhead comparisons.  
- Recommendation: add AT losses to all residual groups and report per-group effects on ImageNet.  
- Recommendation: provide tuned KD baselines (temperature/α sweeps, label smoothing), FitNets-style hints, and full-activation regression under matched budgets.  
- Recommendation: report multiple runs with error bars, training curves, and AT vs KD compute/memory overhead.  
- Clarify and justify normalization choice and β schedule; include sensitivity analyses and interpolation behavior for mismatched spatial sizes.  
- For gradient-based AT: resolve BN handling in second backprop (e.g., fix BN stats/params) and evaluate on BN-equipped architectures and larger-scale tasks.  
- Add systematic qualitative comparisons of teacher vs student attention before/after AT and relate them to accuracy gains.  
- Improve reproducibility by listing all hyperparameters (temperature, α, β per layer, schedules, augmentations).  
- Key question: How does AT compare to a well-tuned KD baseline under matched compute and augmentation on ImageNet?  
- Key question: What is the effect of placing AT losses on each residual group individually versus all groups together?  
- Key question: How sensitive are results to attention mapping choice (Fsum vs F^2sum vs F^pmax) and exponent p on large-scale models?  
- Key question: What are the training-time and memory overheads of AT versus KD and full-activation transfer when using multiple AT losses?  
- Key question: How should BN be handled in the gradient-based second backprop, and what are the trade-offs of fixing BN stats/parameters?  
- Key question: Can you provide error bars across multiple runs for CIFAR and ImageNet to assess statistical significance?  
- Key question: Do AT gains persist for identical teacher/student architectures or when the student is wider but shallower (e.g., WRN variants)?
