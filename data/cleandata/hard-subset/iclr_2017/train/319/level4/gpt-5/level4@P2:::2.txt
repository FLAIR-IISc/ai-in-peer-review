Summary of contributions
- The paper studies how to transfer “where to look” information from a high-performing teacher CNN to a student CNN. It formalizes attention as spatial maps derived from internal representations, proposes two families of attention maps (activation-based and gradient-based), and trains students to match the teacher’s attention.
- For activation-based attention, the paper aggregates channel activations at each spatial location (e.g., sum or sum of powers) and adds losses that encourage the student’s normalized attention maps to match the teacher’s at selected layers.
- For gradient-based attention, it treats the input gradient of the loss as an attention signal and introduces a double-backprop objective to align student and teacher input sensitivities; a symmetry regularizer (horizontal flip consistency) is also proposed.
- Empirically, activation-based attention transfer consistently improves student accuracy across CIFAR, CUB, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, often outperforming FitNets-style hints and full activation matching, and can complement knowledge distillation. The method is simple to implement and code is released.

Decision
Weak accept. Primary reason: clear and consistent empirical gains across multiple datasets and architectures with a simple, well-motivated transfer mechanism; remaining issues are mostly clarity and organization and seem addressable.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets a concrete question—can we transfer spatial focus from a teacher to a student? This is well situated relative to knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., FitNets) and attention/saliency literature (CAM, Grad-CAM, guided backprop).
- Method: The activation-based formulation is straightforward, architecture-friendly for CNNs, and decouples attention from full activation transfer. The normalization in the attention loss is sensible and likely key to stability. The proposed layerwise placement rules (same vs different depth) are practical.
- Empirical support: On CIFAR, activation-based AT improves several teacher–student pairs and beats full-activation transfer in final accuracy while preserving faster convergence. On CUB and MIT Scenes, AT narrows the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34, indicating value in fine-grained and scene tasks. On ImageNet, adding only two AT losses yields notable top-1/top-5 gains for ResNet-18 from a ResNet-34 teacher, which is a strong result given the difficulty of distillation on ImageNet.
- Limitations: The “attention” terminology might overstate novelty, as the core signal is a channel-wise spatial aggregation of activations; still, the framing is reasonable and the empirical benefits are clear. The gradient-based section feels under-integrated: it is evaluated in a restricted setup, not combined with activation-based AT, and comparisons are limited. Several notational details (e.g., norms in Eq. 2) need clarification.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not central to the accept decision)
- Clarify terminology: Explicitly define “attention” here as spatial aggregation of hidden activations or input gradients, and distinguish it from class-specific attention (e.g., CAM/Grad-CAM). This will reduce potential confusion.
- Notation and norms: In Eq. (2), please clarify that the per-map normalization is the vector l2 norm after vectorization and that the loss uses the Euclidean distance between normalized vectors. If by “‖·‖2” you mean the Euclidean/Frobenius norm (not spectral/induced 2-norm), state this unambiguously.
- Organize the gradient-based section: Either (a) integrate it by comparing head-to-head with activation-based AT under matched settings and/or combining the two losses, or (b) move it to an appendix as an exploratory direction. Clarify batch-norm handling during second backprop.
- Ablations and design choices: Summarize effects of p in Fpsum, sum vs max aggregation, number/placement of AT losses, and the importance of l2 normalization. Report sensitivity to β and to combining AT with KD (e.g., scheduling β).
- Computational overhead: Quantify extra compute/memory for both activation-based AT (negligible, but specify) and gradient-based AT (roughly one extra backward pass). Note any training-time slowdowns and no inference-time cost.
- Scope and limitations: State that the current formulation is tailored to convolutional architectures with spatial maps; discuss how one might adapt to non-convolutional models or sequence models, or why that is out of scope.
- Visualizations: Include side-by-side teacher/student attention maps pre/post AT for multiple layers and classes to illustrate qualitative alignment.

Questions for the authors
1. Eq. (2) norm: Do you intend the Euclidean (Frobenius) norm on vectorized attention maps, not the induced spectral norm? Please confirm and adjust notation to avoid implying largest singular value computation.
2. Normalization: Why l2 normalization rather than l1 or temperature scaling? Did you compare these, and how sensitive is performance to the choice?
3. Aggregation choices: What empirical trends did you observe across Fsum, F2sum, F4sum vs Fmax? Does a larger p risk overfitting to small peaks? Any benefits to softmax-normalized attention?
4. Layer selection: How does performance vary with AT applied to early, middle, and late layers? Is there diminishing return from adding more AT sites?
5. KD interaction: When AT and KD are combined, how do you schedule β and KD temperature/α? Any cases where KD hurts but AT helps (e.g., ImageNet) and why?
6. Gradient-based AT: How exactly do you handle batch normalization and other stateful layers during the second backprop? Can you report results with batch norm on CIFAR to enable direct comparison to activation-based AT?
7. Compute costs: What is the wall-clock overhead of activation-based AT and gradient-based AT relative to baseline training for the main models (CIFAR ResNets, ImageNet ResNet-18)?
8. Generality: Have you tried AT on tasks where spatial alignment is crucial (e.g., detection or segmentation) or on architectures with attention modules? Any failure cases?

Overall, this is a clear and practically useful contribution to knowledge transfer in CNNs. With improved clarity around terminology/notation and a tighter treatment of the gradient-based variant, the paper will be stronger.
