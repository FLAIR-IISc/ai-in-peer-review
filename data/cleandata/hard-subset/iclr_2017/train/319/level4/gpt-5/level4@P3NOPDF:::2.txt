Summary
This paper proposes a variant of knowledge distillation that transfers “attention” from a teacher to a student by matching spatial statistics of intermediate feature maps. Concretely, the student is encouraged to align with teacher-generated activation summaries (e.g., sums of squared activations or more generally Lp norms across channels or spatial dimensions). The authors also introduce a gradient-based variant that uses gradients with respect to inputs as attention signals. Empirically, the method outperforms a FitNets-style distillation baseline on multiple vision datasets.

Strengths
- Clear, simple idea that is easy to implement and appears to provide consistent gains over a strong baseline.
- Evaluated across multiple computer vision datasets and architectures, suggesting reasonable robustness within convolutional settings.
- Writing is generally clear, and the empirical section demonstrates practical benefits.

Weaknesses and Concerns
- Terminology: The use of “attention” is misleading. The method mainly matches activation-energy summaries (e.g., summed squared activations or Lp norms) rather than learning or transferring a conventional attention mechanism. If the intention is to argue that these summaries act as spatial attention maps, stronger justification and analysis are needed.
- Scope: The approach appears tailored to convolutional architectures with spatial feature maps. It is unclear how to extend the technique to non-convolutional models or to 1D/sequence modalities without ad hoc adaptations.
- Gradient-based attention transfer: This component feels out-of-place. It is not rigorously compared to nor integrated with the proposed activation-based transfer. As presented, it reads as a parallel idea with limited added value. Either integrate it more tightly (joint training, ablations on complementarity) or move it to an appendix with focused comparisons.
- Notation/ambiguity: Equation (2) refers to the “induced 2-norm” of Q ∈ R^{H×W}. If this is the spectral norm (largest singular value), computing it during training is costly, and the paper does not describe the approximation or computational overhead. If the intent is the Frobenius norm, please state this explicitly. More generally, the paper should precisely define over which axes statistics are computed and how normalization is performed.
- Analysis depth: The empirical study would benefit from more ablations:
  - Choice of statistic (p in Lp norm), and whether per-channel, per-spatial, or channel-summed summaries work best.
  - Normalization schemes (e.g., L1/L2 normalization of attention maps, temperature or scaling).
  - Placement and number of transfer points, and sensitivity to teacher–student depth mismatch.
  - Interaction with standard logit distillation: additive benefits, trade-offs, and hyperparameter robustness.

Questions for the authors
- Which norm is actually used in Eq. (2)? If spectral, how is it computed or approximated (e.g., power iteration), and what is the runtime/memory overhead? If Frobenius, please correct the terminology and clarify.
- How exactly are attention maps formed? Are activations first aggregated over channels or spatially, then normalized? At what stage is normalization applied (per-sample vs. batch)?
- What loss weighting is used between attention transfer and the primary task/distillation losses? How sensitive are results to this weight across datasets and architectures?
- Do you backpropagate through the teacher network? If not, please state that the teacher features/gradients are detached.
- How many layers receive the transfer loss, and how were these layers selected? Is there a performance trade-off with using very shallow vs. deeper layers?
- For the gradient-based method, what targets are used, how stable is training, and can it be combined with activation-based transfer to yield additive gains? A direct, controlled comparison would help.

Suggestions for improvement
- Either justify the “attention” terminology with evidence (e.g., qualitative visualizations that correlate strongly with human-interpretable saliency or attribution) or rename to something like “activation summary transfer” to avoid confusion.
- Consolidate the two transfer mechanisms: present a unified objective, show whether they are complementary, or relegate the gradient-based approach to supplementary material with a clear comparison.
- Clarify all notation and axes of aggregation. Replace “induced 2-norm” with the exact norm used, and provide implementation details for any expensive operations.
- Include computational cost (FLOPs, wall-clock) and memory overhead for different numbers of transfer points.
- Add ablations on Lp choice, normalization, layer selection, and teacher–student capacity gap. Consider experiments showing whether the method combines with standard logit distillation beyond FitNets.

Overall assessment
This is a practical and effective distillation technique with solid empirical results within convolutional vision models. However, the framing around “attention,” the unclear norm specification, and the disconnected gradient-based section detract from clarity and impact. With tighter terminology, clearer notation, and stronger analyses/ablations—including a principled treatment of the gradient-based variant—this could be a strong contribution.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying Eq. (2), tightening the terminology and organization, and adding targeted ablations. Confidence: Medium.
