- Proposal: Introduces attention transfer (AT) to train student CNNs to match teacher spatial attention maps (activation- and gradient-based), aligning l2-normalized maps and optionally combining with KD plus a flip-invariance gradient regularizer.  
- Empirical claims: AT improves student accuracy on CIFAR-10, outperforms full-activation matching, can combine with KD, helps transfer to CUB-200/MIT Scenes, and gives +1.1% top-1 on ImageNet ResNet-18 (code released).  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main reason 1: ImageNet evidence is too limited—sparse experiments, no ablations, no tuned KD or modern distillation baselines.  
- Main reason 2: Gradient-based AT is only shown in a simplified setting (no BN, no standard augmentations), so its practicality with modern architectures is unclear.  
- Motivation and positioning: Problem is well-motivated; AT is simple, architecture-agnostic, cheap (activation-based), and situated appropriately relative to FitNets, KD, and visualization work.  
- CIFAR results: Consistent gains across WRN/ResNet pairs; sum-based attention variants outperform max; normalization is important—supports spatial attention as supervision.  
- ImageNet limitations: Only ResNet-18 evaluated with two AT losses, no hyperparameter tuning, no multiple runs/confidence intervals, and inconclusive KD comparisons.  
- Gradient-AT limitations: Demonstrated only on thin NIN without BN/augmentations, with hand-tuned training choices; second-order backprop cost and stability with BN/deeper nets untested.  
- Experimental rigor: Many results are single runs or medians without variability; key design choices (β schedule, layer selection, p for power-sums) lack systematic analysis.  
- Regularization claim: Flip symmetry regularizer is interesting but under-validated across settings.  
- Reproducibility concerns: Important implementation/training specifics (budgets, augmentations, teacher capacity, KD hyperparameters) are insufficiently detailed for fair comparisons, especially on ImageNet.  
- Suggested ImageNet improvements: run multiple seeds/error bars; tune KD (temperature, α); compare to FitNets and modern KD variants; ablate AT placement, attention function, and normalization.  
- Suggested reporting: Provide compute/throughput and memory overhead for AT; quantify double-backprop cost and BN handling for gradient-AT; show attention similarity metrics.  
- Gradient-AT suggestions: Demonstrate on BN-equipped architectures with modern training pipelines; clarify second backprop/Bn interaction and compare to Jacobian/gradient penalties.  
- Fairness/robustness suggestions: Vary teacher strength, report calibration (ECE), robustness to corruptions, and training dynamics to show whether AT stabilizes or regularizes learning.  
- Broader evaluations: Test on detection/segmentation or weakly supervised localization and explore alternative attention maps (e.g., CAM/Grad-CAM, channel/spatial variants).  
- Key reproducibility/analysis gaps: Need systematic ablations for normalization choice (l1 vs l2), power p in attention, layer selection, and β scheduling.  
- Questions for authors (summary): sensitivity to number/placement of AT losses and normalization; tuned KD comparisons and budgets; best attention function on ImageNet; BN handling and overhead for gradient-AT; justification/sensitivity of l2 normalization and β schedule; dependence on teacher–student mismatch; effects on calibration/robustness.  
- Reconsideration conditions: Would reconsider if ImageNet study is substantially expanded with tuned baselines, ablations, multiple runs, and if gradient-AT is shown on BN-equipped architectures with clear compute cost reporting.
