- Paper proposes transferring attention from teacher to student by minimizing L2 distance between selected-layer attention maps alongside classification loss (and optional KD).
- Defines activation-based attentions (channel aggregation via sum or max of |features|^p) and a gradient-based attention (loss derivative w.r.t. input).
- Experiments on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet show attention transfer consistently improves student test accuracy versus baseline training.
- Even with attention transfer, students do not exceed teacher accuracy.
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks show higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps; Figure 4 is visually compelling but lacks quantitative support.
- Hyperparameter selection is not detailed; the impact and sensitivity of β (attention loss weight) are not reported.
- Figure 7(b) should include the teacher’s training and validation loss for proper comparison.
- Practical trade-offs between attention definitions are unclear; guidance is needed on when to prefer activation-based vs. gradient-based attention and the effect of p or max vs. sum.
- No characterization of computational speed-up (FLOPs, latency, throughput, memory) for smaller student models to contextualize accuracy loss.
- Unclear whether attention transfer provides benefits (regularization or faster/better convergence) when teacher and student share the same architecture.
- Strengths: clearly written, well motivated, and produces consistent student improvements with attention transfer.
- Weaknesses: students still underperform teachers, unclear guidance on attention formulation, and novelty seems incremental relative to FitNets.
