This paper investigates transferring attention from a teacher to a student network. The core idea is to encourage the student to match the teacher’s attention maps at intermediate layers by minimizing the L2 distance between them, in addition to the standard classification loss and, optionally, a knowledge distillation term.

The authors define several activation-based attention mechanisms (e.g., the sum of absolute feature values raised to the power p, or the maximum of feature values raised to the power p). They also propose a gradient-based attention mechanism based on the derivative of the loss with respect to the inputs.

Experiments on several datasets (CIFAR, CUB/Scene, ImageNet) show that attention transfer helps improve the student network’s test performance compared to training the student alone. However, even with attention transfer, the student networks perform worse than the teacher.

Remarks/questions:
- In Section 3, the authors claim that networks with higher accuracy exhibit higher spatial correlation between the object and the attention map. While Figure 4 is compelling, it would be valuable to include quantitative results to substantiate this claim (e.g., a correlation metric across models/datasets).
- How were the hyperparameters chosen? In particular, please report the impact of β and provide a sensitivity analysis.
- In Figure 7b, it would be helpful to report the teacher’s training and validation losses for comparison.
- From the experiments, the pros and cons of the different attention maps are not clear. Please clarify when activation-based vs. gradient-based attention is preferable, and how the choice of p (in the activation-based variants) affects performance and stability.
- Attention transfer (AT) does not yield better results than the teacher. However, the student networks have fewer parameters. Please characterize the corresponding speed-up (e.g., training and inference time, memory), and discuss practical trade-offs. If the student and teacher share the same architecture, is there any benefit to attention transfer?

In summary:
Pros:
- Clearly written and well motivated.
- Consistent improvement of the student with attention transfer compared to the student trained in isolation.

Cons:
- Students perform worse than the teacher models.
- It is unclear which attention mechanism to use in which setting and why.
- The novelty appears somewhat incremental relative to FitNets.

Overall, the paper presents a simple and intuitive approach to leveraging teacher attention to guide student learning, with empirical gains across multiple datasets. Strengthening the quantitative analysis of the claimed attention–object correlation, clarifying the trade-offs among attention types, and providing a more thorough hyperparameter and efficiency study would significantly improve the paper.
