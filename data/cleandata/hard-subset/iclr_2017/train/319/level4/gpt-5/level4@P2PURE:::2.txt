The paper proposes a modified knowledge distillation framework that minimizes the discrepancy between the teacher and student by matching sums of statistics computed over feature maps. The authors empirically demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform a FitNets-style distillation baseline.

Pros:
- The authors evaluate the proposed methods on multiple computer vision datasets.
- The paper is generally well written.

Cons:
- The method appears limited to convolutional architectures.
- The use of the term “attention” is misleading. The method effectively distills the summed squared values (or other statistics, e.g., summed lp norms) of activations in hidden feature maps.
- The gradient-based attention transfer component seems out of place. It is neither directly compared to nor used jointly with the “attention-based” transfer. It reads as a parallel idea appended to the paper that does not add much value.
- It is unclear how the induced 2-norms in Eq. (2) are computed. Q is a matrix in R^{H×W}, whose induced 2-norm is its largest singular value. Computing this within the loss seems expensive. Perhaps the authors actually mean the Frobenius norm?

Overall, the proposed distillation method performs well in practice, but the paper has organizational issues and some unclear notation.
