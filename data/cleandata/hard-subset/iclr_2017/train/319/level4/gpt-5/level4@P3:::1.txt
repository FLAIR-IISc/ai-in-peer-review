Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a knowledge transfer mechanism, in which a student CNN is trained to match the teacher’s spatial attention maps. Two families of attention are defined: activation-based (channel-wise statistics such as sum of absolute activations or power-sum across channels) and gradient-based (input-sensitivity maps). The loss aligns l2-normalized attention maps across selected layers, optionally combined with knowledge distillation (KD). A flip-invariance regularizer for gradients is also introduced.
- Empirically, AT improves student accuracy on CIFAR-10 across several teacher–student pairs (WRNs and ResNets), outperforms matching full activations, and can be combined with KD. Gradient-based AT is demonstrated on CIFAR with a thin NIN. On transfer learning (CUB-200 and MIT Scenes), AT narrows the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34. On ImageNet, AT adds 1.1% top-1 to ResNet-18 with a ResNet-34 teacher. Code is released.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) The empirical evidence on large-scale settings (ImageNet) is too limited to support the central claim that attention transfer consistently and substantially improves over strong baselines. The ImageNet experiments are sparse, lack ablations, and do not include tuned KD or other distillation baselines.
2) The gradient-based AT results are constrained to a simplified setting (no batch normalization, no random cropping), leaving unclear whether the method is practical and competitive in modern architectures and training regimes.

Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled and motivation: The paper addresses how to transfer where a network “looks,” rather than what intermediate features it produces, which is well-motivated by the literature on attention visualization and knowledge distillation. The activation-based attention definitions are simple, architecture-agnostic, and computationally cheap; the gradient-based formulation builds on double backpropagation. Positioning relative to FitNets, KD, Grad-CAM/CAM, and visualization work is appropriate.
- Empirical support on small datasets: On CIFAR-10, AT yields consistent gains across WRN/ResNet pairs and convincingly outperforms full-activation regression. The ablation on attention mapping functions is informative (sum-based variants > max-based), and normalization appears important. These results support the claim that spatial attention is a useful supervision signal.
- Limitations of empirical support:
  - ImageNet: Only ResNet-18 with two AT losses is reported, without hyperparameter tuning, multiple runs, confidence intervals, or ablations (number/placement of AT losses, choice of attention function, normalization type). KD is reported as underperforming but seemingly not tuned, which makes the comparison inconclusive. The strong claim that this is the first successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet is not substantiated with thorough comparisons to modern KD variants or FitNets-like methods adapted to ResNets.
  - Gradient-based AT: Demonstrated only on a thin NIN without BN and without common data augmentation, with hand-tuned training choices (e.g., turning off weight decay). The second-order backprop cost, behavior with batch normalization, and stability on deeper/wider architectures are not evaluated. As a result, the general utility of gradient-based AT remains unclear.
  - Rigor: Many results are reported as single runs or medians without variability; several design choices (e.g., β scheduling, layer selection, p for power-sums) are not systematically analyzed. Claims of regularization (flip symmetry) are intriguing but need broader validation.
- Scientific correctness and clarity: The losses are well defined and the proposed normalizations are sensible. The discussion on placement of losses in same/deeper networks is reasonable. However, several implementation specifics that affect reproducibility and fairness (training budgets, augmentation, teacher capacity, temperature and α for KD across datasets) are insufficiently detailed for key comparisons (especially on ImageNet).

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all required for acceptance)
- Strengthen the ImageNet section:
  - Include multiple runs with error bars; tune KD (temperature, α) and report strong KD baselines; compare to FitNets/hints adapted to ResNets and to recent KD variants.
  - Ablate number and placement of AT losses (all four stages vs last two), attention function (Fsum vs F2sum vs F4sum), and normalization (l1 vs l2).
  - Report compute/throughput and memory overhead for AT; for gradient-based AT, quantify the cost of double backprop and discuss BN handling.
  - Show attention similarity metrics (e.g., cosine, IoU after thresholding) alongside accuracy.
- Gradient-based AT:
  - Demonstrate with batch-normalized architectures and modern training pipelines. Clarify how second-order backprop interacts with BN (frozen stats vs updating; whether a separate forward pass is used).
  - Compare to gradient penalties/min-l2 and Jacobian/norm regularizers across datasets.
- Fairness and robustness:
  - Control for teacher strength (vary teacher accuracy) and analyze how student gains scale with teacher quality and mismatch in depth/width.
  - Report calibration metrics (ECE), robustness to corruptions, and training dynamics to understand whether AT primarily stabilizes or regularizes learning.
- Broader scope:
  - Evaluate on detection/segmentation or weakly supervised localization, where spatial attention should be most impactful.
  - Explore alternative attention definitions, e.g., CAM/Grad-CAM-derived maps, and channel/spatial attention variants.

Questions for the authors
1) ImageNet: What is the sensitivity of AT gains to the number and placement of AT losses? Do gains increase when placing losses in all stages? Did you try l1 normalization?
2) KD baseline: After tuning temperature and α for ImageNet, how does a strong KD baseline compare to AT and AT+KD? Could you report wall-clock and epoch budgets to ensure fair comparisons?
3) Attention mapping: On ImageNet, which attention function performed best (Fsum vs F2sum vs F4sum), and how does the choice of p affect results?
4) Gradient-based AT: How do you handle batch normalization in the second backprop pass? Have you tested gradient-based AT with BN-enabled ResNets, and what is the compute overhead relative to baseline?
5) Normalization: Can you justify theoretically or empirically why l2 normalization of attention maps is crucial? How sensitive are results to the β schedule?
6) Teacher–student mismatch: How do gains vary with teacher depth/width differences? Can AT help when the student is deeper but narrower, or vice versa?
7) Generalization: Do AT-trained students show improved calibration or robustness (e.g., to distribution shifts or corruptions)? Are the improvements uniform across classes or concentrated where localization is challenging?

I would be inclined to reconsider if the authors significantly expand the ImageNet study with tuned baselines, ablations, and multiple runs, and if gradient-based AT is demonstrated in modern BN-equipped architectures with a clear account of computational cost.
