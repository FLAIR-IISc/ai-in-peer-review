- Proposal: Introduces attention transfer (AT) to match spatial attention maps between teacher and student CNNs using activation- and gradient-based attention, plus standard classification loss and optional KD.
- Implementation: Defines normalized attention-map losses, explores layer placement, and shows AT can combine with KD with little overhead.
- Empirical claim: Activation-based AT consistently improves student performance across CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet; gradient-based AT is less robust.
- Code: Authors release code for reproducibility.
- Decision: Accept (weak accept).

- Reason 1: Practical impact and generality — simple, architecture-agnostic loss that improves diverse student models, including ImageNet cases.
- Reason 2: Empirical support — reproducible improvements and clear ablations demonstrating attention’s value over full-feature transfer.

- Problem/motivation: Well-motivated intersection of attention visualization and knowledge transfer; transferring spatial saliency is a compelling and novel direction.
- Method soundness: Activation-based definitions and attention normalization are sensible; gradient-based AT appropriately linked to double backpropagation.
- Empirical rigor: Experiments cover multiple architectures/datasets, include KD and full-activation baselines, and show activation AT outperforms full-feature transfer.
- Scope/limitations: Students don’t exceed teachers (expected); gradient-based AT limited to small datasets due to second-order issues with batch norm.

- Suggestion: Quantify attention–object alignment (e.g., bounding-box IoU, CAM/Grad-CAM metrics) across teachers and students.
- Suggestion: Report sensitivity to β, normalization choice (L1 vs L2), and schedules when combined with KD; ablate number/placement of AT losses.
- Suggestion: Summarize pros/cons of sum vs max and different p values, and explain why sum variants work better.
- Suggestion: Characterize computational trade-offs — training overhead, FLOPs, and inference speedups for representative student–teacher pairs.
- Suggestion: Analyze KD failures on ImageNet (temperature, α, label smoothing, architecture matches) and contrast with AT’s robustness.
- Suggestion: Clarify handling of batch normalization in gradient-based AT and evaluate fixes (freeze BN stats, ghost batches); test symmetry regularizer beyond CIFAR.
- Suggestion: Tone down or precisely scope claims like “first successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet” and compare to other distillation/compression methods.

- Question 1: Can you provide quantitative evidence that higher-accuracy models have attention better aligned with objects across layers?
- Question 2: How sensitive are results to β, attention normalization (L1 vs L2), and number/placement of AT losses; is there a simple heuristic for β?
- Question 3: What are the training and inference time/compute trade-offs and speedups (FLOPs/latency) for typical student–teacher pairs, and the extra training cost due to AT?
- Question 4: Do students ever surpass teachers when capacities are matched (same architecture/depth), indicating a regularization effect of AT?
- Question 5: Why do sum-based attention maps outperform max-based ones — do max maps overemphasize noisy peaks or is the effect dataset/architecture dependent?
- Question 6: For KD on ImageNet, which temperatures and α values were tried; did you try label smoothing or intermediate feature/logit matching, or same-architecture teachers?
- Question 7: For gradient-based AT with batch norm, how should the second-derivative pass be handled (freeze BN stats, recompute, etc.) and what did you try?
- Question 8: Can you report teacher train/validation loss and accuracy curves alongside the student to contextualize convergence and speed?
