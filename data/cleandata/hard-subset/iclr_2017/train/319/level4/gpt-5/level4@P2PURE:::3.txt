This paper investigates transferring attention between a teacher and a student network. The transfer is implemented by minimizing the L2 distance between the teacher’s and student’s attention maps at multiple layers, in addition to the standard classification loss and, optionally, a knowledge distillation term. The authors define several activation-based attention mechanisms (e.g., summing absolute feature activations raised to the power p, or taking the maximum of activations raised to the power p), and they also propose a gradient-based attention defined as the derivative of the loss with respect to the inputs. Experiments on several datasets (CIFAR, CUB/Scene, ImageNet) show that attention transfer improves the student network’s test performance; however, even with attention, the student networks perform worse than the teacher.

Remarks/questions:
- In Section 3, the authors claim that higher-accuracy networks exhibit higher spatial correlation between the object and the attention map. While Figure 4 is compelling, it would be helpful to include quantitative results to substantiate this claim.
- How were the hyperparameters chosen? It would be useful to report the sensitivity to beta.
- Please report the teacher’s training and validation loss in Figure 7b.
- From the experiments, it is not clear what the pros and cons are for the different attention maps.
- AT does not outperform the teacher. However, the student networks have fewer parameters; it would be helpful to quantify the corresponding speed-up. If the student and teacher share the same architecture, is there any benefit to attention transfer?

In summary:
Pros:
- Clearly written and well motivated.
- Consistent improvement of the student with attention compared to the student alone.

Cons:
- Students perform worse than the teacher models.
- It is unclear which attention mechanism to use in which setting.
- The novelty appears somewhat incremental relative to FitNet.
