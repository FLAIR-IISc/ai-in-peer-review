Summary of contributions
The paper studies how to transfer “attention” from a teacher CNN to a student CNN to improve the student’s accuracy. The core idea is to augment the standard classification loss (and optionally knowledge distillation) with losses that encourage the student’s attention maps to match the teacher’s at selected layers. The authors define two families of attention:
- Activation-based: spatial maps computed from hidden activations via channel-wise statistics (sum or max of absolute activations raised to a power p).
- Gradient-based: input sensitivity maps defined as the gradient of the loss with respect to the input, optimized via a double backpropagation procedure; they also introduce a horizontal-flip invariance regularizer on these gradient maps.
They report experiments on CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, demonstrating consistent gains for students trained with attention transfer (AT) over the same students trained without AT. Activation-based AT generally outperforms transferring full activations and can be combined with knowledge distillation. The empirical analysis highlights where AT helps, and provides implementation details that make the approach practical.

Decision
Accept (weak accept), primarily because:
- The approach is simple, well motivated, and broadly applicable; it yields consistent improvements across architectures and datasets, including ImageNet.
- Activation-based AT provides clearer and stronger gains than full activation transfer and is complementary to knowledge distillation.

Supporting arguments
- Problem: The paper addresses whether attention information from a strong teacher can guide a student to focus on relevant spatial regions, improving its performance. This question is important for model compression and understanding CNNs.
- Motivation and placement in the literature: The work builds on attention visualization (e.g., saliency maps, CAM/Grad-CAM) and knowledge distillation/FitNets. The paper argues that attention maps capture crucial spatial cues and that matching these is a more targeted supervisory signal than matching full activations. This is a reasonable and well situated hypothesis.
- Evidence and rigor: On CIFAR-10, several teacher–student pairs (same and different depths) show consistent improvements with AT, and AT+KD further helps in many cases. Activation-based AT outperforms full activation transfer both in convergence and final accuracy. Gradient-based AT and the symmetry regularizer improve performance in a controlled NIN setup. On CUB and Scenes, AT narrows the accuracy gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34. On ImageNet, AT yields 1.1% top-1 and 0.8% top-5 absolute improvements for ResNet-18 using a ResNet-34 teacher, which is notable. Limitations are clearly stated (e.g., complexity of double backprop with batch norm; KD struggles on ImageNet with different depths). The results support the main claims.

That said, the student remains weaker than the teacher (as expected given fewer parameters), and some analyses are qualitative. The novelty is incremental relative to FitNets, but the specific focus on attention maps, their normalization, and the empirical breadth (including ImageNet) add value.

Additional feedback and questions for the authors (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Quantify attention–object correlation: In Section 3, you state that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between attention maps and object regions. Figure 4 is compelling; please add quantitative measures (e.g., pointing game, overlap with bounding boxes/segmentations, CAM-style localization metrics) across depths and datasets.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: How were beta and p chosen? Please report the sensitivity of performance to beta (layer-wise scaling, decay schedules) and to p in the attention mapping functions. A brief ablation on normalization choice (l2 vs l1) would also help.
- Teacher training curves: In Figure 7(b), please include the teacher’s training and validation loss/accuracy to contextualize the speed-up and final gap.
- Choosing attention types: From your experiments, activation-based sum variants appear more robust than max, and F2sum is a good default. Please summarize pros/cons and when to prefer activation-based vs gradient-based AT (e.g., trade-offs in compute, stability with batch norm, sensitivity to data augmentation).
- Compute/latency benefits: Since the students are smaller and still underperform teachers, it would be helpful to quantify speed-ups: parameters, FLOPs, and wall-clock inference time on CPU/GPU. This will clarify the practical value of AT when trading accuracy for efficiency.
- Same-architecture transfer: If teacher and student share the same architecture and depth, does AT still help convergence and final accuracy on larger datasets (as seen on CIFAR)? A brief ImageNet experiment (even partial training) would strengthen the claim that AT benefits training beyond capacity differences.
- Gradient-based AT with batch norm: You note uncertainty about how batch norm should behave in the second backprop pass. Any preliminary experiments (e.g., freezing BN in the second pass, or using running stats) would be valuable. If not, a short discussion of the practical recipe you recommend would help practitioners.
- Flip-invariance regularizer: The symmetry norm performed strongly on CIFAR. Could you show results on at least one larger dataset (or an additional CIFAR architecture) to assess generality?
- ImageNet KD: KD struggled when teacher and student had different depths. Can you provide more detail on the hyperparameters tried (temperature, alpha), or small-scale evidence that KD can work with matched depths on ImageNet?

Overall, the paper is clear and well motivated, and the empirical results substantiate the main claims. Addressing the above points—especially quantifying attention–object alignment, clarifying hyperparameter choices, and characterizing efficiency gains—would further strengthen the work.
