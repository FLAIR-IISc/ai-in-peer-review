1. Summary of contributions
The paper studies whether transferring “where to look” information from a teacher CNN to a student can improve the student’s accuracy. The authors define spatial attention maps for CNNs in two ways: (a) activation-based maps that aggregate per-channel activations into a single H×W map (e.g., sum of absolute values or their powers) and (b) gradient-based maps that measure input sensitivity via gradients of the loss with respect to the input. They introduce simple losses that encourage the student’s attention maps (computed at selected layers) to match the teacher’s normalized attention maps. The method is easy to implement, adds little overhead for activation-based AT, and can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD).

Empirically, the paper presents consistent improvements across CIFAR-10/100 with ResNets and Wide ResNets, as well as on larger-scale settings: ImageNet (ResNet-18 student, ResNet-34 teacher) and transfer learning on CUB-200-2011 and MIT Indoor Scenes. The paper also shows that activation-based attention transfer (AT) outperforms regressing full feature activations and often complements or exceeds KD. The gradient-based variant is related to double backpropagation, with an additional symmetry regularizer, and shows gains on CIFAR.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Simple, well-motivated idea with broad utility for CNNs and strong empirical gains across multiple datasets and architectures, including ImageNet.
- Clear evidence that spatial aggregation of activations provides a more effective transfer signal than full activation regression, making the contribution practically useful.

3. Supporting arguments
- Question/problem: Can a teacher’s spatial focus (attention) be used to guide a student CNN and improve performance? The paper addresses this by defining attention maps and aligning them across layers during training.
- Motivation and placement: The work is well situated at the intersection of model compression/distillation and attention/saliency visualization (e.g., CAM, Grad-CAM, guided backprop). It connects to FitNets while arguing that spatial statistics of activations are a compact, effective hint.
- Evidence: Results are thorough on CIFAR with ablations over attention map definitions (sum vs. max, different exponents), layer selection, and comparisons to KD and feature regression. On larger datasets, improvements on ImageNet and transfer learning tasks substantiate the claim that attention alignment scales. Gradient-based AT is related to established techniques (double backprop) and yields improvements, though it is less integrated into the main empirical narrative.

Caveats:
- The “attention” terminology may be conflated with learned selection mechanisms; here it denotes spatial statistics (activation- or gradient-based) rather than learned soft attention. The paper mostly acknowledges this via links to saliency literature but could be crisper.
- The gradient-based section feels orthogonal and not fully compared against activation-based AT under identical settings, nor are combined results shown.
- Some notation is ambiguous (e.g., the norm in Eq. 2). From the text, it appears the loss uses an l2 distance between l2-normalized, vectorized attention maps; explicit clarification would help.

4. Feedback to improve the paper (for authors; not all are decision-critical)
- Clarify terminology: Emphasize that “attention” here refers to spatial activation/gradient statistics rather than a parametric attention mechanism. Briefly relate to CAM/Grad-CAM and discuss differences.
- Make Eq. (2) precise: Define vec(·) explicitly; state that ||·|| is the Euclidean/Frobenius norm over spatial elements after vectorization; specify whether normalization is per-sample and per-layer. Consider reporting results with cosine similarity, which is equivalent after l2 normalization.
- Integrate the gradient-based section: Provide head-to-head comparisons with activation-based AT under matched protocols, show whether combining them helps, and detail handling of batch normalization and the computational overhead of the second backprop.
- Report computational overhead: Wall-clock and memory impact for activation-based AT (with multiple loss sites) and gradient-based AT. This will aid practitioners.
- Expand ablations: Sensitivity to the exponent p and choice of sum vs. max; impact of which layers are used; effect of normalization choice (l1 vs. l2); scaling of β (per layer, with/without scheduling).
- KD interactions: On ImageNet, KD reportedly harms convergence for ResNet-18/34. Provide a brief hyperparameter sweep (temperature, α) and any KD+AT results, or discuss why KD may hurt when teacher/student depths differ.
- Scope and generality: State explicitly that the current method targets CNNs with spatial feature maps; discuss prospects for non-convolutional models or non-classification tasks (detection/segmentation), even if left for future work.
- Presentation: Tighten reference formatting and unify notation; consider moving some gradient-based material to the appendix if not fully integrated into the main results.

Questions for the authors
- In Eq. (2), is ||·|| the Euclidean (Frobenius) norm applied to the vectorized H×W map? After l2-normalizing Q, the distance is equivalent to 2(1 − cosine). Did you try a cosine loss explicitly?
- How do you choose and schedule β across layers? You mention scaling by 10^3 divided by map size and batch size; is this crucial? Any empirical guidance for new architectures?
- What is the runtime and memory overhead of activation-based AT (with multiple attention losses) per training step? For gradient-based AT, what is the additional cost of the second backprop?
- How do you handle batch normalization in the second backprop for gradient-based AT? Are BN statistics or affine parameters updated during the second pass?
- Can you report results that combine activation- and gradient-based AT on CIFAR (and, if feasible, on a larger dataset)?
- Why does KD hinder convergence on ImageNet for ResNet-18/34? Did you explore different temperatures/α, label-smoothing baselines, or teacher logit calibration?
- How sensitive is performance to the exponent p and to sum vs. max aggregation across channels? Do these preferences generalize across datasets?
- Robustness to teacher quality: What happens if the teacher is only marginally better or even slightly worse than the student? Is AT still helpful or can it be harmful?
- Do you observe any qualitative failure modes where attention alignment leads the student to over-focus on spurious regions?

Overall, this is a practical and well-motivated contribution to knowledge transfer in CNNs, with convincing empirical support and low implementation burden. Clarifying the loss definition, better integrating the gradient-based section, and adding minor ablations would further strengthen the paper.
