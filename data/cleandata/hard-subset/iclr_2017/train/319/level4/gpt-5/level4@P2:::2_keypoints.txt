- Paper studies transferring “where to look” from a teacher CNN to a student by defining attention as spatial maps from internal representations and training students to match them.
- Proposes two families of attention maps: activation-based (channel aggregation) and gradient-based (input-loss gradients).
- Activation-based AT aggregates channel activations per spatial location (sum or p-norm variants) and uses normalized per-map losses at selected layers.
- Gradient-based AT treats input gradients as attention, uses a double-backprop objective to align input sensitivities, and adds a horizontal-flip symmetry regularizer.
- Empirically, activation-based AT consistently improves student accuracy across CIFAR, CUB, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet and often outperforms FitNets/full activation matching.
- Activation-based AT complements knowledge distillation and is simple to implement; code is released.
- Decision: weak accept — clear, consistent empirical gains with a simple, well-motivated transfer mechanism; main issues are clarity and organization.
- Strength: well-situated problem and motivation relative to distillation and attention/saliency literature.
- Strength: method is straightforward, architecture-friendly, and normalization in the attention loss aids stability.
- Strong empirical support: improves various teacher–student pairs, narrows gaps on fine-grained/scene tasks, and gives notable ResNet-18 ImageNet gains from a ResNet-34 teacher.
- Limitation: “attention” phrasing may overstate novelty since core signal is channel-wise spatial aggregation of activations.
- Limitation: gradient-based AT feels under-integrated, evaluated in restricted settings and not combined with activation-based AT.
- Notational issues: Eq. (2) norms and normalization details need clarification.
- Suggestion: explicitly define “attention” as spatial aggregation of activations or input gradients and distinguish from class-specific attention (CAM/Grad-CAM).
- Suggestion: clarify that per-map normalization is vector l2 (Frobenius) norm and that loss uses Euclidean distance; make notation unambiguous.
- Suggestion: reorganize gradient-based section—either integrate with activation AT comparisons/combinations or move to appendix; clarify batch-norm handling in second backprop.
- Suggestion: add ablations for p in Fpsum, sum vs max, number/placement of AT losses, importance of l2 normalization, and sensitivity to β and KD scheduling.
- Suggestion: quantify computational overhead for activation-based AT (negligible) and gradient-based AT (≈ one extra backward pass) and report training-time slowdowns.
- Suggestion: discuss scope/limitations—tailored to CNN spatial maps and how/if it extends to non-convolutional or sequence models.
- Suggestion: include visualizations of teacher/student attention pre/post AT across layers and classes.
- Questions for authors: confirm Eq. (2) uses Euclidean/Frobenius norm, not induced 2-norm.
- Question: why l2 normalization vs l1 or temperature scaling and sensitivity of results to this choice?
- Question: empirical trends across Fsum, F2sum, F4sum vs Fmax and risks of large p overfitting to peaks; benefits of softmax-normalized attention?
- Question: how performance varies with AT at early/mid/late layers and diminishing returns from adding AT sites?
- Question: when combining AT with KD, how are β, KD temperature/α scheduled and are there cases KD hurts but AT helps?
- Question: how is batch normalization and other stateful layers handled during the second backprop in gradient-based AT; can you report batch-norm-on CIFAR results for direct comparison?
- Question: what is wall-clock overhead of activation- and gradient-based AT relative to baseline training for main models?
- Question: have you tried AT on spatially sensitive tasks (detection/segmentation) or architectures with attention modules, and are there failure cases?
