- Paper proposes attention transfer by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps at multiple layers, alongside classification loss and optional knowledge distillation.  
- Defines activation-based attentions (sum/max of absolute feature values raised to p) and a gradient-based attention (derivative of loss w.r.t. inputs).  
- Evaluated on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet; attention transfer consistently improves student test performance but students still underperform teachers.  
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks have stronger spatial correlation between object and attention maps is supported visually (Fig. 4) but lacks quantitative evidence.  
- Hyperparameter selection is unclear; the impact of the attention-loss weight β should be shown.  
- Recommend reporting the teacher’s training and validation loss in Figure 7(b) for completeness.  
- Experiments do not clearly delineate the relative pros and cons of the different attention-map variants.  
- Attention transfer does not beat the teacher; authors should report runtime/parameter speed-up to justify using smaller students.  
- Open question: if student and teacher share the same architecture, is there any benefit to attention transfer?  
- Pros: clearly written, well motivated, and yields consistent student improvement over baseline.  
- Cons: students still worse than teachers, unclear guidance on which attention variant to use, and the contribution is somewhat incremental relative to FitNet.
