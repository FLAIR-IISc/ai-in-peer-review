This paper proposes a variant of knowledge distillation that aligns teacher and student networks by minimizing discrepancies between summed statistics of intermediate feature maps. Empirically, the method outperforms a FitNet-style distillation baseline.

Pros:
- Evaluated on multiple computer vision datasets.
- Generally clear and readable.

Cons:
- The method appears limited to convolutional architectures.
- The use of “attention” is misleading. The approach essentially distills aggregated activation statistics (e.g., sums of squared activations or summed L_p norms) over spatial feature maps, rather than attention in the usual sense.
- The gradient-based attention transfer component feels out of place. It is neither directly compared with nor combined with the proposed “attention-based” transfer, making it read like a parallel idea that adds limited value without a clear narrative connection.
- The definition of the induced 2-norm in Eq. (2) is unclear. If Q is an H-by-W matrix, its induced 2-norm is the spectral norm (largest singular value), which would be expensive to compute within the loss. It seems likely the intended norm is the Frobenius norm; clarification and computational details are needed.

Overall assessment:
The proposed distillation technique is simple and appears effective in practice, but the paper would benefit from clearer terminology, more cohesive organization, and precise notation. In particular, please (i) define “attention” in the context of this work to avoid conflating it with standard attention mechanisms, (ii) clarify the chosen norms and how they are computed efficiently, and (iii) either integrate and evaluate the gradient-based transfer with the main method or clearly separate it as a distinct contribution with appropriate comparisons.
