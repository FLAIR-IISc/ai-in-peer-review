Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes attention transfer as a mechanism for knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student CNN. Instead of matching activations, the student is trained to match spatial attention maps computed from the teacher.
- Two families of attention are defined: activation-based attention (channel-wise reductions of feature magnitudes, e.g., sum or sum of powers) and gradient-based attention (input sensitivity maps). The authors argue these maps capture where the network “looks” across layers.
- A simple normalized matching loss is introduced to align teacher and student attention at selected layers, and it can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD).
- Experiments on CIFAR, fine-tuning (CUB-200, MIT Scenes), and ImageNet show consistent improvements for activation-based attention transfer; gradient-based attention transfer is demonstrated on CIFAR. The authors release code and models.

Decision: Reject (borderline), primarily due to insufficient empirical validation on ImageNet and unclear advantages over strong KD baselines.

Supporting arguments
- Novel and well-motivated idea: Using spatial attention as the transfer signal is intuitive and better aligned with the spatial nature of CNNs than full activation matching. The normalization choice and per-layer placement are sensible, and the relationship to FitNets/KD is clearly positioned.
- Evidence vs. KD is not conclusive: On CIFAR, AT generally improves over the student baseline and sometimes over KD, and AT+KD can work well. However, gains over a well-tuned KD baseline are often modest, and the paper lacks detailed, controlled comparisons (e.g., matched hyperparameters, temperature sweeps, ablations on which layers are used) to establish robust superiority.
- ImageNet evaluation is underdeveloped: The ImageNet section uses only two attention losses, carries over hyperparameters from fine-tuning, and does not present a thorough comparison to KD or FitNets. The claim that KD “struggles” on ImageNet is plausible but unsupported by systematic experiments (e.g., tuning temperatures, loss weights, matching teacher/student depth). Without stronger ImageNet evidence, the main claim of broad applicability remains tentative.
- Gradient-based AT is promising but limited: Results are shown only on CIFAR with a specific NIN variant and without batch normalization. The practical training overhead (double backprop) and stability with batch normalization are acknowledged as open. This makes the gradient-based component interesting but not yet compelling.
- Rigor and completeness: Some choices (p in the attention mapping, L2 normalization, loss placement, β scheduling) are reasonable but not deeply analyzed. The paper would benefit from statistical significance, runtime/memory overhead reports, and more extensive ablations (e.g., attention at different depths, resolution effects, alternative attention definitions such as CAM/Grad-CAM).

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not central to the decision)
- Provide a stronger, controlled comparison to KD and FitNets, including thorough tuning. Report mean/std over multiple runs.
- Expand ImageNet experiments: place attention losses at all residual groups, tune β, compare AT, KD, and AT+KD with matched backbones and also same-depth teacher/student. Include convergence curves and final accuracy with computational budgets.
- Report training cost: overhead from computing attention maps, double backprop for gradient-based AT, wall-clock time, and memory.
- Analyze sensitivity to the attention definition: compare sum vs. max variants, p values, with/without normalization, and the effect of layer selection.
- Clarify behavior with batch normalization during gradient-based AT (how second backprop interacts with BN statistics).
- Improve readability and polish references/figures. Some references are inconsistently formatted; figure captions could be more descriptive. Summarize key tables/figures in the main text with clear takeaways.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide a thorough KD baseline on ImageNet with temperature sweeps and loss-weight tuning? How does AT compare when both are well-tuned, and what about AT+KD?
2. How sensitive is AT to β, the choice of p in Fpsum, and L2 normalization? Do conclusions hold across datasets/architectures?
3. Where does AT help most? Please report per-layer ablations on ImageNet (attention at early vs. mid vs. late stages) and on fine-tuning tasks (CUB/Scenes).
4. What is the compute/memory overhead of AT and gradient-based AT compared to the student baseline? How does double backprop scale to large models with BN?
5. Can you quantify attention alignment (e.g., cosine similarity of attention maps) and correlate it with accuracy gains?
6. How robust is AT to teacher-student mismatches (different capacity/architecture/depth)? Does using a same-depth teacher change the outcome on ImageNet?
7. For gradient-based AT, can you demonstrate results with batch normalization and provide guidance on implementation details (e.g., handling BN in the second backprop)?
8. Have you tried class-discriminative attention variants (CAM/Grad-CAM) for transfer, and if so, how do they compare to your activation-based maps?

I would be inclined to reconsider if the ImageNet section is substantially expanded with controlled KD baselines, full ablations (layer placement, hyperparameters), and runtime analysis, demonstrating clear and consistent gains.
