1. Summary of what the paper claims to contribute (positive and generous)
- The paper investigates transferring “where to look” information from a high-capacity teacher CNN to a smaller student CNN by aligning their attention. Attention is defined as spatial maps derived either from hidden activations or from input gradients.
- For activation-based attention, the authors propose channel-aggregation mappings such as sums of |A|^p and max of |A|^p over channels to yield spatial maps at multiple layers. For gradient-based attention, they use the loss gradient with respect to the input as an input-sensitivity map.
- The student is trained with a joint objective that includes standard classification loss plus an attention-alignment loss (L2 distance between normalized teacher/student attention maps at selected layers), optionally combined with knowledge distillation.
- The approach is evaluated on CIFAR-10/100, transfer learning on CUB and MIT Scenes, and ImageNet. Across architectures (e.g., NIN, ResNet, WRN), attention transfer consistently improves the student over training without attention. The paper also reports that activation-based attention transfer outperforms regressing full activations, and that gradient-based attention and a flip-symmetry regularizer yield further gains on CIFAR.
- Overall, the paper is clearly written, well positioned within attention visualization and knowledge transfer literature, and provides code and models.

2. Decision
Reject.
Key reasons:
- The novelty is incremental relative to prior hint-based/feature transfer methods (e.g., FitNets) and knowledge distillation; using spatial attention summaries as the transfer target is interesting but not sufficiently differentiated or analyzed.
- The empirical evidence lacks crucial quantitative analyses and guidance (e.g., no quantitative validation of the claimed correlation between model accuracy and spatial attention quality, limited hyperparameter sensitivity, and unclear trade-offs among attention definitions).

3. Supporting arguments
- With respect to problem and motivation: The paper addresses a timely question—can attention be used as a vehicle for knowledge transfer?—and relates it to both attention visualization (e.g., CAM/Grad-CAM) and knowledge distillation/feature matching. This is well motivated and clearly presented.
- With respect to placement in the literature: The method is close in spirit to FitNets’ hint transfer and other representation-matching approaches, differing mainly in moving from full feature tensors to spatial attention summaries. While this is a neat idea, the paper does not convincingly establish why attention summaries are fundamentally preferable beyond the observed empirical gains over full activation regression in selected settings.
- With respect to support for claims: The student consistently improves over its baseline across datasets and architectures, which supports the claim that attention transfer can help training. However, key claims remain under-supported:
  - The assertion that higher-accuracy models have “better” spatial attention is shown qualitatively; a quantitative correlation (e.g., localization overlap or discriminativeness metrics) is not provided.
  - There is insufficient analysis of the impact of the attention weight (beta) and of design choices (e.g., which layers, which attention mapping, normalization scheme), making it hard to reproduce the best trade-offs or generalize recommendations.
  - The student does not surpass the teacher, and the paper does not sufficiently characterize the practical benefits in resource terms (e.g., speed/latency/throughput gains due to parameter reductions), especially when AT is used with same or different depths.
  - The pros/cons of the different attention mappings (sum vs max, choice of p) are not distilled into actionable guidance.
- Overall, while the empirical gains are consistent and encouraging, the contribution reads as an incremental variant of representation matching without the deeper analysis needed to elevate it.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not necessarily part of the decision) and questions for the authors
Helpful suggestions:
- Provide quantitative evidence for the claim that better models have more class-relevant attention. For example, report correlations between attention maps and ground-truth object regions (IoU, pointing game, energy within bounding boxes) across models of different accuracy.
- Include a sensitivity study for the attention loss weight (beta), ideally across datasets and architectures, and discuss practical defaults. Clarify how you scale beta across layers (you mention normalizing by map size and batch size).
- Summarize guidance on attention definitions. Based on your experiments, when should one prefer sum vs max aggregation and which exponent p? A concise recommendation table would greatly help practitioners.
- Report teacher training/validation loss and accuracy where relevant (e.g., alongside Fig. 7b) to contextualize the gap and to help interpret student–teacher mismatch with KD on ImageNet.
- Characterize efficiency benefits. Since students are smaller and faster, quantify wall-clock speedup, FLOPs, memory, and latency improvements corresponding to the observed accuracy deltas. This will clarify the practical value even if students don’t surpass teachers.
- Clarify the benefits when student and teacher share the same architecture/depth. Do you still see improvements from AT in that setting (beyond KD), and are they consistent on large-scale data (ImageNet), not only on CIFAR?
- Expand on gradient-based AT with batch normalization. Provide implementation guidance for the second backprop pass (e.g., whether BN statistics/parameters are updated, frozen, or recomputed) and discuss stability.

Questions:
- Can you quantify the “attention-quality improves with accuracy” claim? For example, do attention maps from higher-accuracy models place more mass inside ground-truth boxes, and by how much?
- How were beta and other hyperparameters selected for each experiment, and how sensitive are results to these choices? Are there robust defaults across datasets?
- What practical criteria should guide the choice among Fsum, F^2sum, F^pmax and the choice of p? In which scenarios did max-based maps help, if any?
- In Fig. 7b, could you add teacher training/validation curves to better interpret convergence and the full-activation vs attention-map comparison?
- Do you observe any cases where AT hurts (e.g., early layers, mismatched receptive fields, or when teacher and student capacities diverge too much)?
- When student and teacher are architecturally identical, does AT still provide benefits over standard training and/or KD on ImageNet, given sufficient compute? If not, why do you think KD struggled here?
- For gradient-based AT, how do results change with data augmentation beyond flips (e.g., crops), and have you tested symmetry constraints beyond horizontal flips?
- Could you report end-to-end speed/latency/throughput benefits for the improved students on CUB/Scenes/ImageNet to substantiate the efficiency-accuracy trade-off?
