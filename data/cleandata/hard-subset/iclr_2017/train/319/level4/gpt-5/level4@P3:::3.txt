Summary and contributions
The paper proposes attention transfer (AT) as a simple, general mechanism to transfer knowledge from a teacher CNN to a student CNN. The central idea is to define spatial attention maps within CNNs and to train the student to match the teacher’s attention at one or more layers, in addition to the standard classification loss (and optionally knowledge distillation, KD). Two families of attention are considered:
- Activation-based attention: spatial maps formed by channelwise statistics of absolute activations (sum or max of |A|^p).
- Gradient-based attention: input-gradient maps reflecting sensitivity of the output to input pixels.

The authors present concrete AT losses using normalized attention maps, explore placement across layers/groups, and show that AT can be combined with KD at negligible overhead. Empirically, AT yields consistent improvements over student baselines across CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, with particularly strong and robust gains for activation-based AT. The paper is clearly written, well-motivated in the context of attention/interpretability and knowledge distillation, and releases code.

Decision: Accept (weak accept)
Key reasons:
1) Practical impact and generality: A simple, architecture-agnostic loss that consistently improves students across diverse datasets and models, including ImageNet.
2) Empirical support: Strong, reproducible improvements over strong baselines, with clear ablations (e.g., attention-vs-full-activation transfer) showing the specific value of attention.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles a concrete question—can we transfer where a network “looks” to improve a smaller model? The link to literature is appropriate: the work sits at the intersection of attention visualization (activation/gradient maps) and knowledge transfer (hints/KD). The idea of transferring spatial saliency rather than full features is compelling and novel enough in this context.
- Method soundness: The activation-based attention definitions are reasonable, and the choice to normalize attention vectors before matching is empirically justified and avoids scale issues. The gradient-based AT is correctly related to double backpropagation and symmetry constraints, and the implementation details are sensible.
- Empirical rigor: Experiments span multiple architectures (ResNet, WRN, NIN), datasets, and settings (pretraining/fine-tuning). Baselines include KD and FitNets-style full-activation matching. Results consistently favor activation-based AT over full-activation transfer, indicating that spatial attention is a more efficient signal. The ImageNet experiment is particularly valuable: even with minimal tuning and few AT layers, ResNet-18 improves meaningfully under a ResNet-34 teacher.
- Scope/limitations: The student does not outperform the teacher, which is expected given reduced capacity; the contribution is knowledge transfer that boosts smaller models, not model compression to surpass the teacher. Gradient-based AT is explored only on CIFAR/NIN due to second-order derivative complexity with batch norm; this is a limitation but does not undermine the main activation-based results.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all decision-critical)
- Quantify attention–object alignment: The paper claims stronger models have attention maps better aligned with object regions. Provide quantitative evidence (e.g., correlation/IoU with bounding boxes or CAM/Grad-CAM localization metrics) across teacher/student pairs and layers.
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters: Report sensitivity to β (per-layer and overall), choice of norm (L1 vs L2) for attention normalization, and schedules when combined with KD. An ablation on the number and placement of AT losses (per group vs all blocks) would clarify trade-offs.
- Choice of attention mapping functions: Summarize the pros/cons of sum vs max and different p values across datasets/architectures. The tables suggest sum-based variants are more effective; discuss why (e.g., robustness to spurious peaks, aggregation across channels).
- Computational trade-offs: Characterize training and inference overhead. Report wall-clock or FLOPs-based speedups for representative student–teacher pairs (e.g., WRN-16-1 vs WRN-16-2, ResNet-18 vs ResNet-34) and the incremental training cost due to AT (teacher forward + losses). This will help position AT as a practical compression/acceleration tool.
- KD interplay and ImageNet: The KD failure on ImageNet deserves more analysis (temperature, α, label smoothing, same-arch teacher, different optimizers). Given that AT succeeds where KD struggles, a deeper discussion would strengthen the paper’s claim about AT’s robustness.
- Gradient-based AT: Clarify handling of batch normalization during second backprop, and report experiments with BN where feasible (e.g., freezing BN stats in second pass or using ghost batches). The symmetry regularizer is promising—please evaluate it beyond CIFAR and compare to strong augmentation baselines.
- Claims and positioning: “First successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet” should be precisely scoped (e.g., student–teacher ResNet with attention-based transfer). A brief comparison to other concurrent distillation/compression methods on ImageNet would help.

Questions for the authors
1) Can you provide quantitative evidence that higher-accuracy models have attention better aligned with objects (e.g., bounding-box overlap, pointing game accuracy), and does this hold across layers?
2) How sensitive is performance to β, attention normalization (L1 vs L2), and the number/placement of AT losses? Is there a simple heuristic for setting β per layer?
3) What are the training and inference time/compute trade-offs for typical student–teacher pairs? Please report speedups (FLOPs, latency) and the extra training cost due to AT.
4) Do students ever surpass teachers when capacities are matched (same architecture/depth), suggesting a regularization effect of AT? Any results on same-arch, same-width depth-matched pairs?
5) Why do sum-based attention maps outperform max-based ones in your experiments? Do max maps overemphasize noisy peaks, or is the effect architecture/dataset dependent?
6) For KD on ImageNet, which temperatures and α values were tried? Did you try label smoothing or matching intermediate logits/features? Any improvements when teacher and student share architecture?
7) For gradient-based AT with batch norm, how do you recommend handling the second derivative pass? Did you try freezing BN statistics or recomputing them on the second pass?
8) Can you report teacher train/validation loss and accuracy curves alongside the student in Fig. 7(b) to contextualize convergence speed?

Overall, this is a clear, well-motivated paper with a simple and effective idea, strong empirical support (including on ImageNet), and released code. While some analyses could be deeper and the gradient-based component is less mature, the activation-based AT contribution is solid and practically useful.
