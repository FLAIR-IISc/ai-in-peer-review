- Core idea: Encourage a student network to match teacher attention maps at intermediate layers by minimizing L2 distance, alongside classification loss and optional KD term.
- Attention mechanisms: Define activation-based maps (sum or max of feature values raised to power p) and a gradient-based map using loss derivatives w.r.t. inputs.
- Empirical result: Attention transfer improves student test performance compared to training the student alone.
- Limitations: Student networks still perform worse than the teacher even with attention transfer.
- Claim validation needed: Authors assert higher-accuracy networks show higher spatial correlation between object and attention map; quantitative correlation metrics across models/datasets are requested.
- Hyperparameter concerns: Report how hyperparameters were chosen, especially the impact and sensitivity analysis for β.
- Missing comparisons: In Figure 7b, include teacher training and validation losses for context.
- Ambiguity across attention types: Experiments do not clarify when activation-based vs. gradient-based attention is preferable.
- Effect of p: Need analysis of how the choice of p in activation-based variants affects performance and stability.
- Efficiency/trade-offs: Characterize speed-up (training/inference time, memory) and practical trade-offs given students have fewer parameters.
- Same-architecture question: If student and teacher share architecture, clarify whether attention transfer provides any benefit.
- Pros listed by reviewer: Paper is clearly written, well motivated, and consistently improves students over isolated training.
- Cons listed by reviewer: Students still worse than teachers, unclear recommended attention mechanism, and novelty seems incremental relative to FitNets.
- Suggested improvements: Strengthen quantitative analysis of attention–object correlation, clarify trade-offs among attention types, and provide a thorough hyperparameter and efficiency study.
