- Paper proposes attention transfer by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps at multiple layers, alongside classification loss and optional KD.
- Defines activation-based attention maps (sum/max over channels of |feature|^p) and a gradient-based attention (derivative of loss w.r.t. inputs).
- Experiments on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet show AT consistently improves student test performance but students still underperform teachers.
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks exhibit higher spatial correlation between object and attention map is supported visually (Fig.4) but lacks quantitative evidence.
- Hyperparameter selection procedure is not described; impact of the beta weighting term is not shown.
- Request to report the teacher’s training and validation losses in Figure 7(b) for comparison.
- Experiments do not clarify the relative pros and cons of the different attention map definitions.
- AT does not surpass the teacher; need characterization of speed-up from smaller students and whether AT helps when student and teacher share the same architecture.
- Pros: paper is clearly written, well motivated, and yields consistent student improvements over baseline students.
- Cons: students remain worse than teachers, unclear guidance on which attention mechanism to use, and novelty is somewhat incremental relative to FitNet.
