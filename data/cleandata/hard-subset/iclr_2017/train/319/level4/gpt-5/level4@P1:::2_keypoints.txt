- Proposal: Introduces Attention Transfer (AT) — train a student to match teacher spatial attention maps (activation- or gradient-based) as a form of knowledge distillation.
- Attention maps: Defines activation-based maps (channel-wise sums/powers, sum vs max, p) and gradient-based input-sensitivity maps.
- Implementation details: Uses l2-normalized attention vectors, places AT losses at multiple layers, and can combine AT with standard KD.
- Empirical gains: Consistent improvements on CIFAR-10 (ResNets, WRNs), fine-tuning gains on CUB and MIT Scenes, and +1.1% top-1 on ImageNet (ResNet-18 ← ResNet-34).
- Comparison to baselines: Outperforms FitNets-style full-activation regression in final accuracy and convergence speed.
- Reproducibility: Code is released.
- Decision: Accept — method is simple, well-motivated, and empirically effective across datasets and architectures.
- Complementarity: Aligning spatial attention is more effective than regressing full activations and complements KD.
- Strength: Well-positioned between CNN visualization/attention and knowledge distillation literatures with appropriate ablations.
- Important empirical findings: l2-normalization and placing losses across multiple groups are crucial and supported by experiments.
- Terminology issue: Calling activation statistics “attention” is broad; should clarify they are spatial summaries, not class-discriminative attention like CAM/Grad-CAM.
- Organization issue: Gradient-based AT section feels detached, evaluated only on a thin CIFAR model, lacks direct comparison/combination with activation-based AT, and omits discussion of double-backprop overhead.
- Notation clarity: Explicitly state norms in Eq. (2) are vector p-norms (Frobenius on H×W map), not matrix operator norms.
- Terminology suggestion: Frame activation-based maps as “spatial activation summaries” or “attention proxies” and contrast with class-discriminative attention.
- Gradient-based AT recommendation: Either integrate tightly (head-to-head comparisons or joint loss) or move to appendix.
- Compute/implementation request: Report extra training time/memory for AT (number of added losses/layers) and for gradient-based AT (double backprop); clarify attention interpolation across spatial sizes and why l2-normalization is essential.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Provide sensitivity analysis for β, power p, and l1 vs l2 normalization; discuss stability and default recommendations.
- KD comparison caveat: KD reportedly struggled on ImageNet; authors should report tuned KD baselines (temperature/α) and same-architecture teachers to strengthen claims.
- Scope/limitations: Discuss applicability to non-convolutional architectures and tasks without spatial grids, and potential for detection/segmentation.
- Question 1: Confirm norms in Eq. (2) are vector p-norms after vectorization (Frobenius), not matrix operator norms.
- Question 2: Explain how teacher and student layers are aligned when depths differ and how attention maps are up/down-sampled; any interpolation artifacts?
- Question 3: Quantify performance degradation without l2-normalization and explain why normalization aids optimization.
- Question 4: How sensitive are gains to β, power p in Fpsum/Fpmax, and l1 vs l2 normalization; any recommended defaults?
- Question 5: On which datasets/settings is AT+KD reliably better than AT alone, and why did KD struggle on ImageNet (teacher/student mismatch, temperatures, or optimization)?
- Question 6: What is the training-time and memory overhead of double backprop for gradient-based AT, and was a joint activation+gradient AT tried?
- Question 7: For ImageNet, would more AT locations/losses yield further gains; what are recommended AT placement/budget practices?
- Question 8: Have you tried AT on detection/segmentation or non-convolutional backbones; what challenges or modifications are anticipated?
