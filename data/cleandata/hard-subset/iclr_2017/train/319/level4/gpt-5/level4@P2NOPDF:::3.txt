This paper investigates transferring attention from a teacher network to a student network. The key idea is to encourage the student to match the teacher’s attention maps at selected layers by minimizing the L2 distance between their attention representations, in addition to the standard classification loss and, optionally, a knowledge distillation term.

The authors define several activation-based attentions, aggregating channel activations via either the sum of absolute feature values raised to the power p or the max of values raised to the power p. They also propose a gradient-based attention defined as the derivative of the loss with respect to the input.

Experiments on multiple datasets (CIFAR, CUB/Scene, ImageNet) show that attention transfer improves the student’s test performance relative to training without attention. However, even with attention, students do not surpass the teacher’s accuracy.

Remarks and questions:
- In Section 3, the authors claim that higher-accuracy networks exhibit higher spatial correlation between the object and the attention map. While Figure 4 is compelling, it would be valuable to include quantitative evidence supporting this claim.
- How were the hyperparameters chosen? In particular, please report the impact of β (e.g., a sensitivity analysis or ablation).
- In Figure 7(b), it would be helpful to report the teacher’s training and validation loss for comparison.
- From the experiments, it is not clear what the practical pros and cons are for the different attention definitions. Guidance on when to prefer activation-based versus gradient-based attention (and the effect of p or max vs sum) would improve clarity.
- AT does not outperform the teacher. Since the student networks have fewer parameters, please characterize the resulting speed-up (e.g., FLOPs, latency, throughput, memory) to contextualize the accuracy drop. Additionally, if teacher and student share the same architecture, is there any benefit to attention transfer (i.e., does it act as a regularizer or improve convergence)?

In summary:

Pros:
- Clearly written and well motivated.
- Consistent improvements of the student with attention transfer compared to the student baseline.

Cons:
- Students still underperform the teacher models.
- Unclear guidance on which attention formulation to use in which scenario.
- The novelty appears somewhat incremental relative to FitNets.
