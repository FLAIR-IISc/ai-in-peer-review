This paper investigates attention transfer between a teacher and a student network. The method encourages the student to match the teacher’s attention maps at multiple layers by minimizing their L2 distance, in addition to the standard classification loss and, optionally, a knowledge-distillation term. The authors define several activation-based attentions (sum or max over channels of absolute feature values raised to the power p) and a gradient-based attention (the derivative of the loss with respect to the inputs). Experiments on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet show that attention transfer improves the student network’s test performance. However, even with attention transfer, the student networks perform worse than the teacher.

Remarks and questions:
- In Section 3, the authors claim that networks with higher accuracy exhibit higher spatial correlation between the object and the attention map. While Figure 4 is compelling, it would be helpful to include quantitative results to substantiate this claim.
- How were the hyperparameters chosen? In particular, please show the impact of the beta weighting.
- Please report the teacher’s training and validation losses in Figure 7(b).
- From the experiments, it is not clear what the pros and cons of the different attention maps are.
- Attention transfer (AT) does not outperform the teacher. However, the student networks have fewer parameters. It would be interesting to characterize the corresponding speed-up. If the student and teacher share the same architecture, is there any benefit to AT?

In summary:
Pros:
- Clearly written and well motivated.
- Consistent improvement of the student with attention compared to the student alone.

Cons:
- Students have worse performance than the teacher models.
- It is not clear which attention mechanism to use in which case.
- Somewhat incremental novelty relative to FitNet.
