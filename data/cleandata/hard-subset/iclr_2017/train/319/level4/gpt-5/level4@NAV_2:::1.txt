1. Summary of contributions
- The paper introduces a new knowledge transfer approach that guides a student network by matching the teacher’s attention maps rather than regressing full activations. I find the central idea of transferring attention particularly appealing, as it targets where the network focuses rather than the entire activation tensor.

2. Decision
- Recommendation: Reject.
- Key reasons: (i) The reported gains over knowledge distillation alone appear modest, and (ii) the ImageNet evaluation (Section 4.2.2) is underdeveloped and needs substantially more evidence.

3. Supporting arguments
- While the idea is promising, the experiments as presented do not convincingly demonstrate a clear advantage over knowledge distillation across settings; the improvements seem limited relative to a strong KD baseline.
- The ImageNet section is crucial for establishing practical impact. As it stands, Section 4.2.2 is too brief to support the broader claims, lacking sufficient breadth of experiments and detailed analysis to validate robustness and generality.

4. Suggestions for improvement (not strictly part of the decision)
- Expand Section 4.2.2 with more comprehensive ImageNet experiments: multiple teacher–student pairs, stronger baselines, and careful hyperparameter tuning for both methods.
- Provide direct, apples-to-apples comparisons against knowledge distillation across the same setups, with clear reporting of absolute and relative gains.
- Include ablations that unpack when attention maps help most (e.g., placement of transfer losses, attention map variants, and training schedules).
- Report statistical variability (e.g., multiple runs) and training dynamics to show whether improvements persist and whether convergence behavior is consistently better.
- If feasible, quantify computational overhead and training stability relative to KD to clarify practical trade-offs.
- I would be open to revisiting the assessment if the authors substantially extend and strengthen the ImageNet results in Section 4.2.2.

Questions for the authors
- Can you expand Section 4.2.2 with additional ImageNet experiments that isolate the benefit of attention transfer over knowledge distillation under matched hyperparameters and training budgets?
- Across datasets, how often does attention transfer outperform KD by a meaningful margin, and under what conditions does it not? Please provide a systematic comparison.
- Which attention map configurations (e.g., choice of mapping function, layer locations) contribute most to the gains, and how sensitive are results to these choices?
- How does the method’s computational cost and training stability compare to KD in large-scale settings (e.g., additional forward/backward passes, memory footprint)?
- If the gains are primarily in convergence speed rather than final accuracy in some cases, could you show learning curves and discuss whether this translates to a net wall-clock advantage?
