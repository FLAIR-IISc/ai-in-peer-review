

**Summary of Contributions**  
The paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge distillation by transferring attention maps from a teacher to a student network. The authors define attention as spatial statistics of feature activations (activation-based) or input gradients (gradient-based), and demonstrate that aligning these maps between teacher and student networks leads to consistent performance improvements across multiple vision tasks. The key contributions include: (1) a formalization of attention for CNNs, (2) two practical methods for attention transfer, (3) empirical validation across CIFAR, Scene, CUB, and ImageNet datasets, and (4) a demonstration that attention transfer outperforms standard feature-based distillation (e.g., FitNets) while being compatible with knowledge distillation. The work is well-motivated by human attention mechanisms and builds logically on prior knowledge distillation literature.

**Decision**  
Reject. The paper presents an interesting empirical observation but suffers from conceptual misalignment between its claims and methodology, which undermines the scientific rigor of the contribution.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper's core claim—that attention maps contain transferable information—is empirically supported, but the terminology and methodology are problematic. The term "attention" is misleading: the proposed attention maps (e.g., sum of absolute values of activations) do not correspond to psychological attention or attention mechanisms used in NLP/computer vision (e.g., Bahdanau et al., 2014). Instead, the method transfers statistics of feature maps, which is a subtle but critical distinction. The authors conflate "attention" with "feature map statistics," which misleads readers and weakens the paper's conceptual contribution.

The gradient-based attention transfer is particularly problematic. It is introduced as a separate method with no clear connection to the activation-based approach. The paper neither compares gradient-based transfer to activation-based transfer nor demonstrates that combining them provides additional benefits. This creates a disjointed narrative where the gradient-based method feels tacked on rather than integrated into a cohesive framework. The lack of justification for including this method (e.g., no ablation showing its necessity) further diminishes the paper's coherence.

Additionally, the paper contains a critical technical ambiguity: the loss function in Equation (2) uses the notation $\|Q^S / \|Q^S\|_2 - Q^T / \|Q^T\|_2 \|_p$, but it is unclear whether the authors intend the Frobenius norm (common for matrix comparisons) or the induced 2-norm (which would require expensive singular value decomposition). The draft review correctly notes this as a potential computational cost issue, but the paper does not clarify this. Without this clarification, the method's implementation details are ambiguous, and the reproducibility of results is questionable.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**  
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:  
1. **Reframe the terminology**: Replace "attention" with a more precise term (e.g., "feature map statistics" or "spatial activation patterns") to avoid misleading readers.  
2. **Integrate the gradient-based method**: Either demonstrate its synergy with activation-based transfer or remove it to maintain focus on the core contribution.  
3. **Clarify the loss function**: Explicitly state whether the norm is Frobenius or induced 2-norm, and justify the computational choice.  
4. **Expand ablation studies**: Show why activation-based transfer outperforms gradient-based transfer, and why attention maps are superior to full-activation transfer (as hinted in Table 1 but not fully explained).  

**Questions for Authors**  
1. The paper claims attention maps "encode where the network focuses most for taking its output decision." How do the authors reconcile this with their observation that mid-level attention maps focus on low-level features (e.g., edges) rather than object parts?  
2. In Table 3, the gradient-based attention transfer (12.1%) slightly outperforms activation-based transfer (11.2%), yet the paper asserts activation-based transfer is superior. Can the authors clarify this discrepancy and provide a more nuanced comparison?  
3. The paper states that "normalization of attention maps is important for the success of the student training." However, the normalization method (l2 vs. l1) is not empirically justified. Why was l2 chosen over l1, and how sensitive is the method to this choice?  
4. The gradient-based method requires double backpropagation (Equation 5). How does this impact training time compared to standard knowledge distillation, and why was this not included in the computational cost analysis?  

**Conclusion**  
While the empirical results demonstrate a useful technique for knowledge distillation, the paper's conceptual misalignment and technical ambiguities prevent it from making a clear, scientifically rigorous contribution. The authors' choice of terminology misrepresents the method, and the gradient-based component lacks integration. With significant revisions to clarify the methodology, terminology, and experimental rationale, this work could be a strong contribution to the field. As written, however, it does not meet ICLR's standards for theoretical clarity and scientific rigor.
