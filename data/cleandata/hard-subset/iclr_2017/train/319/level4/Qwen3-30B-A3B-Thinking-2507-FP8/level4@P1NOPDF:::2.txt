

The paper introduces a modified knowledge distillation framework that minimizes the discrepancy in the sum of statistics (e.g., summed squared activations or Lp norms) across feature maps between teacher and student networks. Empirically, the proposed method outperforms the FitNet-style distillation baseline across multiple computer vision datasets.  

**Strengths:**  
+ The method is evaluated comprehensively on diverse vision datasets.  
+ The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured.  

**Concerns:**  
- The approach appears restricted to convolutional architectures, limiting its applicability to other network types (e.g., transformers).  
- The use of "attention" terminology is misleading. The method does not employ attention mechanisms but instead distills the sum of squared (or other Lp-norm) activations within hidden feature maps. This misnomer obscures the core contribution.  
- The gradient-based attention transfer component feels disconnected. It is neither directly compared against the proposed attention-based transfer nor integrated with it. Instead, it reads as a parallel idea added without clear justification or evidence of added value.  
- The computation of the induced 2-norm in Equation (2) is unclear. Given that $ Q \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W} $, its induced 2-norm is its spectral norm (largest singular value), which is computationally expensive to compute. It is unclear whether the authors intended the Frobenius norm (which is standard for such sums and far more efficient) instead.  

Overall, while the distillation method demonstrates practical effectiveness, the paper suffers from conceptual misalignment (particularly regarding "attention"), unclear notation, and a lack of integration between key components. These issues undermine the clarity and technical contribution of the work.
