

**Review for ICLR 2017**

**Summary of Contributions**  
This paper introduces attention transfer as a novel knowledge distillation approach for convolutional neural networks. The authors define two types of attention maps—activation-based (using sum or max of absolute values raised to power p) and gradient-based (input sensitivity maps)—and demonstrate their effectiveness in transferring knowledge from a powerful teacher network to a smaller student network. The method consistently improves student performance across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network), with results showing significant accuracy gains over baseline students. The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation, thorough experimental validation, and publicly available code, making it a valuable contribution to the field of knowledge distillation.

**Decision**  
Accept (Weak Accept)  

**Key Reasons for Decision**  
1. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to knowledge distillation by introducing attention transfer as a distinct approach from existing methods like FitNets and standard knowledge distillation.  
2. The experimental results are consistent, well-documented, and demonstrate significant improvements across diverse settings, including ImageNet—a challenging benchmark where prior distillation methods have struggled.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper addresses a compelling problem: how to effectively transfer knowledge beyond simple output distributions. The authors' attention-based approach is well-motivated, building on visual attention principles from psychology and recent work on network interpretability. Their theoretical formulation of attention maps as spatial representations of network focus is both novel and intuitive.  

The experimental results robustly support the claims. On CIFAR-10, attention transfer (AT) combined with knowledge distillation (KD) consistently outperforms baseline students by 0.8-1.5% error reduction. On ImageNet, AT improves ResNet-18 accuracy by 1.1% top-1 and 0.8% top-5 over the baseline student, a notable achievement given the difficulty of distillation on this dataset. The ablation studies (Tables 1-2) convincingly show that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer, and the authors provide clear explanations for why attention maps contain more useful information than raw activations.  

The paper also makes an important contribution by demonstrating that attention transfer works effectively on ImageNet, where prior knowledge distillation methods have had limited success. The authors rightly note that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," addressing a significant gap in the literature.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**  
While the paper is strong, the following points would further enhance its impact:

1. **Quantify attention quality**: The paper claims "higher accuracy networks have stronger spatial correlation between attention maps and objects" (Section 3.1). While Figure 4 is compelling, adding quantitative metrics (e.g., correlation coefficients between attention maps and ground-truth object locations) would strengthen this claim.

2. **Hyperparameter sensitivity**: The paper states β is "usually about 0.1" but doesn't explore how performance varies with β. A sensitivity analysis showing how error rates change with different β values would provide valuable guidance for practitioners.

3. **Teacher-student comparison**: The paper doesn't compare student performance with attention transfer to a teacher network of the same architecture (not just the same depth). This would clarify whether attention transfer provides benefits beyond what's possible with standard distillation when network architectures are matched.

4. **Attention map comparison**: The paper shows activation-based attention transfer generally outperforms gradient-based, but doesn't systematically explain why. A comparative analysis of when to use each approach would be valuable for practitioners.

**Questions for Authors**  
1. How does attention transfer perform when the teacher and student networks have identical architectures (rather than different depths)? Does the attention transfer still provide benefits in this setting?

2. The paper mentions "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer" (Section 1). Could you provide more insight into why attention maps contain more useful information than full activations, and whether this effect persists across different network architectures?

3. In Table 3, gradient-based attention transfer (11.8%) outperforms knowledge distillation (12.1%) on CIFAR-10. Given that KD is typically a strong baseline, could you elaborate on why gradient-based attention transfer performs better in this case?

**Conclusion**  
This is a well-executed, clearly written paper that makes a meaningful contribution to knowledge distillation. The attention transfer approach is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple benchmarks. While some aspects could be strengthened (particularly regarding hyperparameter sensitivity and comparative analysis), the paper's core contribution is significant and timely. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that addressing the suggested improvements would further enhance the paper's impact.
