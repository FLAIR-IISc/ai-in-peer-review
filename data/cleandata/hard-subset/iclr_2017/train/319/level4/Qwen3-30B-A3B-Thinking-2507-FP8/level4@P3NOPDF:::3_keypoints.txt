- Proposes attention transfer by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps across layers alongside classification and distillation losses.  
- Defines multiple attention types: activation-based (sum or max of |features|^p) and gradient-based (loss derivatives w.r.t. inputs).  
- Experiments on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet show attention transfer consistently improves student performance over non-attention baselines.  
- Students still remain substantially worse than teachers (e.g., 5–10% accuracy gap on ImageNet), and the paper does not investigate whether this gap is inherent or mitigable.  
- Unclear primary goal: improve student vs. non-distilled baseline or close teacher-student gap; results do not demonstrate matching teacher performance.  
- No clear guidance on choosing between attention mechanisms; performance differences are minor and not analyzed by scenario or cost.  
- Hyperparameter β (attention loss weight) is underexplored; sensitivity analysis is missing despite limited reporting (Figure 7a uses β=1).  
- Lacks comparisons to state-of-the-art distillation methods (e.g., FitNet) and does not clearly position or justify novelty over prior work.  
- Figure 4’s spatial correlation claim lacks quantitative support (no correlation metrics provided).  
- Teacher training/validation losses are not reported, obscuring whether the gap stems from teacher limitations or distillation.  
- Computational costs (inference speed, memory overhead) of attention transfer are not quantified, leaving unclear trade-offs.  
- Minor issues: grammar ("Attention transfers does help"), clarify element-wise power notation, and standard phrasing for gradient-based attention derivation.  
- Summary: reasonable extension with consistent student gains but limited novelty, insufficient analysis of gap reduction, mechanism choice, and baselines.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject—method is sound but lacks sufficient novelty and practical impact without substantial revisions and additional experiments.
