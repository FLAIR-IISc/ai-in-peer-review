- Decision: Reject.  
- Proposal: Novel knowledge distillation that transfers spatial activation statistics ("attention maps") from teacher to student.  
- Empirical claim: Consistent performance gains across CIFAR-10/100, ImageNet, CUB, Scenes and multiple architectures.  
- Core contribution: Shows distilling spatial activation statistics (e.g., summed absolute values, max-pooled activations) outperforms raw-activation or standard KD.  
- Major criticism: The term "attention" is misleading—methods distill activation statistics, not attention mechanisms as used in vision/cognitive literature.  
- Supporting evidence: Reported "attention maps" merely reflect high activations at object locations, not selective focus or attention behavior.  
- Gradient-based transfer critique: Poorly integrated, not meaningfully compared or combined with activation-based transfer.  
- Performance/cost note: Gradient-based transfer is slightly worse in results, requires costly double backprop, and feels like an afterthought.  
- Formal error: Equation (2) misstates the norm—authors describe l2 but use the Frobenius norm (not the induced 2-norm requiring SVD).  
- Rename recommendation: Rebrand as "distilling spatial activation statistics" or "feature map normalization transfer" to avoid confusion.  
- Equation clarification: Explicitly state the use of Frobenius norm for the attention-loss computation.  
- Integration recommendation: Either compare and/or combine gradient-based and activation-based transfers (add AT+gradient results) or remove the gradient variant.  
- Motivation gap: Paper needs stronger causal explanation for why activation statistics (not raw activations) are transferable.  
- Question for authors: How do you reconcile calling these maps "attention" when they are purely activation-based—can you show examples of true selective attention?  
- Question for authors: Why was gradient-based transfer not tested jointly with activation-based AT, given potential complementarity?  
- Question for authors: Please clarify the norm used in Equation (2) to avoid ambiguity.
