- Paper proposes transferring CNN knowledge via attention maps (activation- and gradient-based) instead of activations/outputs.  
- Authors define two attention map types, show their correlation with network performance, and propose transfer methods.  
- Empirical validation across architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, NiN) and datasets (CIFAR-10/100, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet).  
- Claim that higher-performing networks exhibit more discriminative attention patterns and that attention transfer (AT) improves student performance.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Core claim that AT provides significant, generalizable improvements beyond KD is not sufficiently supported.  
- ImageNet results do not demonstrate AT’s superiority over KD; KD reportedly fails to converge on ImageNet without explanation.  
- Authors fail to compare AT and KD on identical ImageNet setups or to tune KD hyperparameters for ImageNet.  
- No justification for why KD works on CIFAR but fails on ImageNet (dataset scale, architecture, or hyperparameter issues).  
- Lack of ablation showing AT’s incremental benefit over KD (e.g., AT + KD vs. KD alone on ImageNet).  
- CIFAR gains are modest and may not justify method complexity (improvements are small relative to baselines).  
- Claim that attention maps carry unique transfer information vs. full activations is not rigorously quantified or explained.  
- Suggested fixes: re-evaluate ImageNet with AT + KD and tuned hyperparameters, test identical architectures, and provide ablations isolating AT’s contribution.  
- Suggested fixes: clarify why attention maps are advantageous (reduced noise vs. higher-level semantics) and strengthen theoretical grounding.  
- Questions for authors requested: Why KD fails on ImageNet (were hyperparameters tuned)? How much ImageNet gain is due to AT vs. KD, and would AT+KD help? Provide quantitative comparisons showing how attention maps differ from full activations in predictive value.
