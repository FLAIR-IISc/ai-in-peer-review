

This paper investigates attention transfer between teacher and student networks, minimizing the L2 distance between teacher/student attention maps across layers alongside classification loss and optionally a knowledge distillation term. The authors define multiple activation-based attention mechanisms (sum or max of absolute feature values raised to power p) and propose a gradient-based attention (derivative of loss w.r.t. inputs). Evaluation on CIFAR, Cub/Scene, and ImageNet datasets demonstrates that attention transfer improves student network test performance. However, student networks consistently underperform the teacher, even with attention transfer.

Key concerns:
- Section 3 claims higher-accuracy networks exhibit greater spatial correlation between objects and attention maps. While Figure 4 is compelling, quantitative evidence supporting this claim is needed.
- The selection rationale for hyperparameters (e.g., the impact of β) requires clarification.
- Figure 7b should report teacher network training and validation losses for completeness.
- The relative advantages and disadvantages of the different attention mechanisms across tasks remain unclear.
- Attention transfer does not yield student performance exceeding the teacher. Given the student's reduced parameter count, characterizing the speed-up benefit is essential. If student and teacher architectures are identical, the utility of attention transfer must be justified.

Summary:
Pros:
- Well-written and clearly motivated.
- Consistent student performance improvement with attention compared to student-only training.

Cons:
- Student networks achieve lower accuracy than teacher models.
- Unclear guidance on selecting optimal attention mechanisms for specific scenarios.
- Relatively incremental novelty compared to FitNet.
