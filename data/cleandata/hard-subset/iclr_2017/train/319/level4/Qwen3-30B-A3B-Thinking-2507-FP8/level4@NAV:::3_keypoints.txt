- Paper proposes attention transfer by minimizing L2 distance between teacher/student attention maps across layers, alongside classification loss and optional KD term.
- Authors define activation-based attention mechanisms (sum/max of |features|^p) and a gradient-based attention (loss derivative w.r.t. inputs).
- Experiments on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet show attention transfer improves student test performance versus student-only training.
- Despite improvements, student networks consistently underperform the teacher models.
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks show greater spatial correlation between objects and attention maps needs quantitative evidence beyond Figure 4.
- Rationale and sensitivity analysis for hyperparameter choices (e.g., impact of β) are lacking.
- Figure 7b is incomplete; teacher training and validation losses should be reported for completeness.
- The relative advantages and disadvantages of the different attention mechanisms across tasks are unclear.
- Attention transfer does not enable students to exceed teacher accuracy; speed-up/efficiency benefits for smaller students must be characterized.
- If student and teacher architectures are identical, the practical utility of attention transfer is not justified.
- Paper is well-written and clearly motivated.
- Attention transfer yields consistent student performance improvements compared to student-only training.
- Student models still achieve lower accuracy than teachers.
- Guidance on selecting optimal attention mechanisms for specific scenarios is missing.
- Novelty is relatively incremental compared to prior work like FitNet.
