- Introduces attention transfer (AT) as a novel knowledge distillation method for CNNs.  
- Defines two attention map types: activation-based (sum/max of absolute values^p) and gradient-based (input sensitivity).  
- Demonstrates AT improves student performance across datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, WRN, NiN).  
- Provides clear theoretical motivation, thorough experiments, and publicly available code.  
- Decision: Accept (Weak Accept).  
- Key reason 1: AT is a meaningful, distinct contribution beyond FitNets and standard KD.  
- Key reason 2: Consistent, well-documented experimental gains, notably on ImageNet where prior methods struggled.  
- Empirical gains: CIFAR-10 error reductions of ~0.8–1.5% when combining AT with KD.  
- ImageNet result: ResNet-18 sees +1.1% top-1 and +0.8% top-5 improvement over baseline student.  
- Ablations show activation-based AT outperforms full-activation transfer and gradient-based AT in many cases.  
- Claimed novelty: activation-based AT is the first KD method successfully applied on ImageNet.  
- Suggestion: Quantify attention quality (e.g., correlation with ground-truth object locations) beyond qualitative figures.  
- Suggestion: Perform hyperparameter sensitivity analysis for β (reported as ~0.1).  
- Suggestion: Compare student-with-AT to a teacher of the same architecture to clarify added benefit.  
- Suggestion: Provide systematic analysis explaining when activation-based vs gradient-based AT should be used.  
- Questions: Does AT help when teacher and student share the same architecture?  
- Questions: Why do attention maps contain more useful information than full activations across architectures?  
- Questions: Why does gradient-based AT outperform KD on CIFAR-10 in Table 3?  
- Conclusion: Well-executed, novel, and timely work; recommend acceptance while addressing suggested analyses to strengthen impact.
