- Paper proposes "attention transfer" using activation- and gradient-based spatial attention maps to improve student CNNs, showing empirical gains on CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet.  
- Decision: Reject — main issues are misleading terminology and poor integration of the gradient-based method.  
- Terminology critique: Calling the method "attention" is misleading — it’s essentially feature-map-statistics distillation (sums of absolutes/powers), not conventional attention mechanisms.  
- Gradient-based method critique: The gradient-based approach is poorly integrated, not directly compared to activation-based transfer, and appears tacked on to boost novelty without clear value.  
- Notation/reproducibility issue: Equation (2) ambiguously refers to "l2-normalized attention maps" but cites the matrix 2-norm (largest singular value) instead of the likely intended Frobenius norm, hindering reproducibility.  
- Suggested fixes: Rename method to avoid "attention," either integrate or remove the gradient-based component, clarify the norm used in Eq.(2), and better situate the method relative to existing distillation approaches and ImageNet results.  
- Key question 1: Why emphasize attention maps when transferring full activations shows minimal improvement — could results be experimental artifacts?  
- Key question 2: Why was the gradient-based method not directly compared to activation-based transfer in experiments?  
- Key question 3: Which matrix norm was implemented in Eq.(2) (spectral vs Frobenius) and why choose it?
