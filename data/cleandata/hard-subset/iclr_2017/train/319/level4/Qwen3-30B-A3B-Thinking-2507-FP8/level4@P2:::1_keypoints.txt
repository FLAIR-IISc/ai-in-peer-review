- Introduces attention-map-based knowledge transfer (activation- and gradient-based) and shows consistent improvements across datasets and architectures.  
- Well-motivated by human/visual attention literature; attention definitions align with Grad-CAM/CAM visualization work.  
- Contributions: systematic analysis of attention maps across depths, clear experimental validation framework, and open-sourced code.  
- Decision: Weak reject — promising but lacks strong evidence that attention transfer (AT) significantly outperforms knowledge distillation (KD) on ImageNet.  
- Core issue: ImageNet experiments (Section 4.2.2) report AT gains but do not compare AT to KD using identical hyperparameters.  
- Claim that KD "hurts convergence" on ImageNet is unsubstantiated without quantitative KD results on ImageNet.  
- CIFAR results show AT beating KD, but ImageNet is the critical large-scale benchmark and is not convincingly addressed.  
- Ablation: AT+KD slightly outperforms AT alone, but unclear if KD tuning could reproduce ImageNet gains, which would weaken AT’s novelty.  
- No statistical significance testing or standard deviations reported for ImageNet results to support the 1.1% top-1 claim.  
- Gradient-based transfer is theoretically sound but under-explained; double backpropagation cost is not quantified.  
- Empirical gap: paper does not justify why gradient-based method underperforms activation-based AT.  
- Requested fixes: add direct AT vs KD ImageNet comparisons with identical hyperparameters, clarify layer selection for ImageNet, include run-wise std devs, and report runtime overheads for gradient-based transfer.  
- Clarifying questions: provide exact KD ImageNet results with same hyperparameters, compare AT to prior distillation methods on ImageNet (e.g., FitNets), and justify emphasis on gradient-based transfer given its cost and lack of ImageNet results.
