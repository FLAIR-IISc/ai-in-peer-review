

# Review for ICLR 2017

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel knowledge transfer technique called "attention transfer" that leverages spatial attention maps from a teacher convolutional neural network (CNN) to improve the performance of a student CNN. The authors propose two types of attention maps (activation-based and gradient-based) and demonstrate consistent improvements across multiple datasets and network architectures. The key contribution is the demonstration that attention information, when properly defined, can significantly enhance student network performance through transfer learning. The paper presents extensive empirical validation on CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet datasets, showing that attention transfer outperforms standard knowledge distillation and FitNets-style approaches.

## Decision
Reject. The paper suffers from critical issues with conceptual clarity and presentation that undermine its contributions. Specifically: (1) the attention terminology is misleading and misrepresents the actual technique, and (2) the gradient-based attention transfer method is poorly integrated and fails to demonstrate meaningful value compared to the main contribution.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's primary contribution is the attention transfer framework, but the terminology used is fundamentally misleading. The authors define "attention" as the spatial statistics of feature maps (sum of absolute values, sum of powers, etc.), which bears no conceptual relationship to the psychological or computational attention mechanisms typically discussed in deep learning literature. This misnomer creates confusion about what the method actually achieves. The technique is essentially a form of feature map distillation that uses specific statistics (sum of absolute values, etc.) rather than the attention mechanisms the paper claims to leverage.

Furthermore, the gradient-based attention transfer method appears tacked on rather than integrated into the paper's main narrative. The authors never directly compare gradient-based methods to their proposed activation-based attention transfer, nor do they demonstrate how they might be combined to provide additional value. The method is mentioned in passing, without clear justification for its inclusion or how it advances the paper's central thesis about attention transfer. This creates an impression of a secondary idea that was added to the paper to increase novelty claims rather than a cohesive contribution.

The paper also contains critical notational issues. The description of the loss function in Equation (2) states the use of "l2-normalized attention maps" but then references the 2-norm of a matrix Q ∈ ℝ^(H×W), which would be the largest singular value (not the Frobenius norm). This is computationally expensive and inconsistent with standard practice in deep learning. It's highly likely the authors intended to use the Frobenius norm, which would be the standard interpretation of "l2 norm" for a matrix in this context. This ambiguity significantly hinders the reproducibility of their method.

## Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Completely revise the terminology to avoid the misleading "attention" label. The method is fundamentally a feature map statistics distillation technique, not an attention transfer method.
2. Either integrate the gradient-based method more cohesively with the main contribution (by comparing it directly to the activation-based approach and demonstrating complementary value), or remove it from the paper as it does not advance the core thesis.
3. Clarify the loss function notation in Equation (2), explicitly stating whether the authors used Frobenius norm or another interpretation of the 2-norm.
4. Provide a more nuanced discussion of the relationship between their method and existing knowledge distillation approaches, particularly addressing why their method works better than standard distillation when applied to ImageNet.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper claims that attention maps contain information more valuable for transfer than full activations, yet the experimental comparison (Table 1, column F-ActT) shows minimal improvements from transferring full activations. Given this, why does the paper emphasize the importance of attention maps over full activations? Could this be an artifact of the specific experimental setup?

2. The gradient-based attention transfer method is introduced in Section 3.2 but never directly compared to the activation-based approach in the experiments. Given that the paper's main contribution is attention transfer, why wasn't the gradient-based method evaluated against the activation-based approach to demonstrate relative value? 

3. In Equation (2), the authors use "l2-normalized attention maps" but reference the 2-norm of a matrix. Given that the 2-norm of a matrix is the largest singular value (computationally expensive), and the Frobenius norm is standard for matrix l2 norms in deep learning, can the authors clarify which norm they implemented and why they chose it over the more common Frobenius norm?
