

This paper investigates attention transfer as a mechanism to enhance knowledge distillation between teacher and student networks. The authors propose minimizing the L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps across layers, alongside standard classification and distillation losses. They define multiple attention mechanisms: activation-based (sum of absolute feature values raised to power p, or max values raised to power p) and gradient-based (derivative of loss w.r.t. inputs). Experiments on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet demonstrate that attention transfer consistently improves student performance over baselines without attention. However, student networks consistently underperform the teacher, even with attention transfer.

**Strengths:**  
The paper is clearly written and well-motivated, with a logical progression from problem statement to method. The experimental design is comprehensive, covering multiple datasets and attention variants. The consistent improvement of students with attention transfer (over non-attention distillation) is a valid contribution.

**Major Concerns:**  
1. **Performance Gap:** The student networks, despite attention transfer, remain significantly inferior to the teacher models (e.g., Table 1 shows 5-10% absolute accuracy gap on ImageNet). The authors do not address whether this gap is inherent to distillation or mitigable via architectural adjustments. Crucially, the paper claims attention transfer "helps," but without demonstrating that the student *surpasses* a non-distilled baseline or matches the teacher's performance, the practical value is limited. The authors should clarify: *Is the primary goal to improve student accuracy relative to non-distilled students, or to reduce the gap between student and teacher?* If the latter, the results fall short.

2. **Attention Mechanism Selection:** The paper evaluates multiple attention types (activation-based, gradient-based) but fails to provide clear guidance on *which mechanism to use in which scenario*. Table 2 shows minor variations in performance across attention types, yet the authors do not synthesize when one type outperforms others (e.g., "gradient attention excels on ImageNet due to..."). Without this, readers cannot determine the most appropriate attention for their tasks. The authors should add a comparative analysis (e.g., accuracy vs. computational cost, robustness across datasets) to justify mechanism choice.

3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The role of β (weighting the attention loss) is underexplored. Figure 7a shows performance with β=1, but the authors do not discuss how β affects results (e.g., "β=0.5 yields 0.8% higher accuracy than β=1 on CUB"). A sensitivity analysis is essential to validate the method’s robustness and provide practical guidance. The current presentation implies β=1 is optimal without justification.

4. **Missing Baseline Context:** The paper claims attention transfer improves student performance but does not compare against state-of-the-art distillation methods (e.g., FitNet, AT). While FitNet is mentioned as a related work, the authors do not position their method relative to it. *How does attention transfer specifically improve upon FitNet?* Without this, the novelty appears incremental. The authors should explicitly state how their attention-focused approach differs from and improves upon prior work.

5. **Methodological Gaps:**  
   - Figure 4 (spatial correlation) is compelling but lacks quantitative support. The authors claim "higher accuracy correlates with higher spatial correlation," yet no statistical metric (e.g., Pearson correlation coefficient) is provided. A brief quantitative analysis would strengthen this claim.  
   - Teacher training/validation losses (requested for Figure 7b) are critical for assessing overfitting/underfitting. Reporting these would clarify whether the student’s performance gap stems from the teacher’s limitations or the distillation process.  
   - The computational efficiency of attention transfer (e.g., inference speed, memory overhead) is unaddressed. If the student has fewer parameters but attention transfer adds computation, the net benefit is unclear. The authors should quantify speed/size trade-offs.

**Minor Points:**  
- "Attention transfers does help" → "Attention transfer helps" (grammar).  
- Clarify that "sum of absolute feature values raised to power p" refers to *element-wise* absolute values raised to p.  
- The gradient-based attention is derived from the loss w.r.t. inputs (not "derivative of the Loss w.r.t. inputs" – standard phrasing).

**Summary:**  
The paper presents a reasonable extension to knowledge distillation via attention transfer, with solid experimental validation of student improvement. However, the lack of a meaningful performance gap reduction (vs. teacher), absence of clear guidance for attention mechanism selection, and insufficient novelty relative to FitNet undermine its contribution. The authors should address the hyperparameter sensitivity, provide comparative baselines against state-of-the-art distillation methods, and clarify the practical utility of their approach (e.g., "student with attention achieves 85% of teacher accuracy while using 30% fewer parameters"). Without these, the work remains incremental.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Weak Reject**. The method is technically sound but lacks sufficient novelty and practical impact to warrant publication. The core claim—that attention transfer significantly improves student performance—remains unconvincing without a meaningful reduction in the teacher-student gap. Addressing the concerns above would require substantial revision (e.g., new experiments comparing against FitNet, quantifying the teacher-student gap reduction, and analyzing attention mechanism selection). As-is, the paper offers little beyond a minor extension of existing distillation techniques.
