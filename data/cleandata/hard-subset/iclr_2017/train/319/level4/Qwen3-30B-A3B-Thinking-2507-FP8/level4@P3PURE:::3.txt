

This paper investigates attention transfer between teacher and student networks, where attention maps at multiple layers are aligned by minimizing the L2 distance between teacher and student activations, in addition to standard classification loss and optional knowledge distillation. The authors define several activation-based attention mechanisms (sum of absolute feature values raised to power p, or maximum values raised to power p) and propose a gradient-based attention mechanism derived from the loss gradient with respect to inputs. Experiments on CIFAR, CUB-200/Scene-15, and ImageNet demonstrate that attention transfer consistently improves student network test performance compared to the student model without attention. However, the student networks still underperform the teacher, even with attention transfer.

Major concerns:
- In Section 3, the authors claim that higher-accuracy networks exhibit stronger spatial correlation between objects and attention maps. While Figure 4 is compelling, quantitative metrics (e.g., correlation coefficients) supporting this claim are missing.
- The selection of hyperparameters (particularly β for attention loss weighting) lacks justification. An analysis of β's impact on performance would be valuable.
- Figure 7b should include teacher network training and validation losses to enable clearer comparison of optimization dynamics.
- The experimental results do not clarify the relative advantages or disadvantages of different attention mechanisms (activation-based vs. gradient-based) in specific scenarios.
- Attention transfer does not surpass teacher performance, though students have fewer parameters. The computational efficiency gain (e.g., inference speed, parameter count reduction) should be characterized. If student and teacher share identical architectures, the benefit of attention transfer becomes unclear.

Summary:
Pros:
- Well-motivated and clearly written.
- Demonstrates consistent improvement of student networks through attention transfer compared to baseline students.

Cons:
- Student networks consistently underperform the teacher, even with attention transfer.
- The choice of optimal attention mechanism for different tasks or datasets remains ambiguous.
- The novelty appears relatively incremental compared to prior work (e.g., FitNet).
