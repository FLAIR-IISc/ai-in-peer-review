

This paper investigates attention transfer between teacher and student networks, minimizing the L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps across layers alongside classification loss and optional knowledge distillation. The authors define several activation-based attention mechanisms (sum or max of absolute feature values raised to power p) and propose a gradient-based attention (derivative of loss with respect to inputs). Experiments on CIFAR, CUB-200, Scene-15, and ImageNet demonstrate that attention transfer consistently improves student network test performance. However, student networks still underperform the teacher, even with attention transfer.

Key concerns:
- Section 3 claims higher-accuracy networks exhibit stronger spatial correlation between objects and attention maps. While Figure 4 provides compelling visual evidence, quantitative metrics (e.g., correlation coefficients) would strengthen this claim.
- Hyperparameter selection (particularly β) lacks justification. A sensitivity analysis showing β's impact on performance would be valuable.
- Figure 7(b) should report teacher training and validation losses to contextualize student performance comparisons.
- The paper does not clarify the relative strengths of different attention mechanisms. It remains unclear when to prefer activation-based vs. gradient-based attention for specific tasks or architectures.
- Since attention transfer fails to match teacher performance despite reducing student parameters, the speed/efficiency gains require quantification. If student and teacher share identical architectures, what tangible benefit does attention transfer provide?

Summary:
Pros:
- Well-written and well-motivated.
- Demonstrates consistent student performance gains with attention transfer versus student-only baselines.

Cons:
- Student networks consistently underperform the teacher, even with attention transfer.
- Unclear guidance on selecting the optimal attention mechanism for a given scenario.
- Novelty appears somewhat incremental relative to FitNets.
