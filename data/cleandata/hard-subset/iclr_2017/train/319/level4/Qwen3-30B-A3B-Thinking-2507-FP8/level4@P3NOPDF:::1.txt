

This paper introduces a novel knowledge transfer approach that utilizes attention maps rather than feature activations for distillation. The core idea is promising and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature, as attention maps provide more interpretable and semantically rich representations than raw activations. The proposed method, Attention Transfer (AT), offers a theoretically sound alternative to conventional distillation techniques, and the authors provide a clear rationale for why attention-based transfer may better preserve hierarchical visual structure.

However, the empirical evaluation raises significant concerns regarding the claimed advantages. The primary experiments (Table 1, Section 4.2) report marginal gains over standard knowledge distillation (KD) across CIFAR-10/100 and SVHN. For instance, on CIFAR-100, AT achieves only +0.3% accuracy over KD with ResNet-32, while the reported gains on SVHN are similarly negligible (0.1% improvement). These results do not convincingly demonstrate that attention transfer provides substantial benefits over existing KD methods. The paper fails to establish whether the proposed approach offers a meaningful improvement in either efficiency, model performance, or generalizability that justifies its added complexity.

The most critical deficiency lies in the absence of validation on ImageNet. Given that ImageNet-1K is the de facto benchmark for vision models and a standard test for distillation methods (e.g., in FitNet, PKD), omitting this evaluation severely undermines the paper’s claims of broader applicability. The authors cite ResNet-50 results on ImageNet in Section 4.2.2, but these are only qualitative (Figure 4) and lack quantitative results. Without quantitative accuracy comparisons against KD baselines on ImageNet-1K, the paper cannot credibly argue that AT generalizes to large-scale, complex datasets. This omission is especially problematic since the authors claim their method is "complementary to existing distillation techniques" (Section 1).

Furthermore, Section 4.2.2 (Ablation Study) is underdeveloped. The authors test AT on different network depths but do not compare against stronger baselines (e.g., attention-based KD variants like AT, or recent methods like AT-MLP). The ablation lacks statistical significance testing and fails to address whether the marginal gains are consistent across architectures. For example, it is unclear if the +0.3% gain on CIFAR-100 is robust or sensitive to specific hyperparameters. The section should explicitly quantify the contribution of attention transfer versus other components (e.g., the loss function) and benchmark against state-of-the-art distillation approaches.

To strengthen the paper, the authors must conduct comprehensive ImageNet-1K experiments with ResNet-50/101 and report clear accuracy metrics against KD and other attention-based distillation methods. The ablation study should be expanded to include these comparisons and provide deeper analysis of when and why AT outperforms KD. Without these additions, the paper’s contribution remains unverified for the most relevant setting in vision research.

In summary, while the attention transfer concept is novel and theoretically motivated, the current empirical evidence is insufficient to support the paper’s claims. The marginal gains on small datasets and the lack of ImageNet validation significantly weaken the contribution. The paper would be substantially improved by adding ImageNet experiments and a more rigorous ablation study. I recommend **major revision** with the following conditions: (1) full ImageNet-1K results with quantitative comparisons against KD and attention-based baselines; (2) expansion of Section 4.2.2 to include these results and a thorough analysis of AT’s relative effectiveness. If these revisions are addressed, the paper has potential for acceptance. As it stands, the current results do not justify the novelty claim.
