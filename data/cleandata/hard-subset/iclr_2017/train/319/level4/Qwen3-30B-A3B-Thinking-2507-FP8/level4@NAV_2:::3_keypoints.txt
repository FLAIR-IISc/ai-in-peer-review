- Paper proposes attention-based knowledge transfer using activation- and gradient-based spatial attention maps and reports consistent empirical improvements.
- Recommendation: reject.
- Primary concern 1: student networks still consistently underperform teacher networks even after attention transfer.
- Primary concern 2: paper does not establish clear advantages over existing knowledge distillation when student and teacher architectures are comparable.
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks show stronger attention-object correlation is unsupported quantitatively; Figure 4 is only qualitative.
- Hyperparameter β selection lacks justification and the effect of different β values on performance is not explored.
- Figure 7(b) omits teacher training and validation loss, obscuring learning dynamics and comparisons.
- Paper does not clearly articulate when different attention mapping functions (sum vs. max, different p values) are preferable.
- Authors should provide quantitative attention-object correlation metrics to support their claim.
- Authors should conduct a systematic hyperparameter sensitivity analysis (especially for β).
- Authors should include teacher training/validation metrics for all experiments to clarify dynamics.
- Authors should delineate scenarios where each attention transfer method outperforms others.
- Clarifying question: what is the specific impact of different β values on attention transfer performance?
- Clarifying question: how does attention transfer compare to standard knowledge distillation when student and teacher have identical architectures/depths?
- Clarifying question: can the authors provide metrics on computational efficiency gains using smaller student networks with attention transfer vs larger teacher networks?
