

The paper proposes a novel framework for knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks (CNNs) by leveraging attention maps—spatial representations of where a network focuses during inference—rather than traditional activation or output-based distillation. The authors define two types of attention maps (activation-based and gradient-based), demonstrate their correlation with network performance, and propose methods to transfer these maps from a teacher to a student network. Key contributions include systematic exploration of attention mapping functions, empirical validation across diverse architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network), and consistent improvements over baselines on CIFAR-10/100, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet. The approach is well-motivated by the observation that higher-performing networks exhibit more discriminative attention patterns, and the authors provide clear theoretical and empirical grounding for their methods.

**Decision: Reject**  
The paper’s core claim—that attention transfer (AT) provides significant, generalizable improvements beyond knowledge distillation (KD)—is not sufficiently supported by the evidence. Specifically:  
1. The ImageNet results (Section 4.2.2) fail to demonstrate AT’s superiority over KD, as the authors report KD’s failure to converge on ImageNet without explaining why it works on CIFAR but not ImageNet. This undermines their claim that AT is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet."  
2. On CIFAR, AT + KD outperforms KD alone (Table 3), but the marginal gains (e.g., 11.2% vs. 12.1% error) are small relative to the complexity of the method, and the paper does not establish that AT *adds value* beyond KD (e.g., whether KD alone would suffice).  

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper’s primary weakness lies in the ImageNet evaluation. The authors state that KD "actually hurts convergence" on ImageNet with ResNet-18/34 pairs, yet they do not:  
- Compare AT against KD *on the same ImageNet setup* (e.g., using identical hyperparameters, architectures, and training protocols). Without this, the claim that AT "is the first successful transfer method on ImageNet" is unsubstantiated.  
- Justify why KD fails on ImageNet but succeeds on CIFAR (where they report KD + AT improvements). This discrepancy is critical—e.g., is it due to dataset scale, architecture differences, or hyperparameter sensitivity? The paper glosses over this.  
- Provide ablation studies showing AT’s *incremental* benefit over KD (e.g., AT + KD vs. KD alone on ImageNet). Instead, they only report AT’s standalone results (1.1% top-1 gain), which is insufficient to claim AT’s necessity.  

The CIFAR experiments (Section 4.1) show promise, but the gains are modest (e.g., 7.93% error vs. 8.77% baseline in Table 1), and the paper does not rigorously establish that attention maps carry *unique* information beyond full activations (Section 4.1.2). While the authors note AT outperforms full-activation transfer, they do not quantify *why*—e.g., is it due to reduced noise, or does it capture higher-level semantic information? This ambiguity weakens the core contribution.  

**Additional Feedback**  
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:  
1. **Re-evaluate ImageNet experiments**: Run AT + KD on ImageNet with hyperparameters tuned for KD (e.g., temperature, α) to isolate AT’s contribution. If KD fails due to architectural mismatch (e.g., ResNet-18 vs. ResNet-34), test AT + KD on *identical* architectures (e.g., ResNet-18 teacher/student) to clarify whether AT enables KD’s success.  
2. **Clarify the "attention advantage"**: Add experiments comparing AT to KD on CIFAR using identical hyperparameters (not just AT + KD vs. KD). If AT’s gains are marginal, acknowledge this and discuss trade-offs (e.g., computational cost vs. performance).  
3. **Improve theoretical grounding**: Explain why attention maps (vs. activations) contain transferable knowledge. For instance, do attention maps better encode *discriminative regions* (e.g., eyes in face recognition), or do they simply reduce dimensionality?  

**Questions for Authors**  
1. The paper claims KD fails on ImageNet but works on CIFAR (Section 4.2.2). Could you clarify *why* this discrepancy occurs? Specifically, did you tune KD hyperparameters (e.g., temperature, α) for ImageNet, or did you use CIFAR values directly?  
2. In Table 5, ResNet-18 + AT achieves 1.1% top-1 gain over baseline. How much of this is due to AT vs. KD (which was not used on ImageNet)? If AT + KD were applied, would it further improve results, or would KD’s failure persist?  
3. In Section 4.1.2, you state "attention maps carry information more important for transfer than full activations." Can you provide a quantitative comparison (e.g., feature correlation analysis) showing *how* attention maps differ from full activations in predictive value?
