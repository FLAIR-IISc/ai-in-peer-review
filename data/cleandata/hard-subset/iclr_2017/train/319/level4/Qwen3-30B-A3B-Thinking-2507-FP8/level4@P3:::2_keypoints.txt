- Proposes transferring attention maps (activation- or gradient-based) from teacher to student, showing empirical improvements across vision datasets.  
- Decision: Reject — conceptual misalignment between claims and methodology undermines scientific rigor.  
- Terminology critique: calling the method "attention" is misleading; it transfers feature-map statistics, not psychological or standard attention mechanisms.  
- Gradient-based attention is poorly motivated and presented as a separate, disconnected method.  
- No ablation or comparative analysis demonstrating necessity or benefit of the gradient-based approach.  
- Technical ambiguity in Equation (2): unclear whether the norm is Frobenius or induced 2-norm, impacting computational cost and reproducibility.  
- Recommendation: reframe terminology (e.g., "feature map statistics" or "spatial activation patterns").  
- Recommendation: integrate, justify, or remove the gradient-based method to maintain conceptual focus.  
- Recommendation: explicitly state and justify the norm choice and its computational implications.  
- Recommendation: expand ablation studies to compare activation vs. gradient vs. full-activation transfer and to justify normalization choices.  
- Question: reconcile claim that attention maps encode decision focus with observation that mid-level maps capture low-level features.  
- Question: clarify discrepancy where gradient-based transfer slightly outperforms activation-based transfer in Table 3.  
- Question: justify using l2 normalization over l1 and report sensitivity to normalization choice.  
- Question: quantify double backpropagation cost and its impact on training time versus standard knowledge distillation.  
- Conclusion: empirical results are promising but conceptual misrepresentation and technical ambiguities require significant revision before acceptance.
