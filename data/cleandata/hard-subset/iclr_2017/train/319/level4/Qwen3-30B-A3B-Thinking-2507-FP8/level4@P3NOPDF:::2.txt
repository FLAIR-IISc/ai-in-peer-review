

This paper proposes a modified knowledge distillation framework that minimizes the difference in the sum of statistics (e.g., summed squared activations or Lp norms) across feature maps between teacher and student networks. The authors demonstrate empirical improvements over the FitNet baseline on multiple computer vision datasets. While the core idea shows practical utility, the paper suffers from significant conceptual and notational ambiguities that undermine its contribution.

**Strengths:**  
The work is well-motivated and evaluated across diverse vision benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet), providing solid empirical evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The writing is generally clear and accessible, with a logical flow from problem statement to experiments.

**Major Concerns:**  
1. **Misleading Terminology ("Attention"):**  
   The paper repeatedly uses "attention" to describe the proposed distillation mechanism, which is inaccurate. The method does not model attentional mechanisms (e.g., spatial weighting or feature importance) but rather distills *summed statistics* of feature maps (e.g., ∑|f|² or ∑|f|ᵖ). This misnomer creates confusion, as "attention" implies a learned, adaptive weighting process, whereas the approach is purely statistical. The authors should replace "attention" with precise terms like "statistical distillation" or "feature map summary distillation" throughout.

2. **Irrelevant Gradient-Based Component:**  
   The inclusion of gradient-based attention transfer (Section 3.2) appears disconnected from the main contribution. The authors neither compare this technique directly to the proposed statistical distillation nor integrate it with the "attention-based" approach. This creates a disjointed narrative: the gradient method is presented as a parallel idea with minimal justification, and its lack of synergy with the primary method raises questions about its relevance. If included, it must be rigorously evaluated against the core approach or removed.

3. **Ambiguous Norm Definition (Eq. 2):**  
   Equation (2) defines a cost function involving "induced 2-norms" of matrices Q ∈ ℝ^{H×W}. However, the induced 2-norm (spectral norm) for a matrix is its largest singular value, which is computationally expensive to compute for large feature maps (H, W ≈ 100–200). Given the context—distilling summed statistics—the authors likely intend the Frobenius norm (which is ∑|Q_{ij}|²), a standard choice for such losses and computationally efficient. This discrepancy must be clarified; otherwise, the implementation details are unclear, and the cost function’s feasibility is questionable. A brief justification for the norm choice (e.g., "we use the Frobenius norm as it aligns with the sum of squared activations") is essential.

4. **Limited Scope to Convolutional Architectures:**  
   The method is explicitly designed for convolutional networks (e.g., ResNet, VGG), with no discussion of applicability to transformers, MLPs, or other non-CNN backbones. Given the growing diversity of vision architectures, this limitation is significant but not acknowledged as a constraint. The authors should explicitly state this scope or propose extensions for broader applicability.

**Additional Minor Issues:**  
- The "attention" terminology in the abstract and introduction misleads readers about the method’s nature.  
- The evaluation lacks ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the statistical distillation from other components (e.g., residual connections in the student).  
- The computational cost of the proposed loss (especially for spectral norm) is unaddressed, despite being a key practical consideration.

**Overall Assessment:**  
The core idea—distilling feature map statistics—shows promise and demonstrates empirical gains over FitNet. However, the paper’s conceptual inaccuracies, particularly the misuse of "attention," and the poorly integrated gradient-based component weaken its contribution. The ambiguity around the norm definition further erodes confidence in the method’s implementation. While the experiments are well-executed, the theoretical framing and notation require substantial revision to align with the technical substance.

**Recommendation:**  
**Reject.** The work’s practical value is overshadowed by fundamental conceptual flaws and unclear exposition. Addressing the terminology issues (replacing "attention" with precise descriptions), clarifying the norm definition (Frobenius vs. spectral), and justifying the gradient-based component’s relevance would be necessary for a meaningful revision. As written, the paper risks misleading readers about its technical novelty and feasibility.
