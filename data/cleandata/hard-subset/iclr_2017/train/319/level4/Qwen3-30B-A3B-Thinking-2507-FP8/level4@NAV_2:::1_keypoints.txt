- Introduces using attention maps for knowledge transfer in CNNs instead of direct activation transfer.  
- Proposes two methods: activation-based attention transfer and gradient-based attention transfer.  
- Reports consistent performance gains across multiple datasets and architectures (ResNet variants, Network-In-Network).  
- Shows attention maps contain valuable distillation information; activation-based transfer outperforms full-activation transfer.  
- Demonstrates synergy between activation-based attention transfer and traditional knowledge distillation.  
- Well-situated in related work with clear theoretical motivation and experimental validation.  
- Primary critique: insufficient experimental validation on ImageNet; reported 1.1% top-1 gain is marginal and not rigorously compared to SOTA KD methods.  
- Paper does not explain why knowledge distillation succeeds on CIFAR but fails on ImageNet, leaving scalability questions unanswered.  
- ImageNet results are underdeveloped: improvements reported with minimal hyperparameter tuning and KD reportedly hurts convergence under CIFAR hyperparameters.  
- Claim of being the first successful KT method on ImageNet is contradicted by lack of thorough comparisons with KD variants using identical architectures.  
- Missing ablation studies demonstrating attention transfer significantly outperforms KD on ImageNet.  
- Limited experimental choices (only two residual block losses, no hyperparameter optimization) weaken the conclusions.  
- Recommended: run comprehensive ImageNet experiments comparing attention transfer against KD with identical networks and hyperparameters.  
- Recommended: analyze why KD fails on ImageNet and how attention transfer addresses those failures.  
- Recommended: add ablations on loss placement (all residual groups vs. two) and attention map types (F²sum vs. others) on ImageNet, and clarify symmetry/BatchNorm interactions in gradient-based transfer.  
- Overall: current ImageNet evidence is preliminary and insufficient for acceptance.
