

# Review for ICLR 2017

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks through attention transfer. The authors propose two methods for defining spatial attention maps—activation-based (using sum or max of absolute feature values raised to a power) and gradient-based (using gradients with respect to input)—and demonstrate that transferring these attention maps from a teacher network to a student network can significantly improve the student's performance across multiple datasets and architectures. The paper makes a compelling case for attention as a valuable form of knowledge transfer, showing consistent improvements over baseline students, and demonstrates that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer. The contributions are well-motivated and the experimental evaluation is thorough across CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet datasets.

## Decision
Reject. The paper presents an interesting idea but fails to provide sufficient evidence that attention transfer is a meaningful advance over existing knowledge distillation techniques. The most significant concern is that the student networks, even with attention transfer, consistently underperform the teacher networks, which undermines the primary motivation for knowledge transfer. Furthermore, the incremental novelty relative to existing work like FitNets is not sufficiently justified.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's key contribution is the use of attention maps as a form of knowledge transfer. However, the results show that students with attention transfer still underperform their teachers, which is counter to the fundamental premise of knowledge distillation. The authors state that "the main motivation for residual networks was increasing depth, it was later shown that... improvements came mostly from increased capacity," suggesting that attention transfer should help students achieve comparable performance to teachers with fewer parameters. Yet the paper does not demonstrate that students with attention transfer can match teacher performance while having fewer parameters—a critical requirement for knowledge distillation.

The paper also fails to clearly establish when each attention method should be used. The authors show that activation-based attention transfer with F²sum outperforms other attention mapping functions, but they do not provide sufficient justification for why this particular function is superior across different tasks or architectures. The paper claims "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer," but does not adequately explain why this is the case or provide a principled framework for selecting the most appropriate attention mechanism for a given task.

The novelty relative to existing work is also questionable. The paper builds on knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) and FitNets (Romero et al., 2014), but the authors do not clearly articulate how their attention transfer differs from these approaches in a meaningful way. The paper claims to be "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," but this is not supported by a comprehensive comparison with other distillation techniques on ImageNet.

## Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Clearly establish the performance gap between student and teacher networks with attention transfer, particularly whether students can match teacher performance while having fewer parameters. This is essential for justifying attention transfer as a meaningful knowledge distillation technique.

2. Provide a more thorough comparison with existing knowledge distillation methods on ImageNet, including a full ablation study showing how attention transfer compares to KD, FitNets, and other approaches.

3. Include quantitative measures of the spatial correlation between attention maps and object regions (beyond the qualitative examples in Figure 4), to better support the claim that "higher accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between the object and the attention map."

4. Investigate the impact of hyperparameters (particularly β) on performance and provide guidance on selecting appropriate values for different tasks.

5. Clarify the computational overhead of gradient-based attention transfer, especially regarding batch normalization during second backpropagation, which the authors note is "unclear."

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper claims that "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer," but does not provide a clear explanation for why this is the case. Can the authors provide a theoretical or empirical justification for why attention maps carry more useful information than full activations?

2. The paper states that "attention transfer is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," but does not compare with other distillation methods on ImageNet. How does attention transfer compare to KD, FitNets, and other techniques on ImageNet in terms of performance, computational efficiency, and parameter efficiency?

3. The authors show that students with attention transfer still underperform their teachers. Can they demonstrate that with attention transfer, a student network with fewer parameters can achieve performance comparable to the teacher network (with more parameters), which is the fundamental goal of knowledge distillation?
