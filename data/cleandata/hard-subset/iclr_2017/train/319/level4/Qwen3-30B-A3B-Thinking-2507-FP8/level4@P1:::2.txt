

The paper proposes a novel knowledge distillation approach that transfers attention maps—defined as spatial statistics of feature activations—from a teacher to a student network. The authors demonstrate consistent performance improvements across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10/100, ImageNet, CUB, Scenes) and architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network), using both activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer methods. The core contribution lies in showing that distilling *spatial activation statistics* (e.g., summed absolute values, max-pooled activations) yields superior results compared to distilling raw activations or standard knowledge distillation.

**Decision:** Reject.  
**Key reasons:**  
1. The term "attention" is misleadingly applied; the method distills activation statistics, not attention as understood in vision or cognitive science.  
2. The gradient-based attention transfer is poorly integrated and lacks meaningful comparison to the primary activation-based approach.

**Supporting arguments:**  
The paper’s central claim—that "attention" can be transferred to improve student networks—is fundamentally flawed. The proposed "attention maps" are simply spatial summaries (e.g., ∑|Aᵢ|) of feature activations, not attention mechanisms that model *where* a network focuses during decision-making. This misuses the term "attention" (which implies selective focus on relevant regions), conflating it with generic activation statistics. For example:  
- Figure 2 shows "attention maps" that correlate with object locations, but these are direct artifacts of high activation in relevant regions—not *attention* (e.g., no evidence of the network *choosing* to focus on those regions).  
- The authors acknowledge that "attention maps focus on different parts for different layers," yet this is merely a property of activation magnitudes, not attention.  

The gradient-based transfer (Section 3.2) further undermines the paper’s core premise. It is introduced as a parallel method but never compared to activation-based transfer (the paper’s focus), nor is it combined with it. The results in Table 3 show gradient-based transfer *alone* achieves 11.8% error (vs. 11.2% for activation-based AT), yet the paper treats gradient-based as a "novel" contribution without justification. This feels like an afterthought, not a cohesive extension. The method also requires costly double backpropagation (Section 3.2), while activation-based transfer is simpler and more effective.  

Critically, the paper’s equation (2) is misstated. The authors claim to use "l2-normalized attention maps" (‖Qⱼˢ‖Qⱼˢ‖₂ − Qⱼᵀ‖Qⱼᵀ‖₂‖₂), but the norm described is the *Frobenius norm*, not the induced 2-norm (which would require SVD). The induced 2-norm (largest singular value) is computationally prohibitive for this context. This error suggests a lack of precision in the method’s formulation.  

**Additional feedback for improvement:**  
- **Rename the core contribution:** The paper should abandon "attention" and instead describe the method as "distilling spatial activation statistics" or "feature map normalization transfer." This aligns with the actual mechanism and avoids confusion with established attention literature (e.g., Bahdanau et al., 2014; Xu et al., 2015).  
- **Clarify Equation (2):** Explicitly state that the loss uses the Frobenius norm (not induced 2-norm) for computational feasibility.  
- **Integrate gradient-based transfer:** Either compare it head-to-head with activation-based transfer (e.g., Table 3 should include a combined AT+gradient row) or remove it as a separate method.  
- **Strengthen motivation:** Explain *why* activation statistics (not raw activations) carry transferable information. The current justification ("stronger networks have peaks where weak ones don’t") is descriptive, not causal.  

**Questions for authors:**  
1. How do the authors reconcile the term "attention" with the fact that the maps are purely activation-based (not attention-driven)? Could you provide examples where the network *selectively focuses* on regions (e.g., via attention gates or masks), versus simply having high activation?  
2. Why was gradient-based transfer not combined with activation-based transfer, given the authors’ claim that "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer"? If they are complementary, why not test them jointly?  
3. In Equation (2), the norm is described as "l2," but the implementation likely uses Frobenius. Please clarify this to avoid confusion.  

This work has empirical merit but is undermined by terminological imprecision and a disjointed presentation. With a clear rebranding of the method and tighter integration of the gradient-based component, it could be a strong contribution. As written, however, it misleads readers about the nature of the proposed mechanism.
