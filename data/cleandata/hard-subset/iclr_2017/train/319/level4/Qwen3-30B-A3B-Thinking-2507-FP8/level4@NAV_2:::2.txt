

The paper proposes a novel method for knowledge distillation by transferring spatial attention maps from a teacher convolutional neural network to a student network, demonstrating consistent performance improvements across multiple datasets and architectures. The authors define attention as spatial maps derived from either activation statistics or gradient information, and introduce two distinct transfer mechanisms to enhance student network performance.

Reject. The paper's key contribution is significantly undermined by a fundamental misrepresentation of the proposed method as "attention transfer" when it is actually transferring statistics of feature maps (sum of absolute values, sum of powers, etc.). Additionally, the gradient-based attention transfer component is not properly integrated with the main method and lacks meaningful comparison.

The paper's misuse of the term "attention" is misleading and scientifically inaccurate. The spatial maps described are not attention mechanisms in the psychological or neural network sense, but rather statistics computed from feature maps. The authors should clarify whether they are referring to feature map statistics rather than attention, as the term "attention" implies a cognitive process that is not being modeled or transferred.

The gradient-based attention transfer section appears disconnected from the main contribution. It is never compared directly to the activation-based approach, nor is it used jointly with it. The method seems tacked on rather than integrated, and its value to the main contribution is unclear. The paper would be stronger if this component was either fully integrated with the main method or removed.

The notation in equation (2) is confusing and potentially incorrect. The authors reference an induced 2-norm for matrix Q ∈ ℝ^(H×W), which would require computing the largest singular value (computationally expensive), but the context strongly suggests they mean the Frobenius norm (commonly used for such loss functions). This ambiguity needs clarification.

Additional feedback: The paper would benefit from a clearer distinction between the concept of attention (as used in cognitive science and neural networks) and the feature map statistics being transferred. The authors should consider renaming the approach to avoid confusion with the well-established attention mechanisms in neural networks. The gradient-based method should either be fully integrated with the main approach or omitted to maintain coherence.

Questions for authors:
1. Can you clarify what norm is intended in equation (2) and why it is not the Frobenius norm, which would be more computationally efficient and standard for such loss functions?
2. How does the gradient-based attention transfer specifically relate to the activation-based approach, and why is it presented as a separate component rather than integrated with the main method?
3. Given that the spatial maps described do not correspond to attention mechanisms as understood in the literature, what is the justification for using the term "attention" in the title and throughout the paper?
