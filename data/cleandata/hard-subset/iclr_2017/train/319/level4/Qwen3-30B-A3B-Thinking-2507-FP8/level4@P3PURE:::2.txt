

This paper proposes a modified knowledge distillation framework that minimizes the difference in the sum of statistics (e.g., summed squared activations or other norms) across feature maps between teacher and student networks. The authors empirically demonstrate that their method outperforms the FitNet-style distillation baseline across multiple computer vision datasets.  

**Strengths:**  
+ The method is evaluated on diverse computer vision datasets, providing strong empirical support.  
+ The paper is well-written and clearly structured overall.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- The approach appears restricted to convolutional architectures, limiting its broader applicability.  
- The use of "attention" terminology is misleading. The method does not involve attention mechanisms but rather distills summed statistics (e.g., squared activations or Lp-norms) of feature maps.  
- The gradient-based attention transfer component seems disconnected from the core contribution. It is never directly compared against the "attention-based" transfer, nor is it integrated with it. This appears as an unrelated addition that fails to demonstrate significant added value.  
- The computation of the induced 2-norm in Equation (2) is unclear. Given $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W}$ (a feature map matrix), its induced 2-norm is its largest singular value, which would be computationally expensive. It is unclear whether the authors intended the Frobenius norm instead, which is standard for such statistics.  

Overall, while the proposed distillation method is effective in practice, the paper suffers from organizational issues, unclear notation, and misleading terminology.
