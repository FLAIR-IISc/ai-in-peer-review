

The paper introduces a novel knowledge transfer approach using attention maps, rather than raw activations, to improve student network performance through teacher-student learning. The authors define two attention mechanisms—activation-based and gradient-based—and demonstrate consistent improvements across multiple datasets and architectures, including ResNet and Wide ResNet variants. The concept is well-motivated by human attention theory and recent advances in visualizing neural network behavior, with clear contributions: (1) attention as a knowledge transfer mechanism, (2) dual attention map definitions, (3) empirical validation across tasks, and (4) compatibility with knowledge distillation. The technical execution is thorough, with detailed ablation studies on attention functions and normalization strategies.

**Decision: Reject.** The primary reason is that the claimed "significant improvements" on ImageNet (Section 4.2.2) do not convincingly outperform standard knowledge distillation (KD), which undermines the paper’s core contribution. Specifically, the paper asserts that attention transfer (AT) is "the first successful knowledge transfer method applied on ImageNet," yet KD on ImageNet with the same architecture (ResNet-18/34) *fails to converge* under identical hyperparameters used in CIFAR experiments. The authors acknowledge this but do not provide sufficient evidence that AT resolves this issue—instead, they report only a modest 1.1% top-1 gain for AT on ImageNet, while KD *converges significantly slower* (and they stopped training due to resource constraints). This leaves the key claim unsupported.

**Supporting arguments:**  
The experiments fail to establish AT’s superiority over KD on ImageNet, the paper’s central benchmark. While AT improves ResNet-18 accuracy by 1.1% top-1 (Table 5), KD’s performance is not properly evaluated under the same conditions. The authors note KD "hurts convergence" on ImageNet but do not show *how much* AT improves upon this baseline. Crucially, they admit KD *works well on CIFAR* (Section 4.1.3) but fails on ImageNet *despite identical hyperparameters*, suggesting architectural mismatch—not AT’s inherent superiority—drives the difference. Without rigorous comparison (e.g., AT vs. KD *on ImageNet* with hyperparameter tuning), the claim that AT is "the first successful method" remains unsubstantiated. The paper also omits critical ablations: Why does AT outperform KD on CUB (Section 4.2.1) but not on ImageNet? This discrepancy requires explanation.

**Additional feedback (to improve the paper):**  
1. **Extend Section 4.2.2 with ImageNet experiments comparing AT and KD directly.** Report convergence curves and final accuracy *for both methods* using identical hyperparameters and training resources. Without this, the ImageNet results cannot support the paper’s main claim.  
2. **Clarify the architectural mismatch issue.** The authors note that KD fails on ImageNet due to "different architecture/depth" (citing CIFAR observations), but the ResNet-18/34 pair *is* the same architecture (just different depth). Explain why depth mismatch causes KD failure here but not on CIFAR.  
3. **Quantify AT’s advantage over KD on CUB.** Section 4.2.1 states AT "works much better" than KD on CUB, but no numerical comparison is provided. Include error rates for both methods to justify this claim.  
4. **Address gradient-based AT’s limitations.** Section 4.1.3 notes that gradient-based AT requires "complex automatic differentiation" and excludes batch normalization. Explain why this restricts applicability to modern networks (which rely on BN), and whether AT can be adapted to work with BN in second backpropagation.

**Questions for authors:**  
1. In Table 5, ResNet-18 + AT achieves 72.3% top-1 on ImageNet. What is the *exact* accuracy of ResNet-18 + KD (with hyperparameters tuned *specifically for ImageNet*)?  
2. The paper states KD "struggles to work if teacher and student have different architecture/depth" (citing CIFAR), yet ResNet-18/34 have the same architecture (different depth). Please clarify this contradiction and provide evidence that AT resolves the depth-mismatch issue on ImageNet.  
3. In Section 4.2.1, AT closes the accuracy gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34 on CUB. How does this gap compare to KD’s performance? If KD also closes the gap, why does AT perform "much better"?  

This work has conceptual merit but requires stronger empirical validation to support its central thesis. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper’s contribution to knowledge transfer.
