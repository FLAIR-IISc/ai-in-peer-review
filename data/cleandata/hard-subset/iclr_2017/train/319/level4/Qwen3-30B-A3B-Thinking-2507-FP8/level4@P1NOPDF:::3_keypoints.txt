- Paper proposes attention transfer by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps alongside classification loss and optional KD.
- Defines activation-based attention (sum/max of |features|^p) and a gradient-based attention (loss derivative w.r.t. inputs).
- Experiments on CIFAR, CUB-200, Scene-15, and ImageNet show consistent student test improvements from attention transfer.
- Despite improvements, student networks still lag behind teacher performance even with attention transfer.
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks have stronger spatial correlation between objects and attention maps needs quantitative support (e.g., correlation coefficients).
- Hyperparameter selection—especially the attention weight β—lacks justification; a sensitivity analysis is needed.
- Figure 7(b) should include teacher training and validation losses to contextualize student comparisons.
- Paper does not clarify when to prefer activation-based versus gradient-based attention; relative strengths are unclear.
- Efficiency/speed gains are not quantified; unclear what tangible benefit attention transfer provides when student and teacher architectures are identical.
- Pros: well-written, well-motivated, and shows consistent gains over student-only baselines.
- Cons: students still underperform teachers, unclear mechanism selection guidance, and novelty is incremental relative to FitNets.
