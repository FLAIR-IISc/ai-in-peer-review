

The paper introduces a novel knowledge transfer approach that leverages attention maps rather than raw activations, a concept with clear theoretical merit. The core idea of transferring higher-level semantic attention patterns is compelling and potentially more efficient than conventional activation-based distillation. However, the empirical evaluation presents significant limitations that undermine the claimed advantages. The reported improvements over standard knowledge distillation (KD) are marginal across all benchmarks, with no statistically significant gains observed on the ImageNet dataset—a critical test case for such methods. Specifically, the performance differences between the proposed method and KD alone are negligible (e.g., ≤0.3% top-1 accuracy on ImageNet), failing to demonstrate a meaningful benefit.  

The current experiments lack depth, particularly in Section 4.2.2, where the authors claim superior transferability. Additional ablation studies are essential to isolate the contribution of attention maps from other components (e.g., loss weighting or architecture modifications). Crucially, the ImageNet results remain underdeveloped: the authors report only a single run on ResNet-50 without error bars, comparisons to recent attention-based baselines (e.g., [X], [Y]), or analysis of failure cases. This omission is problematic, as ImageNet scalability is a key validation criterion for knowledge transfer methods. Without robust ImageNet results, the paper’s central claim—that attention transfer outperforms activation-based KD—lacks sufficient evidence.  

To address these concerns, the authors should expand Section 4.2.2 with: (1) comprehensive ImageNet experiments including multiple architectures (e.g., ResNet-18, ResNet-101), (2) comparisons against state-of-the-art attention distillation methods, (3) statistical significance testing for accuracy gains, and (4) analysis of how attention map transfer interacts with different teacher-student model pairs. These additions would substantiate the method’s novelty and practical value.  

As currently presented, the empirical weaknesses prevent a strong endorsement. The method’s potential is evident, but the evidence falls short of supporting its superiority over simpler KD approaches. If the authors provide the requested experiments, the paper would substantially strengthen its contribution. I recommend a conditional acceptance (e.g., 6 → 7) pending these revisions. Without them, the work remains an incremental step with unverified claims.
