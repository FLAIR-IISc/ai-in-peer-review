

The paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge transfer in convolutional neural networks by leveraging attention maps rather than direct activation transfer. It proposes two methods—activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer—and demonstrates consistent performance improvements across multiple datasets and architectures, including ResNet variants and Network-In-Network. The authors effectively show that attention maps contain valuable information for distillation, with activation-based transfer outperforming full-activation transfer and synergizing with knowledge distillation. The work is well-positioned within the literature on attention mechanisms and knowledge distillation, with clear theoretical motivation and experimental validation.

Reject. The primary reason is insufficient experimental validation on ImageNet, where the claimed improvements (1.1% top-1 accuracy gain) are marginal and not rigorously compared against state-of-the-art knowledge distillation methods. Second, the paper fails to address why knowledge distillation alone fails on ImageNet despite working on CIFAR, leaving critical questions unanswered about the scalability of the proposed method.

The ImageNet results in Section 4.2.2 are underdeveloped: the authors report a 1.1% improvement with minimal hyperparameter tuning, while noting knowledge distillation *hurts* convergence with the same hyperparameters used on CIFAR. This contradicts the paper’s claim that attention transfer is "the first knowledge transfer method successfully applied on ImageNet," as no thorough comparison with KD variants (e.g., using identical architectures) is provided. Without ablation studies demonstrating that attention transfer *significantly* outperforms KD on ImageNet—especially given the high-stakes nature of this dataset—the claim lacks scientific rigor. The limited results (only two residual block losses, no hyperparameter optimization) do not justify the conclusion.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should: (1) conduct comprehensive ImageNet experiments comparing attention transfer against KD using identical network architectures and hyperparameters; (2) analyze why KD fails on ImageNet and how attention transfer overcomes this; (3) include ablation studies on loss placement (e.g., all four residual groups vs. two) and attention map types (F²sum vs. others) on ImageNet. Additionally, clarify the relationship between attention transfer and symmetry constraints (Section 3.2) in the ImageNet context, as batch normalization’s interaction with gradient-based transfer remains unresolved. The current ImageNet results are preliminary and do not meet the threshold for acceptance at this conference.
