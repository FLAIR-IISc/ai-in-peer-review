

This paper proposes a modified knowledge distillation framework that minimizes the difference in the sum of activation statistics (e.g., summed squared L2 norms or other lp norms) across feature maps between teacher and student networks. Empirical results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms the FitNet baseline across multiple computer vision datasets.  

**Strengths:**  
- The method is evaluated comprehensively on diverse vision benchmarks, strengthening its empirical claims.  
- The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Scope Limitation:** The approach appears restricted to convolutional architectures, with no discussion of applicability to non-CNNs (e.g., transformers). This limits the method’s perceived generality.  
2. **Misleading Terminology:** The use of "attention" is inaccurate. The core mechanism simply distills summed statistics (e.g., squared activations) from feature maps—not attention distributions. This misnomer risks confusing readers and dilutes the technical contribution.  
3. **Irrelevant Gradient Component:** The gradient-based attention transfer is introduced as a parallel idea but lacks integration with the main framework. It is neither compared directly to the primary method nor used jointly with the "attention-based" transfer. This addition fails to demonstrate significant value and appears disconnected from the paper’s core contribution.  
4. **Ambiguous Norm Computation:** Equation (2) defines an induced 2-norm for matrix $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W}$, which would require computing the largest singular value (computationally expensive). Given the context (summing squared activations), the authors likely intend the Frobenius norm (which equals $\sqrt{\sum_{i,j} Q_{ij}^2}$ and aligns with the described statistics). The current formulation is unclear and potentially misleading.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The distillation technique shows empirical promise, with strong results on standard benchmarks. However, the paper suffers from critical presentation issues: the misleading "attention" terminology obscures the method’s simplicity, the gradient-based component is tangential, and the norm definition lacks precision. These flaws undermine the clarity of the contribution. While the core idea is sound, the current presentation requires significant revision to accurately convey the method and its scope. The work would benefit from a clearer focus on the statistics-based distillation, removal of the ambiguous "attention" label, and explicit clarification of the norm used in Equation (2).  

**Recommendation:**  
**Weak Accept** (conditional on major revisions addressing the terminology, norm ambiguity, and scope limitations). The method has merit but requires substantial clarification to align with its technical claims.
