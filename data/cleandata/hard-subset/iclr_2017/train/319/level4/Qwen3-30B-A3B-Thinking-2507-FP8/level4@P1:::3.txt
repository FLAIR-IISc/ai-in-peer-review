

This paper proposes a novel method for knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention mechanisms. The authors define two types of attention maps—activation-based (sum of absolute values raised to power p, max of absolute values raised to power p) and gradient-based (input sensitivity maps)—and demonstrate how these can be transferred from a teacher network to a student network to improve the student's performance on image recognition tasks. The authors provide extensive experimental validation across multiple datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and network architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network), showing consistent improvements over baseline student networks. Their approach is well-motivated by the observation that attention maps correlate with network performance, and they carefully explore different attention mapping functions and their effects on transfer learning.

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a technically sound method with consistent empirical improvements, the novelty is limited and the benefits over existing knowledge transfer techniques are not sufficiently compelling.

The primary reasons for rejection are:
1. The paper does not demonstrate clear advantages of attention transfer over existing knowledge distillation methods. The student networks consistently underperform the teacher networks, even with attention transfer, and it's unclear whether the improvements justify the additional complexity of attention transfer compared to standard knowledge distillation.
2. The claimed novelty appears incremental relative to prior work. The authors acknowledge FitNets (Romero et al., 2014) as a related approach but don't sufficiently differentiate their attention transfer method from it. Their activation-based attention transfer bears strong resemblance to FitNets' "hints," while their gradient-based approach builds on existing techniques like Grad-CAM.

The supporting arguments for this decision are:
The paper claims that attention maps contain valuable information that can improve student network performance, but the evidence for this is not sufficiently strong. While the authors show consistent improvements over baseline student networks (e.g., 0.8% top-5 accuracy gain on ImageNet), these gains are marginal compared to the teacher networks' performance. The student networks remain substantially worse than the teachers, which undermines the main contribution of knowledge transfer. The paper also fails to convincingly demonstrate why attention transfer is superior to standard knowledge distillation (which is shown to be effective in their experiments but not compared directly to attention transfer in a meaningful way).

The authors claim that "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer," but they don't provide sufficient evidence to support this claim. The experiments in Table 1 show marginal differences between attention transfer methods, and the paper doesn't establish clear guidelines for when to use one attention mapping function over another. The paper also doesn't thoroughly analyze the impact of hyperparameters like β, which could significantly affect the performance of the attention transfer.

Additional feedback to help improve the paper:
- Quantify the relationship between attention map quality and network performance. While Figure 4 provides visual evidence that higher-performing networks have more spatially correlated attention maps, it would strengthen the paper to include numerical metrics showing this correlation.
- Provide a more comprehensive comparison between attention transfer and knowledge distillation. The paper mentions that knowledge distillation "struggles to work if teacher and student have different architecture/depth" but doesn't clearly establish whether attention transfer solves this problem or merely offers a different approach.
- Clarify the computational cost of attention transfer compared to standard knowledge distillation. The paper mentions that attention transfer adds "very little computational cost," but this claim would be more convincing with actual timing measurements.
- Consider comparing attention transfer with other attention-based methods beyond knowledge distillation, such as those used in visual question answering or image captioning, to better establish the method's broader applicability.

Questions for the authors:
1. The paper states that "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer," but the performance differences in Table 1 are marginal. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of when and why certain attention mapping functions outperform others?
2. The paper claims that attention transfer is the "first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," but this appears to contradict existing work on knowledge distillation for ImageNet (e.g., Hinton et al., 2015). Can you clarify this claim and provide a more precise comparison with existing methods?
3. In the gradient-based attention transfer section, the authors mention "it is so far unclear how batch normalization should behave in the second backpropagation step." Have you explored alternative implementations of batch normalization during this process that might resolve this issue?
4. The paper doesn't provide evidence that attention transfer improves student network's generalization capabilities beyond accuracy on validation sets. How might attention transfer affect other aspects of model performance, such as robustness to adversarial examples or out-of-distribution data?
