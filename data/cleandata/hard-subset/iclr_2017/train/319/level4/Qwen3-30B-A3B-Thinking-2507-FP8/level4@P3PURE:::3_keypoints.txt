- Paper studies attention transfer by aligning teacher and student attention maps (activation- and gradient-based) via L2 loss plus classification loss/optional KD.
- Defines activation-based attention (sum/max of feature values^p) and a novel gradient-based attention from loss gradients wrt inputs.
- Experiments on CIFAR, CUB-200, Scene-15, and ImageNet show attention transfer consistently improves student test performance versus baseline students.
- Despite improvements, student networks still underperform the teacher even with attention transfer.
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks show stronger spatial correlation between objects and attention maps lacks quantitative metrics (e.g., correlation coefficients).
- Selection and justification of hyperparameters, especially β for attention loss weighting, is missing and should be analyzed.
- Figure 7b should include teacher training/validation losses to clarify optimization dynamics.
- The relative advantages or disadvantages of activation-based vs. gradient-based attention mechanisms across scenarios are not clarified.
- Computational efficiency and practical benefits (inference speed, parameter reduction) of using smaller students versus teachers are not characterized.
- If student and teacher share identical architectures, the practical benefit of attention transfer is unclear.
- Pros: well-motivated, clearly written, and consistently improves student performance over baselines.
- Cons: students still lag teachers, optimal attention mechanism per task is ambiguous, and novelty is incremental relative to prior work (e.g., FitNet).
