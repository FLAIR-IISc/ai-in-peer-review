

Review:  
This paper investigates attention transfer between teacher and student networks, minimizing the L2 distance between their attention maps across layers alongside classification loss and optional knowledge distillation. The authors propose several activation-based attention mechanisms (sum or max of feature values raised to power p) and a gradient-based attention (derivative of loss w.r.t. inputs). Experiments on CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, Scene-15, and ImageNet demonstrate that attention transfer consistently improves student performance over non-attention-based distillation. However, student networks remain inferior to the teacher, even with attention transfer.  

Key concerns:  
1. Section 3 claims higher-accuracy networks exhibit stronger spatial correlation between objects and attention maps. While Figure 4 is illustrative, quantitative validation (e.g., correlation coefficients) is missing.  
2. Hyperparameter selection (particularly β, weighting attention loss) lacks justification. A sensitivity analysis showing β’s impact on performance would strengthen the methodology.  
3. Figure 7b should include teacher network training/validation loss to clarify generalization behavior.  
4. The paper fails to clarify the relative advantages of different attention mechanisms (activation-based vs. gradient-based) across datasets or tasks.  
5. If student and teacher share identical architecture, does attention transfer provide any benefit beyond parameter reduction? The work does not address this, nor does it quantify the computational efficiency gain from smaller student models.  

Summary:  
Pros:  
- Well-structured and clearly motivated.  
- Attention transfer consistently boosts student performance versus baseline distillation.  

Cons:  
- Student networks consistently underperform the teacher, even with attention transfer.  
- Unclear guidance on selecting attention mechanisms for specific scenarios.  
- Modest novelty relative to prior work (e.g., FitNet), as attention transfer is not fundamentally new.  

The work demonstrates a useful practical technique but requires deeper analysis of attention mechanisms’ efficacy and clearer justification for the attention transfer approach over simpler alternatives.
