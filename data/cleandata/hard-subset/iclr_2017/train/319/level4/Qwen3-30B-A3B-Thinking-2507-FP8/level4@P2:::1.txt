

The paper introduces a novel knowledge transfer approach using attention maps from a teacher network to improve student network performance, proposing both activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer methods. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements across multiple datasets and architectures, including ResNet variants and Network-In-Network, with ablation studies showing that attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer. The work is well-motivated by the role of attention in human perception and artificial vision systems, and the proposed attention definitions align with recent visualization literature (e.g., Grad-CAM, CAM). Contributions include a systematic analysis of attention maps across network depths, a clear experimental validation framework, and open-sourced code.

**Decision: Weak Reject**  
The paper shows promise but requires stronger evidence for its core claim—attention transfer providing *significant* gains over knowledge distillation (KD) on ImageNet. The current results in Section 4.2.2 lack statistical significance and fail to convincingly demonstrate attention transfer’s superiority over KD, which is the primary baseline in the field.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The key issue lies in the ImageNet experiments (Section 4.2.2). The authors report a 1.1% top-1 accuracy gain for ResNet-18 with attention transfer (AT) over baseline, but *do not compare against KD using identical hyperparameters*. The draft claims KD "hurts convergence" on ImageNet with ResNet-18/34, yet the paper provides no quantitative comparison between AT and KD on ImageNet (e.g., Table 5 only shows AT’s absolute improvement, not relative to KD). For context, Hinton et al. (2015) established KD as the *de facto* standard for knowledge distillation, and the paper’s claim that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method successfully applied on ImageNet" is unsubstantiated without direct KD comparisons. The CIFAR results (Table 3) show AT outperforming KD (11.2% vs. 12.1% error), but ImageNet is the critical benchmark for large-scale vision. Without comparable ImageNet KD results, the paper’s central contribution lacks empirical rigor.  

Further, the ablation study in Section 4.1.2 (Table 1) shows AT+KD marginally outperforming AT alone (7.93% vs. 7.99% error), but the paper does not clarify whether this combination is necessary for the ImageNet gains. If KD alone could achieve similar results with proper tuning (e.g., as on CIFAR), the novelty of attention transfer diminishes. The authors also omit statistical significance testing (e.g., standard deviations across runs), which is essential for claiming "significant improvements" on ImageNet.  

The gradient-based transfer method (Section 3.2) is theoretically sound but under-explained. The computational cost of double backpropagation (Equation 5) is not quantified, and the paper does not justify why it underperforms activation-based transfer (Table 3: 11.8% vs. 11.2% error). This omission weakens the argument for the proposed framework’s practicality.

**Additional Feedback**  
1. **ImageNet comparisons**: Add a table showing AT vs. KD *on ImageNet* with identical hyperparameters (e.g., temperature, α, β). Without this, the paper’s core claim is unverified.  
2. **Layer selection**: Clarify why the authors chose "2 last groups" of residual blocks for ImageNet (Section 4.2.1). Ablation on layer depth (e.g., top vs. mid-level) would strengthen the method’s design rationale.  
3. **Statistical validation**: Include standard deviations across multiple runs in Table 5 to confirm the 1.1% improvement is statistically meaningful.  
4. **Computational cost**: Quantify the runtime overhead of gradient-based transfer vs. AT and KD (e.g., time per epoch on ImageNet).  

**Clarifying Questions**  
1. The paper states KD "hurts convergence" on ImageNet with ResNet-18/34 but works well on CIFAR (Section 4.1.3). Could you share the exact KD results (error rates) on ImageNet for the same hyperparameters used in CIFAR? This is critical to assess whether AT’s gains are *independent* of KD.  
2. In Table 5, the AT improvement (1.1% top-1) is small. How does this compare to recent ImageNet distillation methods (e.g., FitNets on ImageNet)? If AT is the first to work on ImageNet, why do prior works (e.g., Romero et al., 2014) fail to report success here?  
3. The gradient-based method requires double backpropagation (Section 3.2). Given the computational cost and lack of ImageNet results, why does the paper prioritize this over activation-based transfer?  

The paper’s conceptual contribution is valuable, but the current evidence does not sufficiently validate its claims for large-scale vision tasks. Addressing these gaps would transform this into a strong contribution to knowledge distillation.
