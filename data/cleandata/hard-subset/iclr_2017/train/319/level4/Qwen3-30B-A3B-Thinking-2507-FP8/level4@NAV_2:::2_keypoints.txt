- Paper proposes transferring spatial attention maps (from activation statistics or gradients) for knowledge distillation and shows consistent performance gains across datasets and architectures.
- Authors define attention as spatial maps derived from activation statistics or gradient information and introduce two distinct transfer mechanisms.
- Reviewer recommends rejection.
- Major critique: calling the method "attention transfer" is misleading because it actually transfers feature-map statistics (e.g., sum of absolutes, sums of powers), not attention mechanisms.
- The gradient-based transfer is poorly integrated, not directly compared to activation-based transfer, and appears tacked on rather than contributing clearly to the main method.
- Terminology concern: the term "attention" is scientifically inaccurate here and should be clarified or replaced to avoid confusion with cognitive or neural-network attention mechanisms.
- Notation issue: equation (2) ambiguously references the induced 2-norm for Q ∈ ℝ^(H×W) when the Frobenius norm is more likely intended and computationally standard.
- Recommendation: clarify terminology (feature-map statistics vs. attention), consider renaming the approach, and either fully integrate or remove the gradient-based component.
- Questions for authors: clarify the intended norm in eq.(2); explain the relation/choice and integration between gradient- and activation-based transfers; justify use of the term "attention" given the methods described.
