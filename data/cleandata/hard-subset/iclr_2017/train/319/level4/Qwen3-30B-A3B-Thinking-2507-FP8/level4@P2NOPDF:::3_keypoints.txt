- Paper proposes attention transfer: minimize L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps alongside classification loss and optional KD.  
- Introduces several activation-based attention mechanisms (sum/max of feature values^p) and a gradient-based attention (loss derivative w.r.t. inputs).  
- Experiments on CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, Scene-15, and ImageNet show attention transfer consistently improves student performance over non-attention distillation.  
- Despite improvements, student networks still underperform teachers even with attention transfer.  
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks show stronger spatial correlation between objects and attention maps lacks quantitative validation (e.g., correlation coefficients).  
- Hyperparameter selection, especially β (attention loss weight), is not justified; sensitivity analysis is missing.  
- Figure 7b should plot teacher training/validation loss to clarify generalization behavior.  
- The paper does not clarify relative advantages of activation-based vs. gradient-based attention across datasets/tasks.  
- Unclear whether attention transfer helps when student and teacher share identical architectures, or if benefits are just parameter reduction.  
- Computational efficiency gains from using smaller student models are not quantified.  
- Pros: well-structured, clearly motivated, and attention transfer consistently boosts student performance versus baseline distillation.  
- Cons: students still underperform teachers; unclear guidance on choosing attention mechanisms; modest novelty relative to prior work like FitNet.
