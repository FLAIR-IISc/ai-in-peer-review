

This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention maps. The authors define two types of spatial attention maps (activation-based and gradient-based) and demonstrate how transferring these maps from a teacher network to a student network can significantly improve the student's performance across various datasets and architectures. The paper provides clear theoretical foundations for attention maps and presents consistent empirical improvements over baseline student networks.

I recommend rejection. The primary reasons are: (1) the student networks consistently underperform the teacher networks even with attention transfer, and (2) the paper fails to establish clear advantages of attention transfer over existing knowledge distillation approaches when student and teacher architectures are comparable.

The paper makes a valid contribution to attention-based knowledge transfer, but the key limitations prevent acceptance. The authors claim that networks with higher accuracy exhibit stronger spatial correlation between attention maps and objects, yet Figure 4 provides only qualitative evidence without quantitative metrics to support this assertion. The hyperparameter selection for β lacks thorough justification, and the impact of different β values on performance is not explored. Additionally, Figure 7(b) does not report the teacher's training and validation loss, making it difficult to assess the full learning dynamics. The paper also fails to clearly articulate the advantages of different attention mapping functions (sum vs. max, different p values) in various scenarios, leaving readers uncertain about optimal implementation choices.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should: (1) provide quantitative evidence supporting the relationship between network accuracy and attention map correlation; (2) conduct a systematic analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity; (3) include teacher network training/validation metrics for all experiments; and (4) clearly delineate when each attention transfer method provides advantages over others.

I have several clarifying questions for the authors: What is the specific impact of different β values on the attention transfer performance? How does attention transfer compare to standard knowledge distillation when student and teacher networks have identical architectures and depths? Could the authors provide metrics on computational efficiency gains from using smaller student networks with attention transfer compared to larger teacher networks?
