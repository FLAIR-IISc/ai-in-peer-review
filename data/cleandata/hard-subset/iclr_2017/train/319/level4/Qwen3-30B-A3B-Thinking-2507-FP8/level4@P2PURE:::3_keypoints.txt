- Paper proposes attention transfer by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps across layers alongside classification loss (optionally with KD).  
- Defines activation-based attention mechanisms (sum/max of feature values raised to power p) and a gradient-based attention mechanism (loss derivative w.r.t. inputs).  
- Experiments on CIFAR, CUB-200, Scene-15, and ImageNet show attention transfer consistently improves student test performance.  
- Despite improvements, student networks still underperform the teacher even after attention transfer.  
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks show stronger spatial correlation between objects and attention maps is visually supported (Fig. 4) but lacks quantitative metrics.  
- Hyperparameter selection is unclear; analysis of the impact of the attention-loss weight β is needed.  
- Figure 7b should include both the teacher’s training and validation losses for a complete comparison.  
- Experiments do not delineate when activation-based vs. gradient-based attention mechanisms are preferable or their respective limitations.  
- It would be valuable to report computational speed-up (e.g., inference time) from the student’s smaller parameter count and whether attention transfer helps when architectures are identical.  
- Pros: well-motivated, clearly written, and yields consistent student gains over baselines without attention.  
- Cons: students still lag teachers, optimal choice/application of attention mechanisms is unclear, and novelty is incremental relative to FitNets.
