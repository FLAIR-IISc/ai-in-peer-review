- Proposes attention transfer (activation- and gradient-based spatial attention maps) for CNN knowledge transfer and shows improvements across CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet.  
- Decision: Reject — idea interesting but lacks sufficient evidence that it meaningfully advances knowledge distillation; incremental novelty.  
- Major concern: Students with attention transfer still underperform teacher networks, contradicting the goal of distillation.  
- The paper does not demonstrate students can match teacher performance with fewer parameters, a key distillation requirement.  
- Unclear when to use each attention method; F²-sum performs best empirically but lacks justification.  
- Claim that activation-based attention outperforms full-activation transfer is not explained or grounded in a principled framework.  
- Novelty relative to KD and FitNets is questionable; the paper fails to clearly articulate meaningful differences.  
- Claim of being the first successful ImageNet transfer is unsupported by comprehensive comparisons to other distillation methods.  
- Recommendation: Clearly quantify the student–teacher performance gap and show whether students can match teachers with fewer parameters.  
- Recommendation: Provide a thorough comparison and ablation study against KD, FitNets, and other methods on ImageNet.  
- Recommendation: Include quantitative measures of spatial correlation between attention maps and object regions beyond qualitative examples.  
- Recommendation: Investigate hyperparameter effects (especially β) and give guidance for selecting values across tasks.  
- Recommendation: Clarify computational overhead of gradient-based attention transfer, including implications for batch normalization during second backprop.  
- Question: Why do attention maps carry more useful information than full activations — any theoretical or empirical justification?  
- Question: How does attention transfer compare to KD and FitNets on ImageNet in terms of accuracy, computation, and parameter efficiency?  
- Question: Can a student with fewer parameters achieve teacher-level performance using attention transfer, addressing the core distillation goal?
