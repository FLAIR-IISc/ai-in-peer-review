

The paper introduces a modified knowledge distillation framework that minimizes the discrepancy in the sum of statistical measures across feature maps between teacher and student networks. The authors empirically show that their method surpasses the FitNet-style distillation baseline.  

Pros:  
+ The proposed approach is evaluated across multiple computer vision datasets.  
+ The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written.  

Cons:  
- The method appears restricted to convolutional architectures, limiting broader applicability.  
- The use of "attention" terminology is misleading; the core mechanism merely distills summed squared (or other statistics, e.g., summed Lp norms) activations within hidden feature maps.  
- The gradient-based attention transfer component feels disconnected. It is neither directly compared against nor integrated with the "attention-based" transfer, appearing as an unrelated addition with minimal contribution.  
- The computation of the induced 2-norm in Eq. (2) is unclear. Given $ Q \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W} $, its induced 2-norm corresponds to the largest singular value, which is computationally costly. The authors likely intended the Frobenius norm instead.  

Overall, while the distillation method demonstrates strong empirical performance, the paper suffers from organizational flaws and ambiguous notation.
