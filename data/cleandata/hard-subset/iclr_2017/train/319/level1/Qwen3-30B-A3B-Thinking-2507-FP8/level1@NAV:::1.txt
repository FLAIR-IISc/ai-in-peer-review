

This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge distillation by transferring attention maps from a teacher network to a student network, rather than just transferring output distributions or full activations. The authors define two types of spatial attention maps (activation-based and gradient-based) and demonstrate that transferring these attention maps leads to consistent performance improvements across multiple datasets and architectures. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by showing that attention information, which has been largely ignored in knowledge distillation, can significantly enhance student network performance. The authors provide thorough experimental validation across CIFAR, ImageNet, CUB, and Scenes datasets, demonstrating that their approach outperforms baseline methods and can be combined with traditional knowledge distillation.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The key reasons for this decision are: (1) the approach is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature, building on recent advances in attention mechanisms and knowledge distillation; and (2) the empirical results are strong and scientifically rigorous, showing consistent improvements across multiple datasets and architectures.

The paper makes a compelling case that attention maps contain valuable information that can be transferred to improve student network performance. The experimental design is robust, with ablation studies showing that attention transfer provides benefits beyond what is achieved through knowledge distillation alone. The authors also demonstrate that activation-based attention transfer is particularly effective, outperforming full-activation transfer.

For further improvement, I recommend clarifying the relationship between attention transfer and existing knowledge distillation methods. Specifically, it would be helpful to see a more detailed comparison with the FitNets approach. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the computational cost of gradient-based attention transfer.

I have two questions for the authors: (1) Can you provide more details about why gradient-based attention transfer showed better results on CIFAR than on ImageNet, despite the authors' claim that attention matters more in networks with higher spatial resolution? (2) Could you elaborate on the connection between the attention transfer method and the concept of "non-attentional and attentional perception processes" mentioned in the introduction?
