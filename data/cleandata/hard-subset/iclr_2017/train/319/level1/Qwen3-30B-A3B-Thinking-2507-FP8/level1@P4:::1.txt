

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention maps from a teacher network to improve the performance of a student network. The authors define attention as spatial maps indicating where a network focuses most for making decisions, and introduce two methods for attention transfer: activation-based and gradient-based. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple datasets (CIFAR, Scene, CUB, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network), showing that attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer and can be effectively combined with knowledge distillation. The authors provide thorough experimental validation, including ablation studies on attention mapping functions and transfer layer configurations, and make their code and models publicly available.

## 2. Decision
Accept.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of knowledge transfer in neural networks. The three key questions are answered as follows:

1. The specific problem tackled is: "Can we use attention information from a teacher network to improve the performance of a student network, and if so, how?" The paper provides a clear definition of attention as spatial maps and proposes two methods to transfer this information.

2. The approach is well motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors provide a comprehensive review of related work on attention mechanisms, attention visualization, and knowledge distillation. They correctly position their work within the context of existing knowledge transfer methods (e.g., FitNets, knowledge distillation) and identify a gap in the literature regarding the use of spatial attention information. Their motivation is strengthened by the observation that attention maps correlate with network performance and contain valuable information that can be transferred.

3. The paper strongly supports its claims with rigorous experiments. The authors conduct extensive experiments across multiple datasets and architectures, providing consistent improvements over baselines. They include ablation studies to demonstrate the importance of different components (e.g., attention mapping functions, transfer layer configurations), and provide visualizations showing that student networks learn to attend to similar regions as teacher networks. The results are statistically sound, with multiple runs reported where appropriate, and the experimental design is thorough and well-justified.

The paper's key contribution is the demonstration that attention maps contain information more valuable for transfer than full activations, which is a novel insight supported by compelling experimental evidence. The activation-based attention transfer method consistently outperforms gradient-based methods and provides significant improvements across diverse settings.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
While the paper is strong, the following points could further enhance it:

- The paper claims that activation-based attention transfer is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," but this statement requires stronger justification. The authors should provide a more thorough literature review of knowledge transfer methods applied to ImageNet to substantiate this claim.

- The computational cost of gradient-based attention transfer (requiring double backpropagation) is mentioned but not quantified. A brief analysis of the computational overhead compared to other methods would strengthen the paper.

- The paper mentions that batch normalization poses challenges for gradient-based attention transfer, but doesn't explore solutions or alternative approaches. This could be addressed in future work.

- The authors could provide more insight into why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations. A theoretical explanation or additional empirical analysis would strengthen the paper's conceptual contribution.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. Could you provide more detailed statistics about the ImageNet experiments, particularly regarding the computational cost of attention transfer compared to standard training and knowledge distillation?

2. The paper states that activation-based attention transfer outperforms gradient-based methods, but the authors should clarify whether this is consistent across all architectures and datasets, or if there are specific cases where gradient-based attention transfer is preferred.

3. In the ImageNet experiments, the authors mention that knowledge distillation "hurts convergence" with the same hyperparameters as on CIFAR. Could the authors provide more insight into why this discrepancy occurs and whether hyperparameter tuning would resolve this issue?

4. The paper claims that attention maps from different layers focus on different regions (e.g., low-level features in early layers, full objects in top layers). Could the authors provide more visualization examples to illustrate this layer-specific attention behavior across different datasets?
