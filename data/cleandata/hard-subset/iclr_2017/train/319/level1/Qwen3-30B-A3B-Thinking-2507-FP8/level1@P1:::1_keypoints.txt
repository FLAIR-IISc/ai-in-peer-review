- Proposes attention transfer for knowledge distillation using spatial attention maps.  
- Defines two attention map types: activation-based (feature statistics) and gradient-based (input sensitivity).  
- Claims attention maps contain transferable information that improves student networks.  
- Demonstrates consistent gains across datasets (CIFAR, ImageNet, Scenes, CUB) and architectures (ResNet, NIN, WRN).  
- Shows attention transfer outperforms standard knowledge distillation and full-activation transfer.  
- Provides thorough experiments, ablation studies, and public code/models.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- Main reasons for acceptance: well-motivated approach and comprehensive, rigorous experimental validation.  
- Identifies a gap in prior work that focused on activations/logits rather than spatial attention patterns.  
- Ablations show best attention mapping (F^2sum), superiority to full-activation transfer, and effective ImageNet results (≈1.1% top-1 gain).  
- Methodological insight: spatial attention is more effective for transfer than full activation tensors.  
- Notes low computational overhead but requests concrete comparisons (training time/memory).  
- Requests explanation why standard knowledge distillation failed on ImageNet and theorize architecture-dependent issues.  
- Suggests more comparison with related methods (e.g., FitNets) and expanded rationale for gradient-based flip-invariance benefits.  
- Asks authors about: impact of ImageNet hyperparameter tuning (β), theoretical reasons attention > activations, batch-norm behavior in second backprop for gradient transfer, and clarification of the claim about being first to succeed on ImageNet.
