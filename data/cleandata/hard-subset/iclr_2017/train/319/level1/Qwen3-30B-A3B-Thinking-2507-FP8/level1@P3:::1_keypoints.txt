- Introduces attention transfer as a novel knowledge distillation method for CNNs.  
- Proposes two attention map definitions: activation-based and gradient-based.  
- Shows attention transfer improves student performance across datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, WRN, NIN).  
- Claims activation-based attention transfer outperforms standard knowledge distillation, notably on ImageNet.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Well-positioned in literature, building on knowledge distillation and attention visualization.  
- Provides theoretical motivation: activation maps capture spatial importance; gradient maps encode input sensitivity.  
- Strong experimental evaluation with ablations showing activation-based transfer beats full-activation (FitNets-style) and a 1.1% top-1 ImageNet gain.  
- Notes minimal computational overhead and justifies implementation choices like l2-normalized attention maps.  
- Request clearer theoretical explanation for why attention maps are more transferable than full activations.  
- Ask for justification why squared-sum (p=2) attention is the default and whether there is theoretical support.  
- Point out misleading wording claiming "first" application to ImageNet; request clarification relative to prior work (e.g., FitNets).  
- Highlight lack of hyperparameter tuning details for ImageNet experiments and suggest reporting optimal settings or acknowledging the limitation.  
- Recommend more layer-wise attention visualizations to illustrate how attention changes across depths.  
- Ask authors to explain why attention outperforms full activations beyond empirical results.  
- Question handling of batch normalization during the second backpropagation in gradient-based attention and whether solutions were explored.  
- Request clarification on how attention transfer differs from prior ImageNet-applied knowledge transfer methods.  
- Ask for examples showing how attention maps at different layers affect performance, especially for fine-grained tasks.  
- Request convergence curves or training-time comparisons to substantiate claims that attention transfer speeds convergence.
