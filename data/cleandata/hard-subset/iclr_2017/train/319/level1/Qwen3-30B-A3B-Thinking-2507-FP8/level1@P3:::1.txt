

# Peer Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a compelling contribution by introducing attention transfer as a novel method for knowledge distillation in convolutional neural networks. The authors propose two distinct ways to define attention maps for CNNs: activation-based (using statistics of neuron activations across channels) and gradient-based (using gradients with respect to input). They demonstrate that transferring these attention maps from a powerful teacher network to a student network consistently improves performance across multiple datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, WRN, NIN). The paper's key contributions include establishing attention as a meaningful knowledge transfer mechanism, proposing practical attention mapping functions, and showing that activation-based attention transfer provides superior improvements over standard knowledge distillation, particularly on ImageNet where previous knowledge transfer methods struggled.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of knowledge distillation with a well-motivated approach that addresses a meaningful problem. The experimental results are thorough, consistent, and demonstrate clear improvements over baselines across multiple benchmarks, including the challenging ImageNet dataset where previous knowledge transfer methods had limited success.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, building on knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) and attention visualization work while introducing a novel perspective on knowledge transfer. The authors provide a strong theoretical foundation for their attention maps, explaining how activation-based maps capture spatial importance of features while gradient-based maps encode input sensitivity.

The experimental evaluation is particularly strong. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements across multiple datasets and architectures, with ablation studies showing that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer (FitNets-style) by a significant margin. Their ImageNet results (1.1% top-1 improvement with ResNet-18) are particularly impressive as they address a known limitation of standard knowledge distillation on large-scale datasets. The paper also provides a thoughtful analysis of why their approach works better than previous methods, noting that attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations.

The authors carefully address the computational considerations of their method, noting that attention transfer adds minimal computational overhead compared to standard knowledge distillation. Their implementation details (including the use of l2-normalized attention maps) are well-justified and contribute to the method's effectiveness.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. The paper claims that "attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations" but doesn't provide a clear theoretical explanation for why this is the case. A brief discussion of the information-theoretic properties of attention maps versus full activations would strengthen the paper.

2. The authors mention "squared sum attention" (F^2sum) as their default, but the paper would benefit from more justification for this choice. Why does p=2 work better than other values? Is there a theoretical basis for this choice?

3. The paper states that "attention transfer is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," which is misleading. FitNets (Romero et al., 2014) and other knowledge transfer methods have been applied to ImageNet. The authors should clarify that attention transfer specifically has been successfully applied to ImageNet, rather than knowledge transfer in general.

4. The authors mention they "didn't have time to tune hyperparameters" for ImageNet experiments. This weakens the claim that their method is effective on ImageNet. The paper would be stronger if they reported the optimal hyperparameters or at least acknowledged this limitation.

5. The paper mentions that attention maps "focus on different parts for different layers" but the visualizations (Fig. 4) don't clearly illustrate this progression across layers. Adding more visual examples showing attention changes from low to high layers would strengthen this claim.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer." Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation for why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations, beyond empirical observation?

2. For gradient-based attention transfer, the paper mentions "it is so far unclear how batch normalization should behave in the second backpropagation step." Have the authors explored techniques to handle batch normalization during the second backpropagation, and if so, what were the results?

3. The paper claims attention transfer "is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." Could the authors clarify how their method differs from previous knowledge transfer methods that have been applied to ImageNet (such as FitNets)?

4. The authors mention that "attention maps can be defined w.r.t. various layers of the network." Could they provide specific examples of how attention maps at different layers contribute to different aspects of the network's performance, particularly for fine-grained recognition tasks?

5. The paper states that "attention transfer greatly speeds up convergence." Could the authors provide convergence curves or training time comparisons to substantiate this claim?
