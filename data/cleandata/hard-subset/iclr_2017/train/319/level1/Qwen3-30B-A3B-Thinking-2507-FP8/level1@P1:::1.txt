

# Peer Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a compelling contribution by proposing a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks using attention maps. The authors define attention as spatial maps indicating where a network focuses for making decisions, and they introduce two types of attention maps: activation-based (using statistics of feature map activations) and gradient-based (using input sensitivity maps). The key insight is that attention maps contain valuable information that can significantly improve student network performance when transferred from a powerful teacher network. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements across multiple datasets (CIFAR, ImageNet, Scenes, CUB) and architectures (ResNet, NIN, Wide Residual Networks), showing that attention transfer outperforms both standard knowledge distillation and transferring full activation tensors. The paper is well-structured, with clear motivation, thorough experiments, and publicly available code and models.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field, and I recommend acceptance for two key reasons: (1) The attention transfer approach is well-motivated and addresses a meaningful gap in knowledge distillation research, and (2) The experimental results are comprehensive, scientifically rigorous, and consistently demonstrate significant improvements over strong baselines.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully identifies a gap in knowledge transfer research. While knowledge distillation has been extensively studied, the authors correctly note that previous work has primarily focused on transferring full network activations or logits, rather than the spatial attention patterns that reveal where networks focus their attention. Their motivation is well-grounded in the literature on attention mechanisms in both human perception and artificial neural networks.

The experimental evidence is particularly strong. The authors conduct thorough ablation studies to demonstrate the importance of different attention mapping functions (F^2sum outperforms others), show that attention transfer is superior to full activation transfer, and verify that the approach works across multiple datasets and architectures. The CIFAR experiments provide detailed comparisons of different attention mapping functions and transfer strategies, while the ImageNet results are especially impressive as they demonstrate the first successful application of knowledge transfer to ImageNet (where standard knowledge distillation struggles). The results consistently show that attention transfer provides significant improvements (1.1% top-1 accuracy on ImageNet) without substantial computational overhead.

The paper also makes a valuable methodological contribution by demonstrating that attention maps can be used as a more effective knowledge transfer mechanism than full activations, which is a counterintuitive and important finding. The authors effectively show that the spatial information in attention maps is more valuable for transfer than the full activation tensors, which is a significant insight for the field.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would further enhance its impact:

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of attention transfer compared to standard knowledge distillation. The authors mention it "adds very little computational cost," but a concrete comparison (e.g., training time or memory usage) would help practitioners understand the trade-offs.

2. The authors note that they couldn't apply knowledge distillation effectively on ImageNet with standard hyperparameters, but they don't explain why this is the case. A brief theoretical discussion of why knowledge distillation might fail for certain network architectures would strengthen the paper.

3. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with recent knowledge transfer methods (e.g., FitNets) that might also leverage intermediate representations, to better position their contribution within the broader literature.

4. The discussion of gradient-based attention transfer could be expanded to explain why the symmetry constraint (flip invariance) is particularly beneficial, as this appears to be one of the strongest results in Table 3.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In the ImageNet experiments, the authors mention they "did not have time to tune any hyperparameters and kept them from finetuning experiments." How would the results change with proper hyperparameter tuning for ImageNet, particularly for the β parameter in Eq. 2?

2. The paper states that "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer." Could the authors provide more insight into why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations? Is there a theoretical explanation for this phenomenon?

3. In the gradient-based attention transfer section, the authors note that "it is so far unclear how batch normalization should behave in the second backpropagation step." Could the authors elaborate on why batch normalization creates a special challenge for gradient-based attention transfer, and whether they have any hypotheses about how to properly handle it?

4. The paper claims that attention transfer is the "first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." Given that the authors cite FitNets and knowledge distillation as prior work, could they clarify why these methods haven't been successfully applied to ImageNet with standard hyperparameters?
