

# Review of "Attention Transfer"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention maps, rather than just transferring final layer activations or class probabilities. The authors define two types of attention maps for convolutional neural networks: activation-based (using statistics of hidden neuron activations) and gradient-based (using input sensitivity maps). They propose methods to transfer attention information from a powerful teacher network to a smaller student network, demonstrating consistent performance improvements across multiple datasets (CIFAR, Scenes, CUB, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, NIN, WRN). The paper makes the following key contributions: (1) formalizing attention as a knowledge transfer mechanism; (2) proposing both activation-based and gradient-based attention maps; (3) demonstrating significant performance improvements across diverse settings; and (4) showing that attention transfer can be combined with knowledge distillation.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The key reasons are: (1) the paper addresses a meaningful problem in knowledge distillation with a novel approach that is well-motivated and well-supported by empirical evidence; (2) the results demonstrate consistent improvements across multiple datasets and architectures, including ImageNet, where prior knowledge transfer methods have struggled.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's approach is well-motivated by both human attention mechanisms and the literature on attention in neural networks. The authors clearly position their work in relation to knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) and attention visualization techniques (e.g., Grad-CAM), showing how their attention transfer method differs from and improves upon existing approaches.

The empirical evidence is scientifically rigorous. The authors conduct comprehensive experiments across multiple datasets (CIFAR, Scenes, CUB, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, NIN, WRN). They include ablation studies showing that different attention mapping functions have varying effectiveness (e.g., sum-based functions outperform max-based ones), and they demonstrate that attention transfer provides greater improvements than full-activation transfer (FitNets-style), which is a significant contribution to the knowledge distillation literature. The results are consistent across different settings, with attention transfer improving performance by 1.1% top-1 accuracy on ImageNet (a significant improvement for a large-scale benchmark), and showing better performance than knowledge distillation in some cases.

The paper also makes a notable contribution by being one of the first to successfully apply knowledge transfer on ImageNet, where previous methods like knowledge distillation have struggled due to architectural differences between teacher and student networks. The authors provide clear evidence that attention transfer overcomes this limitation.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. Clarifying the implementation details of gradient-based attention transfer, particularly regarding batch normalization during the second backpropagation step. The authors note this is unclear in the current version but don't provide a solution. Including a brief discussion of how they handle this would improve the reproducibility of their method.

2. Providing more detailed analysis of when attention transfer is most beneficial. The paper mentions that attention transfer is particularly effective for fine-grained recognition (CUB dataset), but doesn't fully explore why this might be the case. A deeper analysis of the relationship between attention transfer and task difficulty could add significant value.

3. Improving the presentation of results. While the paper includes tables and figures, some of the visualizations (e.g., Figure 4) would benefit from clearer labeling of the networks being compared and more explicit description of the attention patterns.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that attention transfer is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet. Could you elaborate on why previous methods like knowledge distillation struggled on ImageNet, and how attention transfer specifically overcomes these challenges? The authors mention architectural differences as a factor, but a more detailed explanation would be helpful.

2. In Table 3, the gradient-based attention transfer (grad-based AT) shows better performance than activation-based attention transfer (activation-based AT) on CIFAR-10, while the opposite is true for larger datasets (Table 5). What do you believe explains this difference in effectiveness across dataset sizes? Is it related to the spatial resolution of attention maps?

3. The paper mentions that activation-based attention transfer works better than full-activation transfer (FitNets-style). Could you provide more intuition on why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activation tensors? The authors suggest it's because attention maps carry "more important information," but a more detailed explanation would strengthen this claim.
