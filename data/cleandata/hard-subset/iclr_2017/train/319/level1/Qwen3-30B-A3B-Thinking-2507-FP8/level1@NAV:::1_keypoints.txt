- Proposes knowledge distillation via transferring attention maps from teacher to student rather than outputs or full activations.  
- Defines two spatial attention map types: activation-based and gradient-based.  
- Demonstrates attention transfer yields consistent performance improvements across multiple datasets and architectures.  
- Provides thorough experimental validation on CIFAR, ImageNet, CUB, and Scenes, outperforming baseline methods.  
- Shows attention transfer can be combined with traditional knowledge distillation for additional gains.  
- Ablation studies indicate attention transfer benefits beyond standard distillation.  
- Activation-based attention transfer is particularly effective, outperforming full-activation transfer.  
- Clear contribution: attention information is valuable and previously underused in knowledge distillation.  
- Recommendation to accept based on strong motivation and rigorous empirical results.  
- Suggests clarifying the relationship with existing methods, notably FitNets.  
- Recommends discussing the computational cost of gradient-based attention transfer.  
- Asks two questions for authors: why gradient-based AT worked better on CIFAR than ImageNet, and how AT connects to “non-attentional and attentional perception processes.”
