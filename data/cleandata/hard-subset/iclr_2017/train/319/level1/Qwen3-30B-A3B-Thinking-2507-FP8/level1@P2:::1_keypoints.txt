Introduces attention-based knowledge transfer using activation- and gradient-based attention maps for CNNs.  
Proposes methods to transfer attention from a teacher to a student and combine this with knowledge distillation.  
Demonstrates consistent performance improvements across datasets (CIFAR, Scenes, CUB, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, NIN, WRN).  
Shows attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer (FitNets-style) and can beat knowledge distillation in some cases, including +1.1% top-1 on ImageNet.  
Recommendation: accept — novel, well-motivated approach with strong empirical support addressing a meaningful problem.  
Positions work relative to prior KD and attention-visualization literature and motivates approach via human attention.  
Experiments are comprehensive with ablation studies (e.g., sum-based attention > max-based) and consistent results across settings.  
Notable claim: one of the first successful knowledge-transfer methods applied to ImageNet, addressing prior failures due to architectural differences.  
Request to clarify implementation details for gradient-based attention transfer, especially batch-normalization during the second backprop.  
Request deeper analysis of when and why attention transfer is most beneficial (e.g., strong effect on fine-grained CUB).  
Request improved presentation/visualizations (clearer labels and descriptions of attention patterns).  
Question: explain why prior methods struggled on ImageNet and precisely how attention transfer overcomes architectural mismatch.  
Question: why does grad-based AT outperform activation-based AT on CIFAR-10 but not on larger datasets — role of spatial resolution?  
Question: provide stronger intuition why attention maps are more transferable than full activation tensors.
