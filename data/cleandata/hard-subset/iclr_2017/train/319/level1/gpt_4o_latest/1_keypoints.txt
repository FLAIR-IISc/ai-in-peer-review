- Proposes a novel method to transfer attention maps from teacher to student CNNs to improve student performance.  
- Defines attention as spatial maps derived from activation-based or gradient-based information and introduces mechanisms to transfer them.  
- Introduces multiple attention transfer methods and shows they can be combined with knowledge distillation.  
- Demonstrates consistent performance improvements across datasets (CIFAR, ImageNet, CUB, Scenes) and architectures.  
- Provides open-source code and models to enhance reproducibility.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Novelty: Well-motivated, meaningful extension to existing knowledge distillation techniques.  
- Empirical validation: Rigorous, thorough experiments on small- and large-scale datasets with detailed baseline comparisons.  
- Literature placement: Well-grounded in prior work and clearly distinguishes the approach from existing methods.  
- Practical relevance: Computationally efficient (attention computed during forward pass) and compatible with existing architectures.  
- Suggestion: Clarify hyperparameter tuning (e.g., β) and report sensitivity/details for reproducibility.  
- Suggestion: Expand gradient-based attention transfer experiments beyond CIFAR and single architecture to larger datasets and models.  
- Suggestion: Provide more qualitative visualizations of attention maps before and after transfer across datasets/architectures.  
- Suggestion: Discuss limitations explicitly (e.g., computational cost of second backprop in gradient-based transfer, applicability beyond classification).  
- Questions: How do different attention mapping functions (Fsum vs Fmax) affect performance and when is each preferable?  
- Questions: Have you tested or do you anticipate challenges extending attention transfer to detection/segmentation tasks?  
- Questions: For gradient-based attention transfer, how does batch normalization affect the second backpropagation step and what strategies mitigate issues?
