Summary and contributions
The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a simple, general mechanism for knowledge transfer between convolutional networks. It defines spatial attention for CNNs in two ways: (i) activation-based attention obtained by pooling absolute activations across channels at a given layer (e.g., sum, sum of powers, max of powers), and (ii) gradient-based attention defined as sensitivity maps (input gradients of the loss). The core idea is to regularize a student so that its attention maps match those of a stronger teacher at corresponding spatial resolutions/layers, using an L2 loss on L2-normalized attention vectors in addition to the standard task loss. For gradient-based AT the authors leverage “double backpropagation” and also propose a flip-invariance regularizer. Extensive experiments on CIFAR-10 with WRN/NIN, fine-tuning on CUB-200 and MIT Scenes with ResNets, and ImageNet with ResNet-18/34 show consistent gains. They also show that activation-based AT outperforms regressing full activations (FitNets-style) and can complement Knowledge Distillation (KD). Code and models are released.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) A conceptually novel and well-motivated formulation of attention as a distillation target that is simple to implement and broadly applicable; (2) Consistent, meaningful empirical improvements across datasets and architectures (including ImageNet), with sensible ablations demonstrating the importance of the design choices (normalization, layer placement, attention pooling).

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper asks whether spatial “where” information learned by a strong CNN can be transferred to a student and whether this is more effective than aligning full feature tensors or soft targets. This is well placed in the literature: it builds on attention visualization (Simonyan, Zeiler, CAM/Grad-CAM) and knowledge transfer (KD, FitNets). The case for aligning spatial attention is compelling: it is architecture-agnostic, low-dimensional, and encodes class-relevant focus even when channel semantics are not aligned.
- Methodology: The activation-based attention family Fsum, Fpsum, Fpmax is sensible and flexible. The normalization of attention vectors before matching is an important and well-justified design choice to remove scale effects and focus on spatial patterns. The layer pairing strategy (per-block or per-group depending on depth alignment) is reasonable and practical. The gradient-based AT is technically correct (double backprop) and the added flip-invariance regularizer is a nice touch with clear regularization intuition.
- Empirical support: 
  - CIFAR-10: AT consistently improves student WRNs across same-depth and cross-depth pairs; combining AT with KD yields further gains. Ablations show layer-wise contributions and that sum-based pooling outperforms max-based, aligning with the intuition that multiple co-activated channels are informative. The comparison to full activation regression shows AT delivers larger final gains despite both speeding convergence.
  - Gradient-based AT: Even the classic gradient-norm penalty helps; the proposed symmetry term and teacher-student gradient matching further improve. While limited to NIN without BN, results are promising and connect to known regularization effects (double backprop).
  - Transfer learning (CUB/Scenes): AT substantially narrows the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34, outperforming KD on CUB, which plausibly benefits more from mid-level attention transfer.
  - ImageNet: A 1.1% top-1 and 0.8% top-5 improvement for ResNet-18 with only two AT losses is meaningful and rare among compression/distillation methods at this scale in 2016/2017. The negative KD result is consistent with reports that KD can struggle across depths/architectures.
Overall, the claims are supported by a diverse set of experiments, with ablations that probe key design choices.

Caveats and limitations (do not outweigh strengths)
- Gradient-based AT is demonstrated only on small models without BN; the authors note open questions about handling BN in the second backward pass.
- The ImageNet experiment is under-tuned (two losses, inherited hyperparameters); while the gain is notable, stronger baselines (KD with tuned temperature/alpha, FitNets-style hints, Jacobian regularization) and full training curves would strengthen the case.
- Statistical reporting could be more consistent (some results are medians over multiple runs, others single runs).
- AT is largely class-agnostic; class-conditional attention (e.g., Grad-CAM guided transfer) might further improve distillation for multi-object scenes or fine-grained tasks.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not decision-critical)
- Provide a more systematic ablation on normalization (L1 vs L2), the exponent p, and the effect of matching spatial resolutions (with/without interpolation) on performance and stability.
- Report compute/memory overhead precisely for activation-based AT and gradient-based AT (double backprop), including teacher forward cost when combined with KD.
- On ImageNet, add comparisons to (i) feature-hint transfer with 1x1 adapters, (ii) KD with tuned T/alpha and same-architecture teacher, and (iii) Jacobian-based regularizers (double backprop baseline) for completeness.
- Explore class-conditional attention transfer using Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++ to check whether aligning class-specific attention improves over class-agnostic maps.
- Analyze robustness/data-efficiency: does AT help more in low-data regimes or under distribution shift? The CUB/Scenes results suggest yes; quantify this with controlled ablations.
- Clarify the schedule for β (layer-wise scaling, decay) and its sensitivity; provide a simple recipe users can follow.
- Discuss cross-architecture transfer in more depth (e.g., teacher ResNet → student DenseNet/MobileNet), and the effect of teacher quality (what if the teacher is only marginally better?).

Questions for the authors
1. Normalization: You emphasize L2 normalization of attention maps. Did you try per-location normalization (e.g., softmax over spatial positions) or temperature scaling? How sensitive are results to the choice of normalization and p?
2. Layer selection: Beyond per-group vs per-block, which specific layers contribute most? Does adding early-layer AT ever hurt due to low-level edge focus?
3. Class-conditional attention: Have you experimented with transferring Grad-CAM maps for the ground-truth class (or top-k) instead of class-agnostic activation maps? If not, do you anticipate benefits/drawbacks?
4. KD interplay: On CIFAR, AT+KD helps; on ImageNet KD struggled. With tuned KD (different T, label-smoothing, or logit pairing), does AT still add gains? Any insights on when AT dominates KD?
5. Gradient-based AT and BN: Can you share preliminary findings or recommended practice (e.g., freezing BN statistics, using ghost batches) for stable double backprop with BN?
6. Overhead: What is the wall-clock and memory overhead of activation-based AT vs baseline on ImageNet? For gradient AT, what is the slowdown factor?
7. Teacher quality: How do gains scale with teacher-student performance gap? Is there a point where a mediocre teacher harms the student via misaligned attention?
8. Beyond classification: Have you tried AT for detection/segmentation (e.g., transferring F2sum from backbone stages)? Any pitfalls anticipated when spatial resolution is critical?

Overall, the paper presents a clear, useful idea with solid empirical backing and good potential impact.
