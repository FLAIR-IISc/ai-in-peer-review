Summary (positive and generous)
- The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT): training a student CNN to match spatial attention maps of a stronger teacher, in addition to the standard task loss. Two attention definitions are considered: (a) activation-based (channel-wise reductions of feature magnitudes such as sum |A|, sum |A|^p, max |A|^p) at multiple layers; and (b) gradient-based (input sensitivity maps), with an added flip-invariance regularizer.
- The method is simple, architecture-agnostic, and can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD). The authors emphasize normalization of attention maps as crucial, provide ablations over attention functions and layer placement, compare to full activation matching (FitNets-style hints), and report consistent gains on CIFAR-10 across several WRN student/teacher pairs. They also show improvements in transfer learning on CUB and MIT Scenes using ResNet-18 as student and ResNet-34 as teacher, and a notable 1.1% top-1 gain on ImageNet for ResNet-18 via AT from ResNet-34, with code released.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Conceptual novelty and practicality: defining and transferring spatial attention is a clear, well-motivated idea that is easy to implement and broadly applicable.
- Empirical support: consistent improvements across datasets and architectures, including a credible ImageNet result, with ablations indicating why/when AT works (e.g., sum-based attention and multi-layer losses outperform alternatives).

Supporting arguments
1) Problem addressed
- The work asks whether attention—operationalized as spatial maps highlighting “where the network looks”—can be defined for CNNs and used as a knowledge transfer signal to improve a student’s performance. This directly targets the limitations of KD/feature-hint methods that do not explicitly enforce spatial emphasis alignment.

2) Motivation and placement in literature
- The motivation is well grounded in both human/ML attention literature and in knowledge transfer/distillation (Bucila/Hinton, FitNets). The paper clearly distinguishes attention maps from full activation matching: attention emphasizes spatial saliency independent of channel alignment and seems intuitively more transferable across architectural differences. Related visualization methods (saliency, deconv, CAM, Grad-CAM) are discussed; AT leverages similar constructs but turns them into a training signal. The discussion of width vs depth (WRN) further motivates use cases where student networks are shallower/wider and need architecture-agnostic guidance.

3) Do the results support the claims?
- Activation-based AT: On CIFAR-10, multiple teacher/student pairs show consistent error reductions, with AT outperforming full activation transfer. Ablations indicate: (i) sum-based maps beat max-based; (ii) placing losses at multiple groups helps; (iii) normalization is important. These support the claim that attention maps carry transferable supervisory signal beyond full activations.
- Gradient-based AT: On CIFAR-10 (NIN without BN), gradient-based AT and related regularizers (double backprop, flip symmetry) improve over baseline; activation-based AT remains strongest. The gradient-based experiments are more limited (no BN, single runs), but still show promise.
- Larger datasets: In transfer learning (CUB, Scenes), AT narrows the gap between ResNet-18 and -34; on ImageNet, AT yields +1.1% top-1 for ResNet-18 with only two attention losses and minimal tuning. This strengthens the generality claim.
- Rigor: The CIFAR activation-based experiments include some ablations and (in places) medians of 5 runs. However, some parts (e.g., gradient-based AT and ImageNet) are under-explored and hyperparameters are not exhaustively tuned; KD baselines are not universally strong on ImageNet. Despite these limitations, the weight of evidence is convincing that AT is useful, simple, and broadly applicable.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect my decision)
- Report variance across seeds more systematically (CIFAR: already partly done; extend to more settings, and provide CIs where feasible).
- Quantify computational overhead (forward/backward time, memory) for activation-based AT and for gradient-based AT (cost of second backprop), including with/without BN.
- Provide a more thorough KD baseline on ImageNet (temperature, alpha sweeps, label smoothing) to ensure fairness; discuss why KD struggles with depth mismatch and how AT avoids it.
- Explore layer selection: compare “all blocks” versus “last k groups” on ImageNet, and sensitivity to attention resolution mismatches (alignment/interpolation choices).
- Analyze hyperparameter sensitivity (β, choice of p, normalization type) and provide practical guidelines.
- Consider per-class attention transfer (e.g., Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM-like) versus class-agnostic activation maps; discuss trade-offs.
- Add qualitative visualizations showing student attention convergence over training, and failure cases.
- Discuss whether AT affects robustness (e.g., sensitivity/gradient norms, common corruptions).

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are the gains to β and to the set of layers where AT is applied? Can you provide a brief sweep on ImageNet/CUB?
- Why do sum-based attention maps consistently outperform max-based ones? Is it due to multi-channel redundancy or noise sensitivity of max?
- How do you handle spatial mismatches when teacher/student have different strides/receptive fields? Did you try dilated/atrous variants to better align attention scales?
- Are attention maps normalized per-sample or per-batch? Did you try l1 normalization or temperature scaling of maps, and with what effect?
- What is the exact compute overhead of AT in practice (CIFAR, ImageNet), and for gradient-based AT with and without BN? Any stability issues in second backprop with BN, and did you try freezing BN statistics/affines?
- For ImageNet KD, which temperatures/alphas were tried? Did you compare to label smoothing or distance-to-teacher-logits without softmax?
- Does AT still help when teacher and student have similar capacity/accuracy, or when the teacher is only marginally better? Any negative transfer observed?
- Have you tried class-conditional attention transfer (e.g., matching attention conditioned on the ground-truth class vs. predicted class), and does it help fine-grained tasks further?
- Can AT be extended to detection/segmentation by aligning attention in intermediate feature maps that drive region proposals or masks?

Overall, this paper introduces a simple, well-motivated idea with broad applicability and compelling empirical gains, meriting acceptance.
