- Proposes attention transfer (AT) to transfer knowledge by matching spatial attention maps between teacher and student CNNs.

- Two AT families: activation-based (aggregate absolute activations across channels, L2-normalize, match with L2 loss) and gradient-based (match input sensitivity maps ∂L/∂x via double backprop + symmetry regularizer).

- Empirical gains reported on CIFAR-10/100 (WRN, NIN), fine-tuning on CUB and MIT Scenes, and ImageNet (ResNet-18 student, ResNet-34 teacher); code released.

- Key empirical findings: activation-based AT outperforms full-activation transfer (FitNets), sum-based aggregation beats max-based, and AT complements KD on smaller datasets.

- Decision: Accept.

- Main reasons for acceptance: novel, clear, simple spatial-attention formulation for transfer; strong empirical support including notable ImageNet improvement and ablation studies.

- Clarity: attention definitions, AT loss, normalization, layer choices, and gradient-based double-backprop rationale are explicit and easy to implement.

- Technical soundness: normalized spatial matching avoids scaling issues, multi-stage loss placement is sensible, gradient-based derivation is correct and limitations are acknowledged.

- Novelty/related work: using attention maps as the transfer medium is distinct from prior attention-as-prediction/visualization uses and yields better results than full-activation regression.

- Empirical rigor: diverse teacher/student pairings, ablations on mapping functions and layer selection, and comparisons to strong baselines strengthen the claims.

- ImageNet result: +1.1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18 with two AT losses is substantive; authors transparently report KD difficulties on ImageNet.

- Caveat: gradient-based AT shown only on CIFAR/NIN without batch norm; BN treatment for second backward pass is unresolved.

- Caveat: ImageNet experiments are limited (only two transfer points, no hyperparameter tuning, no multi-run statistics).

- Caveat: claim of first knowledge-transfer success on ImageNet not fully substantiated versus all contemporaneous baselines; some reference formatting and missing figures/tables.

- Suggestion: report training-time overhead for activation- and gradient-based AT (wall-clock multipliers) and memory costs.

- Suggestion: report mean±std across multiple runs and statistical significance for key CIFAR and ImageNet results.

- Suggestion: expand ImageNet experiments (place AT at all stages, vary p, try more student/teacher pairs, include tuned KD baselines).

- Suggestion: provide sensitivity analysis for β, p, layer selection, and normalization (l1 vs l2), and clarify β scheduling.

- Suggestion: for gradient-based AT, recommend BN handling (freeze BN stats or use ghost batches) for the second pass and document procedure.

- Suggestion: evaluate AT on tasks where spatial attention is critical (object detection, segmentation, weakly supervised localization).

- Suggestion: clean up reference formatting and ensure fair comparison to contemporaneous distillation/transfer methods.

- Question: how are β and p chosen per dataset/layer and how sensitive are results to these hyperparameters and loss placements?

- Question: did l1 or softmax normalization or channel-wise normalization before aggregation change performance?

- Question: what is the exact training-time and memory overhead per iteration for activation- and gradient-based AT?

- Question: are ImageNet gains stable across random seeds and multiple runs (provide variance estimates)?

- Question: can KD be tuned to recover benefits on ImageNet with matched architectures, and how does AT+KD perform then?

- Question: what BN behavior was observed during the second backprop, and did freezing BN statistics or using eval mode help?

- Question: how does AT scale with teacher strength (marginally better vs substantially stronger teachers)?

- Question: have you tested AT in detection/segmentation frameworks and do intermediate activation-based attention maps transfer well there?
