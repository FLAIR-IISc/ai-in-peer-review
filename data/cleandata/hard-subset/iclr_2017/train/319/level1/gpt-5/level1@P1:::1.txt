Summary of contributions
- The paper introduces “attention transfer” (AT), a simple, general, and effective mechanism to transfer knowledge from a teacher CNN to a student CNN by matching spatial attention maps at selected layers during training. Two complementary notions of attention are proposed:
  - Activation-based attention: spatial maps computed from hidden activations by (channel-wise) statistics such as sum of absolute values, with a strong practical choice being the squared-sum (F^2_sum), followed by l2 normalization and an l2 matching loss.
  - Gradient-based attention: spatial maps defined as the gradient of the loss w.r.t. the input; the student is trained to match the teacher’s input-gradient, requiring a double backpropagation; a flip-invariance (symmetry) regularizer on gradients is also proposed.
- The method is architecture-agnostic and incurs negligible overhead for activation-based AT. It is complementary to Knowledge Distillation (KD) and can be combined with it.
- Empirically, AT consistently improves student performance across CIFAR-10/100 with WRN/NIN/ResNet, transfer learning on CUB-200 and MIT Scenes with ResNet-18/34, and large-scale ImageNet (ResNet-18 with ResNet-34 teacher), where it yields +1.1% top-1, +0.8% top-5 absolute gains.
- The paper also shows that matching attention maps outperforms regressing full activations (FitNet-style hints) and provides ablations on attention definitions (sum vs max, effect of p), placement of losses, and normalization.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel, well-motivated idea with a simple formulation that is broadly applicable and easy to adopt.
- Strong and consistent empirical gains across diverse settings, including a notable improvement on ImageNet where prior distillation-style methods struggled.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets a timely problem—how to transfer “where to look” rather than the full representational content. The choice to define attention maps that summarize spatial “importance” is well motivated by cognitive and interpretability literature and by prior visualization work (Simonyan et al., Zeiler & Fergus, Guided Backprop, CAM, Grad-CAM). Positioning relative to distillation and FitNets is clear: AT aims to transfer spatial saliency rather than logits or full features, which is attractive for cross-architecture transfer and training efficiency.
- Method soundness and clarity: The activation-based AT objective is simple, differentiable, and justified (l2-normalized maps avoid scale issues). The layer-placement strategy (per block or per group) is reasonable, and attention definitions are explored (sum vs max, p>1). The gradient-based formulation correctly derives the need for double backprop and relates to Drucker & LeCun’s regularization; the flip-invariance term is a neat, low-cost regularizer.
- Empirical support:
  - CIFAR: Meaningful and consistent gains with both same-depth and different-depth teacher/student pairs; AT+KD often strongest. Ablations show sum-based maps outperform max-based; all three group-level losses help; AT beats full activation regression while also speeding convergence.
  - Gradient-based: Improves over baseline and over double backprop; flip-invariance is a strong regularizer; activation-based AT still best in same setup. The section candidly discusses practicalities (e.g., BN) and complexity.
  - Transfer learning: AT effectively closes a substantial part of the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34 on CUB and Scenes; notably, AT outperforms KD on fine-grained CUB, supporting the claim that intermediate spatial attention is especially valuable.
  - ImageNet: The +1.1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18 with minimal tuning is a compelling result; KD is reported to be ineffective under the same conditions, highlighting AT’s robustness.
- Reproducibility: Code and models are released; implementation details for major experiments are given.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all affect the accept decision)
- Statistical rigor: Report mean±std over multiple runs, especially on CIFAR, and specify whether reported numbers are medians or single runs across all tables. Some sections already use medians; standardize this choice.
- Fair comparisons: For ImageNet, detail training schedules, data augmentation, early stopping, and compute budgets for both student baselines and AT to ensure fairness. For KD on ImageNet, provide stronger hyperparameter sweeps (temperature, α) before concluding it “does not work.”
- Gradient-based AT practicality: Clarify computational overhead and memory usage (e.g., exact factor vs standard training) and provide guidance for frameworks with BN (frozen vs updated stats during the second pass).
- Sensitivity analyses: Systematically study the effects of β scaling, l1 vs l2 normalization of maps, choice of layers, and p in Fpsum; include cross-architecture transfers (e.g., Inception→ResNet).
- Broader baselines: Include FitNets and KD on CUB/Scenes, and (if feasible) a modern logit matching or feature distillation baseline on ImageNet.
- Qualitative analysis: Add more visualizations contrasting teacher/student attentions pre/post AT to illustrate what is being transferred and potential failure modes (especially on ImageNet).
- Scope of claims: Rephrase “first successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet” more cautiously or cite attempts; alternatively, strengthen the claim by adding additional teacher/student pairs and ablations.

Questions for the authors
- Normalization: You emphasize l2-normalized attention maps. How sensitive are results to normalization choice (none, l1, temperature-scaled softmax)? Did you try matching probability distributions over spatial locations (e.g., KL on softmaxed maps)?
- Layer selection: How do gains scale with the number and positions of AT losses? On ImageNet, what is the effect of using all four residual stages vs only the last two?
- Teacher quality: How does AT perform with a weak teacher or when teacher and student have similar accuracy? Can the student ever surpass the teacher? Any negative transfer observed?
- Cross-architecture transfer: Do results hold when teacher and student have very different macro-architectures (e.g., ResNet→MobileNet/NASNet) or different strides/dilations? How do you align spatial scales?
- Computational cost: For gradient-based AT, what is the exact training-time/memory overhead relative to baseline? Is it practical for ImageNet-scale training?
- Batch normalization: In gradient-based AT, how do you handle BN during the second backprop? Do you freeze running stats and γ/β, or update them? Any empirical comparison?
- KD on ImageNet: Did you sweep KD temperatures and α thoroughly? Could the negative results stem from insufficient tuning or label smoothing already providing similar benefits?
- Failure cases: Are there categories where AT does not help or hurts? Any insights into when max-based maps might be preferable to sum-based?

Overall, this paper proposes a simple, well-motivated, and effective idea with compelling empirical evidence, particularly the ImageNet result. With strengthened experimental rigor and broader comparisons, it should have strong impact.
