Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Attention Transfer (AT) as a simple, general mechanism to transfer knowledge from a teacher CNN to a student by aligning spatial “attention maps.” Two attention definitions are explored: activation-based maps (channel-wise reductions of feature magnitudes, with normalization) and gradient-based maps (input saliency, optimized via a double-backprop objective). The method is architecture-agnostic, can be applied at multiple layers, and complements knowledge distillation (KD). Extensive experiments on CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet show consistent improvements across ResNets, NIN, and WRNs; notably, activation-based AT outperforms full activation regression and yields gains even on ImageNet. Code is released.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) A clear, well-motivated idea that is novel in how it operationalizes attention as a transferable inductive bias rather than merely a visualization tool. (2) Strong empirical evidence across datasets and architectures, including nontrivial gains on ImageNet with minimal overhead.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets a concrete gap in KD—what aspects of a representation are most beneficial to transfer—and argues that spatial attention captures class-discriminative focus while being lower-dimensional and robust. This is well positioned relative to FitNets, KD, CAM/Grad-CAM, and double backprop.
- Methodology: The activation-based formulation is clean (normalized spatial maps; choice of sum/max and p), layer-wise losses are sensible, and integration with KD is straightforward. The gradient-based AT connection to double backprop and symmetry constraints is principled.
- Evidence: CIFAR ablations (map types, layer placement) and comparisons to full activation transfer support the central claim. Gains on CUB/Scenes (closing ResNet-18/34 gaps) and ImageNet (ResNet-18 improved by 1.1% top-1) demonstrate breadth. Limitations are acknowledged (e.g., gradient AT with BN).
- Rigor: While results are compelling, some experiments use single runs; gradient AT is restricted to NIN on CIFAR; KD baselines on ImageNet are under-tuned.

Additional feedback (to help improve, not decisive)
- Report variability (mean/std over seeds) and hyperparameter sensitivity (β schedule, normalization, choice of p, layer selection).
- Provide compute/memory overhead for AT and double backprop; clarify BN handling in second backprop.
- Strengthen ImageNet baselines (better-tuned KD, FitNets-style hints) and explore cross-architecture transfer more systematically.
- Analyze failure modes and attention similarity vs. accuracy correlation; add qualitative maps for success/failure.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to l1 vs l2 normalization of attention maps and to the exponent p?
- What is the exact compute overhead per batch for activation-based AT and gradient-based AT?
- How do you handle batch norm parameters during the second backprop in gradient AT?
- Does AT still help when teacher and student differ substantially in downsampling schedules?
- Could you report multi-run statistics on ImageNet and ablate which groups contribute most?
- Any insights on why AT > full activation transfer—e.g., sparsity/low-rank advantages?
