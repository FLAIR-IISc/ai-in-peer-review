- Paper proposes Attention Transfer (AT): train a student CNN to match teacher spatial attention maps alongside task loss.
- Two attention types studied: activation-based (channel-wise reductions like sum |A|^p, max |A|^p) and gradient-based (input sensitivity maps) with flip-invariance regularizer.
- Method is simple, architecture-agnostic, combinable with knowledge distillation (KD), and code is released.
- Normalization of attention maps is emphasized as crucial for effective transfer.
- Ablations examine attention functions, layer placement, and compare to full activation (FitNets) matching.
- Consistent error reductions reported on CIFAR-10 across multiple WRN student/teacher pairs.
- Transfer learning improvements shown on CUB and MIT Scenes using ResNet-18 student and ResNet-34 teacher.
- Notable ImageNet result: +1.1% top-1 for ResNet-18 when transferring from ResNet-34 with minimal tuning.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason 1: Conceptual novelty and practicality—spatial attention is a well-motivated, easy-to-implement transfer signal.
- Key reason 2: Empirical support—consistent improvements across datasets and architectures with informative ablations.
- Supporting argument: AT addresses limitations of KD/feature-hint methods by enforcing spatial emphasis alignment instead of channel-wise activation matching.
- Literature placement: Grounded in attention/visualization and KD/transfer literature, and distinguishes AT from full activation matching.
- CIFAR activation-based results: sum-based attention and multi-layer losses outperform alternatives; normalization matters.
- Gradient-based AT shows promise (with double backprop and flip symmetry) but is weaker and more limited in experiments.
- Larger-dataset evidence (CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) strengthens the generality claim, though some experiments lack exhaustive tuning.
- Rigor notes: CIFAR includes some 5-run medians, but gradient-based and ImageNet experiments are under-explored and KD baselines on ImageNet are not fully strong.
- Suggested improvement: report variance across seeds more systematically and provide confidence intervals where feasible.
- Suggested improvement: quantify computational overhead (time/memory) for activation- and gradient-based AT, including BN effects.
- Suggested improvement: provide a stronger KD baseline on ImageNet (temperature/alpha sweeps, label smoothing) and explain depth-mismatch effects.
- Suggested improvement: explore layer selection strategies, attention resolution mismatches, and sensitivity to interpolation choices.
- Suggested improvement: analyze hyperparameter sensitivity (β, p, normalization) and give practical guidelines.
- Suggested improvement: consider per-class (class-conditional) attention transfer and discuss trade-offs with class-agnostic maps.
- Suggested improvement: add qualitative visualizations of student attention convergence and failure cases.
- Suggested improvement: evaluate whether AT affects robustness (gradient norms, common corruptions).
- Questions for authors: sensitivity of gains to β and layer choices; brief sweep on ImageNet/CUB?
- Question: why do sum-based maps outperform max-based—multi-channel redundancy or max noise sensitivity?
- Question: how are spatial mismatches handled between teacher/student strides/receptive fields; tried dilated variants?
- Question: are attention maps normalized per-sample or per-batch; tried l1 normalization or temperature scaling?
- Question: exact compute overhead of AT in practice and stability of second backprop with BN; any fixes like freezing BN?
- Question: which KD temperatures/alphas were tried on ImageNet; compared to label smoothing or logit distance?
- Question: does AT help when teacher/student capacities are similar or marginally different; any negative transfer observed?
- Question: tried class-conditional attention transfer (conditional on ground-truth or predicted class) and effect on fine-grained tasks?
- Question: can AT be extended to detection/segmentation by aligning intermediate feature maps driving proposals or masks?
