Summary of contributions
The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a mechanism for knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student CNN. The central idea is to define spatial attention maps for convolutional layers and to encourage a student to match the teacher’s attention at selected layers while learning the primary task. Two families of attention are explored:

- Activation-based attention: spatial maps constructed by aggregating absolute activation magnitudes across channels (e.g., sum of |A|^p), with an AT loss that matches l2-normalized attention maps between teacher and student at multiple layers.
- Gradient-based attention: input sensitivity maps (∂L/∂x), matched via a “double backpropagation” objective; a symmetry regularizer enforcing horizontal flip invariance is also introduced.

Empirically, the paper reports consistent improvements across CIFAR-10/100 with WRNs and NIN, fine-tuning on CUB and MIT Scenes, and ImageNet (ResNet-18 student, ResNet-34 teacher), with code released. The authors also show: (i) activation-based AT outperforms transferring full activations (FitNets-style hints), (ii) sum-based attention mappings outperform max-based variants, and (iii) AT can complement knowledge distillation (KD) on smaller datasets.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Clear, simple, and well-motivated formulation of spatial attention for CNNs that is novel in the context of knowledge transfer; (2) Strong empirical support across models and datasets, including a notable gain on ImageNet, plus ablations demonstrating design choices (normalization, mapping functions, layer placement).

Supporting arguments
Clarity
- The attention definitions are explicit and easy to implement (channel-wise aggregation for activation-based attention; Jacobians for gradient-based attention).
- The AT loss is precisely described, including the importance of l2 normalizing spatial maps, the choice of layers for transfer, and compatibility with KD.
- The gradient-based section clearly explains the need for a second backprop pass and relates it to classic double backprop regularization. The symmetry term is well-defined.

Technical soundness
- The activation-based AT objective is straightforward and well-posed: matching normalized spatial distributions avoids trivial scaling issues and is justified empirically.
- The choice of placing AT losses at multiple stages (per block or per group) is sensible; an ablation indicates each loss contributes.
- The gradient-based AT derivation is correct; the implementation cost and stability considerations are acknowledged, and it is related to prior second-order regularization work.
- The authors appropriately limit gradient-based experiments due to complexities with batch normalization and describe practical concerns to be addressed.

Novelty and relation to literature
- Prior works on attention mainly use it as a mechanism for prediction or as a visualization tool. Using attention maps as the medium for knowledge transfer is a distinct and well-placed idea relative to KD and FitNets.
- The paper contrasts attention transfer to full activation regression and demonstrates superior effectiveness of attention matching, which is a substantive insight for the distillation community.

Empirical support and rigor
- CIFAR experiments cover multiple teacher/student width/depth pairings, consistently showing gains for AT and AT+KD. The ablations on mapping functions (sum vs. max) and on layer selection increase confidence in the design.
- The comparison to direct full-activation transfer (with and without 1x1 adapters) is a strong baseline and supports the claim that attention is a better transfer signal.
- The gradient-based section evaluates several related objectives (double backprop, symmetry, KD, activation-based AT), showing AT performs best in the tested setting.
- On transfer learning (CUB, Scenes), AT narrows the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34. This is a practical and convincing application scenario.
- On ImageNet, the reported +1.1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18 with a ResNet-34 teacher using only two AT losses is substantive. The paper candidly reports KD difficulties on ImageNet, strengthening the case for AT.

Caveats (do not outweigh strengths)
- Gradient-based AT is only demonstrated on CIFAR/NIN without batch norm, and the batch norm treatment for the second backward pass is left unresolved.
- ImageNet results are limited (two transfer points, no hyperparameter tuning) and lack multi-run statistics.
- The claim of being the first knowledge transfer success on ImageNet is not comprehensively substantiated against all possible contemporaneous baselines.
- Some references are formatted inconsistently; some experimental tables/figures are referred to but not fully included in the provided text.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not necessarily affecting the decision)
- Provide training-time overhead for activation-based AT (expected to be small) and exact overhead for gradient-based AT (roughly one extra backward pass). Report wall-clock multipliers.
- Report mean and standard deviation over multiple runs for key results (especially CIFAR and ImageNet), and include statistical significance where feasible.
- Expand ImageNet experiments: (i) place AT losses at all stages, (ii) evaluate different p values, (iii) test other student/teacher widths/depths, and (iv) include tuned KD baselines with matched architectures.
- Clarify beta selection and scheduling, and provide a sensitivity analysis for β, p, layer selection, and normalization (l1 vs l2).
- For gradient-based AT, document a recommended procedure in the presence of batch normalization (e.g., freezing BN statistics in the second pass, or using ghost batches).
- Consider additional transfer tasks where spatial attention should be more critical (object detection, weakly supervised localization) to further validate the premise.
- Clean up reference formatting and ensure all cited prior distillation/transfer methods contemporary to ICLR 2017 are fairly compared where possible.

Questions for the authors
- Hyperparameters: How are β and p chosen per dataset and layer? How sensitive are results to these choices and to the number/placement of attention losses?
- Normalization: You use l2 normalization over the spatial map. Did l1 or softmax normalization change performance? Does normalization over channels before aggregation help?
- Computational cost: What is the exact training-time overhead of activation-based AT per iteration? For gradient-based AT, what is the measured overhead and memory footprint compared to baseline?
- Robustness: For ImageNet, are the reported gains stable across random seeds and training runs? Please provide variance estimates or multiple-run averages.
- KD on ImageNet: With matched student/teacher architectures and tuned temperature/α, can KD recover its benefits? How does AT+KD perform on ImageNet under matched architectures?
- Batch normalization in gradient-based AT: What behavior did you observe when attempting the second backprop with BN? Did freezing BN statistics or using evaluation mode in the second pass help?
- Teacher strength: How does AT scale with teacher quality? E.g., what happens if the teacher is only marginally better than the student, or substantially stronger?
- Wider applicability: Have you tested AT in detection/segmentation frameworks (e.g., Faster R-CNN, FCN)? Do activation-based attention maps at intermediate feature resolutions transfer well there?
