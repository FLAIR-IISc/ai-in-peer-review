This paper proposes a method for improving the performance of a student convolutional neural network (CNN) by transferring attention maps from a more powerful teacher network. The authors define attention as spatial maps representing areas of focus and explore both activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer techniques. They demonstrate consistent improvements across various datasets and CNN architectures, including both residual and non-residual networks, and show that activation-based transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to knowledge transfer, focusing on the often-overlooked aspect of attention maps. The empirical results demonstrate consistent and significant improvements across a diverse set of experiments, lending strong support to the claims. The availability of code and models further strengthens the paper’s contribution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of transferring knowledge between CNNs, going beyond simple weight or feature transfer to focus on attention mechanisms. The motivation stemming from human visual attention is well-explained and serves as a strong foundation for the work.
* **Well-Placed in Literature:** The related work section is comprehensive and accurately contextualizes the proposed approach within the existing landscape of attention mechanisms, knowledge distillation, and visualization techniques. The authors correctly position their work as a revisiting of knowledge transfer, shifting focus towards thinner student networks.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are extensive and cover a range of datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet). The consistent improvements observed across these varying conditions provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed attention transfer methods. The ablation studies (comparing different attention mapping functions, full activation transfer vs. attention transfer) are insightful and help to understand the key factors contributing to the success of the approach. The quantitative results are supported by qualitative visualizations of attention maps, further bolstering the claims.
* **Novelty:** The idea of explicitly transferring attention maps as a form of knowledge distillation is a novel contribution. The specific formulations for activation-based and gradient-based attention, and their application to improving student network performance, are original.
* **Reproducibility:** Providing the code and models significantly improves the reproducibility of the results, which is a crucial aspect of scientific rigor.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Gradient-based Attention Details:** While the gradient-based attention transfer section is present, it feels less developed than the activation-based transfer section. More detailed analysis of the experimental results for gradient-based transfer (e.g., a discussion of the challenges encountered and the reasons for its performance relative to activation-based transfer) would be beneficial. The second backpropagation step could be elaborated with more detail.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper mentions briefly tuning hyperparameters, but a more thorough investigation of the sensitivity of the approach to hyperparameters (e.g., the weight β in the loss function) would strengthen the findings. A discussion of the optimal settings for different datasets and architectures would be valuable.
* **Computational Cost:** A more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the attention transfer process would be useful. Specifically, quantifying the overhead introduced by calculating and transferring attention maps could help assess the practical applicability of the approach.
* **Combination with other techniques:** Exploring the combination of attention transfer with other knowledge distillation methods beyond the simple addition mentioned (e.g., incorporating feature-based distillation along with attention transfer) could lead to further performance gains.
* **Impact of network depth difference:** The impact of larger differences in depth between teacher and student networks requires more investigation. While the paper shows benefits for some depth differences, further exploration of this aspect could reveal limitations or optimal configurations.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. In the gradient-based attention transfer section, you mention challenges related to batch normalization during the second backpropagation step. Could you elaborate on these challenges and the strategies you considered for addressing them?
2. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the β parameter (weighting of the attention transfer loss)? Did you perform a systematic search over different values of β for each dataset and architecture?
3. What is the approximate computational overhead introduced by calculating and transferring attention maps, compared to standard training without attention transfer?
4. Can you discuss the potential limitations of this approach? Are there specific scenarios or datasets where attention transfer might not be effective?
5. In the ImageNet experiments, you mention that KD did not perform well. Could you provide more details about the KD setup (temperature, alpha) and the observed convergence behavior? Did you experiment with different KD hyperparameters?
