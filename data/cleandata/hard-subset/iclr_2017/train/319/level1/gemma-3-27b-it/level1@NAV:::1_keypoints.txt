- Paper proposes transferring spatial attention maps (from activations and gradients) between teacher and student CNNs to guide learning.  
- The core idea—using a well-trained network’s “where to look” information to improve a student—is compelling and well-motivated.  
- Authors show comprehensive experiments across datasets and architectures with consistent performance improvements.  
- Activation-based attention transfer shows clear benefits; CIFAR and finetuning results on CUB and Scenes are encouraging.  
- Recommendation: Reject due to insufficient experimental clarity and control despite interesting results.  
- Hyperparameter tuning (e.g., β in the loss) is not well described; sensitivity analysis is missing.  
- Justification for certain design choices (e.g., architectures used for gradient-based transfer) is lacking.  
- Claims about being the first KD method successful on ImageNet are overstated without stronger comparisons to established KD techniques.  
- Inconsistencies across experimental setups (different normalizations for attention types) hinder direct comparisons.  
- Gradient-based attention transfer appears less robust and is constrained by chosen network architectures.  
- Computational cost and scalability (training and inference overhead) are not sufficiently discussed.  
- Paper is heavily empirical and would benefit from more theoretical grounding of the proposed methods.  
- Reproducibility could be improved with clearer instructions despite provided code.  
- Additional suggested studies: β sensitivity analysis, deeper ablation of layer-wise attention transfer, and a detailed comparison/explanation for ImageNet/KD results.
