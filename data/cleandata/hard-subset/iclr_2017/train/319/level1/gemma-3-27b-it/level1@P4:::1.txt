This paper explores the transfer of attention mechanisms from a teacher convolutional neural network (CNN) to a student CNN to improve the student's performance. The authors define attention based on activation and gradient maps and propose novel methods for transferring this information. They demonstrate improvements across several datasets and CNN architectures.

**Reject**

The paper presents an interesting idea, but the experimental results lack sufficient clarity and rigor to support the claims of significant improvement. Furthermore, the novelty, while present, feels incremental given the existing body of work on knowledge distillation and attention mechanisms.

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of convincing empirical evidence. While the paper reports improvements on several datasets, the presentation of results is often vague. For example, in the CIFAR experiments, table 1 shows improvements, but the exact experimental setup (hyperparameter tuning, training schedule) isn’t clearly described, making reproducibility difficult. The claim of a "significant" improvement feels unsupported when the reported gains are often less than 1% accuracy. The ImageNet experiments are even less convincing, with a brief mention of improvements without detailed results or comparisons. The comparisons to full activation transfer are also weakly presented – the reader is left unclear about the specifics of the implementation and why attention transfer outperforms it.

Secondly, the paper doesn’t convincingly differentiate itself from existing knowledge distillation techniques. While the authors argue attention transfer is distinct from simply transferring activations, the described methods appear closely related, and the performance gains aren't consistently substantial enough to justify this claim. The comparison to FitNets, while mentioned, lacks depth. A more thorough comparison with recent knowledge distillation approaches (e.g., those employing more sophisticated distillation losses) would strengthen the contribution. The discussion of KD failing on ImageNet feels more like an observation than a detailed analysis.

Additional feedback to improve the paper:

*   **Reproducibility:** Provide a detailed description of the experimental setup, including hyperparameters, optimization algorithms, training schedules, and data augmentation techniques. Release the code with sufficient documentation to allow others to reproduce the results.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more comprehensive ablation studies to isolate the impact of different components of the proposed approach (e.g., different attention mapping functions, different loss weights, different layers for attention transfer).
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** Include stronger and more comprehensive baselines, especially more recent knowledge distillation methods.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests to demonstrate that the observed improvements are not due to chance.
*   **Clearer Presentation:** Improve the clarity and organization of the paper. The descriptions of the different attention mapping functions could be more concise. The figures could be improved to better illustrate the key findings. The paper could benefit from a more focused discussion of the limitations of the approach.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** While the paper is empirical, providing some theoretical justification for why attention transfer works could strengthen the contribution.
*   **Gradient-Based Attention Details:** Clarify the implementation details of the gradient-based attention transfer, especially the challenges related to batch normalization and the second backpropagation step.

Questions for the authors:

1.  What specific hyperparameter tuning was performed for the CIFAR experiments? Can you provide more detail on the learning rate schedule and optimizer used?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the ImageNet results? What were the specific architectures used for the teacher and student networks? What was the training schedule and learning rate used?
3.  Why do you believe attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer? What are the key differences in the information captured by attention maps versus full activations?
4.  How does the proposed approach compare to other recent knowledge distillation methods that employ more sophisticated distillation losses (e.g., feature-based distillation)?
5.  Can you elaborate on the challenges of applying gradient-based attention transfer to networks with batch normalization? How can these challenges be addressed?
