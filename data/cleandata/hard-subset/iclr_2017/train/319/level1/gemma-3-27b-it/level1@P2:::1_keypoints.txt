- Proposal: Transfer knowledge from teacher to student CNNs via attention maps (activation- and gradient-based) to improve student performance, with demonstrated consistent gains.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Problem clarity: Clear framing of knowledge transfer using attention, motivated by human visual attention.  
- Novelty: Attention transfer (vs. activations/predictions) is a novel contribution; explores activation- and gradient-based methods and multiple activation mapping functions (sum, p-sum, p-max).  
- Empirical strength: Extensive experiments on CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet across architectures show consistent improvements and useful ablation studies.  
- Related work: Comprehensive literature review situating the contribution among attention, distillation, and visualization methods.  
- Reproducibility: Code and models available.  
- Gradient-based transfer: Results/analysis for gradient-based attention feel underdeveloped and perform worse than activation-based methods—needs deeper explanation.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Lack of detailed analysis on sensitivity to β (loss weight) and how optimal values vary by dataset/architecture.  
- Computational cost: Quantitative analysis of overhead (training time, memory) from attention transfer/double backpropagation is missing.  
- Visualizations: Attention map visualizations are helpful but should include direct teacher–student comparisons (overlays or difference maps) to highlight mimicry.  
- ImageNet KD failure: KD failed on ImageNet; authors should explain whether this was due to hyperparameters, implementation, or inherent limitations.  
- Questions for authors: Explain weak gradient-based results; report β sensitivity and hyperparameter search; provide quantitative training/memory overhead; detail troubleshooting and hypotheses for ImageNet KD failure.
