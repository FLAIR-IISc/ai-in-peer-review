- Decision: Accept.

- Proposal: Transfer attention maps from a powerful teacher CNN to improve student CNN performance.

- Attention definition: Spatial maps of focus; two transfer types explored — activation-based and gradient-based.

- Main empirical claim: Consistent improvements across datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet).

- Activation-based result: Activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and complements knowledge distillation.

- Novelty: Explicit attention-map transfer as a form of knowledge distillation is a novel contribution.

- Motivation & framing: Clear problem definition linking attention transfer to human visual attention and thinner student networks.

- Related work: Literature survey is comprehensive and properly positions the approach among attention, distillation, and visualization methods.

- Experiments & ablations: Extensive experiments and ablations (attention mappings, full vs. attention transfer) with quantitative and qualitative support.

- Reproducibility: Code and models are provided, improving reproducibility.

- Gradient-based transfer concern: Gradient-based attention section is underdeveloped; needs more analysis and explanation of second backpropagation.

- Hyperparameter sensitivity: More thorough analysis of sensitivity to β and other hyperparameters is needed.

- Computational cost: Lack of quantified overhead for computing/transferring attention maps; practicality needs assessment.

- Combination with other methods: Potential to combine attention transfer with other distillation techniques remains underexplored.

- Depth mismatch impact: Effects of larger teacher-student depth differences require further investigation.

- Questions for authors: Clarify BN challenges in gradient-based transfer; sensitivity/testing of β; approximate computational overhead; failure modes or dataset limitations; detailed KD setup and hyperparameter experiments on ImageNet.
