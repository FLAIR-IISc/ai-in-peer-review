This paper proposes a method for transferring knowledge from a teacher CNN to a student CNN by matching attention maps. The authors define attention maps as activation-based and gradient-based spatial maps and demonstrate improvements across various datasets and network architectures. The core idea is intuitive and the experimental results are promising, suggesting that attention information is a valuable channel for knowledge transfer.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a compelling idea and demonstrates some success, the experiments lack sufficient depth and clarity to convincingly establish the method’s robustness and generalizability. The experimental design feels somewhat ad-hoc, with hyperparameters not consistently tuned, and a tendency to report results selectively. Crucially, the ablation studies are limited, making it difficult to isolate the contribution of different components. The writing, while generally clear, suffers from redundancy and lacks concise explanations of key decisions.

The main reasons for rejection are: (1) the lack of rigorous ablation studies to isolate the impact of attention transfer versus other factors (like knowledge distillation or simply pre-training), and (2) the inconsistencies in experimental setup and hyperparameter tuning, which cast doubt on the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.

The paper suffers from several issues that weaken its overall impact. First, the comparison between attention transfer and full activation transfer is not fully convincing. While the authors claim attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer, the implementation details are vague – it's unclear whether the 1x1 convolutions used for shape matching introduce a significant performance bottleneck. A more direct comparison (e.g., a regression loss directly on activations with appropriate regularization) is needed. Second, the gradient-based attention transfer experiments are limited to a specific network and dataset. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don’t offer a strong justification.  The claim that it’s difficult to implement with batch normalization without a comprehensive analysis of *why* is unconvincing. Third, the experimental results on ImageNet, while positive, are presented without significant detail.  The claim that KD did *not* work well in this setting needs further explanation – what hyperparameters were tried? What was the training behavior? The limited reporting of results on ImageNet significantly weakens the overall claim of generalizability. Fourth, the connection to existing knowledge distillation methods is not fully explored. The authors mention that their method can be combined with KD, but don’t delve into how attention transfer complements or differs from existing techniques.  A more nuanced discussion of the relationship between attention transfer and KD is required. Finally, the appendix adds information but is not well integrated with the main text.

Here are some suggestions for improvement:

* **Rigorous Ablation Studies:**  Systematically investigate the impact of each component of the proposed method.  For example, compare different attention mapping functions (sum, max, p-norm) in a controlled manner.  Compare attention transfer with and without knowledge distillation.  Experiment with different weighting factors (β) for the attention transfer loss.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:**  Provide a clear explanation of the hyperparameter tuning process.  Report the range of hyperparameters explored and the criteria used to select the best values.
* **Reproducibility:**  Release a comprehensive code base that allows others to reproduce the results.  Provide detailed instructions on how to run the experiments.
* **Clearer Experimental Setup:**  Clearly state the experimental setup for each experiment.  Specify the datasets used, the network architectures, the training parameters, and the evaluation metrics.
* **Deeper Analysis of ImageNet Results:**  Provide more details about the ImageNet experiments.  Report the training curves, the validation accuracy, and the top-5 accuracy.  Explain why KD did not work well in this setting.
* **Theoretical Justification:** Explore any theoretical foundations for why attention transfer might work. Could it be related to representational similarity, or to improving the robustness of the student network?
* **Address the Batch Normalization Issue:**  Investigate the interaction between batch normalization and gradient-based attention transfer more thoroughly. Is the problem a mathematical one (e.g., related to the gradient calculation)? Or is it an implementation detail?

Specifically, I'd like to ask the authors the following:

1.  For the ImageNet experiments where KD failed, can you provide a detailed description of the hyperparameters tested (temperature, alpha, β) and the resulting training curves?
2.  In the activation-based vs. full activation transfer experiment, did you ensure that the 1x1 convolutional layers had sufficient capacity (number of filters) to accurately represent the full activation tensors? If not, could this be a confounding factor?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost of attention transfer versus full activation transfer?
4.  Regarding the choice of the l2 norm for the attention transfer loss, did you explore other norms (e.g., l1, cosine similarity)? If so, what were the results?
5.  Can you elaborate on the potential connection between attention transfer and existing knowledge distillation methods, such as feature-based KD or relation-based KD? How does attention transfer differ from these approaches?
