This paper proposes a method for transferring knowledge from a teacher CNN to a student CNN via attention maps. The core idea is to force the student network to mimic the attention patterns of a more powerful teacher network, thereby improving its performance. The authors explore both activation-based and gradient-based attention maps, and demonstrate consistent improvements across various datasets and CNN architectures.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper addresses a relevant and interesting problem – knowledge transfer in CNNs – and proposes a novel approach leveraging attention mechanisms. The experimental results are comprehensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly the activation-based attention transfer. The significant and consistent improvements across different architectures and datasets support the claims made in the paper.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of transferring knowledge between CNNs and proposes attention maps as a mechanism for doing so. The motivation stemming from human visual attention is well-explained.
* **Novelty and Approach:** The idea of transferring attention, rather than just activations or predictions, is a novel contribution. The exploration of both activation-based and gradient-based attention provides a thorough investigation of the approach. The different activation-based mapping functions (sum, p-sum, p-max) are also well-considered.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper presents extensive experimental results on CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet. The results consistently demonstrate improvements in performance when using attention transfer, both with and without knowledge distillation. The ablation studies (e.g., comparing activation transfer to full-activation transfer) provide valuable insights.
* **Well-Placed in Literature:** The related work section is comprehensive and covers relevant areas such as attention mechanisms, knowledge distillation, and visualization techniques. The paper appropriately positions its contribution within this landscape.
* **Code Availability:** The availability of code and models further strengthens the paper and facilitates reproducibility.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Gradient-Based Attention Detail:** The section on gradient-based attention transfer feels less fleshed out than the activation-based section. It would be beneficial to provide more detailed results and analysis for this approach. Why did it perform comparatively less well?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A more detailed discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., the β parameter in the loss function) would be helpful. How does the optimal value of β vary across different datasets and architectures?
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention the computational cost of double backpropagation, a more quantitative analysis of the overhead introduced by attention transfer would be valuable.
* **Visualization Enhancement:** The visualization of attention maps (Fig. 6 in the appendix) is helpful, but could be improved by including a more direct comparison between the teacher and student attention maps. Overlaid or difference maps could highlight the regions where the student successfully mimics the teacher.
* **ImageNet KD Failure:** The authors state that KD failed on ImageNet. Can they elaborate on *why* they suspect this is the case? Was it a hyperparameter issue? Or is there an inherent limitation to KD when combined with attention transfer on this particular dataset and architecture?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. In the gradient-based attention transfer section, why did the results seem less impressive compared to activation-based transfer? Were there specific challenges or limitations encountered during implementation or optimization?
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of the β parameter in the loss function (Eq. 2)? Did you perform a hyperparameter search to determine the optimal value, and how did it vary across different datasets and architectures?
3. Could you provide a more quantitative analysis of the computational overhead introduced by the attention transfer mechanism, specifically in terms of training time and memory usage?
4. Regarding the failure of KD on ImageNet, what specific steps were taken to troubleshoot this issue, and what hypotheses do you have regarding the underlying cause?
