### **Review of "Paying More Attention to Attention: Improving the Performance of Convolutional Neural Networks via Attention Transfer"**

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel and effective method for knowledge transfer called Attention Transfer (AT). The core idea is to improve a smaller "student" network by training it to mimic the spatial attention patterns of a larger, pre-trained "teacher" network. The authors propose two distinct ways to define these attention maps: (1) an "activation-based" method, which generates spatial maps by computing summary statistics (e.g., sum of absolute values powered by p) across the channel dimension of activation tensors, and (2) a "gradient-based" method, which uses the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image as a sensitivity map.

The primary contributions are:
*   The proposal of attention as a new and powerful medium for knowledge transfer, differing from prior work that focused on logits (Knowledge Distillation) or full intermediate features (FitNets).
*   The simple yet highly effective formulation of activation-based AT, which requires no additional learnable parameters.
*   Extensive empirical validation showing consistent performance improvements across a wide range of tasks (CIFAR-10, ImageNet, fine-grained classification on CUB, scene recognition) and modern architectures (NIN, ResNet, Wide ResNet).
*   A key finding that transferring these compressed attention maps is more effective than transferring the full activation tensors, and that AT can be successfully combined with Knowledge Distillation.
*   One of the first successful applications of a knowledge transfer technique to the large-scale ImageNet dataset, demonstrating a significant accuracy boost for ResNet-18.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novelty and Practicality:** The paper presents a simple, intuitive, and novel approach to knowledge distillation. By focusing on *where* a network looks, rather than matching its raw feature representations, it provides a more flexible and, as demonstrated, more effective form of guidance. The activation-based method is particularly elegant due to its simplicity and lack of overhead.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Support:** The claims are substantiated by a thorough set of experiments across multiple challenging benchmarks and state-of-the-art network architectures. The consistent gains, especially the significant improvement on ImageNet, provide compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness and generalizability, setting a strong new baseline for knowledge transfer research.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strength lies in its clear hypothesis and rigorous validation.

*   **Conceptual Soundness:** The motivation for using attention is well-grounded. The idea that a better-performing network has more refined attention patterns (as suggested by Fig. 4) provides a strong intuition for why transferring this information should be beneficial. It is a clever abstraction that captures salient spatial information while discarding redundant or overly constraining details present in full feature maps, which might be difficult for a smaller student network to replicate.

*   **Empirical Rigor:** The experimental design is excellent. The authors compare their method not only against the student baseline but also against relevant prior art (Knowledge Distillation) and a strong alternative (transferring full activations, dubbed "F-ActT"). The results in Table 1 clearly show that AT outperforms F-ActT and that combining AT with KD yields the best results on CIFAR-10. The success on ImageNet is particularly noteworthy; the authors report that KD struggles on this task with heterogeneous architectures, making their 1.1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18 a very significant result that validates the method's utility on large-scale problems. The transfer learning experiments on CUB and Scenes further demonstrate the method's robustness.

*   **Analysis and Insights:** The paper provides useful ablations, such as the comparison of different attention mapping functions (Table 2), which shows that sum-based functions are generally superior to max-based ones. This provides practical guidance for implementation. The qualitative visualizations of attention maps (Fig. 6) compellingly illustrate that the student's attention does become more teacher-like after training with AT.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended as constructive feedback and do not detract from the overall strength of the paper.

*   **Clarification on Normalization:** In Section 3.1, you state that L2-normalizing the vectorized attention maps `Q` is "important for the success of the student training." This is a strong claim that would be more convincing with quantitative evidence. An ablation study on a key task showing performance with and without this normalization would strengthen the paper and provide insight into why it is so crucial.
*   **Guidance on Hyperparameter β:** The guidance for setting the loss weight `β` is somewhat empirical ("set it to 10^3 divided by number of elements..."). A more principled explanation or a sensitivity analysis for this important hyperparameter would enhance the paper's practical value and reproducibility.
*   **Gradient-Based AT Section:** The gradient-based attention transfer experiments feel less developed than the activation-based ones. They are restricted to a simpler network on CIFAR without modern training components like batch normalization, and the authors acknowledge the technical challenges involved. While the results are interesting (especially the symmetry norm), this section feels preliminary. Focusing the main paper more on the thoroughly-validated activation-based AT and perhaps moving the gradient-based exploration to an appendix might result in a more streamlined narrative.
*   **Relation to Class Activation Maps (CAM):** The proposed activation-based attention maps are conceptually related to Class Activation Maps (Zhou et al., 2016), particularly when computed on the final convolutional layer. A brief discussion of the relationship would be insightful. The key difference appears to be that your maps are class-agnostic, while CAMs are class-specific. An interesting point for discussion would be why a class-agnostic signal might be a more general and effective form of knowledge to transfer.

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**
*   **Choice of Transfer Layers:** You apply AT losses at the end of each group of residual blocks. Have you explored the trade-offs of using more or fewer transfer points? For example, is there a benefit to applying the loss after every residual block, or is using only the final layer's attention map sufficient?
*   **On the Failure of KD on ImageNet:** You mention that Knowledge Distillation hurt convergence for the ResNet-18/34 pair on ImageNet. This is a very valuable "negative result" for the community. Could you elaborate on the hyperparameters (e.g., temperature, `α`) you tried? Understanding the conditions under which KD fails is as important as understanding when AT succeeds.
*   **Generality of Attention Functions:** You found that `F_sum^2` works well across your experiments. Do you have any intuition about whether the optimal attention function (e.g., the power `p`) might depend on the specific architecture, dataset, or layer depth?
