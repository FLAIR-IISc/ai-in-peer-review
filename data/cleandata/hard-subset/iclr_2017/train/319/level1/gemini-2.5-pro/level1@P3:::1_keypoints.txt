- Proposes Attention Transfer (AT) for knowledge transfer: student mimics teacher spatial attention maps.  
- Defines two attention map types: activation-based (channel-summary) and gradient-based (input-sensitivity).  
- Activation-based AT is simple, effective, and needs no extra learnable parameters.  
- AT consistently improves student performance across datasets (CIFAR-10, ImageNet, CUB, Scenes) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, Wide ResNet).  
- Transferring compressed attention maps outperforms transferring full activation tensors; AT can be combined with Knowledge Distillation (KD).  
- Demonstrates significant ImageNet gains (e.g., +1.1% top-1 for ResNet-18), one of the first large-scale successes for transfer methods.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Novel, practical approach that guides where networks look rather than raw features.  
- Key reason 2: Strong, comprehensive empirical validation and consistent gains establish a new baseline.  
- Conceptual soundness: attention captures salient spatial info while avoiding over-constraining students.  
- Empirical rigor: thorough comparisons vs. baselines (KD, F-ActT), ablations, visualizations, and transfer experiments.  
- Ablations show sum-based attention functions outperform max-based ones; visualizations confirm attention alignment.  
- Suggest providing quantitative evidence for L2-normalization’s importance (ablation).  
- Recommend more principled guidance or sensitivity analysis for the loss weight β.  
- Gradient-based AT experiments are preliminary and could be moved to an appendix or expanded.  
- Discuss relation to Class Activation Maps (CAM) and why class-agnostic maps may be preferable.  
- Ask authors to clarify choice/trade-offs of transfer layers (frequency and placement).  
- Request details on KD failures on ImageNet (temperatures, α, other hyperparameters tried).  
- Question whether the optimal attention function/power (p) depends on architecture, dataset, or layer depth.
