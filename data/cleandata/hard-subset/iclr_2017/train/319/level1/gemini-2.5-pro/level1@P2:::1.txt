### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel and intuitive mechanism for knowledge transfer between neural networks, termed "Attention Transfer" (AT). The core idea is that a smaller "student" network can be trained more effectively by forcing it to mimic the spatial attention maps of a more powerful, pre-trained "teacher" network. The authors make several key contributions: (1) They introduce the general concept of using attention as a form of "hint" for knowledge distillation. (2) They propose two concrete methods for defining spatial attention maps: an "activation-based" method that summarizes statistics of feature map activations across channels, and a "gradient-based" method that uses the gradient of the loss with respect to the input. (3) They provide extensive experimental validation showing that Attention Transfer consistently improves student network performance across a variety of datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and modern architectures (NIN, ResNet, Wide ResNet). Notably, the method is shown to be effective on the large-scale ImageNet dataset, a setting where prior hint-based methods have not demonstrated clear success.

### **Decision: Accept**

My recommendation is to **Accept** this paper. The two primary reasons for this decision are:
1.  The paper introduces a simple, well-motivated, and novel approach to knowledge distillation that is conceptually elegant and easy to implement (especially the activation-based variant).
2.  The claims are substantiated by a strong and comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the method's effectiveness and generality, highlighted by a significant performance improvement on the challenging ImageNet benchmark.

### **Supporting Arguments**

**1. Novelty and Strong Motivation:** The approach is well-motivated by the analogy to human attention, hypothesizing that a more capable network develops more refined attention patterns. This provides a compelling alternative to prior "hint" based methods like FitNets, which transfer entire, high-dimensional intermediate feature maps. Transferring a compressed 2D attention map is not only more efficient but, as the authors show, also more effective. This suggests that the spatial *focus* of a layer is more important transferable knowledge than the raw feature values themselves. The paper is well-situated within the literature, clearly distinguishing its contributions from prior work in knowledge distillation and attention visualization.

**2. Comprehensive and Rigorous Empirical Validation:** The authors have conducted a thorough empirical study that strongly supports their claims.
*   **Generality:** The method's effectiveness is demonstrated across multiple diverse datasets, from the small-scale CIFAR-10 to the large-scale ImageNet, as well as fine-grained (CUB) and scene recognition tasks. It is also shown to work with different modern architectures (WRNs, ResNets) and in different student-teacher pairings (same vs. different depth).
*   **Significant Performance Gains:** The reported improvements are consistently positive and often significant. The 1.1% top-1 accuracy gain for ResNet-18 on ImageNet is particularly impressive and serves as powerful evidence for the method's practical value. This result is made stronger by the authors' claim (which appears credible for the time) that other hint-based methods and even standard knowledge distillation have struggled to show benefits in similar cross-architecture ImageNet settings.
*   **Ablation and Comparison:** The paper includes valuable comparisons that strengthen its conclusions. The direct comparison in Table 1 showing that AT outperforms transferring full activations (F-ActT) is a crucial piece of evidence. The exploration of different attention mapping functions in Table 2 adds to the paper's completeness. The ability to combine AT with standard Knowledge Distillation (KD) for further gains is another practical advantage.

**3. Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is clearly written, the method is explained well, and the figures (especially Fig. 4, showing attention maps from networks of varying quality) provide excellent intuition. The authors' decision to release their code and models is highly commendable and greatly enhances the paper's contribution to the community by enabling verification and future work.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended as constructive feedback and are not central to my decision to accept the paper.
*   **Gradient-Based Attention Transfer:** This part of the work feels less developed than the activation-based approach. The experiments are confined to a simplified setting on CIFAR-10 without batch normalization, and the authors acknowledge the technical challenges. While interesting, the paper would be even stronger if this method were either demonstrated in a more modern setting or if the discussion on its limitations (e.g., interaction with batch norm during the second backpropagation) were expanded upon.
*   **Analysis of Attention Functions:** The paper explores several activation-based attention functions (`F_sum`, `F_psum`, `F_pmax`). While it shows `F_2sum` works well, a deeper analysis or intuition into *why* certain functions are better would be beneficial. For example, does increasing the exponent `p` in `F_psum` act as a sharpening mechanism, and is there an optimal degree of sharpness?
*   **Importance of Normalization:** The paper states that l2-normalizing the vectorized attention maps before computing the loss is "important for the success of the student training." This is a key implementation detail. Including a small ablation study in the appendix that quantifies the performance drop when normalization is omitted would make this point more concrete and impactful.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points:
1.  Regarding the gradient-based method: You mention the complexity of using double backpropagation with batch normalization. Could you elaborate on the specific challenge? Is it related to whether the BN statistics should be updated during the second pass, or something else? Have you considered a simpler approximation where the teacher's gradient `J_T` is pre-computed and treated as a fixed target, thus avoiding the second derivative term `∂J_S/∂W_S`?
2.  The ImageNet results are a key strength. You note that standard KD with a ResNet-34 teacher and ResNet-18 student "hurts convergence." Could you provide more detail on this negative result? Does the student's accuracy plateau at a lower level, or does the training become unstable? This finding is important context for motivating AT.
3.  For the activation-based method, transfer losses are placed at the end of each "group" of residual blocks. Have you experimented with placing these transfer losses at a finer granularity (e.g., after every block) when the student and teacher have identical architectures? I am curious whether more frequent guidance is helpful or if it becomes overly constraining for the student network.
