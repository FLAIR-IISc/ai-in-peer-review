This paper introduces Attention Transfer (AT), a novel and effective method for knowledge transfer between a powerful "teacher" network and a smaller "student" network. The core idea is to improve the student's performance by training it to mimic the spatial attention maps of the teacher. The authors make several key contributions: they formalize two distinct methods for defining attention maps (activation-based and gradient-based), demonstrate that transferring this distilled spatial information is more effective than transferring full activation tensors, and show that AT can be successfully combined with traditional knowledge distillation. The method's efficacy is convincingly demonstrated through a comprehensive suite of experiments across various datasets, including CIFAR, CUB, and ImageNet, and across different modern CNN architectures.

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are its well-motivated novelty and rigorous empirical validation. First, the concept of using attention maps as the medium for knowledge transfer is an elegant and intuitive contribution that sits logically between full feature-map matching and output distribution matching. The authors provide a compelling argument that this summarized spatial information is a more potent "hint" for the student network. Second, the claims are substantiated by extensive experiments that are well-designed and thorough. The consistent performance gains across multiple challenging benchmarks, particularly the successful application to ImageNet where other knowledge transfer methods have struggled, represent a significant achievement and a valuable contribution to the community.

To further improve the paper, the authors could consider the following points. The term "attention" here is a functional definition based on activation/gradient statistics. It would be helpful to briefly situate this definition in relation to the more common self-attention or sequence-to-sequence attention mechanisms, if only to clarify the distinction. The discussion around the gradient-based method's interaction with batch normalization is noted as an area for future work; any additional insight or preliminary thoughts on resolving this would be a valuable addition. Finally, while the experiments are strong, a brief analysis of the sensitivity to the hyperparameter β would enhance the paper's completeness.

I have a few questions for the authors to clarify my understanding:
1.  Could you provide more intuition on why transferring summarized attention maps is more beneficial than transferring full activation tensors? Does this mechanism act as a form of regularization, preventing the student from overfitting to the teacher's specific feature implementation?
2.  Your results on the fine-grained CUB dataset are particularly impressive. Do you believe AT is uniquely suited for tasks where localizing small, discriminative regions is critical for success?
