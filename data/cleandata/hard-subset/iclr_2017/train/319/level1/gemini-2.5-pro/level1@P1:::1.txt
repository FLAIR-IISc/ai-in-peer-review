### Review of "Paying More Attention to Attention: Improving the Performance of Convolutional Neural Networks via Attention Transfer"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel and intuitive method for knowledge transfer called Attention Transfer (AT). The core idea is to improve a smaller "student" network by training it to mimic the spatial attention maps of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The authors propose two ways to define these attention maps: (1) an activation-based method, which computes spatial maps by summing functions of the absolute values of activation tensors across the channel dimension, and (2) a gradient-based method, which uses the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image. Through a comprehensive set of experiments, the paper demonstrates that this technique provides significant and consistent performance gains for the student network across a variety of datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, ImageNet) and modern CNN architectures (NIN, ResNet, Wide ResNet). Notably, the authors show that transferring attention is more effective than transferring the full activation tensors (as in FitNets) and that the proposed method is the first of its kind to demonstrate a significant improvement on the large-scale ImageNet dataset.

**2. Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are:
1.  **Novelty and Efficacy:** The paper introduces a simple, well-motivated, and novel mechanism for knowledge transfer. The core idea of using compressed attention maps as the "hint" from teacher to student is elegant and proves to be highly effective.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The claims are backed by extensive experiments that are well-designed and convincing. The method's success across diverse datasets and architectures, particularly the significant gains on ImageNet, provides strong evidence of its general applicability and usefulness.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper has several key strengths that make it a solid contribution to the field of knowledge distillation and model compression.

*   **A Well-Motivated and Elegant Idea:** The motivation for attention transfer is grounded in the role of attention in both human and artificial perception. The hypothesis that a better-performing network has more "correct" spatial attention is intuitive, and the paper's visualizations (Fig. 4) provide qualitative support for this. Using these attention maps—a compact summary of the network's focus—as the medium for knowledge transfer is a clever departure from prior work that used either the final output distribution (Knowledge Distillation) or the full, high-dimensional intermediate feature maps (FitNets).

*   **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical work is a major strength. The authors evaluate their method on four different datasets, from small-scale (CIFAR-10) to large-scale (ImageNet), and across multiple competitive architectures. The results consistently show that AT improves student performance.

*   **Crucial Ablation Studies:** The paper includes several important comparisons that strengthen its claims. The experiment comparing AT to transferring full activations (F-ActT) is particularly compelling (Table 1). It shows that the compressed attention signal is not just sufficient, but actually *more* beneficial for transfer than the entire activation tensor, suggesting that AT provides a less noisy and more focused training signal. Furthermore, the paper demonstrates that AT can be combined with Knowledge Distillation (AT+KD) for even better performance, indicating that the two methods capture complementary forms of knowledge.

*   **Standout ImageNet Results:** Achieving a 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement for a ResNet-18 on ImageNet is a significant result. The authors' claim that this is the first successful application of hint-based knowledge transfer to ImageNet appears credible and highlights the practical impact of their method. This result alone makes the paper noteworthy.

While the paper is strong, the section on gradient-based attention transfer is less developed than the activation-based approach. The experiments are confined to a simplified setting on CIFAR-10 without batch normalization, and the authors acknowledge the technical challenge of implementing the required double backpropagation with modern architectures. While the results are interesting (especially the symmetry norm), this part feels preliminary. However, the strength and completeness of the activation-based attention transfer portion are more than sufficient to carry the paper.

**4. Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Why is AT superior to Full Activation Transfer?** The paper empirically demonstrates this but offers little discussion as to *why*. Is it because forcing the student to match the teacher's full representation is too restrictive, especially for networks with different capacities or architectures? Does the attention map act as a form of regularization, filtering out noise from the full activation tensor and providing a more distilled guidance signal? Adding a paragraph of discussion on this point would significantly improve the paper's insight.

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The weighting factor `β` is crucial for balancing the standard classification loss and the attention transfer loss. The paper describes a heuristic for setting it but does not discuss its sensitivity. How robust is the method to the choice of `β`? A brief analysis or comment on this would be helpful for practitioners seeking to apply the method.

*   **Computational Overhead:** Could you briefly quantify the computational cost of AT? For activation-based AT, the overhead seems minimal, as it only requires simple statistics on activations already computed during the forward pass. For gradient-based AT, the double backpropagation is known to be expensive. Clarifying this would be useful.

*   **Details on ImageNet KD Failure:** You mention that Knowledge Distillation (KD) hurt convergence on the ImageNet experiments. This is a strong claim against a widely-used method. Could you provide more details? Were the hyperparameters (temperature, `α`) tuned specifically for ImageNet, or were the CIFAR settings simply reused? A more detailed account of the attempts to get KD to work would strengthen the claim about AT's unique success on this benchmark.

*   **Gradient-based AT with Batch Normalization:** You mention that it is unclear how batch normalization should behave during the second backpropagation step. Do you have any hypotheses on this? For instance, should the batch statistics be frozen from the first forward pass, or should they be re-calculated? Discussing potential solutions, even if not implemented, would add value to this section.
