- Proposes "Attention Transfer" (AT), training a student network to mimic teacher spatial attention maps for knowledge distillation.  
- Introduces two attention map methods: activation-based (channel summary) and gradient-based (loss gradient w.r.t. input).  
- Demonstrates AT improves student performance across datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN).  
- Reports notable ImageNet result: +1.1% top-1 accuracy for ResNet-18 using AT, where prior hint methods struggled.  
- Recommendation: Accept — method is simple, well-motivated, and easy to implement (especially activation-based).  
- Novelty: transferring compressed 2D attention maps is argued to be more efficient and effective than full intermediate feature hints.  
- Empirical rigor: shows generality across tasks, consistent and often significant gains, and successful combinations with standard KD.  
- Ablations and comparisons: AT outperforms full-activation transfer and explores different attention mapping functions.  
- Clarity and reproducibility: paper is well-written, illustrative figures aid intuition, and code/models are released.  
- Weakness: gradient-based AT is underdeveloped, tested only in simplified CIFAR-10 settings and has technical BN challenges.  
- Weakness: needs deeper analysis on why certain activation-based attention functions (e.g., F_2sum) work better.  
- Weakness: importance of l2-normalizing attention maps is asserted but lacks a small ablation quantifying its effect.  
- Question: clarify double-backpropagation challenge with batch normalization in gradient-based AT and possible approximations (pre-computing J_T).  
- Question: detail on why standard KD with ResNet-34→ResNet-18 "hurts convergence" — plateauing accuracy or instability?  
- Question: have authors tried placing activation-based transfer losses more granularly (e.g., after every block) when architectures match?
