### Review of "Paying More Attention to Attention: Improving the Performance of Convolutional Neural Networks via Attention Transfer"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Attention Transfer (AT), a novel method for knowledge transfer from a high-performing "teacher" network to a smaller "student" network. The central idea is to compel the student network to mimic the spatial attention patterns of the teacher. The authors define "attention" as a 2D spatial map derived from the activations of a convolutional layer. Two distinct methods for generating these attention maps are proposed:

1.  **Activation-based AT:** Attention maps are generated by computing summary statistics (e.g., sum of absolute values powered by p) across the channel dimension of a layer's activation tensor. This method is computationally inexpensive.
2.  **Gradient-based AT:** Attention is defined as the gradient of the loss function with respect to the input image, creating an input sensitivity map. This method is more computationally intensive as it requires double backpropagation.

The paper's primary contributions are:
*   The conceptualization of transferring knowledge via spatial attention maps, providing a new form of "hint" for network training.
*   The formulation of both activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer mechanisms.
*   An extensive set of experiments across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, ImageNet) and modern architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN).
*   Empirical evidence showing that AT provides consistent performance improvements, can be combined with knowledge distillation, and is more effective than transferring full, uncompressed activation tensors (a method similar to FitNets). Notably, the authors demonstrate a significant performance gain on ImageNet, a task where previous knowledge transfer methods have reportedly struggled.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **High Novelty and Sound Methodology:** The paper presents a simple, intuitive, yet highly novel approach to knowledge transfer. Defining the transferred knowledge as a summary statistic of spatial activations (i.e., an "attention map") is a clever and effective idea that is well-differentiated from prior work like Knowledge Distillation (transferring output distributions) and FitNets (transferring full intermediate representations). The proposed methods are clearly explained and technically sound.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Support:** The claims are substantiated by a thorough and convincing set of experiments. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated consistently across diverse datasets, from the small-scale CIFAR-10 to the large-scale ImageNet, and on state-of-the-art architectures. The successful application to ImageNet is a particularly strong result, and the direct comparison showing superiority over full-activation transfer provides compelling evidence for the value of the proposed approach.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strength lies in its elegant core idea and the robust experimental validation that supports it.

The concept of Attention Transfer is well-motivated and provides a new perspective on what constitutes useful "knowledge" for transfer. Instead of forcing the student to replicate the teacher's exact high-dimensional feature maps, AT guides the student to focus on the same salient spatial regions. The experiments suggest this compressed form of guidance is more beneficial. The comparison in Table 1 (WRN-16-2 -> WRN-16-1) shows AT achieving a 7.93% error rate, whereas a FitNets-style full-activation transfer (F-ActT) only reaches 8.44%, barely improving over the 8.77% baseline. This is a crucial result that validates the central hypothesis of the paper.

The breadth of the experimental evaluation is a major strength. The authors show positive results on:
*   **Different Architectures:** NIN, ResNets, and Wide ResNets.
*   **Different Datasets:** CIFAR-10, CUB-200 (fine-grained), MIT Scenes, and ImageNet.
*   **Different Scenarios:** Training from scratch and fine-tuning pre-trained models.

The ImageNet experiment (ResNet-34 -> ResNet-18) is particularly noteworthy. Achieving a 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement on such a challenging benchmark is a significant result. The authors' claim that this is the first successful application of a knowledge transfer method to ImageNet seems plausible given the literature and further solidifies the contribution's importance.

The paper also explores a second, more complex formulation (gradient-based AT). While the experiments for this method are more preliminary due to computational challenges, its inclusion demonstrates the generality of the "attention transfer" concept beyond simple activation statistics. The exploration of a self-regularization technique based on gradient symmetry is an interesting additional contribution.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended for improvement and do not detract from the decision to accept.

*   **Clarification on "Attention":** The term "attention" is used here to describe post-hoc saliency maps derived from activations or gradients. This differs from the explicit, learnable attention mechanisms common in NLP and computer vision (e.g., Bahdanau et al., 2014; Xu et al., 2015). A brief paragraph clarifying this distinction could prevent confusion and better position the work within the broader context of attention research.
*   **Ablation on Normalization:** The paper states that l2-normalizing the attention maps before calculating the transfer loss is "important for the success of the student training." This is a strong claim that would be more convincing if supported by a small ablation study showing the performance with and without this normalization step.
*   **Gradient-based AT with Batch Normalization:** The authors correctly identify the ambiguity of how Batch Normalization should behave during the second backpropagation step in gradient-based AT. While a full solution is beyond the scope of this work, a brief discussion of potential approaches or hypotheses would be a valuable addition for future research in this direction.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper reports results using `F_sum^2` for most experiments. While Table 2 explores different mapping functions, a brief discussion on the sensitivity to the hyperparameter `p` in `F_sum^p` and the loss weight `β` would strengthen the practical guidance for applying the method.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the empirical effect of not normalizing the attention maps before computing the transfer loss? How significantly does performance degrade, and do you have an intuition as to why normalization is so crucial?
2.  Regarding the ImageNet experiment, you mention adding losses only at the last two residual groups. Do you have any insight into whether adding AT losses at earlier layers would provide further gains, or if there is a point of diminishing returns?
3.  The claim that Knowledge Distillation (KD) struggles when the teacher and student have different architectures is an important finding. Could you provide more detail on the failure modes you observed when attempting to apply KD on CIFAR and ImageNet in these heterogeneous settings?
4.  For the gradient-based method, have you considered using approximations to the second-order derivatives (e.g., finite differences or approximations to the Hessian-vector product) to potentially make the method more scalable?
