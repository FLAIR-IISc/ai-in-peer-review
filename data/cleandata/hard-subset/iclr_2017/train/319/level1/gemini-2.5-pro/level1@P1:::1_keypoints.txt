- Introduces Attention Transfer (AT) to train a student network to mimic teacher spatial attention maps.  
- Defines two attention map types: activation-based (channel-wise aggregated activations) and gradient-based (loss gradient w.r.t. input).  
- Demonstrates consistent student performance gains across datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, Wide ResNet).  
- Shows AT outperforms transferring full activation tensors (FitNets) and can be combined with Knowledge Distillation (AT+KD) for additional improvement.  
- Reports a significant 1.1% top-1 accuracy boost for ResNet-18 on ImageNet, claimed as the first successful hint-based transfer at that scale.  
- Reviewer recommends accept based on the method’s novelty, simplicity, and empirical effectiveness.  
- Praises thorough experimental validation and well-designed evaluations across scales and architectures.  
- Ablation studies (e.g., comparing AT vs. full-activation transfer) strongly support the claim that compressed attention is a superior training signal.  
- Activation-based AT is well-motivated and supported by qualitative visualizations showing meaningful attention maps.  
- Gradient-based AT is underdeveloped and tested only in a simplified CIFAR-10 setting without batch normalization; feels preliminary.  
- Requests more discussion on why AT is superior to full-activation transfer (e.g., regularization or noise filtering).  
- Asks for analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity, especially the weighting factor β between losses.  
- Requests quantification of computational overhead (activation-based likely cheap; gradient-based requires expensive double backprop).  
- Requests more details on ImageNet experiments where KD hurt convergence and whether KD hyperparameters were tuned.  
- Asks for discussion/hypotheses on handling batch normalization during gradient-based AT’s second backpropagation.
