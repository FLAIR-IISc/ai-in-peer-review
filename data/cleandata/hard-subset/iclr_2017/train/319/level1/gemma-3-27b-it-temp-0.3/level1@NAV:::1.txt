## Review of "Attention Transfer"

This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks by transferring attention maps from a powerful teacher network to a student network. The authors define attention using both activation-based and gradient-based methods and demonstrate consistent performance improvements across various datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and network architectures (NIN, ResNet). The work is well-motivated by the observation of attention mechanisms in both human and artificial vision systems, and builds upon existing research in knowledge distillation and attention visualization. The availability of code and models is a significant positive aspect.

**Reject** – While the idea is interesting and the results show some improvement, the paper lacks sufficient depth in analysis and justification for certain design choices, and the ImageNet results, while promising, aren’t fully convincing given the limited hyperparameter tuning.

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of rigorous ablation studies and justification for specific implementation details. For example, the choice of the l2 norm for attention map comparison, the specific form of the loss function (Eq. 2), and the scaling factor β are not thoroughly explored or justified. The claim of being the "first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" feels overstated given the limited experimentation reported. The ImageNet results are presented with a caveat of limited hyperparameter tuning, which weakens the claim of significant improvement. Furthermore, the discussion of why KD failed on ImageNet is superficial. 

The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of the attention maps themselves. Visualizing and comparing the attention maps of the teacher and student networks would provide stronger evidence for the effectiveness of the transfer process. Additionally, exploring different normalization techniques for attention maps beyond l2 normalization could be valuable. The impact of different layers for attention transfer (e.g., shallow vs. deep layers) should be investigated.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   Could the authors elaborate on why they chose to use a squared sum (F2sum) attention mapping function? What are the theoretical benefits of this choice compared to other options?
*   The paper mentions that gradient-based attention transfer requires a second backpropagation step. What is the computational overhead of this step, and how does it scale with network size?
*   For the ImageNet experiments, what specific hyperparameters were held constant from the CIFAR experiments? A more detailed description of the experimental setup would be helpful.
*   The authors mention that the symmetry norm improves performance in the gradient-based attention transfer experiments. Can they provide a more intuitive explanation of why this is the case?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of the teacher network? Would a different teacher network architecture or pre-training dataset affect the performance of the student network?
