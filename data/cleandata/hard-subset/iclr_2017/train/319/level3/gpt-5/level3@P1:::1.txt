The paper introduces Attention Transfer (AT), a new knowledge transfer mechanism that encourages a student CNN to mimic a teacher’s spatial attention. Attention is defined in two complementary ways: (i) activation-based attention maps that aggregate channel activations into spatial maps via sum or max statistics (with p-exponents), and (ii) gradient-based attention that treats input gradients as sensitivity maps. The core idea is to add losses that align student and teacher attention at multiple layers (with l2-normalized maps), optionally combining AT with standard knowledge distillation (KD). The paper reports consistent improvements across CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, shows that activation-based AT improves convergence and final accuracy, and that it is more effective than transferring full activations. Gradient-based AT is also explored via double backprop, with an additional flip-invariance regularizer. The approach is simple, architecture-agnostic, and code is provided.

Decision: Reject
- The empirical evidence, while promising, is not yet strong enough: improvements over KD are marginal or inconsistent, and the large-scale ImageNet results are too limited to substantiate the central claims.
- Gradient-based AT is intriguing but presented with insufficient clarity and evaluation, especially regarding practicality and interactions with batch normalization.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and motivation: Defining attention maps for CNNs and transferring them is a clear, interesting idea, well-motivated by human and machine attention literature, and well-positioned relative to KD/FitNets and attention visualization work (CAM/Grad-CAM). The loss design (normalization, layerwise transfer) and the “sum vs. max vs. p” discussion are thoughtful.
- Activation-based AT vs full activation transfer: The paper convincingly shows AT outperforms full-activation regression on CIFAR, supporting the claim that spatial attention is a compact and effective carrier of “where to look” knowledge.
- AT vs KD: On CIFAR, AT and KD can be combined, but AT alone often yields gains comparable to KD; improvements over KD alone appear small in several settings. On MIT Scenes, AT ~ KD; on CUB, AT > KD, but the gains are not consistently quantified against strong, tuned KD baselines (e.g., temperature/alpha sweeps, calibration of logits). The paper’s own observation that KD struggles on ImageNet with the chosen pair underlines the need for a more systematic comparison.
- ImageNet (Section 4.2.2): The single teacher–student pair with two AT losses shows a 1.1% top-1 improvement, which is promising, but the exploration is shallow: no sensitivity analysis (number/placement of AT losses, p, normalization), no multiple runs or error bars, no head-to-head tuned KD, and no other pairs/architectures. Given the paper’s central claim and the importance of large-scale validation, this section needs to be more substantial.
- Gradient-based AT: The method requires double backprop and raises unresolved questions around batch normalization and computational cost. The CIFAR-only experiments, limited architecture, and absence of comparisons under modern training setups make it hard to assess its practical value.
- Rigor and reporting: Many results are single runs or medians of few runs without variability estimates. Hyperparameter choices (β per layer, decay schedules) appear task-specific without ablation. Compute/memory overheads of AT (and especially gradient-based AT) are not quantified.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Strengthen ImageNet validation: evaluate multiple teacher–student pairs (e.g., ResNet-34→18, 50→18/34, 101→50), place AT losses in all groups, run multiple seeds with confidence intervals, and provide thorough comparisons to tuned KD and FitNets-style hints. Report training curves and final accuracies for all.
- Ablate key design choices: normalization (l1 vs l2), p and mapping function (Fsum vs Fpsum vs Fpmax), number/placement of layers for AT, β scheduling, and the interaction with data augmentation.
- Quantify cost: report wall-clock, memory, and extra FLOPs for teacher forward and AT losses; for gradient-based AT, report overhead of double backprop and any stabilization tricks.
- Correlate attention alignment with accuracy: track attention similarity metrics vs. performance during training to support the mechanism’s role.
- Gradient-based AT: clarify behavior with batch normalization (frozen vs train-time stats during second backprop), and test on a modern BN-enabled architecture with standard augmentations. The flip-invariance regularizer is interesting—evaluate it beyond CIFAR and position it relative to group-equivariant or data-augmentation consistency regularizers.
- Broaden tasks: given the spatial nature of attention, consider preliminary experiments on object detection or weakly supervised localization where attention alignment may matter more.

Questions for the authors
- KD comparison: What are the best-tuned KD baselines per setting (temperature, alpha, label-smoothing)? On CIFAR/CUB/Scenes, how much does AT outperform a KD baseline after hyperparameter sweeps? Can you report mean±std over multiple seeds?
- ImageNet: Can you extend Section 4.2.2 with additional teacher–student pairs, more AT placements, and multiple runs? How does AT compare with tuned KD and FitNets on ImageNet when all methods are given equal tuning budgets?
- Loss design: How is β chosen per layer and over training? Did you try adaptive or uncertainty-weighted multi-task weighting? How sensitive is performance to normalization choice (l1 vs l2) and to p?
- Attention choices: Why do sum-based maps outperform max-based ones empirically? Any insights on datasets/architectures where max-based might be better?
- Practicality: What is the compute/memory overhead of AT during training (and none at inference)? For gradient-based AT, what is the actual overhead and how feasible is it for large-scale training?
- BN and double backprop: How exactly do you handle batch norm in the second backprop pass? Did you try freezing BN stats/affines vs updating them, and what are the effects?
- Generalization: Do the learned attention patterns transfer across domains better than KD (e.g., in few-shot or fine-tuning setups)? Any negative transfer cases?

I would be inclined to raise my score if the authors substantially expand and solidify the ImageNet experiments (Section 4.2.2), include stronger KD baselines with tuning, and provide clearer evidence that AT consistently outperforms or complements KD at scale.
