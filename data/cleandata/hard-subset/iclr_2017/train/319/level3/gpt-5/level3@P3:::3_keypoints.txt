- Introduces Attention Transfer (AT): a loss encouraging a student CNN to match teacher spatial attention maps alongside standard classification loss and optional KD.
- Defines activation-based attentions (channelwise |A|^p reductions; sum/max variants) and a gradient-based attention (∂Loss/∂input) with double-backprop and flip-invariance regularizer.
- Empirically, AT consistently improves student accuracy on CIFAR-10/100, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet; activation-based AT outperforms full activation regression and combines well with KD on small datasets.
- Activation-based AT improves ResNet-18 using ResNet-34 on ImageNet.
- Method is easy to implement, architecture-agnostic, and adds low computational overhead for activation-based AT.
- Decision: Weak Accept.
- Key reason 1: Idea is well-motivated, clearly presented, and broadly applicable.
- Key reason 2: Results show consistent, non-trivial gains across datasets and architectures, making the simple loss practically useful.
- Problem addressed: transferring spatial attention from a stronger teacher to a weaker student to improve student performance without increasing capacity.
- Literature placement: connects attention visualization (gradients, CAM/Grad-CAM) with knowledge transfer (KD, FitNets); offers a compact, semantically aligned target for transfer.
- Positive results details: AT beats FitNet-style activation regression; sum-based attentions and multi-stage placement help; gradient-based AT helps in controlled CIFAR experiments.
- Limitation a: Students typically still trail teachers; surpassing the teacher is rare/not shown.
- Limitation b: The claim that better models have higher object–attention correlation is only visually supported and lacks quantitative evidence.
- Limitation c: Hyperparameter selection (especially β and p) is under-specified and sensitivity is unclear.
- Limitation d: Guidance is limited on when to prefer activation- vs gradient-based AT; experiments don’t delineate regimes of advantage.
- Limitation e: Gradient-based AT demonstrated only on CIFAR/NIN without BN; scalability to modern BN-heavy networks is unresolved.
- Limitation f: Compute/latency benefits are not quantified; practical speed-ups and inference implications are unreported.
- Limitation g: Convergence comparisons omit teacher curves, reducing contextual clarity.
- Additional feedback: Quantify attention–object correlation using pointing game, IoU with boxes/masks, or saliency overlaps across layers/models.
- Additional feedback: Provide hyperparameter ablations and a principled recipe for β, p, normalization choice, number/placement of AT losses, and interaction with KD.
- Additional feedback: Systematically compare activation- vs gradient-based AT across tasks (fine-grained vs generic), depths, and data sizes; discuss cost, stability, interpretability, BN dependence.
- Additional feedback: Report training overhead and inference speed/throughput/energy with and without AT and evaluate effect on accuracy vs FLOPs/latency Pareto frontier.
- Additional feedback: Evaluate same-architecture teacher–student settings (e.g., teacher better optimized or ensembled) to test AT’s benefit.
- Additional feedback: Include stronger baselines (tuned FitNets on ImageNet, KD variants) and richer contextual comparisons.
- Additional feedback: Add teacher training/validation curves to convergence figures and more side-by-side attention visualizations with quantitative similarity metrics.
- Additional feedback: Clarify or demonstrate a scalable implementation of gradient-based AT with BN or discuss workarounds.
- Question: How should practitioners choose β across datasets and layers, and how sensitive is final accuracy to β and normalization choice?
- Question: How do p and reduction (sum vs max) affect performance across tasks; any guidance beyond the small CIFAR table?
- Question: Can AT help when teacher and student share architecture but teacher is better optimized (longer training/better regularization); if so, by how much?
- Question: What are the training and inference overheads (wall-clock, memory) for activation- and gradient-based AT, and does AT change inference-time cost?
- Question: For gradient-based AT, how do you handle BN in the second backprop pass; is a recipe for modern networks available?
- Question: On ImageNet, why does KD hurt while AT helps; were KD temperature/α sweeps and AT+KD combinations explored at scale?
- Question: Can you provide quantitative evidence for the attention–object correlation claim (pointing game or CAM overlap metrics) across accuracy spectrum?
- Question: How many AT insertion points are needed for most gains, and which stages are most beneficial on large-scale datasets?
