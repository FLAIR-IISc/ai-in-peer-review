Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes attention transfer (AT) as a simple, general mechanism for knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student by aligning their spatial attention maps in intermediate layers, in addition to the standard classification loss (and optionally KD). Attention maps are defined either activation-based (channel reductions of |A|^p via sum or max) or gradient-based (input sensitivity ∂L/∂x).
- The authors motivate attention as an interpretable, spatially structured signal that better captures “where the network looks” than full activations. They introduce several attention mappings, justify L2-normalization before comparison, and describe layer placements for AT across architectures of same or different depth.
- Empirically, AT yields consistent improvements over the student baseline across CIFAR-10, transfer learning on CUB and MIT Scenes, and ImageNet (ResNet-18 student with ResNet-34 teacher). Activation-based AT outperforms regressing full activations, and can be combined with KD. Gradient-based AT is feasible via double backprop and shows gains on CIFAR in a controlled setting. Code and models are released.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) The idea is simple, well-motivated, and empirically effective across datasets and architectures, including a meaningful ImageNet improvement with minimal overhead at inference. 2) The formulation bridges attention visualization and knowledge distillation in a principled way and outperforms full-activation regression.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets a clear question—how to transfer “where to look” from a stronger model to a weaker one. The connection to both attention visualization and distillation is timely and well-situated in the literature (FitNets, KD, CAM/Grad-CAM, guided backprop, residual/WRN).
- Method: The activation-based attention definitions (sum or max of |A|^p) are intuitive, architecture-agnostic, and easy to compute; normalization is a sensible design choice. The loss placement strategy is practical for both same- and different-depth pairs. Gradient-based AT leverages double backprop; the symmetry regularizer is a nice addition.
- Empirical support: 
  - CIFAR: Consistent gains across WRN configurations; sum-based mappings outperform max; AT > full activations; AT+KD often best. Ablations (loss placement per group) are informative.
  - Gradient-based AT: Demonstrates feasibility and improvement versus baseline and double backprop on CIFAR.
  - Transfer learning (CUB/Scenes): AT narrows the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34; particularly strong on fine-grained CUB where spatial attention plausibly matters more.
  - ImageNet: +1.1% top-1 for ResNet-18 with only two AT losses and no hyperparameter tuning, which is compelling at scale. Inference-time cost is unchanged.
- Rigor and limitations: While the experiments substantiate the core claim that AT improves students, some aspects are underexplored: quantitative validation of the “better models have better attention” hypothesis is mainly visual; sensitivity to β and mapping choices lacks systematic study; gradient-based AT is demonstrated in a restricted setting. Still, the breadth of datasets and the consistent improvements support acceptance.

Additional feedback for improvement (not all critical to the decision)
- Quantify attention quality: Provide a metric (e.g., correlation/IoU with localization cues, bounding boxes, or CAM/Grad-CAM) to support the claim that higher-accuracy models have more object-aligned attention. Include statistics beyond visuals (Fig. 4).
- Hyperparameter guidance: Analyze sensitivity to β, normalization choice (L1 vs L2), and the number/placement of AT losses. Report a sweep and a recommended recipe.
- When to use which attention: Offer clearer guidance on the choice of mapping (sum vs max, p values) and on activation- vs gradient-based AT, possibly stratified by dataset/architecture.
- Training dynamics and compute: Quantify convergence speedups and training-time overhead (especially for gradient-based AT with double backprop); report wall-clock and FLOPs. Clarify that inference cost is unchanged.
- Comparisons and variance: Report run-to-run variance (mean/std over multiple seeds) especially on CIFAR; include teacher curves in Fig. 7b for context. Strengthen comparisons to KD under matched hyperparameter tuning for ImageNet, and to FitNets/full-activation regression where applicable.
- Batch normalization details for gradient-based AT: Clarify how BN statistics/affine params are handled during the second backward pass; discuss stability.
- Scope: Consider additional tasks where spatial attention is critical (detection/weakly supervised localization) to showcase broader applicability.

Questions for the authors
1) β sensitivity and scheduling: How sensitive are results to β across datasets and layers? Did you try adaptive schedules or layer-wise scaling beyond “103 divided by elements and batch size”?
2) Mapping choice: Beyond the small CIFAR ablation, do trends (sum > max, moderate p) hold on ImageNet and transfer learning setups? Is there a principled rationale for preferring Fsum vs F^2sum?
3) Layer placement: How many AT losses are needed for diminishing returns? On ImageNet, what is the effect of adding AT to all four groups?
4) KD synergy: When AT and KD are combined, how do you set temperatures/α? Are the methods complementary or redundant on large-scale ImageNet when architectures differ?
5) Gradient-based AT: Can you detail the implementation of the second backprop with BN (frozen vs updating stats), and its computational overhead? Any stability issues? Have you tried gradient-based AT on modern BN-equipped models?
6) Attention quality: Can you quantify the correlation between attention maps and object regions (e.g., using segmentation masks or objectness) and how AT changes this correlation in the student?
7) Teacher choice: How does AT perform with weaker teachers or mismatched architectures (e.g., Inception teacher, ResNet student)? Does a too-strong teacher ever hinder learning?
8) Data efficiency: Does AT help in low-data regimes (e.g., few-shot finetuning) more than KD?
9) Same-capacity pairs: If student and teacher share the same architecture/capacity, does AT still yield gains or faster convergence?
10) Compute trade-offs: For practitioners, can you report end-to-end training time changes and any memory implications for AT (activation-based and gradient-based)?

Overall, this is a well-written and practical contribution that advances knowledge transfer via an interpretable mechanism. The method’s simplicity, empirical gains, and scalability justify acceptance.
