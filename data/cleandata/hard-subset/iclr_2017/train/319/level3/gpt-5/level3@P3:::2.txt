1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a simple, effective variant of knowledge distillation for convolutional neural networks that transfers “attention” by matching spatial statistics of intermediate feature activations between a teacher and a student. Concretely, the method computes attention maps by summing (or taking lp-norms/max) across channels at a layer, normalizes these maps, and penalizes discrepancies between teacher and student at one or more layers. The approach is lightweight, architecture-agnostic within CNNs, and can be combined with KD. Empirically, activation-based attention transfer (AT) consistently improves student accuracy across CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, and typically outperforms FitNet-style full-activation regression. The paper also explores a gradient-based “attention transfer” via double backpropagation, though only on CIFAR/NIN due to differentiation complexity. The paper is generally well-written, code is released, and results are compelling.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical gains with a simple, low-overhead mechanism that improves upon FitNet-style hints and scales to ImageNet.
- Clear practical value: easy to implement in existing CNN training pipelines, with consistent improvements across datasets and architectures.

3. Supporting arguments
Answers to key questions:
- What is the problem? Transferring useful supervisory signals from a teacher to a student by aligning where the teacher focuses spatially within convolutional feature maps, rather than regressing full activations or logits alone.
- Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. It builds on KD and FitNets, and on visualization work (CAM/Grad-CAM, guided backprop) to argue that spatial focus contains distilled knowledge. The contrast to full-activation regression and to KD is appropriate and timely.
- Do the results support the claims? Largely yes for activation-based AT: consistent improvements across multiple datasets, ablations over attention definitions (sum vs max, p), placement of losses, and combination with KD. The ImageNet result (ResNet-18 improved by ~1.1% top-1) is particularly persuasive. However, the gradient-based AT section is underdeveloped: limited to a narrow setup, not compared/combined with activation-based AT in larger settings, and lacks conclusive evidence of unique benefit beyond existing regularizers.

Strengths
- Method: Intuitive, simple loss on normalized spatial summaries across channels; robust to channel permutation; little computational overhead.
- Empirics: Broad coverage (CIFAR, fine-grained CUB, Scenes, ImageNet). AT > FitNet-style full-activation regression; sum-based maps outperform max-based maps; combining AT with KD often helps on CIFAR.
- Clarity/Presentation: Generally clear writing and practical guidance (e.g., normalization is important, β scheduling).

Weaknesses/limitations
- Scope: Inherently tailored to CNNs with spatial feature maps; not directly applicable to non-convolutional architectures.
- Terminology: “Attention” is potentially misleading—these are channel-summed activation energies (or lp-norms), not learned attention mechanisms or class-discriminative attention maps.
- Gradient-based AT: Conceptually interesting but out-of-place; not integrated with main method, no direct comparison on larger datasets, and limited by BN/second-derivative complications.
- Notation/clarity: Eq. (2) and norm choices are unclear; the use of “2-norm” on spatial maps could be misread as spectral norm rather than Euclidean/Frobenius; normalization details should be made unambiguous.
- Organization: The gradient-based section interrupts the otherwise coherent narrative around activation-based AT.

4. Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all affect the accept decision)
- Clarify Eq. (2) rigorously: since Q = vec(F(A)), ‖Q‖2 is the Euclidean norm; if F(A) is treated as H×W, please specify Frobenius norm to avoid confusion with the induced 2-norm (largest singular value). Explicitly state the normalization choice and its motivation.
- Terminology: Consider renaming to “activation-map energy transfer” or similar, and clearly distinguish from attention mechanisms/visualizations (CAM/Grad-CAM).
- Gradient-based AT: Either (a) integrate it meaningfully—compare against activation-based AT on at least one larger benchmark, or (b) move to appendix and frame as exploratory. Discuss batch-norm handling in double backprop more concretely.
- Ablations: Add systematic studies on (i) which layers to align; (ii) p in lp-sum; (iii) sensitivity to normalization (l1 vs l2); (iv) β scheduling and per-layer weighting; and (v) teacher-student depth/architecture mismatch.
- Baselines: Strengthen FitNet-style baselines (ensure best-practice adapters/normalization) and report variance over multiple seeds. Clarify KD hyperparameter tuning on each dataset, especially on ImageNet where KD underperformed.
- Overheads: Report training/inference cost and memory overhead of AT and of gradient-based AT.
- Extensions: Discuss how to adapt the idea to tasks where spatial structure is crucial (detection/segmentation) and to non-CNN models (e.g., token-wise channel pooling for sequences).

Questions for the authors
- Norm ambiguity: In Eq. (2), do you use Euclidean norm on vec(F(A)) or Frobenius on H×W maps? Please confirm you do not use the induced 2-norm (spectral norm). Did you try l1 normalization or per-location normalization?
- Choice of F: Beyond sum of squares, did you explore channel-wise standardization before summation (e.g., per-channel l2/l1 normalization) to reduce scale mismatch? Any results with softmaxed spatial maps?
- Loss placement: What is the most effective subset of layers for AT on ImageNet? Do more losses across all stages further improve ResNet-18?
- β scheduling: How sensitive are results to β and its decay? Any guidance for scaling β across layers with different spatial sizes?
- KD + AT interactions: On CIFAR, combining helps; on ImageNet, KD struggled. Did you try temperature/α sweeps for KD when combined with AT? Any evidence that AT makes KD redundant at scale?
- Gradient-based AT: Why not combine with activation-based AT? Are there cases where gradient-based AT distinctly outperforms activation-based AT under matched training (with BN)?
- Cross-architecture transfer: How robust is AT when teacher and student differ substantially (e.g., ResNet → NIN/Inception)? Any results beyond WRN/ResNet within-CNN families?
- Practicality: What is the wall-clock overhead of AT and gradient-based AT relative to baseline? Any effect on calibration or robustness (e.g., input perturbations)?
