Summary: The paper proposes a simple, effective knowledge-transfer mechanism for CNNs that distills “attention” by matching spatial maps formed from summed activation statistics across channels (e.g., sum of absolute values or lp-sums), normalized and compared across corresponding layers of teacher and student. Across CIFAR-10, CUB, MIT Indoor Scenes, and ImageNet, the approach consistently improves student accuracy and typically outperforms FitNet-style full-activation matching; it is complementary to standard KD. The method is easy to implement and incurs minimal overhead, and the paper is generally well written with clear empirical takeaways.

Decision: Weak Accept. The core idea—distilling spatial activation statistics—is well-motivated, simple, and empirically strong across multiple datasets, including ImageNet, where many distillation baselines struggle.

Supporting arguments:
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets how to transfer spatial focus from a stronger CNN to a weaker one, arguing that spatial energy of feature maps captures discriminative regions better than full activation regression. This is plausibly motivated and situated relative to FitNets and KD.
- Rigor and evidence: The paper reports consistent gains over competitive baselines, ablates the choice of statistic (p-sum vs max), positions losses at multiple depths, and demonstrates scalability to ImageNet. Results suggest superiority over FitNet-style activation matching and at least additivity with KD on some settings.
- Limitations: The approach is inherently tied to convolutional feature maps and is not evaluated beyond CNNs. The “attention” nomenclature is potentially misleading—these are summed activation maps, not learned attention mechanisms. The gradient-based section feels orthogonal to the main contribution and is not integrated or thoroughly compared.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper):
- Clarify Eq. (2) notation: when normalizing Q ∈ R^{HW}, confirm it is the vector l2 (or Frobenius) norm, not the induced spectral norm. State this explicitly and unify norm notation throughout.
- Rename or precisely define “attention” to avoid conflation with attention mechanisms (e.g., “spatial activation energy”).
- Either integrate the gradient-based transfer with the main method (comparisons/combination) or move it to an appendix to improve focus.
- Report training/inference overheads (time/memory) and sensitivity to p, β, layer choices, and normalization.
- Provide stronger FitNet/KD baselines (tuning, seeds) and report variance/significance.

Questions for authors:
1) In Eq. (2), is ‖Q‖2 the vector l2 norm after flattening or the matrix spectral norm? Please clarify and justify.
2) What is the wall-clock and memory overhead of AT (per batch, with/without teacher forward pass)?
3) How sensitive are results to p, β, and the set of layers used? Any guidance for practitioners?
4) Can AT be combined with gradient-based AT to further improve results? If not, why?
5) How does AT fare with non-residual CNNs and with transformers or MLP-Mixers (via token-wise maps)?
6) Are improvements robust across multiple seeds on larger datasets (CUB/Scenes/ImageNet)?
7) How does AT compare to CAM/Grad-CAM-derived maps as the distilled target?
