- Paper introduces Attention Transfer (AT): a mechanism for student CNNs to mimic teacher spatial attention.  
- Defines attention as activation-based maps (sum/max/p-exponents) and gradient-based maps (input gradients).  
- Core method: add losses that align l2-normalized student and teacher attention at multiple layers, optionally with KD.  
- Reports consistent improvements on CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and a limited ImageNet experiment; code released.  
- Decision: Reject.  

- Main reason: empirical evidence is promising but not strong enough; improvements over KD are marginal or inconsistent.  
- ImageNet results are too limited to substantiate central claims (single pair, no sensitivity analysis, no error bars).  
- Gradient-based AT is intriguing but lacks clarity, evaluation, and raises practical issues (double backprop, BN interactions, cost).  
- Paper’s novelty and motivation are solid and well-positioned relative to KD, FitNets, and attention visualization prior work.  
- Activation-based AT outperforms full-activation regression on CIFAR, supporting attention as a compact “where to look” signal.  
- AT vs KD: AT often matches or complements KD on smaller datasets, but gains over well-tuned KD are not consistently shown.  
- ImageNet experiment: 1.1% top-1 gain is promising but exploratory setup lacks multiple runs, ablations, and tuned KD comparisons.  
- Gradient-based AT experiments are CIFAR-only, limited architectures, and omit comparison under modern training setups.  
- Reporting rigor is weak: many single runs, few variance estimates, task-specific hyperparameters without ablation, and no compute/memory overhead quantification.  

- Suggested improvements: expand ImageNet validation (multiple teacher–student pairs, placements, seeds, tuned KD/ FitNets baselines).  
- Ablate key design choices: normalization type, p and mapping functions, number/placement of layers, β scheduling, and data-augmentation interaction.  
- Quantify training cost: wall-clock, memory, extra FLOPs for AT and double backprop overhead for gradient-based AT.  
- Correlate attention alignment metrics with accuracy during training to support the proposed mechanism.  
- Clarify gradient-based AT behavior with batch norm (freeze vs update stats) and test on BN-enabled architectures; evaluate flip-invariance beyond CIFAR.  
- Broaden tasks where spatial attention matters (object detection, weakly supervised localization).  

- Questions for authors: What are best-tuned KD baselines per setting (temperature/alpha/label-smoothing) and mean±std over multiple seeds?  
- Can Section 4.2.2 be extended with multiple teacher–student pairs, AT placements, and tuned KD/FitNets comparisons?  
- How is β chosen per layer and over training; were adaptive weighting schemes tried and how sensitive are results to normalization and p?  
- Why do sum-based maps outperform max-based ones empirically; any cases where max is better?  
- What is the compute/memory overhead of AT in training and of double backprop for gradient-based AT?  
- How is batch norm handled in the second backprop; were frozen vs updating BN stats compared?  
- Do learned attention patterns transfer better than KD in downstream tasks (few-shot/fine-tuning) or are there negative transfer cases?
