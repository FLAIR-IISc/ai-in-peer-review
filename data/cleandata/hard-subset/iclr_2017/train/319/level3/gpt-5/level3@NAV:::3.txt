Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Attention Transfer (AT) as a knowledge transfer mechanism that trains a student CNN to match a teacher’s attention maps while optimizing standard classification loss (optionally combined with KD). It defines activation-based attention (channel-wise reductions of |A|^p via sum or max) and a gradient-based attention (input gradients) and minimizes an L2 distance between normalized teacher/student attentions across selected layers. Empirically, AT yields consistent, sometimes substantial, improvements for students across CIFAR-10/100, CUB, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, often complementing KD and outperforming full-activation regression. The paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and provides code.

Decision: Reject (novelty/analysis insufficient)
While the idea is appealing and results are consistently positive, the contribution feels incremental relative to FitNets/KD-style hint losses, and several empirical claims are insufficiently substantiated or analyzed to support acceptance.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets student-teacher transfer via spatial attention alignment, well connected to visualization literature and to prior distillation methods. This is a reasonable and timely question.
- Method and placement: Formulating attention as |A|^p reductions and as input gradients is sensible; normalizing maps and placing losses at multiple stages are practical. However, relative to FitNets’ intermediate-hint regression, AT’s novelty is modest (a specific choice of reduced statistics and a normalized L2 loss).
- Evidence and rigor: Improvements over baseline students are consistent, including on ImageNet, which is commendable. Yet several claims are qualitative only (e.g., “higher-accuracy networks have higher object-attention correlation” lacks quantitative measures). Hyperparameter selection is unclear, and sensitivity to the AT weight β, the exponent p, and attention type (sum vs. max; activation vs. gradient) is under-explored. The gradient-based AT requires double backprop but its compute/memory cost and stability (esp. with batch norm) are not characterized. Finally, students never surpass teachers; speed/latency gains from smaller students are not reported.

Suggestions to improve (not decisive for my recommendation)
- Provide quantitative analysis of spatial correlation (e.g., IoU with boxes, pointing game, CAM/Grad-CAM metrics) across model accuracies.
- Systematically study β, p, attention type, and placement of AT losses; include guidance on when to use activation vs. gradient attention.
- Report teacher training/validation loss in Fig. 7b and student/teacher curves for fairness.
- Characterize compute: training overhead (AT and gradient-based AT), inference speedup, and memory; include wall-clock speed/latency and parameter/FLOP reductions.
- Analyze same-architecture student/teacher cases and cross-architecture transfer.
- Clarify KD interactions on ImageNet and provide broader hyperparameter sweeps.

Questions for the authors
- Can you quantify object-attention alignment and show its correlation with accuracy?
- How sensitive are results to β, p, normalization choice, and the number/locations of AT losses?
- What is the training-time overhead and memory footprint of activation-based and gradient-based AT, and how do batch norm layers behave during double backprop?
- When does activation-based AT outperform gradient-based AT (and vice versa)?
- Do students ever match/beat teachers with AT under same architecture/regularization, and what inference speedups are achieved?
