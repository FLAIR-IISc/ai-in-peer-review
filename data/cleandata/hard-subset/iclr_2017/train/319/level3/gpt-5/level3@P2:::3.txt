Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes Attention Transfer (AT), a simple and general framework to transfer “where to look” from a teacher CNN to a student CNN by penalizing discrepancies between their spatial attention maps during training. Two families of attention are defined: (i) activation-based (statistics over |feature maps|^p such as sum/max) and (ii) gradient-based (input gradients of the loss), with optional combination with knowledge distillation (KD).
- The authors give concrete instantiations of activation-based attention (Fsum, Fpsum, Fpmax) and motivate normalization of attention maps and multi-layer placement of transfer losses.
- Empirical results on CIFAR-10, fine-tuning on CUB-200 and MIT Scenes, and ImageNet show consistent student improvements over the same student trained without AT. AT outperforms full activation matching and often complements KD. The ImageNet result (ResNet-18 student, ResNet-34 teacher) shows +1.1% top-1 and +0.8% top-5 without hyperparameter tuning.
- The paper is clearly written, well situated with respect to attention visualization and distillation (FitNets/KD), and releases code.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- A simple, well-motivated idea that is broadly applicable and easy to implement yields consistent gains across architectures and scales, including ImageNet.
- The activation-based formulation is novel as a transfer signal, improves over full activation regression, and can complement KD.

Supporting arguments
- Problem: Improve student CNNs by transferring inductive biases about spatial focus. This is a clear and important problem for efficient models.
- Motivation and placement: The work bridges attention visualization and knowledge transfer. Distillation methods typically match logits or intermediate features; AT uses spatial attention statistics instead, justified by the intuition that spatial focus encodes task-relevant structure while being lower-dimensional and architecture-agnostic. Related work is well covered (CAM, Guided Backprop, Grad-CAM, FitNets, KD, double backprop).
- Claims and evidence: The main empirical claim—AT reliably improves students—holds across CIFAR (WRN, NIN), fine-tuning (CUB/Scenes), and ImageNet with consistent gains. AT beats full activation matching and often stacks with KD. The ablation comparing sum vs max attentions and layer placement is helpful. The gradient-based AT experiments, while limited, corroborate the idea and connect to double backprop and symmetry regularization. However, the claim that better models have higher object-attention correlation is only visually supported; quantitative evidence is missing. Hyperparameter sensitivity (β, p, normalization) is underexplored, and the conditions under which one attention variant is preferable remain unclear. Overall, results are scientifically sound and reproducible (code provided), with some analysis gaps that could be addressed.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (non-blocking)
- Quantify “attention quality”: Go beyond qualitative figures. Report alignment metrics between attention and object/part regions (e.g., pointing game accuracy, IoU with bounding boxes/segmentations, foreground–background AUC) across teacher/student and training stages.
- Guidance on choosing attention type: Provide a systematic study of Fsum vs Fpsum vs Fpmax and gradient-based AT across datasets and layers. Include a sweep over p, normalization (L1 vs L2), and β to provide practical recipes.
- Hyperparameters and sensitivity: Analyze β’s effect, scheduling, and the impact of placing AT at different depths. Summarize recommended defaults per architecture/scale.
- Computational cost and speed: Report training-time overhead (forward teacher pass, extra backprop for gradient-based AT), wall-clock and memory footprint, and any convergence speed-ups. Since students are smaller, characterize end-to-end speed/energy benefits at inference; discuss whether AT changes compute at test time (it should not).
- KD comparisons: On ImageNet, KD is reported to struggle. A more thorough hyperparameter search (temperature, α), and comparisons under identical architectures/depths would strengthen the conclusion about AT vs KD. Consider newer distillation baselines if feasible.
- Teacher quality and robustness: Explore how teacher strength affects AT gains; is there a threshold beyond which improvements saturate? Analyze robustness to noisy teachers or mismatched domains.
- Curves and statistics: Add teacher training/validation curves in Fig. 7b for context. Report means and standard deviations across multiple runs, especially on CIFAR.

Questions for the authors
1. Attention choice and guidance:
   - Under what conditions do you recommend Fsum vs Fpsum vs Fpmax? How sensitive are gains to the exponent p and to L1 vs L2 normalization?
   - Do you observe consistent benefits from placing AT at every block vs only later stages on larger-resolution datasets?

2. Hyperparameters:
   - How was β chosen on each dataset? Did you try automated schedules (e.g., cosine or curriculum) or adaptive normalization? Please include a sensitivity plot.

3. Quantitative attention-object correlation:
   - Can you provide quantitative measures (e.g., pointing game, CAM-based localization, Grad-CAM overlap) to substantiate that higher-accuracy teachers have more “object-aligned” attention and that students inherit this alignment with AT?

4. Computational aspects:
   - What is the additional training-time overhead (percentage) for activation-based AT and for gradient-based AT? What is the memory overhead per AT location? Any observed convergence speed-up?
   - For gradient-based AT, how do you handle batch normalization during the second backprop? Did you try fixed BN statistics or separate passes?

5. KD and other baselines:
   - On ImageNet, how much KD tuning was attempted (temperature, α, label smoothing)? Did you try AT+KD with more diverse settings or stronger teachers (e.g., ResNet-50)?
   - Can you compare to FitNets or related feature regression methods on ImageNet or at least on higher-resolution fine-tuning tasks?

6. Teacher–student pairing:
   - How do AT gains vary with teacher accuracy gaps? Does AT still help when student and teacher share the same architecture and depth (e.g., same ResNet-18 with different random seeds)?

7. Reproducibility:
   - Please provide exact seeds, training schedules, and data preprocessing for the main tables, and report mean±std over multiple runs, particularly for CIFAR and fine-tuning.

Overall, this is a clear, simple, and useful contribution that should interest both distillation and interpretability communities. The suggested analyses would further strengthen the paper and its practical guidance.
