1. Summary of contributions
The paper addresses the problem of knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student by proposing to transfer attention maps rather than full activations or logits. It introduces two mechanisms—activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer—motivated by the hypothesis that spatial attention encapsulates where a network “looks” to make decisions. The authors detail attention mapping functions, normalization, and multi-layer losses, and report improvements on CIFAR, transfer learning to CUB/Scenes, and an initial ImageNet experiment. The work is easy to reproduce (code released) and the idea is clear, intuitive, and potentially impactful.

2. Decision: Reject (borderline)
Primary reasons: (a) Empirical support is not yet compelling—improvements over standard knowledge distillation (KD) are marginal/inconsistent and sometimes absent; (b) The ImageNet section (4.2.2) is too limited to substantiate the central claim at scale.

3. Supporting arguments
- While the method is well-motivated and situated among attention visualization and distillation literature, the evidence that attention transfer (AT) is decisively better than KD is not robust. On CIFAR, gains over KD are small; on ImageNet, KD is reported to “not work,” suggesting insufficient tuning rather than a fundamental limitation. Without a strong KD baseline, the advantage of AT is unclear.
- The ImageNet evaluation uses a single teacher–student pair (R34→R18), two AT losses, no hyperparameter tuning, and yields ~1.1% top-1 improvement. This is promising but too narrow: no comparisons against tuned KD, FitNets-style hints, or AT+KD on ImageNet; no ablations on loss placement or mapping functions.
- Gradient-based AT is explored only on CIFAR with constrained settings and no demonstration at scale; the extra cost (double backprop) and stability with batch normalization are not quantified.
- Claims such as being “the first” successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet are not sufficiently evidenced.

4. Feedback to improve (not all tied to decision)
- Substantially expand Section 4.2.2: multiple teacher–student pairs (e.g., R50→R18/34, cross-architecture), thorough KD baselines (temperature, alpha, label smoothing), FitNets-style baselines, and AT+KD synergy.
- Provide statistical significance (mean±std over ≥3 seeds) and fair compute budgets; report wall-clock/compute overhead for AT and gradient-based AT.
- Deeper ablations: attention mapping choices (p, sum vs max), normalization, layer placement, β schedules, and sensitivity to spatial resolution.
- Clarify theoretical intuition: why attention maps outperform full activations for transfer; analyze failure cases and where AT helps most (fine-grained vs generic, depth/width mismatches).

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide tuned KD baselines on ImageNet (temperature, α, label smoothing) and compare to AT and AT+KD under matched training budgets?
- How do results change when applying AT losses to all residual groups on ImageNet? Any cross-architecture transfers (e.g., R50→MobileNet)?
- What is the training-time and memory overhead of activation-based AT and of gradient-based AT (double backprop) on large-scale models?
- How sensitive is performance to attention map normalization and the choice of p in Fpsum? Any qualitative/quantitative evidence that student attentions align better with teacher on ImageNet?
- Can you report variability across seeds and error bars for CIFAR and ImageNet?

I would be inclined to raise my score if Section 4.2.2 is significantly extended with the above comparisons and ablations demonstrating clear, reproducible gains over strong KD baselines.
