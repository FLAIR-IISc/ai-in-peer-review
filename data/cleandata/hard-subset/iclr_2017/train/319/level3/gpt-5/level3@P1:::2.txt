Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT): a simple, general way to distill a teacher CNN into a student by matching spatial “attention maps” computed from intermediate activations. Attention is defined as channel-wise statistics over absolute activations (sum, lp-sum, or max), optionally normalized, at multiple layers. The training objective adds an l2 distance between teacher and student attention maps to the standard classification loss, and can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD).
- A second variant defines attention via gradients w.r.t. input (double backprop), and explores (i) using the gradient norm as a regularizer, (ii) enforcing flip-equivariant gradient maps, and (iii) student-teacher gradient-matching.
- Extensive experiments demonstrate consistent gains across datasets (CIFAR-10/100, CUB, MIT Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNets, WRNs, NIN). Notably, AT improves ImageNet top-1 for ResNet-18 using ResNet-34 as teacher with only a few transfer sites, and consistently outperforms FitNets-style full activation transfer. Ablations analyze which attention statistics help, where to place losses, and combinations with KD.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) The approach is simple, well-motivated, and shows consistent, non-trivial gains across multiple settings, including on ImageNet where prior distillation often struggles. (2) The paper’s empirical evidence supports the central claim that matching spatial activation statistics is a powerful distillation signal, outperforming full-activation regression while adding minimal overhead.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and framing: The paper targets a concrete and important question in knowledge transfer: can a student benefit from learning where a teacher “looks”? The framing is clear and connects to both interpretability (attention maps) and practical compression/distillation.
- Motivation and positioning: The method is placed against KD and FitNets. The rationale for spatial statistics (vs. regressing full tensors) is compelling: it preserves spatial saliency while discarding channel-specific idiosyncrasies that are hard to match across architectures. The paper also relates to attention visualization literature.
- Evidence and rigor: The CIFAR experiments include multiple teacher–student pairs, layer-placement ablations, mapping-function comparisons, and synergy with KD. The paper tests transfer to fine-grained and scene recognition via finetuning and shows ImageNet results with reasonable settings and no heavy tuning. The finding that activation-based AT beats full activation transfer is supported by both final accuracy and convergence curves. Where the method is weaker (gradient-based AT), the paper is transparent about implementation constraints and mixed results.
- Limitations are reasonable: AT is defined for CNNs with spatial structure; the gradient-based AT is less mature and somewhat orthogonal to the main contribution. These do not detract from the core value of the activation-based AT.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all affect my decision)
- Terminology: Calling the channel-summed activation statistics “attention” is potentially misleading, given the established meaning of attention mechanisms. Consider explicitly stating that these are spatial saliency maps derived from activations, not learned attention modules, and consistently using “activation-based spatial saliency/attention” to avoid confusion.
- Notation clarity in Eq. (2): Qj is defined as vec(F(A)), so the outer norm is over a vector. Please clarify that “‖·‖2” refers to the vector l2 norm (Frobenius norm of the 2D map), not the spectral norm of the H×W matrix. Also restate the inner normalization step clearly: Q ← Q/‖Q‖2 before computing the loss.
- Organization: The gradient-based AT section feels detached from the primary story and is evaluated in a limited regime (no BN, small NIN). Consider moving most of it to the appendix, or better integrate it by (i) directly comparing to activation-based AT in matched settings, and (ii) showing whether the two can be combined additively.
- Scope and limitations: Make explicit that AT relies on spatially meaningful intermediate feature maps and is presently targeted at CNNs; discuss whether and how it might extend to sequence models or non-convolutional architectures.
- Experimental details: Provide more guidance on β scheduling and scaling across layers, and on how attention maps are rescaled/interpolated when resolutions mismatch. Summarize the computational overhead (forward-only for teacher activations; negligible runtime increase observed) and memory footprint.

Questions for the authors
1. Eq. (2) norm: Please confirm that the loss uses the vector l2 norm of vec(F(A)) after l2 normalization (i.e., the distance between two unit-norm vectors). If not, specify the exact norm and justify the choice.
2. Sensitivity to design choices:
   - How sensitive are gains to the choice of p in Fpsum and the use of max vs sum across channels on larger datasets (beyond CIFAR)?
   - How sensitive are results to which layers/groups receive AT losses? On ImageNet, do more AT sites yield further gains?
3. β setting: You state β is set to 10^3 divided by the number of elements and batch size and is decayed when combined with KD. Can you provide a principled rationale or sensitivity analysis across datasets?
4. Interaction with KD: On ImageNet, KD underperformed while AT helped. Did you try temperature/α sweeps or same-architecture teachers? Any insight into when AT+KD is synergistic vs. antagonistic?
5. Cross-architecture transfer: Beyond ResNet-34→ResNet-18, did you test more heterogeneous pairs (e.g., Inception→ResNet, WRN→thin ResNet)? Does AT still help when channelization and receptive fields differ substantially?
6. Gradient-based AT: Do you have a practical recipe for using BN with double backprop (e.g., freezing BN statistics in the second pass)? Could you report any preliminary results with BN and gradient-based AT to clarify its promise?
7. Generalization vs. regularization: To what extent are AT gains due to better inductive bias (learning spatial saliency) vs. generic regularization? Did you compare to matching random projections of activations or to other simple map-level penalties?
8. Visualization and diagnosis: Can you provide quantitative measures correlating attention-map similarity with accuracy gains (e.g., cosine similarity evolution during training) to strengthen the causal narrative?

Overall, this is a well-executed, practically useful contribution that advances knowledge transfer for CNNs with a simple and effective mechanism.
