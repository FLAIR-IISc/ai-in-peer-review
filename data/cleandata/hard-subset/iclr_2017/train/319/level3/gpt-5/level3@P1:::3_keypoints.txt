- Proposal: Introduces Attention Transfer (AT) to align spatial attention maps of teacher and student CNNs alongside classification loss (optionally with KD).  
- Attention definitions: Uses activation-based mappings (channel reductions of |A|^p via sum or max) and gradient-based input-sensitivity (∂L/∂x).  
- Motivation: Argues attention is an interpretable spatial signal that captures “where the network looks” better than full activations.  
- Design choices: Justifies L2-normalization before comparison and provides layer placement strategies for same- and different-depth architectures.  
- Empirical claim: AT consistently improves student performance on CIFAR-10, transfer learning (CUB/MIT Scenes), and ImageNet (ResNet-18 ← ResNet-34).  
- Activation vs full-activation: Activation-based AT outperforms regressing full activations and combines well with KD.  
- Gradient-based AT: Feasible via double backprop, shows gains on CIFAR in controlled experiments.  
- Practicality: Minimal inference overhead; code and models released.  
- Decision: Reviewer recommends accept.  
- Key reason 1: Simple, well-motivated idea with empirical gains across datasets and architectures, including a meaningful ImageNet improvement.  
- Key reason 2: Bridges attention visualization and knowledge distillation and outperforms full-activation regression.  
- Problem framing: Clear target—transfer “where to look” from strong to weak models, well situated in related work.  
- Method strengths: Activation-based mappings are intuitive, architecture-agnostic, easy to compute; normalization and loss placement are sensible; symmetry regularizer for gradient AT is useful.  
- CIFAR results: Consistent gains across WRN variants; sum mappings beat max; AT > full activations; AT+KD often best; ablations informative.  
- Transfer learning: AT narrows ResNet-18 vs ResNet-34 gap, especially effective on fine-grained CUB.  
- ImageNet result: +1.1% top-1 for ResNet-18 with only two AT losses and no tuning, compelling at scale.  
- Rigor/limitations: Experiments support claim but some areas underexplored (quantitative attention quality, sensitivity to β/mapping choices, limited gradient-AT settings).  
- Suggested improvement—attention quality: Provide quantitative metrics (correlation/IoU with localization cues) beyond visualizations.  
- Suggested improvement—hyperparameters: Analyze sensitivity to β, normalization choice, number/placement of AT losses and provide recommended settings.  
- Suggested improvement—mapping guidance: Give clearer advice on choosing sum vs max, p values, and activation- vs gradient-based AT per task/architecture.  
- Suggested improvement—compute: Report convergence speed, training-time overhead (especially for double backprop), wall-clock and FLOPs; clarify inference cost unchanged.  
- Suggested improvement—variance and comparisons: Report run-to-run variance, include teacher curves, and strengthen KD/FitNets comparisons with matched tuning.  
- Suggested improvement—BN details: Clarify handling of batch-norm during second backprop for gradient-based AT and discuss stability.  
- Suggested extension: Evaluate AT on tasks where spatial attention is critical (detection/weakly supervised localization).  
- Key questions for authors: Sensitivity and scheduling of β; whether mapping trends hold on ImageNet/transfer; diminishing returns with more AT losses; KD hyperparameters and complementarity; implementation and cost/stability of gradient-AT with BN; quantifying attention-object correlation; teacher selection effects; data-efficiency benefits; same-capacity pair behavior; end-to-end training time and memory trade-offs.
