- Proposes Attention Transfer (AT): student CNNs mimic teacher spatial attention maps via L2 losses between normalized maps.  
- Formalizes two attention families: activation-based spatial maps (channel-wise sum/sum-of-powers/max-of-powers) and gradient-based input-sensitivity attention with a flip/symmetry regularizer.  
- AT can be combined with Knowledge Distillation (KD); shows empirical improvements on CIFAR-10, CUB, and MIT Scenes, and a preliminary ImageNet result (ResNet-18←ResNet-34) of +1.1% top-1, +0.8% top-5.  
- Finds attention-map transfer more effective than full-activation transfer, speeds convergence, and includes ablations on attention types and loss placement.  
- Decision: Reject (borderline); recommends strengthening Section 4.2.2 (ImageNet) to raise the score.  
- Major concern: ImageNet evidence is too limited and under-tuned to substantiate central claims; KD comparisons are inconclusive.  
- Against strong KD baselines, observed gains are marginal and not convincingly superior in realistic settings.  
- Motivation and literature placement are sound and well-grounded (ties to CAM/Grad-CAM, FitNets, double backprop).  
- CIFAR experiments: consistent improvements and useful ablations, but relative gains over KD are small and AT+KD gives only modest extra benefit.  
- Gradient-based AT: conceptually interesting but experimentally weak (single-run results, BN issues, altered architectures, significant second-pass overhead).  
- Transfer learning (CUB, Scenes): positive, especially for CUB, but lacks comparisons to strong KD/FitNets baselines and variance reporting.  
- ImageNet shortfall: only one teacher-student pair tested, no hyperparameter tuning, no multi-seed statistics, and KD purportedly “doesn’t work” without thorough exploration.  
- Claim that attention > activation is well supported on CIFAR; normalization is important but sensitivity (l1 vs l2, per-layer scaling) needs deeper study.  
- Overhead: activation-based AT is cheap but gradient-based AT can be costly; this trade-off requires clearer reporting (time, memory).  
- Recommended experiments: evaluate multiple teacher-student pairs, layer sets, p values, β schedules, and include tuned KD/FitNets baselines with multiple seeds and stds.  
- Clarify gradient-AT practicality: handling BN in second backprop, alternatives, and cost vs. accuracy trade-offs on large datasets.  
- Ask for analyses linking attention alignment to accuracy, qualitative failure cases, and layer-wise benefit breakdowns on ResNets/ImageNet.  
- Suggest broader generalization tests: detection/localization tasks, robustness to label noise and distribution shift, and architecturally different students (MobileNet/ShuffleNet).  
- Reproducibility requests: release exact configs, seeds, scripts, parameter counts, FLOPs, and training time; report mean ± std over ≥3 runs.  
- Overall: AT is appealing and potentially impactful, but needs substantially stronger, compute-matched ImageNet evidence and rigorous baselines to be convincing.
