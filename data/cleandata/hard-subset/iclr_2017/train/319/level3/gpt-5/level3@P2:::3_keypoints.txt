- Proposal: Introduces Attention Transfer (AT) to transfer “where to look” from teacher to student by penalizing discrepancies between spatial attention maps during training.  
- AT families: Defines activation-based attentions (statistics over |feature maps|^p) and gradient-based attentions, and can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD).  
- Instantiations: Proposes concrete activation variants Fsum, Fpsum, Fpmax, and motivates normalization and multi-layer placement of AT losses.  
- Empirical gains: Shows consistent student improvements on CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet (e.g., ResNet-18 student +1.1% top-1 on ImageNet).  
- Comparison to baselines: AT outperforms full activation matching and often complements KD.  
- Writing and code: Paper is clearly written, well-situated relative to prior work, and code is released.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Main reasons for acceptance: Simple, broadly applicable, easy-to-implement idea that yields consistent gains across architectures and scales.  
- Novelty: Activation-based attention as a transfer signal is novel and effective.  
- Problem framing: Addresses transferring spatial-focus inductive biases to improve compact student CNNs—an important and clear problem.  
- Motivation: AT bridges attention visualization and distillation; attention is lower-dimensional and architecture-agnostic compared to full feature matching.  
- Evidence strength: Empirical claims are supported across datasets and architectures; ablations on attention type and layer placement are useful.  
- Gradient AT: Limited experiments support the idea and connect it to double backprop and symmetry regularization.  
- Missing quantitative claim: The claim that better models have higher object-attention correlation is only visually supported and lacks quantitative evidence.  
- Hyperparameter gaps: Sensitivity to β, p, normalization, and when to prefer attention variants is underexplored.  
- Reproducibility: Results are reproducible in principle (code provided) but exact seeds/schedules and run statistics are missing.  
- Suggested analysis — attention quality: Quantify attention alignment to objects/parts (pointing game, IoU, foreground–background AUC).  
- Suggested analysis — guidance: Systematically study Fsum vs Fpsum vs Fpmax and gradient AT across datasets/layers; sweep p, normalization, and β.  
- Suggested analysis — hyperparameters: Analyze β effects, scheduling, and placement recommendations; provide default settings per architecture/scale.  
- Suggested analysis — computational cost: Report training-time, wall-clock, and memory overheads for activation- and gradient-based AT and any convergence effects; clarify inference-time impact.  
- Suggested KD comparisons: Conduct more thorough KD hyperparameter searches (temperature, α) and compare to stronger distillation baselines and teacher sizes.  
- Suggested robustness study: Evaluate how teacher strength, noisy teachers, and domain mismatch affect AT gains and saturation behavior.  
- Statistical reporting: Include training/validation curves, means and standard deviations across multiple runs, especially for CIFAR and fine-tuning tasks.  
- Questions for authors: Request guidance on attention choice/placement, β selection and schedules, quantitative attention-object correlation metrics, training-time/memory overheads and BN handling in gradient AT, extent of KD tuning, teacher–student pairing effects, and reproducibility details (seeds, schedules, preprocessing).
