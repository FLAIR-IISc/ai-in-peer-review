Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a new knowledge transfer method—Attention Transfer (AT)—which encourages a student CNN to mimic teacher attention maps rather than raw activations or softened label distributions. It formalizes two families of attention: (i) activation-based spatial maps computed via channel-wise statistics (sum, sum of powers, max of powers), and (ii) gradient-based attention defined as input sensitivity (with a symmetry/flip regularizer). The method adds simple l2 losses between normalized teacher/student attention maps at matched layers and can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD). Empirically, AT improves student performance on CIFAR-10, fine-grained CUB, and MIT Scenes; an initial ImageNet experiment (ResNet-18 student from ResNet-34 teacher) reports +1.1% top-1 and +0.8% top-5. The paper also finds that attention maps are more effective to transfer than full activations, speeds up convergence, and provides helpful ablations on attention types and loss placement.

Decision: Reject (borderline), with a path to raise the score if Section 4.2.2 (ImageNet) is extended/strengthened
Key reasons:
- The empirical support on large-scale (ImageNet) is too limited and under-tuned to substantiate the central claim; KD comparisons are inconclusive. Section 4.2.2 is the crux and needs substantially more evidence.
- Against strong KD baselines, gains appear marginal overall; the paper does not convincingly establish that AT is consistently better than standard KD in realistic settings.

Supporting arguments
1) What problem is tackled? The paper addresses how best to transfer knowledge from a teacher to a student CNN, arguing that “where” the teacher attends (spatial attention) is more informative than transferring full activations or only outputs.

2) Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? Yes. The motivation is clear and intuitive, grounded in prior work on attention visualization (e.g., CAM, Grad-CAM) and knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., FitNets). The connection to double backprop for gradient-based attention is apt. The paper correctly identifies limitations of transferring full activations and motivates attention as a compact, semantically aligned supervisory signal.

3) Do the results support the claims?
- CIFAR: AT shows consistent improvements; ablations (sum vs. max; layer placement) are helpful. However, relative to KD, improvements are small, and AT+KD offers only modest additional gains.
- Gradient-based AT: Conceptually interesting but experimentally weak—single-run results, no batch normalization, altered architectures, and non-trivial second-order overhead. These results are informative but not yet robust.
- Transfer learning (CUB, Scenes): Positive and plausible, particularly for CUB where intermediate attention likely matters. However, comparisons to strong KD or FitNets baselines are thin, and variance/seed sensitivity is not reported.
- ImageNet (Section 4.2.2): The most critical gap. Only one teacher-student pair with two AT losses and no hyperparameter tuning; KD is reported to “not work” but not thoroughly investigated. Claiming AT as the “first successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet” needs broader evidence (e.g., more pairs, sensitivity analysis, statistical significance, compute-matched baselines). The observed +1.1% top-1 is promising but not yet convincing without multiple runs and stronger baselines.

Other points:
- The claim that attention maps are superior to full activation transfer is well supported on CIFAR with meaningful ablations.
- Normalization of attention maps is emphasized as important, but the rationale and sensitivity analysis could be deeper (e.g., l1 vs l2; per-layer scaling).
- Overheads are small for activation-based AT but potentially substantial for gradient-based AT (second pass). This trade-off deserves clearer reporting.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (meant to help, not all part of the decision)
- Strengthen Section 4.2.2 (ImageNet):
  - Evaluate multiple student-teacher pairs (e.g., ResNet-18←ResNet-34/50; ResNet-34←ResNet-101; cross-family teachers like VGG/WRN).
  - Report AT at different layer sets (1–4 groups), different attention mappings (p values), and different β schedules; include AT+KD when possible.
  - Provide KD and FitNets baselines with tuning. If KD “fails,” explore temperatures, α, data augmentation, and teacher matching depth; show learning curves and final accuracies.
  - Run multiple seeds and report mean ± std; analyze training budget and convergence speed.
  - Quantify computational overhead and wall-clock for teacher+student training.
- Clarify gradient-based AT practicality:
  - How to handle batch normalization in the second backprop? Compare alternatives and show results with modern BN settings.
  - Provide cost vs. accuracy trade-offs and whether gradient AT adds value over activation AT on larger datasets.
- Analysis and visualization:
  - Systematically relate accuracy gains to attention similarity metrics; show that the student’s attention alignment increases during training and correlates with accuracy.
  - Provide qualitative failure cases where AT helps or hurts.
- Generalization:
  - Evaluate on detection/localization or weakly supervised tasks where spatial attention should matter most.
  - Test robustness to label noise and distribution shifts; does AT regularize better than KD?
- Reproducibility: Release exact configs, seeds, and scripts; report parameter counts, FLOPs, and training time.

Questions for the authors
- On ImageNet, what prevents KD from working with ResNet-18/34? Which temperatures/α did you try, and how sensitive are results? Could KD+AT converge if temperatures are tuned?
- How do choices of p and normalization (l1 vs l2; per-layer scaling) affect results on ImageNet? Any guidance for practitioners?
- What is the compute overhead of AT (activation-based) and gradient-based AT in practice (extra memory, time, backward passes)?
- How robust are gains across seeds and training budgets, especially for ImageNet? Please report mean ± std over ≥3 runs.
- Does AT still help when the student is not just narrower/shallower but architecturally different (e.g., MobileNet, ShuffleNet)?
- Can you provide layer-wise analyses showing which stages benefit most from AT in ResNets on ImageNet?
- For gradient-based AT, how do you handle BN in the second backprop, and what are the empirical consequences of different choices?

If the authors substantially expand and solidify Section 4.2.2 with rigorous, compute-matched comparisons and multiple runs, I would be inclined to raise the score; the method is appealing and potentially impactful.
