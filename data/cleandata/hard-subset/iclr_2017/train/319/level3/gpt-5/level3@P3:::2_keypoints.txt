- Proposes activation-based attention transfer (AT): match normalized spatial summaries (sum/lp-norm/max across channels) of intermediate CNN feature maps between teacher and student.  
- AT is lightweight, CNN-architecture-agnostic, easy to implement, and can be combined with standard knowledge distillation (KD).  
- Empirically, AT consistently improves student accuracy on CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, often outperforming FitNet-style full-activation regression.  
- Also experiments with gradient-based AT (double backprop) but results are limited to small-scale CIFAR/NIN setups and are underdeveloped.  
- Decision: Accept — main reasons are strong empirical gains from a simple, low-overhead method that scales to ImageNet and has clear practical value.  
- Problem framing and motivation are good: builds on KD/FitNets and visualization work to argue spatial focus contains transferable knowledge.  
- Results largely support claims for activation-based AT, with useful ablations on attention definitions, loss placements, and KD combination; ImageNet gain (~1.1% top-1 on ResNet-18) is persuasive.  
- Strength: intuitive, simple loss on normalized spatial summaries; robust to channel permutation and computationally cheap.  
- Strength: broad empirical coverage and generally clearer performance than FitNet; sum-based maps typically beat max-based maps; KD+AT often helps on CIFAR.  
- Strength: clear writing, released code, practical tips (e.g., normalization, β scheduling).  
- Weakness: inherently tailored to CNNs with spatial maps — not directly applicable to non-convolutional models.  
- Weakness: terminology “attention” is misleading — these are channel-summed activation energies, not learned or class-discriminative attention mechanisms.  
- Weakness: gradient-based AT is conceptually interesting but not integrated, not compared on large datasets, and complicated by BN/second-derivative issues.  
- Weakness: notation/clarity issues (Eq. (2) and norm choices ambiguous; need to disambiguate Euclidean/Frobenius vs induced 2-norm).  
- Weakness: organization — gradient-based section interrupts the main narrative and should be integrated or moved.  
- Suggested improvements: clarify norm/normalization choices, consider renaming (e.g., “activation-map energy transfer”), and either integrate gradient AT or move it to appendix.  
- Suggested experiments: more systematic ablations on layer choices, p in lp-sum, normalization sensitivity, β scheduling, and teacher-student architecture mismatch.  
- Baseline suggestions: strengthen FitNet baselines, report variance over seeds, and clarify KD hyperparameter tuning (especially on ImageNet).  
- Practical reporting: provide training/inference cost and memory overhead for AT and gradient-based AT.  
- Extensions: discuss adaptation to detection/segmentation and to non-CNN models (e.g., token-wise pooling for sequences).  
- Questions for authors: clarify norm used in Eq. (2) and normalization choices, explore channel-wise standardization or softmaxed spatial maps, report best layer placements on ImageNet, and sensitivity to β scheduling.  
- Questions for authors: did they tune KD hyperparameters when combining with AT on ImageNet, why not combine gradient- and activation-based AT, how robust is AT across divergent architectures, and what is the wall-clock overhead and effect on calibration/robustness?
