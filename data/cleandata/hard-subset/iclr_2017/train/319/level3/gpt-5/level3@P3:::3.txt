Summary of contributions
- The paper introduces Attention Transfer (AT): a simple, generic loss that encourages a student CNN to match the spatial “attention maps” of a teacher, in addition to the standard classification loss, and optionally a Knowledge Distillation (KD) term.
- It defines activation-based attentions (channelwise reductions of |A|^p over feature tensors; variants include sum and max with exponent p) and a gradient-based attention defined as ∂Loss/∂input (with a double-backprop objective and an optional flip-invariance regularizer).
- Empirically, AT consistently improves student accuracy against training the student alone across CIFAR-10/100, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet. Activation-based AT generally outperforms regressing full activations and combines well with KD on small datasets. On ImageNet, activation-based AT improves ResNet-18 when using ResNet-34 as teacher.
- The method is easy to implement, architecturally agnostic, and adds low computational overhead for activation-based AT.

Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons: (1) The idea is well-motivated, clearly presented, and broadly applicable; (2) Results show consistent and non-trivial gains across datasets and architectures, including on ImageNet, with a simple loss that is likely to be useful to practitioners.

Supporting arguments
1) What problem is tackled? The paper addresses how to transfer knowledge from a stronger CNN to a weaker one using attention—i.e., where the network focuses spatially—rather than full activations or softened logits alone. The goal is to improve student performance without increasing its capacity.

2) Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The paper connects attention visualization literature (gradients, CAM/Grad-CAM) with knowledge transfer (KD, FitNets). The key insight—matching spatial focus is a compact, semantically aligned target—is intuitive and novel relative to FitNets (which regress feature tensors) and KD (which matches output distributions). The flip-invariance regularizer for gradient-based attention is a nice, principled addition.

3) Do the results support the claims?
- Positive: Across CIFAR (WRNs), CUB and Scenes (ResNets), and ImageNet (ResNet-18 ← ResNet-34), activation-based AT consistently improves over the student baseline. AT beats full activation regression (FitNet-style hints) and sometimes complements KD. Ablations indicate sum-based attention variants outperform max-based ones, and placing AT at multiple stages helps. Gradient-based AT also helps in a controlled CIFAR setup.
- Limitations: (a) Students still trail teachers; surpassing the teacher is rare/not shown. (b) The claim that more accurate models have higher object–attention spatial correlation is only visually supported (Fig. 4); no quantitative evidence is provided. (c) Hyperparameter selection is under-specified; especially the role and sensitivity of β (AT weight) and p. (d) Guidance on when to prefer activation- vs gradient-based attention is limited; experiments do not clearly delineate regimes where each shines. (e) Gradient-based AT is demonstrated only on CIFAR/NIN without BN due to implementation complexity; scalability to modern BN-heavy nets is left unresolved. (f) The method’s compute/latency benefits are not quantified; if the motivation is smaller/faster students, speed-ups should be reported. (g) Figure 7b compares convergence for the student variants but does not include teacher curves.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all affect my acceptance)
- Quantify the attention–object correlation claim: use pointing game accuracy, IoU overlap with bounding boxes/segmentation masks, or correlation with saliency/Grad-CAM, across layers and models. Report statistics comparing low- vs high-accuracy networks.
- Hyperparameters and sensitivity: provide a principled recipe and ablations for β (e.g., scaling with map size, schedule, robustness bands), p (1, 2, 4), normalization choice (L1 vs L2), number and placement of AT losses, and the interaction with KD temperature/α.
- When to use which attention: run a systematic comparison of activation-based vs gradient-based AT across tasks (fine-grained vs generic classification), depths, and data sizes. Discuss pros/cons (cost, stability, interpretability, dependence on BN).
- Efficiency: report training overhead and inference speed/throughput/energy for students with and without AT. Since the student is smaller than the teacher, characterize the practical speed-up and whether AT changes the Pareto frontier of accuracy vs FLOPs/latency.
- Same-architecture setting: evaluate AT when teacher and student share architecture but differ in training (e.g., early-stopped vs fully trained, or same capacity but teacher ensembling). Does AT still help?
- Stronger baselines and context: include FitNets with tuned hints for ImageNet if feasible, or at least deeper comparison to logit-based KD variants that sometimes surpass the teacher (e.g., with label smoothing or intermediate self-distillation).
- Figures: add teacher training/validation losses to Fig. 7b for context and include convergence overlays. Add more side-by-side attention visualizations before/after AT, with quantitative similarity metrics.
- Gradient-based AT with BN: clarify and, if possible, demonstrate a scalable implementation; otherwise discuss constraints and workarounds.

Questions for the authors
- How should practitioners choose β in Eq. (2) across datasets and layers? What is the sensitivity of final accuracy to β and to the normalization choice?
- For activation-based attention, how do p and the reduction (sum vs max) affect performance across tasks? Any guidance beyond the small CIFAR table?
- Can AT help when teacher and student have the same architecture and capacity but the teacher is better optimized (e.g., longer training or better regularization)? If so, by how much?
- What is the training and inference overhead of AT (activation-based and gradient-based) in wall-clock time and memory, and does AT alter the student’s inference-time cost?
- For the gradient-based variant, how do you handle BN in the second backprop pass? Could you share a recipe that works with modern networks, or is a separate forward with frozen BN required?
- On ImageNet, why does KD hurt while AT helps? Did you explore KD temperature/α sweeps and AT+KD combinations at scale?
- Can you provide quantitative evidence for the attention–object correlation claim (Fig. 4), e.g., pointing game or CAM overlap metrics across the accuracy spectrum?
- How many AT insertion points are needed for most of the gains, and at which stages are they most beneficial on large-scale datasets?
