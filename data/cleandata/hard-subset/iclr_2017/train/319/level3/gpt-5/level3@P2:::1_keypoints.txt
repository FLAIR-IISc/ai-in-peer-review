- Proposes attention transfer (AT): train student to match teacher spatial attention maps instead of full intermediate activations.  
- Introduces activation-based attention (channel-wise stats with power p and l2 normalization) and gradient-based attention (loss gradients with double-backprop and optional flip-invariance).  
- AT is simple to add, architecture-agnostic, and can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD).  
- Empirical claims: consistent gains on CIFAR, CUB, MIT Scenes; activation-based AT outperforms regressing full activations; ImageNet ResNet-34→ResNet-18 shows +1.1% top-1.  
- Decision: Reject (borderline).  
- Key reason 1: ImageNet evidence is too limited — single, lightly tuned experiment and inconclusive comparisons to strong KD baselines.  
- Key reason 2: Gains over KD on small benchmarks are marginal in several settings; gradient-based AT evidence is restricted to a non-BN setup, limiting generality.  
- Motivation and problem framing are well connected to saliency, CAM/Grad-CAM, and KD literature; l2-normalized spatial maps are a clean target.  
- Novelty is incremental but practical: attention-map stats are standard, insight is normalizing and using them as auxiliary losses.  
- Gradient-based AT links to existing regularizers (double backprop, flip invariance) and is reasonable in concept.  
- CIFAR/medium-scale results show consistent but modest improvements; AT≥KD sometimes and AT+KD often best.  
- Ablation showing AT>full activation transfer is useful evidence.  
- Statistical rigor is uneven: mixed median-of-5 and single-run reporting, missing standard deviations/confidence intervals.  
- Hyperparameter tuning parity between KD, AT, and baselines is not fully convincing (KD temp/α not thoroughly explored).  
- Gradient-based AT only tested on a thin NIN without batch normalization, which likely prevents application to modern BN architectures.  
- ImageNet section is underpowered: only one student–teacher pair, only two AT placements, no hyperparameter sweeps, and no fair KD tuning or other transfer-objective comparisons.  
- Need broader ImageNet experiments (multiple teacher–student pairs, more placements, full tuning, comparisons to 1×1 hint matching and FitNets, and training-cost analysis).  
- For gradient-based AT, resolve BN behavior in second backprop and demonstrate viability on BN-equipped architectures and larger datasets.  
- Compare gradient-based AT to strong saliency/gradient-norm regularizers under matched tuning.  
- Recommended ablations: sensitivity to p, sum vs max, l1 vs l2 normalization, β scheduling, and loss placement (per-block vs per-stage).  
- Provide attention-alignment analyses: attention similarity metrics, qualitative maps, and correlation with accuracy.  
- Temper or substantiate the “first successful ImageNet transfer” claim with literature check and stronger evidence.  
- Improve clarity and reproducibility: tighten AT loss notation, state all training details, and clarify interplay and fair tuning with KD.  
- Questions for authors include requests for more ImageNet experiments and hyperparameter sensitivity, compute/memory overhead of AT vs baseline/KD, extent of KD tuning, BN handling for gradient AT, comparisons to gradient-norm penalties, robustness to teacher/student differences, and impact of normalization and early-layer losses.
