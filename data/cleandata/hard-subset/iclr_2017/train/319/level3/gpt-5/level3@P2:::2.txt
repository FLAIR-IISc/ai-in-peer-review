Summary and contributions
The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT), a simple and effective knowledge-transfer scheme for convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The core idea is to define spatial “attention maps” at intermediate layers by aggregating activations across channels (e.g., sum, sum of powers), normalize these maps, and train a student to match the teacher’s normalized attentions at selected layers in addition to the standard task loss (and optionally KD). The paper also explores a gradient-based variant that matches input-gradient “attentions” using double backprop. Empirically, activation-based AT consistently improves student performance across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB, MIT-Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network). The method outperforms FitNet-style full-activation matching, is complementary to KD on several settings, and—importantly—shows positive results on ImageNet. The paper is generally well-written, accessible, and releases code/models.

Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Strong empirical gains with a simple, broadly applicable procedure for CNNs, including on large-scale ImageNet where prior KD-style techniques often struggle.
- Clear ablations demonstrating that spatial activation aggregation is a better transfer signal than full activation regression and can complement KD.

Supporting arguments
1) What problem is tackled? The paper addresses how to transfer knowledge from a stronger “teacher” CNN to a smaller/shallower “student,” focusing on where the network “looks” spatially. It seeks a more robust and architecture-friendly transfer signal than logits or full activations.

2) Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? The motivation is sound: spatial saliency/attention in CNNs correlates with discriminative regions, and matching these attentions should guide students to develop better internal representations. The paper relates to KD, FitNets/hints, and multiple attention/visualization works (CAM, guided backprop, Grad-CAM). Compared to FitNets, AT avoids additional adapters for full-activation matching and is less brittle across architectural mismatches.

3) Do the results support the claims? The CIFAR experiments cover several teacher/student pairs (same/different depths, widths), compare multiple attention mappings, and show that AT > full-activation transfer, with further gains when combined with KD. On CUB/Scenes, AT substantially narrows the ResNet-18 vs ResNet-34 gap, and the ImageNet experiment shows non-trivial top-1/top-5 improvements. The ablations on which layers to transfer from and which mapping to use (sum vs max, p) are helpful. While the gradient-based AT results are modest and limited to small settings, they provide an initial exploration.

Caveats:
- The “attention” terminology is somewhat misleading: the main signal is a spatial aggregation of feature magnitudes (not an explicit attention mechanism or class-discriminative map). The paper is careful in parts, but the framing could be tightened.
- The gradient-based AT section feels orthogonal to the main story, is not combined with activation-based AT, and lacks comprehensive comparisons; it adds limited value to the central thesis.
- Some notation/implementation details are unclear (see next section), though these are fixable.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Clarify Eq. (2): You define QjS = vec(F(AjS)) and use ||·||2. Please state explicitly that this is the l2 norm of the vectorized map (equivalently, the Frobenius norm of the 2D map), not the matrix induced spectral norm. As written, “2-norm” could be misread as the largest singular value.
- Tighten the “attention” terminology. Consider consistently calling the main signal “spatial activation aggregation (attention) maps” and explicitly differentiate from class-discriminative methods (CAM/Grad-CAM).
- Reorganize to foreground the activation-based AT (the main contribution) and move the gradient-based section to an appendix or present it as a brief auxiliary experiment. Alternatively, show a stronger empirical story: direct comparisons and/or combinations with activation-based AT, sensitivity to hyperparameters, and results beyond CIFAR.
- Provide more guidance on hyperparameters: β scaling, decay schedules, choice of p and layer selection, and the role of l2 vs l1 normalization. A small sensitivity analysis would aid reproducibility.
- Expand ImageNet experiments if possible: include AT losses on all residual groups, report wall-clock/compute overhead, and give further comparison to KD under tuned hyperparameters (and perhaps same-architecture teacher/student).
- Discuss limitations: current formulation is tied to convolutional architectures with spatial feature maps; note if/how it might be adapted to non-convolutional models or sequence tasks.
- Notation polish: clearly define I (layer pairs), interpolation method for mismatched resolutions, and whether teacher is strictly frozen. Minor editorial passes will further improve readability.

Questions for the authors
- Norm in Eq. (2): Please confirm that ||·||2 denotes the l2 norm of the vectorized attention map (i.e., Frobenius norm), not the induced spectral norm. Do you ever use l1 normalization in practice, and how does it compare?
- Layer selection: How sensitive are gains to which layers/groups are used? On ImageNet, do AT losses on all groups yield larger improvements than using the last two?
- Hyperparameters: How was β chosen and scheduled across datasets? How sensitive are results to p in Fpsum and to normalization (l2 vs l1)? Any general recommendations?
- Computational overhead: What is the added training cost (time/memory) of activation-based AT and of gradient-based AT (double backprop)? Is teacher forward always required, or can teacher attentions be cached?
- Interaction with KD: On CIFAR, AT+KD helps. On ImageNet, KD struggled. Is this due to architecture mismatch, temperature/α choices, or optimization instability? Did same-architecture teacher/student KD fare better?
- Gradient-based AT: Can you clarify how batch normalization is handled during the second backprop? Did you try combining gradient-based and activation-based AT, and if so, what were the results?
- Generalization: Have you tried AT in detection/segmentation or weakly supervised localization? Do gains persist when spatial supervision is crucial?
- Capacity gap: How does AT perform when the teacher is only marginally better than the student, or when the student is much smaller? Any failure modes?
- Interpretability: Beyond qualitative figures, can you quantify that students’ attentions become closer to teachers’ (e.g., correlation metrics) and relate that to accuracy gains?
