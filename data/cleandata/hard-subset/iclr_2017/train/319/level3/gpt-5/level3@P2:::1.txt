Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes attention transfer (AT) as a form of knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student CNN. Rather than regressing full intermediate activations, the student is trained to match the teacher’s spatial attention maps.
- Two complementary definitions of attention are introduced: activation-based maps (channel-wise statistics, e.g., sum of absolute activations with optional exponent p, followed by l2 normalization) and gradient-based maps (loss gradients w.r.t. input, with a double-backprop objective and an optional flip-invariance regularizer).
- The method is simple to add to standard training, is architecture-agnostic, and can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD).
- Empirically, the paper reports consistent gains on CIFAR, CUB, and MIT Scenes, shows that activation-based AT outperforms regressing full activation tensors, and provides an initial ImageNet experiment (ResNet-34 → ResNet-18) with a reported 1.1% top-1 improvement.

Decision: Reject (borderline)
Key reasons:
1) The empirical support on large-scale (ImageNet) is too limited to substantiate the central claim; the single, lightly tuned experiment is insufficient, and comparisons to strong KD baselines are inconclusive.
2) While promising, the gains over KD on small-scale benchmarks are marginal in several settings, and the gradient-based AT evidence is restricted to a narrow setup without batch normalization, leaving unclear how broadly applicable that variant is.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The idea that “where” a network attends encodes useful inductive bias is well motivated and well connected to literature on saliency, CAM/Grad-CAM, and KD/FitNets. Using l2-normalized spatial maps as the transfer target is a clean, interpretable design. The paper positions itself fairly with respect to attention visualization and distillation work.
- Novelty and placement: Transferring attention maps (rather than full activations) is a sensible and interesting twist on hint-based supervision. The activation-map constructions (sum, power-sum, max) are standard statistics; the insight is to normalize and impose them as auxiliary losses. Gradient-based AT via double backprop and flip invariance is also reasonable and relates to known regularizers. Overall novelty is incremental but practical.
- Empirical validation on small/medium scale: CIFAR results show consistent but mostly modest improvements, with AT≥KD in some cases and AT+KD best. The ablation that AT>full activation transfer is useful. However, statistical rigor is uneven: some results report median-of-5, others are single runs; standard deviations/confidence intervals are largely missing. Hyperparameter tuning parity between KD, AT, and baselines is not fully convincing (e.g., KD temperature and α are given but not explored).
- Gradient-based AT evidence: Only shown on a thin NIN without batch norm, with comments that BN complicates the second backprop. This materially limits claims for that variant’s practicality on modern architectures.
- ImageNet evidence: The core claim that AT scales and is the “first” transfer to work on ImageNet needs much stronger support. There is only one student–teacher pair (ResNet-18 ← ResNet-34), only two AT loss placements, no hyperparameter sweep, and KD is reported to “struggle” without substantial effort to tune it comparably. No comparisons to other transfer objectives (e.g., 1×1-conv-matched hints) on ImageNet. Without broader experiments (e.g., other teachers, layer placements, ablations, training-cost analysis), it is hard to conclude that AT is robustly beneficial at scale.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all part of the decision)
- Strengthen ImageNet section (4.2.2):
  - Multiple student–teacher pairs (e.g., RN50→RN18, RN101→RN34), more attention-loss placements (all four groups), and full training with tuned hyperparameters for both AT and KD.
  - Include comparisons to: full-activation hints with 1×1 matching; FitNets-style intermediate hints; recent distillation variants (temperature sweeps, α sweeps).
  - Report statistical variation over at least 3 runs and training curves.
  - Provide training cost (FLOPs/time/memory) overhead of AT vs baseline/KD.
- Gradient-based AT:
  - Resolve BN behavior in the second backprop; demonstrate viability on BN-equipped architectures and larger datasets.
  - Compare to strong saliency regularizers (e.g., gradient norm penalties, input-Jacobian penalties) under matched tuning.
- Ablations and analysis:
  - Sensitivity to attention-map definition (p for power-sum, max vs sum), normalization choice (l1 vs l2), and β scheduling.
  - Where-to-place losses: per-block vs per-stage; impact on early vs late layers.
  - Qualitative and quantitative alignment: show attention similarity metrics and their correlation with accuracy.
- Claims: Temper or substantiate the “first successful ImageNet transfer” claim with a comprehensive literature check and stronger evidence.
- Clarity:
  - Tighten notation around AT loss; explicitly state all training details for reproducibility.
  - Clarify interplay with KD (temperature, α) and ensure fair tuning.

Questions for the authors
1) ImageNet:
   - Can you provide additional experiments with more AT placements, multiple teacher–student pairs, and tuned KD baselines (temperature/α sweeps)? How sensitive is the 1.1% gain to β, normalization, and p?
   - What is the compute/memory overhead (per iteration and overall) of AT relative to baseline and KD?
2) KD parity:
   - How extensively was KD tuned on CIFAR and ImageNet? Did you try temperature>4, label-smoothing baselines, or alternative KD losses?
3) Gradient-based AT:
   - How do you handle batch normalization in the second backprop? Can you show results on BN-equipped networks and larger datasets?
   - How does gradient-based AT compare to simply penalizing the gradient norm (double backprop) when both are tuned?
4) Robustness and generality:
   - How does AT perform when the teacher is only moderately better than the student, or when architectures differ substantially (e.g., Inception→ResNet)?
   - Does AT help on tasks where spatial attention is crucial (e.g., detection, weakly supervised localization), and can you share preliminary results?
5) Loss placement and definition:
   - What is the impact of using l1 vs l2 normalization or no normalization? Are there cases where Fmax-based attention outperforms Fsum?
   - Do early-layer attention losses ever harm performance due to overconstraining low-level features?

With stronger, broader experiments on ImageNet and clearer evidence that AT provides consistent, non-marginal gains over well-tuned KD and hint baselines, I would be inclined to raise my score. The idea is promising and practically appealing; the current evidence is not yet sufficient at large scale.
