- Proposes transferring spatial "attention" maps (activation- or gradient-based) from teacher to student to improve distillation across datasets and architectures.  
- Decision: Reject due to misleading terminology and organizational problems.  
- Main criticism: The paper misuses "attention"—it actually transfers summed activation statistics, not class-specific discriminative attention maps.  
- Misnomer causes confusion, especially in the "Attention Transfer" section where activation statistics are presented as attention.  
- The gradient-based attention transfer section feels disconnected from the main activation-based method and lacks integration or direct comparison.  
- Notation is unclear in Eq. 2: stated l2 norm appears to imply computing a largest singular value; needs clarification (Frobenius vs spectral norm).  
- Empirical results are strong and consistent, but terminology and organization prevent clear evaluation of novelty.  
- Recommend renaming/describing the method as activation-statistics distillation rather than "attention transfer."  
- Recommend removing or substantially reworking the gradient-based section, or properly integrating and comparing it with the main approach.  
- Recommend explicitly clarifying which matrix/vector norm is used in Eq. 2 and the computational implications.  
- Recommend providing a clearer explanation of why activation statistics are effective for knowledge transfer.  
- Recommend adding comparisons to other activation-based distillation methods for better context.  
- Question: Clarify what specific aspect of "attention" the method captures and how it differs from standard attention mechanisms (e.g., Bahdanau, Xu).  
- Question: Confirm whether Eq. 2 uses the Frobenius norm or another norm and justify the choice.  
- Question: Explain why the gradient-based section was included, how it relates to activation-based transfer, and whether they should be combined.  
- Question: Provide theoretical justification (not just empirical) for the claim that attention maps are more important for transfer than full activations.
