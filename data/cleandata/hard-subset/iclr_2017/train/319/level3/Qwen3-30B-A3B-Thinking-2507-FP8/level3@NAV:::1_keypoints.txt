- Proposes attention transfer: transferring attention maps (activation- and gradient-based) from teacher to student instead of activations.
- Defines two spatial attention map types and shows effectiveness across multiple datasets and architectures.
- Motivation grounded in human attention and prior attention literature; linked to knowledge distillation techniques.
- Claims attention maps contain useful information that improves student performance.
- Recommendation: borderline accept conditional on substantially strengthening ImageNet experiments (Section 4.2.2).
- ImageNet results are marginal (only ~1.1% top-1 improvement) with minimal hyperparameter tuning, weakening the novelty claim.
- Claim to be the first successful knowledge transfer method on ImageNet needs stronger evidence.
- Main weakness: marginal improvement over standard knowledge distillation, especially on large-scale ImageNet.
- Current ImageNet experiments are insufficient to support broad claims about attention transfer on large-scale vision tasks.
- Required experiments: more extensive ImageNet runs with proper hyperparameter tuning and comparisons to state-of-the-art methods.
- Suggested improvements: (1) expand ImageNet experiments and ablations on attention types/locations, (2) compare with recent large-scale KD methods, (3) clarify whether attention transfer is complementary or redundant to standard KD.
- Questions for authors: why did standard KD fail on ImageNet for ResNet-18/34 while attention transfer succeeded?
- Question for authors: what are the computational costs of attention transfer versus standard KD for large-scale tasks?
