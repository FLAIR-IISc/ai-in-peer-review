- Proposed method: attention transfer (AT) — student CNNs learn normalized spatial attention maps (activation- and gradient-based) from teacher networks; integrates with KD and is empirically tested across datasets and architectures.  
- Decision: Reject — core claim of significant improvement over KD lacks sufficient evidence, especially on ImageNet; CIFAR gains are marginal.  
- Problem clarity: Problem is well-posed — leveraging spatial attention (not just logits) for knowledge transfer is meaningful.  
- Motivation: Literature context is good, but theoretical justification for why attention maps should be superior to full activations is weak.  
- CIFAR evidence: Results are convincing — AT consistently outperforms full-activation transfer and complements KD (e.g., 7.93% vs. 8.11% error on CIFAR-10).  
- ImageNet evidence: Inadequate — only ~1.1% top-1 gain, KD fails to converge with CIFAR hyperparameters, and AT is not shown to outperform a properly tuned KD.  
- Claim strength: The “significant improvement” claim is unsupported by ImageNet experiments and marginal CIFAR gains (0.18–0.2% error reduction).  
- Recommended ImageNet work: Provide hyperparameter ablations (β, attention layers), compare to tuned KD, and include losses on all residual blocks.  
- Explain causality: Explicitly justify why attention maps are more transferable than full activations rather than only showing correlation.  
- KD comparison: Quantify AT’s advantage over KD on ImageNet after proper KD tuning to substantiate claims.  
- Questions for authors: Report exact KD hyperparameters used on ImageNet and potential gains from tuning; clarify how ResNet-18+AT (73.3% top-1) compares to best-reported ResNet-18; provide broader experiments supporting attention > activations.  
- Overall verdict: AT is promising and well-executed on CIFAR but needs rigorous, tuned ImageNet validation to be a compelling, significant contribution.
