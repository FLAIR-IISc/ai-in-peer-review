- Proposes "attention transfer" (AT) where a student mimics teacher spatial attention maps derived from activations or gradients.  
- Shows consistent empirical improvements across CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet, outperforming standard KD and full-activation transfer in most settings.  
- Well-motivated by human attention and clearly written with diverse architecture experiments.  
- Rejected due to insufficient novelty: AT is incremental and resembles FitNets' feature-map "hints" without clear differentiation.  
- Lack of rigorous comparison to FitNets using identical teacher-student architecture pairs (e.g., WRN-16-2 → WRN-16-1) to isolate AT’s benefit.  
- Central claim that higher-accuracy networks have stronger attention-object correlation is anecdotal and lacks quantitative metrics (e.g., IoU or correlation coefficients).  
- Hyperparameter β is set heuristically with no ablation showing its impact on performance.  
- Experiments omit critical comparisons: same-architecture teacher→student tests (e.g., ResNet-18→ResNet-18) to determine if AT benefits require architectural disparity.  
- Incorrect or unsupported claim that AT is first to work on ImageNet; prior KD work does apply to ImageNet and this discrepancy is unexplained.  
- No analysis explaining why KD fails on ImageNet in their setup (hyperparameters, architecture, or dataset scale).  
- Table 2 suggests minimal variation across attention mapping functions, raising the question of whether simpler mappings suffice without further ablation.  
- Constructive recommendations: quantify attention-object overlap, perform β ablation, compare directly to FitNets with identical pairs, test same-architecture transfers, and report teacher training/validation loss.  
- Key questions for authors: How is attention-object correlation quantified across teacher accuracies? Why does KD fail on ImageNet here? Can simpler activation-based maps replace complex mappings?
