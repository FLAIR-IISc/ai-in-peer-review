

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge distillation in convolutional neural networks by transferring attention information from a teacher network to a student network. The authors define "attention" as spatial maps derived from either activation statistics (sum of absolute values, sum of powers, max of powers) or gradient-based sensitivity maps. They demonstrate that by minimizing the difference between these attention maps in teacher and student networks, they can significantly improve student network performance across multiple datasets (CIFAR, ImageNet, CUB, Scenes) and architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network). The paper makes several key contributions: (1) proposing attention as a knowledge transfer mechanism, (2) introducing both activation-based and gradient-based attention maps, (3) showing consistent performance improvements across datasets and architectures, and (4) demonstrating that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper's core contribution is interesting but is significantly undermined by misleading terminology and organizational issues that prevent the reader from understanding what is actually being proposed.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The main reason for rejection is that the paper misuses the term "attention" in a way that is misleading to the community. As the key points indicate, the method does not transfer attention maps as understood in the literature (which typically refer to class-specific, discriminative spatial regions), but rather distills summed statistics of activations. This misnomer creates significant confusion throughout the paper, particularly in the "Attention Transfer" section where the authors claim to transfer attention information but are actually transferring activation statistics.

The paper also contains organizational issues that weaken its contribution. The gradient-based attention transfer section feels disconnected from the main contribution (activation-based transfer), with little explanation of why it's included or how it relates to the primary approach. The section neither directly compares with nor combines the main method, adding little value to the paper's core contribution.

Additionally, the paper contains unclear notation (particularly regarding the 2-norm in Eq. 2). The authors claim to use the l2 norm but the description suggests they're computing the largest singular value of Q ∈ R^{H×W}, which would be computationally expensive. The paper should clarify whether they're using the Frobenius norm (which would be more standard and computationally efficient) or something else.

The empirical results are strong and consistently show improvements, but the misleading terminology and poor organization prevent the reader from evaluating the true novelty of the work. The paper would be much stronger if it accurately described the method as a novel activation statistics distillation technique rather than "attention transfer."

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, I recommend the following:

1. Change the title and terminology to accurately reflect the method as a novel activation statistics distillation technique, not "attention transfer." This would eliminate the central confusion in the paper.

2. Remove or substantially rework the gradient-based attention transfer section. If the authors believe this method has value, it should be integrated with the main approach and properly compared. As currently written, it feels like an afterthought.

3. Clarify the norm used in Eq. 2. If it's the Frobenius norm (which is standard for matrix norms), state this explicitly. If it's something else, explain the computational considerations.

4. Provide a clearer explanation of why activation statistics are useful for knowledge transfer, beyond the current vague references to "attention."

5. Consider adding a comparison with other activation-based distillation methods to better contextualize the contribution.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims to transfer "attention maps" but the proposed method actually transfers statistics of activation values. Can you clarify what specific aspect of "attention" the method is capturing, and how this differs from standard attention mechanisms in the literature (e.g., in Bahdanau et al., 2014 or Xu et al., 2015)?

2. In Equation 2, you state you use the l2 norm but the description suggests you're computing the largest singular value of Q. Can you clarify whether you're using the Frobenius norm (which would be standard for vectorized attention maps) or something else, and why?

3. The gradient-based attention transfer section appears disconnected from the main contribution. Can you explain why this section was included, and how it relates to the activation-based approach? Would you expect the two methods to be combined or used in different scenarios?

4. The paper claims that "attention maps carry information that is more important for transfer than full activations" (Section 4.1.2). Can you provide more theoretical justification for this claim, rather than just empirical observations?
