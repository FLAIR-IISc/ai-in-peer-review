

This paper proposes a novel knowledge distillation framework that transfers "attention" from a teacher network to a student network by minimizing differences in spatial attention maps defined through activation statistics. The authors define two types of attention maps: activation-based (using sum, sum-of-powers, or max-of-powers of absolute activations) and gradient-based (using input gradients). They claim that their method consistently improves performance across multiple datasets (CIFAR, ImageNet, Scenes, CUB) and architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network), with activation-based attention transfer outperforming both full-activation transfer and gradient-based methods.

I recommend rejection. The paper's core contribution is a modified knowledge distillation approach that minimizes differences in summed statistics across feature maps, but the misleading use of "attention" terminology fundamentally misrepresents the method's contribution. The paper repeatedly uses "attention" to describe simply distilled activation statistics, which is inconsistent with the established definition of attention mechanisms in neural networks. This misnomer creates significant confusion about the actual contribution, as the method does not involve attention mechanisms but rather a specific form of activation-based distillation.

Furthermore, the gradient-based attention transfer section is poorly integrated with the main contribution. It's neither compared to nor combined with the activation-based method, and the paper doesn't justify why both approaches are presented as equally valid "attention" transfer methods. The lack of clear comparison between these two methods weakens the paper's central claim.

The paper also contains critical technical ambiguities. Equation (2) claims to use an "induced 2-norm" but the description suggests the Frobenius norm (which would be more computationally feasible). The authors state they normalize attention maps by their l2-norm, but it's unclear whether this normalization is applied before or after vectorization. The computational cost of the gradient-based approach is also not properly justified, especially regarding the second backpropagation step.

While the experimental results are promising and the paper is well-written, the misrepresentation of the method as "attention transfer" rather than activation-based distillation is a serious flaw. The paper's title, abstract, and key contributions all overstate the novelty by using "attention" terminology when the method does not involve attention mechanisms. This misrepresentation would lead readers to misunderstand the actual contribution and potentially waste effort trying to apply this to actual attention-based systems.

The paper would be significantly improved by:
1. Changing the title and terminology to accurately reflect the method as an activation-based distillation approach
2. Removing the gradient-based attention transfer section or thoroughly comparing it with the main method
3. Clarifying the norm calculation in Equation (2) and providing justification for the choice of normalization
4. Including a clear discussion of how this method differs from prior work like FitNets, which also uses activation hints

I have several questions for the authors to clarify the contribution:
1. Why do you claim this is "attention transfer" when your method does not involve attention mechanisms but rather activation statistics? What specific connection to attention mechanisms do you believe exists?
2. The paper states "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer" - can you provide a theoretical justification for why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations?
3. In Equation (2), you claim to use an "induced 2-norm" but describe vectorized attention maps. Please clarify whether this refers to the Frobenius norm (which would be standard for vectorized matrices) or something else.
4. The gradient-based section is presented as a separate approach but never combined with the activation-based method. Why not explore hybrid approaches that might yield better results?

The paper presents an interesting distillation technique, but the misleading terminology and organizational issues prevent me from fully assessing its contribution. The authors should clarify the actual method and its relationship to existing knowledge distillation work before this paper can be considered for publication. The current formulation risks confusing readers about the nature of attention mechanisms in deep learning.
