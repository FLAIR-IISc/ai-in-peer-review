

# Review of "Attention Transfer" (ICLR 2017)

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel knowledge transfer method that transfers attention maps from a teacher network to a student network, rather than transferring activations as in standard knowledge distillation. The authors define two types of spatial attention maps (activation-based and gradient-based) and develop methods to transfer these maps between networks. They claim consistent improvements across multiple datasets and architectures, including ImageNet, with the key innovation being the use of attention information instead of activation values for knowledge transfer. The paper presents a comprehensive literature review of attention mechanisms and knowledge distillation, and provides theoretical motivation for their approach.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper should be rejected because the experimental results show only marginal improvements over standard knowledge distillation, and the ImageNet experiments are insufficiently comprehensive to support the claimed significance.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's key contribution is the idea of transferring attention maps rather than activations. While this is conceptually interesting and well-motivated by the literature on attention in both human and artificial vision, the empirical results fail to demonstrate sufficient significance to justify the method's complexity.

The most critical issue is the marginal performance gains reported. For ImageNet, the authors report only 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement (with minimal hyperparameter tuning) compared to standard knowledge distillation, which has been shown to provide much larger gains in previous work. The paper states "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," but this claim is undermined by the minimal performance improvement. If the authors had achieved significantly larger gains, this would be a substantial contribution, but the marginal results do not justify the paper's claims.

The ImageNet section (4.2.2) requires particularly extensive experiments. The authors admit they "did not have time to tune any hyperparameters and kept them from finetuning experiments" and only added "two losses in the 2 last groups of residual blocks." This is insufficient for a major dataset like ImageNet, where state-of-the-art results typically require careful hyperparameter tuning and comprehensive experimental validation.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
The paper would benefit significantly from:

1. More extensive ImageNet experiments - the authors should include results with all four residual block groups, proper hyperparameter tuning, and comparison to other state-of-the-art knowledge distillation methods on ImageNet. This would address the most significant weakness of the paper.

2. A more thorough analysis of why attention transfer works better than activation transfer. The paper shows attention transfer provides better results than transferring full activations, but the explanation is not convincing. The authors should provide deeper insights into what specific information in attention maps is more valuable for transfer than in full activations.

3. Better contextualization of the marginal improvements within the literature. The paper should clearly state how these marginal gains compare to other knowledge distillation approaches and explain why these small improvements are significant enough to warrant a new method.

4. A clearer explanation of the computational cost of attention transfer compared to standard knowledge distillation, which would help readers assess the practical value of the method.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- How do the authors explain the marginal improvements over standard knowledge distillation, given that attention transfer is more complex and computationally expensive? What specific advantages does attention transfer provide that justify this complexity?
- If the authors had properly tuned hyperparameters for ImageNet (beyond the limited experiments reported), what improvements might they expect to see? Would these improvements be large enough to justify the paper's claims?
- The paper claims "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," but the results are marginal. How do the authors reconcile this claim with the lack of substantial performance gains compared to other methods? Are there specific scenarios where attention transfer would provide significantly larger gains than standard knowledge distillation?
