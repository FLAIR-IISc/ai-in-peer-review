

This paper proposes "attention transfer" (AT), a knowledge distillation method where a student CNN learns to mimic the spatial attention maps of a teacher network, defined via activation-based (sum/max of |features|^p) or gradient-based (input sensitivity) maps. The authors demonstrate consistent performance improvements across CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet datasets, showing AT outperforms standard knowledge distillation (KD) and full-activation transfer in most settings. The work is well-motivated by human attention mechanisms and clearly articulated, with strong empirical validation on diverse architectures.

Reject. The paper fails to demonstrate sufficient novelty relative to prior work (e.g., FitNets), and key claims lack scientific rigor due to incomplete experimental analysis.

The primary reasons for rejection are: (1) The novelty of AT as a knowledge transfer method is incremental, as it resembles FitNets' "hints" but replaces feature maps with attention maps without establishing distinct advantages; (2) Critical claims about attention maps' utility are unsubstantiated by quantitative evidence, and experimental design omits essential comparisons.

Supporting arguments:  
First, the paper's core contribution—using attention maps for knowledge transfer—is not sufficiently differentiated from existing distillation techniques. FitNets (Romero et al., 2014) already transfers feature maps via L2 losses, and AT merely substitutes these with attention maps (which are derived from the same features). The authors claim AT "gives better improvements than full-activation transfer" (Table 1), but fail to compare AT against FitNets *with the same architecture* (e.g., WRN-16-2 → WRN-16-1), which would isolate AT's benefit. Without this, it remains unclear whether AT’s gains stem from the attention abstraction or simply from distillation itself.  

Second, the paper’s central claim—that higher-accuracy networks exhibit stronger spatial attention-object correlation (Fig. 4)—lacks quantitative support. While visual examples suggest attention aligns with objects, the authors do not provide metrics (e.g., attention-object IoU, correlation coefficients) to prove this correlation *increases* with teacher accuracy. This weakens the foundational motivation for AT. Additionally, the hyperparameter β (Eq. 2) is set heuristically ("usually varies about 0.1"), and its impact on performance is unanalyzed. The authors report β=10³/(elements×batch size) but omit ablation studies showing how β selection affects results.  

The experimental design also omits critical comparisons: (1) AT’s performance when student and teacher share identical architecture (e.g., ResNet-18 → ResNet-18) is never tested, making it impossible to determine if AT’s gains are due to knowledge transfer or inherent model differences. (2) The paper claims AT "is the first knowledge transfer method successfully applied on ImageNet" (Section 4.2.2), but KD *does* work on ImageNet (e.g., Hinton et al., 2015; Zagoruyko & Komodakis, 2016), and the authors do not address why KD fails here (e.g., hyperparameter sensitivity vs. AT).  

Constructive feedback to improve the paper:  
1. Quantify the attention-object correlation claim: Compute metrics like attention-object overlap (e.g., IoU) for teacher networks of varying accuracy on ImageNet.  
2. Conduct ablation on β: Show how β values impact performance across datasets and architectures.  
3. Compare AT to FitNets *with identical student/teacher pairs* (e.g., WRN-16-2 → WRN-16-1) to isolate AT’s contribution.  
4. Test AT on same-architecture student-teacher pairs to clarify whether AT’s gains require architectural disparity.  
5. Report teacher training/validation loss in Fig. 7b to contextualize student convergence.  

Key questions for authors:  
- How do you quantify the "stronger attention-object correlation" in higher-accuracy networks? Without metrics, the claim remains anecdotal.  
- Why does KD fail on ImageNet with ResNet-18/34 but succeed on CIFAR? Is this due to architectural differences, hyperparameters, or dataset scale?  
- Could activation-based AT be replaced by a simpler attention map (e.g., F²sum) without additional ablation, given Table 2 shows minimal variation across mapping functions?  

While the paper presents well-executed experiments with consistent student improvements, its incremental novelty and unresolved methodological gaps prevent it from making a compelling contribution to the field. The authors should address these gaps before the work can be considered for publication.
