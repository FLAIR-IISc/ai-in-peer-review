- Paper proposes a knowledge distillation method that transfers "attention" by matching spatial attention maps computed from activation statistics.
- Two attention-map types are defined: activation-based (sum, sum-of-powers, max-of-powers of absolute activations) and gradient-based (input gradients).
- Authors report consistent improvements across CIFAR, ImageNet, Scenes, CUB and across architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, NiN), with activation-based transfer outperforming full-activation and gradient-based methods.
- Reviewer recommends rejection.
- Core critique: the term "attention" is misleading—method is activation-statistic distillation, not an attention mechanism.
- Misnomer risks confusing readers and overstates novelty in title, abstract, and contributions.
- Gradient-based attention-transfer section is poorly integrated, never compared or combined with activation-based method, weakening the central claim.
- Technical ambiguity in Equation (2): "induced 2-norm" vs. likely Frobenius norm is unclear.
- Unclear whether l2-normalization of attention maps is applied before or after vectorization.
- Computational cost and justification of the gradient-based approach (second backpropagation) are not addressed.
- Experimental results are promising and the paper is generally well-written despite conceptual issues.
- Suggested fix 1: change title/terminology to reflect activation-based distillation rather than "attention transfer."
- Suggested fix 2: remove the gradient-based section or provide thorough comparisons/hybrid experiments with the activation-based method.
- Suggested fix 3: clarify the norm calculation in Eq(2) and justify the normalization choice and ordering.
- Suggested fix 4: include explicit discussion of differences from prior activation-hint works (e.g., FitNets).
- Questions for authors: why label this "attention transfer" and what specific connection to attention mechanisms exists?
- Question: why should attention maps be more transferable than full activations—any theoretical justification?
- Question: explicitly clarify whether Eq(2) uses an induced 2-norm or Frobenius norm.
- Question: why not explore hybrid activation+gradient approaches that might improve results?
- Overall: interesting distillation technique but misleading terminology and organizational/technical ambiguities prevent proper assessment and need resolution before publication.
