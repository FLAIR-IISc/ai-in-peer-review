

This paper proposes a novel knowledge transfer method that focuses on transferring attention maps rather than activations from a teacher to a student network. The authors define two types of spatial attention maps (activation-based and gradient-based) and demonstrate their effectiveness across multiple datasets and architectures. The contributions are well-motivated by human attention mechanisms and the literature on attention in neural networks, with a strong connection to knowledge distillation techniques. The paper makes a compelling case for why attention maps contain valuable information that can improve student network performance.

I recommend a borderline acceptance with the condition that the authors substantially strengthen Section 4.2.2 (ImageNet experiments). While the paper shows consistent improvements on CIFAR and smaller datasets, the ImageNet results are marginal (only 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement) with minimal hyperparameter tuning, which significantly undermines the novelty claim. The paper claims to be the first knowledge transfer method successfully applied on ImageNet, but this assertion requires stronger evidence.

The main weakness is the marginal improvement over standard knowledge distillation, particularly on ImageNet where the gains are modest. The experiments in Section 4.2.2 are insufficient to support the paper's broader claims about the significance of attention transfer for large-scale vision tasks. The authors should conduct more extensive ImageNet experiments with proper hyperparameter tuning and comparison to state-of-the-art methods.

To improve the paper, I recommend:
1. Expanding Section 4.2.2 with comprehensive ImageNet experiments including ablation studies on different attention map types and transfer locations.
2. Providing a more thorough comparison with recent knowledge distillation approaches specifically designed for large-scale vision tasks.
3. Clarifying the relationship between attention transfer and knowledge distillation - does attention transfer truly provide complementary information, or is it redundant with standard KD?

I have two questions for the authors: First, why did standard knowledge distillation fail to work on ImageNet with ResNet-18/ResNet-34 pairs while attention transfer succeeded? Second, what are the computational costs of attention transfer compared to standard KD, especially for large-scale vision tasks? These would help clarify the practical significance of the proposed method.
