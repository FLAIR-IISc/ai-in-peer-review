

# Review of "Attention Transfer" (ICLR 2017)

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel knowledge distillation framework that transfers spatial attention information from a teacher network to a student network. The authors define "attention" as statistics of feature activations (sum of absolute values, sum of powers, or max of powers) rather than actual attention mechanisms. They claim this approach outperforms FitNet-style distillation by having the student network mimic these statistics across feature maps. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple computer vision datasets, including CIFAR-10, CUB-200, Scenes, and ImageNet. The authors also introduce a gradient-based attention transfer method as a secondary contribution.

## 2. Decision
Reject. While the method shows empirical improvements over existing approaches, the paper's misleading terminology and unclear methodology significantly undermine its contribution.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's core issue is the misleading use of the term "attention." The authors explicitly state they're not using attention mechanisms but rather statistics of feature activations (Section 3.1). Calling this "attention transfer" is highly misleading, as attention mechanisms typically refer to methods that dynamically weight different parts of the input (e.g., in Transformer models or spatial attention mechanisms). This misrepresentation creates significant confusion about the paper's actual contribution.

The gradient-based attention transfer section (Section 3.2) feels like an afterthought rather than an integral part of the main contribution. It's neither compared with nor combined with the main method, adding little value to the paper. The authors could have either integrated this with their main contribution or removed it entirely.

Equation (2) contains a critical ambiguity: the authors claim to use a "2-norm" but it's unclear how this is computed. The notation suggests they're using the largest singular value of Q ∈ R^{H×W}, which would be computationally expensive (O(HW^2)). The authors likely meant the Frobenius norm, which would be more reasonable. This ambiguity makes it difficult to properly evaluate their approach.

The paper's limitation to convolutional architectures is not adequately addressed in the context of the broader literature, especially given the growing popularity of non-convolutional architectures like Transformers. This limits the paper's potential impact.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
- **Title and Terminology:** The title and abstract should be revised to accurately reflect the method. "Summed Feature Statistics Transfer" or "Activation-Based Knowledge Transfer" would be more appropriate than "Attention Transfer."
- **Clarify Methodology:** The paper should explicitly distinguish between their approach and actual attention mechanisms used in the literature (e.g., in Transformer models, or in visual attention papers like Grad-CAM).
- **Equation Clarification:** Equation (2) needs to be clarified to specify whether it's using Frobenius norm or another norm. The computational cost of the normalization step should be addressed.
- **Integration of Gradient-Based Approach:** The gradient-based attention transfer section should either be removed or integrated with the main contribution to provide a cohesive method.
- **Limitations Discussion:** The paper should more clearly address the limitations of the method, particularly its applicability only to convolutional architectures.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. Could you clarify how the normalization in Equation (2) is computed? Is it the Frobenius norm, or is there a different interpretation that makes the computation efficient?
2. Given that your method doesn't transfer actual attention mechanisms but rather statistics of activations, why did you choose to call it "attention transfer" in the title and abstract?
3. How would you expect this method to perform on non-convolutional architectures, such as Transformer-based networks?
4. Why was the gradient-based attention transfer method included as a separate approach rather than being integrated with the main activation-based method? Did you attempt to combine both approaches but found they didn't work well together?
5. The paper claims this is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet. Could you elaborate on why other methods (like FitNets or KD) wouldn't work on ImageNet, given that they've been applied to similar tasks in the literature?
