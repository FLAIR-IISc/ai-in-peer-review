- Proposes Attention Transfer (AT): minimize L2 between teacher and student attention maps (activation- and gradient-based) combined with classification loss and optional KD; shows improvements on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, ImageNet.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions — meaningful contribution with consistent improvements but lacking deeper hyperparameter analysis and comparisons.  
- Strong motivation and positioning versus KD and FitNets; well-written and conceptually sound.  
- Experimental results are convincing across datasets and architectures; visualizations (Fig.4) are compelling.  
- Missing quantitative evidence for the claim that higher-accuracy networks have better spatial correlation between object and attention maps.  
- No systematic analysis of the β hyperparameter (weight of AT loss); only anecdotal “about 0.1” provided.  
- Recommend adding quantitative metrics (e.g., spatial correlation or localization accuracy) to support Fig.4.  
- Recommend ablation studies showing performance as a function of β across datasets and architectures.  
- Suggest adding teacher training/validation loss to Fig.7b to compare student dynamics properly.  
- Ask whether AT helps when student and teacher share the same architecture to isolate AT’s effect.  
- Ask for guidance on choosing attention map types (sum vs. max, activation- vs. gradient-based) and why sum-based works better.  
- Recommend clarifying computational benefits of AT students (inference time, memory) compared to teachers.  
- Question: provide quantitative metrics supporting the attention-quality correlation claim.  
- Question: how does AT performance vary with different β values across datasets/architectures?  
- Question: clarify the claim that AT is the first knowledge transfer method successfully applied on ImageNet and compare to other methods.
