

**Review for ICLR 2017**  

**1. Summary of Contributions**  
This paper proposes a novel knowledge transfer method—*attention transfer*—where a student convolutional neural network (CNN) learns to mimic the spatial attention maps of a teacher network, rather than directly transferring activations or logits. The authors define two types of attention maps (activation-based and gradient-based), demonstrate their correlation with network performance, and show consistent improvements across multiple datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes) and architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, NIN). Key innovations include the use of normalized attention maps for training, integration with knowledge distillation (KD), and empirical validation that attention maps carry more transferable information than full activations. The approach is well-motivated by the role of attention in human perception and recent advances in attention visualization (e.g., Grad-CAM).  

**2. Decision**  
**Reject**. The paper’s core claim—that attention transfer provides *significant* improvements over standard KD—lacks sufficient evidence, particularly on large-scale ImageNet. The marginal gains on CIFAR (e.g., 0.18% error reduction over KD) and the underdeveloped ImageNet results undermine the contribution’s impact.  

**3. Supporting Arguments**  
- **Question 1 (Problem)**: The paper clearly identifies the problem: leveraging *spatial attention information* (not just output logits) to improve student network performance. This is a meaningful extension of knowledge distillation.  
- **Question 2 (Motivation)**: The literature review contextualizes the work well (citing KD, attention visualization, and residual networks). However, the *justification for why attention maps are superior to full activations* (beyond empirical results) is weak. The claim that attention maps "contain valuable information" is not rigorously grounded in theory.  
- **Question 3 (Evidence)**:  
  - *CIFAR results* (Section 4.1) are convincing: attention transfer (AT) consistently outperforms full-activation transfer (e.g., 7.93% vs. 8.11% error on CIFAR-10) and adds value to KD.  
  - *ImageNet results* (Section 4.2.2) are **inadequate**. The paper claims AT is "the first knowledge transfer method successfully applied on ImageNet," but the results are underwhelming:  
    - Only 1.1% top-1 gain (ResNet-18 with AT vs. baseline) with minimal hyperparameter tuning.  
    - KD *fails to converge* on ImageNet with the same hyperparameters used on CIFAR (Table 5), yet the authors do not demonstrate that AT *solves* this limitation (e.g., by tuning KD hyperparameters for ImageNet).  
    - The comparison to KD is superficial; the paper does not show AT *outperforms KD* on ImageNet (only that AT works while KD fails without tuning).  
  - The key claim of "significant improvement" is **not supported** by the ImageNet experiments, which are the most critical test for a large-scale vision paper. The marginal gains on CIFAR (0.18–0.2% error reduction) also fail to justify the novelty over existing KD.  

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**  
- **Strengthen ImageNet validation**: Extend Section 4.2.2 to include:  
  (a) Ablation of hyperparameters (e.g., β, attention layers) on ImageNet,  
  (b) Comparison against *tuned KD* (e.g., using the same hyperparameters as in Hinton et al. 2015) to isolate AT’s contribution.  
  (c) Results with losses on *all four residual blocks* (not just two) as mentioned in the paper.  
- **Clarify the "why"**: Explicitly explain *why* attention maps are more transferable than full activations. The paper shows attention maps correlate with performance (Fig. 4), but does not prove this is the *cause* of improved transfer.  
- **Address the KD comparison**: Quantify AT’s advantage over KD on ImageNet (e.g., "AT achieves X% top-1 accuracy vs. KD’s Y% after hyperparameter tuning"). Without this, the claim that AT is "the first successful transfer method on ImageNet" is unsubstantiated.  

**5. Questions for Authors**  
1. The paper states KD "hurts convergence" on ImageNet with CIFAR hyperparameters (Section 4.2.2). Could you share the exact hyperparameters used for KD on ImageNet? How much improvement would tuning these parameters yield?  
2. In Table 5, ResNet-18+AT achieves 73.3% top-1 accuracy. How does this compare to the best-reported ResNet-18 accuracy on ImageNet (e.g., 73.6% in He et al. 2015)? If AT does not surpass the standard ResNet-18 baseline, its contribution is minimal.  
3. The claim that "attention maps carry more important information than full activations" (Section 4.1.2) is based on a single experiment (Fig. 7b). Could you provide a broader analysis (e.g., on multiple datasets/architectures) to confirm this is a general phenomenon?  

**Final Note**  
The attention transfer concept is promising and well-executed for CIFAR, but the paper’s strongest claim hinges on ImageNet results that are currently underdeveloped. Addressing the above points—especially rigorous ImageNet validation—would transform this into a compelling contribution. As submitted, the evidence does not justify the significance of the work for ICLR.
