- Paper proposes distillation of "attention" by matching summed statistics (e.g., sum of squared activations) across feature maps.
- Authors claim consistent accuracy improvements over FitNet-style distillation on CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet.
- Paper is well-written with thorough experiments, but reviewer recommends rejection.
- Core objection: the method misrepresents "attention" — it distills aggregated activation statistics, not spatial attention maps as commonly understood.
- Misleading terminology fundamentally mischaracterizes the technical contribution and could mislead the community.
- Gradient-based attention transfer section appears tacked on and not clearly integrated or compared to the main method.
- Technical issue: Equation (2) ambiguously defines "induced 2-norm"; authors likely meant Frobenius norm, but this undermines clarity.
- Empirical results are strong, but the conceptual misalignment is considered a fundamental flaw.
- Suggested fix 1: Rename the approach to reflect the technique (e.g., "summed statistics distillation" or "activation statistics transfer").
- Suggested fix 2: Remove misleading "attention" terminology throughout the paper.
- Suggested fix 3: Clarify Equation (2) by explicitly defining the norm as Frobenius (if intended).
- Suggested fix 4: Either integrate the gradient-based section properly or remove it for conceptual coherence.
- Reviewer question: Why use the term "attention" when the method does not produce/transfer spatial attention maps?
- Reviewer question: What evidence shows improvements are due to "attention" rather than general distillation of feature statistics?
