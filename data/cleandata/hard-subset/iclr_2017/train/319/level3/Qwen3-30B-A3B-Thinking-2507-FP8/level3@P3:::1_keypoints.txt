- Proposes transferring attention maps (activation- and gradient-based) from teacher to student instead of full activations; includes literature review and theoretical motivation.  
- Decision: Reject — empirical results are marginal and ImageNet experiments are insufficient.  
- Key critique: Reported gains are small (∼1.1% top-1 on ImageNet) and do not justify the method's added complexity.  
- ImageNet experiments lack comprehensive evaluation and hyperparameter tuning; losses were only added to the last two residual block groups.  
- The paper's claim that activation-based AT is the first KD method successfully applied to ImageNet is undermined by minimal improvement.  
- Recommend more extensive ImageNet experiments: apply AT to all residual groups, perform proper hyperparameter tuning, and compare to state-of-the-art KD methods.  
- Recommend deeper empirical/analytical investigation into why attention maps transfer more useful information than full activations.  
- Recommend better contextualization of marginal improvements relative to prior KD literature and clearer justification of significance.  
- Recommend reporting and comparing computational cost of attention transfer versus standard knowledge distillation.  
- Questions for authors: Why are improvements so marginal despite higher complexity/cost? What improvements would full tuning on ImageNet yield? In what scenarios would AT give substantially larger gains?
