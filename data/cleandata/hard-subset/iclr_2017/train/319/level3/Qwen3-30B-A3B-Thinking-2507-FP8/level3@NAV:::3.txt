

The paper proposes attention transfer (AT) as a novel method for knowledge distillation, where a student network learns to mimic the attention maps of a teacher network. The authors define two types of attention maps—activation-based (sum/max of |features|^p) and gradient-based (∂Loss/∂input)—and demonstrate consistent performance improvements across CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet datasets. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and addresses an important aspect of knowledge distillation by focusing on spatial attention rather than just feature representations.

I recommend **weak accept**. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of knowledge distillation by introducing attention transfer as a complementary technique to existing methods like FitNets. The empirical results consistently show that AT improves student performance over baseline, and the visualizations in Figure 4 effectively illustrate the relationship between network accuracy and attention map quality.

The main reasons for this decision are: (1) the paper demonstrates clear, consistent improvements across multiple datasets and architectures, and (2) the approach is well-placed in the literature with strong connections to prior work on attention mechanisms and knowledge distillation.

However, the paper has several limitations that prevent a stronger recommendation. The claim about higher-accuracy networks having higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps is visually supported but lacks quantitative evidence. The impact of the AT weight β is not properly analyzed, and the paper fails to characterize the speed-up benefits of using smaller student networks. Additionally, the paper does not clarify when to use different attention map types or provide guidance on selecting the most effective approach for specific scenarios. The authors should also report teacher training/validation loss in Figure 7b for proper comparison.

To improve the paper, I recommend: (1) providing quantitative evidence for the attention-accuracy correlation claim, (2) analyzing the impact of β and other hyperparameters, (3) characterizing the speed-up benefits of the student network, and (4) clarifying the relative advantages of different attention map types. The authors should also investigate whether AT helps when student and teacher share the same architecture, as this would better establish the value of their approach.
