- Proposal: attention transfer (AT) for knowledge distillation where a student learns to mimic teacher attention maps.  
- Defines two attention map types: activation-based (sum/max of |features|^p) and gradient-based (∂Loss/∂input).  
- Empirical results show consistent performance improvements across CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet.  
- Paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and emphasizes spatial attention rather than only feature representations.  
- Recommendation: weak accept.  
- Contribution: AT complements existing methods (e.g., FitNets) and consistently improves student performance.  
- Visualizations (Figure 4) effectively illustrate the link between network accuracy and attention map quality.  
- Weakness: the claim that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial attention-object correlation is only visually supported and lacks quantitative evidence.  
- Weakness: impact of the AT weight β and other hyperparameters is not properly analyzed.  
- Weakness: the paper does not characterize the speed-up or computational benefits of using smaller student networks.  
- Weakness: no clear guidance on when to use different attention map types or how to choose between them.  
- Missing comparison: teacher training/validation loss should be reported in Figure 7b for proper comparison.  
- Recommended improvements: provide quantitative attention-accuracy evidence, analyze β/hyperparameters, quantify student speed-up, clarify attention-map tradeoffs, and test AT when student and teacher share architecture.
