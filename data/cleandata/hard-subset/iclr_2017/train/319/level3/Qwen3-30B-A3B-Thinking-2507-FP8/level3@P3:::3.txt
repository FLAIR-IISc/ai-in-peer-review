

# Review of "Attention Transfer" (ICLR 2017)

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention maps. The authors propose attention transfer (AT), which minimizes the L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps while optimizing classification loss and optionally incorporating knowledge distillation (KD). The paper introduces two types of attention maps: activation-based (sum/max of |features|^p) and gradient-based (∂Loss/∂input). Through extensive experiments on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet, the authors demonstrate consistent performance improvements across various architectures and datasets. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and provides a compelling case for attention as a valuable knowledge transfer mechanism.

## 2. Decision

Accept with minor revisions. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of knowledge distillation with a clear, well-motivated approach that consistently improves student performance across multiple benchmarks. The main limitations are related to the lack of deeper analysis of hyperparameters and insufficient comparison with alternative approaches.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper effectively addresses the question of how attention information can be transferred from a teacher to a student network. The motivation is strong, drawing on both human visual perception and the growing literature on attention mechanisms in deep learning. The authors correctly position their work against knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) and FitNets (Romero et al., 2014), arguing that attention maps contain information beyond what's captured by feature maps alone.

The experimental results are largely convincing, showing consistent improvements across multiple datasets and architectures. The attention transfer approach outperforms baseline student networks and often surpasses knowledge distillation alone. The visualizations in Figure 4 compellingly demonstrate the relationship between attention maps and network performance.

However, the paper falls short in two key areas: First, it lacks quantitative evidence for the claim that "higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps" (Figure 4). While the visual comparison is suggestive, the paper should provide metrics like correlation coefficients or attention localization accuracy to support this claim. Second, the hyperparameter β (the weight of attention transfer loss) is not systematically analyzed. The paper mentions β is "usually about 0.1" but doesn't explore how varying β affects performance or what the optimal value might be for different architectures.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, I recommend the following revisions:

1. Provide quantitative evidence for the attention-quality correlation claim in Figure 4. Calculate metrics like spatial correlation coefficients between attention maps and object locations for different network architectures.

2. Conduct a systematic analysis of the β hyperparameter. Include ablation studies showing performance as a function of β across different datasets and architectures.

3. Add teacher training and validation loss to Figure 7b for proper comparison with student training dynamics.

4. Address the question of whether AT provides benefits when student and teacher share the same architecture. This would help clarify if AT's benefits are due to the attention transfer mechanism itself or simply because the student is learning from a more accurate teacher.

5. Provide more guidance on when to use different attention map types (sum vs. max, activation-based vs. gradient-based). The paper mentions "sum-based functions work very similar, and better than max-based ones" but doesn't explain why or when to prefer one over the other.

6. Clarify the speed-up benefits of AT. Since students have fewer parameters, the paper should characterize the computational benefits of the student model (inference time, memory usage) compared to the teacher.

## Questions for Authors

1. Could you provide quantitative metrics (e.g., correlation coefficients) supporting the claim that higher-accuracy networks have better attention maps? This would strengthen the visual evidence in Figure 4.

2. How does the performance of AT vary with different values of β across different datasets and architectures? The paper mentions β is "usually about 0.1" but doesn't justify this choice or explore its impact.

3. In the ImageNet experiments, the paper claims AT is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," but doesn't compare with other knowledge transfer methods that might have been applied to ImageNet. Could you clarify this claim?

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to knowledge distillation with a clear, well-motivated approach that consistently improves student performance. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent addition to the ICLR proceedings.
