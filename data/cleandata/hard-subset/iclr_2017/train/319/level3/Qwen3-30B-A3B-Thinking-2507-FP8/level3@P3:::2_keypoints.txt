- Proposes knowledge distillation that transfers spatial "attention" defined as statistics of feature activations (sum of absolutes, sums/max of powers) rather than actual attention mechanisms.
- Claims student networks mimicking these activation statistics outperform FitNet-style distillation.
- Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements on CIFAR-10, CUB-200, Scenes, and ImageNet.
- Introduces a gradient-based attention transfer method as a secondary contribution.
- Decision: Reject due to misleading terminology and unclear methodology.
- Core criticism: calling activation statistics "attention" is misleading compared to dynamic attention mechanisms (e.g., Transformers, spatial attention).
- Gradient-based transfer reads like an afterthought, not integrated with or compared to the main method.
- Equation (2) is ambiguous about the "2-norm" (unclear if Frobenius norm or largest singular value), raising computational concerns.
- Paper inadequately addresses limitation to convolutional architectures amid the rise of non-convolutional models like Transformers.
- Recommend renaming (e.g., "Summed Feature Statistics Transfer" or "Activation-Based Knowledge Transfer") to avoid confusion.
- Authors should explicitly distinguish their method from true attention mechanisms (e.g., Transformer attention, Grad-CAM).
- Need to clarify Equation (2) normalization and its computational cost.
- Either integrate the gradient-based approach with the main method or remove it for cohesion.
- Paper should more clearly discuss limitations and applicability beyond CNNs.
- Questions posed: clarify normalization in Eq.(2); explain choice of the term "attention transfer"; expected performance on non-CNNs like Transformers; why gradient-based method is separate and whether combination was attempted; justify claim of being first to apply knowledge transfer on ImageNet.
