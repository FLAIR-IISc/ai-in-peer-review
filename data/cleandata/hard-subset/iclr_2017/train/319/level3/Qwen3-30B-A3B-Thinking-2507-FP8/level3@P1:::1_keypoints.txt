- Paper proposes "attention transfer" for CNN knowledge distillation: student mimics teacher attention maps instead of full activations.
- Two attention map types introduced: activation-based (sum of abs, sum of powers, max of powers) and gradient-based.
- Authors show effectiveness across multiple datasets and architectures, arguing attention maps encode spatial importance useful for distillation.
- Reviewer recommends rejection primarily because improvements over standard KD are marginal in most experiments.
- Second rejection reason: ImageNet experiments are shallow and not convincing for broad applicability.
- Empirical gains are small: CIFAR-10 AT+KD 11.2% vs KD 12.1% test error and ablation shows modest reductions (e.g., 8.77%→7.93%).
- Claim that attention maps transfer more than activations is supported qualitatively (Fig. 7b) but absolute gains remain modest.
- ImageNet results are limited: only 1.1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18 vs ResNet-34 and lack comparisons to other distillation methods.
- Authors admit limited hyperparameter tuning on ImageNet and provide insufficient ablation across layers and architectures.
- Paper lacks theoretical explanation for why attention maps are more transferable than activations.
- Gradient-based attention outperforming with symmetry constraints is noted but lacks deeper analysis.
- Suggested improvements: more extensive ImageNet experiments with hyperparameter tuning and ablations.
- Suggested improvements: compare against other recent distillation methods on ImageNet (e.g., Zagoruyko & Komodakis).
- Reviewer asks clarifying questions on why CIFAR gains are marginal, scalability to larger ResNets, the claim of being "first" on ImageNet, and required architectural/training modifications for ImageNet success.
