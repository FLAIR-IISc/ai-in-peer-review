

The paper introduces "attention transfer" as a novel knowledge distillation method for convolutional neural networks, where the student network is trained to mimic the attention maps of a teacher network rather than directly transferring activations. The authors propose two types of attention maps—activation-based (using sum of absolute values, sum of powers, and max of powers) and gradient-based—and demonstrate their effectiveness across multiple datasets and architectures. The key insight is that attention maps, which highlight spatial regions of importance for classification, contain valuable information that can improve student network performance beyond standard knowledge distillation.

I recommend rejection, primarily due to two reasons: (1) the improvements over standard knowledge distillation are marginal across most experiments, and (2) the ImageNet experiments lack sufficient depth and scale to validate the approach's broader applicability.

The paper's main contribution is conceptually interesting, but the empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate significant advantages. On CIFAR-10, attention transfer (AT) combined with knowledge distillation (KD) achieves slightly better results than KD alone (11.2% vs. 12.1% test error in Table 3), but the difference is minimal. The ablation study in Table 2 shows that activation-based attention transfer (using F^2_sum) reduces error from 8.77% to 7.93%, but this improvement is comparable to standard KD on the same datasets. The claim that attention maps carry more transferable information than full activations is supported by Figure 7(b), but the absolute gains remain modest.

The most concerning issue is the limited scope of the ImageNet experiments (Section 4.2.2). The authors report only a 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement for ResNet-18 with attention transfer compared to ResNet-34, which is marginal given the scale of ImageNet. The paper claims this is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," but the evidence is weak—there's no comparison to other state-of-the-art distillation methods, and the authors acknowledge they "did not have time to tune hyperparameters." The justification for why KD fails on ImageNet (while working on CIFAR) is insufficient, and the paper lacks ablation studies across different attention transfer layers or architectures on ImageNet.

Additional feedback for improvement: The paper would be significantly strengthened by (1) more extensive ImageNet experiments with thorough hyperparameter tuning, (2) comparison against other recent distillation methods on ImageNet (e.g., [Zagoruyko and Komodakis, 2016]), and (3) a clearer theoretical explanation for why attention maps contain more transferable information than activations. The authors should also clarify why gradient-based attention transfer performs better with symmetry constraints (Section 4.1.3), as this is an important finding but lacks deeper analysis.

To clarify the contribution's significance, I ask: 1) Why does attention transfer yield marginal gains over KD on CIFAR but show more promise on ImageNet (where the gains are still modest)? 2) How does the proposed method scale to larger architectures like ResNet-50 or ResNet-101, which are standard for ImageNet? 3) Why does the paper claim attention transfer is "the first" method to work on ImageNet when KD has been successfully applied to ImageNet (e.g., [Hinton et al., 2015])? 4) What specific architectural or training modifications are required to make attention transfer effective on ImageNet, and how do they differ from standard KD? Addressing these questions would provide stronger evidence for the method's novelty and practical utility.
