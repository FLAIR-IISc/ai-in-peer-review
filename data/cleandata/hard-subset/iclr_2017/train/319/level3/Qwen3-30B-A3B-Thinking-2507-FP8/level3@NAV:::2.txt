

**Review**

The paper proposes a knowledge distillation framework that transfers "attention" information from a teacher to a student network by minimizing differences in summed statistics (e.g., sum of squared activations) across feature maps. The authors claim this approach outperforms FitNet-style distillation across multiple computer vision datasets, with empirical results showing consistent improvements in accuracy. The paper is well-written and presents thorough experiments on CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet datasets.

I recommend **rejecting** this paper primarily because the core contribution misrepresents the concept of "attention." The method simply distills summed (squared or Lp-norm) activations, not attention maps as the title and abstract suggest. This misleading terminology fundamentally mischaracterizes the technical contribution, as attention maps typically refer to spatial importance maps (e.g., Grad-CAM), not aggregated channel statistics. Additionally, the gradient-based attention transfer section appears tacked on without clear integration or comparison to the main method, adding little value.

The technical formulation contains critical issues, notably in Equation (2), where the "induced 2-norm" is ambiguously defined. The authors likely intended the Frobenius norm (not largest singular value), but this confusion undermines the methodological clarity. While the empirical results are strong and the paper is well-organized overall, the conceptual misalignment between the claimed contribution ("attention transfer") and the actual method (activation statistics distillation) constitutes a fundamental flaw that would mislead the community.

To improve the paper, the authors should:
1. Rename the approach to accurately reflect the technique (e.g., "summed statistics distillation" or "activation statistics transfer")
2. Remove the misleading "attention" terminology throughout
3. Clarify Equation (2) by explicitly defining the norm as Frobenius
4. Either integrate or remove the gradient-based section to maintain conceptual coherence

I would like to ask the authors: How do you justify using the term "attention" when your method does not produce or transfer spatial attention maps as commonly understood in computer vision? What specific evidence do you have that the observed improvements are due to "attention" rather than general knowledge distillation of feature statistics?
