

# Review for ICLR_2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention information. The authors define attention as spatial maps indicating where a network focuses during decision-making, and propose two methods for attention transfer: activation-based (using sum/max of |features|^p) and gradient-based (using ∂Loss/∂input). The key contribution is demonstrating that transferring attention maps from a powerful teacher network to a smaller student network consistently improves student performance across multiple datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scene, ImageNet) and architectures, including both residual and non-residual networks. The paper shows that activation-based attention transfer provides better improvements than full-activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

## 2. Decision

**Weak Accept**. The paper makes a solid contribution with consistent empirical results, but the novelty is somewhat incremental, and some key aspects of the approach require further clarification.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-motivated by human attention mechanisms and properly placed within the literature on attention in neural networks and knowledge distillation. The experimental results across multiple datasets and architectures are convincing, showing consistent improvements over baseline student networks. The clear writing and well-structured presentation are significant strengths.

However, the paper has several limitations that prevent a stronger recommendation. First, the novelty is somewhat incremental relative to FitNets, as attention transfer essentially extends the "hints" concept to spatial attention maps rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach. Second, the claim that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between objects and attention maps (Fig. 4) lacks quantitative evidence, relying solely on visual inspection. Third, the paper doesn't adequately address whether attention transfer helps when student and teacher share the same architecture (a more natural comparison for knowledge transfer), which is a significant gap given that students still underperform teachers. Fourth, the hyperparameter selection (particularly β) is unclear and not analyzed, making it difficult to understand how to implement the approach effectively.

The paper also fails to provide guidance on when to use different attention map types (sum vs. max, various p values), which is crucial for practical application. While the authors note that sum-based functions work better than max-based ones, they don't provide a principled explanation for this behavior.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- Provide quantitative metrics to support the claim about higher-accuracy networks having better spatial correlation between objects and attention maps (e.g., correlation coefficients, segmentation overlap metrics).
- Include a thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity, particularly for β, and provide guidelines for its selection based on network architecture and dataset.
- Clarify whether attention transfer provides benefits when student and teacher have identical architectures, and compare results with and without attention transfer in this scenario.
- Extend the experiments to include a comparison with knowledge distillation when student and teacher share the same architecture (not just different architectures).
- In Figure 7b, include teacher training and validation loss for comparison to better understand the training dynamics.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How do you quantify the spatial correlation between object locations and attention maps for networks with different accuracy levels? Can you provide statistical evidence beyond visual inspection?

2. The paper states that β "usually varies about 0.1" but doesn't analyze how performance varies with different β values. Could you provide a sensitivity analysis showing how performance changes with β for different network architectures and datasets?

3. In cases where teacher and student networks have the same architecture (e.g., ResNet-18 as both teacher and student), does attention transfer still provide benefits compared to standard knowledge distillation? If so, why?

4. The paper claims "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer," but doesn't provide a clear explanation for why attention maps contain more useful information for transfer than full activations. Could you elaborate on this insight?

5. Why does knowledge distillation not work well on ImageNet with different architecture teacher-student pairs (as noted in Section 4.2.2), while it works well on CIFAR? Is this a fundamental limitation of knowledge distillation for larger datasets, or is it specific to the implementation choices?
