## Review of "Attention Transfer"

This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer, termed Attention Transfer (AT), by minimizing the L2 distance between attention maps of a teacher and student network, alongside the standard classification loss and an optional knowledge distillation term. The authors define several activation-based and a gradient-based attention mechanisms, and demonstrate consistent performance improvements of the student network across CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet datasets. 

I recommend **Reject** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the paper presents a well-motivated and clearly written approach with consistent improvements in student performance, the claims are not fully supported by rigorous analysis, and the overall contribution feels incremental given related work like FitNets. 

The core idea of transferring attention is sound and the experimental results demonstrate a consistent, positive effect of AT. However, the paper falls short in several key areas. Firstly, while the authors claim improved performance, the student networks consistently underperform the teacher networks, raising the question of the practical benefit of this transfer. A crucial analysis missing is a comparison of AT when the teacher and student share the same architecture – does AT help overcome limitations in training, or is it simply a method for improving smaller models? Secondly, the paper lacks a thorough investigation of the different attention map types. The authors define three activation-based and one gradient-based attention, but provide limited guidance on which to use and under what circumstances. The impact of the AT weight (β) is also not analyzed, hindering reproducibility and practical application. Finally, the claim regarding the correlation between network accuracy and spatial attention map correlation (Fig. 4) is visually supported but lacks quantitative evidence.

To improve this paper, I suggest the following:

*   **Analyze speed-up:** Characterize the computational cost of the student network after AT, and compare it to the teacher.
*   **Ablation study of β:** Conduct a systematic analysis of the impact of the AT weight (β) on performance.
*   **Quantitative validation of Fig. 4:** Provide quantitative metrics to support the claim of higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps in higher-accuracy networks.
*   **Comparison with same architecture:** Evaluate AT when the teacher and student networks have identical architectures.
*   **Figure 7b:** Include teacher training and validation loss in Figure 7b for a more comprehensive comparison.
*   **Guidance on attention map selection:** Provide clear recommendations on which attention map type to use based on the dataset and network architecture.



I would be interested to hear the authors' perspective on the following questions:

*   What is the computational overhead introduced by the attention transfer loss, and how does it scale with network size and dataset complexity?
*   Why does the student network consistently underperform the teacher network, even after attention transfer?
*   What is the intuition behind the observed differences in performance between the different attention map types?
*   Have you explored other distance metrics beyond L2 for comparing attention maps?
