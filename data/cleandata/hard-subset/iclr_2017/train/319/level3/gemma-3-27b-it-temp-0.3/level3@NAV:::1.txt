## Review of "Attention Transfer"

This paper proposes a novel knowledge transfer method leveraging attention maps between a teacher and student convolutional neural network. The core idea – transferring attention rather than activations – is well-motivated by the analogy to human visual attention and the potential for more focused knowledge transfer. The authors explore both activation-based and gradient-based attention maps and demonstrate improvements across several datasets and network architectures. The provided code and models are a valuable contribution to reproducibility.

I recommend **Reject** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the idea is promising, the empirical results, particularly in the ImageNet section, are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance at a competitive conference. The improvements over standard knowledge distillation are marginal, and the claims of significant improvement feel overstated given the presented data.

My decision rests on the limited strength of the empirical evidence. While CIFAR experiments show consistent gains, the ImageNet results (Section 4.2.2) are presented with limited detail and hyperparameter tuning. The statement that this is the “first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet” is a strong claim that requires more robust support. The reported 1.1% top-1 improvement, without a thorough ablation study or comparison to more recent distillation techniques, doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a breakthrough. The authors acknowledge the need for further work in this section, which is encouraging, but the current presentation is insufficient.

To improve the paper, I suggest the following:

*   **Expand Section 4.2.2 significantly.** Conduct a more comprehensive hyperparameter search, compare against state-of-the-art knowledge distillation methods on ImageNet, and provide a more detailed analysis of the results. Include error bars or standard deviations to demonstrate the robustness of the findings.
*   **Investigate the impact of different layers for attention transfer.** The paper mentions attention maps capturing information at different levels. A more detailed analysis of which layers are most effective for transfer would be valuable.
*   **Explore the computational cost.** While the authors mention minimal overhead, a quantitative analysis of the computational cost of attention transfer would be beneficial.

**Questions for the authors:**

*   What specific hyperparameters were used for the ImageNet experiments, and how were they chosen?
*   Have you experimented with different normalization techniques for the attention maps beyond l2 normalization?
*   Could you elaborate on why KD struggled on ImageNet in your experiments? Was it specific to the architecture combination, or did you observe this across different teacher-student pairs?
*   What is the sensitivity of the results to the choice of the β parameter in Equation 2?
