- Paper proposes activation- and gradient-based attention transfer using spatial attention maps and shows consistent student improvements on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet.
- Decision: Reject — judged insufficient novelty and analysis.
- Incremental contribution: method is conceptually similar to prior work (FitNet, knowledge distillation) and not sufficiently differentiated.
- Incomplete analysis: missing quantitative support for key claims and under-specified methodology (e.g., hyperparameter effects).
- Performance gap: students still underperform teachers and the paper lacks insight into why or how to reduce this gap.
- Unclear attention-map selection: pros/cons of different mapping functions (sum vs max) are not analyzed or explained.
- Unexplored scenario: no experiments where teacher and student share identical architectures to provide a baseline.
- Hyperparameter analysis needed, particularly sensitivity of β and its impact on results.
- Teacher performance reporting is incomplete (e.g., missing teacher train/validation loss curves in Figure 7b).
- Speed-up characterization missing: no evaluation of computational/inference time benefits from smaller student networks.
- Need quantitative evidence (correlation metrics/statistical tests) for the claim that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between objects and attention maps.
- Recommend broader application experiments where spatial information matters (object detection, weakly-supervised localization).
- Questions for authors requested: provide quantitative spatial-correlation evidence; show β’s impact and trends; explain why attention transfer cannot exceed teacher performance; report results for identical teacher/student architectures.
