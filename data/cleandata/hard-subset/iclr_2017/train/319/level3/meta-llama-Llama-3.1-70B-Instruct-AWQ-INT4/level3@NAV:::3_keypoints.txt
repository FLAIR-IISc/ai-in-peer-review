- Proposes attention transfer (AT) to transfer knowledge from teacher to student using spatial attention maps.  
- Defines two attention map types: activation-based and gradient-based.  
- Claims AT consistently improves student performance across datasets and architectures (CIFAR, CUB, Scene, ImageNet).  
- Claims visual evidence that higher-accuracy networks show higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps.  
- Decision: accept with minor revisions.  
- Strengths: well-written, well-motivated, theoretically and empirically supported, shows consistent improvements.  
- Minor issue: lacks quantitative evidence for the claimed correlation between accuracy and attention-object spatial correlation.  
- Minor issue: unclear hyperparameter selection process and no analysis of the AT weight β’s impact.  
- Minor issue: Figure 7b should report teacher training and validation loss for comparison.  
- Minor issue: experiments do not clarify relative pros and cons of different attention map types.  
- Minor issue: no analysis of speed-up or computational cost of the proposed method.  
- Question: provide details on selection of AT weight β and its effect on student performance.  
- Question: provide additional quantitative results to support the accuracy–attention correlation claim.  
- Question: provide insights into strengths and weaknesses of each attention map type and their performance impact.  
- Question: provide details on computational cost and comparison to other knowledge transfer methods.
