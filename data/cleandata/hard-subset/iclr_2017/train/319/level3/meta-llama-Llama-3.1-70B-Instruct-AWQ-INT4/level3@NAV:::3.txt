**Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions**

The paper proposes a novel method for transferring knowledge from a powerful teacher network to a smaller student network, called attention transfer (AT). The authors define two types of spatial attention maps, activation-based and gradient-based, and show that these maps can be used to improve the performance of the student network. The paper claims that AT consistently improves student performance across various datasets and architectures, including CIFAR, CUB, Scene, and ImageNet. The authors also provide visual evidence that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps.

**Decision: Accept with Minor Revisions**

I decide to accept the paper with minor revisions. The paper is well-written, well-motivated, and provides consistent improvements in student performance across various datasets and architectures. However, there are some minor issues that need to be addressed.

**Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper provides a clear and concise introduction to the problem of knowledge transfer in deep neural networks and motivates the need for a new approach. The authors propose a novel method for attention transfer, which is well-supported by theoretical and empirical evidence. The experimental results show consistent improvements in student performance across various datasets and architectures, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Minor Issues and Suggestions for Improvement**

1. The paper lacks quantitative evidence to support the claim that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps. The authors should provide additional quantitative results to support this claim.
2. The hyperparameter selection process is unclear, and the impact of the AT weight β is not analyzed. The authors should provide more details on how they selected the hyperparameters and how they affect the performance of the student network.
3. Figure 7b should also report teacher training and validation loss for comparison. This would provide a more complete picture of the performance of the teacher and student networks.
4. The experiments do not clarify the relative pros and cons of using different attention map types. The authors should provide more insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each attention map type.
5. The paper does not provide any analysis on the speed-up and computational cost of the proposed method. The authors should provide more details on the computational cost of the method and how it compares to other knowledge transfer methods.

**Questions to the Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how you selected the hyperparameters for the AT weight β and how they affect the performance of the student network?
2. Can you provide additional quantitative results to support the claim that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps?
3. Can you provide more insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each attention map type and how they affect the performance of the student network?
4. Can you provide more details on the computational cost of the proposed method and how it compares to other knowledge transfer methods?
