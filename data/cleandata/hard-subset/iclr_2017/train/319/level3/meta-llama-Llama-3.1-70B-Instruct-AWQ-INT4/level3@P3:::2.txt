The paper proposes a novel method for transferring knowledge from a powerful teacher network to a smaller student network using attention maps. The authors define two types of spatial attention maps, activation-based and gradient-based, and explore how these maps change over various datasets and architectures. They show that these attention maps contain valuable information that can be used to significantly improve the performance of convolutional neural network architectures.

I recommend accepting this paper, but with some reservations. The main reason for acceptance is that the paper presents a well-motivated approach to knowledge distillation that is grounded in the literature. The authors provide a clear explanation of their method and demonstrate its effectiveness on several datasets.

However, there are some issues with the paper that need to be addressed. Firstly, the terminology "attention" is somewhat misleading, as the method simply distills summed (squared or lp-norm) activations, not attention maps. This could lead to confusion among readers who are familiar with the concept of attention in deep learning.

Secondly, the gradient-based attention-transfer section feels out-of-place and is neither directly compared with nor combined with the main attention-based transfer. This section adds little value to the paper and could be removed or significantly shortened.

Thirdly, the notation in the paper is sometimes unclear, particularly in the definition of the attention mapping functions. For example, the authors use the symbol "F" to denote both the activation-based mapping function and the gradient-based mapping function, which could lead to confusion.

Lastly, the paper could benefit from more detailed analysis of the results, particularly in terms of the computational cost and the effect of hyperparameters on the performance of the method.

To improve the paper, I would suggest the following:

* Clarify the terminology and notation throughout the paper.
* Remove or shorten the gradient-based attention-transfer section.
* Provide more detailed analysis of the results, including computational cost and hyperparameter tuning.
* Consider adding more visualizations of the attention maps to help illustrate the method.

Some specific questions I would like the authors to address in their response are:

* Can you provide more details on how the induced 2-norm in Eq.(2) is computed, particularly in terms of the computational cost?
* How do you plan to extend this method to non-convolutional architectures?
* Can you provide more insight into why the gradient-based attention-transfer section was included, and how it relates to the main attention-based transfer method?

Overall, the paper presents a promising approach to knowledge distillation, but could benefit from some revisions to clarify the terminology, notation, and results.
