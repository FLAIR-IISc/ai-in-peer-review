- Proposes attention transfer (AT), a novel knowledge-transfer method using attention maps to guide student training.  
- Defines two attention map types: activation-based and gradient-based.  
- Shows consistent student performance improvements on CIFAR, CUB, Scene, and ImageNet.  
- Paper is clear, well-motivated, novel, and situates work with a thorough related-work review.  
- Provides detailed analysis linking attention maps to object locations to build intuition.  
- Acceptance recommended based on clarity, novelty, and consistent empirical gains.  
- Lacks quantitative evidence that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps.  
- Insufficient analysis of the effect of the AT weight β on student performance.  
- Experiments do not clarify relative pros and cons or guidance for choosing attention map types.  
- Needs evaluation of speed-up when student and teacher share the same architecture.  
- Suggests adding experiments in other scenarios (object detection, weakly-supervised localization).  
- Requests details on hyperparameter selection, particularly for AT weight β.  
- Asks for analysis of how attention maps evolve during student training and their stagewise relation to object locations.  
- Inquires whether other attention map types/variants were explored and their results.  
- Asks why AT improves students but does not surpass teacher performance.  
- Queries plans to extend AT to other domains or tasks (e.g., NLP, speech).
