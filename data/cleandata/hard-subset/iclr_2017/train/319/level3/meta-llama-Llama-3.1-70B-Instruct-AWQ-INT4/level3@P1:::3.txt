This paper proposes a novel method for transferring knowledge from a powerful teacher network to a smaller student network, leveraging the concept of attention. The authors introduce two types of attention transfer methods: activation-based and gradient-based. The activation-based method defines attention as a set of spatial maps that encode the importance of different spatial areas of the input for the network's output decision. The gradient-based method defines attention as the gradient of the loss with respect to the input, which can be viewed as an input sensitivity map. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on various image classification datasets, including CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet.

The paper's main contributions are: (1) proposing attention as a mechanism for transferring knowledge from one network to another, (2) introducing two novel methods for transferring attention, and (3) showing consistent improvements in student network performance across various datasets and architectures.

Decision: Accept

The paper is well-written, well-motivated, and presents a clear and concise contribution to the field of deep learning. The authors provide a thorough analysis of their approach and demonstrate its effectiveness through extensive experiments. The paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses, and it is likely to be of interest to the ICLR community.

Supporting arguments for the decision:

* The paper proposes a novel and well-motivated approach to knowledge transfer, which is a key challenge in deep learning.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of their approach, including a clear explanation of the attention transfer methods and their implementation.
* The experiments are extensive and well-designed, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach across various datasets and architectures.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

Additional feedback to improve the paper:

* The authors could provide more quantitative evidence to support their claim that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the impact of the attention transfer weight β on the student network's performance.
* The authors could provide more guidance on when to use different attention map types and their relative pros and cons.
* The paper could explore the potential benefits of using attention transfer when the student and teacher networks share the same architecture.
* The authors could provide more information on the computational cost of their approach and its potential applications in real-world scenarios.

Questions to the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the implementation of the gradient-based attention transfer method, particularly with regards to the second backpropagation step?
* How did you select the hyperparameters for the attention transfer weight β, and what was the impact of different values on the student network's performance?
* Can you provide more information on the relative pros and cons of using activation-based versus gradient-based attention transfer methods?
* Have you explored the potential benefits of using attention transfer in other computer vision tasks, such as object detection or segmentation?
