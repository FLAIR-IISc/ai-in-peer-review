This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks, dubbed attention transfer (AT), which leverages attention maps to improve the performance of a student network by mimicking the attention patterns of a more powerful teacher network. The authors define two types of spatial attention maps, activation-based and gradient-based, and demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach across various datasets and architectures.

I decide to accept this paper, primarily due to its well-motivated approach, clear writing, and consistent improvements in student performance over the baseline. The paper provides a thorough analysis of the proposed method, including a detailed description of the attention mapping functions and the attention transfer process.

The authors provide strong empirical evidence to support their claims, with experiments on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet demonstrating the effectiveness of AT in improving student performance. The paper also explores the combination of AT with knowledge distillation (KD), showing that AT can be used in conjunction with KD to further improve performance.

However, I do have some concerns regarding the paper's limitations and potential areas for improvement. Firstly, the paper does not provide a clear analysis of the hyperparameter selection process, particularly with regards to the AT weight β. The impact of this hyperparameter on the performance of the student network is not thoroughly explored, and it is unclear how the authors arrived at the chosen value.

Furthermore, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the relative pros and cons of different attention map types. While the authors provide some insight into the differences between activation-based and gradient-based attention maps, a more comprehensive comparison of these methods would strengthen the paper.

Additionally, the paper does not provide a clear characterization of the speed-up achieved by using AT, particularly when the student and teacher networks share the same architecture. This is an important consideration, as the primary motivation for using AT is to improve the performance of a smaller student network.

Lastly, I would like to see more quantitative evidence to support the claim that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps. While the paper provides some visual evidence to support this claim, a more rigorous analysis would be beneficial.

To address these concerns, I would suggest that the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the hyperparameter selection process, including a sensitivity analysis of the AT weight β. Additionally, a more comprehensive comparison of different attention map types would be beneficial, as would a characterization of the speed-up achieved by using AT.

I would also like to see more quantitative evidence to support the claim that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps. This could involve conducting additional experiments or providing a more detailed analysis of the existing results.

Overall, however, this paper provides a valuable contribution to the field of knowledge transfer and attention mechanisms in neural networks. With some additional analysis and experimentation, the authors can further strengthen their claims and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method.

Questions to the authors:

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the hyperparameter selection process, particularly with regards to the AT weight β? How did you arrive at the chosen value, and what is the impact of this hyperparameter on the performance of the student network?
2. Can you provide a more comprehensive comparison of different attention map types, including a discussion of their relative pros and cons? How do the different attention mapping functions affect the performance of the student network?
3. Can you provide a characterization of the speed-up achieved by using AT, particularly when the student and teacher networks share the same architecture? How does the use of AT affect the computational resources required for training and inference?
4. Can you provide more quantitative evidence to support the claim that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps? How do you plan to further investigate this claim in future work?
