- Proposes transferring knowledge from a teacher to a student using attention.  
- Introduces two attention transfer methods: activation-based and gradient-based.  
- Activation-based attention: spatial maps encoding importance of input regions for output.  
- Gradient-based attention: input sensitivity maps given by loss gradient w.r.t. input.  
- Validated on CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet datasets.  
- Main contributions: attention as transfer mechanism, two transfer methods, consistent student improvements.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Paper is well-written, well-motivated, and presents a clear, concise contribution.  
- Provides thorough analysis and clear implementation details of attention transfer methods.  
- Experiments are extensive and well-designed across datasets and architectures.  
- Likely of interest to the ICLR community and broadly accessible.  
- Suggestion: add quantitative evidence linking higher teacher accuracy to higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps.  
- Suggestion: analyze the impact of the attention transfer weight β on student performance in more detail.  
- Suggestion: provide guidance on when to use different attention map types and their relative pros/cons.  
- Suggestion: explore benefits when student and teacher share the same architecture.  
- Suggestion: report computational cost and discuss potential real-world applications.  
- Question: provide implementation details of gradient-based attention transfer, especially the second backpropagation step.  
- Question: how were β hyperparameters selected and what was the effect of different values?  
- Question: clarify relative pros and cons of activation-based vs. gradient-based methods.  
- Question: have you explored attention transfer in other vision tasks such as detection or segmentation?
