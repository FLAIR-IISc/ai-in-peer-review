This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks, leveraging attention maps to guide the learning process of a student network. The authors introduce two methods for transferring attention: activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on various image classification datasets, including CIFAR, ImageNet, and fine-grained recognition tasks.

The key contribution of this work is the proposal of attention as a mechanism for transferring knowledge between networks. The authors argue that attention maps contain valuable information that can be used to improve the performance of a student network. They provide experimental evidence to support this claim, showing that attention transfer can lead to significant improvements in accuracy, especially when combined with knowledge distillation.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their methods and results. The writing is concise, and the figures and tables are well-structured and easy to follow. The authors also provide a thorough review of related work, situating their contribution within the broader context of knowledge transfer and attention mechanisms.

However, I have some concerns regarding the experimental evaluation. While the authors provide some results on CIFAR and ImageNet, I believe that the IMAGENET section requires more extensive experiments to fully demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach. Specifically, it would be beneficial to see more results on different architectures, depths, and hyperparameter settings to gain a deeper understanding of the strengths and limitations of attention transfer.

Additionally, I would like to see a more detailed comparison with standard knowledge distillation methods. While the authors provide some results showing that attention transfer can lead to marginal improvements over knowledge distillation, I believe that a more thorough evaluation is necessary to fully understand the benefits and trade-offs of their approach.

Overall, I believe that this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of knowledge transfer and attention mechanisms. However, I would like to see more extensive experiments and a more detailed comparison with existing methods before making a final decision.
