This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks, called attention transfer (AT), which leverages attention maps to guide the training of a student network. The authors define two types of attention maps, activation-based and gradient-based, and demonstrate their effectiveness in improving the performance of the student network on various image classification datasets.

I decide to accept this paper, with the main reasons being the clarity and novelty of the proposed approach, as well as the consistent improvements in student performance observed in the experiments.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem they aim to tackle. They also provide a thorough review of related work, which helps to situate their contribution within the broader context of knowledge transfer and attention mechanisms in neural networks.

The approach is well-supported by the experimental results, which demonstrate the effectiveness of AT in improving student performance on CIFAR, CUB, Scene, and ImageNet datasets. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the attention maps and their relationship to object locations, which helps to build intuition about the proposed approach.

However, there are some limitations and areas for improvement. For example, the paper lacks quantitative evidence to support the claim that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps. Additionally, the impact of the AT weight β is not thoroughly analyzed, and the experiments do not clarify the relative pros and cons of using different attention map types.

To further improve the paper, I would suggest the following:

* Provide more quantitative evidence to support the claim about the relationship between network accuracy and attention maps.
* Analyze the impact of the AT weight β on the performance of the student network.
* Clarify the guidance on which attention map type to use and when.
* Characterize the speed-up achieved by AT when the student and teacher share the same architecture.
* Consider adding more experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of AT in other scenarios, such as object detection or weakly-supervised localization.

Some questions I would like the authors to answer to help clarify my understanding of the paper and provide additional evidence to support their claims are:

* Can you provide more details on the hyperparameter selection process, particularly for the AT weight β?
* How do the attention maps change as the student network trains, and what is the relationship between attention maps and object locations at different stages of training?
* Have you explored other attention map types or variants, and if so, what were the results?
* Can you provide more insights into why AT does not surpass teacher performance, despite consistently improving student performance?
* Are there any plans to explore the application of AT to other domains or tasks, such as natural language processing or speech recognition?
