- Proposes Attention Transfer (AT) for knowledge transfer using attention maps to improve student networks.
- Defines activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps and attention mapping functions.
- Strong empirical results on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet showing consistent student performance improvements.
- Shows AT can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD) for additional gains.
- Paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and thoroughly described — merits acceptance.
- Lacks clear analysis of hyperparameter selection, notably sensitivity and justification for AT weight β.
- Needs a more comprehensive comparison and discussion of pros/cons between activation- vs gradient-based attention maps.
- Omits characterization of computational speed-up and resource impact of AT, especially when student and teacher share architecture.
- Provides primarily visual (not quantitative) evidence that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps.
- Suggested additions: sensitivity analysis for β, detailed attention-map comparisons, speed-up/resource characterization, and rigorous quantitative spatial-correlation experiments.
- Questions for authors: detail β selection and its impact; compare attention map types and their performance effects; quantify AT speed-up/resource costs; provide quantitative evidence for attention–object spatial correlation.
