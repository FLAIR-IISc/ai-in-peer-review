**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel method for transferring knowledge from a powerful teacher network to a smaller student network, leveraging the concept of attention in convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors define attention as a set of spatial maps that encode the importance of different spatial areas of the input for the network's output decision. They introduce two types of spatial attention maps: activation-based and gradient-based. The proposed method, called attention transfer, aims to train a student network to mimic the attention maps of the teacher network, leading to improved performance. The authors evaluate their method on various image classification datasets, including CIFAR, ImageNet, and fine-grained classification tasks, demonstrating consistent improvements over baseline methods.

**Decision**

Based on the review, I would recommend accepting the paper, with some minor revisions to address the organizational issues and unclear notation.

**Reasons for Decision**

The paper presents a well-motivated and well-executed study on attention transfer in CNNs, with a clear and concise writing style. The proposed method is novel and shows promising results on various datasets. The authors provide a thorough analysis of their approach, including visualizations of attention maps and ablation studies.

However, there are some organizational issues and unclear notation that need to be addressed. For example, the gradient-based attention-transfer section feels out-of-place and is not directly compared with or combined with the main attention-based transfer method. Additionally, the notation in Eq.(2) is unclear, and the authors may have meant to use the Frobenius norm instead of the 2-norm.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main contribution is the proposal of a novel attention transfer method, which is well-motivated by the concept of attention in human visual experience and artificial neural networks. The authors provide a thorough analysis of their approach, including visualizations of attention maps and ablation studies. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over baseline methods on various datasets, including CIFAR, ImageNet, and fine-grained classification tasks.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. For example, the method appears to be limited to convolutional architectures, and the "attention" terminology is misleading, as the method simply distills summed (squared or lp-norm) activations, not attention maps.

**Additional Feedback**

To improve the paper, the authors should address the organizational issues and unclear notation. Specifically, they should:

* Reorganize the paper to integrate the gradient-based attention-transfer section with the main attention-based transfer method.
* Clarify the notation in Eq.(2) and specify whether they meant to use the Frobenius norm or the 2-norm.
* Provide more details on the computational cost of the proposed method and its scalability to larger datasets.
* Discuss the limitations of the method and potential future directions for research.

**Questions for the Authors**

* Can you provide more details on the computational cost of the proposed method and its scalability to larger datasets?
* How do you plan to extend the method to other types of neural network architectures, such as recurrent neural networks or transformers?
