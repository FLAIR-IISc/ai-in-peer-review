### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel and intuitive method for knowledge transfer between a powerful "teacher" neural network and a smaller "student" network, termed Attention Transfer (AT). The core idea is to compel the student network to mimic the spatial attention maps of the teacher. The authors motivate this approach by drawing parallels with human visual attention and hypothesize that where a network "looks" is a powerful and compact form of knowledge to transfer. They define two types of attention maps: a simple and efficient activation-based map (derived from statistics of feature map activations) and a more complex gradient-based map (derived from input gradients). Through a series of experiments on CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, the authors demonstrate that their method can consistently improve the performance of student networks. They notably show that transferring these summarized attention maps can be more effective than transferring the full, uncompressed activation tensors, and that AT can be combined with standard knowledge distillation (KD) for further gains.

### **Decision**

**Weak Accept.** The paper introduces a simple, novel, and well-motivated method for knowledge transfer. The core idea is elegant and the results on small to medium-scale datasets are promising. However, the experimental evaluation on the large-scale ImageNet dataset is preliminary and lacks sufficient rigor, which weakens some of the paper's central claims about its advantages over existing methods in that setting.

**Key Reasons:**
1.  (Pro) The proposed method of transferring attention maps is a novel and effective form of knowledge distillation. The experiments convincingly show that it can outperform or complement standard KD and is more effective than transferring full activation maps (FitNets-style) on several benchmarks.
2.  (Con) The experimental results on ImageNet are not comprehensive enough to fully support the paper's claims. The improvements over the baseline are presented without a serious comparison to other knowledge transfer methods, and the claims about the failure of KD on ImageNet are anecdotal rather than empirically demonstrated.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The main strength of this paper is the novelty and simplicity of its core idea. Instead of transferring complex, high-dimensional feature maps as in FitNets, or soft labels as in KD, the authors propose to transfer a compressed, 2D representation of where the network focuses its computational resources. This is an intuitive form of "hint" that is computationally cheap and, as the results show, quite effective.

On the CIFAR-10 and CUB-200 datasets, the method demonstrates clear benefits. In Table 1, AT consistently outperforms a strong student baseline and standard KD. Furthermore, the combination of AT+KD yields the best results, suggesting that attention maps provide a complementary source of supervisory signal to the soft labels from KD. The comparison in Section 4.1.2 against transferring full activations (F-ActT) is particularly interesting; the fact that the compressed attention map leads to better final accuracy than the full activation tensor is a compelling finding that suggests that "what" to transfer is as important as the transfer mechanism itself.

However, the paper's primary weakness lies in its experimental validation, particularly as the scale of the dataset increases.

1.  **Inconsistent Gains Over Knowledge Distillation:** While AT is superior to KD on CIFAR and CUB, the results are less decisive on the MIT Scenes dataset (Table 4), where AT and KD perform identically. The authors speculate this is due to the nature of the task (fine-grained vs. scene recognition), but this is not explored further, leaving questions about the general applicability of AT's superiority over KD.

2.  **Insufficient ImageNet Experiments:** The ImageNet section (4.2.2) feels preliminary and rushed. The authors report a promising 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement for a ResNet-18 student, but this is based on a single teacher-student pair, untuned hyperparameters, and an incomplete application of their own method (using only 2 of 4 possible transfer locations). Most critically, the claims made in this section are not well-supported. The authors state that KD "hurts convergence" on ImageNet but provide no data, training curves, or discussion of hyperparameters to substantiate this. The subsequent claim that AT may be "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is therefore unsubstantiated, as no fair comparison to existing methods is presented. This section is pivotal for demonstrating the method's scalability and impact, and in its current form, it is not convincing.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not the primary basis for my decision, which rests on the arguments above. The core idea is strong, and strengthening the experimental section would make this a much more impactful paper.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Strengthen Section 4.2.2:** This is the most critical area for improvement. To make the ImageNet results compelling, I would strongly recommend: (a) running the experiment to completion with transfer losses on all 4 residual groups; (b) conducting a fair and rigorous comparison against a well-tuned KD baseline, including training curves and final results for both methods; (c) tempering the claim of being the "first" successful method on ImageNet unless a more thorough literature review can support it. Even showing that AT is easier to tune or converges faster than KD on ImageNet would be a valuable contribution.
*   **Analyze the F-ActT Baseline:** The reported performance for the FitNets-style baseline (F-ActT) in Table 1 is surprisingly low. Given that FitNets is a well-established method, this result may overstate the relative gains of AT. Could you provide more details on the implementation (e.g., were 1x1 convolutions used to match dimensions, how was the loss weight scheduled) to ensure it was a strong baseline?
*   **Elaborate on Gradient-Based AT:** The gradient-based method is an interesting contribution but feels underdeveloped, being limited to a single experiment on CIFAR with a non-standard architecture. It would be beneficial to discuss the challenges (like the issue with batch normalization) in more detail or to show its applicability to more modern networks.

**Questions for the Authors:**
1.  Could you please elaborate on the difficulties encountered with Knowledge Distillation on the ResNet-18/34 ImageNet experiment? What temperature and alpha values were tried? Showing the learning curves that support the claim of "hurts convergence" would be very helpful.
2.  How sensitive is the activation-based AT method to the choice of the loss weight `β`? The paper provides a heuristic for setting it, but a brief sensitivity analysis would improve the reproducibility and practicality of the method.
3.  Have you explored which layers are most effective for attention transfer? For instance, does transferring attention from early, mid, or late stages of the teacher network have a differential impact on the student's performance?
