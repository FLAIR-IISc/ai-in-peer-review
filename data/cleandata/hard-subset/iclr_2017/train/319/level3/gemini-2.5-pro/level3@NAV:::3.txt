This paper introduces Attention Transfer (AT), a knowledge distillation method where a smaller "student" network is trained to mimic the spatial attention maps of a larger, pre-trained "teacher" network. The authors propose two main ways to define attention maps: activation-based maps, which summarize feature activations across channels (e.g., sum of squares), and gradient-based maps, derived from the gradient of the loss with respect to the input. The AT loss, calculated as the L2 distance between normalized teacher and student attention maps, is combined with the standard classification loss. The method is evaluated on CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet datasets, showing that students trained with AT consistently outperform students trained from scratch, and that AT can be effectively combined with traditional knowledge distillation.

**Decision: Weak Accept**

My decision is based on two key factors: 1) The paper presents a simple, intuitive, and well-motivated method that yields consistent empirical improvements across multiple benchmarks. 2) However, the experimental analysis lacks the depth and rigor expected for a top-tier conference, leaving several important questions about the method's properties and applicability unanswered.

**Supporting Arguments:**
The primary strength of this work is its clear motivation and consistent positive results. The idea of forcing a student to "look" at the same regions as a powerful teacher is elegant. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that AT provides a significant boost over the baseline student model across various architectures and datasets, including the challenging ImageNet benchmark. The writing is clear and the method is easy to understand.

However, the paper's claims are not fully supported by a rigorous scientific evaluation. A central claim, that higher-accuracy networks produce more spatially correlated attention maps (Fig. 4), is supported only by qualitative examples. This requires quantitative evidence, for instance, by measuring the correlation between attention maps and ground-truth object locations. Furthermore, the analysis of the method itself is superficial. The impact of the crucial hyperparameter β is not studied, and the paper provides no guidance on how to choose between the different proposed attention map types (sum vs. max, p-value). Since the student model never surpasses the teacher, a discussion of the practical trade-offs (e.g., inference speed-up vs. accuracy drop) is essential but missing. The novelty is also somewhat incremental when compared to methods like FitNets, which also transferred knowledge from intermediate layers.

**Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper:**
The following points are suggestions for strengthening the paper:
- To substantiate the claim in Figure 4, please provide a quantitative analysis comparing attention maps from different models to object bounding boxes or segmentation masks.
- Please include an ablation study on the sensitivity of the results to the AT loss weight β. This is critical for reproducibility and understanding the method's stability.
- The comparison in Figure 7b would be more informative if it also included the teacher's training and validation loss curves as a reference.
- Adding a table comparing the parameter counts, FLOPs, and inference speeds of the teacher and final student models would clearly characterize the efficiency gains that justify the drop in accuracy.

**Questions for the Authors:**
- Could you clarify the rationale for choosing one type of activation-based attention map over another? Is there a principled reason to prefer F_sum^2 over F_max, or is this choice purely empirical?
- Have you investigated whether Attention Transfer is beneficial when the student and teacher networks share the same architecture? This would help determine if AT primarily functions as a cross-architecture distillation technique or also as a general-purpose regularizer.
