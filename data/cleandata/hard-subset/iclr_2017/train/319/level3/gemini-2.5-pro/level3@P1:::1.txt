### Review

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel method for knowledge transfer between a powerful "teacher" neural network and a smaller "student" network, aiming to improve the student's performance. The core idea, termed Attention Transfer (AT), is to compel the student network to mimic the spatial attention maps of the teacher. The authors propose two distinct methods for defining these attention maps: an activation-based approach, which computes spatial maps by summarizing feature activations across channels (e.g., using sum of p-norm), and a gradient-based approach, which uses the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image as a sensitivity map. Through a series of experiments on CIFAR, CUB, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, the paper demonstrates that transferring these compact attention maps can improve the student model's accuracy. The authors claim that this method is more effective than transferring full activation tensors (as in FitNets) and can be successfully combined with traditional knowledge distillation (KD). Notably, they present preliminary results suggesting AT is effective on the large-scale ImageNet dataset, where other knowledge transfer methods have reportedly struggled.

**Decision: Reject**

I am recommending rejection for this paper at this time, primarily for two key reasons:
1.  The experimental evidence does not convincingly demonstrate that Attention Transfer provides a significant advantage over existing, strong baselines like standard Knowledge Distillation (KD). The experiments often combine AT with KD, making it difficult to isolate the benefit of AT alone.
2.  The claims of success on the ImageNet dataset are based on very preliminary and incomplete experiments, which are not sufficient to support the paper's broad claims of applicability and superiority on large-scale tasks.

While the core idea is intuitive and interesting, the paper in its current form lacks the rigorous empirical validation needed for acceptance at ICLR.

**Supporting Arguments**

My decision is based on the following observations from the paper:

1.  **Insufficient Ablation Against Knowledge Distillation:** The primary comparison for a new knowledge transfer method should be against Hinton et al.'s Knowledge Distillation. In the main results table for CIFAR-10 (Table 1), the authors show a baseline student, the student with AT, and the student with AT+KD. Critically, a "KD only" baseline is missing. For example, for the WRN-16-2/WRN-16-1 pair, the error drops from 8.77 (baseline) to 7.93 (AT), and further to 7.48 (AT+KD). Without knowing the performance of KD alone, it is impossible to determine if AT is providing a complementary signal or if KD is responsible for the majority of the gains in the combined setting. The paper's contribution is positioned as an improvement or alternative to existing methods, and this missing comparison is a significant flaw in the experimental design.

2.  **Weak and Preliminary ImageNet Results:** The ImageNet experiment (Section 4.2.2) is presented as a key result, with the authors stating, "we can assume that proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." This is a very strong claim that is not supported by the evidence provided. The experiment consists of a single teacher-student pair (ResNet-34/ResNet-18). The authors explicitly state they "did not have time to tune any hyperparameters" and "plan to update the paper with losses on all 4 groups." Furthermore, they report that their implementation of KD failed on ImageNet, which might suggest suboptimal hyperparameter settings for all transfer methods, not just KD. For a benchmark as critical as ImageNet, a single, untuned experiment is not sufficient to claim state-of-the-art success or to serve as a cornerstone of the paper's contributions.

3.  **Inconsistent Experimental Setups for Gradient-based AT:** The experiments for gradient-based attention transfer (Section 4.1.3) are conducted under a constrained setting (thin NIN model, no batch normalization, no random crops) that is inconsistent with the modern training setups used in the rest of the paper. The authors acknowledge this is due to implementation complexities with batch normalization during the second backpropagation step. While the initial results are interesting, this section feels more like a proof-of-concept than a fully-fledged method, making it difficult to assess its practical utility and how it compares to the activation-based method under identical, state-of-the-art training conditions.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

I believe the core idea has merit, and the paper could be significantly improved with further work. The following points are intended as constructive feedback:

*   **Strengthen Experimental Comparisons:** The most critical next step is to conduct a thorough comparison on CIFAR that includes a "KD only" baseline for all teacher-student pairs. This will properly situate the contribution of AT. It would also be valuable to report results for FitNets using the authors' own implementation to ensure the comparison in Table 1 (F-ActT) is fair.
*   **Complete ImageNet Experiments:** To support the claims made, the ImageNet experiments need to be completed. This includes tuning hyperparameters for both AT and KD baselines, running the experiments to convergence, and ideally testing more than one teacher-student architecture pair. Reporting that KD fails without a rigorous hyperparameter search is not a convincing argument for AT's superiority.
*   **Deepen the Analysis:** The paper finds that transferring attention maps is better than transferring full activations, which is an interesting result. The paper would be stronger if it provided more intuition or analysis as to *why* this might be the case. Is it a form of regularization? Does it prevent the student from overfitting to noise in the teacher's specific feature activations while still capturing the high-level localization signal?
*   **Clarify Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The parameter `beta` is crucial for balancing the losses. The paper states it is set via a formula involving the size of the attention map. It would be helpful to include a brief sensitivity analysis to show how robust the method is to the choice of `beta`.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please provide the results for a "Knowledge Distillation only" baseline in Table 1? This is essential for understanding the additive value of your proposed AT method when compared to KD.
2.  In the ImageNet experiments, you mention that KD hurts convergence. Could this be due to the specific hyperparameters chosen (e.g., temperature, alpha), which were inherited from CIFAR experiments? A fair comparison would require some tuning for KD on ImageNet as well.
3.  What is your primary hypothesis for why transferring summarized attention maps works better than transferring the full, information-rich activation tensors (FitNets-style)?
4.  The choice of p=2 for the `F_psum` function seems to work best. Did you find that this hyperparameter was stable across different datasets and architectures, or did it require tuning for each experimental setup?
