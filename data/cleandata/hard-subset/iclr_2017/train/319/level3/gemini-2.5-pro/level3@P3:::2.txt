### Review

**1. Summary**

This paper proposes a novel and effective method for knowledge distillation in convolutional neural networks, termed "Attention Transfer" (AT). The core idea is to improve a smaller "student" network by compelling it to mimic the intermediate representations of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. Rather than transferring the full activation tensors, as in methods like FitNets, the authors propose transferring a compressed representation of activations, which they refer to as spatial attention maps. These maps are generated by computing simple statistics (e.g., the sum of the p-th power of absolute values) across the channel dimension of a feature map, effectively summarizing where the network "focuses" its computational resources spatially. The distillation process is implemented as an additional loss term that minimizes the L2 distance between the normalized attention maps of the student and teacher at corresponding layers. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments on CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet that this technique provides consistent and significant performance improvements. The method is shown to outperform a FitNet-style full activation transfer and can be effectively combined with traditional knowledge distillation.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and demonstrates impressive and consistent performance gains across a variety of datasets, network architectures, and tasks (classification and fine-tuning). The successful application to ImageNet is a particularly strong result, as many knowledge transfer methods have struggled to show benefits at that scale.
2.  **Novel and Efficient Distillation:** The paper introduces a compelling alternative to full activation transfer (FitNets). By distilling a statistical summary of feature maps rather than the full tensors, the method is more focused and is empirically shown to be more effective, suggesting that this compressed spatial summary carries the most critical information for knowledge transfer.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this work lies in its clear and substantial empirical contributions. The activation-based attention transfer method consistently closes the performance gap between student and teacher networks. On CIFAR-10 (Table 1), AT provides significant improvements for various WRN pairs and, crucially, is more effective than transferring full activations ("F-ActT"). This direct comparison validates the central hypothesis that a summary of activations can be more beneficial for distillation than the raw activations themselves. The experiments on larger-scale datasets further solidify these claims. The improvements on CUB-200 and MIT Scenes during fine-tuning are noteworthy, and the 1.1% top-1 accuracy gain for ResNet-18 on ImageNet is a significant achievement that demonstrates the method's practical utility.

The proposed method is also elegant in its simplicity. It introduces a straightforward mechanism for defining intermediate "hints" without requiring auxiliary parameters or complex architectural modifications, making it easy to implement and computationally efficient. This simplicity, combined with its demonstrated effectiveness, makes it a valuable contribution to the knowledge distillation literature.

However, the paper is not without its weaknesses, which primarily relate to presentation and clarity. The use of the term "attention" is misleading. In the context of deep learning literature, "attention" typically refers to a mechanism that produces a context-dependent, learnable soft-weighting of features (e.g., Bahdanau et al., 2014). The proposed method, in contrast, computes a fixed, post-hoc statistic of activations. While these maps do highlight salient spatial regions, using a term like "Activation Summary Transfer" or "Saliency Transfer" would be more precise and avoid confusion with a different class of well-established mechanisms.

Furthermore, the paper's organization is weakened by the inclusion of the "Gradient-based Attention Transfer" section. This section feels disconnected from the main, activation-based contribution. The experiments are limited to a simplified setting on CIFAR-10 (no batch normalization, no random crops), and the method is neither directly compared with the activation-based approach under identical conditions nor combined with it. As presented, this section distracts from the paper's primary focus and adds little to the overall narrative, feeling more like a preliminary exploration of a separate idea.

Despite these presentational issues, the core contribution—the activation-based transfer method—is sound, well-evaluated, and impactful enough to warrant acceptance.

**4. Additional Feedback**

The following suggestions are intended to help improve the manuscript and are not central to the acceptance decision:

*   **Terminology:** I strongly recommend reconsidering the use of the term "attention." Adopting more precise terminology would strengthen the paper's clarity and positioning within the literature.
*   **Paper Structure:** To improve focus, consider moving the section on gradient-based transfer to an appendix. This would allow the main body of the paper to concentrate on the much stronger and more thoroughly validated activation-based method.
*   **Equation (2) Clarity:** The notation for the norm in Equation (2) could be slightly ambiguous. While `Q` is defined as a vectorized map, making `||Q||2` the standard Euclidean norm, a reader might momentarily confuse it with a matrix norm on the 2D map `F(A)`. A brief clarifying sentence (e.g., "where `||.||2` denotes the standard vector L2-norm") would eliminate any potential confusion.
*   **Exploring the `p` parameter:** The choice of `p` in `F_psum` seems to control the "peakiness" of the resulting maps. A brief analysis or discussion of how different values of `p` affect transfer performance could provide additional insight into the mechanism.

**5. Questions for Authors**

*   Could you please clarify the motivation for using the term "attention" given its established meaning in the context of learnable weighting mechanisms? Have you considered alternative names for the method?
*   The gradient-based attention transfer method is an interesting idea but seems underdeveloped in the paper. What was the reasoning for including it alongside the activation-based method, and do you have plans to explore it further in a more comparable experimental setting?
*   The experiments show that `sum`-based attention functions work better than `max`-based ones. Do you have an intuition as to why aggregating information from all channels via a sum is more beneficial for transfer than selecting the maximal activation at each spatial location?
