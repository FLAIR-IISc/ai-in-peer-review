This paper introduces a novel knowledge distillation technique, termed "Attention Transfer" (AT), designed to improve a "student" network's performance by leveraging a "teacher" network. The core contribution is a method that encourages the student's intermediate feature maps to mimic the spatial statistics of the teacher's maps. The authors define these statistics, which they call attention maps, by summing the p-norm of activations across the channel dimension. The proposed method is evaluated on multiple computer vision datasets, where it is shown to consistently improve student performance and empirically outperform FitNet-style distillation.

Decision: Reject

My decision is based on two primary concerns: 1) The paper's central framing around "attention" is misleading and misrepresents the technical contribution. 2) The paper suffers from significant organizational issues and a lack of clarity in its presentation.

The term "attention" implies a mechanism for selectively focusing on relevant information, with well-established definitions in the community (e.g., self-attention, Grad-CAM). The proposed method, which simply computes a channel-wise sum of activation magnitudes, is more accurately described as a form of spatial feature statistic matching. While effective, this framing overstates the novelty and creates confusion. Furthermore, the paper's structure is disjointed. The "gradient-based attention transfer" section is presented as a key contribution but is evaluated in a very limited setting, separate from the main activation-based method. It is neither compared with nor integrated into the primary framework, making its inclusion feel underdeveloped and distracting. Finally, the paper lacks mathematical precision; for example, it is unclear how the 2-norm in Eq. (2) is computed for normalization, which could be interpreted in several ways.

For future revisions, I would offer the following suggestions. The core empirical result—that distilling spatial summary statistics is more effective than distilling full feature maps—is a strong and interesting finding. I recommend reframing the paper to highlight this contribution more directly. The method also appears limited to convolutional architectures; discussing this limitation would strengthen the work.

I would like the authors to answer the following questions to clarify my assessment:
1.  Could you justify the use of the term "attention" over a more direct description like "spatial activation statistics"? Have you compared your maps to more conventional attention maps to validate this terminology?
2.  What was the rationale for including the gradient-based transfer method without a direct comparison or integration with the activation-based approach?
3.  Please clarify the exact computation of the norm used for normalization in Equation (2). Is it the standard L2 (Euclidean) norm of the vectorized map?
