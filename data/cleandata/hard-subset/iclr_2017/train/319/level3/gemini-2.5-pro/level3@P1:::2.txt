### Summary

This paper introduces a novel knowledge transfer technique, termed Attention Transfer (AT), designed to improve the performance of a smaller "student" network by leveraging knowledge from a larger "teacher" network. The core idea is to compel the student network to mimic the "attention maps" of the teacher. The authors propose two methods for defining these spatial attention maps: one based on summary statistics of activation tensors (e.g., the sum of absolute values or p-th powers of activations across channels) and another based on the gradient of the loss with respect to the input. Through extensive experiments on CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, the authors demonstrate that this method consistently improves the student network's performance. Notably, the proposed activation-based AT outperforms a FitNets-style transfer of full activation tensors and is shown to be one of the first knowledge transfer methods to successfully scale to ImageNet, improving a ResNet-18 model's accuracy by over 1% top-1.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The proposed activation-based attention transfer is a simple, intuitive, and effective method for knowledge distillation. The empirical results are strong and consistent across multiple datasets and network architectures, demonstrating a clear improvement over strong baselines.
2.  The paper makes a significant contribution by successfully applying a knowledge transfer method to the large-scale ImageNet dataset, a domain where previous methods like Knowledge Distillation and FitNets have reportedly struggled. This demonstrates the scalability and practical value of the proposed approach.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this work lies in its compelling empirical results and the simplicity of the core idea. The concept of using summary statistics of activation maps as a proxy for "attention" provides an efficient and effective "hint" for the student network. The experiments convincingly show that this method works. For instance, on CIFAR, AT consistently improves performance for various WRN architectures and, when combined with Knowledge Distillation (KD), yields even better results (Table 1).

Crucially, the authors provide a direct comparison to a FitNets-style approach that transfers the full activation tensors. The results show that AT is significantly more effective (Table 1, Figure 7(b) in Appendix). This is an interesting and important finding, suggesting that transferring a compressed, summary representation of spatial importance is more beneficial than forcing the student to match the teacher's raw intermediate representations.

The success on ImageNet is a standout result. While many knowledge transfer methods are benchmarked on smaller datasets, showing a 1.1% top-1 accuracy gain for ResNet-18 by learning from ResNet-34 is a strong testament to the method's utility. The claim that this is one of the first successful applications of such a method on ImageNet seems credible and marks a valuable contribution to the field.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong overall, there are several areas where it could be improved for clarity and impact. These points are meant as constructive feedback and are not central to my decision.

1.  **"Attention" Terminology:** The use of the term "attention" could be seen as slightly misleading. In much of the literature, attention refers to a mechanism that learns to dynamically weight parts of an input or feature representation. The proposed method, by contrast, uses a fixed, post-hoc summary statistic of activations. While this statistic certainly captures the spatial focus of the network, clarifying this distinction or using a more precise term like "activation-based spatial summary transfer" might prevent confusion and better situate the work.

2.  **Organization of Gradient-Based Transfer:** The section on gradient-based attention transfer (3.2) feels disconnected from the rest of the paper. It is evaluated in a constrained setting (NIN without batch normalization on CIFAR) and is never directly compared with or combined with the primary activation-based method. Its inclusion feels like a separate, less-developed idea. To improve focus, this section could be better integrated (e.g., by combining the two transfer types) or potentially moved to an appendix.

3.  **Clarity of Notation in Eq. (2):** The notation for the loss function could be clarified. The loss is defined using `||Q||_2` where `Q` is a vectorized attention map. The notation `||.||_2` for a matrix (before vectorization) typically denotes the spectral norm, which is computationally expensive to compute. It is highly likely that the authors mean the standard vector L2-norm (Euclidean norm), which is equivalent to the Frobenius norm of the un-vectorized map. Specifying this would improve reproducibility.

4.  **Scope of Applicability:** The method as presented is inherently tied to convolutional architectures that preserve spatial information. A brief discussion of this limitation and potential avenues for extending the core idea (transferring summary statistics of hidden states) to other architectures like Transformers would be a welcome addition.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  The finding that transferring summed activation statistics is more effective than transferring full activation tensors is very interesting. What is your intuition for why this compressed representation serves as a better hint for the student network? Does it perhaps act as a form of regularization, forcing the student to learn the high-level spatial focus without being overly constrained by the teacher's exact feature implementation?
2.  In Equation (2), could you please confirm that the `||.||_2` notation on the vectorized attention maps refers to the standard Euclidean (vector) norm, and not the spectral norm of the original 2D maps?
3.  The gradient-based and activation-based transfer methods seem to capture different aspects of the teacher's knowledge. Have you experimented with combining them in a single loss function to see if they provide complementary benefits?
