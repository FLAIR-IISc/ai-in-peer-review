### Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel knowledge transfer method, termed "attention transfer" (AT), designed to improve the performance of a smaller "student" convolutional neural network (CNN) by leveraging the knowledge from a larger, pre-trained "teacher" CNN. The core idea is to compel the student network to mimic the teacher's "attention maps" at intermediate layers. The authors define two types of such maps: (1) activation-based maps, which are spatial maps derived by computing channel-wise statistics (e.g., sum of squared activations) of a layer's feature tensor, and (2) gradient-based maps, which are defined as the gradient of the loss with respect to the input. The proposed method is evaluated across a range of datasets, including CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, and is shown to consistently improve student network performance. Notably, the authors demonstrate that activation-based AT outperforms a FitNet-style approach of transferring full activation tensors and report it as the first knowledge transfer method successfully applied to ImageNet classification.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Misleading Terminology and Framing:** The paper's central concept of "attention transfer" is based on a definition of "attention" that is inconsistent with its common usage in the machine learning literature. The proposed "attention maps" are simple, post-hoc statistical summaries of activation tensors rather than learned, dynamic mechanisms that re-weight features. This framing overstates the conceptual contribution and creates confusion; the method is more accurately described as a form of feature-statistic distillation.
2.  **Disjointed Structure and Incomplete Evaluation:** The paper presents two distinct methods (activation-based and gradient-based transfer) but fails to integrate them into a cohesive narrative. The gradient-based section is underdeveloped, evaluated only on a simplified CIFAR setup, and is not compared with or combined with the primary activation-based method. This makes the paper feel like a combination of two separate, incomplete ideas, weakening the overall contribution.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

**Regarding Reason 1 (Misleading Terminology):** The term "attention" in modern neural networks (e.g., in NLP and computer vision) typically refers to a mechanism that learns to assign importance weights to different parts of an input or representation, often dynamically. The proposed activation-based maps, defined as `F(A) = sum(|A_i|^p)`, are a fixed, deterministic function of the activations. While these maps may visually highlight salient regions, they do not constitute an attention mechanism in the conventional sense. The core contribution here is the empirical finding that distilling these compact, channel-wise statistics is more effective for knowledge transfer than distilling the full, high-dimensional activation tensors (as in FitNets). This is an interesting and valuable finding in the domain of knowledge distillation. However, framing it as "attention transfer" is misleading and obscures the true nature of the contribution, which is a new and effective target for distillation loss.

**Regarding Reason 2 (Disjointed Structure):** The paper is split between activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer. The activation-based method is clearly the primary contribution, with extensive experiments on multiple datasets and architectures. In contrast, the gradient-based method (Section 3.2 and 4.1.3) feels disconnected. It is evaluated only on a non-standard NIN model on CIFAR-10 (without batch normalization or random crops), making the results difficult to contextualize. The authors state this is due to the complexity of the required double backpropagation, but this limitation means the method is not demonstrated as a practical or generalizable alternative. The two approaches are never directly compared on a level playing field or combined, leaving the reader wondering about their relative merits. The inclusion of the gradient-based section detracts from the focus of the paper and adds little convincing evidence to support its claims, ultimately weakening the paper's narrative coherence.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The empirical results for the activation-based transfer method are strong and represent a potentially valuable contribution to the field of knowledge distillation. I would encourage the authors to revise the paper with the following points in mind:

*   **Reframing the Contribution:** Consider reframing the paper to focus exclusively on the activation-based method. A more accurate title and terminology, such as "Distilling Activation Summaries" or "Feature Statistic Transfer," would clarify the contribution and avoid confusion with conventional attention mechanisms.
*   **Clarifying Notation:** The notation in Equation (2) is ambiguous. The term `||Q||_2` for a matrix `Q` typically denotes the spectral norm, which is computationally expensive to compute. Please clarify if this is indeed what was used, or if it refers to the Frobenius norm or the L2 norm of the vectorized map. This is a critical implementation detail.
*   **Strengthening the Ablation:** The ablation study on which layers to apply the transfer loss is useful. This could be strengthened by providing more intuition or analysis on why certain layers (e.g., later groups in ResNet) are more effective for transfer than others.
*   **Expanding on ImageNet Results:** The successful application to ImageNet is a key result. The paper would be stronger if it included more analysis on *why* this method works where, as the authors claim, prior methods like knowledge distillation have struggled, especially between networks of different depths.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the exact definition of the matrix norm used in Equation (2) for normalizing the attention maps?
2.  Could you provide a justification for using the term "attention" to describe a fixed, post-hoc statistical summary of activations, and how you see this relating to the broader literature on learned attention mechanisms?
3.  What was the motivation for including the gradient-based transfer method, given its limited evaluation and disconnect from the activation-based method? Do you see specific scenarios where it might be preferable?
4.  In your ImageNet experiments, you mention that standard Knowledge Distillation (KD) "hurts convergence." Could you elaborate on this finding and offer any hypotheses as to why your proposed activation-based transfer is more stable and effective in this large-scale, cross-architecture setting?
