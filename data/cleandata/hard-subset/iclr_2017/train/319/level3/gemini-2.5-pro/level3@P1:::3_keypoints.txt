- Proposal: Attention Transfer (AT) — train a student to mimic teacher attention maps to improve student performance.
- Attention map types: activation-based summaries (e.g., channel-wise sum/max with p) and gradient-based maps (gradient of loss wrt input).
- Training objective: add L2 loss between normalized teacher and student attention maps to align spatial focus.
- Empirical claim: AT consistently improves student networks across CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet.
- Complementarity: AT can be combined with Knowledge Distillation (KD) for additional gains.
- Comparison to prior work: AT outperforms full-activation transfer (FitNets-style), suggesting spatial summaries are more transferable than raw activations.
- Robustness: improvements hold across multiple architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN) and teacher/student pairs.
- ImageNet result: AT improves ResNet-18 student by ~1.1% top-1, reducing the gap to ResNet-34.
- Ablations: experiments show AT > full-activation transfer and that AT + KD yields further improvements.
- Qualitative support: visualizations indicate better alignment of attention maps with semantically relevant object parts.
- Weakness 1: lack of quantitative validation that teacher attention maps better localize objects (e.g., IoU with bounding boxes).
- Weakness 2: limited analysis/guidance on choosing among attention map types (activation vs gradient, sum vs max, choice of p).
- Weakness 3: hyperparameter sensitivity (loss weight β) not analyzed; selection process unclear.
- Weakness 4: results could be better framed in terms of accuracy vs efficiency (parameters, FLOPs, inference speed).
- Clarifying Q1: have you tried AT when teacher and student share the same architecture to see if AT acts as a regularizer?
- Clarifying Q2: please include teacher validation curves in Figure 7 to show how much gap AT closes visually.
- Clarifying Q3: expand on batch normalization difficulties in gradient-based AT — are they theoretical or implementation issues?
