### **1. Summary**

This paper introduces a novel method for knowledge transfer between neural networks, termed Attention Transfer (AT). The core idea is to improve a smaller "student" network by training it to mimic the "attention maps" of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The authors propose two distinct ways to define these spatial attention maps: one based on summaries of activation tensors across channels (activation-based AT) and another based on the gradient of the loss with respect to the input (gradient-based AT).

The paper's main contributions are: (1) proposing attention as a new modality for knowledge transfer; (2) defining several specific activation-based attention mapping functions and a gradient-based alternative; (3) demonstrating experimentally that transferring attention consistently improves student network performance across a variety of datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN). A particularly interesting finding is that transferring these compressed attention maps can be more effective than transferring the full activation tensors, as is done in prior work like FitNets.

### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Weak Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  While the proposed Attention Transfer (AT) method shows consistent improvements over student baselines, its performance gains over standard Knowledge Distillation (KD) are often marginal. The primary benefit appears when AT is combined with KD, suggesting it provides a small, complementary signal rather than being a superior standalone alternative.
2.  The experimental results on ImageNet, which could be the most impactful contribution, are presented as preliminary. The authors acknowledge a lack of hyperparameter tuning and incomplete experiments, which undermines confidence in the reported improvements and makes it difficult to fairly assess the method's effectiveness on large-scale tasks.

### **3. Supporting Arguments**

The core concept of transferring attention is intuitive and well-motivated. However, the experimental support for its superiority over existing methods is not consistently strong.

On the CIFAR-10 dataset (Table 1), using a WRN-16-2 teacher and WRN-16-1 student, the baseline student achieves 8.77% error. Standard Knowledge Distillation (KD) reduces this to 7.74%. The proposed Attention Transfer (AT) method on its own only achieves 7.93%, which is a smaller improvement than KD. The best result of 7.55% is achieved by combining AT+KD, but the incremental improvement over KD alone is only 0.19%. This suggests that AT is a useful regularizer but perhaps not a revolutionary step beyond KD on this benchmark.

The transfer learning experiments (Table 4) present a more mixed picture. On the MIT Scenes dataset, AT and KD perform identically (17.0% accuracy), while their combination offers a minor boost. On the CUB fine-grained classification dataset, however, AT provides a significant improvement over the student (22.8% vs. 25.1%) and is substantially better than KD (24.1%). This is a strong result for the paper, hinting that AT is particularly well-suited for tasks requiring localization of discriminative regions. However, this success in a specific domain does not fully offset the less compelling results elsewhere.

The most significant concern lies with the ImageNet experiments (Section 4.2.2). The authors report a compelling 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement for a ResNet-18 student. They also claim that KD fails to work in their setup and that AT may be the "first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." These are very strong claims that require exceptionally rigorous evidence. Unfortunately, the authors candidly state, "we did not have time to tune any hyperparameters" and "we plan to update the paper with losses on all 4 groups of residual blocks." This preliminary nature makes the results difficult to interpret. The failure of KD could be due to the same lack of tuning, and without a properly optimized baseline, the significance of AT's improvement is unclear. For a claim of this magnitude, a more thorough and complete experimental evaluation is necessary.

### **4. Additional Feedback**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not the primary basis for my decision.
*   The paper is well-written, and the central idea is elegant. The connection between network performance and the quality of its attention maps (Fig. 4) is a nice piece of analysis.
*   The strong performance on the CUB dataset is very promising. Expanding on *why* AT excels here compared to KD would strengthen the paper's narrative. Is it because fine-grained recognition relies more heavily on intermediate features that capture specific object parts, which is precisely what AT transfers? A short qualitative analysis could be very insightful.
*   The gradient-based attention transfer experiments feel somewhat disconnected and underexplored. They are limited to a simple network on CIFAR-10 without standard components like batch normalization. Given the complexity and limited results, it might be better to focus the paper entirely on the more developed and successful activation-based AT, perhaps moving the gradient-based section to an appendix.
*   Strengthening the ImageNet section is the most critical path to improving this paper. A full run with tuned hyperparameters for both AT and a KD baseline would provide the necessary evidence to substantiate the paper's main claims.

### **5. Questions for Authors**

*   Regarding the ImageNet experiments, could you elaborate on the difficulties encountered with Knowledge Distillation? Were the same hyperparameters used as on CIFAR, and was any tuning attempted? It is crucial to establish whether KD's failure is fundamental or a result of the experimental setup.
*   You show that transferring full activations (F-ActT) is less effective than AT. Could you clarify if your F-ActT implementation is a direct replication of the FitNets methodology (including their specific loss formulation and regressor layers), or a simplified L2 loss on the raw activations? This detail is important for making a direct comparison to prior work.
*   The combination of AT and KD consistently yields the best results. This suggests the information they transfer is complementary. Do you have any intuition or analysis on why this is the case? For example, is KD more about capturing the class probability distribution, while AT is about the spatial evidence for that distribution?
