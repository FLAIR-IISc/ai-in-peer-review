- Paper proposes "Attention Transfer" (AT): student mimics teacher's spatial statistics by summing p-norm of activations across channels.  
- Empirical results show AT consistently improves student performance and outperforms FitNet-style distillation on multiple vision datasets.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main concern 1: Framing as "attention" is misleading—method is more accurately spatial feature statistic matching, not true attention.  
- Main concern 2: Organizational and clarity issues throughout the paper.  
- The gradient-based attention transfer section is presented as a key contribution but is evaluated separately, underdeveloped, and not integrated or compared with the activation-based method.  
- Mathematical precision is lacking; e.g., the normalization in Eq. (2) and the exact 2-norm computation are unclear.  
- Suggested revision: Reframe paper to emphasize the core empirical finding that distilling spatial summary statistics outperforms distilling full feature maps.  
- Suggested revision: Discuss the method’s limitation to convolutional architectures.  
- Question 1: Justify using the term "attention" instead of "spatial activation statistics" and compare to conventional attention maps.  
- Question 2: Explain the rationale for including the gradient-based method without direct comparison or integration with the activation-based approach.  
- Question 3: Clarify the exact computation of the norm in Equation (2)—is it the standard L2 norm of the vectorized map?
