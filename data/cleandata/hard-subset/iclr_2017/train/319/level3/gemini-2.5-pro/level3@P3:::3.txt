### 1. Summary

This paper proposes Attention Transfer (AT), a knowledge transfer technique designed to improve the performance of a smaller "student" network by leveraging a larger, pre-trained "teacher" network. The core idea is that a student can learn more effectively by not only matching the teacher's output predictions but also by mimicking where the teacher "looks." To this end, the authors define spatial attention maps derived from a network's intermediate layers. They propose two main types of attention: activation-based maps, which are computed by summarizing feature map activations across channels (e.g., the sum or max of absolute activations raised to a power p), and gradient-based maps, which are defined as the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image. The training objective is augmented with an L2 loss that encourages the student's normalized attention maps to match the teacher's. The authors demonstrate through experiments on CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet that AT consistently improves the performance of student networks compared to training them from scratch. The method can also be combined with traditional knowledge distillation for further gains.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Weak Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper introduces an intuitive, simple, and effective method for knowledge transfer. The motivation to use "attention" as the transferred knowledge is well-explained and the implementation is straightforward. The experimental results show consistent and sometimes significant improvements for the student network across a wide range of datasets and architectures.
2.  However, the experimental evaluation lacks the depth required to make the paper's claims fully convincing. Key claims are supported only by qualitative evidence, there is no analysis of important hyperparameters, and the paper fails to provide clear guidance on how to apply its own proposed methods.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this work lies in its clear motivation and consistent empirical success. The idea of transferring a model's focus of attention is a natural extension of knowledge distillation. The method is simple to implement, requiring only an additional L2 loss term, and the results presented in Tables 1, 4, and 5 convincingly show that AT improves student performance over the baseline in all tested scenarios. The successful application to ImageNet is particularly noteworthy, as the authors point out that prior methods like FitNets and KD have not been widely shown to work in this setting. The paper is also well-written and easy to follow.

Despite these strengths, the paper has several weaknesses in its experimental rigor and analysis that temper its impact.

*   **Lack of Quantitative Evidence for Core Claims:** A central motivating claim is that higher-accuracy networks produce higher-quality attention maps that better correlate with object locations (visualized in Fig. 4). While visually plausible, this claim is never quantitatively verified. For datasets with available bounding boxes (like CUB or ImageNet), the authors could have measured the IoU or pointing accuracy between attention maps and ground-truth objects to substantiate this crucial point.
*   **Insufficient Hyperparameter Analysis:** The loss function includes a hyperparameter β that balances the attention loss against the classification loss. The paper provides only a vague rule of thumb for setting this value ("10^3 divided by number of elements in attention map and batch size") and offers no analysis of how performance varies with β. This makes the method difficult to reproduce and apply, as practitioners are left to guess this critical value.
*   **Unclear Guidance on Method Choice:** The paper proposes several activation-based maps (F_sum, F^p_sum, F^p_max) and a gradient-based map. However, the experiments do not provide a clear picture of the relative strengths and weaknesses of these choices or guidance on when to use one over the other. The comparison in Table 2 is only on one teacher-student pair on CIFAR-10.
*   **Performance and Efficiency Trade-offs Are Not Explored:** As is common with knowledge transfer, the student models never outperform their teachers. This is acceptable, but the paper misses the opportunity to characterize the efficiency gains. A comparison of model parameters, FLOPs, or inference speed would be necessary to understand the accuracy-vs-efficiency trade-off. Furthermore, a key experiment is missing: what happens when the student and teacher have the same architecture? This would test whether AT can act as a powerful regularizer to push performance beyond the teacher's baseline.

### 4. Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the paper and are not necessarily part of the decision assessment.

*   In Figure 7b, the student's validation loss with AT appears to increase towards the end of training, suggesting overfitting, even though its final accuracy is better. It would be beneficial to also plot the teacher's training and validation loss curves for a more complete comparison and to comment on this behavior.
*   The novelty of the method is somewhat incremental compared to FitNets, which also transferred knowledge from intermediate layers using an L2 loss. The main distinction is the aggregation of features into a 2D attention map. While the paper shows AT outperforms full activation transfer (F-ActT), a more detailed discussion of the conceptual differences would strengthen the paper.

**Questions for Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis or intuition on when to choose a specific attention map definition (e.g., sum vs. max, activation-based vs. gradient-based) for a given task or architecture?
2.  Could you elaborate on the selection of the hyperparameter β? An ablation study showing the sensitivity of the results to this parameter would significantly strengthen the paper's practical utility.
3.  Have you considered quantitatively evaluating the core claim that better models produce better attention? For example, by measuring the correlation between attention maps and ground-truth object locations on a dataset like CUB-200?
4.  What is the effect of Attention Transfer when the student and teacher networks share the same architecture? Does AT act as a regularizer and help the student surpass the teacher's final performance? This seems like a critical experiment to understand the method's full potential.
