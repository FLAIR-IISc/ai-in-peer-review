### Summary

This paper introduces Attention Transfer (AT), a novel knowledge transfer technique designed to improve the performance of a smaller "student" convolutional neural network by leveraging information from a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The core idea is to train the student not only on the primary task (e.g., classification) but also to mimic the "attention maps" of the teacher. The authors propose two methods for defining these spatial attention maps: 1) activation-based maps, which summarize the statistics (e.g., sum of squared values) of activation tensors across the channel dimension, and 2) gradient-based maps, which capture the sensitivity of the network's output to its input. Through extensive experiments on CIFAR, CUB, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, the paper demonstrates that AT provides consistent and significant performance improvements for student networks across various architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN). Notably, the authors show that AT outperforms a similar transfer of full activation tensors and is one of the first knowledge transfer methods to show success on the large-scale ImageNet dataset.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a well-written and well-motivated paper that presents a simple, intuitive, and effective method for knowledge distillation. The key reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Strong and Consistent Empirical Results:** The proposed Attention Transfer method consistently improves the performance of student networks across a diverse set of datasets and modern architectures. The successful application to ImageNet is particularly noteworthy, as the authors rightly point out that other distillation methods have had limited reported success on this scale.
2.  **Novel Formulation of Knowledge Transfer:** The paper makes a valuable contribution by framing knowledge transfer through the lens of attention. The idea of compressing intermediate layer information into spatial attention maps, rather than using the full, high-dimensional activation tensors (as in FitNets), is elegant and empirically shown to be more effective.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully addresses the key questions for a strong submission.

**1. Problem Tackled:** The paper tackles the problem of model compression and knowledge transfer, specifically how to train a compact and efficient "student" network to achieve performance closer to that of a much larger "teacher" network.

**2. Motivation and Literature Placement:** The approach is well-motivated by drawing an analogy to human visual attention and is correctly positioned within the existing literature on knowledge distillation (e.g., Hinton et al., 2015; Romero et al., 2014) and network visualization (e.g., Simonyan et al., 2014). It presents a logical evolution of hint-based learning by proposing a more structured and compressed form of "hint" in the form of an attention map.

**3. Support for Claims:** The central claim—that forcing a student to mimic a teacher's attention maps improves its performance—is strongly supported by the experimental results in Tables 1, 3, 4, and 5. For instance, on ImageNet, AT improves a ResNet-18 student by 1.1% top-1 accuracy, closing a significant portion of the gap to the ResNet-34 teacher. The claim that transferring summarized attention is more effective than transferring full activations is directly supported by the "F-ActT" comparison in Table 1.

While the paper is strong, there are some weaknesses. The qualitative claim that better networks produce better-focused attention maps (Fig. 4) is compelling but lacks quantitative validation. The student models, while improved, never surpass the teacher models, which is typical for distillation but frames the contribution as improving efficiency rather than achieving a new state-of-the-art. Finally, the novelty could be seen as incremental over FitNets, but the proposed formulation and strong results justify its contribution.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Quantitative Analysis of Attention Maps:** The visual evidence in Figure 4 is insightful, but the claim that stronger networks have better attention could be made much more rigorous. For datasets like CUB or ImageNet, you could quantify the quality of attention maps by measuring their spatial overlap (e.g., IoU) with ground-truth object bounding boxes and show a correlation between this metric and model accuracy.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The loss weighting parameter `β` is critical to balancing the task loss and the attention transfer loss. The paper provides a rule of thumb for setting it but would benefit from a sensitivity analysis showing how student performance varies with different values of `β`.
*   **Guidance on Attention Map Choice:** The paper proposes several activation-based maps (sum vs. max, different powers `p`) and a gradient-based map. Some practical guidance or analysis on when one type might be preferable to another would be very useful for practitioners.
*   **Expanded Gradient-Based AT Experiments:** The gradient-based experiments are limited to a specific setting on CIFAR-10 without batch normalization. Broadening these experiments would strengthen this aspect of the paper's contribution.

### Questions for Authors

*   The paper shows strong results for transferring knowledge from a larger teacher to a smaller student. Have you experimented with using AT between a teacher and a student of the *exact same architecture*? Could this serve as a form of self-distillation or regularization to help the student converge to a better local minimum, or does the benefit disappear when capacities are matched?
*   You mention that using batch normalization with gradient-based AT is challenging. Could you elaborate on the specific difficulties encountered in the "second backpropagation" step and any potential solutions you have considered?
*   Your experiments apply attention transfer at the end of each residual group. Have you explored the impact of the location and number of transfer points? For example, is transferring attention from just the final layers as effective as transferring from multiple stages of the network?
