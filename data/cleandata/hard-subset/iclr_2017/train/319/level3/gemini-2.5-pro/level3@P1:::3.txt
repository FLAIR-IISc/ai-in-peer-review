### Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel knowledge transfer method called Attention Transfer (AT). The core idea is to improve a smaller "student" network by training it to mimic the "attention maps" of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The authors define attention maps in two ways: (1) activation-based, by summarizing activation tensors across channels (e.g., sum of absolute values powered by p), and (2) gradient-based, using the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image. By adding an L2 loss between the student's and teacher's normalized attention maps, the student is encouraged to focus on similar spatial regions as the teacher. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments on CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet that this technique consistently improves the student network's performance. The method is shown to be complementary to knowledge distillation and more effective than transferring full activation tensors, as done in prior work like FitNets.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and demonstrates consistent and significant performance improvements for student models across a wide variety of datasets (from CIFAR to ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN). This robustness is a key strength of the paper.
2.  **Novel and Effective Form of Knowledge Transfer:** The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of knowledge distillation. By showing that transferring compressed attention information is more effective than transferring full, uncompressed activation maps (a FitNets-style approach), the authors provide a new insight into what constitutes important, transferable knowledge between networks. The successful application of this method to ImageNet is particularly noteworthy.

### Supporting Arguments

The motivation for this work is clear and well-grounded in both human cognition and existing work on artificial attention. The central hypothesis—that a student network can learn more effectively if it is guided to focus on the same image regions as a proficient teacher—is compelling.

The empirical validation is thorough. The authors test various teacher/student pairs with different depths and widths on multiple standard benchmarks. In all cases, Attention Transfer (AT) provides a clear boost over the baseline student model (Table 1, 4, 5). For example, on ImageNet, AT improves a ResNet-18 student by 1.1% top-1 accuracy, closing a significant portion of the gap to the ResNet-34 teacher. This is a strong result, especially given the authors' claim that this is the first successful application of such a knowledge transfer method on ImageNet.

The ablation studies strengthen the paper's claims. The comparison in Table 1 between AT and full-activation transfer ("F-ActT") is crucial. The finding that AT outperforms full-activation transfer suggests that the spatial summary of feature importance is a more potent and distilled form of knowledge than the raw feature activations themselves. Furthermore, demonstrating that AT can be combined with Knowledge Distillation (KD) to yield further gains shows its complementarity to existing techniques. The visual evidence provided in Figure 4 and Figure 6, while qualitative, effectively illustrates the core concept: as models become more accurate (or are trained with AT), their attention maps appear to align better with semantically relevant object parts.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Quantitative Evidence for Attention Maps:** The paper's motivation relies on the claim that higher-accuracy networks produce "better" attention maps that correlate more strongly with target objects (Sec 3.1, Fig. 4). This is only supported visually. The work would be significantly stronger if this claim were supported with quantitative evidence. For example, on datasets with available bounding boxes (like CUB or ImageNet localization), you could measure the Intersection over Union (IoU) between thresholded attention maps and the ground-truth boxes to show that teacher maps have higher IoU than student maps, and that AT improves the student's map IoU.
2.  **Guidance on Attention Map Choice:** The paper introduces several types of attention maps (activation-based `F_sum`, `F_max` with varying `p`, and gradient-based). However, there is limited analysis of the trade-offs between them. Table 2 suggests sum-based functions outperform max-based ones on one CIFAR task, but it is unclear why. When should one prefer gradient-based over activation-based attention? A discussion providing intuition on what different map types capture and in which scenarios they might be most useful would increase the paper's practical impact.
3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The loss weight `β` is a critical hyperparameter, but its selection process and sensitivity are not discussed. The paper mentions it is set based on the number of elements in the attention map, but a sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with `β` would add to the robustness of the claims.
4.  **Framing of Results:** The student models, even with AT, do not outperform their teachers. This is expected, as they have lower capacity. However, the paper could better frame its contribution in terms of the trade-off between accuracy and efficiency (e.g., parameters, FLOPs). For instance, "AT allows a ResNet-18 model to recover X% of the accuracy gap to a ResNet-34, while being Y% faster at inference."

**Clarifying Questions:**

1.  Have you experimented with an attention transfer setup where the student and teacher share the same architecture? It would be interesting to see if AT can act as a regularizer in this setting to improve performance beyond the baseline teacher model.
2.  In Figure 7, the validation curves for the teacher model are not shown. Including them would provide a clearer visual context of how much of the performance gap between the baseline student and the teacher is closed by AT. Could you add these curves?
3.  Regarding the gradient-based AT experiments, you mention difficulties with batch normalization in the second backpropagation step. Could you elaborate on this? Is the issue theoretical (e.g., how to define the gradient through batch statistics) or a software implementation challenge?
