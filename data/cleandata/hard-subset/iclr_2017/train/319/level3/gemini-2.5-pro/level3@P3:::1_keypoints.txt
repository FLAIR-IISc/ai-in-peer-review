- Proposes Attention Transfer (AT): train a student to mimic teacher spatial attention maps as a compact form of knowledge transfer.
- Defines two attention map types: activation-based (efficient) and gradient-based (more complex).
- Experiments on CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet show AT improves student performance and complements Knowledge Distillation (KD).
- AT can outperform transferring full activation tensors (FitNets-style) and combining AT+KD yields best results on several benchmarks.
- Decision: Weak Accept — idea is novel and promising but experimental validation on large-scale data is weak.
- Main strength: simplicity and intuition of transferring compressed 2D attention maps that are computationally cheap and effective.
- Inconsistent gains: AT outperforms KD on CIFAR and CUB but performs identically to KD on MIT Scenes, raising questions about generality.
- ImageNet experiments are preliminary, use a single teacher-student pair, untuned hyperparameters, and only partial application of AT.
- Claims that KD "hurts convergence" on ImageNet are anecdotal and unsupported by training curves or hyperparameter details.
- The claim that AT is the first successful transfer on ImageNet is unsubstantiated without fair comparisons to existing methods.
- Suggestion: strengthen ImageNet section by using all transfer locations, fair KD baselines, training curves, and careful hyperparameter tuning.
- Suggestion: provide more implementation details and stronger evaluation for the FitNets-style (F-ActT) baseline to validate comparative gains.
- Suggestion: expand and clarify the gradient-based AT experiments, including issues like batch normalization and applicability to modern architectures.
- Questions for authors: clarify KD difficulties on ResNet-18/34 ImageNet (temperatures, alphas, learning curves), sensitivity of AT loss weight β, and which teacher layers are most effective for transfer.
