- Proposes Attention Transfer (AT): train student to match teacher's spatial attention maps (activation- and gradient-based) using an added L2 loss on normalized maps.  
- Defines activation-based maps (channel-sum/max with power p) and gradient-based maps (loss gradient w.r.t. input).  
- Demonstrates consistent student performance gains on CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet; can combine with knowledge distillation.  
- Decision: Weak Accept.  
- Strength: Intuitive, simple, easy-to-implement method with consistent empirical improvements and notable ImageNet results.  
- Weakness: Lacks quantitative evidence that higher-accuracy teachers produce better attention (no IoU/pointing metrics against ground-truth).  
- Weakness: Insufficient analysis of hyperparameter β and no sensitivity/ablation study for its setting.  
- Weakness: Unclear guidance on choosing among attention-map variants (sum vs max, activation vs gradient); limited comparisons (Table 2 limited to one pair).  
- Weakness: Does not analyze accuracy-vs-efficiency trade-offs (parameters, FLOPs, inference speed) or show students outperforming teachers.  
- Missing experiment: effect of AT when student and teacher share the same architecture (can AT act as a regularizer to exceed teacher?).  
- Additional note: Student validation loss curves suggest possible overfitting; authors should show teacher curves and discuss.  
- Novelty concern: Method is incremental relative to FitNets; needs clearer conceptual distinction beyond 2D aggregation.  
- Questions for authors requested: guidance on attention-map choice, β selection and sensitivity, quantitative evaluation of attention vs object locations, and same-architecture student-teacher results.
