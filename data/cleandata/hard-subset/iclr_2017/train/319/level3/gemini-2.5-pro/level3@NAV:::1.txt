This paper proposes Attention Transfer (AT), a novel and intuitive knowledge transfer method. The core idea is to guide a smaller student network to mimic the spatial attention maps of a larger, pre-trained teacher network. The authors define attention maps based on either summarizing intermediate activations across channels or using the gradient of the loss with respect to the input. The method is presented as an alternative or complement to existing knowledge distillation techniques, with the claim that transferring summarized attention information is more effective than transferring entire activation tensors. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements on CIFAR, CUB, and Scenes datasets.

I recommend a **Weak Accept**. My decision is based on the novelty of the proposed method, which is well-motivated and shows promise on smaller datasets. However, this positive assessment is tempered by the preliminary and insufficient experimental results on the large-scale ImageNet dataset, which is crucial for validating the method’s general applicability and impact.

The main strength of this paper is its core idea. Framing knowledge transfer as a process of teaching a student network *where* to look is elegant. The activation-based attention maps are simple, computationally efficient, and the experiments on CIFAR convincingly show they are more effective than transferring full activation tensors (FitNets-style hints). The results on the CUB and Scenes fine-tuning tasks also effectively demonstrate the benefits of the approach.

The primary weakness is the experimental evaluation on ImageNet (Section 4.2.2). The results are based on a single student-teacher pair with untuned hyperparameters and a partial application of the method (losses on only two of four groups). The bold claim that AT may be "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is not supported by this limited evidence, especially since the failure of the Knowledge Distillation (KD) baseline may be due to a lack of hyperparameter tuning. This section weakens the paper's overall claims of significance.

To improve the paper, I strongly recommend expanding the ImageNet experiments. A more thorough evaluation with multiple architectures and a properly tuned KD baseline would substantially strengthen the paper's contributions. I would be willing to raise my score if the authors can provide a more comprehensive and rigorous evaluation in this section.

I have a few questions for the authors:
1.  Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter search conducted for the Knowledge Distillation baseline on ImageNet? Why do you believe it failed to converge properly?
2.  The paper states a plan to add transfer losses to all four residual groups for the ImageNet experiment. Have you run this experiment, and if so, could you share the results?
