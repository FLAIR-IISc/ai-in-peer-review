This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks by transferring attention maps from a teacher network to a student network. The authors define attention using both activation-based and gradient-based methods and demonstrate consistent improvements across various datasets and architectures. The core idea – leveraging attention as a transfer mechanism – is well-motivated and aligns with recent research exploring interpretability and knowledge distillation in deep learning.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper presents an interesting idea and demonstrates some positive results, the improvements are often marginal, and the experimental validation, particularly on ImageNet, lacks sufficient depth to justify acceptance at a competitive conference like ICLR.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper’s primary contribution lies in framing attention as a mechanism for knowledge transfer. The exploration of both activation-based and gradient-based attention maps is a sensible approach. The experimental results on CIFAR are encouraging, demonstrating consistent (though not overwhelming) improvements. However, the gains on larger datasets like CUB and Scenes, while positive, don't showcase a substantial leap in performance that would warrant strong acceptance. 

The main weakness lies in the ImageNet experiments (Section 4.2.2). The authors acknowledge limited hyperparameter tuning and report only a 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement. This is not a compelling result, especially considering the computational cost associated with ImageNet training. The statement that they "were not able to achieve positive results with KD" is concerning, as knowledge distillation is a well-established technique, and the lack of success requires further investigation. The claim that this work is “the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet” is also overstated, given the broad history of transfer learning, including knowledge distillation, on this dataset. It feels like this section was included to demonstrate scalability, but it lacks the rigor to support strong claims.

The comparison between attention transfer and full activation transfer is valuable, highlighting the benefits of focusing on attention maps. However, a more thorough investigation into *why* attention transfer is more effective than full activation transfer would strengthen the paper.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more targeted ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components, such as the specific attention mapping functions (Fsum, Fpsum, Fpmax) and the placement of transfer losses across different layers.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the results to the β parameter in the loss function (Eq. 2).
*   **Computational Cost:** Briefly discuss the computational overhead introduced by the attention transfer mechanism, especially during training and inference.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** Include more qualitative examples of the attention maps learned by the student network compared to the teacher network.  Visualizing the alignment of attention maps would be particularly informative. (The mention of Fig 6 in the paper is helpful in this regard).
*   **Related Work:** Expand the related work section to include more recent advances in knowledge distillation and attention mechanisms beyond those cited. Specifically, mention more recent advancements in attention visualization techniques.
* **ImageNet Experiment Detail:** Elaborate on why KD failed on ImageNet. Was it parameter sensitivity, different learning rates, or something else? What specific modifications were attempted?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why does attention transfer seem to outperform full activation transfer? Is there a theoretical explanation for this phenomenon, or is it purely empirical?
2.  The ImageNet results are somewhat underwhelming. What specific steps were taken to try and improve the performance of knowledge distillation in that setting? Could you provide more detail on the hyperparameters used and any troubleshooting efforts?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the β parameter in equation 2? Did you perform any hyperparameter search on this value?
4.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with calculating and applying the attention transfer losses, especially for deeper networks?
5.  Are there any scenarios where you would *not* expect attention transfer to be beneficial? Are there specific network architectures or datasets where this approach might be less effective?
