This paper proposes a modified knowledge distillation framework that improves student network performance by minimizing differences in summed statistics across feature maps of teacher and student networks. The method is empirically demonstrated to outperform FitNet-style distillation on multiple computer vision datasets. The paper is generally well-written and presents a clear motivation for the work.

**Reject** with a request for major revisions. While the method demonstrates promising results, organizational issues, unclear notation, and a somewhat misleading framing of the “attention” concept hinder a clear understanding and assessment of the contribution. Furthermore, a substantial portion of the work (gradient-based attention transfer) feels disconnected from the core contribution and adds little value.

The central idea of distilling summed feature statistics is a valuable extension to existing knowledge distillation techniques. The empirical results presented across several datasets suggest the approach is effective in improving student network performance. The authors correctly identify limitations of prior methods like FitNets and propose a viable alternative. The provided code repository is also a positive aspect, facilitating reproducibility. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. First, the framing of the work around “attention transfer” is misleading. The method distills summed (or max) activations—statistics of feature maps—and does *not* directly transfer attention maps in the sense of spatial weighting reflecting network focus. This terminology could lead readers to believe the approach is related to attention mechanisms in a more direct way than it is. Second, the gradient-based attention transfer section feels tacked on and lacks integration with the main contribution. It's unclear why it’s included, as it is neither directly compared to activation-based transfer, nor combined with it in a systematic way. The computational complexity described for calculating the 2-norm in Eq.(2) raises concerns; the authors likely intended the Frobenius norm, and this should be clarified. Third, the notation is occasionally unclear, making it difficult to follow the mathematical derivations. For example, the meaning of *QjS* and *QjT* isn’t immediately obvious without careful reading. Finally, the organization could be improved; the experimental section feels somewhat disjointed, with subsections that don’t flow logically.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Reframe the “attention” terminology:**  Consider using terms like “feature statistic transfer” or “representation distillation” to more accurately reflect the method.
*   **Streamline the paper:**  The gradient-based attention transfer section could be removed or significantly condensed if it doesn’t provide a clear benefit to the main contribution. If retained, a direct comparison with the activation-based method is necessary.
*   **Clarify the 2-norm calculation:** Explicitly state whether the Frobenius norm or the spectral norm is used in Eq.(2) and justify the choice. If it's the Frobenius norm, revise the equation accordingly.
*   **Improve notation:** Define all variables clearly and consistently throughout the paper.
*   **Enhance organization:** Restructure the experimental section to present a more coherent narrative.
*   **Ablation studies:** Provide more detailed ablation studies to analyze the contribution of different components of the method (e.g., the effect of different norm types, the importance of layer selection for transfer).
*  **Discussion of computational cost:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed method compared to other knowledge distillation techniques.



**Questions for the authors:**

1.  Regarding Eq.(2), can you clarify which norm (*l2*, Frobenius, or spectral) is used, and what is the computational complexity of calculating it given the dimensions of *QjS* and *QjT*?
2.  Why was the gradient-based attention transfer section included, and what specific insight or benefit does it provide to the overall work? If it’s not central to the main contribution, would it be acceptable to remove it to streamline the paper?
3.  What design choices were considered when selecting which layers to apply attention transfer to (e.g., why the last two residual blocks in the ImageNet experiments)? Was a systematic investigation performed to determine the optimal layer selection strategy?
4.  In the experiments, you mention decaying β during training. What schedule was used for this decay, and how was it determined?
