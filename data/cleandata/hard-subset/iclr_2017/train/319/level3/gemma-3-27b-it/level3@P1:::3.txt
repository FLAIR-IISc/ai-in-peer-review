## Review of "Attention Transfer"

This paper proposes a method for transferring knowledge from a powerful teacher convolutional neural network (CNN) to a student network by aligning their attention maps. The authors define attention as spatial maps encoding network focus, exploring both activation-based and gradient-based approaches.  Experimental results across various datasets and architectures demonstrate consistent performance improvements in student networks via this attention transfer, and the authors provide code for reproducibility.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents a well-motivated and clearly written approach, and demonstrates consistent improvements in student network performance, the magnitude of these improvements and the lack of a comprehensive analysis of the proposed methods lead to a rejection. The work feels incremental and lacks a strong justification for its contribution to the field beyond demonstrating that attention maps can be useful for knowledge transfer. 

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary concern is the limited practical impact of the observed improvements.  While the authors consistently show gains with attention transfer, the student networks still significantly underperform the teacher networks. The reported improvements, while statistically significant in some cases, are often modest (e.g., ~1% on ImageNet) and do not represent a breakthrough in knowledge distillation or student-teacher learning.  Furthermore, the paper doesn’t address whether these gains justify the added computational cost of calculating and matching attention maps.  

The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the trade-off between performance and efficiency. Since students have fewer parameters, a demonstration of speedup achieved by using a smaller, attention-informed student network instead of the larger teacher would be compelling.  The paper currently focuses solely on accuracy.

Another weakness lies in the analysis of different attention map types.  While the authors explore activation-based and gradient-based attention, they lack a clear justification for *when* to use one over the other. The comparison is largely empirical and lacks theoretical grounding. A more rigorous evaluation, perhaps with an ablation study specifically focused on the impact of different attention map definitions, is needed.

Finally, the impact of the regularization parameter, β, controlling the strength of the attention transfer loss, is not thoroughly investigated.  A sensitivity analysis exploring the effect of different β values on performance would be valuable. The statement that "β usually varies about 0.1" is vague and lacks justification.

**Additional Feedback:**

The following points, while not directly influencing the rejection decision, could significantly improve the paper:

*   **Quantitative Validation of Attention Map Correlation:** The authors claim that higher-accuracy networks exhibit stronger spatial correlation between predicted objects and attention maps (Figure 4). This observation should be supported by quantitative metrics (e.g., Intersection over Union or correlation coefficients) instead of relying solely on visual inspection.
*   **Comparison to FitNets:** The paper positions its work relative to FitNets, but a more direct comparison – including a fair evaluation of FitNets under the same experimental conditions – would clarify the novelty and benefits of the proposed approach.
*   **Full Training Curves:**  Providing complete training curves (loss and accuracy) for both the teacher and student networks would offer valuable insights into the learning dynamics and the impact of attention transfer.  Specifically, Figure 7b needs to include the teacher's training and validation loss for a meaningful comparison.
*   **Ablation Study:**  Conduct an ablation study to isolate the contributions of different components (e.g., activation-based vs. gradient-based attention, knowledge distillation, symmetry constraints).
*   **Horizontal Flip Invariance:** The reasoning behind enforcing horizontal flip invariance on gradient attention maps (equation 6) isn't clearly explained.  A more detailed explanation of the motivation and expected benefits would be helpful.
*   **Clarify Data Augmentation:**  Be explicit about all data augmentation techniques used during training, including for ImageNet experiments.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the computational overhead of calculating and transferring attention maps, and how does this impact the overall training time and inference speed?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the optimal β value and its sensitivity to different datasets and architectures?
3.  How does the performance of attention transfer compare to FitNets under identical experimental conditions?
4.  What is the rationale behind using l2 normalization of attention maps, and have you experimented with other normalization schemes?
5.  How does the method perform when the teacher and student networks have the *same* architecture and capacity? Would attention transfer still provide a benefit, and if so, what could explain it?
