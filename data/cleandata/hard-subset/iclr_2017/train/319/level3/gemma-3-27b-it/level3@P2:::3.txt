This paper proposes a knowledge transfer method based on matching attention maps between a teacher and student convolutional neural network. The authors define activation-based and gradient-based attention maps and demonstrate consistent performance improvements across various datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and network architectures by encouraging the student to mimic the teacher’s attention. The approach is well-motivated by the role of attention in both human and artificial vision, and the code is publicly available.

**Reject** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the paper presents a reasonable approach and demonstrates consistent improvements, the gains are relatively modest, the analysis lacks depth in several areas, and the claims regarding the interpretability of attention maps are not rigorously supported.

The paper clearly articulates the problem of transferring knowledge between CNNs, specifically focusing on attention mechanisms. The motivation drawing parallels between human and artificial attention is well-established, and the literature review adequately covers relevant work in attention mechanisms, knowledge distillation, and visualization techniques. The proposed methods – activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer – are logically sound and build upon existing concepts. The experimental setup, encompassing diverse datasets and architectures, is commendable. The consistent performance gains observed across these experiments demonstrate the potential of the proposed approach.

However, the support for the claims is insufficient in several key areas. While the authors claim that higher-accuracy networks have stronger spatial correlation between objects and attention maps (Fig. 4), this is presented visually without any quantitative measure of correlation. A statistical analysis comparing attention map alignment with accuracy would strengthen this argument. The impact of the β parameter (controlling the weight of the attention transfer loss) isn’t thoroughly explored. A sensitivity analysis demonstrating how performance varies with different β values is crucial for understanding the method's robustness. Furthermore, the experimental results on ImageNet, while positive, are limited by the lack of hyperparameter tuning. Simply stating that hyperparameters were kept from finetuning experiments is insufficient, as optimal values might differ for the AT task. Figure 7(b) needs to include the teacher’s training and validation loss for a meaningful comparison. A crucial aspect missing is a direct comparison of student and teacher inference speeds. Since students have fewer parameters, the paper should investigate the speed-up achieved by the student network and ask whether AT helps when student and teacher share the same architecture (e.g., a smaller ResNet-18 vs. a larger ResNet-34). The comparison between different attention mapping functions (Table 2) is also somewhat superficial. A more in-depth analysis of the characteristics of each function and their impact on transfer performance is needed.  The statement that "activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is a strong claim that requires careful justification. The paper doesn't adequately address whether existing KD methods were exhaustively explored.

Here are some points for improvement:

*   **Quantitative Analysis of Attention Maps:** Provide a quantitative metric (e.g., correlation coefficient, Intersection over Union) to support the claim about the relationship between attention map strength and network accuracy.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Conduct a sensitivity analysis of the β parameter to determine its optimal value and impact on performance.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to assess the individual contribution of activation-based vs. gradient-based attention transfer.
*   **Speed-Accuracy Trade-off:** Characterize the speed-up achieved by the student network compared to the teacher and investigate the impact of AT on this trade-off.
*   **Comparison with Baselines:** Provide a more comprehensive comparison with existing knowledge distillation techniques, particularly on ImageNet.
*   **Detailed Implementation:** Clarify the implementation details, such as the specific normalization techniques used and the optimization algorithm.
*   **Error Analysis:** Analyze the types of errors made by the student network and how attention transfer affects these errors.

I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  What is the computational overhead of calculating and applying the attention transfer loss compared to standard training?
2.  Did you explore other loss functions for matching attention maps besides the L2 loss, and if so, what were the results?
3.  How does the performance of AT vary with different student-to-teacher capacity ratios (e.g., ResNet-18 to ResNet-50)?
4.  Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process, especially for the ImageNet experiments?
5.  Regarding the claim of being the first successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet, could you elaborate on the KD methods that were tried and why they didn't work?
