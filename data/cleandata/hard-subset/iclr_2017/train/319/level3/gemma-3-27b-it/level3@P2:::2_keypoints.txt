- Core idea: transfer knowledge by aligning teacher and student activation maps; activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer proposed.  
- Empirical validation: method yields consistent performance improvements across datasets and architectures.  
- Verdict: paper rejected due to misleading terminology, underdeveloped gradient-based section, and limited presentation clarity.  
- Positive result: forcing student to mimic teacher activation patterns improves performance, with substantial ImageNet gains.  
- Experimental setup: comprehensive evaluation on CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet and multiple architectures (NIN, ResNet).  
- Ablations: useful comparisons (activation-based vs full activation transfer) clarify what is being transferred.  
- Reproducibility: code and models available, which is a strong point.  
- Terminology issue: calling activation maps “attention” is misleading; should use terms like “activation alignment” or “feature map transfer.”  
- Gradient-based transfer: feels disconnected, lacks unified experiments vs activation-based method, and double backprop explanation is terse.  
- Symmetry constraints: observed to help, but their connection to the main contribution is unclear.  
- Notation ambiguity: Eq. (2)’s norm is unclear (2-norm of flattened map vs Frobenius norm) and may be computationally expensive; needs clarification.  
- Requests for clarification/extension: analyze hyperparameter sensitivity (β), clarify norm computation and computational cost, discuss applicability beyond CNNs (e.g., transformers), and provide training/inference overhead breakdown.
