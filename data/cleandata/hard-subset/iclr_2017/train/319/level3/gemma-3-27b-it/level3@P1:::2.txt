## Review for ICLR 2017 Submission

This paper explores knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks via the transfer of “attention” information, specifically spatial attention maps derived from network activations and gradients. The authors propose methods for defining and transferring these attention maps from a teacher network to a student network, demonstrating performance improvements across several datasets and network architectures. They investigate both activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer, and show that activation-based transfer outperforms direct activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents a novel approach to knowledge transfer that yields promising empirical results, the framing of the work as “attention transfer” is misleading, and the paper suffers from organizational issues and a lack of clarity in certain areas. The benefits of the gradient-based approach are not convincingly demonstrated.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary issue is the overstatement of the connection to the concept of “attention” as used in cognitive science and increasingly in deep learning. The proposed method essentially transfers statistical summaries of feature activations (sums, maxes, powers) – these are not attention maps in the sense of dynamically weighting different input regions based on task relevance. Using the term “attention transfer” suggests a more nuanced mechanism than is actually being implemented.  This framing diminishes the contribution, which is a well-performing regularization/distillation technique.

Secondly, the paper feels somewhat disjointed. The gradient-based attention transfer section (Section 3.2) appears almost as an afterthought.  While the authors acknowledge the complexities of integrating it with batch normalization and don’t present a compelling comparison to the primary activation-based approach, including it without clear justification dilutes the focus of the paper. The results presented for gradient-based transfer are also limited to a specific configuration, making it difficult to assess its generalizability.

Finally, there are technical details that require clarification. The computation of the 2-norm in Equation (2) is computationally expensive and raises questions about implementation efficiency. It's unclear whether the authors intended the largest singular value or a more tractable approximation like the Frobenius norm. This detail impacts the scalability of the method.



**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

The core contribution – distilling knowledge through the transfer of summarized feature activations – is valuable. Here are suggestions to improve the paper:

*   **Reframing:**  Rename the method to something more accurate, such as “feature map distillation” or “activation-based knowledge transfer”. Remove the pervasive use of “attention” terminology.
*   **Streamlining:**  Focus the paper on the activation-based approach. If the gradient-based method shows potential, it could be presented as future work or a very brief preliminary investigation.
*   **Clarification of Implementation:**  Explicitly state how the 2-norm in Equation (2) is computed, and discuss its computational cost. Consider exploring a more efficient approximation.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Provide a more thorough ablation study to demonstrate the contribution of each component of the proposed method, such as the different attention mapping functions (Fsum, Fpsum, Fpmax).
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** Expand the comparison to include more relevant and recent knowledge distillation techniques beyond FitNets.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate and report the sensitivity of the method to the β parameter (Eq. 2).
* **Code Availability:** While code is available, providing a more detailed README and example scripts would improve reproducibility.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why was the term “attention transfer” chosen, given that the method primarily transfers statistical summaries of activations rather than dynamically weighted attention maps?
2.  What is the computational complexity of computing the 2-norm in Equation (2)?  Was the largest singular value used, or a different approximation?
3.  Why was the gradient-based attention transfer section included without a more thorough evaluation and comparison to the activation-based approach?  What were the specific challenges encountered with the gradient-based method that limited its exploration?
4.  Could you provide more details regarding the implementation of the second backpropagation step in the gradient-based method, particularly concerning the handling of batch normalization layers?
5. Can you elaborate on why KD does not work on ImageNet? What hyperparameter changes were tested?
