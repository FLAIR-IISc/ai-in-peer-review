- Proposes transferring attention maps from teacher to student (activation- and gradient-based) to improve knowledge transfer performance.  
- Experiments across multiple datasets and architectures show consistent but often marginal improvements over standard KD.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Primary reason: limited magnitude of improvement, especially weak/underdeveloped ImageNet results.  
- ImageNet results show only modest gains (≈1.1% top-1 for ResNet-18), raising concerns about practical significance.  
- Section 4.2.2 (ImageNet) is underdeveloped and needs further investigation to demonstrate scalability.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and builds appropriately on prior KD and attention work.  
- Ablation studies (e.g., attention mapping functions) are useful and implementation/code availability support reproducibility.  
- CIFAR improvements are consistent but modest, not clearly compelling.  
- Claim that this is the first successful transfer on ImageNet is overstated given existing KD literature; novelty is in transfer method, not feasibility.  
- Comparison with full-activation transfer favors attention maps but doesn’t constitute a groundbreaking advance.  
- Authors’ difficulty replicating KD on ImageNet raises concerns about robustness and generalizability.  
- Recommended ImageNet extensions: test more teacher/student architectures, run extensive hyperparameter search, compare to recent KD methods, and visualize attention maps.  
- Suggested deeper ablations: vary loss weights (β) and normalization techniques to better understand effects.  
- Lacks theoretical discussion on why attention maps might outperform full activations; a brief justification would strengthen the paper.  
- Reviewer requests clarification on KD replication failure, detailed hyperparameter search (β ranges), dataset-specific performance differences (CUB vs Scenes), BN issues in gradient-based transfer, and what specific attention-map information enables better transfer.
