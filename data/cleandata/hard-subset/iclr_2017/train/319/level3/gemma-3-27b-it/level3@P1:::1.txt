## Review of “Attention Transfer” for ICLR 2017

This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks by transferring attention maps from a teacher network to a student network. The authors define attention using both activation-based and gradient-based methods, and demonstrate consistent performance improvements across various datasets and network architectures. The work is well-motivated by the observation of differing attentional strategies within artificial vision systems, and connects to concepts from both computer vision and cognitive science. The availability of code and models is a definite strength.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting idea and demonstrates some positive results, the improvements reported are often marginal and, particularly in the larger-scale ImageNet experiments, lack sufficient evidence to justify acceptance. The reported gains, while present, don’t appear substantial enough to represent a significant advancement in the field, and the experimental section feels somewhat incomplete.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The central claim – that transferring attention maps improves student network performance – is generally supported by the experimental results. The authors demonstrate consistent, albeit often small, improvements on CIFAR datasets using both activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer. The ablation studies on CIFAR (Table 1 & 2) offer insights into the impact of different attention mapping functions and the benefits of combining attention transfer with knowledge distillation. The analysis showing that attention transfer outperforms direct activation transfer is a noteworthy finding. 

However, the impact appears to diminish as the complexity of the task and dataset increases. While improvements are reported on CUB and Scenes, the ImageNet results, crucial for demonstrating the generalizability of the approach, are less compelling. The statement that “attention transfer provides significant improvements” feels overstated given the 1.1% and 0.8% gains on ImageNet top-1 and top-5 accuracy respectively. These improvements, while positive, are not dramatically larger than what might be expected from careful hyperparameter tuning or other standard optimization techniques. Furthermore, the authors acknowledge a lack of thorough hyperparameter tuning for the ImageNet experiments, and report difficulties in getting Knowledge Distillation to work effectively, raising concerns about the robustness of their conclusions. The comparison to KD is particularly weak; a functioning KD baseline would have provided a stronger basis for evaluating the added value of attention transfer.

The gradient-based attention transfer experiments are relatively limited, focusing primarily on a specific architecture and dataset (NIN on CIFAR). While the results are promising, the scope is too narrow to draw firm conclusions about the effectiveness of this approach more broadly. The discussion around the complexities of applying gradient-based transfer in conjunction with batch normalization suggests an incomplete understanding of the interaction between these techniques, and the need for further investigation.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Expand ImageNet Experiments:** A more comprehensive evaluation on ImageNet is crucial. This should include a wider range of network architectures, careful hyperparameter tuning for both attention transfer and knowledge distillation, and a more rigorous comparison to strong KD baselines.
*   **Investigate Gradient-Based Attention:**  Expand the gradient-based attention transfer experiments to include more complex architectures and datasets. Address the challenges of combining this approach with batch normalization.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Further ablation studies could provide deeper insights into the specific contributions of different components of the proposed method. For instance, examining the impact of transferring attention from different layers of the teacher network.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests for all experimental results to determine whether the observed improvements are reliable.
*   **Computational Cost:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational overhead associated with attention transfer. Is the added cost justified by the performance gains?
* **Clarify the novelty:** Compared to existing knowledge distillation and hint learning approaches (like FitNets), what is the unique contribution of this method? Is the attention map truly providing *new* information, or is it simply a more efficient way of encoding existing knowledge?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   You mention difficulties with KD on ImageNet. Could you elaborate on the specific challenges you encountered and the attempts you made to overcome them?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of the parameter β in the attention transfer loss (Equation 2)? Did you perform a sensitivity analysis?
*   Do you observe any qualitative differences in the attention maps learned by the student network with and without attention transfer? Could you provide some visualizations?
*   Beyond the datasets presented, do you anticipate that attention transfer will be more or less effective for tasks where attention is particularly crucial, such as object detection or semantic segmentation?
