This paper proposes a method for transferring knowledge from a teacher convolutional neural network to a student network by minimizing the difference between their activation maps. The authors define attention as spatial maps encoding where the network focuses, and propose both activation-based and gradient-based methods for transferring this “attention.” Extensive experiments demonstrate consistent performance improvements across various datasets and architectures.

**Reject**

The core idea of transferring knowledge through activation map alignment is interesting and empirically validated. However, the use of the term “attention” is misleading, and the gradient-based section feels disconnected and underdeveloped, weakening the overall contribution. While the results are positive, the presentation and clarity of some aspects limit the impact of the work.

The paper successfully demonstrates that forcing a student network to mimic the activation patterns of a teacher network improves performance. The experimental setup is comprehensive, covering a range of datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet). The ablation studies, particularly comparing activation-based transfer to full activation transfer, provide valuable insight into *what* is being transferred. The reported improvements, especially on ImageNet, are substantial and suggest the method has practical merit. The availability of code and models is a significant plus for reproducibility and further research.

However, the central premise relies on equating activation statistics with “attention,” which is a misnomer. Attention mechanisms, as typically understood, involve weighting inputs based on relevance – a dynamic process. The proposed method, conversely, focuses on static alignment of overall activation patterns, which is more akin to feature matching or representation distillation. This semantic imprecision detracts from the conceptual clarity of the work.

The section on gradient-based attention transfer feels largely disconnected from the rest of the paper. It is presented as an alternative method, but lacks a direct comparison to the activation-based approach, particularly in a unified experimental setup. The description of the double backpropagation is also somewhat terse and could benefit from further explanation regarding practical implementation challenges and optimization strategies. The observation that symmetry constraints improve performance is interesting, but its connection to the main contribution remains unclear.

Furthermore, the notation in Eq. (2) is ambiguous. Computing the 2-norm of a flattened vectorized attention map (as implied) for large spatial dimensions is computationally expensive. It's unclear whether the authors intended the Frobenius norm or a different simplification. Clarification on this point is crucial.

**Additional Feedback & Questions:**

*   **Terminology:** The authors should reconsider the use of "attention" and perhaps adopt a more accurate descriptor, such as "activation alignment" or "feature map transfer.” This would avoid confusion with established attention mechanisms.
*   **Gradient-Based Transfer:** Either expand this section to provide a more thorough comparison with the activation-based method, or consider removing it to streamline the paper. If retained, a more detailed discussion of the computational cost and implementation challenges of double backpropagation is necessary.
*   **Norm Calculation:** Please clarify exactly how the norm in Eq. (2) is computed. Specifically, is it the Frobenius norm or a different method? What is the computational cost and how is it addressed?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions setting β based on attention map size, a more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would be valuable. How does performance vary with different values of β?
*   **Architectural Limitations:** The method currently appears limited to convolutional architectures. Could the approach be extended to other types of networks, such as transformers?
*   **Computational Cost:** Provide a breakdown of the computational overhead introduced by the attention transfer losses, both during training and inference.



Addressing these points would strengthen the paper’s clarity, impact, and overall scientific rigor.
