- Proposes transferring attention maps (activation- and gradient-based) from teacher to student CNNs as a novel knowledge transfer method.  
- Defines attention using both activation-based and gradient-based formulations.  
- Demonstrates consistent but often marginal performance improvements across datasets and architectures.  
- Availability of code and models is a clear strength.  
- CIFAR ablation studies show insights on attention mapping functions and benefit from combining attention transfer with knowledge distillation.  
- Attention transfer outperforms direct activation transfer in the reported experiments.  
- ImageNet results are weak (≈1.1% top-1, 0.8% top-5 gains) and insufficient to prove broad generalizability.  
- Lack of thorough hyperparameter tuning and difficulties getting KD to work on ImageNet raise concerns about robustness.  
- Comparison to Knowledge Distillation is inadequate; a working KD baseline is missing.  
- Gradient-based attention experiments are limited in scope (mainly NIN on CIFAR) and need broader validation.  
- Interaction between gradient-based transfer and batch normalization is unclear and requires further study.  
- Recommend expanding ImageNet experiments: more architectures, careful tuning, and stronger KD comparisons.  
- Recommend expanding gradient-based experiments to larger architectures/datasets and addressing BN challenges.  
- Call for more ablation studies (e.g., transferring attention from different teacher layers) and statistical significance testing.  
- Request a detailed analysis of computational overhead to justify the added cost of attention transfer.  
- Ask authors to clarify novelty relative to KD/Hint learning (e.g., FitNets) and whether attention maps add genuinely new information.  
- Questions for authors: elaborate on KD difficulties on ImageNet; sensitivity analysis for β in the loss; visualizations showing qualitative differences in student attention; expected effectiveness on tasks like detection/segmentation.
