This paper proposes a novel knowledge transfer method leveraging attention maps, specifically demonstrating that transferring attention maps from a teacher network to a student network can improve performance. The authors explore both activation-based and gradient-based attention maps, presenting experimental results across several datasets and architectures. While the proposed approach shows consistent improvements, the gains over standard knowledge distillation are often marginal.

**Decision: Reject**

The primary reason for rejection is the limited magnitude of improvement demonstrated, particularly on the ImageNet experiments. While the paper presents a technically sound approach and thorough experimentation on CIFAR, the lack of substantial gains on a larger, more challenging dataset like ImageNet raises concerns about the practical significance of the contribution. Secondly, section 4.2.2 (ImageNet results) feels underdeveloped and requires further investigation to convincingly demonstrate the method’s efficacy at scale.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper is well-motivated, building upon existing work in knowledge distillation and attention mechanisms. The exploration of both activation-based and gradient-based attention is a reasonable approach, and the ablation studies (e.g., comparing different attention mapping functions) provide valuable insights. The implementation details and code availability are commendable, promoting reproducibility.

However, the empirical results don't fully support the claim of *significant* improvement. On CIFAR, the gains are consistent but modest. The ImageNet results, while showing some improvement with ResNet-18 as a student, lack a more comprehensive evaluation. The authors acknowledge that they didn’t fully tune hyperparameters and only explored a limited range of configurations. Crucially, the performance gains on ImageNet (1.1% top-1 accuracy) seem relatively small considering the computational cost and effort involved. The statement that this might be the first knowledge transfer method to successfully apply on ImageNet feels overstated given the extensive literature in knowledge distillation; the novelty lies in *how* knowledge is transferred, not necessarily that transfer is possible.

The comparison with full-activation transfer is insightful, showing the advantage of attention maps over direct activation transfer, but this doesn’t necessarily translate to a groundbreaking contribution. Furthermore, the authors mention struggling to replicate KD results on ImageNet which adds to the concern regarding the robustness and generalizability of their findings.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **ImageNet Extension:** The authors are correct to suggest that strengthening section 4.2.2 is critical. I would recommend several additions: (1) Explore different student/teacher architectures (e.g., ResNet-50). (2) Perform a more extensive hyperparameter search. (3) Compare against more recent and competitive knowledge distillation techniques. (4) Analyze the attention maps visually to understand *why* the transfer is (or isn't) working. Showing example attention maps of the teacher and student networks, particularly highlighting differences, would be highly valuable.
*   **Ablation Study Depth:** While the ablation study on attention mapping functions is good, further ablation could explore the impact of different loss weights (β) and normalization techniques.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** While the paper focuses on empirical results, a brief discussion on why attention maps might be more effective for knowledge transfer than full activations could strengthen the argument. Is there a theoretical reason to believe that attention captures more essential information?
* **Clarification on KD failure:** A more detailed explanation for the failure of KD to work on ImageNet would be helpful. Was it related to hyperparameter tuning, architectural differences, or something else?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the ImageNet experiments, can you provide more details on the hyperparameter search performed? Specifically, what range of β values were explored, and how were they selected?
2.  Why do you think the attention transfer method performs better on CUB than on Scenes? Do you believe the fine-grained nature of CUB benefits more from attention-guided transfer?
3.  In the case of gradient-based attention transfer, you mention challenges with batch normalization during the second backpropagation step. Could you elaborate on these challenges and potential solutions?
4.  You state that “attention maps carry information that is more important for transfer than full activations.” Can you speculate on what specific information within attention maps contributes to this improved transfer performance? Is it the sparsity, the focus on relevant features, or something else?
