## Review of "Attention Transfer"

This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer by focusing on transferring attention maps between convolutional neural networks, rather than activations. The authors explore both activation-based and gradient-based attention mechanisms and demonstrate improvements across several datasets and network architectures. The core idea of distilling *where* a network looks, rather than *what* it computes, is a compelling one, and the exploration of different attention map definitions is valuable. The code release is also a definite positive.

I recommend **Reject** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the work is promising, the observed improvements are marginal, and the ImageNet experiments, specifically section 4.2.2, require substantial strengthening before the paper can be considered for acceptance. 

The paper addresses the question of whether transferring attention information from a teacher network can improve the performance of a student network. The approach is well-motivated, building upon the existing literature in knowledge distillation and attention mechanisms. The authors clearly position their work within these areas and highlight the potential benefits of focusing on attention transfer. However, the empirical results, while consistently positive, are not overwhelmingly significant. The improvements over standard knowledge distillation, as presented, are relatively small and do not fully justify the complexity of the proposed methods. The CIFAR experiments are more convincing than the ImageNet ones. Specifically, the ImageNet results (section 4.2.2) lack sufficient detail. The claim of a 1.1% top-1 improvement is notable, but without a more thorough investigation of hyperparameter tuning and comparison with stronger baselines (e.g., other state-of-the-art KD methods on ImageNet), it is difficult to assess the true impact of the proposed approach. 

To improve the paper, I suggest the authors:

*   **Expand Section 4.2.2:** Conduct a more comprehensive set of experiments on ImageNet, including comparisons with stronger KD baselines, a detailed hyperparameter search, and an analysis of the impact of transferring attention from different layers of the teacher network.
*   **Ablation Study:** Provide a more detailed ablation study to assess the contribution of each component of the proposed method (e.g., different attention mapping functions, normalization techniques).
*   **Error Analysis:** Investigate *why* attention transfer works in certain cases and fails in others. This could involve visualizing attention maps and analyzing the differences between teacher and student networks.
*   **Clarify KD results:** The claim that KD doesn’t work on ImageNet requires further exploration. Why does this happen? Is it specific to the architecture, hyperparameters, or something else?

I would be open to reconsidering a revised submission if the authors can significantly strengthen the ImageNet experiments and provide a more compelling demonstration of the benefits of their proposed approach.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why did Knowledge Distillation alone not work well on ImageNet in your experiments? What parameters were tested?
2.  In section 4.2.2, you mention not having time to tune hyperparameters. How sensitive are the results to the choice of β (the weighting factor for the attention transfer loss)?
3.  Could you provide more detailed visualizations of the attention maps for both the teacher and student networks on ImageNet, particularly for cases where attention transfer yields the largest improvements?
4.  What is the computational overhead of incorporating the attention transfer loss during training?
