## Review of "Attention Transfer"

This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer, termed Attention Transfer (AT), which aims to improve student CNN performance by mimicking the attention maps of a teacher network. The authors define attention maps based on activations (sum/max of |features|^p) and gradients (∂Loss/∂input), and demonstrate consistent improvements in student performance across CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet datasets. The work is clearly written, well-motivated, and presents a logical progression of ideas.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents a solid contribution and yields consistent improvements, the limitations and lack of thorough analysis prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance at this time. The primary reason for rejection is that the student networks consistently *underperform* the teacher networks, despite the transfer of attention. This raises questions about the effectiveness of AT in truly capturing the teacher's knowledge. A significant improvement is demonstrated *over the baseline student*, but not reaching teacher performance diminishes the impact. 

The authors rightly point out that students have fewer parameters, so a characterization of speed-up and an analysis of AT when student and teacher share the same architecture are crucial to understanding the method’s true value. Without this, it’s difficult to assess the trade-off between performance and efficiency. 

Furthermore, the paper lacks a clear guidance on which attention map type to use. The experiments don't sufficiently clarify the pros and cons of activation-based vs. gradient-based attention, leaving the reader uncertain about the best choice for different scenarios. The novelty of the approach is also somewhat incremental, building on existing work like FitNet, and a more compelling demonstration of its advantages is needed.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of the AT weight (β) is not discussed. A sensitivity analysis of β would be highly valuable.
*   **Figure 7b:**  Including teacher training and validation loss in Figure 7b would provide a crucial point of comparison and help assess the effectiveness of AT and KD.
*   **Quantitative Evidence:**  The claim regarding higher-accuracy networks having higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps (Fig. 4) lacks quantitative support. Providing a correlation metric would strengthen this observation.
*   **Ablation Studies:**  What is the contribution of the l2 normalization? Is it crucial?
*   **Computational Cost:** A discussion of the computational overhead introduced by AT (especially the second backpropagation step in gradient-based transfer) would be beneficial.
*   **Why does AT not surpass teacher performance?** Is it a capacity issue, a limitation of the attention transfer mechanism itself, or something else?



I hope this review is helpful. I encourage the authors to address these points in a revised version.
