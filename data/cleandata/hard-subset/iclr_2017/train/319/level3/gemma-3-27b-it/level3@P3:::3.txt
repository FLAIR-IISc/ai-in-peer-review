This paper proposes attention transfer (AT) as a method for improving the performance of student convolutional neural networks by mimicking the attention maps of a teacher network. The authors define activation-based and gradient-based attention maps and demonstrate consistent performance gains on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet datasets. The work is clearly written, well-motivated, and presents a sensible approach to knowledge transfer.

**Reject** with a request for major revisions. While the paper demonstrates consistent improvements in student performance, it falls short of convincingly establishing the *why* and *when* of its approach. Crucially, students consistently underperform teachers, and the paper doesn’t fully explore the benefits of AT when student and teacher architectures are identical – a scenario where parameter efficiency gains would be paramount.

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of a compelling argument for the broader impact of AT. The improvements demonstrated, while consistent, are incremental and do not surpass teacher performance. The paper positions AT as a novel knowledge transfer method, but its effectiveness remains limited to boosting student performance *towards* that of the teacher, without ever exceeding it. A strong revision would address this by characterizing the speed-up achieved with smaller student networks and investigating the impact of AT when the student and teacher share the same architecture. This would highlight whether AT enables a performance/efficiency trade-off.

Secondly, the paper lacks clear guidance on selecting the most appropriate attention map type. While different attention map types are defined and experimented with, the results do not offer clear insights into their relative pros and cons or when to prefer one over another. A more thorough analysis comparing the characteristics and performance of activation-based and gradient-based attention would significantly strengthen the contribution.

Supporting arguments:

*   **Limited Performance Gain:** The student networks, even with AT, consistently lag behind the teacher networks in accuracy. This suggests that AT is not fully capturing the knowledge embedded in the teacher.
*   **Unclear Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of the attention transfer weight (β) is not explored. A sensitivity analysis of β is crucial to understand its influence on performance and to provide practical guidance to users.
*   **Incomplete Results:** Figure 7b lacks teacher training and validation loss, making it difficult to assess the convergence and training dynamics when AT is combined with knowledge distillation. This comparison is vital for understanding the effect of AT on the learning process.
*   **Incremental Novelty:** While technically sound, the core idea of transferring attention maps is an incremental step from prior work on knowledge distillation (e.g., FitNets). The paper doesn't fully articulate how AT significantly advances beyond these existing techniques.
*   **Spatial Correlation Claim:** The claim about higher-accuracy networks having stronger spatial correlation between object and attention maps (Fig. 4) is visually supported but lacks quantitative evidence. A statistical analysis of this correlation would be necessary to substantiate the claim.

Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):

*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component of the AT framework (e.g., L2 normalization, different attention map types, KD).
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational overhead introduced by AT, particularly the second backpropagation step in the gradient-based approach.
*   **Visualizations:** Include more visualizations of attention maps for different layers and datasets to provide a deeper understanding of how AT influences the student's attention mechanisms.
*   **Error Analysis:** Perform an error analysis to identify the types of errors that AT helps to correct and those where it struggles.
*   **Alternative Loss Functions:** Investigate alternative loss functions for attention transfer, such as cosine similarity or perceptual loss.

Questions for the authors:

1.  Given that students consistently underperform teachers, what is the potential upper bound of performance improvement achievable through AT? What limitations prevent AT from surpassing the teacher's performance?
2.  How does the performance of AT vary when the student and teacher architectures are identical? What are the implications for model compression and efficiency?
3.  Can you provide a sensitivity analysis of the attention transfer weight (β)? How does varying β affect the learning dynamics and final performance?
4.  Could you provide the teacher training and validation loss curves for Figure 7b to allow for a more complete assessment of the effect of knowledge distillation with attention transfer?
5.  Regarding the claim of stronger spatial correlation in higher-accuracy networks, what quantitative metrics were considered (even if not ultimately included in the paper) and why were they deemed insufficient for inclusion?
