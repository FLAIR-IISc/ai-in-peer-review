- Proposed method: match activation-based and gradient-based attention maps from teacher to student to transfer knowledge in CNNs.
- Empirical claim: consistent performance improvements across CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet and across architectures; code released.
- Decision: Reject with encouragement to revise and resubmit.
- Main criticism: gains are modest and analysis lacks depth; interpretability claims about attention are not rigorously supported.
- Motivation and related work: problem and literature review are well-articulated, drawing sensible parallels between human and machine attention.
- Method soundness: activation- and gradient-based attention transfer are logically justified and build on existing ideas.
- Experimental breadth: diverse datasets and architectures are tested, showing potential of the approach.
- Lack of quantitative support: claim that higher-accuracy networks have stronger spatial correlation with attention maps is only shown visually, not with metrics.
- Hyperparameter issues: the role and sensitivity of the β weight for attention loss are not explored.
- ImageNet experiments: results positive but hyperparameters were not tuned specifically for AT, weakening conclusions.
- Missing comparisons in Fig. 7(b): teacher’s training and validation losses are not shown for context.
- Speed/efficiency omission: no measurement of student inference speedup or discussion of speed–accuracy trade-offs.
- Architecture comparison gap: paper doesn’t assess AT when student and teacher share the same architecture family/scale.
- Superficial analysis of attention mappings: comparisons across attention mapping functions (Table 2) lack in-depth characterization.
- Strong claim unchecked: statement that activation-based AT is the first successful KD on ImageNet requires justification and exhaustive KD comparisons.
- Requested improvements: add quantitative attention-map metrics (e.g., correlation, IoU) to back interpretability claims.
- Requested improvements: perform sensitivity analysis over β to show robustness and optimal settings.
- Requested improvements: include ablations separating activation- vs gradient-based AT contributions.
- Requested improvements: report student vs teacher inference speed and analyze speed–accuracy trade-offs.
- Requested improvements: provide comprehensive comparisons with other KD baselines, especially on ImageNet.
- Requested improvements: clarify implementation details (normalization, optimizer, etc.) and hyperparameter tuning procedures.
- Requested improvements: include error analysis to show how AT changes types of errors made by students.
- Author questions: what is the computational overhead of computing/applying the AT loss versus standard training?
- Author questions: were alternative losses to L2 for matching attention maps explored, and with what results?
- Author questions: how does AT perform across different student-to-teacher capacity ratios (e.g., ResNet-18 vs ResNet-50)?
- Author questions: provide more details on hyperparameter tuning for ImageNet experiments.
- Author questions: which KD methods were tried and why do authors claim AT is the first successful KD on ImageNet?
