- Paper proposes attention transfer (AT) by minimizing L2 distance between teacher and student attention maps, alongside classification loss and optional KD term.  
- Defines activation-based attentions (sum/max of |features|^p) and a gradient-based attention (∂Loss/∂input).  
- Experiments on CIFAR, CUB/Scene, and ImageNet show AT consistently improves student performance vs. student alone, but students still underperform teachers.  
- Claim that higher-accuracy networks have higher spatial correlation between object and attention maps is visually supported (Fig.4) but lacks quantitative evidence.  
- Hyperparameter selection is unclear; the impact of the AT weight β is not analyzed.  
- Figure 7b should also report teacher training and validation loss for comparison.  
- Experiments do not clarify the relative pros/cons or when to use different attention map types.  
- AT does not surpass teacher performance; since students have fewer parameters, the paper should characterize speed-up and ask whether AT helps when student and teacher share the same architecture.  
- Pros: clearly written, well motivated, and yields consistent student improvements over baseline.  
- Cons: students still worse than teachers, unclear guidance on which attention to use, and novelty is somewhat incremental relative to FitNet.
