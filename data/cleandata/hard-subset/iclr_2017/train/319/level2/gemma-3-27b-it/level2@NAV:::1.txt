## Review of "Attention Transfer"

This paper investigates the transfer of attention mechanisms from a powerful teacher convolutional neural network (CNN) to a student network to improve performance. The authors propose defining attention as activation-based and gradient-based spatial maps and explore several novel methods for transferring this attention information. The core idea – leveraging the attentional focus of a well-trained network to guide the learning of a smaller network – is promising and intuitively sound, mirroring observations about human learning and visual perception. The experimental results demonstrate consistent performance improvements across various datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and CNN architectures.

I recommend **Accept** this paper. The work presents a novel and effective technique for knowledge transfer, supported by a comprehensive experimental evaluation. The consistent improvements observed across diverse settings suggest a genuine contribution to the field.

The strength of the paper lies in its clear methodology and thorough experimentation. The authors explore different attention map definitions and transfer methods, providing insightful comparisons. The ablation studies on CIFAR (Table 1 & 2) effectively demonstrate the benefit of attention transfer over full activation transfer and highlight the impact of different attention mapping functions. The expansion to larger datasets like ImageNet, even with limited hyperparameter tuning, provides further validation of the approach’s generalizability. The readily available code and models are also a significant positive aspect, fostering reproducibility and future research.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification and analysis. Specifically, the authors state that KD struggles on ImageNet, but don't explore *why*. Is it a hyperparameter issue? A fundamental incompatibility with the chosen architectures? More exploration here would be valuable. Additionally, the discussion of gradient-based attention transfer feels less developed than activation-based transfer. A more detailed comparison of the two approaches and their respective strengths/weaknesses would strengthen the paper. Finally, while the paper acknowledges limitations, further discussion of the computational cost associated with the second backpropagation step in gradient-based transfer would be beneficial.

I would like to ask the authors: Could you elaborate on the reasons for the failure of Knowledge Distillation on ImageNet, and discuss potential remedies? Also, could you provide a more in-depth analysis of the computational overhead associated with the gradient-based attention transfer method, compared to standard training or activation-based transfer?
