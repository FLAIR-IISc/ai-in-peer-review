This paper proposes a method for transferring knowledge from a teacher convolutional neural network (CNN) to a student CNN by transferring attention maps. The authors define attention as spatial maps encoding areas of focus and explore both activation-based and gradient-based attention. They demonstrate improvements across various datasets and architectures, showing that attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper addresses an interesting and relevant problem – knowledge transfer via attention mechanisms – and presents a well-motivated and experimentally validated approach. The consistent improvements observed across different datasets and architectures suggest a significant contribution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of leveraging attention mechanisms for knowledge transfer, building on the observation that different networks “attend” to different parts of an image.
* **Well-Motivated Approach:** The rationale for transferring attention is sound, drawing parallels to human attention and the idea that focusing a student network on important areas can improve performance. The exploration of both activation-based and gradient-based attention is a strength.
* **Strong Experimental Results:** The extensive experiments across diverse datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and network architectures (NIN, ResNet) demonstrate the robustness of the proposed methods. The consistent improvements observed, particularly the superiority of attention transfer over full activation transfer, provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the approach. The ablation studies (e.g., the impact of different attention mapping functions) are valuable.
* **Novelty:** While knowledge distillation is a well-established technique, the specific approach of transferring attention maps, particularly the demonstration on ImageNet, represents a novel contribution.  The combination with knowledge distillation is also a positive aspect.
* **Code Availability:**  The public availability of code and models is a significant asset for reproducibility and further research.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Related Work:** While the related work section is reasonably comprehensive, the discussion of attention transfer *specifically* could be expanded. It would be beneficial to highlight existing work on aligning feature representations between networks, and how this approach differs. A discussion of recent work exploring interpretability and attention visualization beyond the cited papers could be valuable.
* **Gradient-Based Attention Details:** The gradient-based attention section feels less thoroughly explored than activation-based attention.  The authors acknowledge limitations due to the complexity of double backpropagation. More discussion of the challenges and potential future directions for gradient-based attention would be beneficial. Why does it appear to work well on the smaller dataset (CIFAR) but not clearly on ImageNet?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention briefly tuning hyperparameters, a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the approach to parameters like the loss weight (β) would be valuable. Is the optimal value of β consistent across datasets and architectures?
* **Visualization of Attention Maps:** More extensive visualization of the attention maps, particularly in the ImageNet experiments, would help to illustrate the impact of attention transfer. Showing how the student network’s attention maps become more similar to the teacher’s could strengthen the argument.
* **Clarify ‘hints’ comparison:** The paper draws a comparison to “hints” in FitNets. Clarify how the attention maps differ from FitNets’ hints and the advantages of this approach.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** Briefly discussing the computational overhead of attention transfer (e.g., the cost of computing and applying the attention losses) would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you elaborate on why the gradient-based attention transfer method did not perform as well on ImageNet as it did on CIFAR? Were there specific implementation challenges or limitations encountered?
* What is the impact of choosing different normalization methods for the attention maps (e.g., L1 vs. L2 normalization)?
* Is the optimal value of the loss weight (β) consistent across different datasets and network architectures? If not, what strategies did you use to select an appropriate value?
* Could you provide more examples of the student network's attention maps evolving to resemble those of the teacher network, especially for the ImageNet experiments?
* Is there any theoretical justification for why attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer?
