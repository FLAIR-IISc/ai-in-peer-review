This paper proposes a method for improving the performance of a student convolutional neural network (CNN) by transferring attention maps from a powerful teacher network. The authors define attention as spatial maps encoding the areas of the input the network focuses on, using both activation-based and gradient-based approaches. They propose several methods for transferring this attention information and demonstrate consistent improvement across various datasets and architectures.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to knowledge transfer, demonstrating consistent and significant improvements in performance across a variety of experiments. The empirical results are convincing and the proposed methods are clearly explained.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of transferring knowledge between CNNs, specifically leveraging attention mechanisms. The motivation, drawing parallels to human attention, is well-established and the hypothesis that teacher attention maps contain valuable information for student learning is plausible.
* **Novelty and Approach:** The exploration of both activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer is a novel contribution. The proposed methods for defining and transferring attention (sum/max of absolute activations, gradient-based transfer, and the combination with knowledge distillation) are well-defined and appear to be effective. The comparison between attention transfer and full activation transfer is insightful.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a comprehensive set of experiments across diverse datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and network architectures (NIN, ResNet). The reported improvements are consistent and significant, particularly on the larger datasets like ImageNet. The ablation studies (e.g., different attention mapping functions, impact of KD) provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The visualization of attention maps further supports the claims.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors provide a link to the code and models, promoting reproducibility.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

* **Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, a more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the results to parameters like the attention transfer weight (β) would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** A brief discussion of the computational overhead introduced by the attention transfer process would be helpful. This is especially relevant for gradient-based transfer, which involves a second backpropagation pass.
* **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are strong, a more formal theoretical justification for why attention transfer works could strengthen the paper. For example, exploring the relationship between attention maps and the learned feature representations might provide further insights.
* **Error Analysis:** A more detailed error analysis, identifying cases where the attention transfer method fails, could provide direction for future research.
* **Connection to Existing Literature:** The related work section is somewhat broad. Focusing more specifically on recent advances in knowledge distillation and attention mechanisms would be helpful. Consider citing papers that explore the information bottleneck principle as a possible explanation for the effectiveness of attention transfer.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* In the ImageNet experiments, you mention not having time to tune hyperparameters. How much do you anticipate the results would improve with more thorough hyperparameter optimization, particularly for the attention transfer weight (β)?
* Could you elaborate on the challenges you encountered when trying to combine gradient-based attention transfer with batch normalization? Are there specific modifications to the batch normalization process that could potentially address these challenges?
* You found that attention transfer performed better than full activation transfer. Do you have any intuition as to why this is the case? Is it possible that the attention maps act as a form of regularization, preventing overfitting?
* The paper demonstrates improved performance with both activation and gradient based attention transfer. Are there situations where one approach might be preferred over the other, and if so, what characteristics of the dataset or network architecture would influence this choice?
