This paper proposes a method for transferring knowledge from a teacher convolutional neural network (CNN) to a student CNN by transferring attention maps. The core idea is that attention maps, defined either as activation-based or gradient-based spatial maps, encapsulate valuable information about where the teacher network focuses its processing, and this information can be used to improve the performance of the student network. The work explores different ways of defining and transferring these attention maps, demonstrating improvements across various datasets and CNN architectures.

The decision is to **accept** with minor revisions. The work addresses a relevant problem – knowledge transfer in CNNs – and proposes a novel approach based on attention mechanisms. The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating consistent improvements in performance across different architectures and datasets.

The paper is well-motivated and builds upon existing work in attention mechanisms, knowledge distillation, and visualizing CNNs. The authors appropriately position their work within the existing literature, referencing relevant papers in each of these areas. The introduction clearly articulates the problem and the proposed solution. The definitions of activation-based and gradient-based attention maps are clearly presented, and the methods for transferring attention are explained in detail. The experimental setup is comprehensive, covering a variety of datasets and network architectures. The presentation of results is clear and concise. The inclusion of a publicly available code repository is a significant positive aspect.

However, the paper could be improved with a few revisions. Firstly, the connection between the proposed attention transfer and existing knowledge distillation techniques could be further clarified. While the authors mention combining attention transfer with knowledge distillation, a more thorough discussion of the differences and potential synergies would be beneficial. Secondly, the explanation of why attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer could be elaborated. A deeper analysis of the information content of attention maps versus full activations would strengthen this claim. Thirdly, the discussion of gradient-based attention transfer is somewhat limited. Expanding on the challenges and potential solutions related to batch normalization in this context would be valuable. Finally, while the ablation studies are present, more detailed analysis on the impact of different attention mapping functions would be helpful, perhaps with a more systematic comparison of their performance on various datasets.

Specific questions for the authors:

1. Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the l2 norm for comparing attention maps? Were other distance metrics considered, and if so, why was the l2 norm chosen?
2. The paper mentions that gradient-based attention transfer requires a second backpropagation step. Could the authors provide more detail on the computational cost of this second backpropagation step compared to standard backpropagation?
3. The authors found that KD did not work well on ImageNet. Did they experiment with different temperature settings or other hyperparameters specifically for the ImageNet dataset?
4. The horizontal flip invariance constraint seems promising. Could the authors explore applying this constraint to activation-based attention transfer as well?
5. The paper touches upon the potential for extending this work to object detection or weakly-supervised localization. Could the authors provide some initial thoughts on how attention transfer might be adapted to these tasks?
