1. Proposal: transfer attention maps from teacher CNN to student CNN (activation- and gradient-based) to improve knowledge transfer.  
2. Main result: attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.  
3. Decision: Accept — problem is relevant, approach well-motivated, experimentally validated.  
4. Clear problem definition: paper clearly frames leveraging attention for knowledge transfer.  
5. Well-motivated approach: draws parallels to human attention and justifies focusing students on important spatial areas.  
6. Exploration of both activation-based and gradient-based attention is a strength.  
7. Strong experiments: consistent improvements across CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet and across architectures (NIN, ResNet).  
8. Ablation studies: useful analyses (e.g., different attention mapping functions) bolster findings.  
9. Novelty: attention-map transfer (including ImageNet demo) and combination with KD represent a novel contribution.  
10. Reproducibility: public release of code and models is a significant asset.  
11. Related work: related-work discussion should better cover attention-specific alignment and broader interpretability/visualization literature.  
12. Gradient-based attention: section feels underexplored; double backprop complexity and inconsistent ImageNet results need more discussion.  
13. Hyperparameter sensitivity: need deeper analysis of sensitivity to loss weight β and whether optimal β generalizes.  
14. Visualizations: more extensive attention-map visualizations (especially for ImageNet) would strengthen the paper.  
15. FitNets comparison: clarify differences between attention maps and FitNets’ “hints” and the advantages of the new approach.  
16. Computational cost: discuss overhead of computing/applying attention losses.  
17. Question: why did gradient-based attention work on CIFAR but not clearly on ImageNet — implementation or intrinsic limitations?  
18. Questions: effects of different attention-map normalizations (L1 vs L2), consistency/selection strategy for β, more examples of student attention evolving, and theoretical justification for superiority over full activation transfer.
