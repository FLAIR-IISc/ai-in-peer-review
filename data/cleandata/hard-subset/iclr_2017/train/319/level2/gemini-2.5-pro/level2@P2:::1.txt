### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel knowledge transfer technique called Attention Transfer (AT), where a smaller "student" neural network is trained to improve its performance by mimicking the spatial attention maps of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The authors define two types of attention maps: activation-based maps, which summarize the activations across channels at a given layer to produce a spatial importance map, and gradient-based maps, which represent the sensitivity of the output to the input. The core idea is to add a loss term that minimizes the difference between the student's and teacher's normalized attention maps at one or more intermediate layers. The paper provides extensive experimental validation, demonstrating that AT consistently improves student model performance across a range of datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, ImageNet) and modern architectures (NIN, Wide ResNets). Key claims include that activation-based AT is more effective than transferring full, uncompressed activation maps, can be successfully combined with knowledge distillation, and represents the first knowledge transfer method of its kind to demonstrate significant gains on the large-scale ImageNet dataset.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Effective Knowledge Transfer Mechanism:** The paper introduces a simple, intuitive, and novel method for knowledge distillation. By proposing to transfer "attention" rather than final logits (Hinton et al., 2015) or full intermediate feature maps (FitNets), the authors define a new and effective form of "hint" that a student network can learn from. This is a strong conceptual contribution to the field.
2.  **Comprehensive and Compelling Experimental Results:** The claims are supported by a strong and thorough empirical evaluation. The method shows consistent improvements across various tasks and architectures. Most notably, the successful application to ImageNet—improving a ResNet-18 by over 1% top-1 accuracy—is a significant result, especially given the authors' claim that similar intermediate-layer transfer methods have struggled to show benefits on this scale.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's strengths lie in its simple but powerful core idea and the rigorous experiments that validate it.

*   **Motivation and Novelty:** The motivation for using attention as the transfer medium is clear and compelling. Instead of forcing the student to replicate the teacher's exact high-dimensional feature representations, AT guides the student to focus on the same spatial regions of the input that the teacher finds important. This compressed, distilled guidance appears to be a more effective learning signal, as evidenced by the experiments. The comparison against a FitNets-style full-activation transfer (F-ActT in Table 1) supports this, showing AT yields better results.
*   **Strong Empirical Evidence:** The experimental section is a major strength.
    *   On CIFAR-10, AT provides significant error reduction on Wide ResNets and demonstrates that it can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD) for further gains.
    *   The transfer learning experiments on CUB-200 and MIT Scenes are particularly convincing. AT substantially closes the performance gap between a ResNet-18 student and a ResNet-34 teacher. The result on CUB, where AT is significantly more effective than KD, suggests that transferring spatial attention is especially valuable for tasks like fine-grained recognition where localization of key features is critical.
    *   The ImageNet result is the most impactful contribution. Achieving a 1.1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18 is a non-trivial gain on this benchmark. The authors' assertion that this is a first-of-its-kind result for intermediate-layer knowledge transfer on ImageNet makes the contribution highly significant.
*   **Good Analysis and Ablation:** The paper provides useful analysis, such as comparing different functions for generating activation-based maps (Table 2) and exploring the impact of placing the transfer loss at different network stages. The inclusion of gradient-based transfer, while less developed, adds depth to the exploration of what constitutes "attention" and is an interesting avenue for future work. The authors are also transparent about its current limitations (e.g., interaction with batch normalization).

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended as constructive suggestions and are not critical to the decision to accept.

*   **Terminology of "Attention":** The term "attention" is used broadly. The proposed activation-based maps are an intuitive proxy for spatial importance, but it may be beneficial to explicitly position them with respect to the wider literature on attention (e.g., self-attention) and saliency. A brief discussion clarifying that these are effective *proxies* for attention or better described as "spatial activation summary maps" could help manage reader expectations and situate the work more precisely.
*   **Gradient-based AT:** This part of the work is interesting but feels less central to the paper's main success story, which is the simple and highly effective activation-based method. Given that it performed worse than the activation-based method on CIFAR-10 and has unresolved technical challenges (e.g., double backpropagation with batch norm), it might be better framed as a preliminary investigation or moved to an appendix to allow more space to analyze the primary contribution.
*   **Importance of Normalization:** The paper states that l2-normalizing the vectorized attention maps before computing the transfer loss is "important for the success of the student training." This is a key implementation detail. An ablation study in the appendix showing the performance difference with and without this normalization would be a valuable addition to quantify its impact.
*   **F-ActT Comparison:** In Section 4.1.2, the comparison to FitNets-style transfer (F-ActT) is described as showing "minimal" improvements over the baseline. While Table 1 shows AT is superior, F-ActT also provides a clear improvement (e.g., 8.77% -> 8.54%). Rephrasing this to state that "AT provides a significantly larger improvement than full-activation transfer" would be more precise.

### **Questions for Authors**

*   Could you further substantiate the claim that similar intermediate-layer transfer methods like FitNets have not been successfully applied to ImageNet? Citing works that have tried and failed, or noting a clear absence in the literature, would further strengthen the significance of your ImageNet results.
*   The hyperparameter β balances the task loss and the AT loss. You provide a heuristic for setting it based on map size. How sensitive is the model's final performance to this value? Was this tuned per dataset/model pair, or did the heuristic work robustly across experiments?
*   The ImageNet results are impressive, especially given the statement that hyperparameters were not tuned. This suggests there might be further room for improvement. Do you have any intuition on how much more gain could be expected by tuning β and adding AT losses to all residual groups, as you plan?
