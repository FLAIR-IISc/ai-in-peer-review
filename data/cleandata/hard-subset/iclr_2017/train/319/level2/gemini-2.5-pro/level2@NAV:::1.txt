This paper introduces Attention Transfer (AT), a novel and effective knowledge distillation technique. The core contribution is to train a smaller "student" network to improve its performance by mimicking the spatial attention maps of a larger, powerful "teacher" network. The authors propose two distinct methods for generating these attention maps: a simple and efficient activation-based approach and a more complex gradient-based one. Through extensive experiments, they claim that this method provides significant and consistent improvements for the student network across a variety of datasets, tasks, and architectures.

I recommend **Accept**. The paper is built on a simple, intuitive, and powerful idea. The primary reasons for this decision are: (1) The proposed activation-based attention transfer method is well-motivated and demonstrates strong, consistent empirical gains across multiple challenging benchmarks, including CIFAR, fine-grained classification datasets, and notably, ImageNet. (2) The experimental evaluation is thorough and provides crucial comparisons to relevant baselines, such as standard knowledge distillation (KD) and transferring full feature maps (FitNets-style), convincingly showing that transferring summarized attention information is more effective.

The strength of this work lies in the simplicity and effectiveness of the activation-based method. By forcing the student to learn *where* to look from the teacher, it transfers a more abstract and potentially more useful form of knowledge than just matching output logits or intermediate features. The results on ImageNet, where the ResNet-18 student closes a significant portion of the performance gap with the ResNet-34 teacher, are particularly compelling, especially given the authors' report that standard KD struggled in the same setting. The ablation studies on CIFAR further strengthen the claims by exploring different attention mapping functions and demonstrating the individual contribution of each transfer loss.

For improvement, the gradient-based attention transfer section feels less developed. The experiments are limited to a specific setting without batch normalization, and the results are not as strong as the activation-based counterpart. This part could either be strengthened with more extensive experiments or framed as promising preliminary work. Furthermore, the claim of being the "first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" might be an overstatement and could be softened to reflect that it performs strongly where other methods have proven difficult to apply.

I have a few questions for the authors:
1.  Could you provide more intuition on why decaying the attention loss weight `β` when combining AT with KD is beneficial? Does it suggest a curriculum-like effect?
2.  Have you explored the qualitative differences between the attention maps generated by the activation-based (`F_sum`) and gradient-based methods? Do they focus on different aspects of the input image?
