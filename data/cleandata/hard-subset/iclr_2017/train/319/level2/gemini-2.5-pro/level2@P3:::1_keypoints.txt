- Introduces Attention Transfer (AT): train a student to mimic teacher spatial attention maps to improve performance.  
- Proposes two attention map definitions: activation-based (channel aggregation) and gradient-based (loss gradient sensitivity).  
- Claim: attention maps are compact, informative “hints” that teach the student where to look, not just what to predict.  
- Extensive experiments on CIFAR-10, CUB, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet across NIN, ResNet, and Wide ResNet show consistent gains.  
- AT can be combined with existing knowledge distillation methods and outperforms transferring full activation tensors.  
- Significant ImageNet result: ResNet-18 student gains ~1.1% top-1 accuracy, demonstrating scalability and practical impact.  
- Method is novel, simple, and creates a useful link between interpretability/visualization and model compression.  
- Activation- and gradient-based formulations are clear and straightforward to implement; use L2 loss on normalized attention maps.  
- Direct comparison shows attention-map transfer is more beneficial than FitNets-style full-feature transfer.  
- Evaluation is rigorous: broad datasets, modern architectures, and training paradigms (scratch and fine-tuning).  
- Ablation studies strengthen conclusions (different mapping functions, multi-stage AT benefits).  
- Public code and model release improves reproducibility and community impact.  
- Hyperparameter beta selection is heuristic; needs formalization and clarity on per-loss calculation and decay when combined with KD.  
- Gradient-based AT experiments are limited (CIFAR-10, simple network) due to batch-norm/backprop challenges on larger models.  
- Recommend more discussion of gradient-AT technical difficulties to guide future work.  
- Suggest adding qualitative failure-case analyses to show when student attention fails and how that correlates with errors.  
- Question: clarify effects and necessity of normalizing attention maps—stability vs suboptimal convergence without it.  
- Question: justify applying AT loss to only the final two residual groups on ImageNet—preliminary choice or computational constraint?  
- Question: provide direct evidence that AT improves student focus on fine-grained discriminative parts (e.g., bird beak/eye).  
- Question: was the gradient-based symmetry norm combined with student-teacher gradient AT, and could they be complementary?
