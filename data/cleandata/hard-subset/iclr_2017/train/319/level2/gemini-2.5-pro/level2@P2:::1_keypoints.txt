- Proposes Attention Transfer (AT): student trained to match teacher spatial attention maps (activation- and gradient-based) via a loss on normalized maps.  
- Activation-based AT is more effective than transferring full intermediate activations and can be combined with knowledge distillation.  
- Strong empirical improvements across datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, Wide ResNets).  
- Notable ImageNet result: +1.1% top-1 for ResNet-18, claimed as first large-scale success for intermediate-layer transfer.  
- Conceptual contribution: simple, intuitive new form of "hint" that guides student to focus on teacher-important spatial regions.  
- CIFAR-10: significant error reductions and additive gains when combined with KD.  
- Transfer learning on CUB-200 and MIT Scenes: AT substantially closes the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34, especially for fine-grained tasks.  
- Ablation and analysis: compares attention map functions and loss placement; gradient-based AT explored but underperforms and has technical challenges (e.g., batch-norm, double backprop).  
- Recommends clarifying the term "attention" and positioning activation maps relative to attention/self-attention and saliency literature.  
- Suggests treating gradient-based AT as preliminary (or moving it to appendix) due to weaker results and unresolved issues.  
- Calls for an ablation demonstrating the importance of l2-normalizing attention maps.  
- Requests a more precise phrasing for the FitNets-style (F-ActT) comparison to emphasize AT’s larger improvement.  
- Asks authors to substantiate the claim that prior intermediate-layer methods (e.g., FitNets) have not succeeded on ImageNet with citations or evidence.  
- Queries sensitivity and tuning strategy for hyperparameter β—whether the heuristic was robust across datasets/models.  
- Wonders how much additional ImageNet gain is possible with β tuning and adding AT losses to all residual groups.
