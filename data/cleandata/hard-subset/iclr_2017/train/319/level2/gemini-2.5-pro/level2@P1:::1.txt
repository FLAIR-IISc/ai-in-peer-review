### Summary

This paper introduces "Attention Transfer" (AT), a novel method for knowledge transfer from a high-performing "teacher" network to a smaller "student" network. The core idea is to train the student to mimic the teacher's "attention maps," which are defined as spatial maps that highlight the regions of an input image that the network focuses on. The authors propose two ways to define these attention maps: (1) an "activation-based" method, which computes statistics (e.g., the sum of absolute values) across the channel dimension of activation tensors, and (2) a "gradient-based" method, which uses the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image. The student is then trained with an additional loss term that minimizes the L2 distance between its normalized attention maps and those of the teacher. The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and modern architectures (NIN, WRN, ResNet), demonstrating that AT consistently improves student network performance. Notably, AT is shown to be more effective than transferring full activation tensors and is the first knowledge transfer method reported to successfully improve performance on ImageNet in a setting with different teacher-student architectures.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Intuitive Contribution:** The paper proposes a simple, yet powerful, new mechanism for knowledge distillation. The idea of transferring "where a network looks" rather than what it represents in its entirety (as in FitNets) or its final output distribution (as in Knowledge Distillation) is an elegant and well-motivated concept.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Evidence:** The claims are well-supported by extensive experiments across a variety of datasets, network architectures, and settings (standard classification and fine-tuning). The consistent performance gains, especially the significant result on ImageNet where other methods have reportedly struggled, make a compelling case for the method's effectiveness and generality.

### Supporting Arguments

The proposed method is well-situated within the existing literature on knowledge distillation and model compression. It provides a clear alternative to Hinton et al.'s knowledge distillation (KD) and Romero et al.'s FitNets. The authors make a convincing argument, supported by experiments (Table 1), that transferring compressed, salient information in the form of attention maps is more effective than transferring entire, high-dimensional activation tensors.

The empirical validation is a major strength of this work.
*   **Variety of Benchmarks:** The method is shown to work not just on small datasets like CIFAR-10 but also on more complex, larger-image datasets like CUB, Scenes, and crucially, ImageNet.
*   **Strong Baselines:** The experiments use modern, high-performing architectures like Wide Residual Networks and ResNets, demonstrating that the improvements are not merely due to weak baselines.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The paper includes valuable ablation studies that investigate the choice of attention mapping function (Table 2) and the impact of transferring attention at different network stages, which strengthens the conclusions. The direct comparison to full-activation transfer is particularly insightful.
*   **ImageNet Success:** The 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement for ResNet-18 on ImageNet is a significant result. The observation that AT succeeds where KD did not in their experiments highlights a key advantage of the proposed method, potentially due to the structural nature of attention hints being more robust to architectural differences than output logit distributions.

The activation-based AT method is computationally efficient, adding little overhead to the training process, which makes it a practical tool for improving smaller models. The code is also made available, which is commendable and facilitates reproducibility.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Motivation for Attention Formulation:** The activation-based attention formulation (e.g., `F_sum^2`) is simple and works well empirically. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on why this particular formulation serves as a good proxy for "attention." While intuitive, a deeper connection to the concept of attention in vision or neuroscience could strengthen the motivation.
*   **Details on Gradient-Based AT:** The gradient-based experiments are interesting but are limited to a simpler network setting due to complexities with batch normalization during the second backpropagation. It would be valuable if the authors could expand on the nature of this technical challenge in the main text. Even without a full solution, discussing the theoretical hurdles would be insightful for future work in this area.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper provides a rule of thumb for setting the loss weight `β`. A brief analysis or discussion on the sensitivity of the method to this hyperparameter would be helpful for practitioners seeking to apply this technique.

**Questions for Clarification:**
*   Could you provide a quantitative measure of the training time overhead for activation-based AT compared to baseline training? For instance, what is the percentage increase in wall-clock time for the ImageNet experiment?
*   In the cases where AT is combined with KD, how did you balance the two corresponding loss terms (`β` for AT and `α` for KD)? Did you find any interplay between these two hyperparameters?
*   The paper hypothesizes that AT works better than KD on CUB because intermediate attention is more important for fine-grained recognition. This is a very interesting point. Do the qualitative visualizations of attention maps support this? For instance, do the teacher's attention maps on CUB focus more sharply on discriminative parts (e.g., a bird's beak or head) compared to its maps on a more general dataset like ImageNet?
*   Have you considered the possibility that the benefit of AT comes from it acting as a powerful regularizer, in a manner similar to feature-norm penalties, rather than purely from the "mimicking" aspect? The success of the gradient-norm minimization (`min-l2` in Table 3) seems to suggest this possibility.
