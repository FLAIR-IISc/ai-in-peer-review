- Introduces Attention Transfer (AT): train a student to mimic teacher spatial attention maps (activation-based and gradient-based) via L2 loss on normalized maps.
- Activation-based AT computes channel-wise statistics (e.g., sum of absolutes); gradient-based AT uses loss gradients w.r.t. input.
- Extensive experiments across CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet and architectures (NIN, WRN, ResNet) show consistent student performance improvements.
- AT outperforms transferring full activation tensors and is the first reported method to improve student performance on ImageNet with differing teacher-student architectures.
- Novel and intuitive idea: transfer "where a network looks" rather than full activations (FitNets) or output distributions (KD).
- Strong empirical validation using modern baselines (WRN, ResNet) demonstrating gains are not due to weak baselines.
- Ablation studies analyze attention mapping choices and stage-wise transfer, strengthening conclusions.
- Notable ImageNet result: 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement for ResNet-18 where KD failed in their experiments.
- Activation-based AT is computationally efficient with little overhead and code is released for reproducibility.
- Suggestion: strengthen motivation for the chosen activation-based attention formulation (e.g., link to vision/neuroscience).
- Suggestion: expand on technical challenges and limitations of gradient-based AT, particularly batch-norm complications during second backprop.
- Suggestion: include analysis of sensitivity to the AT loss weight β to aid practitioners.
- Question: quantify training time overhead for activation-based AT (e.g., percentage increase in wall-clock time on ImageNet).
- Question: clarify how AT and KD loss terms were balanced when combined and whether α and β interact.
- Question: provide qualitative attention visualizations to confirm sharper, discriminative focus on fine-grained datasets like CUB versus general datasets.
- Question: consider and discuss whether AT’s benefits stem from regularization effects (e.g., gradient-norm minimization) rather than pure mimicking.
