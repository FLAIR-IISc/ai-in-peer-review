### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Attention Transfer (AT), a novel and effective method for knowledge distillation. The core idea is to improve the performance of a smaller "student" network by training it to mimic the spatial attention maps of a larger, pre-trained "teacher" network. The work proposes two distinct methods for defining these spatial attention maps: 1) an activation-based approach, which aggregates activation tensor statistics across the channel dimension to highlight spatially important regions, and 2) a gradient-based approach, which uses the gradient of the loss with respect to the input as a sensitivity map.

The central claim is that these attention maps serve as a potent and compact form of "hint" from the teacher, guiding the student to learn not just *what* to predict, but *where* to look. This claim is substantiated through extensive experiments across a variety of datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200-2011, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet) and modern CNN architectures (NIN, ResNet, Wide ResNet). The results consistently show that Attention Transfer improves student network performance, can be effectively combined with existing knowledge distillation techniques, and outperforms related methods that transfer full, dense activation tensors. A particularly notable contribution is the successful application of the method to the large-scale ImageNet dataset, demonstrating a clear improvement over a strong baseline.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

1.  **Novelty and Strong Motivation:** The paper presents a simple, intuitive, and novel approach to knowledge transfer. By framing the problem as transferring "attention," the work creates a compelling bridge between the fields of network interpretability/visualization and model compression. The motivation to transfer a compressed summary of important spatial information, rather than full feature maps, is well-argued and elegant.
2.  **Comprehensive and Strong Empirical Support:** The claims are backed by thorough and convincing experimental results. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated across multiple datasets, state-of-the-art architectures, and different training paradigms (training from scratch and fine-tuning). The consistent performance gains, especially the significant improvement reported on ImageNet, provide strong evidence for the method's general applicability and value.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper is well-executed and presents a valuable contribution to the field of knowledge distillation.

*   **Methodological Soundness:** The proposed formulations for both activation-based and gradient-based attention are clear, well-defined, and appear straightforward to implement. The use of a simple L2 loss on normalized attention maps is a reasonable choice for the transfer mechanism. A key strength of the experimental design is the direct comparison in Section 4.1.2 between transferring attention maps and transferring full activation tensors (FitNets-style), which effectively demonstrates that the proposed attention abstraction is a more beneficial form of knowledge for transfer.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The evaluation is extensive and robust.
    *   **Generality:** By testing on datasets ranging from CIFAR-10 to ImageNet, and including specialized tasks like fine-grained recognition (CUB) and scene classification (Scenes), the paper demonstrates the broad utility of Attention Transfer.
    *   **Relevance:** The use of modern, high-performing architectures like ResNets and Wide ResNets ensures the results are relevant to current practices and are not benchmarked against weak baselines.
    *   **Ablation Studies:** The paper includes valuable analyses, such as comparing different activation-based mapping functions (Table 2) and showing the cumulative benefit of applying the AT loss at multiple network stages, which strengthens the conclusions.
*   **Significant Result on ImageNet:** The reported 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement for a ResNet-18 student on ImageNet is a significant result. As the paper notes, many existing hint-based knowledge transfer methods have not been successfully demonstrated on this scale, particularly between networks of different depths. This finding alone makes the paper a noteworthy contribution. The public release of code and models further enhances the work's impact and reproducibility.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions aimed at further strengthening the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Hyperparameter Details:** The procedure for setting the loss weight `beta` is described as a heuristic (10^3 divided by map size and batch size). It would be beneficial to formalize this slightly. For instance, is `beta` calculated independently for each transfer loss in the summation in Eq. 2? Additionally, more detail on the decay schedule used for `beta` when combining AT with knowledge distillation would improve clarity and reproducibility.
*   **Scope of Gradient-based AT:** The experiments for the gradient-based method are limited to a simpler network on CIFAR-10, with the paper citing difficulties regarding batch normalization in the second backpropagation step. This is understandable, but it limits the impact of this portion of the work. A more detailed discussion of these specific challenges could be very valuable, as it might spur future research into overcoming these technical hurdles and applying gradient-based transfer to more complex, modern architectures.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** The provided visualizations of attention maps are insightful. The paper could be enhanced by including a few examples of failure cases. For instance, are there cases where the student's attention still fails to match the teacher's, and does this correlate with misclassification? Such an analysis could provide deeper insight into the mechanism and its limitations.

### **Questions for the Authors**

These questions are intended to clarify certain aspects of the work.

*   The importance of normalizing the attention maps before computing the transfer loss is emphasized. Could the authors elaborate on the observed behavior when training without this normalization? Does the training become unstable, or does it converge to a less optimal solution?
*   In the ImageNet experiments, the AT loss was applied to the final two residual groups. Was this choice based on preliminary experiments, or was it primarily for computational efficiency? The plan to update the paper with results from applying losses to all four groups is noted and would be a welcome addition.
*   The paper hypothesizes that AT is particularly effective for fine-grained recognition (CUB) due to the importance of intermediate attention. This is a compelling idea. Is it possible to provide more direct evidence, for example, by qualitatively showing that the student's attention maps after AT focus more accurately on small, discriminative object parts (e.g., a bird's beak or eye) compared to the baseline student?
*   The gradient-based symmetry norm (Eq. 6) acts as a regularizer. Was this method ever combined with the student-teacher gradient-based AT? These two ideas seem complementary and could potentially yield further improvements.
