- Proposes Attention Transfer (AT), a knowledge distillation technique where a student mimics teacher spatial attention maps.  
- Trains students to improve by learning "where to look" from a larger teacher network.  
- Introduces two attention-map generation methods: simple activation-based and more complex gradient-based.  
- Reports extensive experiments showing significant, consistent student improvements across datasets, tasks, and architectures.  
- Recommends Accept.  
- Activation-based AT is well-motivated and yields strong empirical gains, including on ImageNet.  
- Thorough experimental comparisons to baselines (KD, FitNets-style feature transfer) convincingly favor summarized attention transfer.  
- Strength lies in simplicity and effectiveness; transferring attention is more abstract/useful than matching logits or full feature maps.  
- Notable ImageNet result: ResNet-18 student closes much of the gap with ResNet-34 while standard KD struggled.  
- Ablation studies on CIFAR explore attention mappings and show individual contributions of transfer losses.  
- Weakness: gradient-based AT is underdeveloped, tested only in a non–batch-norm setting and performs worse than activation-based AT.  
- Suggests strengthening or reframing the gradient-based work as preliminary; soften the claim of being the first KD method successful on ImageNet.  
- Questions for authors: why decaying AT loss weight β helps when combined with KD (curriculum-like effect?), and how activation- vs. gradient-based attention maps qualitatively differ.
