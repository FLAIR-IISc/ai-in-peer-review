1. Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a simple, well-motivated method to transfer “where to look” information from a teacher CNN to a student CNN by aligning spatial attention maps. Two attention definitions are explored:
- Activation-based attention: spatial maps computed by aggregating absolute activation magnitudes across channels at intermediate layers (e.g., sum, sum of powers, max of powers), with l2 normalization.
- Gradient-based attention: input-gradient maps used as sensitivity proxies, trained via a double-backprop objective; additionally, a flip-symmetry regularizer is proposed.

Attention Transfer (AT) is imposed as an auxiliary loss across corresponding layers (or groups) in teacher and student, optionally combined with Knowledge Distillation (KD). Extensive experiments on CIFAR, fine-grained CUB, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet show consistent gains across architectures (NIN, ResNets, WRNs), with low overhead for activation-based AT. The paper reports that AT compares favorably to full activation regression (FitNets-style) and can complement KD. Code and models are released.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Clear, simple idea with strong empirical support across datasets/architectures, including a noteworthy improvement on ImageNet (ResNet-18 gains +1.1% top-1).
- Novelty lies in defining and leveraging spatial attention as the distilled signal, which is both intuitive and computationally light, and shown to be more effective than full activation matching in these settings.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem importance: How to transfer inductive biases beyond logits/activations is central to distillation. “Where to look” is a natural and underexplored target; the paper fills this gap.
- Method clarity and motivation: The choice of attention mappings (Fsum, Fpsum, Fpmax) is justified by the hypothesis that magnitude encodes importance; normalization is motivated and empirically shown to matter. The placement of losses across residual groups is reasonable and explored.
- Empirical breadth and rigor:
  - CIFAR: Consistent reductions in error for multiple teacher–student pairs; ablations over attention mappings show sum-based functions outperform max; study of per-group losses adds insight.
  - Comparison to full activation transfer: AT provides larger final accuracy gains while also accelerating convergence—an important and specific finding.
  - Gradient-based AT: Despite implementation complexity, the authors provide careful baselines (double backprop, symmetry norm) and show improvements; symmetry regularization itself is a useful side result.
  - Transfer learning: On CUB and MIT Scenes, AT substantially narrows the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34, supporting the claim that mid-level attention is particularly valuable in fine-grained tasks.
  - ImageNet: Positive results with minimal tuning are compelling; few distillation works show convincing gains at this scale.
- Reproducibility: Public code and detailed implementation notes increase confidence.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Quantify compute and memory overhead: Provide wall-clock and GPU memory comparisons for AT vs baseline (and vs full activation transfer), especially on ImageNet. This will help practitioners weigh trade-offs.
- Statistical reporting: Where feasible, add variance/error bars across runs for key results (e.g., CIFAR, ImageNet) to characterize stability.
- Clarify the KD combination: Report hyperparameters and schedules for AT+KD, including the annealing of β and KD temperature/α. It would help to include sensitivity plots or a brief hyperparameter sweep.
- Class-conditional vs class-agnostic attention: Activation-based maps are class-agnostic; discussing whether class-conditional attention (e.g., CAM/Grad-CAM variants) helps or hurts transfer would enrich the story.
- Broader teacher–student mismatches: Include results where architectures differ more substantially (e.g., VGG teacher to ResNet student), to test generality.
- Gradient-based AT with batch normalization: The paper notes open questions. A short methodological appendix with a recommended implementation recipe (e.g., frozen BN statistics for the second pass) would reduce ambiguity.
- Additional tasks: Given the spatial nature of attention, small-scale experiments on detection/segmentation or weakly supervised localization could strengthen the claim that AT transfers useful spatial priors.

Questions for the authors
- Loss placement and weighting:
  - How sensitive are results to the choice and number of layers where AT is applied? Did you try weighting later layers more heavily, or a simple schedule over depth?
  - On ImageNet, do you expect further gains when adding AT to all four residual groups? Any preliminary results?
- Normalization choice:
  - You emphasize l2 normalization of attention maps; did you compare to l1 or batch-wise standardization across spatial locations? Any intuition for why l2 works best?
- Hyperparameters:
  - Please provide exact β settings per layer/batch and the decay schedule used when combining AT with KD. How sensitive are results to β?
  - For Fpsum, how does p affect results beyond the few values shown (e.g., p in [1.5, 3])?
- KD interplay:
  - On CIFAR, AT+KD helps, but on ImageNet KD hurts convergence. Is this primarily due to architectural depth mismatch, temperature choice, or label smoothing interactions? Did you try tuning KD alone more extensively on ImageNet?
- Gradient-based AT:
  - Could you detail the exact autodiff strategy for the second pass, including how BN (if present) and dropout are handled? Any numerical stability issues?
  - The symmetry norm performs well; did you try other equivariances (e.g., rotations) or applying symmetry at intermediate feature maps?
- Generalization and failure modes:
  - Do students ever overfit to teacher attention in a way that harms robustness (e.g., under domain shift)? Any observations on OOD performance or adversarial sensitivity?
- Claim about “first successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet”:
  - Please clarify the scope: do you mean first to show gains for convolutional teacher–student with intermediate supervision beyond logits? A brief literature note would avoid overclaiming.

Overall, this is a clear, impactful contribution that advances distillation by focusing on spatial attention. The method is simple, general, and practically useful, with convincing empirical evidence.
