- Proposes "attention transfer" (AT) to transfer spatial attention maps (activation- or gradient-based) from teacher to student CNNs by matching selected internal-layer attention maps.
- Introduces a normalized attention-matching loss that can be combined with cross-entropy and optionally with knowledge distillation; gradient-based AT uses a double-backprop objective and a symmetry regularizer.
- Reports extensive experiments: activation-AT on CIFAR-10, transfer learning (CUB, MIT Scenes), and ImageNet (ResNet-18 student, ResNet-34 teacher); gradient-AT evaluated on CIFAR.
- Empirical findings: activation-AT consistently improves student accuracy; AT+KD often helps on CIFAR; activation-AT outperforms full-activation regression; gradient-based AT and symmetry/gradient-norm regularizers help; ImageNet shows +1.1% top-1/+0.8% top-5 for ResNet-18.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reasons for accept: clear and well-motivated idea, consistent non-trivial empirical gains including ImageNet, simplicity and low overhead of activation-AT, and released code.
- Problem framed as transferring inductive biases about “where to look,” distinct from KD and FitNets; spatial reductions give compact, transferable supervision signals.
- Literature placement and motivation are sound, linking attention visualization and distillation literatures and formalizing attention maps as transfer targets.
- Ablations on CIFAR examine attention mapping choices, block placement, and show attention-map matching beats full activation regression.
- Gradient-based experiments compare double-backprop and symmetry regularizers and show AT improves over other gradient-based regularizers.
- Transfer-learning experiments show AT can close much of the performance gap between smaller and larger networks; ImageNet results indicate scalability.
- Scientific correctness: losses and normalization choices are clearly defined and computational issues (second-order derivatives) discussed; claims are appropriately scoped.
- Essential suggested improvements: strengthen ImageNet evaluation (full training details, seeds/variance, more AT groups, tuned KD baseline), clarify fairness of comparisons and report time/memory overhead, and expand ablation on normalization and p-choice.
- Nice-to-have suggestions: provide quantitative attention-similarity metrics, explore cross-architecture ImageNet transfers, test robustness (occlusion/corruptions), and give practical guidance for batch-norm in double backprop.
- Clarification questions requested: exact ImageNet AT hyperparameters and sensitivity; consistency of sum vs max attention across datasets; how attention maps were aligned across spatial resolutions; extent of KD tuning on ImageNet; wall-clock overheads for AT on ImageNet and gradient-AT on CIFAR; effectiveness when teacher is only marginally better or architecturally different.
- Overall assessment: simple, well-motivated, broadly applicable method with consistent gains and code release; with stronger large-scale evaluation and extra ablations it would be a solid contribution.
