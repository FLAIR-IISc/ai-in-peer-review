Summary of claims and contributions: The paper proposes “attention transfer” as a knowledge transfer mechanism for CNNs, defining attention as spatial maps derived either from activations (channel-wise statistics such as sum of absolute activations with p-power) or from input gradients. The core claim is that aligning a student’s attention maps to a teacher’s improves student performance across datasets and architectures, sometimes synergistically with knowledge distillation. The paper also claims activation-based attention transfer (AT) is more effective than transferring full activations and presents preliminary results suggesting AT scales to ImageNet.

Decision: Accept. Key reasons: (1) Clear, simple, and novel formulation of attention-based knowledge transfer with strong empirical gains on several datasets and architectures; (2) Good practical utility (low overhead, code released), with evidence of applicability from CIFAR to ImageNet and fine-tuning scenarios.

Supporting arguments: The activation-based AT loss is well specified, with normalization choices and layer placement guidelines, and is evaluated on WRNs and ResNets, showing consistent improvements (e.g., WRN-16-1 error reductions and ResNet-18 +1.1% top-1 on ImageNet). Ablations compare different attention mappings (sum vs max, effect of p) and show AT outperforms full-activation transfer while accelerating convergence. The paper situates itself well in the literature (distillation, FitNets, CAM/Grad-CAM, double backprop) and extends transfer beyond logits/features to a meaningful intermediate representation (spatial focus). Gradient-based AT and a symmetry regularizer offer additional avenues, with initial gains on CIFAR. Code availability supports reproducibility.

Additional feedback (to help improve; not all are decision-critical):
- Strengthen statistical rigor: report mean±std over multiple seeds on CIFAR and provide confidence intervals on ImageNet/CUB/Scenes; clarify when medians are used and why.
- Expand ablations: sensitivity to β and normalization choice; which layers matter most on ImageNet; interactions with KD across temperatures/α; robustness to teacher quality.
- Gradient-based AT: demonstrate with batch normalization and on larger models; detail the computational/memory overhead of the second backprop and how BN parameters are handled.
- Temper or substantiate the “first on ImageNet” claim by surveying contemporary distillation-on-ImageNet results or reframing as “to our knowledge.”
- Consider additional baselines (e.g., FitNets-style hints on comparable layers, other saliency/CAM-derived transfers) for completeness.

Questions for the authors:
- How sensitive is AT to the teacher’s accuracy? Does a marginally better teacher still help, and is there a failure mode with weak or miscalibrated teachers?
- What is the wall-clock and memory overhead of AT on ImageNet per training step and overall?
- Did you evaluate l1 vs l2 normalization of attention maps systematically, and how does β scale across resolutions?
- How are mismatched spatial resolutions handled in practice (interpolation choices), and do they affect results?
- Does AT improve calibration or localization (e.g., CAM quality) of the student, beyond top-1/5 accuracy?
