Summary and contributions
The paper proposes using “attention transfer” (AT) as a form of knowledge transfer from a trained teacher CNN to a student CNN. The key idea is to define spatial attention maps for CNNs and encourage the student to match the teacher’s attention while training on the primary task. Two definitions of attention are explored: (1) activation-based maps obtained by aggregating absolute activations across channels (e.g., sum of powers), and (2) gradient-based maps defined as input gradients of the loss (input sensitivity). The authors introduce a simple normalized loss to match attention maps across corresponding layers, optionally combining AT with knowledge distillation (KD). Experiments on CIFAR-10 across multiple teacher/student pairs (WRN and NIN), on transfer to CUB-200 and MIT Indoor Scenes, and on ImageNet (ResNet-34→ResNet-18) show consistent improvements, including +1.1% top-1 and +0.8% top-5 on ImageNet without hyperparameter tuning. They also show AT improves convergence, outperforms FitNets-style full activation matching, and is complementary to KD on CIFAR. Code is released.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons: (1) Conceptual novelty with practical simplicity: defining and matching spatial attention as a compact, architecture-agnostic signal for transfer is elegant and broadly applicable. (2) Empirical support across datasets and architectures, including successful application on ImageNet, with consistent gains and low overhead. The method is easy to adopt and addresses known limitations of KD/hints in cross-architecture settings.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper clearly targets the challenge of transferring knowledge beyond logits and full feature tensors, arguing that attention maps capture where the network focuses, which is a compact and salient supervisory signal. This is well motivated by prior visualization/attention literature and by FitNets/hints.

- Method soundness: The activation-based attention functions (Fsum, Fsum^p, Fmax^p) are reasonable, differentiable, and scale-invariant via l2 normalization; the loss formulation is straightforward. The gradient-based variant correctly leverages double backprop and relates to classical gradient-regularization; extending it to transfer is a neat idea.

- Empirical validation:
  • CIFAR-10: Multiple student–teacher pairs show consistent improvements. Sum-based functions outperform max-based. AT > full activation matching, and AT+KD > KD alone, supporting the claim that attention is a better transfer target than raw activations.
  • Gradient-based AT: Improves over baseline and over double backprop/flip-invariance regularizers in a controlled (deterministic) setting, demonstrating feasibility.
  • Transfer learning (CUB/Scenes): AT narrows the ResNet-18 vs ResNet-34 gap and can outperform KD, especially on fine-grained CUB where intermediate spatial attention is plausibly more informative.
  • ImageNet: A simple two-layer AT setup yields a non-trivial +1.1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18 without tuning, indicating scalability and practical utility.

- Positioning and related work: The paper connects to attention visualization (Guided BP, CAM/Grad-CAM), to KD/FitNets, and to double backprop. Using attention as the medium for transfer is a clear and differentiated contribution relative to these works.

- Reproducibility and practicality: Code availability, clear loss definitions, and minimal computational overhead are strong positives for impact.

Limitations and concerns (non-blocking)
- Statistical rigor: Some CIFAR experiments report single runs (for gradient-based AT) or medians without variances; broader reporting of mean±std over multiple seeds would strengthen claims of consistent gains, especially for small margins.

- Gradient-based AT: The scope is limited (small NIN without BN), and the handling of BN in second-order backprop is left unresolved. As a result, this part feels more preliminary than the activation-based AT, which carries the weight of the contribution.

- ImageNet baselines: The KD baseline is reported as underperforming with shared hyperparameters and not fully trained due to compute. While this supports the claim that KD can be brittle, a tuned KD baseline (or citation to a strong KD-on-ImageNet result) would temper the claim that this is the first successful transfer method on ImageNet.

- Analysis: More systematic ablations on number/placement of AT layers, the effect of p, and normalization choices would help generalize practical recommendations beyond the selected defaults.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Please report mean±std across ≥3 seeds for the main CIFAR and transfer results, and include confidence intervals for ImageNet if feasible. Highlight the typical effect size and variance.

- Provide wall-clock overhead measurements due to computing teacher attention maps (forward only) and, for gradient-based AT, the double backprop pass. This will help practitioners weigh accuracy vs. cost.

- Layer selection: Include an ablation on which layers to apply AT and whether a simple schedule (e.g., all groups vs last two) is optimal across architectures.

- Normalization/scaling: Consider learning per-layer AT weights (β_j) or using temperature-like scaling to reduce per-dataset tuning.

- Attention definitions: Briefly discuss pre- vs post-activation attention (especially in ResNets) and whether batch norm statistics impact activation-based attention.

- ImageNet: Since AT improved ResNet-18 notably with minimal tuning, pushing a full ablation (all 4 groups, varied β) would considerably strengthen the paper. A tuned KD baseline would also clarify relative strengths.

- Gradient-based AT: Clarify recommended handling of BN during the second backprop. If possible, add a small-scale BN experiment to demonstrate feasibility.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are the gains to the choice of p in Fsum^p and to the l2 normalization? Did you try l1 normalization or temperature scaling of attention maps, and how did they compare?
2. How did you select the layers for AT on ImageNet? Do earlier groups add value beyond the last two?
3. What is the wall-clock training overhead of AT on ImageNet relative to baseline and KD?
4. For CIFAR and transfer experiments, can you report mean±std over multiple seeds and indicate whether improvements are statistically significant?
5. Did you attempt cross-architecture transfers beyond depth/width changes (e.g., VGG→ResNet, Inception→ResNet)? How robust is AT in such settings?
6. For gradient-based AT, what is your recommended approach for BN during the second backprop? Have you tried freezing BN statistics and parameters in that pass?
7. The claim about being the first successful transfer method on ImageNet is intriguing; can you add context or citations contrasting with prior KD/FitNets results on ImageNet?

Overall, this paper introduces a simple, well-motivated, and effective technique with strong practical promise. The activation-based AT results are sufficiently convincing for acceptance; the gradient-based AT is interesting but could be expanded in future work.
