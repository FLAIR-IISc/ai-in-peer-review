Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a knowledge transfer mechanism for convolutional neural networks. Spatial attention maps are defined from internal representations in two ways: activation-based (channel-wise reductions of absolute activations, e.g., sum or power-sum) and gradient-based (input gradients of the loss), and a student is trained to match a teacher’s attention at selected layers.
- A simple, normalized attention-matching loss is introduced and can be combined with standard cross-entropy and optionally with knowledge distillation (KD). For gradient-based AT, a double-backprop-type objective is used; an additional symmetry regularizer enforces horizontal flip invariance of gradient attention maps.
- Extensive experiments are reported: activation-based AT on CIFAR-10 (WRN and NIN variants), on transfer learning (CUB-200-2011 and MIT Scenes), and on ImageNet (ResNet-18 student with ResNet-34 teacher). Gradient-based AT is explored on CIFAR with NIN (without batch normalization due to second-derivative complications).
- Empirically: (a) activation-based AT consistently improves student accuracy across teacher–student pairs and datasets; (b) combining AT with KD often helps on CIFAR; (c) activation-based AT outperforms “full activation” regression; (d) gradient-based AT and related gradient norms/symmetry regularizers also help; (e) on ImageNet, AT yields a reported +1.1% top-1 and +0.8% top-5 improvement for ResNet-18 over its baseline using a ResNet-34 teacher. Code is released.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) A clear and well-motivated idea—match teacher–student attention maps—placed appropriately relative to KD/FitNets and attention visualization literature; (2) Consistent, non-trivial empirical gains across architectures and datasets, including a noteworthy ImageNet result; (3) Method simplicity, low overhead for activation-based AT, and availability of code.

Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: transferring inductive biases about “where to look” in CNNs. This is distinct from KD (which matches softened outputs) and from FitNets (which match full intermediate activations with auxiliary regressors). The proposed spatial reductions produce compact supervision signals that appear more transferable and robust than full activation matching.
- Motivation and literature placement are sound. The paper connects to attention visualization (Simonyan et al., Zeiler & Fergus, Guided Backprop, CAM/Grad-CAM) and to distillation (Hinton et al., FitNets). The contribution is to repurpose attention maps as transfer targets and to formalize simple, normalized objectives for that purpose.
- Evidence: Ablations on CIFAR examine attention mapping choices (sum vs. max; powers), placement across blocks, and comparison to full activation transfer. The observation that attention-map matching outperforms full activation regression is informative and practically important. The gradient-based section compares several gradient-related regularizers (double backprop, symmetry) and shows AT improves over them. Transfer-learning experiments show AT can close much of the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34. ImageNet results, even without extensive tuning, indicate scalability beyond small benchmarks.
- Scientific correctness: The losses are clearly defined, normalization choices are justified empirically, and computational aspects are discussed (e.g., second-order requirements for gradient-based AT). Claims about KD struggling across depths/architectures on ImageNet are consistent with the reported experience; the paper appropriately limits the claim to this setting.

Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful; not all essential)
Essential
- Strengthen ImageNet evaluation: report full training details (schedules, augmentations, β values per layer, normalization choice), run multiple seeds, and provide variance/confidence intervals. Consider adding AT losses to more groups as planned, and include a comparison to a fair, tuned KD baseline under the same compute budget.
- Clarify fairness of comparisons: ensure equal total training epochs and similar regularization across baselines, AT, KD, and full-activation transfer; report training wall-clock and memory overhead (activation-based AT should be small; quantify it).
- Expand ablation on the importance of l2 normalization of attention maps (e.g., l1 vs l2 vs none) and on the choice of p in Fpsum across datasets.

Nice-to-have
- Provide a quantitative measure of attention similarity (e.g., cosine similarity across layers during training) along with qualitative visualizations to connect accuracy gains with attention alignment.
- Explore cross-architecture transfers on ImageNet (e.g., Inception/ResNet teacher to ResNet student) to evaluate generality.
- Investigate robustness effects: occlusion sensitivity or corruption robustness could be improved if attention is better guided; a small study would add value.
- For gradient-based AT: include a brief treatment of batch normalization in the second backprop pass (frozen vs. updated statistics and parameters), even if only as a practical recipe, and quantify its cost.

Questions for clarification
- What are the exact hyperparameters for AT on ImageNet (β per layer, normalization choice, schedule/decay), and how sensitive are results to these choices?
- In the CIFAR ablations, sum-based attention outperforms max-based. Is this consistent on ImageNet and fine-grained tasks? Any insight into when max-based could be preferable?
- How were attention maps matched across different spatial resolutions when teacher and student differ in depth/stride? Was interpolation bilinear, and was any alignment (e.g., padding/centering) needed?
- For the KD comparisons that struggled on ImageNet, were temperature and α thoroughly tuned for the student–teacher pair? Under equal tuning effort, does AT still outperform?
- What is the wall-clock/training time overhead of AT on ImageNet (activation-based) and of gradient-based AT on CIFAR relative to the baseline?
- Does AT still help when the teacher is only marginally better than the student, or when the teacher is architecturally different but similar in accuracy?

Overall assessment
The paper introduces a simple, well-motivated, and broadly applicable idea that yields consistent empirical gains and scales to ImageNet. The work is clearly presented, compares against reasonable baselines, and releases code. With strengthened large-scale evaluation and additional ablations, this submission would make a solid contribution to knowledge transfer and interpretability-aware training.
