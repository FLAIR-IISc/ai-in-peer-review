## Review of "Attention Transfer"

This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks by transferring attention maps from a powerful teacher network to a student network. The authors define attention using both activation-based and gradient-based methods and demonstrate consistent performance improvements across various datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and network architectures (NIN, ResNet). The work is well-motivated, building on the growing understanding of attention mechanisms in both human and artificial vision systems. The idea of using attention as a transfer mechanism is insightful and potentially valuable.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The core idea is novel and the experimental results, while not exhaustive, provide compelling evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The consistent improvements observed across different datasets and architectures suggest a robust and generalizable method. 

The paper provides a clear explanation of the proposed methods, including the different attention map definitions and transfer loss functions. The experimental setup is reasonably well-described, and the inclusion of a code repository is a significant positive. The ablation studies on CIFAR, comparing different attention mapping functions and the impact of full activation transfer, are particularly valuable. The authors also acknowledge limitations, such as the computational cost of gradient-based transfer and the need for further investigation on more complex tasks like object detection.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification and expansion. The discussion of the impact of batch normalization on gradient-based attention transfer feels incomplete. A more detailed analysis of the hyperparameters used for knowledge distillation, especially on ImageNet where it didn’t perform well, would be helpful. It would also be beneficial to include visualizations of the attention maps themselves, beyond those presented in the appendix, to provide a more intuitive understanding of what the networks are “attending” to. 

Specifically, I would like to know: 
* What is the sensitivity of the results to the choice of β (the weight of the attention transfer loss)?
* How does the performance change with different normalization schemes for the attention maps (e.g., L1 normalization)?
* Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of why KD didn't work well on ImageNet, and what steps were taken to troubleshoot it?
