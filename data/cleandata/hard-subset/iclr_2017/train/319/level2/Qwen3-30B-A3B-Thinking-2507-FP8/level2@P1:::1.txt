

Review for "Attention Transfer"

1. Summary: The paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge distillation by transferring attention maps from a teacher network to a student network. The authors define two types of attention maps (activation-based and gradient-based) and demonstrate how these can be used to significantly improve the performance of smaller student networks across various datasets and architectures. The contributions include a formal definition of attention for CNNs, novel attention transfer methods, and extensive experimental validation showing consistent improvements over baselines, including when combined with knowledge distillation. The work is well-motivated by both human attention mechanisms and the need for more efficient network architectures.

2. Decision: Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of knowledge distillation and network compression. The core idea of attention transfer is novel and well-justified, and the empirical results are convincing across multiple datasets and architectures.

3. Supporting arguments: The paper successfully addresses the key question of whether attention information can improve student network performance. The motivation is well-grounded in the literature on human attention and existing neural attention mechanisms. The authors demonstrate that attention maps contain valuable information that can be transferred to improve student networks, with consistent improvements across multiple datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network). The ablation studies in Section 4.1.1-4.1.2 effectively show that attention transfer is superior to transferring full activations (FitNets-style) and that the specific attention mapping functions they propose (particularly F^2sum) work best. The results on ImageNet are particularly impressive as they represent the first successful application of knowledge transfer methods to ImageNet, which is a significant contribution.

4. Additional feedback: 
- The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of why attention transfer works better than full activation transfer (Section 4.1.2). While the paper notes that attention maps carry more important information, a more detailed analysis of what specific information is captured by attention maps that isn't present in full activations would strengthen the theoretical foundation.
- The authors should clarify the relationship between attention transfer and knowledge distillation. While they mention combining both methods, it would be helpful to explicitly state how attention transfer complements knowledge distillation (e.g., attention transfer focuses on spatial information while knowledge distillation focuses on class distribution).
- The paper mentions that gradient-based attention transfer was limited to CIFAR due to computational constraints, but it would be valuable to discuss why batch normalization causes issues in gradient-based attention transfer (Section 4.1.3). This could help future work address this limitation.
- The paper claims "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" (Section 4.2.2), but this should be qualified by noting that other knowledge transfer methods might have been applied to ImageNet but not reported in the literature. A more precise statement would be "to the best of our knowledge, this is the first successful application of attention-based knowledge transfer on ImageNet."

5. Questions for authors:
- In Table 2, the error rates for F^2sum and Fsum are very close (7.93 vs 7.99). Could the authors elaborate on why these two functions perform so similarly, and whether there are specific scenarios where one might be preferable to the other?
- The paper notes that attention maps from higher layers tend to be more blurry (Fig. 4). How does this affect the attention transfer process, and what measures do the authors take to handle this issue during training?
- In Section 4.2.1, the authors mention that attention transfer works better on CUB than on Scenes. Could they provide more insight into why fine-grained recognition benefits more from attention transfer than scene classification?
- The paper states that "attention maps focus on different parts for different layers in the network" (Section 3.1). Could the authors provide more specific examples or analysis of how attention maps change across layers for different types of tasks (e.g., object detection vs. fine-grained classification)?
