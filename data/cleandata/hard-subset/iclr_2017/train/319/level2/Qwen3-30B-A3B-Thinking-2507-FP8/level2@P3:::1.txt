

The paper introduces attention transfer as a novel knowledge transfer technique for convolutional neural networks, where a student network learns to mimic the spatial attention maps of a teacher network. It proposes two mechanisms—activation-based (using statistics of feature activations) and gradient-based (using input sensitivity maps)—to transfer attention information, demonstrating consistent performance improvements across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network). The work positions itself as addressing a gap in knowledge distillation by leveraging attention maps rather than logits or full activations, with the key claim that attention maps encode more transferable information than standard distillation methods.

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution through its empirical validation of attention transfer on ImageNet, which the authors correctly identify as the first successful application of knowledge transfer for this dataset. The approach is well-motivated by the correlation between attention map patterns and network performance, and the experimental design rigorously compares attention transfer against ablation baselines (e.g., full-activation transfer) and prior methods (e.g., knowledge distillation).

The paper supports its claims through comprehensive experiments. On CIFAR-10, activation-based attention transfer (using F²sum) consistently improves accuracy over baselines (e.g., 7.93% error vs. 8.77% without transfer) and outperforms full-activation transfer by a significant margin. The ImageNet results are particularly compelling: ResNet-18 with attention transfer achieves 1.1% higher top-1 accuracy than the baseline, with ablation studies confirming the necessity of attention maps over alternative transfer mechanisms. The analysis of attention map properties (e.g., spatial correlation with object regions across network depths) provides strong theoretical grounding for the approach. The gradient-based method is also validated, though the paper acknowledges limitations in handling batch normalization during second-order backpropagation, which is appropriately noted as a future direction.

The work is well-placed within the literature. It correctly contextualizes prior attention mechanisms (e.g., Grad-CAM, visual question answering) and knowledge distillation (Hinton et al.), while clearly differentiating its contribution: attention transfer focuses on spatial information rather than output distributions or intermediate activations. The comparison to FitNets and knowledge distillation is thorough, and the paper’s claim of being the first to apply knowledge transfer successfully on ImageNet is substantiated by the absence of such results in prior literature.

To further strengthen the paper, the following points are recommended:  
1. Clarify the handling of batch normalization in gradient-based attention transfer. The paper notes uncertainty about batch norm behavior during second backpropagation but does not explore solutions (e.g., fixed batch norm statistics). A brief discussion or ablation on this would address a critical implementation concern.  
2. Expand the analysis of the F²sum attention function’s superiority. The paper shows F²sum outperforms F¹max and F⁴sum but does not explain why the squared sum captures discriminative regions more effectively. A qualitative analysis of attention maps across different p values would add depth.  
3. Explore the combination of attention transfer with knowledge distillation on ImageNet. While the paper notes KD’s poor convergence on ImageNet with standard hyperparameters, it does not test whether attention transfer could mitigate this issue. A small experiment here would strengthen the practical utility claim.  

The authors should also address whether attention transfer is beneficial for tasks beyond classification (e.g., object detection), as the paper briefly mentions this as future work. While not essential for acceptance, a sentence on the potential applicability to spatially sensitive tasks would better contextualize the contribution.

The paper’s core claims are well-supported, its methodology is sound, and its results are significant—particularly the ImageNet validation. The minor suggestions for clarification and expansion do not undermine the paper’s contributions but rather refine its presentation and scope. The work advances knowledge distillation by demonstrating that attention maps contain transferable information beyond standard distillation, with clear empirical evidence across diverse settings.
