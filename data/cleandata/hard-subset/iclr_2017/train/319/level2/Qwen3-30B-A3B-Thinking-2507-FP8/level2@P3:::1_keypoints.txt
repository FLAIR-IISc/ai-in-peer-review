- Introduces attention transfer: student network learns to mimic teacher spatial attention maps as a novel knowledge transfer technique.  
- Proposes two mechanisms: activation-based (feature activation statistics) and gradient-based (input sensitivity maps).  
- Demonstrates consistent performance gains across datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network).  
- Key claim: attention maps encode more transferable information than logits or full-activation distillation.  
- Empirical validation is strong, notably first successful large-scale knowledge transfer on ImageNet (ResNet-18 + attention transfer: +1.1% top-1).  
- Rigorous experimental design with ablations and comparisons to prior methods (FitNets, KD, full-activation transfer).  
- Activation-based F²sum attention function consistently outperforms F¹max and F⁴sum on CIFAR-10.  
- Analysis links attention map spatial patterns and network performance, providing theoretical grounding.  
- Gradient-based method validated but has limitations handling batch normalization during second-order backpropagation.  
- Well-placed in literature, clearly differentiating spatial attention transfer from previous attention/KD methods.  
- Recommendations: clarify batch-norm behavior in gradient-based transfer (e.g., fixed stats), analyze why F²sum is superior (qualitative maps across p values), and test combining attention transfer with knowledge distillation on ImageNet.  
- Suggest exploring applicability beyond classification (e.g., object detection) to contextualize broader utility.
