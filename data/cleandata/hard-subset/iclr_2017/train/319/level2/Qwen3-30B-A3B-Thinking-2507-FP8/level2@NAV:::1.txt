

This paper presents an interesting approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks through attention maps. The authors propose defining attention for CNNs as spatial maps indicating where the network focuses during decision-making, and demonstrate that transferring these attention maps from a teacher to a student network significantly improves performance. The key contributions include novel methods for both activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer, experimental validation across multiple datasets (including ImageNet), and the claim that attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer while being compatible with knowledge distillation.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a compelling case for attention transfer as a valuable knowledge transfer technique, with strong experimental support across multiple architectures and datasets. The results on ImageNet (achieving 1.1% top-1 improvement) are particularly significant as the authors claim this is the first successful knowledge transfer method applied to ImageNet, which appears well-supported by their experiments.

The experimental design is sound, with appropriate baselines and multiple runs to demonstrate statistical significance. The motivation is well-placed within the literature, and the paper demonstrates good understanding of related work on attention mechanisms and knowledge distillation. The proposed activation-based attention transfer method shows consistent improvements across various settings, and the ablation studies effectively demonstrate the importance of the key components.

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend addressing the following points: (1) Provide more detailed discussion of why attention maps contain more useful information than full activations (as observed in Table 1), (2) Include more thorough analysis of the computational cost of gradient-based attention transfer, particularly regarding the second backpropagation step, (3) Clarify the relationship between the proposed attention transfer and existing methods like FitNets, and (4) Expand on the limitations of gradient-based attention transfer, especially regarding batch normalization.

I would also appreciate clarification on whether the attention transfer method works effectively with other architectures beyond ResNet and Network-in-Network, and whether it can be applied to non-classification tasks like object detection. The paper's claim that it's the first knowledge transfer method to work on ImageNet seems plausible, but additional context comparing with recent work would be helpful.
