

**Review**

The paper proposes "attention transfer" as a novel method for knowledge distillation in convolutional neural networks, where a student network learns to mimic the attention maps of a more powerful teacher network. The authors define two types of attention maps (activation-based and gradient-based) and demonstrate that transferring these attention maps leads to consistent performance improvements across various datasets and network architectures. The paper makes a strong case for the value of attention information beyond standard knowledge distillation, showing that attention maps contain more useful transferable information than full activation tensors. The experiments on CIFAR and ImageNet datasets demonstrate significant accuracy gains, and the authors provide code and models for reproducibility.

I recommend **Reject** for the following key reasons: (1) The paper fails to sufficiently distinguish its contribution from prior knowledge distillation work, and (2) The claim that attention transfer is the "first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" lacks proper justification.

The paper's main weakness lies in its inadequate positioning within the literature. While the authors cite relevant prior work on attention visualization and knowledge distillation, they don't adequately contrast their approach with existing knowledge transfer methods. The paper claims that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," but this is unsubstantiated. The authors fail to compare their method with existing knowledge transfer approaches on ImageNet (e.g., FitNets, Hinton et al.'s KD), and the paper doesn't cite key works like "Deep Networks with Stochastic Depth" (Huang et al., 2016) or "Network Morphism" (Wang et al., 2016) that also address knowledge transfer on ImageNet. The authors' claim about ImageNet is particularly problematic since they acknowledge that KD struggles with different architectures but don't demonstrate how their method overcomes this limitation compared to prior work.

Additionally, the paper's experimental validation has several issues. The ImageNet results are limited to ResNet-18 with attention transfer versus baseline, with no comparison to other knowledge transfer methods on ImageNet. The authors state that "KD converges significantly slower" on ImageNet but don't provide evidence of improved final accuracy, only convergence speed. The paper also lacks statistical significance tests for the reported improvements, making it difficult to assess whether the gains are meaningful. The claim that "attention maps carry information that is more important for transfer than full activations" is supported by a single comparison in Table 1, but the paper doesn't provide deeper analysis of why this is the case.

The paper's strength lies in its clear definition of attention maps and the thorough exploration of different attention mapping functions. The visualization examples (Fig. 4) effectively demonstrate how attention maps correlate with discriminative regions in images. The dual focus on activation-based and gradient-based attention provides a comprehensive approach to the problem. The code availability is also a positive aspect.

To improve the paper, the authors should:
1. Conduct a thorough comparison with existing knowledge transfer methods on ImageNet, not just on CIFAR.
2. Provide statistical significance tests for all reported improvements.
3. Better contextualize their contribution within the knowledge distillation literature, explaining how attention transfer differs from and improves upon existing methods.
4. Investigate why attention maps contain more useful information than full activations, rather than simply stating it as a finding.
5. Address the computational cost of gradient-based attention transfer more thoroughly, as it requires double backpropagation which may be prohibitive for many applications.

I have two specific questions for the authors:
1. Can you provide evidence comparing attention transfer to other knowledge distillation methods (like FitNets or Hinton et al.'s KD) on ImageNet, including final accuracy metrics rather than just convergence speed?
2. Why do activation-based attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations, and how does this relate to the network's internal representations? The paper mentions this as a key finding but doesn't provide a theoretical explanation.
