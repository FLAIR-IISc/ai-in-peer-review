- Paper proposes "attention transfer" for distillation where students mimic teacher attention maps (activation- and gradient-based) and shows consistent improvements across datasets and architectures.
- Authors claim attention maps are more transferable than full activation tensors and provide code/models for reproducibility.
- Recommendation: Reject, mainly because the paper fails to sufficiently distinguish its contribution from prior work and makes an unsubstantiated claim about being the first method applied successfully on ImageNet.
- Major weakness: inadequate positioning within the literature and insufficient contrast with existing knowledge transfer methods.
- Unsubstantiated ImageNet claim: no comparison to prior knowledge transfer methods on ImageNet (e.g., FitNets, Hinton KD).
- Missing citations: does not cite relevant works addressing transfer on ImageNet (e.g., "Deep Networks with Stochastic Depth" and "Network Morphism").
- Experimental limitation: ImageNet results are limited to ResNet-18 AT vs baseline with no comparisons to other transfer methods.
- KD convergence claim: authors state KD converges slower on ImageNet but provide no evidence of improved final accuracy, only convergence speed.
- Lack of statistical testing: no statistical significance tests for reported improvements, making significance unclear.
- Insufficient analysis: claim that attention maps are more informative than full activations is supported by a single comparison and lacks deeper explanation.
- Computational concern: gradient-based attention transfer requires double backpropagation and its computational cost is not thoroughly addressed.
- Strength: clear definitions of attention maps and thorough exploration of different attention mapping functions.
- Strength: visualizations effectively show attention maps correlate with discriminative image regions.
- Strength: dual focus on activation-based and gradient-based attention provides a comprehensive approach.
- Strength: code and models released, aiding reproducibility.
- Suggested improvements: compare AT to existing transfer methods on ImageNet, include statistical significance tests, and better contextualize within KD literature.
- Suggested improvements: investigate why attention maps outperform full activations and analyze computational costs of gradient-based AT.
- Questions for authors: provide ImageNet comparisons (including final accuracy) against methods like FitNets/KD; explain theoretically or empirically why activation-based attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations.
