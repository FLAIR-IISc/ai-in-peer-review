### **ICLR Review**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes "Attention Transfer" (AT), a novel method for knowledge distillation where a smaller "student" network is trained to mimic the spatial attention maps of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The core idea is that transferring information about *where* a powerful network focuses its computational resources is a more effective form of guidance than transferring the full, dense activation tensors. The authors define two types of spatial attention maps: 1) Activation-based maps, which are simple, parameter-free statistics (e.g., sum or max of powered absolute values) computed across the channel dimension of activation tensors, and 2) Gradient-based maps, which represent the sensitivity of the output with respect to the input pixels. The authors conduct extensive experiments across CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, using modern architectures like Wide Residual Networks (WRNs) and ResNets. They demonstrate that their proposed AT method consistently improves student network performance, outperforms a FitNets-style full activation transfer, can be combined with traditional knowledge distillation (KD), and notably, achieves significant gains on ImageNet where they claim other KD methods have struggled.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Novel and Intuitive Approach:** The paper introduces a simple yet powerful idea. Instead of forcing a student to replicate a teacher's complex internal representations verbatim (as in FitNets), it distills a higher-level concept: the spatial focus of attention. This is conceptually elegant and the proposed activation-based method is computationally cheap and easy to implement.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Support:** The claims are well-supported by a thorough set of experiments across multiple datasets and state-of-the-art architectures. The consistent improvements, especially the significant 1.1% top-1 accuracy gain for ResNet-18 on ImageNet, demonstrate the method's practical value and effectiveness in a challenging, large-scale setting.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The core strength of this paper lies in its re-framing of hint-based knowledge transfer. The authors make a compelling case, supported by results, that the *summary* of activations contains more valuable transferable knowledge than the full activation tensor itself.

*   **Activation-based AT vs. Full Activation Transfer:** The experiment in Table 1 and Figure 7(b) showing that AT provides much better final accuracy than transferring full activations (F-ActT) is a crucial result. It suggests that forcing a smaller student network to match a teacher's high-dimensional feature space exactly may be an overly restrictive constraint. In contrast, guiding the student to simply "look at the right places" provides a more effective regularization, allowing the student to develop its own efficient internal representations for those regions. This is a significant insight for the knowledge distillation community.

*   **Robustness and Generality:** The method's effectiveness is demonstrated across a variety of scenarios:
    *   **Architectures:** WRNs and ResNets.
    *   **Datasets:** From small-scale (CIFAR) to large-scale (ImageNet) and fine-grained (CUB).
    *   **Tasks:** Standard classification and transfer learning/fine-tuning.
    *   **Teacher-Student Setups:** Both same-depth (WRN-16-2 -> WRN-16-1) and different-depth (ResNet-34 -> ResNet-18) transfers are shown to be effective.

*   **Gradient-based AT:** While the experiments for this method are more preliminary, it presents an interesting and complementary direction. The idea of enforcing symmetry via a "symmetry norm" on gradient attention maps (Eq. 6) is a clever self-regularization technique that could be explored further on its own. The authors are transparent about the method's current limitations (e.g., complexity with batch normalization), which is commendable.

*   **ImageNet Results:** The successful application to ImageNet is a major selling point. The authors claim that AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied to ImageNet. While this claim might be slightly too strong (see feedback below), achieving a >1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18 is non-trivial and validates the method's scalability and utility.

**4. Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Clarity on Hyperparameter `β`:** The description of how `β` is set ("10^3 divided by number of elements in attention map and batch size") is quite heuristic. Could you provide more intuition on this? It seems to be a way of normalizing the magnitude of the attention loss relative to the cross-entropy loss. How sensitive is the final performance to this hyperparameter? A small sensitivity analysis would strengthen the paper's claims of simplicity and ease of use.

*   **Overstated Claim on ImageNet KD:** The statement "we could not find applications of FitNets, KD or similar methods on ImageNet in the literature" is likely inaccurate. Several works have explored knowledge distillation on ImageNet, though sometimes with mixed or difficult-to-reproduce results. I would recommend toning down this claim to something like, "In contrast to some prior knowledge transfer methods that have shown limited success or require complex tuning on large-scale datasets, our proposed attention transfer provides a simple and effective path to significant improvements on ImageNet." This does not diminish the strength of your excellent result but places it more accurately within the literature.

*   **Gradient-based AT and Batch Normalization:** You correctly identify that the interaction with batch normalization during the second backpropagation step is unclear. This is a key hurdle for the practical application of this method to modern networks. Do you have any preliminary thoughts on how this could be handled? For instance, would one freeze the batch statistics during the second pass, or compute a second set of statistics? Exploring this would be a very interesting avenue for future work.

*   **Choice of Attention Mapping Function:** Table 2 shows that `F_2sum` performs best on the WRN-16-2/WRN-16-1 pair, and you use it for subsequent experiments. Is this choice robust across different architectures and datasets? Intuitively, a higher power `p` places more emphasis on the most highly activated regions. Is there a connection between the optimal `p` and the nature of the dataset (e.g., fine-grained vs. general)?
