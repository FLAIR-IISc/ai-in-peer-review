### Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel knowledge transfer mechanism called Attention Transfer (AT), where a student network is trained to mimic the spatial attention patterns of a larger, pre-trained teacher network. The core idea is that the spatial regions a powerful model "looks at" constitute a valuable form of knowledge that can guide the training of a smaller model. The authors introduce two distinct methods for defining these spatial attention maps: one based on summary statistics of activation maps within the network (activation-based AT), and another based on the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image (gradient-based AT). Through extensive experiments, the paper demonstrates that activation-based AT significantly improves the performance of student networks across a variety of datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, WRN, ResNet). Notably, the proposed method is shown to outperform transferring full activation maps (a la FitNets) and can be effectively combined with traditional knowledge distillation.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Effective Method:** The paper introduces a simple, intuitive, and highly effective method for knowledge transfer. The concept of using summarized spatial attention maps as the medium for distillation, rather than logits (KD) or full feature maps (FitNets), is a novel contribution that is well-motivated and easy to implement.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Evidence:** The claims are supported by a thorough and convincing set of experiments. The consistent improvements across multiple datasets, architectures, and training paradigms (from scratch, fine-tuning) are compelling. The reported 1.1% top-1 accuracy gain for ResNet-18 on ImageNet is a particularly strong result that validates the method's effectiveness on large-scale, challenging benchmarks.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this work lies in the elegance and demonstrated power of activation-based attention transfer. The authors convincingly show that this is not just another variant of hint-based learning. The direct comparison in Table 1 against transferring full activations ("F-ActT") is a critical experiment, revealing that the proposed summarization of activations into a 2D attention map is significantly more beneficial than transferring the entire, high-dimensional activation tensor. This suggests that AT provides a more distilled and potent form of guidance, perhaps by focusing the student on learning the teacher's spatial reasoning process without overwhelming it with low-level feature details.

The experimental setup is rigorous. The authors test their method on both residual and non-residual networks, with student/teacher pairs of both same and different depths. The ablation study on different attention mapping functions (Table 2) is valuable, as is the analysis showing that transfer losses at multiple network stages are beneficial. The success of AT on fine-tuning tasks (CUB, Scenes) further broadens the applicability of the method.

Finally, the paper makes a strong case for the utility of AT in scenarios where standard knowledge distillation struggles. The authors report that KD failed to provide benefits for their ResNet-18/34 pair on ImageNet, whereas AT delivered a substantial improvement. This positions AT as a valuable and perhaps more robust tool in the knowledge transfer toolkit, especially for transferring knowledge between networks of different architectures or depths on complex datasets. While the gradient-based attention transfer section is less developed, the activation-based method alone is a significant contribution to the field.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Analysis of "Why AT Works":** The paper does an excellent job of showing *that* AT works, but could benefit from a deeper discussion on *why* it is more effective than transferring full activations. Is it because AT acts as a stronger regularizer by abstracting away from specific feature implementations? Does it prevent the student from being constrained by the teacher's exact feature values, allowing it more flexibility while still enforcing high-level spatial focus? A bit more qualitative analysis or discussion here would be insightful.
*   **Gradient-Based AT Section:** This section feels more preliminary than the rest of the paper. The experiments are limited to a specific setting (NIN without BN on CIFAR) due to technical challenges with double backpropagation and batch normalization. While the results are interesting (especially the symmetry norm), the contribution is less impactful than the activation-based AT. It might be beneficial to frame this section more explicitly as an exploration of a promising future direction, clarifying the current limitations.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions a heuristic for setting the loss weight `β`. It would be helpful to include a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the method to this hyperparameter. Does the performance vary significantly if `β` is an order of magnitude larger or smaller? A small sensitivity analysis would increase confidence in the method's robustness.
*   **Wording of "First on ImageNet":** The claim of being "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is very strong. While it may be true for hint-based transfer between different architectures at the time of writing, it is difficult to verify definitively. I would suggest softening this to something like: "To our knowledge, this represents one of the first successful applications of hint-based knowledge transfer on the ImageNet dataset, particularly in a setting where standard knowledge distillation did not yield improvements."

### Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate clarification on the following points to improve my understanding of the work:

1.  Could you elaborate on the negative result with Knowledge Distillation on the ImageNet ResNet-18/34 experiment? You mention it "hurts convergence." This is a very interesting finding. Do you have a hypothesis for why this occurs? Is it possible that the logits of the deeper ResNet-34 are too complex or sharp for the shallower ResNet-18 to mimic effectively on such a complex dataset, leading to conflicting gradient signals?
2.  Table 2 shows that the `F^2_sum` attention mapping function works best on CIFAR-10. Did you experiment with different mapping functions on the fine-grained CUB dataset? One might hypothesize that a function with a higher power `p` (e.g., `p=4`), which more strongly emphasizes peak activations, would be particularly beneficial for fine-grained tasks where the key is to focus on small, highly discriminative regions.
3.  Regarding the technical challenges of gradient-based AT with Batch Normalization: Have you considered alternatives to a full second backpropagation through the BN layers? For instance, could the BN statistics from the first forward pass be frozen and re-used during the second backpropagation, or could gradients be taken with respect to the normalized activations (post-BN) instead of the network inputs, thus isolating the effect to a single block?
