This paper introduces "Attention Transfer" (AT), a novel and compelling method for knowledge transfer. The core idea is to train a smaller "student" network to mimic the spatial attention patterns of a more powerful "teacher" network. The authors propose two distinct ways to define these attention maps: 1) activation-based maps, which are spatial summaries of the feature activations within a layer, and 2) gradient-based maps, which represent the sensitivity of the output to the input. By adding a loss term that encourages the student's attention maps to match the teacher's, the paper demonstrates significant and consistent performance improvements for the student network across a variety of datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and modern architectures (ResNets, WRNs). One of the most significant claims is that transferring this compressed "attention" information is more effective than transferring the full, detailed activation tensors, as is done in prior work.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that presents a bold, intuitive, and effective new direction for knowledge distillation. The central concept of transferring a network's "focus" is a significant conceptual advance over existing hint-based methods. The strength of the idea, combined with convincing empirical evidence of its effectiveness—particularly on the challenging ImageNet benchmark—makes this a valuable contribution that is likely to inspire follow-up work.

**Supporting Arguments for the Decision:**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The primary strength of this paper is its core idea. Shifting the focus of knowledge transfer from mimicking raw feature values ("what" a network sees) to mimicking spatial attention ("where" a network looks) is a simple but profound change. It provides a more abstract and potentially more powerful form of guidance. This is a genuinely new concept in the knowledge distillation space, moving beyond simple logits-matching or feature regression. The idea is intuitive and well-motivated.

2.  **Strong and Surprising Empirical Results:** The paper provides compelling evidence that the proposed method works and works well. The consistent gains across multiple datasets and architectures demonstrate its generality. Most notably, the positive result on ImageNet (a 1.1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18) is a significant achievement, especially given the authors' claim that standard Knowledge Distillation and FitNets-style methods are difficult to apply successfully in this setting. The most interesting result is the direct comparison with full-activation transfer, which shows that transferring the summarized attention map is *more* effective. This non-obvious finding suggests that providing high-level strategic guidance is more beneficial than forcing the student to replicate the teacher's precise internal representations, which might be overly constraining.

3.  **Bold Exploration of Multiple Formulations:** The paper proposes and investigates two fundamentally different approaches to defining attention (activation-based and gradient-based). While the activation-based method is more thoroughly validated and appears more practical, the exploration of the computationally intensive gradient-based method is laudable. It demonstrates a commitment to exploring the core idea from different angles and provides interesting preliminary results, such as the effectiveness of the symmetry norm, which could be an interesting regularization technique in its own right.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

While I am strongly in favor of acceptance, the following points could help strengthen the paper further. These are suggestions for improvement, not obstacles to publication.

*   **Refining the Gradient-Based Section:** The experiments for gradient-based attention transfer are clearly preliminary. The authors are transparent about the limitations (no BN, simplified data augmentation). Given that the activation-based method is the star of the show, it might be better to frame the gradient-based section more explicitly as a preliminary investigation or future work, perhaps moving some of it to an appendix to tighten the main narrative around the highly successful activation-based approach.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions that the loss weight `β` is set based on the size of the attention map. A brief discussion or ablation study on the sensitivity to this hyperparameter would be beneficial. How crucial is its exact value for successful training?
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** The visualizations of attention maps are very helpful. It would be even more insightful to see a few examples where the student network *initially* has a poor attention map (compared to the teacher) and then see how it improves over the course of AT training. This would provide a more dynamic view of how the method works.
*   **The "First on ImageNet" Claim:** The claim of being "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" should be phrased more carefully. While it may be true for hint-based/intermediate representation transfer methods, standard knowledge distillation has been applied to ImageNet. Clarifying this distinction (e.g., "the first *hint-based* method...") would make the claim more precise and less open to dispute.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The comparison against full-activation transfer is a key result. Can you speculate further on *why* transferring less information (the attention summary) is more effective than transferring the entire activation tensor? Is it a form of implicit regularization, preventing the student from overfitting to the teacher's specific feature implementation?
2.  Your difficulties with applying standard Knowledge Distillation (KD) on the ImageNet ResNet-18/34 pair are interesting. Could you provide more detail on what went wrong? Did the training become unstable, or did it simply converge to a worse result than the baseline? Understanding the failure modes of KD in this setting helps to better contextualize the success of AT.
3.  Have you considered applying attention transfer between different modalities? For example, could attention maps derived from a different data source (e.g., heatmaps from human gaze) be used to guide a student network's training?
