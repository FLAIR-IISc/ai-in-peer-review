### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces "Attention Transfer" (AT), a method for knowledge transfer from a high-performing teacher network to a smaller student network. The core idea is to compel the student network to mimic the "attention maps" of the teacher. The authors propose two distinct types of attention maps. The first, "activation-based," is a novel formulation derived by summarizing activation tensors across the channel dimension (e.g., by summing the squared values of activations at each spatial location). The second, "gradient-based," uses the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image, a concept similar to existing saliency and input regularization methods. The authors present experiments across several datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN), claiming that their proposed attention transfer method, particularly the activation-based variant, provides significant performance improvements. They further claim that AT is superior to transferring full activation maps (a-la FitNets) and is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied to ImageNet.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

1.  **Insufficient Validation Against Established Baselines:** The central claim that transferring summarized "attention maps" is superior to transferring full feature maps is not adequately supported. The comparison to FitNets-style transfer ("F-ActT") is limited to a single table on CIFAR-10 with minimal implementation details, making it difficult to assess the fairness and rigor of the comparison. This is the paper's primary claim of novelty over prior art, and the evidence provided is not compelling.

2.  **Significant Methodological Weaknesses and Limited Novelty of Proposed Methods:** The proposed "attention maps" lack strong theoretical grounding. The activation-based method is a heuristic pooling operation, and it is not clear why this specific information compression is beneficial. The gradient-based method is a minor variation of the well-established "double backpropagation" regularizer from Drucker & LeCun (1992). Furthermore, its evaluation is confined to a non-standard setting (no batch normalization) due to unresolved technical challenges, rendering its practical utility for modern architectures questionable.

### Supporting Arguments

**On Validation:** The paper's most critical contribution is the idea that transferring a compressed representation of activations (the proposed "attention map") is more effective than transferring the full activations, which is the standard approach in hint-based knowledge transfer methods like FitNets. The evidence for this claim rests almost entirely on Table 1, which shows AT outperforming "F-ActT". However, this baseline is a custom implementation. The performance of such methods is highly sensitive to the design of the regressor (e.g., 1x1 convolutions), the choice of loss function, and the weighting of the hint loss (β). The paper provides insufficient detail to ascertain whether "F-ActT" was implemented and tuned to its full potential. A more convincing study would involve a thorough comparison against a well-established FitNets implementation, including ablation studies on the hint loss weight and regressor architecture.

Furthermore, the claim in Section 4.2.2 that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is a significant overstatement and likely incorrect. Various forms of knowledge transfer have been explored in the literature for large-scale datasets. The authors' failure to get Knowledge Distillation (KD) to work on ImageNet is more likely an issue of hyperparameter tuning than a fundamental failure of the method itself, and it does not justify the claim of being the first successful application of any transfer method.

**On Methodology:** The activation-based attention formulation, `F_psum(A) = ∑|Ai|^p`, is presented without a clear motivation. It is essentially a form of channel-wise pooling. While the empirical results are positive relative to the student baseline, the paper does not provide insight into *why* this specific summary statistic should be a more effective transfer signal than the full feature map. One might hypothesize it acts as a form of regularization, forcing the student to focus on spatial locations rather than mimicking the teacher's exact feature representation, but this is not explored.

The gradient-based transfer method is presented as a novel contribution, but it is functionally very similar to regularizing the L2 norm of the input-output Jacobian, a technique dating back decades. The novelty lies in using a teacher's gradient as the target instead of zero. However, this section is methodologically weak. The authors state they could not integrate it with batch normalization, forcing them to conduct experiments on a simplified NIN architecture without standard augmentations. This severely limits the applicability and relevance of the findings to current state-of-the-art models. Given that the simpler, cheaper activation-based method is shown to be superior (Table 3), the contribution of the gradient-based section appears marginal.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

1.  **Strengthen Baseline Comparisons:** The paper would be significantly stronger if the comparison to FitNets-style transfer was more rigorous. I recommend either using an official implementation or providing a much more detailed account of your own implementation and tuning process to convince the reader that it is a fair and strong baseline.

2.  **Provide Deeper Motivation:** The motivation for using the specific activation-based attention map formulations is lacking. Please add discussion on why this form of information compression might be beneficial. Does it prevent the student from "overfitting" to the teacher's features? Does it provide a more stable signal?

3.  **Revisit Claims of Novelty:** Please revise the claim about being the first to apply knowledge transfer to ImageNet. A more thorough literature search is needed. Claims should be precise and well-supported.

4.  **Gradient-Based Method:** Given the unresolved issues with batch normalization and its inferior performance compared to the activation-based method, I suggest either strengthening this section by resolving the technical issues or considering its removal to focus the paper on the more promising activation-based approach.

### Questions for the Authors

*   Could you elaborate on the implementation details of the "F-ActT" baseline in Table 1? Specifically, what was the architecture of the 1x1 convolutional layers used for regression, and was the loss weight β tuned separately for this baseline?
*   What is the intuition for why discarding channel-wise information via the `F_sum` operation provides a better knowledge transfer signal than the full activation tensor, which contains richer information?
*   Regarding the ImageNet experiments, the student (ResNet-18) and teacher (ResNet-34) networks are quite similar. Have you explored AT between more architecturally diverse pairs, where knowledge transfer is often more challenging?
