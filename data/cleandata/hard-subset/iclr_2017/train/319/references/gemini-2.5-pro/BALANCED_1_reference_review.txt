### 1. Summary

This paper introduces Attention Transfer (AT), a novel knowledge transfer method designed to improve the performance of a smaller "student" neural network by leveraging guidance from a larger, pre-trained "teacher" network. The core idea is that the student network is trained not only on the primary task loss but also to mimic the teacher's spatial "attention" patterns. The authors propose two distinct ways to define these spatial attention maps: 1) activation-based, where maps are generated by computing summary statistics (e.g., sum of squared values) across the channel dimension of activation tensors at various layers, and 2) gradient-based, which uses the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image as a sensitivity map. The paper provides an extensive empirical evaluation across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN). The results demonstrate that AT consistently improves student model accuracy, can be effectively combined with traditional knowledge distillation, and notably, that transferring summarized attention maps is more effective than transferring the full, dense activation tensors as done in prior work like FitNets.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Effective Method:** The paper presents a simple, intuitive, and highly effective method for knowledge transfer. The idea of using summarized spatial attention maps as the medium for transfer is a clever and efficient simplification of transferring entire feature maps, and the results convincingly show it is more beneficial.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The claims are backed by extensive experiments across a variety of standard benchmarks, from small-scale (CIFAR) to large-scale (ImageNet). The positive results on ImageNet are particularly significant, as many knowledge transfer techniques are not validated at this scale. This demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed method for training efficient yet powerful models.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

**Strengths:**
*   The paper's primary contribution, activation-based attention transfer, is well-motivated and straightforward to implement. It requires no additional learnable parameters and adds minimal computational overhead during training (just a forward pass through the frozen teacher).
*   The experimental results are strong and consistent. AT provides a significant boost over student baselines across all tested datasets and architectures (Tables 1, 4, 5). The improvement of 1.1% top-1 accuracy for a ResNet-18 on ImageNet is a particularly compelling result.
*   The comparison against a FitNets-style transfer of full activations (Table 1, "F-ActT") is a crucial experiment that directly supports the paper's central hypothesis: the compressed information in attention maps is a more valuable "hint" for the student than the complete, and possibly noisy, activation tensors.
*   The paper includes valuable ablation studies, such as the analysis of different activation-based mapping functions (Table 2) and the investigation of placing transfer losses at different network depths (Section 4.1.1), which strengthen the authors' conclusions and provide practical insights.
*   The demonstration that AT can be successfully combined with Knowledge Distillation (KD), often leading to further gains, shows that the proposed method captures a complementary form of knowledge not present in the final output logits.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The section on gradient-based attention transfer is significantly less developed than the activation-based portion. The experiments are confined to a simplified setting on CIFAR-10 (NIN without batch normalization), and the authors themselves highlight unresolved technical challenges regarding its application to modern architectures. This part of the contribution feels preliminary and lacks the convincing evidence presented for activation-based AT.
*   The selection of the hyperparameter β, which balances the standard loss and the attention transfer loss, is based on a heuristic. While it appears to work well, the paper would benefit from a more thorough sensitivity analysis to understand how robust the method is to the choice of this crucial parameter.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to be constructive and are not critical to the decision to accept.
*   The motivation drawing parallels to human attention in the introduction is a good framing device, but the connection remains metaphorical. The specific activation mapping functions (e.g., sum of squares) are not directly derived from cognitive science principles. Strengthening this link or slightly toning down the analogy could make the introduction more precise.
*   The qualitative visualizations are helpful, but could be expanded. For instance, showing a side-by-side comparison of a student's attention maps before and after AT training on a specific example it initially misclassifies could provide powerful visual evidence of how the method works.
*   For the gradient-based method, it might be interesting to explore using more refined saliency maps from the teacher, such as those from Grad-CAM or Guided Backpropagation, instead of the raw input gradients. These maps are often less noisy and more class-discriminative, which could provide a better-quality teaching signal.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate clarification on the following points to further solidify my understanding of the work:
1.  You note that β is decayed when combining AT with KD. Could you please provide more details on the decay schedule used? What is the intuition for this decay—is it to allow the model to focus more on the "hard" KD loss later in training? Have you explored if a similar decay schedule is beneficial for AT when used alone?
2.  Your finding that knowledge distillation performs poorly on ImageNet in the ResNet-18/34 setting is very interesting. Do you have any hypotheses for why KD struggles in this cross-architecture, large-scale setting, while your attention transfer method succeeds? Does this suggest that matching intermediate representations is a more robust transfer strategy than matching output distributions for significantly different architectures?
3.  Regarding the technical challenges of gradient-based AT with batch normalization: Could you elaborate on the specific issue? Is the problem that the second backpropagation would incorrectly update the running mean/variance statistics of the BN layers, or is there a more fundamental difficulty in computing the second-order derivatives through the BN operation?
4.  Could you provide a brief analysis of the computational overhead? Specifically, what is the approximate percentage increase in training time for a ResNet-18 on ImageNet when using activation-based AT compared to the baseline training?
