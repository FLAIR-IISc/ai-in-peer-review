### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Attention Transfer (AT), a novel method for transferring knowledge from a high-performing teacher network to a smaller student network. The core idea is to compel the student network to mimic the spatial attention patterns of the teacher. The paper proposes two distinct methods for defining these spatial attention maps: (1) an activation-based method, which constructs maps from summary statistics (e.g., sum of absolute values) of activation tensors across channels, and (2) a gradient-based method, which uses the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image as a sensitivity map. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments that this approach provides significant performance improvements for the student network. The method is evaluated across a variety of datasets, including CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, and on several modern architectures like Network-In-Network and Wide Residual Networks. Key findings include that activation-based AT outperforms a FitNets-style transfer of full activation tensors, it can be synergistically combined with traditional Knowledge Distillation (KD), and it successfully scales to the large-scale ImageNet dataset, improving a ResNet-18 model's accuracy by leveraging a ResNet-34 teacher.

### 2. Decision: Accept

This paper should be accepted. The key reasons for this decision are:
1.  The proposed method, Attention Transfer, is a novel and intuitive contribution to the field of knowledge distillation. It introduces a new form of information to be transferred—spatial attention—that is conceptually distinct from prior work focusing on output logits or full intermediate feature maps.
2.  The claims are supported by a comprehensive and rigorous set of experiments across multiple datasets and modern, relevant architectures. The finding that transferring a compressed "attention map" is more effective than transferring the full activation tensor is a significant and non-obvious result.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The motivation for using attention as a knowledge transfer mechanism is well-articulated, drawing parallels to human perception. The proposed methods for both activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer are defined precisely with clear mathematical formulations (Eq. 2 and 4). The experimental setup and results are presented in a straightforward manner, making the work easy to follow and understand.

**Technical Soundness:** The methodology is sound. The definitions of activation-based attention maps are simple yet effective heuristics grounded in the visualization literature. The use of l2-normalization on the vectorized attention maps before applying the transfer loss is a critical and well-justified detail for stable training. The gradient-based approach is correctly framed as an application of double backpropagation. The experimental design is robust, utilizing strong baselines (e.g., Wide ResNets on CIFAR) and a variety of tasks (image classification, fine-grained recognition). Crucially, the authors include a direct comparison against transferring full activation tensors (a FitNets-style approach), which serves as a powerful ablation study validating their core hypothesis that the summarized attention information is more valuable for transfer than the raw features.

**Novelty:** The primary contribution is the novel concept of using spatial attention maps as the "hint" in a teacher-student framework. This contrasts with Hinton et al. (2015), which distills knowledge from the final output distribution, and Romero et al. (2014), which transfers knowledge from the full intermediate feature maps. This paper demonstrates that a compressed, summary representation of intermediate features, focused on spatial importance, can be a more effective medium for knowledge transfer. The successful application of this technique to ImageNet is also a significant contribution, as the authors note that many competing knowledge transfer methods have not been shown to work effectively at this scale.

### 4. Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to the decision.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The term "attention" for the activation-based maps is used heuristically. While justified by the visualization literature, these maps are fundamentally summary statistics of activation magnitudes rather than a learned attention mechanism. It might be beneficial to briefly acknowledge this distinction to avoid confusion with other forms of attention (e.g., self-attention).
*   The experiments on gradient-based attention transfer are limited to a specific setting (NIN on CIFAR without batch normalization). While the authors are transparent about the technical challenges, this section would be strengthened if they could offer some discussion on potential strategies for handling batch normalization statistics during the second backpropagation step.
*   The parameter `β` is described as being set via a heuristic based on the size of the attention map. A small sensitivity analysis or discussion on the effect of this hyperparameter would add to the paper's completeness.

**Questions for Clarification:**
1.  One of the most compelling findings is that transferring the summarized attention map is more effective than transferring the full activation tensor. Could you elaborate on your intuition for why this is the case? Does the summation/max operation act as a form of noise reduction, forcing the student to focus only on the most salient spatial information from the teacher while ignoring low-level feature details that might be specific to the teacher's larger capacity?
2.  The "symmetry norm" regularizer (Eq. 6) for gradient maps showed strong performance on its own (Table 3). Have you considered evaluating this as a standalone regularization technique, independent of attention transfer, on larger-scale experiments? It appears to be a generally applicable method for enforcing flip equivariance.
3.  For the ImageNet experiments, you mention that transfer losses were applied only to the last two residual groups and that you planned to update the paper with results using all four. Have you completed this experiment? If so, did adding more transfer points further improve performance, or is there a point of diminishing returns?
