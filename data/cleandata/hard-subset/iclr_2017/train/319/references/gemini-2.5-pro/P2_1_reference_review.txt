### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Attention Transfer (AT), a novel and intuitive method for knowledge transfer from a high-performing "teacher" network to a smaller "student" network. The core idea is that a student network can be improved by training it to mimic the spatial attention patterns of its teacher. The authors propose and investigate two distinct forms of attention maps: (1) **activation-based maps**, which are spatial summaries of feature activations within a layer (e.g., sum of absolute values across channels), and (2) **gradient-based maps**, which represent the sensitivity of the network's output to its input pixels. The proposed method is evaluated across a variety of datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, ImageNet) and modern architectures (NIN, ResNet, Wide ResNet). The key contributions are the proposal of attention as a new knowledge transfer medium, the demonstration that it consistently improves student performance, the finding that it is more effective than transferring full, unsummarized activation tensors, and its successful application to the large-scale ImageNet dataset, where other knowledge transfer methods have reportedly struggled.

### **Decision and Justification**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper with a clear, simple, and effective idea. My decision to accept is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Novel and Intuitive Approach:** The paper presents a compelling new mechanism for knowledge distillation. Instead of forcing a student to match a teacher's raw logits (as in Knowledge Distillation) or entire intermediate feature maps (as in FitNets), Attention Transfer distills a more abstract and arguably more fundamental piece of information: *where* the teacher network is looking. This is an elegant idea that is well-motivated by the role of attention in both human and machine perception.

2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Evidence:** The authors provide convincing evidence that AT works and is widely applicable. The method delivers significant performance improvements across multiple datasets, architectures, and tasks (classification from scratch and fine-tuning). The experiments on ImageNet, showing a >1% top-1 accuracy gain for ResNet-18, are particularly impressive and represent a significant result for the knowledge transfer field. The ablation studies and direct comparison against full-activation transfer further strengthen the paper's claims.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's strengths lie in its novelty, effectiveness, and thorough evaluation.

*   **Novelty and Insight:** The key insight is that summarizing feature maps into spatial attention maps is a better form of "hint" for the student. The experimental result showing AT outperforms full-activation transfer (Table 1, F-ActT column) provides strong support for this hypothesis. It suggests that forcing a smaller student to exactly replicate the rich feature space of a larger teacher is too restrictive, whereas guiding its spatial focus is a more effective and flexible form of supervision.

*   **Effectiveness and Generality:** The gains reported are consistent and meaningful. For instance, AT closes a significant portion of the accuracy gap between teacher and student models in the fine-tuning experiments on CUB and Scenes (Table 4). The activation-based method is simple to implement, adds minimal computational overhead, and proves effective for both residual and non-residual networks.

*   **Rigorous Evaluation:** The paper includes valuable analysis. The comparison of different attention mapping functions (Table 2) provides practical guidance. The qualitative visualizations (e.g., Fig. 4) help build intuition for why the method works, showing that better models have more focused attention maps. The successful combination of AT with knowledge distillation (AT+KD) further demonstrates its utility as a complementary technique.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, a few points could be addressed to improve it further.

*   **Gradient-Based AT Section:** The section on gradient-based attention transfer (3.2, 4.1.3) feels less developed than the activation-based counterpart. The experiments are confined to a non-standard setting (NIN without batch normalization on CIFAR) due to technical challenges. While the idea is interesting, its limited evaluation makes it a much weaker contribution. The paper might be stronger and more focused if this section were condensed or perhaps moved to the appendix, allowing the main body to focus entirely on the highly successful activation-based method.

*   **Strong Claims:** The paper claims that AT is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." This is a very strong claim. While the authors justify it by noting the difficulties of other methods like KD and FitNets in this setting, it would be beneficial to soften the language slightly or provide a more exhaustive literature search to be certain of this positioning.

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The main hyperparameter, β, is set using a heuristic. While this seems to work well, the paper would benefit from a brief sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with different values of β. This would give readers a better sense of the method's robustness.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1.  You mention that KD struggles when the teacher and student have different architectures, particularly on ImageNet. Your method, AT, seems to work well in this exact scenario. What is your primary hypothesis for why guiding intermediate spatial attention is a more robust transfer signal than matching the final softmax distribution in these challenging, cross-architecture settings?
2.  Could you provide a more quantitative assessment of the computational overhead of activation-based AT? For example, what is the percentage increase in training time for the ResNet-18 student on ImageNet when AT is added?
3.  The gradient-based method requires a "double backpropagation." Can you confirm if this roughly doubles the per-iteration training time compared to standard training? What is the impact on memory usage?
4.  Given the importance of spatial reasoning, have you considered applying AT to other vision tasks like object detection or semantic segmentation? It seems like a very natural fit for such problems.
